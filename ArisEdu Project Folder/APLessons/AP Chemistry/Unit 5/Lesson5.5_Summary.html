AP Chemistry - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Reaction Mechanisms are the step-by-step sequences of elementary reactions that make up an overall reaction. Each elementary step has a molecularity (unimolecular, bimolecular, rarely termolecular) that directly determines its rate law. The rate-determining step (slowest step) controls the overall rate. A valid mechanism must: (1) have elementary steps that sum to the overall equation, (2) be consistent with the experimentally determined rate law, and (3) have each step be physically reasonable.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Elementary step: A single-step reaction event at the molecular level; its rate law can be written directly from its molecularity
  • Rate-determining step: The slowest elementary step in a mechanism; acts as a 'bottleneck' that controls the overall reaction rate
  • Intermediate: A species produced in one elementary step and consumed in a subsequent step; does not appear in the overall equation
  • Molecularity: The number of reactant particles in an elementary step: unimolecular (1), bimolecular (2), or termolecular (3, very rare)
  • Steady-state approximation: The assumption that the concentration of an intermediate remains approximately constant (rate of formation ≈ rate of consumption)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Ozone depletion mechanism: Cl from CFCs catalytically destroys O₃ in a two-step mechanism—Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂, then ClO + O → Cl + O₂ (Cl regenerated)
  • SN1 and SN2 mechanisms in organic chemistry: SN1 (two-step, carbocation intermediate) vs. SN2 (one-step, bimolecular) determine reaction outcomes in drug synthesis
  • Chain reaction mechanisms in combustion: initiation produces radicals, propagation sustains the reaction, termination removes radicals (relevant to engine knocking and explosives)
