AP Chemistry - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Spontaneity and Entropy explores the second law of thermodynamics: the entropy of the universe increases for any spontaneous process (ΔS_univ > 0). Entropy (S) measures the number of possible microstates (disorder). Entropy increases when: solids melt or liquids vaporize, gases expand, temperature increases, or the number of particles increases. The third law states that a perfect crystal at 0 K has S = 0. Standard molar entropy (S°) values allow calculation of ΔS°rxn = ΣS°(products) - ΣS°(reactants).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Entropy (S): A measure of the number of possible microstates (arrangements) of a system; greater disorder = higher entropy
  • Second law of thermodynamics: The entropy of the universe increases for any spontaneous process: ΔS_univ = ΔS_sys + ΔS_surr > 0
  • Third law of thermodynamics: The entropy of a perfect crystal at absolute zero (0 K) is zero
  • Microstates: The number of possible arrangements of particles and energy; related to entropy by Boltzmann equation: S = k ln W
  • ΔS prediction rules: Entropy increases for: solid→liquid→gas transitions, dissolution, increased temperature, increased number of moles of gas products

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • The spontaneous rusting of iron: despite being exothermic (ΔH < 0), rust formation is also driven by the entropy increase from solid Fe converting to flaky Fe₂O₃
  • Refrigerators and air conditioners: external work (electricity) is required to decrease entropy locally (cool the interior) while increasing total universal entropy
  • Dissolution of ammonium nitrate: endothermic (ΔH > 0) but spontaneous because the large increase in entropy (ΔS >> 0) from ions dispersing in solution makes ΔG < 0
