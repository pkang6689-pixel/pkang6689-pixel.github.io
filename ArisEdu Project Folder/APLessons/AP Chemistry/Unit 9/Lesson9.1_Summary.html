AP Chemistry - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Gibbs Free Energy (G) determines reaction spontaneity: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. A reaction is spontaneous when ΔG < 0, nonspontaneous when ΔG > 0, and at equilibrium when ΔG = 0. The relationship between ΔG° and K: ΔG° = -RT ln K. Reactions with ΔG° < 0 (K > 1) are spontaneous under standard conditions. Temperature determines spontaneity when ΔH and ΔS have the same sign: ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0 → always spontaneous; ΔH > 0 and ΔS < 0 → never spontaneous.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Gibbs free energy (ΔG): The energy available to do useful work; ΔG = ΔH - TΔS; negative ΔG = spontaneous
  • Spontaneous process: A process that occurs without continuous external input; ΔG < 0; does NOT mean fast (kinetics is separate from thermodynamics)
  • ΔG° = -RT ln K: Relates standard free energy change to the equilibrium constant; ΔG° < 0 means K > 1 (product-favored)
  • Temperature dependence: When ΔH and ΔS have the same sign, temperature determines spontaneity: T_crossover = ΔH/ΔS
  • Standard free energy of formation: ΔG°f: the free energy change for forming 1 mole of compound from elements in standard states; ΔG°rxn = ΣΔG°f(products) - ΣΔG°f(reactants)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Diamond-to-graphite conversion: thermodynamically spontaneous (ΔG < 0) but kinetically extremely slow—diamonds are 'metastable' and effectively permanent
  • Biological coupling: ATP hydrolysis (ΔG° = -30.5 kJ/mol) drives nonspontaneous reactions like protein synthesis and active transport in cells
  • Predicting reaction feasibility in industrial processes: ΔG calculations determine whether a proposed reaction path is thermodynamically viable before investing in equipment
