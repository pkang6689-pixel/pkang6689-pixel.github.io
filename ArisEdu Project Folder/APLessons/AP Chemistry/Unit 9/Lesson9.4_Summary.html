AP Chemistry - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electrochemical Cells and Batteries are classified as galvanic (voltaic) cells that convert chemical energy to electrical energy spontaneously (ΔG < 0, E° > 0), or electrolytic cells that use electrical energy to drive nonspontaneous reactions (ΔG > 0). Galvanic cells have two half-cells connected by a salt bridge (maintains electrical neutrality). The relationship ΔG° = -nFE° connects thermodynamics to electrochemistry. Faraday's laws relate charge transferred to moles of substance reacted.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Galvanic (voltaic) cell: An electrochemical cell that produces electricity from a spontaneous redox reaction; E°cell > 0, ΔG° < 0
  • Electrolytic cell: An electrochemical cell that uses external electricity to drive a nonspontaneous redox reaction; E°cell < 0, ΔG° > 0
  • Salt bridge: A tube filled with inert electrolyte (KNO₃, KCl) connecting two half-cells; allows ion migration to maintain electrical neutrality
  • ΔG° = -nFE°: Relates free energy to cell potential; n = moles of electrons transferred, F = Faraday's constant (96,485 C/mol)
  • Faraday's law of electrolysis: The mass of substance deposited/dissolved is proportional to the charge passed: moles = Q/nF, where Q = It (current × time)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Lithium-ion batteries: rechargeable cells using Li⁺ intercalation in graphite (anode) and metal oxide (cathode); powering smartphones, laptops, and electric vehicles
  • Electroplating using electrolysis: depositing a thin layer of metal (chrome, gold, nickel) onto objects for corrosion protection or aesthetics
  • Aluminum production by Hall-Héroult process: electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃ in cryolite at ~960°C; consumes ~5% of US electricity production
