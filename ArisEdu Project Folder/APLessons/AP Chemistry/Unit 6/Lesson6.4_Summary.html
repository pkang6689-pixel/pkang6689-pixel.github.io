AP Chemistry - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Bond Energy estimates reaction enthalpy from the energies required to break and form chemical bonds. ΔH°rxn ≈ Σ(bond energies broken) - Σ(bond energies formed). Breaking bonds requires energy (endothermic), forming bonds releases energy (exothermic). Average bond energies are approximations because actual bond energies vary with molecular context. Stronger bonds (triple > double > single) have shorter bond lengths and higher bond energies.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Bond energy (bond dissociation energy): The energy required to break one mole of a specific bond in the gas phase; always positive (endothermic)
  • Average bond energy: The average energy to break a particular type of bond across many different molecules; used for ΔH estimation
  • Bond order: The number of shared electron pairs between two atoms: single (1), double (2), triple (3); higher order = shorter, stronger bond
  • Bond length: The equilibrium distance between two bonded nuclei; decreases as bond order increases: triple < double < single
  • ΔH from bond energies: ΔH ≈ Σ(bonds broken) - Σ(bonds formed); positive ΔH if more energy is needed to break bonds than is released forming new ones

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Nitrogen's triple bond (N≡N, 945 kJ/mol) making N₂ extremely unreactive and requiring the high-energy Haber process to fix nitrogen for fertilizers
  • Ozone (O₃) having weaker average O-O bonds (~303 kJ/mol) than O₂ (498 kJ/mol), making it more reactive and useful as a disinfectant and water treatment agent
  • Fuel energy density comparison using bond energies: hydrogen (H-H = 436 kJ/mol) vs. octane (many C-H and C-C bonds) to predict energy released per gram
