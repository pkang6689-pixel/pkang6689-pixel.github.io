AP Chemistry - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Acid-Base Definitions have evolved from Arrhenius (acids produce H⁺, bases produce OH⁻ in water) to Brønsted-Lowry (acids donate H⁺, bases accept H⁺) to Lewis (acids accept electron pairs, bases donate electron pairs). The Brønsted-Lowry definition is most commonly used in AP Chemistry. Strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HClO₄) and strong bases (group 1/2 hydroxides) dissociate completely. Weak acids/bases partially dissociate with Ka/Kb values <1.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Arrhenius acid/base: Acid: produces H⁺(aq); base: produces OH⁻(aq); limited to aqueous solutions
  • Brønsted-Lowry acid/base: Acid: proton (H⁺) donor; base: proton acceptor; broader than Arrhenius, applicable in non-aqueous solutions
  • Lewis acid/base: Acid: electron pair acceptor; base: electron pair donor; the broadest definition, encompassing reactions without proton transfer
  • Strong vs. weak acid: Strong acids dissociate 100% (Ka very large); weak acids partially dissociate (Ka < 1, typically 10⁻² to 10⁻¹⁰)
  • Amphoteric substance: A substance that can act as either an acid or a base (e.g., water: H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻; Al(OH)₃)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Household acids and bases: vinegar (acetic acid, Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵), lemon juice (citric acid), baking soda (NaHCO₃ acts as a weak base)
  • Aluminum's amphoteric nature: Al(OH)₃ dissolves in both acids and bases, exploited in the Bayer process to purify alumina from bauxite ore
  • Lewis acid catalysis in organic synthesis: BF₃ and AlCl₃ as Lewis acids in Friedel-Crafts reactions for pharmaceutical manufacturing
