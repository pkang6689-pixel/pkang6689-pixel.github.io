AP Chemistry - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Buffer Solutions resist pH change when small amounts of acid or base are added. A buffer consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (e.g., CH₃COOH/CH₃COO⁻) or a weak base and its conjugate acid (e.g., NH₃/NH₄⁺). The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) predicts buffer pH. Buffer capacity depends on the total concentration of the acid-base pair and is maximum when [HA] = [A⁻] (pH = pKa).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Buffer solution: A solution that resists pH changes upon addition of small amounts of acid or base; contains a weak acid-conjugate base pair
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]); relates buffer pH to the ratio of conjugate base to weak acid concentrations
  • Buffer capacity: The amount of acid or base a buffer can absorb before significant pH change; maximized when [HA] = [A⁻] and increased by higher total concentration
  • Buffer range: The pH range over which a buffer effectively resists pH change; approximately pKa ± 1
  • Conjugate pair: A weak acid and its conjugate base (HA/A⁻) that together create a buffer system

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Blood bicarbonate buffer system: H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻ maintaining blood pH 7.35-7.45; regulated by breathing (CO₂ removal) and kidneys (HCO₃⁻ retention/excretion)
  • Biological research: phosphate buffer (H₂PO₄⁻/HPO₄²⁻, pKa = 7.20) commonly used in cell culture media to maintain physiological pH
  • Ocean buffering against acidification: carbonate/bicarbonate system absorbing excess CO₂, but capacity being overwhelmed by current emission rates
