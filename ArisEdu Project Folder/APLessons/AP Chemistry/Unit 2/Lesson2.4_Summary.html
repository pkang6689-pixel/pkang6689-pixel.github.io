AP Chemistry - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs) are attractions between molecules that determine physical properties like boiling point, viscosity, and surface tension. In order of increasing strength: London dispersion forces (LDFs, present in all molecules, increase with molar mass and surface area), dipole-dipole interactions (polar molecules), and hydrogen bonds (H bonded to N, O, or F interacting with lone pairs on N, O, or F). Ion-dipole forces are the strongest IMF and govern dissolution of ionic compounds in polar solvents.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • London dispersion forces: Temporary dipole-induced dipole attractions present in ALL molecules; strength increases with molar mass, polarizability, and surface area
  • Dipole-dipole interactions: Attractions between the positive end of one polar molecule and the negative end of another; present in polar molecules
  • Hydrogen bonding: A strong dipole-dipole interaction between H bonded to N, O, or F and a lone pair on another N, O, or F atom
  • Ion-dipole force: The attraction between an ion and a polar molecule; the dominant force when ionic compounds dissolve in water
  • Polarizability: The ease with which an electron cloud can be distorted; increases with atomic/molecular size and number of electrons

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Water's anomalously high boiling point (100°C vs. H₂S at -60°C) due to extensive hydrogen bonding networks between molecules
  • Gecko adhesion: billions of tiny setae on gecko feet exploit London dispersion forces to adhere to surfaces, inspiring synthetic 'gecko tape' technologies
  • Soap and detergent action: amphipathic molecules with ionic heads (ion-dipole with water) and nonpolar tails (LDF with grease) bridging oil and water
