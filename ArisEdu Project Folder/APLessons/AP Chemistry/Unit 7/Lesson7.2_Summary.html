AP Chemistry - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Equilibrium Constants quantify the position of equilibrium. Kc uses molar concentrations, Kp uses partial pressures for gases (Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn). K is dimensionless and temperature-dependent. Only gases and aqueous species appear in K expressions; pure solids and liquids are excluded (activity = 1). For the reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: K = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ/[A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ. Large K (>10³): product-favored; small K (<10⁻³): reactant-favored.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Equilibrium expression: K = [products]^coefficients / [reactants]^coefficients; concentrations at equilibrium; pure solids and liquids excluded
  • Kc: Equilibrium constant expressed in terms of molar concentrations (mol/L)
  • Kp: Equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures (atm); related to Kc by Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn
  • Heterogeneous equilibrium: Equilibrium involving species in more than one phase; pure solids and liquids have activity = 1 and are excluded from K
  • Manipulating K: If a reaction is reversed, K_new = 1/K; if multiplied by n, K_new = K^n; if reactions are added, K_net = K₁ × K₂

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Solubility predictions: Ksp of CaF₂ = 3.9 × 10⁻¹¹ indicates very low solubility, explaining why fluoride in drinking water doesn't exceed toxic levels
  • Ammonia synthesis economics: K for N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ is large at low T (favors products) but reaction is too slow; industrial compromise at ~450°C, 200 atm gives usable rate and yield
  • Blood buffering: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ with specific K values maintaining blood pH in the narrow range of 7.35-7.45
