AP Chemistry - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Dynamic Equilibrium is reached when a reversible reaction's forward and reverse rates become equal, with no net change in concentrations. At equilibrium, the system is NOT static—both reactions continue at equal rates (dynamic). Equilibrium can be approached from either direction and is characterized by constant macroscopic properties (concentrations, color, pressure). The time to reach equilibrium depends on kinetic factors, while the position of equilibrium depends on thermodynamic factors.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Reversible reaction: A reaction that can proceed in both forward and reverse directions, denoted by the double arrow ⇌
  • Dynamic equilibrium: The state where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; concentrations are constant but molecular-level reactions continue
  • Position of equilibrium: The relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium; lies to the right (product-favored) when K > 1
  • Closed system: A system that can exchange energy but not matter with the surroundings; required for true chemical equilibrium
  • Equilibrium vs. completion: Most reactions are reversible and reach equilibrium; very few go to completion (irreversible) unless products are removed from the system

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Nitrogen dioxide equilibrium (2NO₂ ⇌ N₂O₄): the brown-to-colorless color change visually demonstrates equilibrium shifting with temperature changes
  • Soda carbonation: CO₂(g) ⇌ CO₂(aq) equilibrium maintained under pressure; opening the bottle shifts equilibrium toward gas release (fizzing)
  • Hemoglobin-oxygen binding equilibrium shifting in lungs (high O₂, favoring HbO₂) vs. tissues (low O₂, favoring O₂ release)
