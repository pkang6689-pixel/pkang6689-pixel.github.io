AP Chemistry - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Stoichiometry and Limiting Reagents determine the maximum amount of product that can form in a reaction. The limiting reagent is completely consumed first and determines the theoretical yield. The excess reagent remains partially unreacted. Percent yield (actual/theoretical × 100%) measures reaction efficiency. Stoichiometric calculations convert between grams, moles, and particles using molar mass and mole ratios from balanced equations.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Limiting reagent: The reactant that is completely consumed first, determining the maximum amount of product (theoretical yield)
  • Excess reagent: The reactant that remains partially unreacted after the limiting reagent is consumed
  • Theoretical yield: The maximum amount of product calculated from stoichiometry assuming 100% conversion of the limiting reagent
  • Percent yield: (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100%; measures how efficient a reaction is in practice
  • Stoichiometric conversion: Using molar mass and mole ratios to convert between grams, moles, and number of particles in calculations

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Rocket fuel calculations: precise stoichiometry of liquid H₂ and O₂ (2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O) ensures maximum thrust without wasting expensive fuel
  • Industrial ammonia production optimizing the ratio of N₂ to H₂ to minimize waste and maximize yield in the Haber process
  • Baking chemistry: using the correct stoichiometric ratio of baking soda (NaHCO₃) to acid (e.g., cream of tartar) to produce the right amount of CO₂ for leavening
