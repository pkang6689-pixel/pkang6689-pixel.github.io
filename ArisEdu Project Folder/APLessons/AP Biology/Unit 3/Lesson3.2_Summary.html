AP Biology - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Enzyme Structure and Function explains how biological catalysts accelerate reactions by lowering activation energy without being consumed. Enzymes are typically proteins with a specific active site that binds substrates via the induced fit model. Enzyme activity follows Michaelis-Menten kinetics characterized by Vmax (maximum velocity) and Km (substrate concentration at half Vmax). Enzyme specificity arises from the precise 3D shape of the active site, which is determined by the amino acid sequence.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Enzyme: A biological catalyst (usually a protein) that speeds up a specific chemical reaction by lowering the activation energy
  • Active site: The specific region of an enzyme where the substrate binds and the catalytic reaction occurs; shape determines specificity
  • Induced fit model: The active site subtly changes shape upon substrate binding, optimizing the interaction and lowering the activation energy
  • Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for a reaction to proceed; enzymes lower Ea, increasing the reaction rate without altering ΔG
  • Michaelis-Menten kinetics: v = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]); describes the relationship between substrate concentration and reaction rate for many enzymes

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Lactose intolerance caused by deficiency of the enzyme lactase, which normally catalyzes the hydrolysis of lactose into glucose and galactose
  • Industrial enzyme applications: proteases in detergents, amylases in food processing, and DNA polymerase in PCR for forensics and diagnostics
  • Drug design targeting specific enzyme active sites: HIV protease inhibitors blocking viral protein processing to treat AIDS
