AP Biology - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Cellular Respiration: Electron Transport Chain (ETC) and oxidative phosphorylation occur on the inner mitochondrial membrane. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to protein complexes (I-IV), which pump H⁺ from the matrix to the intermembrane space, creating a proton gradient. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor, forming water. ATP synthase uses the proton-motive force (chemiosmosis) to produce ~30-32 ATP per glucose. The ETC produces the vast majority of ATP in aerobic respiration.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Electron transport chain: A series of protein complexes (I, II, III, IV) embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that transfer electrons and pump protons
  • Oxidative phosphorylation: ATP synthesis driven by the proton gradient across the inner mitochondrial membrane; produces ~30-32 ATP per glucose
  • ATP synthase: The enzyme that synthesizes ATP as H⁺ flows down its concentration gradient from the intermembrane space back to the matrix
  • Proton-motive force: The combination of the H⁺ concentration gradient and electrical potential across the inner mitochondrial membrane that drives ATP synthesis
  • Final electron acceptor: O₂; accepts electrons at the end of the ETC and combines with H⁺ to form water; without O₂, the ETC stops

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Cyanide poisoning blocking Complex IV of the ETC, preventing electron transfer to O₂ and halting all aerobic ATP production
  • Brown fat thermogenesis: uncoupling protein (UCP1) dissipates the proton gradient as heat instead of ATP, warming newborns and hibernating animals
  • Mitochondrial diseases caused by mutations in ETC complex genes, affecting energy-demanding tissues (brain, heart, muscles) and causing conditions like Leigh syndrome
