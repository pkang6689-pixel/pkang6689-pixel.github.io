AP Biology - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Cellular Respiration: Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm and breaks down one glucose molecule into two pyruvate molecules, producing a net gain of 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation) and 2 NADH. Glycolysis is anaerobic (does not require oxygen) and is the most evolutionarily ancient metabolic pathway, occurring in virtually all living organisms. The 10-step pathway is regulated at key irreversible steps by allosteric enzymes (hexokinase, PFK, pyruvate kinase).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Glycolysis: A 10-step metabolic pathway in the cytoplasm that converts glucose (6C) into 2 pyruvate (3C), producing 2 net ATP and 2 NADH
  • Substrate-level phosphorylation: Direct transfer of a phosphate group from a substrate molecule to ADP to form ATP; occurs in glycolysis and the Krebs cycle
  • Phosphofructokinase (PFK): The key allosteric regulatory enzyme of glycolysis; inhibited by ATP and citrate (when energy is abundant), activated by AMP and ADP
  • NAD⁺/NADH: An electron carrier; NAD⁺ is reduced to NADH during glycolysis (and other steps), carrying electrons to the electron transport chain
  • Fermentation: Anaerobic regeneration of NAD⁺ from NADH; alcohol fermentation produces ethanol + CO₂; lactic acid fermentation produces lactate

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Lactic acid fermentation in muscles during intense exercise when oxygen supply is insufficient for aerobic respiration, causing muscle fatigue and soreness
  • Brewing and winemaking using yeast alcohol fermentation to convert glucose to ethanol and CO₂
  • Cancer cell metabolism (Warburg effect): many cancer cells preferentially use glycolysis even in the presence of oxygen, a target for anticancer drug development
