AP Biology - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Carbohydrates: Structure and Function range from simple sugars (monosaccharides like glucose, C₆H₁₂O₆) to complex polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin). Monosaccharides join via glycosidic bonds through dehydration synthesis to form disaccharides and polysaccharides. The specific bond orientation (α vs. β linkages) determines digestibility and function: α-1,4 bonds in starch are digestible by humans, while β-1,4 bonds in cellulose are not.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Monosaccharide: A simple sugar (glucose, fructose, galactose) with the general formula (CH₂O)n; the monomer of carbohydrates
  • Glycosidic bond: A covalent bond formed between two sugar molecules by dehydration synthesis; α or β orientation determines the polysaccharide's properties
  • Starch: A storage polysaccharide in plants made of α-glucose linked by α-1,4 (amylose) and α-1,4/α-1,6 (amylopectin) glycosidic bonds
  • Cellulose: A structural polysaccharide in plant cell walls made of β-glucose linked by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds; forms rigid, indigestible fibers
  • Chitin: A structural polysaccharide in arthropod exoskeletons and fungal cell walls; similar to cellulose but with nitrogen-containing groups

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Blood glucose regulation via insulin and glucagon controlling the interconversion of glucose and glycogen in liver and muscle cells
  • Cellulose in dietary fiber promoting digestive health despite being indigestible because humans lack cellulase enzymes
  • Chitin-based biomaterials used in medical sutures and wound dressings due to their biocompatibility and structural strength
