AP Biology - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Nucleic Acids (DNA and RNA) store and transmit genetic information through sequences of nucleotide monomers. DNA's double helix has antiparallel strands connected by complementary base pairing (A-T with 2 H-bonds, G-C with 3 H-bonds), while RNA is typically single-stranded with uracil replacing thymine. The central dogma of molecular biology—DNA → RNA → Protein—describes the flow of genetic information in cells.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Nucleotide: The monomer of nucleic acids: a 5-carbon sugar (deoxyribose or ribose), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base
  • Complementary base pairing: A pairs with T (DNA) or U (RNA) via 2 hydrogen bonds; G pairs with C via 3 hydrogen bonds
  • Antiparallel strands: DNA's two strands run in opposite directions (5'→3' and 3'→5'), essential for replication and transcription
  • Phosphodiester bond: The covalent bond between the 3' carbon of one nucleotide's sugar and the 5' phosphate of the next, forming the sugar-phosphate backbone
  • Central dogma: The flow of genetic information: DNA is transcribed to mRNA, which is translated to protein

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • DNA profiling (forensic fingerprinting) using short tandem repeat (STR) polymorphisms for criminal identification with error rates below 1 in a billion
  • mRNA vaccines (Pfizer, Moderna COVID-19) delivering synthetic mRNA encoding spike protein, leveraging the central dogma to produce immune response
  • CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing using guide RNA complementary base pairing to target specific DNA sequences for precise genetic modification
