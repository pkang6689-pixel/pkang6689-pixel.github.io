AP Biology - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Gene Regulation controls when, where, and how much of a gene product is made. In prokaryotes, the lac operon model demonstrates inducible gene regulation: the repressor blocks transcription until lactose (inducer) binds and removes it, while CAP protein enhances transcription when glucose is low. In eukaryotes, regulation occurs at multiple levels: chromatin remodeling (acetylation, methylation), transcription factors, mRNA processing, mRNA stability, translation regulation, and post-translational modification.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Operon: A cluster of prokaryotic genes under control of a single promoter and operator, transcribed as one polycistronic mRNA (e.g., lac operon, trp operon)
  • Transcription factor: A protein that binds to regulatory DNA sequences (enhancers, silencers, promoters) to activate or repress transcription of specific genes
  • Epigenetics: Heritable changes in gene expression without altering the DNA sequence; includes DNA methylation (usually silencing) and histone modification
  • Chromatin remodeling: Changes in chromatin structure that affect gene accessibility: histone acetylation opens chromatin (activating), deacetylation compacts it (silencing)
  • microRNA (miRNA): Small non-coding RNA molecules (~22 nucleotides) that bind to complementary mRNA sequences, targeting them for degradation or blocking translation

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Cancer epigenetics: abnormal DNA methylation patterns silencing tumor suppressor genes or activating oncogenes without DNA mutations
  • Stem cell differentiation controlled by sequential gene regulation, with the same genome producing >200 different specialized cell types in the human body
  • Lac operon as a model for understanding antibiotic resistance gene regulation in bacteria, where environmental signals trigger expression of resistance genes
