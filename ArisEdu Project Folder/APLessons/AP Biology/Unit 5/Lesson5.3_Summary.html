AP Biology - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Non-Mendelian Inheritance includes patterns that do not follow simple dominant-recessive rules: incomplete dominance (blending phenotypes, e.g., red × white = pink in snapdragons), codominance (both alleles fully expressed, e.g., AB blood type), multiple alleles (>2 alleles in the population, e.g., ABO blood groups), polygenic inheritance (multiple genes affecting one trait), and epistasis (one gene affects expression of another). Sex-linked traits show characteristic patterns because males are hemizygous for X-linked genes.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Incomplete dominance: Heterozygotes show an intermediate phenotype between two homozygotes (e.g., Cʀ Cᵂ = pink flowers)
  • Codominance: Both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygote (e.g., Iᴬ Iᴮ blood type produces both A and B antigens)
  • Polygenic inheritance: Multiple genes contribute to a single trait, producing continuous variation (e.g., skin color, height, intelligence)
  • Epistasis: One gene affects the expression of another gene at a different locus (e.g., coat color in Labrador retrievers: B/b for color, E/e for deposition)
  • Sex-linked (X-linked) inheritance: Genes on the X chromosome; males (XY) express all X-linked alleles; conditions like hemophilia and color blindness are more common in males

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • ABO blood type compatibility in transfusions determined by codominant Iᴬ and Iᴮ alleles and recessive i allele (3 alleles, 6 genotypes, 4 phenotypes)
  • Human skin color as a polygenic trait involving at least 15 genes, producing continuous variation across populations adapted to different UV radiation levels
  • Hemophilia in European royal families: X-linked recessive inheritance traced through pedigrees from Queen Victoria to multiple royal houses
