AP Biology - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Community Ecology examines interactions between species in ecological communities. Interspecific competition (competition exclusion principle or resource partitioning), predation (including coevolutionary arms races), mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism shape community structure. Trophic structure (food webs), keystone species (disproportionate ecological impact), and ecological succession describe how communities are organized, maintained, and change over time.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Interspecific competition: Competition between different species for shared resources; leads to competitive exclusion or resource partitioning (niche differentiation)
  • Predation: One organism (predator) killing and consuming another (prey); drives coevolutionary adaptations like camouflage, warning coloration, and mimicry
  • Mutualism: An interaction beneficial to both species (e.g., mycorrhizal fungi and plant roots; clownfish and sea anemones)
  • Keystone species: A species whose impact on community structure is disproportionately large relative to its abundance (e.g., sea otters, wolves in Yellowstone)
  • Ecological succession: Predictable changes in community composition over time: primary (bare substrate) or secondary (after disturbance with existing soil)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Wolf reintroduction into Yellowstone (1995) demonstrating trophic cascades: wolves reduced elk overgrazing, allowing riparian vegetation recovery, which stabilized stream banks and increased beaver populations
  • Coral reef ecosystems as examples of high-diversity communities maintained by complex mutualistic and competitive interactions among thousands of species
  • Batesian mimicry in butterflies: harmless viceroy butterflies mimicking toxic monarch butterflies to avoid predation, a classic example of coevolutionary interaction
