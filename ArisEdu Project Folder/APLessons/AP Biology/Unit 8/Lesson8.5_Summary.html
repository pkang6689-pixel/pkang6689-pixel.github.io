AP Biology - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Biodiversity and Conservation addresses the variety of life at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels and the threats to it. Current extinction rates are 100-1,000 times higher than background rates due to habitat destruction, invasive species, overexploitation, pollution, and climate change (HIPPO threats). Conservation biology applies ecological principles to preserve biodiversity through protected areas, habitat restoration, captive breeding, and sustainable resource management.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Biodiversity: The variety of life at three levels: genetic diversity (within species), species diversity (number and relative abundance), and ecosystem diversity
  • Endangered species: A species at serious risk of extinction throughout all or a significant portion of its range, often due to small population size and habitat loss
  • Habitat destruction: The leading cause of biodiversity loss; conversion of natural habitats to agriculture, urban development, or resource extraction
  • Invasive species: Non-native organisms that spread aggressively in new environments, outcompeting native species (e.g., kudzu, cane toads, zebra mussels)
  • Biodiversity hotspot: A region with exceptionally high species richness and endemism that is under significant threat; 36 hotspots contain 50% of plant species on 2.5% of land area

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Amazon deforestation averaging ~10,000 km² per year, threatening the most biodiverse terrestrial ecosystem with an estimated 10% of all species on Earth
  • Captive breeding and reintroduction programs recovering the California condor from 22 individuals (1982) to over 500, including 300+ in the wild
  • Coral reef bleaching events threatening 25% of marine species that depend on reef ecosystems, driven by ocean warming and acidification from climate change
