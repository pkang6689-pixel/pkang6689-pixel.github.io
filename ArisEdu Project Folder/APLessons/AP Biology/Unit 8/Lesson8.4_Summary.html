AP Biology - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Biogeography studies the geographic distribution of species and the factors influencing it. Island biogeography theory (MacArthur and Wilson) predicts that species richness on an island reflects a balance between immigration and extinction rates, influenced by island size and distance from the mainland. Continental drift, climate zones, and barriers to dispersal explain global distribution patterns. Biogeography provides evidence for evolution and informs conservation strategies.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Island biogeography: Theory predicting species richness as an equilibrium between immigration (increases with proximity to mainland) and extinction rates (decreases with island size)
  • Species richness: The number of different species in an area; generally increases with area, decreases with latitude, and increases toward the tropics
  • Species-area relationship: S = cAᶻ; the number of species increases with habitat area, where z ≈ 0.3 for islands and c is a constant
  • Dispersal: The movement of organisms from their birthplace to new locations; barriers include oceans, mountains, deserts, and climate zones
  • Latitudinal diversity gradient: The pattern of increasing species richness from poles to tropics, driven by higher energy input, greater habitat area, and longer evolutionary time in the tropics

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Conservation biology applying island biogeography to design nature reserves: larger, connected reserves support more species than small, isolated fragments
  • Madagascar's unique biodiversity (90% of species found nowhere else) explained by 88 million years of isolation after separating from Africa and India
  • Habitat fragmentation creating 'islands' of forest surrounded by agriculture, reducing species richness as predicted by island biogeography theory
