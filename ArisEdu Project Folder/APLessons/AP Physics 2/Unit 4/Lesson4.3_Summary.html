AP Physics 2 - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Lenses use refraction to form images. Converging (convex) lenses are thicker in the middle and bring parallel rays to a focus; diverging (concave) lenses are thinner in the middle and spread rays apart. Like mirrors, the thin lens equation 1/d_o + 1/d_i = 1/f applies (with sign conventions). Converging lenses produce real inverted images when the object is beyond f, and virtual upright magnified images when the object is inside f. Diverging lenses always produce virtual, upright, reduced images.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Converging lens (convex): f > 0; brings parallel light to a real focal point; produces real or virtual images depending on object position
  • Diverging lens (concave): f < 0; causes parallel light to diverge as if from a virtual focal point; always produces virtual, upright, reduced images
  • Thin lens equation: 1/d_o + 1/d_i = 1/f; same form as mirror equation; positive d_i = real image (opposite side); negative d_i = virtual image (same side)
  • Ray diagrams: Three principal rays: (1) parallel to axis → through F, (2) through center → straight, (3) through F → parallel to axis
  • Magnification: m = -d_i/d_o; same as for mirrors; determines image size and orientation

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Cameras: converging lenses focus light from objects at varying distances onto the film/sensor by adjusting the lens-to-sensor distance (focusing)
  • Corrective lenses: nearsightedness (diverging lens to push focus back) and farsightedness (converging lens to push focus forward) are corrected by choosing the appropriate focal length
  • Projectors: a converging lens creates a magnified real image of a small display on a distant screen, with the object placed just outside the focal length
