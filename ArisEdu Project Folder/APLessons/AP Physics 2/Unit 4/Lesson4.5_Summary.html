AP Physics 2 - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Thin Lens Equation and Applications applies the quantitative lens/mirror relationships to multi-element optical systems. The lensmaker's equation: 1/f = (n-1)(1/R₁ - 1/R₂) determines focal length from lens shape and material. For multi-lens systems, the image from the first lens becomes the object for the second lens. Power of a lens: P = 1/f (in diopters, m⁻¹). Combined power: P_total = P₁ + P₂ for thin lenses in contact. Sign conventions must be applied consistently for accurate results.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Lensmaker's equation: 1/f = (n-1)(1/R₁ - 1/R₂); relates focal length to the lens material (n) and surface curvatures (R₁, R₂)
  • Lens power: P = 1/f in diopters (D = m⁻¹); converging lenses have P > 0; diverging lenses have P < 0; a quick measure of focusing ability
  • Multi-lens systems: The image from lens 1 serves as the object for lens 2; d_o2 = separation - d_i1; apply thin lens equation sequentially
  • Combined power of thin lenses in contact: P_total = P₁ + P₂; equivalent to adding the reciprocals of focal lengths
  • Sign conventions: d_o > 0 (real object); d_i > 0 (real image, opposite side); d_i < 0 (virtual image, same side); f > 0 (converging); f < 0 (diverging)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Prescription eyeglasses: optometrists measure required correction in diopters; a -3.0 D prescription means a diverging lens with f = -0.33 m for myopia correction
  • Camera lens design: modern camera lenses contain 10-20 elements to correct for aberrations, provide zoom capability, and maintain image sharpness across the frame
  • Laser systems: multi-lens arrangements expand, collimate, and focus laser beams with precise control for applications from surgery to manufacturing
