AP Physics 2 - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Magnetism studies magnetic fields and the forces they exert on moving charges and current-carrying conductors. Moving charges create magnetic fields, and magnetic fields exert forces on moving charges: F = qv × B (the Lorentz force). For a current-carrying wire in a field: F = IL × B. Right-hand rules determine the direction of magnetic force and of fields produced by currents. Magnetic field lines form closed loops (no magnetic monopoles exist). Magnetic field sources include permanent magnets, current loops, and solenoids.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Magnetic force on a charge: F = qv × B; force is perpendicular to both v and B; magnitude F = qvB sin θ; does no work (changes direction, not speed)
  • Magnetic force on a wire: F = IL × B; force on a straight current-carrying wire of length L in a uniform field B
  • Right-hand rule (force): Point fingers in direction of v (or I), curl toward B; thumb points in force direction for positive charge
  • Magnetic field of a wire: B = μ₀I/(2πr); circular field lines around a long straight wire; strength decreases with distance r from wire
  • Solenoid field: B = μ₀nI inside a solenoid (n = turns/length); uniform field inside, nearly zero outside; like a bar magnet

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • MRI machines use strong magnetic fields (1.5-7 Tesla) to align hydrogen nuclei in the body; radio pulses flip the alignment and the return signal creates detailed tissue images
  • Electric motors: current-carrying coils in a magnetic field experience torque (F = IL × B), converting electrical energy to rotational mechanical energy
  • Earth's magnetic field (25-65 μT) is generated by convection currents in the liquid iron outer core (geodynamo); it deflects solar wind and protects life from harmful radiation
