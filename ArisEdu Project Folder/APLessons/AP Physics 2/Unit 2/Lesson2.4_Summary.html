AP Physics 2 - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Capacitors and Dielectrics store electrical energy in an electric field. A parallel-plate capacitor has capacitance C = ε₀A/d (area A, plate separation d). The stored charge Q = CV and energy U = ½CV² = ½Q²/C = ½QV. Inserting a dielectric (insulating material) between the plates increases capacitance by a factor κ: C = κε₀A/d. The dielectric reduces the electric field between plates and allows more charge to be stored at the same voltage. Capacitors in parallel add: C_total = C₁ + C₂; in series: 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Capacitance: C = Q/V; the ratio of charge stored to voltage applied; measured in farads (F); C = ε₀A/d for parallel plates
  • Energy stored in a capacitor: U = ½CV² = ½Q²/C = ½QV; energy is stored in the electric field between the plates
  • Dielectric constant (κ): Insulating material between plates increases capacitance: C = κε₀A/d; κ > 1 for all materials (κ = 1 for vacuum)
  • Parallel combination: C_total = C₁ + C₂ + ...; equivalent to a single larger capacitor; total charge = sum of individual charges
  • Series combination: 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ...; total voltage = sum of individual voltages; same charge on each capacitor

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Camera flashes: a capacitor charges slowly from the battery, then discharges rapidly through the flash tube—delivering high power in a short burst
  • Touchscreens (capacitive): your finger changes the local capacitance of the screen's sensor grid, detected as a touch at that location
  • Energy storage: supercapacitors bridge the gap between batteries and conventional capacitors, providing rapid charge/discharge for regenerative braking in electric vehicles
