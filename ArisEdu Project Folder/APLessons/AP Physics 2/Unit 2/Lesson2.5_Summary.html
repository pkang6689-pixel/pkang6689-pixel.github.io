AP Physics 2 - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electric Circuits involve the flow of charge through conductive paths driven by a potential difference. Current I = dQ/dt (charge per unit time, in amperes). Ohm's law: V = IR relates voltage, current, and resistance. Kirchhoff's junction rule (conservation of charge) states that currents entering a junction equal currents leaving. Kirchhoff's loop rule (conservation of energy) states that the sum of voltage changes around any closed loop is zero. Power dissipated: P = IV = I²R = V²/R.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Electric current: I = dQ/dt; the rate of charge flow; measured in amperes (A = C/s); conventional current flows from + to -
  • Ohm's law: V = IR; voltage across a resistor equals current times resistance; resistance R measured in ohms (Ω = V/A)
  • Kirchhoff's junction rule: ΣI_in = ΣI_out at any junction; based on conservation of charge
  • Kirchhoff's loop rule: ΣΔV = 0 around any closed loop; based on conservation of energy; EMF gains = resistive drops
  • Resistors in series/parallel: Series: R_total = R₁ + R₂; same current through each. Parallel: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂; same voltage across each

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Household wiring: outlets are wired in parallel (each at 120V) with circuit breakers in series (trip at 15-20A) to prevent overheating and fires
  • Battery internal resistance: real batteries have internal resistance r, so terminal voltage = EMF - Ir; explains why batteries 'die' under heavy load
  • LED circuits: resistors are placed in series with LEDs to limit current (V_supply - V_LED)/R = I, preventing the LED from burning out
