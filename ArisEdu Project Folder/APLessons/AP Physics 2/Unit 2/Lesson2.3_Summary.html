AP Physics 2 - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electric Potential relates to the potential energy per unit charge in an electric field. Voltage (electric potential difference) is ΔV = ΔU/q = -∫E·ds. For a point charge: V = kQ/r. Equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to electric field lines and represent locations of equal potential. Moving a charge along an equipotential requires no work. The relationship E = -dV/dx means the electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential. Potential energy: U = kq₁q₂/r.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Electric potential (voltage): V = U/q = kQ/r for a point charge; scalar quantity measured in volts (V = J/C)
  • Potential difference: ΔV = -∫E·ds; the work per unit charge needed to move a charge between two points; what batteries provide
  • Equipotential surfaces: Surfaces of constant V; perpendicular to field lines; no work done moving charge along them
  • E-V relationship: E = -dV/dx (or E = -∇V in 3D); E points from high V to low V; stronger field where equipotentials are closer together
  • Electric potential energy: U = kq₁q₂/r for two point charges; positive when charges have same sign (repulsive); negative when opposite (attractive)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Batteries maintain a fixed potential difference (EMF) between terminals; the chemical reaction supplies energy to move charges from low to high potential
  • Defibrillators deliver a large potential difference (~1000-3000 V) across the heart to reset its electrical rhythm during cardiac arrest
  • Van de Graaff generators accumulate charge on a conducting sphere, creating potentials exceeding 100,000 V—used in nuclear physics and dramatic classroom demonstrations
