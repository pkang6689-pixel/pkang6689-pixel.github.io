AP Physics 2 - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electromagnetic Induction is described by Faraday's law: a changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an EMF: ε = -dΦ_B/dt. Lenz's law states the induced current opposes the change in flux (the negative sign). EMF can be induced by changing B, changing A, or changing the angle θ between B and the area vector. For a coil of N turns: ε = -N·dΦ_B/dt. Motional EMF: when a conductor moves through a field, ε = BLv (B = field, L = conductor length, v = velocity).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Faraday's law: ε = -dΦ_B/dt; induced EMF equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux; the basis for generators and transformers
  • Lenz's law: The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the flux change that caused it; a consequence of energy conservation
  • Motional EMF: ε = BLv; induced when a conductor of length L moves with velocity v perpendicular to field B
  • Eddy currents: Induced currents in bulk conductors; dissipate energy as heat; used in magnetic braking and induction heating
  • Self-inductance: ε = -L(dI/dt); a changing current in a coil induces an EMF in itself; L = inductance in henries (H)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Electric generators convert mechanical energy to electrical energy: rotating coils in magnetic fields produce alternating EMF (AC power) via Faraday's law
  • Induction cooktops: rapidly alternating magnetic fields induce eddy currents in the metal pot, heating it directly while the cooktop surface stays cool
  • Wireless charging (Qi standard): a changing magnetic field in the charging pad induces an EMF in the receiving coil inside the phone
