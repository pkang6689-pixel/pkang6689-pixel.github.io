Key Concepts

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave produced by vibrating objects. Sound requires a medium (air, water, solid) and travels at different speeds in each (≈343 m/s in air at 20°C, ≈1480 m/s in water, ≈5000 m/s in steel). Frequency determines pitch; amplitude determines loudness. The Doppler effect causes the observed frequency to shift when the source and/or observer are in relative motion: approaching → higher pitch, receding → lower pitch. Sound intensity follows an inverse-square law: I ∝ 1/r².

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Speed of sound: v = √(B/ρ) for fluids, where B is bulk modulus; in air at 20°C: v ≈ 343 m/s; increases with temperature
  • Doppler effect: f_observed = f_source · (v ± v_observer)/(v ∓ v_source); approaching gives higher pitch, receding gives lower pitch
  • Sound intensity level: β = 10 log₁₀(I/I₀) in decibels (dB); I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m² is the threshold of hearing; each 10 dB = 10× intensity
  • Resonance: When a driving frequency matches an object's natural frequency, the amplitude of oscillation increases dramatically
  • Beats: When two waves of slightly different frequencies f₁ and f₂ interfere, the beat frequency is |f₁ - f₂|; perceived as a pulsing volume

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Doppler radar: police speed guns and weather radar measure the frequency shift of reflected radio waves to determine object velocity
  • Noise-canceling headphones use destructive interference: a microphone detects ambient sound, and speakers produce the inverted wave to cancel noise
  • Sonar uses sound pulse travel time in water to measure ocean depth and detect objects—essential for submarine navigation and marine biology research
