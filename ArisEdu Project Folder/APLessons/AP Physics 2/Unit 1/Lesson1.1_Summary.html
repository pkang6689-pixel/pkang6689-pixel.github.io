AP Physics 2 - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Fluids and Pressure examines the behavior of liquids and gases under pressure. Pressure is defined as force per unit area: P = F/A. In a static fluid, pressure increases with depth: P = P₀ + ρgh (Pascal's principle of hydrostatic pressure). Archimedes' principle states that the buoyant force on a submerged object equals the weight of the displaced fluid: F_b = ρ_fluid · V_displaced · g. Fluid dynamics is governed by the continuity equation (A₁v₁ = A₂v₂) and Bernoulli's equation (P + ½ρv² + ρgy = constant along a streamline).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Pressure: Force per unit area: P = F/A; measured in pascals (Pa = N/m²); 1 atm ≈ 101,325 Pa
  • Hydrostatic pressure: P = P₀ + ρgh; pressure increases linearly with depth; depends on fluid density ρ and depth h, not container shape
  • Buoyant force (Archimedes): F_b = ρ_fluid · V_displaced · g; an object floats when its weight equals the buoyant force (average density < fluid density)
  • Continuity equation: A₁v₁ = A₂v₂; conservation of mass in fluid flow; fluid speeds up in narrow sections (smaller A → larger v)
  • Bernoulli's equation: P + ½ρv² + ρgy = constant; higher velocity means lower pressure; the basis for lift, venturi effects, and flow measurement

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Hydraulic systems use Pascal's principle: a small force on a small piston creates a large force on a large piston (F₂ = F₁ · A₂/A₁), powering car brakes and industrial presses
  • Airplane wing lift: the curved upper surface forces air to move faster (lower pressure above) than the flat lower surface (higher pressure below), creating net upward force via Bernoulli's principle
  • Blood pressure measurement: a sphygmomanometer measures arterial pressure as the heart pumps—systolic (heart contracting) and diastolic (heart relaxing) values in mmHg
