AP Physics 2 - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electrons and Atomic Models traces the evolution of atomic theory from Thomson's plum pudding model to Bohr's quantized orbits to the quantum mechanical model. Rutherford's gold foil experiment proved atoms have tiny, dense, positive nuclei. Bohr's model: electrons orbit in quantized energy levels with angular momentum L = nℏ. The standing-wave condition (electron wavelength must fit the orbital circumference: nλ = 2πr) explains quantization. The modern quantum model replaces orbits with probability clouds (orbitals) described by wave functions.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Rutherford model: Gold foil experiment: most alpha particles pass through, some scatter greatly → atom is mostly empty space with a tiny dense positive nucleus
  • Bohr model of hydrogen: Electrons in quantized circular orbits: E_n = -13.6/n² eV; r_n = n² × 0.0529 nm; explains hydrogen spectral lines
  • Quantization condition: Angular momentum L = nℏ (n = 1,2,3,...); equivalently, nλ = 2πr (standing wave condition for electron orbit)
  • Ground state vs. excited state: Ground state: lowest energy (n=1); excited states: n > 1; atoms absorb energy to reach excited states, emit to return
  • Quantum mechanical model: Electrons are described by wavefunctions (probability amplitudes); orbitals are regions of high probability, not definite paths

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Rutherford's experiment was done with gold because it's malleable enough to make foils only a few atoms thick—thicker foils would scatter too many alpha particles
  • Hydrogen fuel cells exploit the well-understood quantum energy levels of hydrogen for efficient energy conversion
  • Atomic clocks use the precise frequency of electron transitions in cesium atoms (9.19 GHz) to define the second—accurate to 1 part in 10¹⁴
