AP Physics 2 - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Radioactivity and Decay describes the spontaneous transformation of unstable nuclei. Alpha decay (α): nucleus emits ⁴₂He, losing 2 protons and 2 neutrons (A→A-4, Z→Z-2). Beta-minus decay (β⁻): neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino (Z→Z+1). Beta-plus decay (β⁺): proton → neutron + positron + neutrino (Z→Z-1). Gamma decay (γ): excited nucleus emits a high-energy photon without changing A or Z. Radioactive decay follows N(t) = N₀e^(-λt), with half-life t₁/₂ = ln(2)/λ.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Alpha decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_(Z-2) Y + ⁴₂He; massive, +2 charge; stopped by paper; most ionizing; common in heavy nuclei (Z > 82)
  • Beta-minus decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z+1) Y + e⁻ + ν̄; neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino; stopped by aluminum; neutron-rich nuclei
  • Beta-plus decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z-1) Y + e⁺ + ν; proton → neutron + positron + neutrino; occurs in proton-rich nuclei
  • Gamma decay: Excited nucleus emits high-energy photon; no change in A or Z; often follows alpha or beta decay; stopped by lead/concrete
  • Half-life: t₁/₂ = ln(2)/λ; the time for half the radioactive atoms to decay; ranges from nanoseconds to billions of years

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • PET scans use beta-plus emitters (e.g., ¹⁸F): the positron annihilates with an electron, producing two 511 keV gamma rays detected simultaneously to locate tumors
  • Radon testing: radon-222 (α emitter, t₁/₂ = 3.8 days) seeps from soil into basements; second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking
  • Radiometric dating: uranium-238 (t₁/₂ = 4.5 billion years) decays to lead-206; the U/Pb ratio in rocks determines geological age of the Earth (~4.5 billion years)
