AP Physics 2 - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Nuclear Reactions involve the transformation of nuclei through bombardment or collision. Conservation laws apply: total charge (Z), total mass number (A), total energy, and total momentum are conserved. Fission: a heavy nucleus (e.g., ²³⁵U) splits into lighter nuclei + neutrons + energy. Fusion: light nuclei (e.g., ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n) combine to form a heavier nucleus + energy. Both release energy because the products have higher binding energy per nucleon. Fission is the basis of nuclear reactors; fusion powers the Sun.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Nuclear fission: A heavy nucleus splits into two lighter nuclei + 2-3 neutrons + energy; ²³⁵U + n → ¹⁴¹Ba + ⁹²Kr + 3n + ~200 MeV
  • Chain reaction: Fission neutrons trigger more fissions; critical mass: self-sustaining; supercritical: explosive; moderated in reactors
  • Nuclear fusion: Light nuclei combine: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV; requires extreme temperatures (>10⁷ K) to overcome Coulomb barrier
  • Binding energy per nucleon curve: Peaks at iron-56; lighter nuclei release energy by fusion; heavier nuclei release energy by fission
  • Conservation laws in nuclear reactions: Total A (mass number), total Z (charge), total energy (including mc²), and total momentum are all conserved

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Nuclear power plants: controlled fission of ²³⁵U in fuel rods heats water to produce steam that drives turbines, generating ~20% of US electricity without direct CO₂ emissions
  • Thermonuclear weapons use fission to trigger fusion: the fission bomb creates temperatures and pressures needed to fuse hydrogen isotopes, releasing vastly more energy
  • Fusion research: tokamak reactors (ITER) confine plasma at 150 million °C using magnetic fields to achieve controlled fusion—potentially limitless clean energy
