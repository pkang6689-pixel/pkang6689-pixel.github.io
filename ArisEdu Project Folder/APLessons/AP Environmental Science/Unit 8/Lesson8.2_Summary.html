AP Environmental Science - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Eutrophication and Dead Zones result from enrichment of water bodies with excess nitrogen and phosphorus, triggering algal blooms that deplete dissolved oxygen creating "dead zones" where fish suffocation and aquatic life collapse. Coastal dead zones now exceed 400 worldwide, with the largest in the Gulf of Mexico (averaging ~15,000 km²) receiving Mississippi River nitrogen-enriched runoff from agriculture across the corn belt. Preventing eutrophication requires reducing agricultural nutrient inputs, separating sewage from stormwater, and restoring riparian buffer vegetation.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Algal bloom: Rapid growth of algae in nutrient-enriched water, often toxin-producing cyanobacteria harmful to human and animal health
  • Hypoxia: Dissolved oxygen depletion (<2 mg/L) in water, causing fish mortality and ecosystem collapse
  • Dead zone: Area of severely hypoxic bottom water where most aquatic life cannot survive, expanding and contracting seasonally
  • Limiting nutrient: Nutrient in shortest supply (often phosphorus in freshwater, nitrogen in marine) controlling primary productivity
  • Restoration: Removal of septic aging systems, installation of advanced wastewater treatment, and agricultural conservation practices

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Restoring the Mississippi River basin through application of conservation practices (cover crops, riparian buffers, wetland restoration) to reduce Gulf hypoxia
  • Managing harmful algal blooms (cyanobacteria) in freshwater lakes through nutrient reduction and copper sulfate applications in emergency situations
  • Implementing marine protected areas in hypoxic regions to prevent bottom trawling that resuspends anoxic sediments and prevents recovery
