AP Environmental Science - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Water Pollution Types and Sources degrade drinking water quality, aquatic ecosystem integrity, and human health through point sources (factories, wastewater plants) and nonpoint sources (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater) introducing nitrogen, phosphorus, pathogens, toxic chemicals, and plastic. Freshwater and marine ecosystems receive approximately 3.5 billion tons of wastewater annually, with developing countries treating <10% before discharge. Water pollution is accelerating due to industrial expansion, urbanization, and agricultural intensification but is preventable through regulation and treatment technologies.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Point source: Pollutant discharge from identifiable location (industrial pipe, sewage outfall), regulated under Clean Water Act permits
  • Nonpoint source: Pollution from dispersed sources (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater, atmospheric deposition), difficult to regulate and largest U.S. water pollution source
  • Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD): Mass of oxygen required for microbial decomposition of organic matter, indicating organic pollution extent
  • Water quality standard: Regulatory threshold for pollutant concentration in water bodies, protecting aquatic life and human uses
  • Wastewater treatment: Multi-stage process removing solids, organic matter, nutrients, and pathogens before water discharge or reuse

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Monitoring BOD and dissolved oxygen in rivers to assess organic pollution severity and predict fish kills during low-flow periods
  • Treating municipal wastewater through primary (settling), secondary (biological), and tertiary (nutrient removal) steps to protect receiving waters
  • Implementing stormwater pollution prevention through retention ponds, rain gardens, and permeable pavements in urban development
