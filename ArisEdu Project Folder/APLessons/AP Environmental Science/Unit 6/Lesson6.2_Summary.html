AP Environmental Science - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Coal, Oil, and Natural Gas represent distinct fossil fuel types with different formation, extraction, and combustion characteristics. Coal, the most abundant fossil fuel formed from ancient plant material, is extracted through surface and deep mining, providing 27% of global energy but causing severe air and water pollution. Oil formed from marine organisms is extracted through conventional and unconventional methods (tar sands, shale oil), driving global transportation. Natural gas, primarily methane, provides cleaner combustion but has significant methane emissions from production and distribution contributing to climate change.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Coal rank: Classification of coal from lignite to anthracite based on carbon content and energy density, reflecting formation conditions
  • Crude oil: Unrefined petroleum extracted from subsurface deposits, requiring refining into various products (gasoline, diesel, heating oil)
  • Hydraulic fracturing (fracking): Technique injecting pressurized fluid to crack rock, enabling extraction of oil and gas from shale formations
  • Methane: Primary component of natural gas, a potent greenhouse gas with 28–34 times the 100-year warming potential of CO₂
  • Unconventional fossil fuels: Oil and gas from difficult-to-extract sources (tar sands, shale) requiring energy-intensive extraction

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Assessing coal seam gas development impacts on groundwater aquifers and agricultural productivity in coal-mining regions
  • Evaluating tar sands development economic viability and environmental costs including water use, GHG emissions, and habitat disturbance
  • Monitoring methane leakage from natural gas infrastructure to quantify climate impacts and identify equipment upgrades reducing emissions
