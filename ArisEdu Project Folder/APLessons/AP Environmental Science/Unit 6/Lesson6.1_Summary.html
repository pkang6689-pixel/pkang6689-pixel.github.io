AP Environmental Science - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Fossil Fuel Formation and Use involves formation of coal, oil, and natural gas from decomposed organic matter under pressure and heat over millions of years, storing ancient solar energy in chemical bonds. Exploitation of fossil fuels has powered industrialization and modern civilization but has extracted resources representing millions of years of photosynthesis, increasing atmospheric carbon dioxide from 280 ppm to 425 ppm. Understanding fossil fuel formation, reserves, and thermodynamic limitations is essential for energy and climate policy.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Fossil fuel: Energy resource formed from ancient organic matter (coal, oil, natural gas) representing millions of years of accumulation
  • Reserves: Identified mineral or fuel deposits economically extractable with current technology and prices
  • Energy return on investment (EROI): Ratio of energy produced by an energy source to energy required for its extraction and processing
  • Peak oil: The point of maximum global oil production after which extraction declines due to depletion of economically viable reserves
  • Carbon footprint: Total greenhouse gas emissions from extracting, processing, transporting, and burning a fuel

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Calculating oil field production decline curves to forecast supply disruptions and investment needs for exploration and production
  • Analyzing EROI of unconventional oil sources (oil sands, shale) to evaluate economic viability and energy efficiency
  • Monitoring atmospheric CO₂ from fossil fuel combustion using ice cores and continuous monitoring to inform climate policy
