AP Environmental Science - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Agricultural Practices and Impacts shape more than 40% of earth's land surface, fundamentally altering soil, water, biodiversity, and biogeochemical cycles through cultivation, fertilizer application, pesticide use, and irrigation. Conventional agriculture provides food security but causes soil degradation, water depletion, and pollution through runoff, while sustainable and organic approaches offer alternatives with tradeoffs in productivity and profitability. Understanding agricultural impacts is essential for feeding growing populations while preserving ecosystem integrity.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Monoculture: Growing a single crop repeatedly, reducing genetic diversity and soil health while increasing pest and disease vulnerability
  • Pesticide: A chemical used to kill unwanted organisms (insects, weeds, pathogens), with potential environmental persistence and ecosystem impacts
  • Soil degradation: Reduction in soil quality, productivity, and biodiversity through compaction, erosion, salinization, and organic matter loss
  • Subsidy: Government economic support for agricultural production, often promoting unsustainable practices and overproduction
  • Sustainable agriculture: Farming practices maintaining productivity while preserving soil, water, biodiversity, and profitability long-term

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Implementing integrated pest management (IPM) to reduce pesticide use by combining monitoring, biological control, and targeted chemical applications
  • Adopting precision agriculture technology using GPS and soil sensors to optimize fertilizer and water application, reducing inputs and runoff
  • Supporting agroforestry systems integrating trees with crops to improve soil quality, increase biodiversity, and enhance farmer resilience
