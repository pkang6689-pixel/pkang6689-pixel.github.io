AP Environmental Science - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Human Population Growth has accelerated from 1 billion (1800) to 8 billion (2023), driven primarily by mortality reduction while fertility rates lag in developing countries, creating a population momentum continuing growth to 9–10 billion by 2050–2070 despite declining fertility. Population growth concentrates in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia while developed countries and East Asia face stagnation/decline, creating demographic divergence. Population growth exacerbates resource scarcity, environmental impact, and inequality, requiring family planning, education (especially female), and economic development.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Total fertility rate (TFR): Average number of children per woman; replacement level is ~2.1 in developed countries, <2.0 in many developed countries
  • Demographic transition: Change from high birth and death rates (pre-industrial) to low rates (post-industrial) through education and development
  • Population momentum: Continued population growth despite replacement fertility, from age structure weighted toward reproductive years
  • Population doubling time: Time for population to double at current growth rate; presently ~59 years globally but varying substantially by region
  • Urbanization: Concentration of human population in cities, now exceeding 55% globally and projected to reach 70% by 2050

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Projecting national population growth using age structure and fertility rates to guide development planning (infrastructure, education, healthcare)
  • Supporting family planning programs providing contraceptive access and reproductive education, reducing fertility and improving maternal health
  • Addressing population concentration in megacities through infrastructure expansion and satellite city development
