AP Environmental Science - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Climate Change Causes and Effects result from anthropogenic greenhouse gas accumulation altering temperature, precipitation, sea levels, and extreme weather patterns globally, with impacts accelerating as climate forcing increases and natural systems respond. Climate change effects include ecosystem disruption (species range shifts, phenological mismatches), agricultural impacts (yield reductions, new pests), public health threats (heat stress, vector-borne disease expansion), and infrastructure damage (flooding, drought). Mitigation (emissions reduction) and adaptation (resilience building) are both essential given committed warming from historical emissions.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Climate change: Long-term shift in average global temperature and weather patterns primarily from anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions
  • Climate feedback: Process amplifying or dampening climate change (albedo feedback, water vapor feedback, ice-albedo feedback)
  • Atmospheric CO₂ forcing: Energy imbalance (~4 W/m² for doubled CO₂) from increased greenhouse gas concentration
  • Committed warming: Additional temperature increase inevitable from existing atmospheric CO₂ due to thermal inertia (ocean warming lag)
  • Representative concentration pathways (RCP): Greenhouse gas emission scenarios (RCP 2.6, 4.5, 6.0, 8.5 W/m²) used for climate projections

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Projecting regional climate impacts (temperature, precipitation, extremes) using downscaled climate models to inform infrastructure planning and adaptation
  • Coral bleaching monitoring using satellite sea surface temperature data to predict and manage impacts on tropical fisheries and tourism
  • Modeling sea-level rise impacts on coastal cities and small island nations, informing relocation planning and adaptation infrastructure investment
