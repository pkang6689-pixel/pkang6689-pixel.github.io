AP Environmental Science - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Global Environmental Justice addresses disproportionate exposure of low-income communities and communities of color to pollution, toxic facilities, and environmental hazards while having less access to environmental amenities and political power in environmental decisions. Environmental racism—deliberate placement of polluting facilities near marginalized communities—has created health disparities in air quality, water contamination, soil pollution, and climate impacts affecting these populations most severely despite contributing least to global emissions. Environmental justice requires equitable decision-making, remediation of existing injustices, and prevention of future impacts.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Environmental justice: Fair treatment and meaningful involvement of all people regardless of race, color, or income in environmental decisions, codified by Executive Order 12898 (1994)
  • Sacrifice zone: Geographic area permanently degraded by heavy pollution or industrial activity, disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color
  • Environmental racism: Systemic placement of hazardous facilities (landfills, refineries, incinerators) in communities of color and low-income areas
  • Cumulative impact assessment: Analysis of combined health and environmental effects from multiple pollution sources in a community, revealing disparities invisible when evaluating facilities individually
  • Climate justice: Recognition that vulnerable populations experience disproportionate climate impacts despite minimal greenhouse gas emissions

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Remediating lead contamination in low-income neighborhoods through soil remediation, housing rehabilitation, and water system replacement
  • Siting environmental facilities (wind farms, solar arrays, waste facilities) through inclusive processes ensuring community benefit and addressing historical inequities
  • Advancing climate adaptation in vulnerable communities (low-income, indigenous) through participatory planning, green infrastructure investment, and resilience building
