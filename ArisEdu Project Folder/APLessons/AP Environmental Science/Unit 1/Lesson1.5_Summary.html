AP Environmental Science - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Succession and Restoration applies succession principles to deliberately return degraded ecosystems toward reference conditions, incorporating both natural recovery processes and active management interventions. Successful restoration requires understanding the original ecosystem's composition, structure, and ecological processes, then removing limiting factors and facilitating species reestablishment. Restoration projects demonstrate how ecological knowledge translates into practical habitat recovery and can enhance both biodiversity and ecosystem services.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Ecological restoration: Active process of intentionally restoring degraded ecosystems to a more natural or desired state
  • Reference ecosystem: An intact or minimally impacted ecosystem serving as a target model for restoration efforts
  • Keystone species: Species whose impact on ecosystems is disproportionate to their abundance; removal causes ecological collapse
  • Habitat fragmentation: The division of continuous habitat into isolated patches, reducing species connectivity and population viability
  • Restoration intensity: The level of management intervention from passive protection to intensive restoration with species reintroduction

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Restoring prairie ecosystems through prescribed burns to eliminate woody encroachment and reestablish native grasses and wildflowers
  • Removing dams to restore river connectivity and fish passage, allowing natural flow regimes and sediment transport restoration
  • Replanting native species in degraded forests while removing invasive species to accelerate succession and restore ecosystem functions
