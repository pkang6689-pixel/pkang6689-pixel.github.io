AP Environmental Science - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Biodiversity Hotspots are geographic areas with exceptional species richness and endemism (species found nowhere else) that face severe habitat loss and extinction risk, concentrating conservation efforts and resources. Conservation International identified 34 hotspots covering only 2.4% of earth's land but containing 75% of threatened vertebrate and 50% of plant species. Prioritizing hotspots for protection is a strategic response to limited conservation resources and the accelerating extinction crisis.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Biodiversity hotspot: A biogeographic region with exceptionally high species richness and endemism, facing rapid habitat loss
  • Endemism: The restriction of a species to a particular geographic area, increasing vulnerability to local environmental changes and extinction
  • Species richness: The total number of species in a community or region, a key metric of biodiversity
  • Threatened species: A species classified as vulnerable, endangered, or critically endangered with elevated extinction risk
  • In situ conservation: Protection of species in their natural habitat and ecosystems, preferred over ex situ conservation where possible

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Prioritizing conservation funding for the tropical Andes hotspot (richest vertebrate hotspot) to protect cloud forests and prevent species extinction
  • Establishing biodiversity hotspot conservation programs in Madagascar to protect lemurs and other endemic species found nowhere else on earth
  • Protecting the Mediterranean Basin hotspot through transnational conservation agreements coordinating protection across Europe, Africa, and Asia
