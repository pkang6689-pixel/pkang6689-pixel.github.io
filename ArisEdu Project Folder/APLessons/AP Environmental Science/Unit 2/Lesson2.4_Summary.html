AP Environmental Science - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Conservation of Biodiversity employs science-based strategies to prevent extinction and preserve ecosystem function, including protected areas, habitat restoration, captive breeding, and sustainable resource management. Conservation strategies operate at multiple scales from individual species recovery programs to ecosystem-level protection and global agreements on biodiversity targets. Effective conservation requires integrating ecological science with economics, policy, and local community engagement.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Conservation biology: Applied science focused on protecting biodiversity and maintaining ecosystem integrity and function
  • Protected area: Land or water formally designated and managed to achieve conservation objectives, ranging from strict nature reserves to sustainable-use areas
  • Umbrella species: A wide-ranging species whose conservation indirectly protects other species sharing its habitat and ecosystem
  • Metapopulation: A collection of isolated populations connected by occasional migration, whose persistence depends on source and sink dynamics
  • Habitat corridor: A landscape feature linking isolated habitat patches, enabling gene flow and organism movement among populations

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Establishing national parks and marine protected areas (like the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park) to maintain viable populations of multiple species
  • Using spotted owls as umbrella species in Pacific Northwest forest conservation to protect old-growth forest ecosystems benefiting hundreds of species
  • Creating wildlife corridors such as the Path of the Panther in Florida to connect fragmented habitats and enable panther population recovery
