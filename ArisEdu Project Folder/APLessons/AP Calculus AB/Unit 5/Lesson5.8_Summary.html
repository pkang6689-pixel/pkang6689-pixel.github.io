AP Calculus AB - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.8 - Summary

Key Concepts

Rolle's Theorem is a special case of the Mean Value Theorem where f(a) = f(b). It states: if f is continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), and f(a) = f(b), then there exists at least one c in (a,b) where f'(c) = 0. Geometrically, if the function starts and ends at the same height, there must be at least one horizontal tangent in between. Rolle's Theorem is used to prove the MVT and to establish the existence of roots of derivative equations.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Rolle's Theorem: If f is continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), and f(a) = f(b), then ∃c ∈ (a,b) with f'(c) = 0
  • Special case of MVT: Rolle's Theorem is MVT with the additional condition f(a) = f(b), making the average rate of change = 0
  • Geometric interpretation: If a curve starts and ends at the same height, it must have at least one horizontal tangent (peak or valley) between
  • Proving root existence: If f'(x) = g(x), then showing f satisfies Rolle's conditions proves g(x) = 0 has a root in (a,b)
  • Hypothesis checking: Must verify all three conditions: continuity on [a,b], differentiability on (a,b), and f(a) = f(b)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • A ball thrown upward returns to its starting height: Rolle's Theorem guarantees it had zero velocity (was momentarily at rest) at some point during its flight
  • Proving that a polynomial of degree n has at most n-1 critical points: applying Rolle's Theorem between consecutive roots of f
  • Temperature cycles: if the temperature at dawn equals the temperature at dusk, Rolle's Theorem guarantees the rate of temperature change was zero at some point during the day
