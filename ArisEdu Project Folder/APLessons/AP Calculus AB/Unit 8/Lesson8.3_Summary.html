AP Calculus AB - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Volume of Solids of Revolution uses integration to find volumes when a region is rotated around an axis. The disk method: V = ∫ₐᵇ π[R(x)]²dx when rotating around the x-axis (each cross-section is a disk of radius R(x)). The washer method: V = ∫ₐᵇ π([R(x)]² - [r(x)]²)dx when there is a hole (outer radius R, inner radius r). The shell method: V = ∫ₐᵇ 2πx·f(x)dx uses cylindrical shells instead of disks.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Disk method: V = ∫ₐᵇ π[R(x)]²dx; each cross-section perpendicular to the axis of rotation is a solid disk of radius R(x)
  • Washer method: V = ∫ₐᵇ π([R(x)]² - [r(x)]²)dx; for regions with a hole: outer radius R minus inner radius r
  • Shell method: V = ∫ₐᵇ 2πx·h(x)dx; cylindrical shells of radius x and height h(x); useful when disk/washer would require splitting
  • Axis of rotation: Can be the x-axis, y-axis, or any horizontal/vertical line; adjust radius formulas accordingly
  • Choosing the method: Disk/washer: axis of rotation is parallel to slicing direction; shell: axis is perpendicular to slicing direction

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Architecture: computing volumes of domes, arches, and other rotationally symmetric structures for material estimation and structural analysis
  • Manufacturing: determining the volume (and thus weight) of turned metal parts produced on a lathe—each is a solid of revolution
  • Medical imaging: CT scan data integrates cross-sectional areas to compute organ volumes, aiding diagnosis and surgical planning
