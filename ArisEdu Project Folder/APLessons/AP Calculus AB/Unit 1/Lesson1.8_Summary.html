AP Calculus AB - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.8 - Summary

Key Concepts

Continuity and Types of Discontinuity classifies discontinuities into three types. Removable discontinuities (holes) occur when the limit exists but f(c) is undefined or differs from the limit—fixable by redefining f(c). Jump discontinuities occur when left and right limits exist but are unequal. Infinite discontinuities occur at vertical asymptotes. The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that a continuous function on a closed interval [a,b] attains both a maximum and minimum value.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Removable discontinuity: A point where lim(x→c) f(x) exists but f(c) is undefined or ≠ the limit; the 'hole' can be filled by redefining f(c)
  • Jump discontinuity: A point where left and right limits both exist but are not equal: lim(x→c⁻) f(x) ≠ lim(x→c⁺) f(x)
  • Infinite discontinuity: A point where at least one one-sided limit is ±∞; associated with vertical asymptotes
  • Extreme Value Theorem: If f is continuous on closed interval [a,b], then f attains an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on [a,b]
  • Continuity on an interval: f is continuous on [a,b] if it is continuous at every interior point and continuous from the right at a and from the left at b

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Step functions in postage pricing: the cost jumps at each weight threshold, creating jump discontinuities (e.g., $0.58 for 1 oz, $0.78 for 2 oz)
  • Tax brackets as piecewise-continuous functions: the marginal tax rate has jump discontinuities at bracket boundaries while the total tax function remains continuous
  • Digital signal processing: the staircase approximation of analog signals introduces jump discontinuities, requiring careful analysis for reconstruction
