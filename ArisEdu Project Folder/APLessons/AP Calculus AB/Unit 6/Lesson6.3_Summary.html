AP Calculus AB - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) connects differentiation and integration as inverse operations. Part 1: if f is continuous, then d/dx[∫ₐˣ f(t)dt] = f(x)—the derivative of an accumulation function equals the integrand. Part 2: ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx = F(b) - F(a), where F is any antiderivative of f—exact area computation using antiderivatives. FTC Part 1 with chain rule: d/dx[∫ₐ^(g(x)) f(t)dt] = f(g(x))·g'(x).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • FTC Part 1: d/dx[∫ₐˣ f(t)dt] = f(x); differentiating the integral gives back the original function
  • FTC Part 2: ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx = F(b) - F(a); the definite integral equals the net change in any antiderivative
  • Accumulation function: F(x) = ∫ₐˣ f(t)dt; represents the accumulated area from a to x; its derivative is f(x) by FTC Part 1
  • FTC with chain rule: d/dx[∫ₐ^(g(x)) f(t)dt] = f(g(x))·g'(x); apply chain rule when the upper limit is a function of x
  • Net change interpretation: ∫ₐᵇ f'(x)dx = f(b) - f(a); the integral of a rate of change gives the net change over [a,b]

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Computing total displacement from velocity: ∫₀ᵀ v(t)dt = s(T) - s(0), where the integral of velocity rate gives the net position change
  • Net income calculation: integrating the rate of earnings minus expenses over a fiscal year gives the total profit for the year
  • FTC in engineering: the work done by a variable force F(x) over a distance is W = ∫ₐᵇ F(x)dx = potential energy change
