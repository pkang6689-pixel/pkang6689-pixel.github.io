AP Calculus AB - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Applications of Derivatives connects derivatives to real-world rate-of-change problems. If s(t) is position, then s'(t) = v(t) is velocity and s''(t) = v'(t) = a(t) is acceleration. The derivative represents instantaneous rate of change in any context: population growth rate, marginal cost, rate of temperature change, or rate of chemical reaction. Linear approximation uses the tangent line to estimate function values near a known point: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x-a).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Velocity and acceleration: v(t) = s'(t) is the rate of position change; a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) is the rate of velocity change
  • Instantaneous rate of change: The derivative f'(a) gives the exact rate of change of f at the specific point x = a, not an average over an interval
  • Linear approximation: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x-a); using the tangent line at x = a to estimate nearby function values
  • Marginal analysis: In economics: marginal cost C'(x), marginal revenue R'(x), and marginal profit P'(x) = R'(x) - C'(x) at production level x
  • Related rates setup: When two quantities change with time, their derivatives are related through the chain rule: differentiate the equation relating them with respect to t

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Speedometer readings as instantaneous velocity: the derivative of position with respect to time at each moment, computed electronically in modern vehicles
  • Medical imaging: CT scans reconstruct 3D images by computing rates of change of X-ray attenuation through tissue from multiple angles
  • Financial markets: the 'Greeks' (delta, gamma, theta) in options pricing are derivatives that measure how option value changes with respect to stock price, rate of change, and time
