AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Universal Gravitation states that every mass attracts every other mass with force F = Gm₁m₂/r², where G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg². This inverse-square law governs planetary orbits, satellite motion, and tidal effects. The gravitational field g = GM/r² at distance r from a mass M gives the local acceleration due to gravity, explaining why g varies with altitude and location on Earth.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation: F = Gm₁m₂/r²; every pair of masses attracts each other with a force proportional to the product of masses and inversely proportional to the square of distance
  • Gravitational constant: G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²; a universal fundamental constant measured by Cavendish in 1798
  • Gravitational field: g = GM/r²; the gravitational acceleration at distance r from the center of a mass M
  • Superposition principle: The gravitational force on an object is the vector sum of the gravitational forces from all other masses
  • Shell theorem: A uniform spherical shell attracts external masses as if all its mass were at the center; inside the shell, gravitational force from the shell is zero

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • GPS satellite corrections accounting for the variation of g with altitude to maintain precise timing and positioning
  • Calculating the mass of Earth from the known value of g at the surface and Earth's radius: M = gR²/G
  • Tidal force analysis showing how the Moon's gravity varies across Earth's diameter, creating two tidal bulges on opposite sides
