AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Rotational Inertia (moment of inertia, I) quantifies an object's resistance to angular acceleration, analogous to mass in linear motion. For discrete particles, I = Σm_i r_i², and for continuous bodies, I = ∫r² dm. The parallel axis theorem I = I_cm + Md² relates the moment of inertia about any axis to that about the center of mass. Standard formulas exist for common shapes (rod, disk, sphere, hoop) that appear frequently on the AP exam.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Moment of inertia (discrete): I = Σm_i r_i²; sum of each particle's mass times the square of its distance from the rotation axis
  • Moment of inertia (continuous): I = ∫r² dm; requires integration over the mass distribution of the object
  • Parallel axis theorem: I = I_cm + Md²; the moment of inertia about any axis equals I about the center of mass plus Md²
  • Common moments of inertia: Solid cylinder: ½MR², hoop: MR², solid sphere: ⅖MR², rod (center): 1/12 ML², rod (end): ⅓ML²
  • Rotational kinetic energy: KE_rot = ½Iω²; energy due to rotation, analogous to ½mv² for translation

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Figure skating spins: pulling arms in reduces I, increasing ω to conserve angular momentum (demonstrating I's physical significance)
  • Flywheel energy storage systems using high-I, high-ω wheels to store kinetic energy for regenerative braking in vehicles and power grid stabilization
  • Engine crankshaft design optimizing moment of inertia to smooth out power delivery from individual cylinder firings
