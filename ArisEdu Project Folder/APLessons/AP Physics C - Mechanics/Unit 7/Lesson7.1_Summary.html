AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) occurs when the restoring force is proportional to displacement: F = -kx, producing sinusoidal oscillations x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ). The angular frequency ω = √(k/m) depends on system parameters (spring constant and mass), not amplitude. Energy oscillates between kinetic (½mv²) and potential (½kx²) with total energy E = ½kA² constant. SHM is the foundation for understanding waves, circuits, and quantum mechanics.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Simple harmonic motion: Oscillation where F = -kx and x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ); characterized by constant period independent of amplitude
  • Angular frequency: ω = √(k/m) = 2πf = 2π/T; determines how fast the oscillation occurs
  • Amplitude: A; the maximum displacement from equilibrium; determines the total energy E = ½kA²
  • Phase constant (φ): The initial phase angle that determines the starting position and velocity of the oscillation at t = 0
  • Velocity and acceleration in SHM: v(t) = -Aω sin(ωt + φ), a(t) = -Aω² cos(ωt + φ) = -ω²x; acceleration is proportional and opposite to displacement

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Earthquake-resistant building design using tuned mass dampers (large SHM systems) to counteract the building's oscillation and reduce structural damage
  • Quartz crystal oscillators in watches using the precise SHM frequency of a quartz crystal (32,768 Hz) for accurate timekeeping
  • Automotive suspension system analysis modeling shock absorbers as damped SHM to optimize ride comfort and handling characteristics
