AP Statistics - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Categorical Data Analysis examines the distribution and relationships of categorical variables using frequency tables, relative frequency tables, and bar charts. Two-way tables (contingency tables) organize counts for two categorical variables and allow calculation of marginal, joint, and conditional distributions. Comparing conditional distributions across categories reveals whether an association exists between two categorical variables.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Two-way table (contingency table): A table displaying counts for every combination of two categorical variables, with row and column totals (margins)
  • Marginal distribution: The distribution of one categorical variable alone, found in the row or column totals of a two-way table
  • Joint distribution: The distribution of counts for every combination of categories in a two-way table, expressed as proportions of the grand total
  • Conditional distribution: The distribution of one variable given a specific value of another; found by dividing cell counts by the appropriate row or column total
  • Simpson's Paradox: A phenomenon where an association between two variables reverses direction when data are combined across a lurking variable

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Analyzing whether treatment outcome (improved vs. not improved) is associated with treatment type by comparing conditional distributions in clinical trial data
  • Detecting Simpson's Paradox in university admission data where overall acceptance rates appear to favor one group but reverse within individual departments
  • Market research using two-way tables to determine whether product preference varies by age group for targeted advertising strategies
