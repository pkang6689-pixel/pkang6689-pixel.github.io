AP Statistics - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Independent Events are events where the occurrence of one does not change the probability of the other: P(A|B) = P(A). For independent events, the multiplication rule simplifies to P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B). Testing for independence—by checking whether P(A|B) equals P(A) or whether P(A and B) equals P(A)P(B)—is essential for correctly applying probability rules and for understanding real-world phenomena like random sampling with replacement.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Independent events: Two events A and B are independent if P(A|B) = P(A), meaning knowing B occurred does not change the probability of A
  • Dependent events: Events where the occurrence of one changes the probability of the other; P(A|B) ≠ P(A)
  • Multiplication rule for independents: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B); valid only when A and B are independent events
  • "At least one" problems: P(at least one success in n independent trials) = 1 - P(no successes) = 1 - P(failure)^n
  • Sampling with vs. without replacement: With replacement: draws are independent; without replacement: draws are dependent because the population changes after each draw

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Calculating the probability of at least one defective item in a sample of 20 from a production line with 2% defect rate using independence: P(at least 1) = 1 - 0.98²⁰
  • Genetics probability: determining the likelihood two carrier parents produce a child with a recessive trait by multiplying independent allele transmission probabilities
  • Reliability engineering: computing system failure probability when multiple independent components must all function (series system) vs. at least one must function (parallel system)
