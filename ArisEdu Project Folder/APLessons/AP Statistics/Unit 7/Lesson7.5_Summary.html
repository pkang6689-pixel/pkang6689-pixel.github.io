AP Statistics - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Comparing Two Means tests whether two independent populations have different means using the two-sample t-statistic: t = (x̄₁ - x̄₂)/√(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂). The degrees of freedom are approximated by the Welch formula (or conservatively using the smaller of n₁-1 and n₂-1). Confidence intervals for μ₁ - μ₂ take the form (x̄₁ - x̄₂) ± t*√(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂), providing a range of plausible values for the difference in population means.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Two-sample t-test: Tests H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ (or μ₁ - μ₂ = 0) using t = (x̄₁ - x̄₂)/√(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)
  • Two-sample t-interval: (x̄₁ - x̄₂) ± t*·√(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂); estimates the true difference in population means
  • Welch's degrees of freedom: A formula approximating df for unequal variances; generally calculated by technology rather than by hand
  • Conservative df: Using df = min(n₁-1, n₂-1) when technology is unavailable; produces wider intervals and larger p-values
  • Conditions for two-sample t: Random and independent samples, both n < 10% of respective populations, and both populations approximately normal or both samples large (n ≥ 30)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Comparing mean test scores between students taught with traditional lectures vs. active learning methods to evaluate pedagogical effectiveness
  • Medical trials comparing average recovery times between a new surgical technique and the standard procedure across two independent patient groups
  • Business comparing mean customer satisfaction scores at two store locations to identify differences in service quality
