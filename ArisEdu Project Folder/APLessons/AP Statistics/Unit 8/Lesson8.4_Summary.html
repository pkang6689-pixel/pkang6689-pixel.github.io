AP Statistics - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Interpreting Chi-Square Results requires understanding what a significant χ² value means and what it does not. A significant result indicates that the observed data are unlikely under H₀ but does not identify which specific categories differ or quantify the strength of association. Post-hoc analysis—examining individual cell contributions to χ², computing standardized residuals, or performing pairwise comparisons—is needed to understand the nature and location of the departure from H₀.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Standardized residual: (O - E)/√E for each cell; values beyond ±2 indicate that cell contributes notably to the overall χ² statistic
  • Cell contribution to χ²: (O - E)²/E for each cell; identifies which observed-expected discrepancies drive the overall test result
  • Practical vs. statistical significance: A significant χ² test says the association exists, but effect size measures (Cramér's V, phi) quantify how strong it is
  • Cramér's V: V = √(χ²/(n·min(r-1, c-1))); ranges from 0 to 1, measuring the strength of association between two categorical variables
  • Limitations of chi-square: Chi-square tests require expected counts ≥ 5 in all cells; small expected counts inflate χ² and may invalidate the test

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Interpreting a significant χ² test for independence between education level and voting behavior by examining which education-vote combinations have the largest standardized residuals
  • A manufacturer finding a significant chi-square result for defect type vs. production shift, then identifying that the night shift drives most of the association
  • Reporting chi-square results in a research paper with both the test outcome (χ² = 15.3, df = 4, p = 0.004) and effect size (Cramér's V = 0.18) for complete interpretation
