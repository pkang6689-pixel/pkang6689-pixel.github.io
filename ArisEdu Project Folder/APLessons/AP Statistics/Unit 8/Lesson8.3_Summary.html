AP Statistics - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Two-Way Tables and Conditional Distributions organize data for two categorical variables and provide the foundation for chi-square analysis. Marginal distributions describe each variable alone (row or column totals divided by the grand total), while conditional distributions show the distribution of one variable within levels of the other. Comparing conditional distributions across rows or columns reveals whether the variables are associated.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Two-way table: A table displaying observed counts for every combination of two categorical variables, with row totals, column totals, and grand total
  • Marginal distribution: The distribution of one variable ignoring the other; calculated from the row or column totals divided by the grand total
  • Conditional distribution: The distribution of one variable within a specific category of the other; cell counts divided by the appropriate row or column total
  • Association in two-way tables: Two variables are associated if conditional distributions of one variable differ across categories of the other
  • Segmented bar chart: A graphical display of conditional distributions; bars divided into segments showing the proportion in each category of the response variable

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Analyzing survey data on exercise frequency (daily, weekly, rarely) conditioned on age group (18-30, 31-50, 51+) to identify demographic health patterns
  • Hospital data examining whether survival rates (survived/died) are conditionally distributed differently across different treatment regimens
  • College admissions data examining acceptance rates conditional on gender to investigate potential bias, while being alert for Simpson's Paradox across departments
