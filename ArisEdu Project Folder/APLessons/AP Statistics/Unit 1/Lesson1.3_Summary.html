AP Statistics - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Normal Distribution is a symmetric, bell-shaped continuous probability distribution defined by its mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ), and it serves as the most important distribution in statistics. The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7) states that approximately 68%, 95%, and 99.7% of observations fall within 1, 2, and 3 standard deviations of the mean. Assessing normality through histograms, normal probability plots, and the Empirical Rule is essential before applying many inferential procedures.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Normal distribution: A symmetric, bell-shaped probability distribution fully characterized by mean μ (center) and standard deviation σ (spread)
  • Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7): For normal distributions: ~68% of data within ±1σ, ~95% within ±2σ, and ~99.7% within ±3σ of the mean
  • Normal probability plot (Q-Q plot): A graphical tool that plots ordered data against expected normal quantiles; a roughly linear pattern indicates approximate normality
  • Standard normal distribution: The normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1, used as the reference distribution for z-score calculations
  • Density curve: A smooth curve that models the overall shape of a distribution; the total area under the curve equals 1 and represents probability

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Quality control in manufacturing using μ ± 3σ control limits to detect when a production process drifts out of specification
  • Standardized test score reporting (SAT, ACT) using normal distribution properties to convert raw scores to percentile ranks
  • Analyzing biological measurements (human heights, birth weights) that naturally approximate normal distributions to identify atypical values
