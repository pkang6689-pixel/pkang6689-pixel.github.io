AP Statistics - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Chi-Square Goodness of Fit tests whether an observed distribution of counts across categories matches an expected distribution. The test statistic χ² = Σ(observed - expected)²/expected measures overall departure from the expected distribution, with larger values indicating worse fit. The test uses a chi-square distribution with df = (number of categories - 1) and is always right-sided because large χ² values indicate poor fit to H₀.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Chi-square goodness of fit test: Tests H₀ that a categorical variable follows a specified distribution by comparing observed and expected counts
  • Chi-square statistic: χ² = Σ(O - E)²/E; sums the squared standardized differences between observed (O) and expected (E) counts across all categories
  • Expected counts: E = n × p_i for each category; the counts predicted by the null hypothesis distribution
  • Degrees of freedom: df = k - 1, where k is the number of categories; determines the shape of the chi-square distribution
  • Conditions: Random sample, all expected counts ≥ 5 (some textbooks use ≥ 1 with no more than 20% below 5), and independent observations

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Testing whether a die is fair by rolling it 600 times and comparing observed frequencies to the expected 100 per face using χ² goodness of fit
  • Genetics: testing whether observed offspring ratios match the expected Mendelian 9:3:3:1 ratio in a dihybrid cross experiment
  • Market research testing whether customer color preferences match the company's assumed distribution to guide product design decisions
