Lesson 6.3: Multiple-Angle Equations - Summary

Key Concepts: Solving Multiple-Angle Equations

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'multiple-angle equation' in trigonometry?

A multiple-angle equation has a trig function whose argument is nθ (n > 1) or θ/n, rather than just θ.

What is the double-angle identity for sin(2θ)?

The double-angle identity states sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ.

What is the half-angle identity for sin(θ/2)?

The half-angle identity is sin(θ/2) = ±√((1 − cos θ)/2), with the sign determined by the quadrant of θ/2.

What is meant by the 'argument' of a trigonometric function?

The argument is the input expression — in sin(2θ + π), the argument is 2θ + π.

What is the double-angle identity for cos(2θ)?

The double-angle identity for cosine has three equivalent forms: cos²θ − sin²θ, 2cos²θ − 1, and 1 − 2sin²θ.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is a 'multiple-angle equation' in trigonometry?

A multiple-angle equation has a trig function whose argument is nθ (n > 1) or θ/n, rather than just θ.

Example: What does 'expanding the interval' mean when solving multiple-angle equations?

When solving sin(nθ) = k for θ ∈ [0, 2π), you find all nθ values in [0, 2nπ), which is the expanded interval.

Example: What does 'back-substitution' mean in multiple-angle problems?

After solving sin(u) = k for u, you replace u with nθ and divide by n to find the original θ values.

Important Concepts

What does 'frequency' mean for a trig function like sin(nθ)

For sin(nθ), n is the frequency — the function completes n full cycles in [0, 2π).
