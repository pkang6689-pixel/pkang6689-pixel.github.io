Lesson 10.4: Applications in Calculus (limits, derivatives of trig functions) - Summary

Key Concepts: Calculus Connections & Applications

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the derivative of sin(x)?

d/dx[sin(x)] = cos(x). This fundamental derivative is derived from the limit definition using sin(A+B) expansion and the limits lim sin(h)/h = 1 and lim (cos(h)-1)/h = 0.

What is the derivative of cos(x)?

d/dx[cos(x)] = -sin(x). The negative sign is crucial and often a source of errors in calculus.

What is the integral of cos(x) dx?

Since d/dx[sin(x)] = cos(x), the antiderivative of cos(x) is sin(x) + C.

What is lim(x->0) sin(x)/x?

lim(x->0) sin(x)/x = 1 is one of the most important limits in calculus. It is used to derive the derivatives of all trig functions and can be proven geometrically using the squeeze theorem.

What is the derivative of arcsin(x)?

d/dx[arcsin(x)] = 1/sqrt(1 - x^2) for |x| < 1. This is derived using implicit differentiation on y = arcsin(x), i.e., sin(y) = x.

What is the derivative of arctan(x)?

d/dx[arctan(x)] = 1/(1 + x^2). This derivative appears frequently in integration and is the basis for integral of 1/(1+x^2) dx = arctan(x) + C.

What is the relationship between derivatives and rates of change in trig applications?

The derivative f'(x) gives the instantaneous rate of change of f at x. For trig functions like sin(omega*t) modeling oscillations, the derivative omega*cos(omega*t) gives the velocity (rate of displacement change).

What is a Taylor series?

A Taylor series represents a function as an infinite sum of terms involving its derivatives at a single point: f(x) = sum of f^(n)(a)(x-a)^n/n!. When a = 0, it is called a Maclaurin series.

What is L'Hopital's Rule and how does it relate to trigonometric limits?

L'Hopital's Rule states that for indeterminate forms 0/0 or infinity/infinity, lim f/g = lim f'/g'. For example, lim sin(x)/x = lim cos(x)/1 = 1. However, note that using L'Hopital on sin(x)/x is circular since d/dx[sin(x)] = cos(x) itself relies on this limit.

What is the integral of sec^2(x) dx?

Since d/dx[tan(x)] = sec^2(x), the antiderivative of sec^2(x) is tan(x) + C.

What is the Fourier series concept?

A Fourier series represents any periodic function as a sum of sines and cosines: f(x) = a0/2 + sum of [an cos(nx) + bn sin(nx)]. This is fundamental in signal processing, acoustics, and physics.

What is implicit differentiation and how is it used with trig?

Implicit differentiation differentiates both sides of an equation with respect to x, treating y as a function of x. For sin(y) = x, differentiating gives cos(y) * dy/dx = 1, so dy/dx = 1/cos(y) = 1/sqrt(1-x^2).

What is the squeeze theorem and how does it prove lim sin(x)/x = 1?

The squeeze theorem bounds sin(x)/x between cos(x) and 1 for small x (geometrically, using areas of triangles and a circular sector). Since both bounds approach 1, sin(x)/x must also approach 1.

What is the derivative of sec(x)?

d/dx[sec(x)] = d/dx[1/cos(x)] = sin(x)/cos^2(x) = (1/cos(x))(sin(x)/cos(x)) = sec(x)tan(x).

What is related rates and how do trig functions appear in them?

Related rates problems involve quantities changing with time that are connected by an equation. When the equation involves trig (like angles), differentiating with respect to time using the chain rule yields the rate relationships.

Using the Maclaurin series for cos(x), what is cos(0.1) to 4 decimal places?

cos(x) ≈ 1 - x^2/2 + x^4/24. cos(0.1) ≈ 1 - 0.01/2 + 0.0001/24 = 1 - 0.005 + 0.0000042 ≈ 0.9950.

A mass on a spring has displacement y(t) = 3sin(4t). What is the velocity at t = pi/8?

v(t) = y'(t) = 3(4)cos(4t) = 12cos(4t). At t = pi/8: v = 12cos(pi/2) = 12(0) = 0. At this moment, the mass is at maximum displacement and momentarily stationary.

The position of a pendulum is theta(t) = 0.2cos(sqrt(g/L) * t) where g = 9.8 m/s^2 and L = 1 m. What is the maximum angular velocity?

omega_0 = sqrt(g/L) = sqrt(9.8) ≈ 3.13 rad/s. theta'(t) = -0.2 * omega_0 * sin(omega_0 * t). Max angular velocity = 0.2 * omega_0 = 0.2 * 3.13 ≈ 0.626 rad/s.

A Ferris wheel of radius 25 m rotates at 1 revolution per minute. What is the maximum vertical speed of a rider?

Height: y(t) = 25sin(2pi*t/60). Vertical speed: y'(t) = 25(2pi/60)cos(2pi*t/60). Maximum when cos = 1: max speed = 25(2pi/60) = 50pi/60 = 5pi/6 ≈ 2.62 m/s.

The Fresnel integral C(x) = integral from 0 to x of cos(pi*t^2/2) dt appears in optics. Using the Maclaurin series for cos, what is the first nonzero correction term beyond C(x) ≈ x?

cos(pi*t^2/2) ≈ 1 - (pi*t^2/2)^2/2 + ... = 1 - pi^2*t^4/8 + .... Integrating: C(x) ≈ x - pi^2*x^5/(8*5) + ... = x - pi^2*x^5/40 + .... The first correction is -pi^2*x^5/40.
