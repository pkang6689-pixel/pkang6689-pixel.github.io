Lesson 3.2: Amortization Schedules - Summary

Key Concepts: Amortization Schedules

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an 'amortization schedule'?

An amortization schedule shows the payment number, interest portion, principal portion, and remaining balance for every payment. It reveals how the interest-to-principal ratio shifts over time, with early payments being mostly interest.

What is a 'balloon payment'?

A balloon payment is a large sum due at the end of a loan where regular payments did not fully amortize the balance. This can be risky because the borrower must either pay the lump sum, refinance, or sell the asset.

A $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years has a monthly payment of $1,199.10. If the first month's interest is $1,000, how much principal is paid in the first month, and what is the remaining balance after payment 1?

Principal paid = payment - interest = $1,199.10 - $1,000 = $199.10. Remaining balance = $200,000 - $199.10 = $199,800.90. Only about 17% of the first payment reduces the principal.

Compare two mortgages on $200,000: Mortgage A is 6% for 30 years (payment: $1,199, total interest: $231,676). Mortgage B is 6% for 15 years (payment: $1,688, total interest: $103,788). What is the monthly payment difference and total interest savings of choosing the 15-year option?

Monthly difference: $1,688 - $1,199 = $489. Interest savings: $231,676 - $103,788 = $127,888. By paying $489 more per month, you save nearly $128,000 in interest and own your home free and clear 15 years sooner.

A homeowner has a $250,000 mortgage at 7% for 30 years (payment: $1,663). After 5 years, rates drop and she refinances the remaining balance of approximately $238,000 to 5% for 25 years. Closing costs are $4,000. What is her new payment and approximate monthly savings?

New payment on $238,000 at 5% for 25 years: approximately $1,391. Monthly savings: $1,663 - $1,391 = $272. Break-even: $4,000 / $272 = approximately 15 months. Over 25 years, net savings (after closing costs) exceed $77,000.

A $350,000 mortgage at 5.5% for 30 years has a monthly payment of $1,987.26. The total interest over the life of the loan is approximately $365,414. What is the ratio of total interest to original principal?

Interest-to-principal ratio: $365,414 / $350,000 = 1.044. The borrower pays slightly more in interest than the original loan amount. Total cost: $350,000 + $365,414 = $715,414, more than double the purchase price.

Real-World Applications

Example: For a $200,000 mortgage at 6% annual rate for 30 years, the monthly rate r is:

When making monthly payments, the annual rate must be divided by 12 to get the monthly rate: 6% / 12 = 0.5% = 0.005 as a decimal.

Example: The total number of payments (n) for a 30-year monthly mortgage is:

Total monthly payments = 30 years x 12 months per year = 360 payments.

Example: In early mortgage payments, the majority of each payment goes toward:

Since interest is calculated on the outstanding balance, early payments are interest-heavy. As the principal is gradually paid down, more of each subsequent payment goes toward principal.

Example: The monthly payment on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years is approximately $1,199.10. Total payments over 30 years equal:

$1,199.10 x 360 payments = $431,676. This means the total interest paid is $431,676 - $200,000 = $231,676, which is more than the original loan amount.

Example: Biweekly mortgage payments (26 half-payments per year) effectively result in how many full monthly payments per year?

26 half-payments / 2 = 13 full monthly payments per year instead of 12. This one extra payment per year goes entirely to principal, significantly reducing total interest and loan duration.

Example: The monthly payment on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 15 years is approximately $1,688. Total interest paid is approximately:

Total payments: $1,688 x 180 = $303,840. Total interest: $303,840 - $200,000 = $103,840 (approximately $103,788). This is $127,888 less than the 30-year option, saving over half the interest despite higher monthly payments.

Example: Making an extra $100 per month toward principal on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years saves approximately:

An extra $100/month significantly accelerates principal paydown. Since early payments are mostly interest, even small additional principal payments compound into massive savings over time.

Example: What does 'equity' mean in the context of a mortgage?

Home equity equals the current market value minus what you still owe. Equity builds through principal payments and property appreciation. On a $300,000 home with a $200,000 remaining mortgage, your equity is $100,000.

Example: A $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years has a monthly payment of $1,199.10. If the first month's interest is $1,000, how much principal is paid in the first month, and what is the remaining balance after payment 1?

Principal paid = payment - interest = $1,199.10 - $1,000 = $199.10. Remaining balance = $200,000 - $199.10 = $199,800.90. Only about 17% of the first payment reduces the principal.

Example: After 15 years (180 payments) of a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years, approximately what percentage of the original principal has been paid off?

Due to front-loaded interest, principal paydown is much slower in the first half of the loan. After 15 years of a 30-year mortgage at 6%, only about 30% of the original principal has been repaid, with roughly $140,000 still owed on a $200,000 loan.

Example: The Johnsons have a $300,000 mortgage at 6.5% for 30 years with a monthly payment of $1,896. They switch to biweekly payments of $948 (half the monthly payment). How much do they save over the life of the loan?

Biweekly payments result in 26 half-payments = 13 full payments per year instead of 12. This one extra payment per year ($1,896) goes entirely to principal, reducing total interest by approximately $80,000 and cutting about 5 years off a 30-year term.

Example: A borrower has a mortgage with a $180,000 balance and makes a one-time extra principal payment of $10,000. If the interest rate is 6% (0.5% monthly), how much does this extra payment save in interest during the very next month?

Before extra payment: $180,000 x 0.005 = $900 monthly interest. After extra payment: $170,000 x 0.005 = $850 monthly interest. Savings = $50 per month. This $50 monthly savings compounds over the remaining life of the loan, totaling thousands in savings.

Example: A family is choosing between two options: Option A is a 30-year mortgage at 6% with payments of $1,199/month, and Option B is a 30-year mortgage at 6% with payments of $1,199/month plus an extra $300/month toward principal from the start. How much sooner does Option B pay off the loan?

Adding $300/month extra principal to a $200,000 mortgage at 6% pays it off in about 20 years instead of 30, saving approximately 10 years and over $90,000 in total interest. The power of extra principal payments is greatest early in the loan when the balance is highest.
