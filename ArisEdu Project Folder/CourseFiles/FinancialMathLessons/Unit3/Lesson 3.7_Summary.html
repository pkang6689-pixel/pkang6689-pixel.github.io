Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Borrowing - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Debt Management

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the 'debt avalanche' method?

The debt avalanche strategy involves making minimum payments on all debts except the one with the highest interest rate, which receives all available extra funds. Once that debt is paid off, the freed-up money rolls to the next highest rate.

What is 'lifestyle inflation' (lifestyle creep)?

Lifestyle inflation occurs when people upgrade their spending habits every time their income increases, channeling raises into upgraded expenses rather than debt payoff or savings, keeping them in the same financial position.

What is 'Chapter 13 bankruptcy'?

Chapter 13 allows individuals to keep their property while repaying debts through a court-approved 3-5 year plan. It stays on credit reports for 7 years (vs. 10 for Chapter 7) and is suited for those with regular income.

What is 'debt fatigue'?

Debt fatigue is a psychological phenomenon where borrowers become discouraged by slow progress and abandon their repayment plan. The snowball method's quick wins help combat this by providing early motivation.

What is a 'hardship program' offered by creditors?

Many creditors offer hardship programs for borrowers experiencing job loss, medical emergencies, or other financial difficulties. These may temporarily reduce rates or payments but typically require documentation of hardship.

Real-World Applications

Example: A debt management plan (DMP) through a nonprofit credit counselor:

A DMP through a reputable nonprofit counselor negotiates reduced interest rates with creditors, combines multiple payments into one, and typically takes 3-5 years to complete. It is far less damaging to credit than bankruptcy.

Example: A couple earns $7,000/month gross and has the following monthly debt payments: mortgage $1,800, car loan $400, student loans $300, credit card minimums $200. Their DTI ratio is:

Total monthly debt payments: $1,800 + $400 + $300 + $200 = $2,700. DTI = $2,700 / $7,000 = 38.6%. This is below the 43% QM threshold but above the preferred 36% level, suggesting they should reduce non-housing debt.

Example: David consolidated $30,000 in credit card debt (average 22% APR) into a personal loan at 10% for 5 years. His monthly payment is $637. After 2 years of on-time payments, he has accumulated $8,000 in new credit card debt. What went wrong?

David fell into the consolidation trap: he freed up credit card capacity by paying them off with the loan, then accumulated $8,000 in new charges. His total debt is now the remaining consolidation loan balance plus $8,000 — more than the original $30,000.
