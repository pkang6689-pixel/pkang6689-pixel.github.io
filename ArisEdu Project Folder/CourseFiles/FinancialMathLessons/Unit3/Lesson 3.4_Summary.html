Lesson 3.4: Credit Cards & Debt Management - Summary

Key Concepts: Credit Card Mathematics

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an 'annual fee' on a credit card?

Annual fees are charged by some cards, especially premium rewards cards with travel perks or high cashback rates. The card is only worthwhile if the value of rewards and benefits exceeds the annual fee.

A $500 impulse purchase on a 20% APR credit card is paid off over 2 years with monthly payments. The total interest paid is approximately $111. What is the effective markup on the original purchase?

Total cost: $500 + $111 = $611. Markup: $111 / $500 = 22.2%. Every purchase financed on a credit card effectively costs more than the sticker price. This is the hidden cost of carrying a credit card balance.

A cardholder earns 2% cashback on all purchases and spends $3,000/month on the card. They pay the full balance every month. What is their annual cashback earnings, and what would happen if they carried even a $1,000 balance at 20% APR instead?

Annual cashback: $3,000 x 12 x 0.02 = $720. If carrying $1,000 at 20%: $200/year interest. Net: $720 - $200 = $520. While still positive, the $200 in interest significantly reduces the reward value. Paying in full maximizes the benefit.

A college student gets their first credit card with a $1,000 limit. They charge $800 in the first month. What is their credit utilization, and how does this affect their credit score?

Utilization: $800 / $1,000 = 80%. Recommended: below 30%, ideally below 10%. At 80%, this heavily impacts credit score since utilization is about 30% of the FICO score. Even if paid in full by the due date, the high utilization may be reported to credit bureaus before the payment is made.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does 'average daily balance' mean for credit card interest calculations?

Most credit cards use the average daily balance method. Each day's balance is added up and divided by the number of days in the billing cycle. Interest = average daily balance x daily periodic rate x number of days. Paying early in the cycle reduces the average and thus the interest.

Example: Compare: $10,000 credit card debt at 22% APR versus $10,000 mortgage at 6%. How much more annual interest does the credit card cost?

Credit card: $10,000 x 0.22 = $2,200 annual interest. Mortgage: $10,000 x 0.06 = $600 annual interest. Difference: $1,600 per year. The credit card costs 3.67 times more in interest, making it the most expensive common debt.
