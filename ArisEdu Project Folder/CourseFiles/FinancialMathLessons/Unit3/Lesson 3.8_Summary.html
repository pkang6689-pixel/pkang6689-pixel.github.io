Lesson 3.8: Loan Comparison & Decision Making - Summary

Key Concepts: Loan Comparison & Decision-Making

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'HELOC' (Home Equity Line of Credit)?

A HELOC works like a credit card secured by your home. It has a draw period (often 10 years) where you can borrow and repay flexibly, followed by a repayment period. The risk is that your home is collateral.

What is 'TRID' in mortgage lending?

TRID combined two older disclosure requirements (Truth in Lending Act and RESPA) into two standardized forms: the Loan Estimate (provided within 3 days of application) and the Closing Disclosure (provided 3 days before closing).

What is 'payment anchoring' as a cognitive bias?

Payment anchoring causes borrowers to evaluate loans primarily by monthly payment rather than total cost. A $189/month payment looks better than $291/month, but the total cost difference can be hundreds or thousands of dollars.

Real-World Applications

Example: If your mortgage rate is 4% and investments return 8%, extra money is mathematically better:

Mathematically, investing at 8% earns more than the 4% cost of the mortgage. However, investment returns are uncertain while the mortgage interest is guaranteed, making this a risk-adjusted decision.

Example: Paying off a 4% mortgage gives a _____ return compared to uncertain 8% investment returns.

Paying off a 4% mortgage is equivalent to earning a guaranteed 4% return on your money. While investments may yield more, the certainty of eliminating debt has psychological and financial value.

Example: When comparing adjustable-rate vs. fixed-rate mortgages, you should consider:

ARM evaluation requires analyzing the worst case (maximum rate after adjustments), your ownership timeline (ARMs may save money if you sell before the rate adjusts), and whether rates are expected to rise or fall.

Example: Biweekly mortgage payments (26 half-payments per year) vs. monthly payments save money because:

26 biweekly half-payments equal 13 full monthly payments per year. That extra annual payment goes directly to principal, reducing the balance faster. On a $300,000 mortgage at 6%, this can save ~$60,000 in interest and cut 4-5 years off the term.

Example: What does 'APR' (Annual Percentage Rate) represent on a loan?

APR reflects the true annual cost of borrowing by including the interest rate plus origination fees, discount points, and other loan charges. It is always equal to or higher than the stated interest rate and allows apples-to-apples comparison.

Example: What is 'TRID' in mortgage lending?

TRID combined two older disclosure requirements (Truth in Lending Act and RESPA) into two standardized forms: the Loan Estimate (provided within 3 days of application) and the Closing Disclosure (provided 3 days before closing).

Example: What does 'rate lock' mean in mortgage lending?

A rate lock protects the borrower from rate increases during the loan processing period. If rates rise, you keep the locked rate. However, if rates fall significantly, you may want to renegotiate or use a float-down option.

Example: Two mortgage offers: Loan A is $200,000 at 6% for 30 years (monthly payment $1,199, total $431,676). Loan B is $200,000 at 5.75% with $6,000 in fees (monthly payment $1,167, total $426,233). Which is the better deal if you keep the loan the full 30 years?

Loan A total: $431,676. Loan B total: $426,233 + $6,000 fees = $432,233. Loan A is cheaper by $557 over 30 years. However, Loan B has a lower monthly payment ($32 less), which might matter for monthly cash flow.

Example: A homeowner has a $250,000 mortgage at 6.5%. They can refinance at 4.5% with $8,000 in closing costs, saving $330/month. The break-even period is approximately _____, and if they plan to stay 10 more years, the net savings would be:

Break-even: $8,000 / $330 = ~24 months. Total savings over 10 years: $330 x 120 = $39,600 minus $8,000 costs = $31,600 net savings. Since they plan to stay 10 years (well beyond the 24-month break-even), this refinance is clearly worthwhile.

Example: A borrower is comparing a 5/1 ARM starting at 4% vs. a 30-year fixed at 5.5% on a $300,000 mortgage. The ARM saves $282/month for the first 5 years. If the ARM adjusts to 7% after 5 years, the monthly payment increases by approximately $595. If the borrower plans to sell after 7 years, which is cheaper?

ARM savings years 1-5: $282 x 60 = $16,920. Extra ARM cost years 6-7: $595 x 24 = $14,280. Net savings with ARM: $16,920 - $14,280 = $2,640. For a 7-year hold, the ARM still saves money, but the margin is narrow and depends on actual rate adjustments.

Example: A buyer pays 2 points ($6,000) on a $300,000 mortgage to reduce the rate from 6% to 5.5%. This lowers the monthly payment by $99. How many months to break even, and how much does the buyer save over 30 years?

Break-even: $6,000 / $99 = ~61 months (about 5 years). Total savings over 30 years: $99 x 360 = $35,640 minus $6,000 cost = $29,640 net savings. If you plan to stay longer than 5 years, buying points is a strong move.

Example: A couple has $20,000 to either pay down their 3.5% mortgage or invest in a retirement fund averaging 7%. Over 20 years, investing the $20,000 would grow to approximately $77,394 while paying off the mortgage saves approximately $30,000 in avoided interest. The mathematical advantage of investing is:

Investing at 7%: $20,000 grows to ~$77,394 (gain of ~$57,394). Paying mortgage at 3.5%: saves ~$30,000 in interest. Net advantage of investing: ~$47,394. However, the mortgage savings are guaranteed while investment returns carry risk.

Example: A comprehensive loan decision should consider multiple factors. Rank these factors from most to least important for most borrowers:

Total cost is the primary measure of a loan's expense. Monthly affordability ensures you can sustain payments. Flexibility (no prepayment penalties, hardship options) protects against life changes. Opportunity cost considers what else you could do with the money.
