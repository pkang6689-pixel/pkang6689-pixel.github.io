Lesson 3.6: Real Estate Financing - Summary

Key Concepts: Real Estate & Auto Loan Math

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'gap insurance' for auto loans?

GAP (Guaranteed Asset Protection) insurance pays the difference between your loan balance and the car's depreciated value if it is totaled. This is especially important when you owe more than the car is worth.

What is the 'mortgage interest deduction'?

The mortgage interest deduction lets homeowners who itemize deductions subtract mortgage interest paid from their taxable income (on loans up to $750,000), effectively reducing the net cost of borrowing.

What is 'negative equity' in auto financing?

Negative equity occurs when the loan balance exceeds the vehicle's value. This is the same as being 'underwater' and creates financial risk if you need to sell or if the car is totaled.

Effective annual rent: $2,200 x 12 x 0.90 = $23,760. Operating expenses: $3,000 + $2,400 + $1,200 = $6,600. Annual mortgage: $900 x 12 = $10,800. Cash flow: $23,760 - $6,600 - $10,800 = $6,360. A first-time investor buys a duplex for $300,000 with 25% down ($75,000). Each unit rents for $1,200/month. Monthly mortgage is $1,200, property taxes $300, insurance $150, and maintenance reserve $200. With a 5% vacancy rate, what is the monthly cash flow and cash-on-cash return?

Effective monthly rent: $2,400 x 0.95 = $2,280. Monthly expenses: $1,200 (mortgage) + $300 (taxes) + $150 (insurance) + $200 (maintenance) = $1,850. Cash flow: $2,280 - $1,850 = $430/month or $5,160/year. Cash-on-cash return: $5,160 / $75,000 = 6.88%, approximately 6.9%.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does 'leverage' mean in real estate?

Leverage allows you to control a large asset with a relatively small amount of your own money. A 20% down payment on a $200,000 property means $40,000 controls $200,000 in real estate, but losses are also amplified.

Example: What is the 'mortgage interest deduction'?

The mortgage interest deduction lets homeowners who itemize deductions subtract mortgage interest paid from their taxable income (on loans up to $750,000), effectively reducing the net cost of borrowing.

Example: An investor puts 20% down ($50,000) on a $250,000 rental property. After mortgage, taxes, insurance, and maintenance, the property generates $3,600/year in net cash flow. Meanwhile, the property appreciates 3% ($7,500) and $2,400 in equity is built via mortgage paydown. The total return on the $50,000 investment is:

Total return includes cash flow ($3,600) + appreciation ($7,500) + equity buildup ($2,400) = $13,500. Divided by the $50,000 cash invested = 27%. This illustrates the power of leverage in real estate.
