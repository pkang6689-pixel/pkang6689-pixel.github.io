Lesson 1.3: Budgeting Basics - Summary

Key Concepts: Budgeting Basics

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'zero-based budgeting'?

Zero-based budgeting means giving every dollar a job so that your income minus all planned expenditures (including savings) equals zero, ensuring no money goes unaccounted for.

What is an 'envelope budgeting system'?

The envelope system is a cash-based budgeting method where you physically divide cash into labeled envelopes for each spending category, creating a tangible spending limit.

What is a 'sinking fund' in budgeting?

A sinking fund is money set aside regularly over time for a specific known future expense, allowing you to pay for irregular costs without disrupting your monthly budget.

What is 'cash flow' in personal finance?

Cash flow tracks the inflow (income from all sources) and outflow (all expenses and spending) of money over a period, with positive cash flow meaning more comes in than goes out.

What is a 'fixed expense'?

A fixed expense is a cost that remains constant from period to period, making it predictable and easy to plan for in a budget. Common examples include rent, mortgage payments, and insurance.

A family has the following monthly expenses: rent $1,500, car payment $350, groceries $600, utilities $200, entertainment $300, and insurance $250. Which are their fixed expenses and what is the fixed expense total?

Fixed expenses remain the same each month: rent ($1,500) + car payment ($350) + insurance ($250) = $2,100. Groceries, utilities, and entertainment are variable expenses that fluctuate.

A couple earns $8,000/month combined. They track expenses and find: housing $2,400, food $800, transportation $600, utilities $300, insurance $400, debt payments $500, entertainment $400, and miscellaneous $300. What is their monthly surplus?

Total expenses = $2,400 + $800 + $600 + $300 + $400 + $500 + $400 + $300 = $5,700. Surplus = $8,000 - $5,700 = $2,300 available for savings and investments.

Real-World Applications

Example: The 50/30/20 rule allocates what percentages to needs, wants, and savings respectively?

The 50/30/20 rule is a widely recommended budgeting guideline that allocates 50% of after-tax income to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt repayment.

Example: Housing costs should ideally not exceed approximately what percentage of gross income?

Most financial experts and mortgage lenders recommend keeping housing costs (rent or mortgage, insurance, taxes) at or below 28-30% of gross monthly income to maintain financial health.

Example: Kevin earns $3,800/month. His fixed expenses total $2,000 and variable expenses average $1,200. He wants to build a $6,000 emergency fund. At his current surplus, how many months will it take?

Monthly surplus = $3,800 - $2,000 - $1,200 = $600. Months to reach $6,000 = $6,000 / $600 = 10 months. A clear budget helps you calculate exactly how long goals will take.
