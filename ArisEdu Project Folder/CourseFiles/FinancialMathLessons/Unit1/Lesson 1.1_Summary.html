Lesson 1.1: Importance of Financial Literacy - Summary

Key Concepts: Importance of Financial Literacy

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is compound interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods, creating exponential growth over time.

What is the Rule of 72?

The Rule of 72 is a quick estimation tool where dividing 72 by the annual interest rate gives the approximate number of years needed for an investment to double.

What is an 'asset' in personal finance?

An asset is anything of value that you own, such as cash, investments, real estate, or vehicles, which can be converted to money or generate income.

What is 'financial literacy'?

Financial literacy is the possession of knowledge and skills that allow an individual to make informed and effective decisions about money management, including budgeting, saving, investing, and borrowing.

James has assets worth $150,000 (home equity, car, savings) and liabilities of $95,000 (mortgage balance, student loans, credit card debt). What is his net worth?

Net worth = Total Assets - Total Liabilities = $150,000 - $95,000 = $55,000. This figure represents James's actual financial position after accounting for all debts.

A family has a monthly income of $6,000. They spend $4,800 on expenses and save $1,200 in a high-yield savings account at 4.5% APY. What is their approximate annual interest earned on savings after 1 year?

Total saved in 1 year = $1,200 × 12 = $14,400. Average balance over the year is roughly $14,400/2 = $7,200. Interest ≈ 4.5% × $14,400 (simplified) ≈ $648. Even modest savings earn meaningful returns in a high-yield account.

Real-World Applications

Example: Approximately what percentage of Americans cannot cover a $1,000 emergency expense from savings?

Surveys consistently show that roughly 56% of Americans lack sufficient savings to handle a $1,000 emergency, highlighting widespread financial vulnerability.

Example: Financial illiteracy costs the average American approximately how much per year?

Research estimates that lack of financial knowledge costs the average American over $1,500 annually through unnecessary fees, poor interest rates, and suboptimal financial decisions.
