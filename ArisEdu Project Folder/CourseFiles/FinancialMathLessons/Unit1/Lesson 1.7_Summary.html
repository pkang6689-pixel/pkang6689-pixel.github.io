Lesson 1.7: Case Studies in Personal Finance - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Personal Finance

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the 'debt snowball' method?

The debt snowball method involves listing debts from smallest to largest balance, making minimum payments on all but the smallest, and throwing extra money at the smallest debt until it is eliminated, then moving to the next.

What is the 'debt avalanche' method?

The debt avalanche method targets the debt with the highest interest rate first, regardless of balance size. This approach saves the most money in total interest over time.

What is an 'amortization schedule'?

An amortization schedule is a detailed table showing for each payment how much goes to principal (reducing the balance) and how much goes to interest, revealing that early payments are mostly interest.

In a mortgage case study, a $250,000 home with 20% down ($50,000) results in a $200,000 mortgage. At 6.5% for 30 years, the monthly payment is approximately $1,264. What is the approximate total interest paid over the life of the loan?

Total payments = $1,264 × 360 months = $455,040. Total interest = $455,040 - $200,000 principal = $255,040. You pay more in interest than the original loan amount over 30 years.

A case study follows someone who received a $50,000 inheritance. Option A: invest it all at 9% for 20 years. Option B: spend $30,000 and invest $20,000 at 9% for 20 years. What is the approximate opportunity cost of Option B?

Option A: $50,000 × (1.09)^20 ≈ $280,000. Option B: $20,000 × (1.09)^20 ≈ $112,000. Opportunity cost = $280,000 - $112,000 = $168,000. The $30,000 spent actually cost $168,000 in foregone growth.

Example: What does 'risk management' mean in personal finance case studies?

Risk management involves identifying potential financial threats and using tools like insurance, emergency funds, and diversified investments to minimize the impact of adverse events.

Example: Investor A invests $300/month from age 22 to 32 (10 years) then stops. Investor B invests $300/month from age 32 to 62 (30 years). Both earn 8% return. At age 62, who has more money?

Investor A contributed $36,000 over 10 years, which grew to ~$509,000 by age 62. Investor B contributed $108,000 over 30 years, accumulating ~$440,000. Starting early wins even with fewer total contributions.

Example: In a rent-vs-buy case study, renting costs $1,500/month with no equity built. Buying costs $2,000/month (mortgage) with $600/month going to equity. After 7 years, approximately how much equity has the buyer built while the renter has built none?

Equity built = $600/month × 12 months × 7 years = $50,400 (not counting home appreciation). The renter has $0 in housing equity. However, the renter also saves $500/month on the payment difference, which could be invested.

Example: A case study shows that paying $100 extra per month on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% (30-year) can save approximately how much in total interest?

Paying just $100 extra monthly on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% saves approximately $47,000 in interest and pays off the loan about 5 years early. Small extra payments have outsized effects on long-term loans.
