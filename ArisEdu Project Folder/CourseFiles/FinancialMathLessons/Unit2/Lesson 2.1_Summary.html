Lesson 2.1: Simple Interest Formula - Summary

Key Concepts: Simple Interest Formula

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

A Treasury bill pays $3,000 in simple interest on a $50,000 investment over 1 year. What is the interest rate?

r = I/(Pt) = $3,000/($50,000 × 1) = 0.06 = 6%. Rearranging the simple interest formula lets you solve for any unknown variable.

What is 'principal' in the simple interest formula?

Principal (P) is the initial sum of money placed in an investment or the original amount borrowed on a loan, before any interest is calculated.

What is an 'ordinary interest' calculation?

Ordinary interest (banker's rule) uses a 360-day year for simpler calculations. Exact interest uses 365 days. The choice affects how t is computed for periods measured in days.

What is a 'promissory note'?

A promissory note is a legal document where the borrower promises to repay the lender a specified sum plus interest by a specific date. Simple interest is commonly used for these instruments.

A car dealer offers a $15,000 loan at 7% simple interest for 4 years. What is the total amount the buyer will repay?

I = $15,000 × 0.07 × 4 = $4,200. Total repayment = $15,000 + $4,200 = $19,200. Over 4 years, the borrower pays 28% of the principal in interest alone.

A bank offers a 90-day promissory note for $20,000 at 6% simple interest using ordinary interest (360-day year). What is the interest charge?

Using ordinary interest: t = 90/360 = 0.25 years. I = $20,000 × 0.06 × 0.25 = $300. The 360-day year convention slightly increases the interest compared to exact interest.

A small business owner needs to borrow $40,000 for equipment. Bank A offers 7% simple interest for 5 years. Bank B offers 6% simple interest for 6 years. What is the difference in total interest paid?

Bank A: I = $40,000 × 0.07 × 5 = $14,000. Bank B: I = $40,000 × 0.06 × 6 = $14,400. Bank B costs $400 more despite the lower rate because the longer term adds more interest overall.
