Lesson 2.3: Continuous Compounding - Summary

Key Concepts: Continuous Compounding

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is Euler's number (e)?

Euler's number (e ≈ 2.71828) is a fundamental mathematical constant that naturally appears in continuous growth processes. It is the base of the natural logarithm and is used extensively in calculus and finance.

What is the 'limit' concept that gives rise to continuous compounding?

Continuous compounding arises from the mathematical limit: as n → ∞ in P(1 + r/n)^(nt), the expression converges to Pe^(rt). This elegant result connects discrete and continuous mathematics.

A financial analyst needs to find the continuously compounded rate that turns $40,000 into $100,000 in 15 years. What is the approximate rate?

r = ln(A/P)/t = ln(100000/40000)/15 = ln(2.5)/15 = 0.9163/15 ≈ 0.0611 = 6.11%.

A venture capital firm expects a startup investment of $200,000 to grow at a continuously compounded rate of 25% for 5 years. What is the expected value?

A = $200,000 × e^(0.25×5) = $200,000 × e^1.25 ≈ $200,000 × 3.4903 ≈ $698,060. High continuous growth rates produce dramatic results over even short periods.

A Treasury bond has a continuously compounded yield of 3.2%. What is the effective annual rate an investor actually earns?

EAR = e^r - 1 = e^0.032 - 1 ≈ 1.03252 - 1 = 0.03252 = 3.25%. The effective rate is slightly higher than the stated continuous rate.

A pension fund manager must determine how much to invest today at 5.5% continuously compounded to have $2,000,000 in 20 years. What is the present value needed?

P = Ae^(-rt) = $2,000,000 × e^(-0.055×20) = $2,000,000 × e^(-1.1) ≈ $2,000,000 × 0.3329 ≈ $665,800. About one-third of the target amount needs to be invested today.
