Lesson 2.5: Applications in Savings Accounts - Summary

Key Concepts: Applications in Savings Accounts

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is APY (Annual Percentage Yield)?

APY stands for Annual Percentage Yield. It reflects the total amount of interest earned on a deposit over one year, including the effect of compounding. APY allows consumers to compare accounts with different compounding frequencies on an equal basis.

What is a CD ladder?

A CD ladder involves purchasing multiple CDs with different maturity dates (e.g., 1-year, 2-year, 3-year, 4-year, 5-year). As each CD matures, you can reinvest it or use the funds, providing both higher rates and regular access to your money.

What is a Treasury I-bond?

Treasury I-bonds are U.S. government savings bonds with a composite rate consisting of a fixed rate plus a semiannually adjusted inflation rate. They protect against inflation and are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government.

What is a money market account?

A money market account is a type of deposit account that functions as a hybrid between savings and checking accounts. It generally offers higher interest rates and some check-writing ability, but may require higher minimum balances to avoid fees.

What is the 'nominal rate' on a savings account?

The nominal rate (also called the stated rate or annual percentage rate for deposits) is the interest rate quoted by the bank before factoring in how often interest compounds. The APY is always equal to or greater than the nominal rate when compounding occurs more than once per year.

Lisa builds a CD ladder by placing $2,000 into each of five CDs: a 1-year CD at 4.0%, a 2-year CD at 4.3%, a 3-year CD at 4.5%, a 4-year CD at 4.7%, and a 5-year CD at 5.0%. What is her total initial investment, and approximately how much interest will the 5-year CD earn by maturity using annual compounding?

Total invested: 5 x $2,000 = $10,000. The 5-year CD: A = 2000(1.05)^5 = 2000 x 1.27628 = $2,552.56, earning $552.56 in interest. The CD ladder gives Lisa access to one maturing CD each year while earning higher long-term rates.

Priya has $15,000 in a savings account earning 0.5% APY while inflation is 3%. Over one year, what is the approximate change in her money's purchasing power?

Real return = 0.5% - 3% = -2.5%. Loss in purchasing power: $15,000 x 0.025 = $375. Although her account balance grows by $75 (0.5% interest), prices rise by $450 (3% inflation), so she effectively loses $375 in purchasing power.

A family has $30,000 to allocate across savings vehicles. They put $5,000 in a regular savings at 0.1% APY, $10,000 in a high-yield savings at 4.5% APY, and $15,000 in a 3-year CD at 5.0% compounded annually. What is their approximate total interest earned after one year?

Regular savings: $5,000 x 0.001 = $5. High-yield savings: $10,000 x 0.045 = $450. CD: $15,000 x 0.05 = $750. Total first-year interest: $5 + $450 + $750 = $1,205. This diversified approach balances liquidity needs with higher returns.

Real-World Applications

Example: Savings accounts at credit unions are insured by which agency?

Credit union deposits are insured by the NCUA, not the FDIC. However, the coverage amount is the same at $250,000 per depositor, per institution.
