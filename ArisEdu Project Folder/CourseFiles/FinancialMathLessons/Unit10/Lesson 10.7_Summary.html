Key Concepts: AP Financial Math Scenarios

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a W-2 form?

The W-2 is issued by employers by January 31st each year. It shows total gross wages, federal/state taxes withheld, Social Security and Medicare taxes, and retirement contributions — essential information for filing your tax return.

What is the difference between gross pay and net pay?

Gross pay is your full salary or wages. Net pay (take-home) is gross minus deductions: federal tax, state tax, FICA (Social Security + Medicare), health insurance, and retirement contributions. Always budget based on net pay.

What is a FICO credit score?

FICO scores range from 300-850. The five factors: payment history (35%), credit utilization (30%), length of history (15%), credit mix (10%), and new inquiries (10%). Scores above 740 generally qualify for the best rates.

What is a high-deductible health plan (HDHP)?

HDHPs trade lower monthly premiums for higher out-of-pocket costs before insurance kicks in. They are often paired with HSAs, which offer triple tax benefits (tax-deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free qualified withdrawals).

What is the debt-to-income (DTI) ratio?

DTI = Total monthly debt payments / Gross monthly income. Lenders prefer DTI below 36% (ideally under 28% for housing alone). A DTI above 43% typically disqualifies mortgage applicants.

Example: The $225/month 401(k) contribution at 8% for 43 years (age 22 to 65) grows to approximately:

FV = $225/month × [(1.00667)^516 - 1] / 0.00667 ≈ $1,000,000+. Total contributions: $225 × 516 = $116,100. Compound interest adds nearly $900,000. Starting early with even modest amounts produces remarkable results.

Example: To build a strong credit score, the graduate should:

The three most impactful credit actions: 1) Pay every bill on time (35% of score), 2) Keep credit card balances below 30% of limits (30% of score), 3) Keep old accounts open for length of history (15%). A strong score saves thousands on future mortgage rates.
