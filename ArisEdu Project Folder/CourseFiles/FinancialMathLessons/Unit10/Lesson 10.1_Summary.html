Lesson 10.1: Review of Key Formulas - Summary

Key Concepts: Review of Key Formulas

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the future value (FV) formula used to determine?

The future value formula FV = PV × (1 + r)^n calculates how much a present sum will be worth at a future date given a specific interest rate and time period.

What is the internal rate of return (IRR)?

IRR is the discount rate at which NPV = 0. If the IRR exceeds the required rate of return (hurdle rate), the project should be accepted.

What is the Sharpe ratio used to measure?

Sharpe ratio = (Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation. A higher Sharpe ratio indicates better return per unit of risk, making it useful for comparing investments.

What is the contribution margin?

Contribution margin = Price - Variable Cost. It represents the amount each unit sold contributes toward covering fixed costs and generating profit.

What is dividend yield?

Dividend yield = Annual Dividend / Stock Price. For example, a $2.40 annual dividend on a $60 stock gives a 4% yield. It measures the income return from holding a stock.

A company is evaluating a project costing $100,000 that generates $30,000 per year for 5 years. At a 10% discount rate, the PV of the annuity is approximately $113,724. What is the NPV and should they accept?

NPV = PV of inflows - initial cost = $113,724 - $100,000 = +$13,724. Since NPV is positive, the project earns more than the 10% required return and should be accepted.

A company is financed with 50% debt at 5% after-tax cost and 50% equity at 11% cost of equity. What is the company's WACC?

WACC = (0.50 × 5%) + (0.50 × 11%) = 2.5% + 5.5% = 8.0%. This is the minimum return the company must earn on its projects to satisfy both debt holders and equity investors.

An entrepreneur evaluates a new venture with a 60% chance of $50,000 profit, 25% chance of breaking even, and 15% chance of a $30,000 loss. What is the expected value of this venture?

EV = (0.60 × $50,000) + (0.25 × $0) + (0.15 × -$30,000) = $30,000 + $0 - $4,500 = $25,500. The positive expected value suggests the venture is worth pursuing from a mathematical standpoint.

Real-World Applications

Example: Monthly mortgage payment on $300,000 at 6.5% for 30 years is approximately:

Using the PMT formula with r = 6.5%/12 = 0.5417% and n = 360, the monthly payment is approximately $1,896.

Example: Total interest on that 30-year mortgage: ($1,896 × 360) - $300,000 =

Total payments = $1,896 × 360 = $682,560. Total interest = $682,560 - $300,000 = $382,560. You pay more in interest than the original loan amount.

Example: A homeowner pays $15,000 in mortgage interest this year and is in the 24% tax bracket. How much does the mortgage interest deduction save them in taxes?

Tax savings = Deduction × Marginal Tax Rate = $15,000 × 0.24 = $3,600. A deduction reduces taxable income, saving the deduction amount times the marginal rate.

Example: A worker saves $300/month starting at age 25, earning 8% annually (compounded monthly) until retirement at 65 (40 years). Approximately how much will they accumulate?

Using the FV of annuity formula with PMT = $300, r = 0.08/12, n = 480 months: FV = $300 × [(1.00667)^480 - 1] / 0.00667 ≈ $1,050,000. Total contributions are only $144,000 — compound interest adds over $900,000.
