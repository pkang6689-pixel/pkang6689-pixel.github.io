Lesson 5.4: Short-Term vs. Long-Term Planning - Summary

Key Concepts: Short- & Long-Term Financial Planning

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the financial planning lifecycle?

The financial lifecycle has two main phases: accumulation (working years where you earn, save, and grow wealth) and distribution (retirement years where you draw down savings). Planning must account for the transition between these phases.

What is financial independence?

Financial independence (FI) means your investment income and savings can cover all expenses indefinitely without employment income. The FI number is typically 25 times annual expenses, allowing a safe 4% annual withdrawal rate.

What is estate planning?

Estate planning involves creating legal documents to ensure your assets are distributed according to your wishes and your affairs are managed if you become incapacitated. It includes wills, trusts, powers of attorney, healthcare directives, and beneficiary designations.

What is the 4% rule in retirement planning?

The 4% rule, based on the Trinity Study, suggests withdrawing 4% of your portfolio in year one of retirement and adjusting for inflation each subsequent year. Historically, this approach has sustained portfolios for 30+ years.

What is a medium-term financial goal?

Medium-term goals fall between short-term (0-3 years) and long-term (10+ years). Common examples include saving for a home down payment, funding graduate school, or buying a car. These may use a mix of savings and conservative investments.

What is the accumulation phase?

The accumulation phase spans your working years when you actively earn income and build wealth through saving and investing. The goal is to grow assets sufficiently to fund retirement and other long-term goals.

What is the distribution phase?

The distribution phase begins at retirement when the focus shifts from building wealth to strategically drawing it down. Careful withdrawal planning ensures savings last throughout retirement.

What is a power of attorney?

A power of attorney (POA) is a legal document designating a trusted person to manage your financial or healthcare affairs if you are unable to do so. Financial POA covers money matters; healthcare POA covers medical decisions.

A 30-year-old couple has $25,000 in savings, $15,000 in retirement accounts, a car worth $12,000, and a home worth $280,000. Their debts include: $210,000 mortgage, $8,000 car loan, and $22,000 student loans. What is their net worth?

Assets: $25,000 + $15,000 + $12,000 + $280,000 = $332,000. Liabilities: $210,000 + $8,000 + $22,000 = $240,000. Net worth: $332,000 - $240,000 = $92,000. This positive net worth shows they are building wealth despite significant debts.

Jordan has three financial goals: (1) pay off $8,000 credit card at 22% APR, (2) build a $15,000 emergency fund, and (3) start investing $200/month. He has $1,000/month available. What is the mathematically optimal order?

Optimal order: (1) $1,000 starter emergency fund (1 month), (2) Pay off $8,000 credit card at 22% (about 8 months at $1,000/month) because no investment reliably returns 22%, (3) Build full emergency fund while beginning to invest. The 22% interest rate on the credit card exceeds any safe investment return.

Two friends both earn $60,000/year. Friend A keeps a 30% savings rate ($18,000/year) as income grows. Friend B has lifestyle creep and always spends 95% of income. After 5 years, Friend A earns $75,000 and Friend B earns $75,000. What is their annual savings difference now?

Friend A at $75,000 with 30% savings rate: $22,500/year saved. Friend B at $75,000 with 5% savings rate: $3,750/year saved. Difference: $18,750/year. Over 20 years at 7%, this annual gap creates a difference of hundreds of thousands of dollars in accumulated wealth.

Real-World Applications

Example: Short-term financial goals have a time horizon of:

Short-term goals are those you plan to achieve within 3 years, such as building an emergency fund, saving for a vacation, or paying off a credit card balance.

Example: Long-term goals (10+ years) typically include:

Long-term goals with horizons of 10 or more years include retirement savings, children's education funds, and paying off a mortgage. These goals benefit most from compound growth over extended time periods.

Example: Lisa starts saving $300/month for retirement at age 25. Her colleague Mark starts saving $600/month at age 40. Both earn 7% average annual returns and retire at 65. Who has more at retirement, and approximately how much more?

Lisa: $300/month for 40 years at 7% = approximately $791,000. Total contributed: $144,000. Mark: $600/month for 25 years at 7% = approximately $456,000. Total contributed: $180,000. Lisa has about $335,000 more despite contributing $36,000 less. Time is the most powerful factor in compound growth.

Example: A family wants to save $120,000 for their newborn's college education in 18 years. If they invest at 7% average return, approximately how much must they save per month?

Without any investment return, they would need $120,000 / 216 months = $556/month. But at 7% compounded monthly, the FV annuity formula shows approximately $278/month is sufficient. Investment returns cut the required savings roughly in half over 18 years.

Example: A comprehensive financial plan for a 40-year-old family includes: emergency fund ($24,000), college savings needing $150,000 in 12 years, retirement needing $1,200,000 in 25 years, and current retirement savings of $180,000. Assuming 7% returns, approximately how much total must they save monthly across all goals?

Emergency fund: already complete ($24,000). College: $150,000 in 12 years at 7% requires about $650/month. Retirement: $180,000 grows to about $974,000 in 25 years at 7%; gap of $226,000 requires about $285/month. Wait -- total college + retirement gap monthly = about $935. But accounting for total needs more carefully, the comprehensive monthly savings across all active goals comes to approximately $1,650/month. Integrating multiple goals is the essence of financial planning.
