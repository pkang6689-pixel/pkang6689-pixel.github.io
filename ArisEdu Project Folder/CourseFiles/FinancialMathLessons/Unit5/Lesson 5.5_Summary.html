Lesson 5.5: Case Studies in Budgeting - Summary

Key Concepts: Budget Case Studies

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'Dependent Care FSA' and how does it help families?

A Dependent Care FSA allows employees to set aside up to $5,000/year pre-tax for qualifying childcare expenses. In a 25% tax bracket, this saves $1,250/year in taxes -- effectively getting a discount on childcare.

What is a 'budget trade-off' in case study analysis?

Budget trade-offs are decisions about where to allocate limited resources. Choosing to spend more on childcare (a need) might mean spending less on dining out (a want). Every budget reflects priorities through these trade-offs.

What is a 'nanny share' and how does it reduce childcare costs?

A nanny share involves two or more families hiring one nanny to care for their children together. Each family pays less than hiring a private nanny while still getting personalized, in-home care -- often 30-40% cheaper per family.

What is 'intentional spending'?

Intentional spending means every purchase is a conscious choice aligned with your priorities. It does not mean eliminating all fun spending -- it means choosing to spend on things that genuinely bring value while cutting mindless, automatic spending.

What is 'geographic arbitrage' in budget optimization?

Geographic arbitrage takes advantage of cost-of-living differences. A remote worker earning a San Francisco salary ($120K) while living in a lower-cost city where $80K provides the same lifestyle effectively saves $40K/year.

What is 'budget fatigue'?

Budget fatigue occurs when a budget is too restrictive, causing frustration that leads to complete abandonment. Sustainable budgets include some fun spending. This is why the 50/30/20 rule dedicates 30% to wants.

What is a 'financial snapshot' in case study analysis?

A financial snapshot captures all key financial data at a specific point: income, expenses, savings, debts, assets, and net worth. Case studies use snapshots to identify strengths, weaknesses, and opportunities for improvement.

Continuing Emma's case: If she finds a roommate and reduces rent from $1,020 to $600, and cuts dining out from $200 to $100, what is her new monthly surplus and savings rate?

Savings from roommate: $1,020 - $600 = $420/month. Savings from dining cut: $200 - $100 = $100/month. Total new savings: $115 + $420 + $100 = $635. But the exact surplus is approximately $535. New savings rate: $535 / $3,400 = 15.7%. Two changes transformed her financial picture.

Case Study: The Rodriguez family earns $7,200/month net combined. Housing $1,900, childcare $1,350, car expenses $600, groceries $650, utilities $250, insurance $400, dining $300, student loans $450, and misc $300. What is their savings capacity, and what percentage does childcare represent?

Total expenses: $1,900 + $1,350 + $600 + $650 + $250 + $400 + $300 + $450 + $300 = $6,200. Surplus: $7,200 - $6,200 = $1,000. But with additional small expenses, approximately $900 is available. Childcare percentage: $1,350 / $7,200 = 18.8%. Housing + childcare = $3,250, consuming 45% of income.

Case Study: A couple earns $9,000/month combined but has $2,700 in monthly debt payments (mortgage $1,500, car $400, student loans $500, credit cards $300). What is their DTI ratio, and is it considered healthy?

DTI: $2,700 / $9,000 = 30%. Lenders generally prefer DTI under 36%, so 30% is at the upper limit of acceptable. Paying off the credit cards ($300/month) would reduce DTI to 26.7%, significantly improving financial flexibility.

Real-World Applications

Example: A recent graduate earning $3,200/month net with $1,050 rent spends what percentage of income on housing?

$1,050 divided by $3,200 equals 32.8%, approximately 33%. While slightly above the 30% guideline, this is common for new graduates in urban areas where entry-level salaries are still growing.

Example: A young family spending $1,200/month on childcare from a $5,500/month income spends what percentage on childcare?

$1,200 / $5,500 = 21.8%, approximately 22%. Childcare is often the second-largest expense after housing for young families, significantly impacting the budget.

Example: Case Study: A family with $5,500/month income wants to go from 10% ($550) to 20% ($1,100) savings rate. They identify three potential cuts: dining out ($400 to $200), subscriptions ($150 to $50), and clothing ($200 to $100). Do these cuts achieve the goal?

Additional savings needed: $1,100 - $550 = $550. Cuts: dining ($200) + subscriptions ($100) + clothing ($100) = $400 total. Shortfall: $550 - $400 = $150. They need to find an additional $150/month through other cuts or income increases to reach 20%.
