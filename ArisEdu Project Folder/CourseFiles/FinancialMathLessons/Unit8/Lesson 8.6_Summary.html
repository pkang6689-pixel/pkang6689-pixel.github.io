Lesson 8.6: Case Studies in Market Behavior - Summary

Key Concepts: Market Case Studies

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'recency bias' in investing?

Recency bias causes investors to overweight recent experiences. After a crash, they expect more crashes and avoid investing. After a bull market, they expect endless gains and take on too much risk.

What is 'herd mentality' in financial markets?

Herd mentality drives investors to follow the crowd rather than think independently. It amplifies bubbles (everyone rushes to buy) and crashes (everyone rushes to sell), often at the worst possible times.

What is a 'market bubble'?

A market bubble occurs when prices far exceed fundamental value driven by speculation, euphoria, and herd mentality. Examples include the dot-com bubble (2000) and the housing bubble (2007-2008).

What is a 'market correction'?

A market correction is a 10-20% decline from recent highs. Corrections occur frequently (roughly once per year on average) and are a normal part of market behavior, not necessarily a sign of crisis.

What is 'subprime lending' as it relates to the 2008 crisis?

Subprime lending refers to loans made to borrowers with weak credit who have a higher risk of default. Widespread subprime mortgage lending with relaxed standards was a key cause of the 2008 financial crisis.

An investor who stayed fully invested in the S&P 500 from 2000-2020 earned about 6% annually. If they missed the 10 best days, their return dropped to approximately 2% annually. On a $100,000 initial investment over 20 years, what is the approximate dollar difference?

Fully invested at 6%: $100,000 x (1.06)^20 = $320,714. Missed 10 best days at 2%: $100,000 x (1.02)^20 = $148,595. Difference = $320,714 - $148,595 = $172,119. Missing just 10 days over 20 years cost about $172,000.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

The dot-com bubble was concentrated in technology stocks. The NASDAQ Composite fell roughly 78% from its March 2000 peak as speculative internet company valuations collapsed.

Real-World Applications

Example: The COVID crash of March 2020 saw the S&P 500 drop approximately what percentage in just 23 trading days?

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered the fastest bear market in history, with the S&P 500 dropping 34% in just 23 trading days from mid-February to late March 2020.

Example: Market crashes (declines of 20% or more) happen on average every:

Bear markets (20%+ declines) occur roughly every 3-5 years on average. They are a normal part of market cycles, and long-term investors will experience several during their investing lifetime.

Example: An investor had $200,000 in the S&P 500 before the 2008 crisis. After the 57% decline, the portfolio dropped to $86,000. What percentage gain is needed to recover back to $200,000?

Recovery needed = ($200,000 - $86,000) / $86,000 = $114,000 / $86,000 = 132.6%, approximately 133%. This illustrates why avoiding large losses is so important -- a 57% loss requires a 133% gain just to break even.

Example: During the COVID crash, an investor continued dollar cost averaging $2,000/month. They invested $2,000 each in March (S&P at 2,300), April (2,500), and May (2,900). What was their average purchase price?

Units bought: March = $2,000/2,300 = 0.8696, April = $2,000/2,500 = 0.8000, May = $2,000/2,900 = 0.6897. Total units = 2.3593. Average cost = $6,000 / 2.3593 = ~$2,542, approximately $2,536. DCA gave a lower average than the simple average of $2,567.

Example: During the dot-com crash, the NASDAQ fell 78% from 5,049 to about 1,114. What percentage gain was needed to return to 5,049?

Recovery needed = (5,049 - 1,114) / 1,114 = 3,935 / 1,114 = 353%. A 78% decline requires a staggering 353% gain just to break even, illustrating the asymmetric math of losses.
