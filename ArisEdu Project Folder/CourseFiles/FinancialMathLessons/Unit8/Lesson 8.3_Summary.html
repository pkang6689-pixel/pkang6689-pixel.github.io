Lesson 8.3: Market Indices (Dow, S&P 500) - Summary

Key Concepts: Market Indices & Benchmarks

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'market index'?

A market index is a statistical measure that tracks the performance of a selected group of stocks or other assets, serving as a benchmark for market or sector performance.

What is an 'expense ratio' on a fund?

The expense ratio is the annual fee expressed as a percentage of assets under management. A 0.10% expense ratio means $1 per year for every $1,000 invested.

What is a 'Total Stock Market index'?

A Total Stock Market index (like the CRSP US Total Market Index tracked by VTI) includes stocks of all sizes -- large, mid, small, and micro-cap -- providing exposure to the entire domestic stock market.

What is the 'MSCI EAFE' index?

MSCI EAFE stands for Morgan Stanley Capital International Europe, Australasia, and Far East. It tracks developed international stock markets excluding the US and Canada.

What is a 'bond index'?

A bond index (like the Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index) tracks the performance of a diversified basket of bonds and serves as a fixed-income benchmark for bond fund performance.

The Dow Jones goes from 30,000 to 33,000. What is the percentage gain?

Percentage gain = (33,000 - 30,000) / 30,000 x 100 = 3,000 / 30,000 x 100 = 10%.

A portfolio is allocated 60% stocks and 40% bonds. If stocks return 12% and bonds return 4%, what is the blended portfolio return?

Blended return = (0.60 x 12%) + (0.40 x 4%) = 7.2% + 1.6% = 8.8%.

An investor puts $500 per month into an S&P 500 index fund. In Month 1 the price is $50/share, Month 2 is $40/share, and Month 3 is $60/share. What is the average cost per share using DCA?

Shares bought: Month 1 = 10, Month 2 = 12.5, Month 3 = 8.33. Total shares = 30.83. Total invested = $1,500. Average cost = $1,500 / 30.83 = $48.65, which is less than the simple average price of $50.

Real-World Applications

Example: The S&P 500 represents approximately what percentage of the total US stock market value?

The S&P 500 includes 500 large-cap US companies that together represent roughly 80% of the total US stock market capitalization.

Example: The Dow Jones Industrial Average tracks how many companies and is weighted by what method?

The DJIA tracks 30 large blue-chip companies and is price-weighted, meaning stocks with higher share prices have more influence on the index.

Example: Over 20 years, approximately what percentage of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index?

The SPIVA scorecard shows roughly 80-90% of actively managed funds fail to beat their benchmark index over a 20-year period, which is a key reason why index investing has become so popular.

Example: The historical S&P 500 average annual return (1926-present) is approximately:

The S&P 500 has returned roughly 10% annually on average in nominal terms since 1926, or about 7% after adjusting for inflation.

Example: Actively managed funds typically charge expense ratios of:

Active funds typically charge 0.5-1.5% in expense ratios to cover fund managers, research analysts, and higher trading costs. Over decades, this fee difference compounds significantly.

Example: If the S&P 500 returns 10% for the year and your actively managed fund returns 8%, by how many percentage points did your fund underperform the benchmark?

Underperformance = Benchmark return - Fund return = 10% - 8% = 2 percentage points. The fund trailed its benchmark by 2 percentage points for the year.

Example: The Dow Jones goes from 30,000 to 33,000. What is the percentage gain?

Percentage gain = (33,000 - 30,000) / 30,000 x 100 = 3,000 / 30,000 x 100 = 10%.

Example: An investor puts $500 per month into an S&P 500 index fund. In Month 1 the price is $50/share, Month 2 is $40/share, and Month 3 is $60/share. What is the average cost per share using DCA?

Shares bought: Month 1 = 10, Month 2 = 12.5, Month 3 = 8.33. Total shares = 30.83. Total invested = $1,500. Average cost = $1,500 / 30.83 = $48.65, which is less than the simple average price of $50.

Example: A price-weighted index has three stocks priced at $100, $200, and $300. If the $300 stock drops 10% (to $270) while the others stay unchanged, by what percentage does the index decline?

Original index value = ($100 + $200 + $300) / 3 = $200. New value = ($100 + $200 + $270) / 3 = $190. Decline = ($200 - $190) / $200 x 100 = 5%. The $300 stock's 10% drop caused a 5% index decline because it represented half the index weight.

Example: A young investor (age 25) invests $300/month in an S&P 500 index fund averaging 10% annual returns for 40 years. Total contributions are $144,000. Approximately how much will the portfolio be worth, demonstrating the power of compound growth?

Using the future value of annuity formula: FV = $300 x [((1 + 0.10/12)^480 - 1) / (0.10/12)] is approximately $1.6 million. The investor contributed only $144,000; the remaining ~$1.46 million came from compound growth.

Important Concepts

The Dow Jones Industrial Average tracks how many companies and is weighted by what method

The DJIA tracks 30 large blue-chip companies and is price-weighted, meaning stocks with higher share prices have more influence on the index.

The NASDAQ Composite is heavily weighted toward which sector

The NASDAQ Composite is dominated by technology companies such as Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google, giving it a strong tech tilt.

In a price-weighted index like the Dow, a stock at $300 has what influence compared to a stock at $50

In a price-weighted index, each stock's influence is proportional to its share price. A $300 stock affects the index six times more than a $50 stock, regardless of company size.

What does 'market-cap weighted' mean for an index

In a market-cap weighted index like the S&P 500, each company's influence is proportional to its total market capitalization. A $3 trillion company has far more impact than a $30 billion company.

In the S&P 500 (market-cap weighted), Company A has a market cap of $2 trillion and Company B has a market cap of $50 billion. Approximately how many times more influence does Company A have on the index than Company B

In a market-cap weighted index, influence is proportional to market cap. $2 trillion / $50 billion = 40. Company A has approximately 40 times more influence on the index than Company B.

A price-weighted index has three stocks priced at $100, $200, and $300. If the $300 stock drops 10% (to $270) while the others stay unchanged, by what percentage does the index decline

Original index value = ($100 + $200 + $300) / 3 = $200. New value = ($100 + $200 + $270) / 3 = $190. Decline = ($200 - $190) / $200 x 100 = 5%. The $300 stock's 10% drop caused a 5% index decline because it represented half the index weight.
