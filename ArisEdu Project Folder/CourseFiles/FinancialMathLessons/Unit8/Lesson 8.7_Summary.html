Lesson 8.7: Technology in Financial Markets - Summary

Key Concepts: Technology in Markets

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'robo-advisor'?

A robo-advisor is an automated platform that uses algorithms to build, manage, and rebalance diversified portfolios based on each user's risk profile and investment goals, typically at a fraction of the cost of human advisors.

What is 'payment for order flow' (PFOF)?

PFOF is how commission-free brokerages make money. They route customer orders to market makers who pay for the privilege of executing those trades, which raises some concerns about whether investors get the best execution prices.

What is an 'ETF' (Exchange-Traded Fund)?

An ETF is a pooled investment fund that trades on exchanges throughout the day like a stock. Most ETFs track an index, sector, or asset class and offer low-cost diversification.

What is 'algorithmic trading'?

Algorithmic trading uses computer programs to execute trades based on pre-programmed rules (price, volume, timing patterns). It accounts for the majority of stock market trading volume today.

What is a 'flash crash' in the context of market technology?

A flash crash is an extremely rapid market decline and often quick recovery, sometimes within minutes. The May 2010 flash crash saw the Dow drop ~1,000 points in minutes, largely due to algorithmic trading interactions.

What is 'social trading'?

Social trading platforms allow users to see and copy other investors' trades. While it can help beginners learn, past performance of copied traders does not guarantee future results.

A robo-advisor charges 0.25% on $50,000 while a traditional advisor charges 1% on the same amount. What is the annual fee difference, and approximately how much would that difference grow to over 30 years at 8% returns?

Robo fee: $50,000 x 0.0025 = $125/year. Advisor fee: $50,000 x 0.01 = $500/year. Difference = $375/year. Compounded at 8% for 30 years: $375 x [(1.08^30 - 1)/0.08] = ~$42,400.

An ETF tracking the S&P 500 (like VOO) has an expense ratio of 0.03%. On a $200,000 investment, what is the annual fee?

$200,000 x 0.0003 = $60 per year. For comparison, an actively managed fund at 1% on the same amount would cost $2,000/year -- over 33 times more.

A social trading platform shows Trader X earned 45% last year. An investor copies Trader X with $10,000. If Trader X loses 20% this year, what is the copier's portfolio value?

$10,000 x (1 - 0.20) = $8,000. Past performance does not guarantee future results. The investor who copied Trader X after a great year experienced a 20% loss, demonstrating the risk of chasing past performance.

An investor comparing two options: (A) a robo-advisor charging 0.25% that auto-rebalances and tax-loss harvests, or (B) managing their own portfolio with $0 commissions but no automation. If tax-loss harvesting saves ~0.50% annually in taxes, what is the estimated net cost advantage of the robo-advisor?

Tax-loss harvesting value: ~0.50% per year. Robo-advisor fee: 0.25% per year. Net benefit = 0.50% - 0.25% = 0.25% annually. Plus the robo-advisor handles rebalancing automatically, saving time and reducing behavioral errors.

Real-World Applications

Example: Major online brokerages now charge what per stock/ETF trade?

Most major brokerages (Fidelity, Schwab, Robinhood, etc.) now offer $0 commissions on stock and ETF trades, a shift that began in 2019.

Example: Algorithmic trading accounts for approximately what percentage of US stock market volume?

Algorithmic and high-frequency trading now account for 60-75% of US stock market volume, with computers executing trades far faster than any human could.

Example: In 2000, online trading commissions averaged $15 per trade. An active investor making 200 trades per year paid how much in commissions then versus today with commission-free trading?

200 trades x $15 = $3,000 in commissions per year in 2000. Today with $0 commissions, that entire cost is eliminated. Over 30 years at 8% returns, that $3,000/year savings would compound to approximately $340,000.

Example: A young investor uses a mobile app and invests $100/month in a total stock market ETF for 40 years at 10% average return. Approximately how much will they accumulate?

FV = $100 x [((1 + 0.10/12)^480 - 1) / (0.10/12)] = approximately $530,000. Total contributions of only $48,000 grew to over half a million through compound growth and easy automated investing.

Important Concepts

High-frequency trading (HFT) involves:

HFT uses powerful computers and algorithms to execute enormous volumes of trades in milliseconds or microseconds, profiting from tiny price discrepancies that exist for fractions of a second.
