Lesson 4.3: Annuities (ordinary vs. annuity due) - Summary

Key Concepts: Annuities & Pension Calculations

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an 'ordinary annuity'?

An ordinary annuity (annuity in arrears) makes equal payments at the end of each period. Most loan payments, bond coupons, and retirement withdrawals are structured as ordinary annuities.

What is a 'defined benefit pension'?

A defined benefit pension promises a specific retirement income (e.g., 2% x years of service x final average salary). The employer bears the investment risk, not the employee.

What is a 'discount rate' in present value calculations?

The discount rate reflects the time value of money and the required return. A higher discount rate reduces the present value of future payments because money today is worth more when alternative returns are higher.

What is a 'variable annuity'?

Variable annuities link payments to investment performance, offering growth potential but with market risk. They often carry fees of 2-3%+ annually, which can significantly erode returns compared to low-cost index funds.

What is 'pension underfunding'?

Underfunding occurs when the present value of a pension's promised benefits exceeds its current assets. This poses a risk that retirees may not receive their full benefits if the employer becomes insolvent.

Real-World Applications

Example: Lisa starts investing $500/month at age 25 at 7% annual return. Tom starts the same $500/month at age 35. At age 65, Lisa has approximately $566,765 while Tom has approximately $243,994. How much more does Lisa accumulate, and why?

Lisa contributed $240,000 (40 years) vs. Tom's $180,000 (30 years) — only $60,000 more. But Lisa's extra decade of compounding produced $322,771 more wealth, demonstrating that time is the most powerful factor in annuity growth.

Example: An employee earns $80,000/year and her employer offers a pension formula of 2% x years of service x final average salary. After 30 years, her annual pension benefit would be:

Pension = 0.02 x 30 years x $80,000 = $48,000/year ($4,000/month). This replaces 60% of her pre-retirement income, a strong benefit that demonstrates why defined benefit pensions are so valuable.

From 62-70, the early claimer receives $1,500 x 96 months = $144,000. Starting at 70, the higher benefit catches up around age 80-82. If you expect to live past 82, delaying produces more total lifetime income.

Example: A 45-year-old has $150,000 saved for retirement and adds $800/month at 7%. At age 65, the portfolio will be approximately:

Existing $150,000 at 7% for 20 years: $150,000 x 1.07^20 = ~$580,000. New contributions: FV annuity of $800/month at 7% for 20 years = ~$417,000. Combined: approximately $997,000. Both lump sum growth and regular contributions matter.

Example: An employee with a pension of $3,000/month at age 62 can delay to age 67 for $4,200/month. The extra 5-year wait means forgoing $180,000 in payments ($3,000 x 60 months). At $4,200/month, how many months after age 67 does it take to recoup the $180,000?

Monthly increase: $4,200 - $3,000 = $1,200 extra/month. Break-even: $180,000 / $1,200 = 150 months (12.5 years). The retiree breaks even at roughly age 79.5. Living beyond that age makes delaying the more profitable choice.
