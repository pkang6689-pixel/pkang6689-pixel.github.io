Lesson 7.2: Profit & Loss Statements - Summary

Key Concepts: Profit & Loss Analysis

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'revenue' in the context of a profit and loss statement?

Revenue is the total income generated from the sale of goods or services before any costs or expenses are subtracted. It is the starting point of the income statement.

What is 'depreciation' in accounting?

Depreciation is an accounting method that systematically spreads the cost of a tangible long-term asset (like machinery or vehicles) over its estimated useful life, reducing reported profit without an actual outflow of cash.

What is the 'bottom line' on an income statement?

Net income is called the 'bottom line' because it literally appears as the last line on the income statement, representing the final profit or loss after all revenues and expenses are accounted for.

A clothing store has $350,000 in annual revenue, COGS of $140,000, and operating expenses of $120,000. What is the store's net profit before taxes?

Gross profit = $350,000 - $140,000 = $210,000. Net profit before taxes = $210,000 - $120,000 = $90,000.

A tech company has $800,000 in revenue and a 70% gross margin. If operating expenses are $300,000, what is the net income before taxes?

Gross profit = $800,000 x 0.70 = $560,000. Net income before taxes = $560,000 - $300,000 = $260,000.

A restaurant earns $500,000 in revenue. COGS is $175,000 and operating expenses total $225,000. What is the restaurant's net profit margin?

Gross profit = $500,000 - $175,000 = $325,000. Net profit = $325,000 - $225,000 = $100,000. Net profit margin = $100,000 / $500,000 = 20%.

A SaaS company has monthly revenue of $120,000, COGS of $18,000, and operating expenses of $85,000. What is its gross margin percentage and net profit?

Gross profit = $120,000 - $18,000 = $102,000. Gross margin = $102,000 / $120,000 = 85%. Net profit = $102,000 - $85,000 = $17,000.

A coffee shop has $8,000/month fixed costs. Each drink has a selling price of $5 and a variable cost of $1.50. The shop currently sells 3,000 drinks per month. What is its monthly profit and margin of safety in units?

CM per drink = $5.00 - $1.50 = $3.50. Total CM = 3,000 x $3.50 = $10,500. Profit = $10,500 - $8,000 = $2,500. BEP = $8,000 / $3.50 ≈ 2,286 units. Margin of safety = 3,000 - 2,286 = about 714 units above break-even.

A company buys equipment for $60,000 with a 5-year useful life and no salvage value (straight-line depreciation). If annual revenue is $200,000, COGS is $90,000, and other operating expenses total $80,000, what is the annual net profit before taxes?

Annual depreciation = $60,000 / 5 = $12,000. Gross profit = $200,000 - $90,000 = $110,000. Total operating expenses = $80,000 + $12,000 = $92,000. Net profit before taxes = $110,000 - $92,000 = $18,000.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does 'operating leverage' refer to?

Operating leverage describes how a company's cost structure (specifically its mix of fixed vs. variable costs) affects profitability. High operating leverage means small revenue changes lead to large profit swings.

Example: A SaaS company has monthly revenue of $120,000, COGS of $18,000, and operating expenses of $85,000. What is its gross margin percentage and net profit?

Gross profit = $120,000 - $18,000 = $102,000. Gross margin = $102,000 / $120,000 = 85%. Net profit = $102,000 - $85,000 = $17,000.

Example: A company buys equipment for $60,000 with a 5-year useful life and no salvage value (straight-line depreciation). If annual revenue is $200,000, COGS is $90,000, and other operating expenses total $80,000, what is the annual net profit before taxes?

Annual depreciation = $60,000 / 5 = $12,000. Gross profit = $200,000 - $90,000 = $110,000. Total operating expenses = $80,000 + $12,000 = $92,000. Net profit before taxes = $110,000 - $92,000 = $18,000.

Example: A business with high operating leverage has $200,000 in fixed costs and low variable costs (10% of revenue). If revenue increases from $500,000 to $600,000, by how much does profit increase?

At $500K revenue: Variable costs = $50K, Fixed costs = $200K, Profit = $500K - $50K - $200K = $250K. At $600K revenue: Variable costs = $60K, Fixed costs = $200K, Profit = $600K - $60K - $200K = $340K. Profit increase = $340K - $250K = $90K. A 20% revenue increase caused a 36% profit increase, demonstrating high operating leverage.
