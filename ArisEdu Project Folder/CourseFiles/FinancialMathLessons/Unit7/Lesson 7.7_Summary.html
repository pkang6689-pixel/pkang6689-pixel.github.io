Lesson 7.7: Business Risk & Reward - Summary

Key Concepts: Business Risk Management

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

A business with $500,000 in annual revenue depends on one client for 60% of sales. If that client leaves, remaining revenue is $200,000 against $350,000 in fixed costs. What is the annual shortfall?

If the main client leaves, remaining revenue = $200,000. With $350,000 in fixed costs, the shortfall = $350,000 - $200,000 = $150,000. This illustrates the severe danger of concentration risk.

A company has $300,000 in total debt, $150,000 in equity, and generates $80,000 in annual net income. What is the D/E ratio, and can the company comfortably service its debt assuming 8% interest?

D/E = $300,000 / $150,000 = 2.0. Annual interest = $300,000 x 8% = $24,000. Interest coverage ratio = $80,000 / $24,000 = 3.3x, meaning income covers interest payments comfortably.

A restaurant has monthly operating expenses of $25,000. The recommended cash reserve is 3-6 months. What is the minimum recommended reserve?

Minimum recommended reserve = 3 months x $25,000 = $75,000. This provides a buffer to weather temporary revenue disruptions like equipment failures, slow seasons, or unexpected closures.

A manufacturing company relies on one overseas supplier. Shipping delays cause a 2-month production stoppage. Monthly revenue is $100,000 and fixed costs continue at $40,000/month. What is the total financial impact?

Lost revenue = $100,000 x 2 = $200,000. Fixed costs that continue = $40,000 x 2 = $80,000. Total impact = $200,000 lost revenue + $80,000 in costs still owed = $280,000. Having backup suppliers would mitigate this risk.

A business pays $2,400/year for business interruption insurance. A fire forces closure for 3 months, during which the insurance pays $15,000/month for lost income. What is the net benefit of the insurance?

Insurance payout = $15,000 x 3 = $45,000. Annual premium = $2,400. Net benefit = $45,000 - $2,400 = $42,600. The $2,400 annual premium was an excellent investment against the $45,000 loss.

A tech company suffers a data breach. Cyber insurance covers $200,000 in damages. Without insurance, the company would pay $200,000 in notification costs, legal fees, and fines, plus an estimated $100,000 in lost customer trust. If the annual cyber insurance premium is $8,000, what is the ROI on the insurance?

Covered damages = $200,000. Premium = $8,000. ROI = ($200,000 - $8,000) / $8,000 x 100% = $192,000 / $8,000 = 2,400%. Note that the $100K in lost customer trust is not covered by insurance but would still be incurred.

A business runs scenario analysis with three outcomes: Best case (profit $120,000, probability 20%), Most likely (profit $60,000, probability 60%), Worst case (loss $30,000, probability 20%). What is the expected value?

Expected value = ($120,000 x 0.20) + ($60,000 x 0.60) + (-$30,000 x 0.20) = $24,000 + $36,000 - $6,000 = $54,000.

A company with $150,000 in annual profit has two key employees. Key person insurance for each costs $3,000/year and pays $250,000 if either dies or becomes disabled. If one key person leaves and it takes $150,000 to replace them and recover lost business, what is the net insurance benefit?

Insurance payout = $250,000. Cost to replace and recover = $150,000. Net insurance benefit = $250,000 - $150,000 = $100,000 remaining to help the business through the transition (minus the $3,000 annual premium).

Example: A construction company has a D/E ratio of 3.0 and annual interest expense of $45,000. If net income drops from $90,000 to $50,000, how does the interest coverage ratio change, and is this concerning?

Old interest coverage = $90,000 / $45,000 = 2.0x. New coverage = $50,000 / $45,000 = 1.1x. A coverage ratio below 1.5x is considered dangerous because there is almost no margin for further income decline before the business cannot cover its interest payments.
