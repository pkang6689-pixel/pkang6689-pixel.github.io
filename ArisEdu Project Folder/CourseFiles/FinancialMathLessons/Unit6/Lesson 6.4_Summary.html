Lesson 6.4: Insurance Types (health, auto, life) - Summary

Key Concepts: Types of Insurance

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an 'out-of-pocket maximum' in health insurance?

The out-of-pocket maximum is the cap on what you pay for covered services in a plan year, including deductibles, copays, and coinsurance. Once reached, the insurer covers 100%.

What is 'whole life insurance'?

Whole life insurance provides lifelong coverage with level premiums and includes a cash value component that grows tax-deferred. It costs 5-15 times more than a comparable term policy.

What is an 'umbrella insurance policy'?

An umbrella policy provides additional liability coverage beyond what your existing auto and homeowner's policies offer. It protects against large lawsuits or claims that exceed standard policy limits.

What is 'risk pooling' in insurance?

Risk pooling is the fundamental concept behind insurance. Large groups of people contribute premiums, creating a fund that pays out claims for the relatively small number who experience covered losses.

What is 'renter's insurance'?

Renter's insurance protects tenants' personal property, provides personal liability coverage, and often includes loss-of-use benefits. The landlord's policy covers the building structure, not tenant belongings.

Mike earns $85,000 per year and wants life insurance coverage equal to 10 times his salary. He can get a 20-year term policy for $0.50 per $1,000 of coverage per month. What is his monthly premium?

Coverage needed = $85,000 x 10 = $850,000. Number of $1,000 units = 850. Monthly premium = 850 x $0.50 = $425 per month.

Lisa's apartment is burglarized and she loses $8,000 worth of electronics and jewelry. Her renter's insurance has a $500 deductible. She pays $20/month for the policy. How much net benefit does she receive, and what is her effective cost for one year of coverage?

Insurance pays $8,000 minus $500 deductible = $7,500. Annual premium = $20 x 12 = $240. The $240 annual cost protected her against $7,500 in losses — an excellent return on the insurance investment.

Real-World Applications

Example: The recommended amount of life insurance coverage is typically:

Financial advisors generally recommend 10-12 times annual income to replace the earner's income stream and cover obligations like a mortgage, children's education, and living expenses.

Example: Long-term disability insurance typically replaces what percentage of income?

Long-term disability policies typically replace 60-70% of pre-disability income. The benefit is less than 100% to provide an incentive to return to work when possible.

Example: What does 'comprehensive coverage' mean in auto insurance?

Comprehensive auto coverage pays for damage to your vehicle from causes other than collision, including theft, vandalism, hail, fire, flooding, and animal strikes.

Example: What does 'collision coverage' mean in auto insurance?

Collision coverage pays for repairs to your own vehicle after a collision with another vehicle or object, minus your deductible, regardless of fault.

Example: A homeowner with a $500,000 house and $300,000 auto policy causes an accident resulting in a $750,000 lawsuit. Without umbrella coverage, they face $450,000 in personal liability. A $1 million umbrella policy costs $300/year. Should they have purchased it?

The auto policy covers $300,000, leaving $450,000 unprotected. A $1 million umbrella at $300/year would have covered the entire gap. For homeowners with significant assets, umbrella coverage is critical protection.
