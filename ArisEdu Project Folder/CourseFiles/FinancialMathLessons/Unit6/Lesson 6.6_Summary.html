Lesson 6.6: Case Studies in Tax Planning - Summary

Key Concepts: Tax Planning Case Studies

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'tax-loss harvesting'?

Tax-loss harvesting involves selling investments that have declined in value to realize capital losses. These losses offset capital gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income annually.

What is a 'Roth conversion'?

A Roth conversion moves pre-tax retirement funds into a Roth IRA. You pay income tax on the amount converted, but all future growth and qualified withdrawals become permanently tax-free.

What is a 'donor-advised fund' (DAF)?

A DAF allows you to make a large tax-deductible contribution in a high-income year (useful for bunching), then distribute the funds to charities at your own pace over future years.

What is the 'wash-sale rule'?

The wash-sale rule prevents taxpayers from selling a security at a loss and immediately repurchasing it just to claim the tax deduction. The 30-day window applies both before and after the sale date.

What is the 'tax-equivalent yield' of a municipal bond?

Tax-equivalent yield = muni yield / (1 - marginal tax rate). A 3.5% muni bond for someone in the 32% bracket has a tax-equivalent yield of 3.5% / 0.68 = 5.15%, meaning they would need a 5.15% taxable bond to match.

Alex earns $85,000 and expects to be in the 12% bracket during retirement. He contributes $6,500 to a Traditional IRA at his current 22% marginal rate. What is his net lifetime tax savings per contribution compared to a Roth IRA?

Traditional IRA: save $6,500 x 0.22 = $1,430 now, pay $6,500 x 0.12 = $780 in retirement. Net savings = $1,430 - $780 = $650. When your current rate is higher than your expected retirement rate, Traditional wins.

$100,000 invested at 7% for 20 years: one account is tax-deferred, the other is taxable and loses approximately 1% annually to taxes (6% effective growth). What is the approximate difference?

Tax-deferred at 7% for 20 years: $100,000 x 1.07^20 = ~$387,000. Taxable at 6% effective: $100,000 x 1.06^20 = ~$321,000. Difference = ~$66,000. Tax deferral provides more efficient compounding.

Fiona contributes $3,850 to her HSA, invests it, and it grows to $8,000 over 10 years. She withdraws $8,000 for qualified medical expenses. If she is in the 24% bracket, what is the total tax benefit compared to using a regular taxable account?

Contribution deduction saves $3,850 x 0.24 = $924. Growth of $4,150 would have been taxed at ~24% = $996 in a taxable account. Total benefit = $924 + $996 = $1,920. The triple tax advantage saves on contributions, growth, and withdrawals.

Real-World Applications

Example: An HSA (Health Savings Account) is called 'triple tax-advantaged' because:

HSAs provide three tax benefits: (1) pre-tax or tax-deductible contributions, (2) tax-free investment growth, and (3) tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. No other account offers all three.

Example: Beth has a $10,000 stock gain and a $4,000 loss in her taxable brokerage account. She is in the 15% long-term capital gains bracket. How much does tax-loss harvesting save her?

Without harvesting: tax = $10,000 x 0.15 = $1,500. With harvesting: net gain = $10,000 - $4,000 = $6,000; tax = $6,000 x 0.15 = $900. Savings = $1,500 - $900 = $600.
