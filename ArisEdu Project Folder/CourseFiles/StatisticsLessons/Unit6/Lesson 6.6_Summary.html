Lesson 6.6: Case Studies in Political Polling - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Political Polling

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'push poll'?

Push polls are not real surveys; they are campaign tools designed to plant negative information about an opponent under the guise of asking survey questions.

What is a 'tracking poll'?

Tracking polls survey at regular intervals (often daily or weekly) to follow trends and detect shifts in public opinion over time.

What is 'selection bias' in polling?

Selection bias occurs when the sampling method systematically includes or excludes certain types of people, making the sample unrepresentative of the population.

What is a 'likely voter screen'?

A likely voter screen uses questions about past voting behavior, interest in the election, and intention to vote to filter respondents and focus on those most likely to actually cast ballots.

An online news website posts a poll asking readers 'Do you support the new highway project?' Of 50,000 responses, 78% say no. The site reports that 'the vast majority of citizens oppose the project.' What is the primary problem with this conclusion?

Voluntary response polls attract people with strong opinions, typically opponents of a proposal. The 50,000 respondents self-selected, making the results unrepresentative of the general population.

In the weeks before an election, five different polls show Candidate X with support of 42%, 45%, 44%, 43%, and 46%. A poll aggregator averages these to get 44%. What is the primary advantage of this aggregation approach?

Aggregating multiple polls averages out the random sampling error in each individual poll, producing a more stable estimate. However, if all polls share a common bias, aggregation will not eliminate it.

During a presidential election, a tracking poll surveys 500 new voters each night and reports a 3-day rolling average. On Monday through Wednesday, the results are 48%, 50%, and 49%, giving a rolling average of 49%. On Thursday, the new result is 53%. What is the new 3-day rolling average (Tuesday-Thursday)?

The 3-day rolling average for Tuesday through Thursday is (50 + 49 + 53) / 3 = 152 / 3 = 50.67%. Rolling averages smooth out day-to-day variability while still capturing trends.
