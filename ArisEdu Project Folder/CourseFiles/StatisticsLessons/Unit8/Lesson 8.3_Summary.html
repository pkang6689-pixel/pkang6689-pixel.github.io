Lesson 8.3: Residuals & Line Fit - Summary

Key Concepts: Residuals & Line Fit

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a residual plot?

A residual plot shows residuals vs. x (or ŷ) to help assess whether the linear model is appropriate.

What is an outlier in the context of regression?

In regression, an outlier is a point whose observed y deviates substantially from what the model predicts.

What is an influential point?

An influential point has a disproportionate effect on the regression; removing it noticeably alters the fitted line.

What is the residual standard deviation (s) used for?

s quantifies the typical prediction error: smaller s means predictions are more precise.

A financial analyst plots residuals from a regression of monthly revenue (y) on advertising spending (x). The residuals show a clear fan shape — small spread at low spending and large spread at high spending. What is the issue?

A fan shape indicates non-constant variance (heteroscedasticity), meaning the model's accuracy degrades at higher x values.

Real-World Applications

Example: High leverage means a point has:

Leverage refers to how far the x-value is from the mean of x.

Example: What does 'leverage' mean in regression?

A high-leverage point is far from x̄ and has the potential to pull the regression line toward itself.

Example: A teacher fits a regression of test score (y) on study hours (x) and gets s = 4.2 points. What does this tell her about the model's predictions?

The residual standard deviation s = 4.2 means that, on average, the model's predictions miss actual test scores by about 4.2 points.

Example: A researcher studying the effect of age (x) on marathon finish time (y, minutes) notices one data point for a 75-year-old with x far from the mean age of 35. The point lies close to the regression line. This point has:

Leverage depends on the x-value's distance from x̄ (high in this case), while the residual is small because the point follows the overall trend.

Example: A medical researcher regresses patient recovery time (y, days) on drug dosage (x, mg) and finds r² = 0.88. However, the residual plot shows a strong curved pattern. Should the researcher trust this model?

Even a high r² does not validate a model if the residual plot reveals systematic patterns; the relationship may be better modeled with a curve.

Example: A sports analyst models game attendance (y) versus team win percentage (x) and notices residuals are larger for teams in bigger cities. After applying a log transformation to y, the residual plot shows constant spread. What happened?

Transformations like log(y) can compress larger values proportionally, evening out the spread of residuals across all x values.
