Lesson 8.6: Applications in Prediction Models - Summary

Key Concepts: Applications in Prediction Models

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a prediction interval?

Prediction intervals are wider than confidence intervals because they must cover not just where the average falls but where an individual observation might land.

What is feature engineering?

Feature engineering creates derived variables that may capture patterns the original variables alone do not.

What is the difference between a confidence interval for the mean response and a prediction interval?

The prediction interval is wider because it adds individual variation on top of the uncertainty about the mean.

What is the training-testing split in predictive modeling?

Holding out a test set prevents overfitting and gives a realistic measure of how the model performs on new data.

What is the difference between machine learning regression and classical regression?

Both share mathematical foundations, but ML focuses on prediction while classical statistics also prioritizes understanding relationships.

What is 'customer churn prediction'?

Churn prediction helps businesses retain customers by identifying at-risk individuals before they leave.

A health clinic builds a model predicting blood pressure from BMI, age, and smoking status. An engineer suggests also adding shoe size and favorite color as predictors. What is likely to happen?

Adding irrelevant predictors inflates R² through noise but hurts adjusted R² and test-set performance.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does the Framingham Heart Study risk score illustrate about prediction models?

The Framingham score is a classic example of multiple regression applied to medicine, combining several risk factors into a single prediction.

Example: A hospital builds a regression model to predict patient readmission within 30 days using age, diagnosis severity, and length of stay. The model has r² = 0.42 on the training data and r² = 0.38 on the test data. What does the small difference in r² values suggest?

A small drop from training to testing r² indicates the model captures real patterns rather than noise, even though moderate r² is common in healthcare.

Example: A weather service uses a regression model with 12 variables (temperature, humidity, wind speed, etc.) to predict rainfall. After 5-fold cross-validation, the average prediction error is 3.2 mm. If the model were only tested on its training data, the error would be 1.8 mm. Why is the cross-validation error higher?

Training error is optimistically low because the model has already seen those data; cross-validation provides a realistic estimate of out-of-sample performance.

Example: A sports analytics team predicts player performance using age, games played, and training hours. For a 25-year-old with 80 games and 500 training hours, the predicted score is 78 with a 95% prediction interval of (62, 94) and a 95% confidence interval for the mean of (74, 82). A scout asks 'Will this specific player score at least 70?' Which interval should be used?

For predicting an individual outcome, the prediction interval is appropriate because it captures individual variation beyond just the mean.

Example: A retail company builds a sales forecast model in 2020. By 2023, online shopping has grown significantly and consumer behavior has shifted. The model's predictions are increasingly inaccurate. What should the company do?

When the underlying process changes (structural change), models must be refitted with recent data to remain accurate.

Example: An insurance company uses regression to predict annual claim costs from driver age, vehicle type, and driving record. They test three models: Model A (2 variables, r² = 0.45 on test), Model B (5 variables, r² = 0.52 on test), Model C (20 variables, r² = 0.48 on test). Which model is likely best?

Model C's lower test r² compared to B suggests overfitting from too many variables. Model B balances complexity and predictive power.

Example: A marketing team performs 10-fold cross-validation on a customer spending model. The model has r² = 0.65 on training and r² = 0.62 on average across the 10 test folds. The manager asks, 'Can we use this model for next quarter's budget planning?'

Stable cross-validation results indicate reliable generalization; the model can inform planning while acknowledging the inherent uncertainty.

Example: A health clinic builds a model predicting blood pressure from BMI, age, and smoking status. An engineer suggests also adding shoe size and favorite color as predictors. What is likely to happen?

Adding irrelevant predictors inflates R² through noise but hurts adjusted R² and test-set performance.

Example: A university admissions office uses a regression model to predict first-year GPA from SAT score, high school GPA, and extracurricular hours. They notice that after a new feature — parent income — is added, prediction improves significantly. However, using income raises ethical concerns about fairness. What should they consider?

Responsible prediction modeling considers both accuracy and fairness; including sensitive variables may perpetuate systemic inequalities.
