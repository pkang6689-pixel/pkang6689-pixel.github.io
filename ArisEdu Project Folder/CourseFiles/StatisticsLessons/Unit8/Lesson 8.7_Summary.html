Lesson 8.7: Case Studies in Economics & Finance - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Economics & Finance

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the risk-free rate in CAPM?

The risk-free rate represents the baseline return an investor can earn without taking on market risk.

What is the Phillips Curve?

The Phillips Curve describes the tradeoff: lower unemployment tends to coincide with higher inflation.

What is 'tail risk' in financial models?

Financial returns often have 'fat tails' — extreme losses occur more frequently than a normal distribution would predict.

What is a hedonic pricing model?

Hedonic models decompose a product's price into the implicit prices of its characteristics.

What is Value at Risk (VaR)?

VaR answers: 'What is the worst loss I can expect with 95% (or 99%) confidence over this time horizon?'

A financial analyst regresses a tech stock's monthly returns against the S&P 500 returns and finds β = 1.5. If the S&P 500 drops 8% next month, what is the predicted change in the stock's return?

β = 1.5 means the stock moves 1.5 times the market. A −8% market move predicts 1.5 × (−8%) = −12% for the stock.

A real estate appraiser uses a hedonic model: Price ($) = 80,000 + 150(sq ft) + 20,000(bedrooms) + 35,000(pool) − 5,000(age in years). What is the predicted price of a 1,500 sq ft, 3-bedroom, 10-year-old house with a pool?

Price = 80,000 + 150(1,500) + 20,000(3) + 35,000(1) − 5,000(10) = 80,000 + 225,000 + 60,000 + 35,000 − 50,000 = $350,000.

Real-World Applications

Example: A consumer finance company uses regression to predict whether a loan applicant will default. The model includes income, credit score, and debt-to-income ratio. The residuals show strong autocorrelation when sorted by application date. What does this indicate?

Autocorrelation in residuals by date suggests the model misses a time-varying factor, such as economic cycles.

Example: A government economist uses regression to study the effect of education spending on state GDP growth. However, wealthier states both spend more on education and have faster GDP growth for other reasons. This problem is an example of:

The bidirectional relationship between wealth and education spending creates endogeneity, biasing the coefficient of education spending.
