Lesson 8.1: Scatterplots & Correlation Coefficient - Summary

Key Concepts: Scatterplots & Correlation Coefficient

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the correlation coefficient?

The correlation coefficient r quantifies how tightly data cluster around a straight line and in which direction.

What is a scatterplot?

A scatterplot uses the coordinate plane to display paired quantitative observations.

What is meant by the 'direction' of a scatterplot?

Direction describes whether the association between the two variables is positive (upward trend) or negative (downward trend).

What is meant by the 'strength' of a correlation?

Strength is determined by the absolute value of r; values closer to 1 indicate a tighter linear pattern.

What is the explanatory variable in a scatterplot?

By convention the explanatory (independent) variable is placed on the x-axis.

Temperature is a confounding variable that increases both ice cream sales and swimming activity, illustrating that correlation does not imply causation.

An outlier that deviates from the overall linear trend pulls r toward 0; removing it typically strengthens the measured linear relationship.

A pharmaceutical company tests a new drug and reports r = 0.45 between dosage and symptom improvement. A newspaper headline claims 'Drug causes improvement.' What is wrong with this claim?

Without a properly controlled experiment, confounders such as patient health or placebo effects could produce the observed correlation.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Outliers can pull r toward 0 or inflate it, depending on their position.
