Lesson 7.6: Interpreting Statistical Results - Summary

Key Concepts: Interpreting Statistical Results

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'publication bias'?

Publication bias creates a distorted literature where positive findings are overrepresented, because null results are less likely to be submitted or accepted for publication.

What is 'Simpson's Paradox'?

Simpson's Paradox occurs when a trend seen in subgroups reverses when data is aggregated, usually due to a lurking variable that changes the group compositions.

What is 'cherry-picking' in the context of interpreting results?

Cherry-picking is the misleading practice of highlighting favorable evidence while omitting unfavorable evidence, creating a distorted picture of the research findings.

A news article reports: 'Eating chocolate is linked to lower heart disease risk, according to a study of 10,000 adults.' The study is observational. A reader concludes that chocolate prevents heart disease. What is the flaw in this reasoning?

Observational studies can identify associations but cannot establish causation. Wealthier people may eat more high-quality chocolate and also have better healthcare, creating a confounded association.

A researcher studies whether caffeine improves reaction time. She finds r = 0.95 between coffee consumption and reaction speed in a sample of office workers. She concludes caffeine causes faster reactions. What is the primary flaw?

Even a very strong correlation (r = 0.95) in an observational study does not establish causation. Confounding variables may explain the relationship between coffee consumption and reaction time.

A national study finds that students who take music lessons score 20 points higher on the SAT (p < 0.001). A school superintendent decides to require music lessons for all students to raise SAT scores. What is the main problem with this decision?

This is an observational study, so families who can afford music lessons may also provide other educational advantages. Requiring music lessons for all students may not reproduce the 20-point benefit.

Real-World Applications

Example: A study finds that a tutoring program raises math scores by 2 points on a 100-point scale (p = 0.04, n = 5,000). The school board must decide whether to spend $500,000 implementing the program district-wide. What should they consider?

While the effect is real (p = 0.04), a 2-point improvement on a 100-point scale is very small. The board should weigh whether this modest gain justifies the substantial cost.
