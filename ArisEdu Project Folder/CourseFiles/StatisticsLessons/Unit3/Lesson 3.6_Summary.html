Lesson 3.6: Interpreting Data in Context - Summary

Key Concepts: Interpreting Data in Context

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is 'ecological fallacy'?

The ecological fallacy assumes that relationships observed for groups also hold for individuals, which is often false.

A hospital compares surgery success rates. Hospital A: 900/1000 (90%). Hospital B: 800/1000 (80%). Hospital A claims superiority. However, Hospital A mostly handles low-risk cases while Hospital B handles high-risk patients. What is this an example of?

Simpson's Paradox can make an aggregate comparison misleading. Stratifying by patient risk level (context) reveals the true picture.

A study finds that ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. A journalist writes 'Ice cream causes drowning.' What is wrong with this interpretation?

This is a classic example of confounding. Hot summer weather is the lurking variable that drives both ice cream consumption and swimming (hence drowning risk).

A poll reports that 62% of voters support a new policy, with a margin of error of ±4%. What is the correct interpretation?

The margin of error creates a confidence interval (58% to 66%). The true population proportion likely falls within this range.

A state education department reports that 'graduation rates improved from 80% to 85%.' However, the definition of 'graduation' was changed during this period to include GED completers. What is the key interpretive issue?

When the definition of what is being measured changes, before-and-after comparisons become meaningless without adjusting for the definitional change.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Context determines how to handle outliers. Some are data entry errors to be corrected; others are genuine extreme values that provide important information.

Real-World Applications

Example: A study reports that the mean commute time for workers in a city is 35 minutes. Without additional context, we cannot determine:

A mean alone tells you the average but not the variability, sample size, comparison benchmarks, or whether the result is noteworthy.

Example: If a report says 'Sales increased by 50%,' what important context is missing?

A percentage change is meaningless without knowing the base amount. A 50% increase from a tiny base is very different from a 50% increase from a large base.

Example: A city reports that the average household income rose from $55,000 to $62,000 over five years. A politician claims residents are 'much better off.' What contextual information could challenge this claim?

If inflation was 15% over those five years, the real increase is negative. If only a few wealthy newcomers raised the mean while the median stayed flat, typical residents are not better off.

This is a classic example of confounding. Hot summer weather is the lurking variable that drives both ice cream consumption and swimming (hence drowning risk).

Example: A school district compares average test scores between a large urban school (n=2000, mean=72) and a small rural school (n=50, mean=78). An administrator concludes the rural school is definitively better. Why might this conclusion be premature?

With n=50, the rural school's mean has a wider margin of error. Also, differences in student demographics, funding, and resources are confounding factors.

Example: A weight-loss supplement claims 'average weight loss of 15 pounds in 8 weeks.' The study had 10 participants, 3 dropped out, and the remaining 7 had losses ranging from 2 to 40 pounds. What contextual issues exist?

The claim is misleading due to the tiny sample, high dropout rate, no control group, wide variability, and an outlier inflating the mean.
