Lesson 9.1: Law of Large Numbers - Summary

Key Concepts: Law of Large Numbers

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the Gambler's Fallacy?

The Gambler's Fallacy is the mistaken belief that if an event has occurred more frequently in the past, it is less likely in the future (or vice versa) for independent events.

What is the difference between the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem?

LLN says the sample mean converges to the population mean. CLT says the distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal for large n. They are related but distinct.

What is meant by 'independent and identically distributed' (i.i.d.) in the context of LLN?

I.i.d. means each observation comes from the same distribution (identically distributed) and no observation influences another (independent). This is a key assumption of LLN.

What is the 'law of averages' misconception?

The 'law of averages' is a common misinterpretation that assumes short runs must balance out. LLN only applies as n approaches infinity, not over short sequences.

What is 'sampling variability'?

Sampling variability is the natural fluctuation in statistics from sample to sample. LLN tells us this variability decreases as sample size increases.

Real-World Applications

Example: If you roll a fair six-sided die many times, the average roll approaches:

The expected value of a fair die is (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 3.5, and LLN guarantees the average converges to this value.

Example: A factory produces light bulbs with an average lifetime of 1,200 hours. A quality inspector tests 5 bulbs and finds an average lifetime of 1,050 hours. She then tests 5,000 bulbs and finds an average of 1,195 hours. Which average is more likely to be close to the true mean, and why?

By the Law of Large Numbers, the average from 5,000 bulbs (1,195 hours) is far more likely to be close to the true mean of 1,200 hours than the average from only 5 bulbs.

Example: An insurance company insures 200,000 homeowners. Historical data shows that the average annual claim per policy is $800 with a standard deviation of $5,000. Why can the company reliably set premiums based on this average?

Even though individual claims are highly variable (SD = $5,000), with 200,000 policies the Law of Large Numbers ensures the average claim will be very close to $800, allowing reliable premium pricing.

Example: A basketball player has a career free-throw percentage of 75%. In tonight's game, she made 2 out of 6 free throws (33%). A commentator says her career average is 'broken.' What does LLN tell us?

LLN applies to long-run averages, not individual games. A 6-shot sample is far too small to draw conclusions; over thousands of attempts, her percentage will converge near 75%.

Example: A taxi company tracks daily revenue. Over 7 days, average daily revenue is $4,200. Over the entire year (365 days), average daily revenue is $3,850. The company's historical average is $3,800 per day. Why is the yearly average more useful for planning?

By LLN, the 365-day average ($3,850) is far more reliable as an estimate of the true daily revenue ($3,800) than the volatile 7-day average ($4,200).

Example: A die manufacturer wants to verify that a new die is fair. They roll it 60 times and observe the following frequencies: 1 (8 times), 2 (12 times), 3 (11 times), 4 (9 times), 5 (10 times), 6 (10 times). The average roll is 3.45. Does this suggest the die is unfair?

An average of 3.45 from 60 rolls is very close to the expected 3.5. Small deviations are expected with finite samples. LLN tells us the average would converge even closer to 3.5 with more rolls if the die is truly fair.

Example: A streaming service measures average daily watch time. In a sample of 20 users, the average is 2.8 hours. In a sample of 200,000 users, the average is 1.6 hours. The true population average is 1.55 hours. What can we conclude?

The 200,000-user average (1.6) is only 0.05 hours from the true mean (1.55), while the 20-user average (2.8) is 1.25 hours away. LLN explains why larger samples give far more accurate estimates.

Example: A pharmaceutical company runs a clinical trial to estimate the average reduction in cholesterol from a new drug. In a pilot study of 12 patients, the average reduction is 28 mg/dL. In the full trial of 10,000 patients, the average reduction is 18 mg/dL. Which estimate should the company use to design its marketing claims, and why?

The 10,000-patient trial provides a far more reliable estimate by LLN. The pilot study's larger estimate (28 mg/dL) likely reflects sampling variability due to its small size, not a true larger effect.
