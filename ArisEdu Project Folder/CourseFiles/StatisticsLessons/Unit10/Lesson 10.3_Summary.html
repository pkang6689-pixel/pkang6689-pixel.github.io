Lesson 10.3: Interpreting Graphical Data in AP Exams - Summary

Key Concepts: Interpreting Graphical Data in AP Exams

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a stemplot (stem-and-leaf plot) best used for?

Stemplots show individual data values while also displaying the overall distributional shape, making them ideal for small to moderate datasets.

What is the purpose of parallel (side-by-side) boxplots?

Parallel boxplots allow quick visual comparison of center, spread, shape, and outliers across multiple groups.

What is the key difference between a histogram and a bar chart?

Histograms use adjacent bars for continuous numerical intervals, while bar charts use separated bars for distinct categories.

What is the mean-median relationship in a symmetric distribution?

In a symmetric distribution, the mean and median are approximately equal because there is no skewing tail to pull the mean away.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Real-World Applications

Example: When describing a histogram on the AP exam, you should mention:

A complete histogram description covers all four characteristics (SOCS: Shape, Outliers, Center, Spread).

Example: A histogram of household incomes in a small town shows a long tail to the right with a peak near $45,000. A student describes the distribution on the AP exam. Which description would earn full credit?

A full-credit description mentions shape (right-skewed), center (near $45,000), spread (range), and unusual features (high-income outliers), all in context.

Example: Two parallel boxplots compare test scores for Class A and Class B. Class A has median 78, IQR 15, and no outliers. Class B has median 82, IQR 28, and two high outliers. Which comparison is most complete?

A complete comparison addresses center (medians), spread (IQRs), and unusual features (outliers) for both groups.

Example: A scatterplot of hours of exercise per week vs. resting heart rate shows a moderately strong, negative, linear relationship with one point at (12, 85) far from the trend. On the AP exam, how should a student describe this scatterplot?

A complete scatterplot description covers direction (negative), form (linear), strength (moderately strong), unusual points (the outlier), and uses context (exercise, heart rate).

Example: A residual plot from a regression of temperature vs. ice cream sales shows random scatter with no pattern. A student is asked to assess the linear model. What should she conclude?

Random scatter in a residual plot (no curves, no fan shapes) supports the appropriateness of the linear model.

Example: A Q-Q plot for a dataset of exam scores shows the points curving upward at the right end. What does this pattern suggest about the distribution?

Points curving above the reference line at the right end of a Q-Q plot indicate that the upper tail is heavier than expected under normality, consistent with right skew.

Example: A teacher creates a back-to-back stemplot of quiz scores for Period 1 and Period 2. Period 1 has stems ranging from 5 to 10 and Period 2 has stems from 6 to 10. What can be concluded from the stemplots?

Back-to-back stemplots let you compare ranges, centers, and shapes of two distributions simultaneously while preserving individual values.

Example: A time series plot of monthly sales shows an increasing trend with a regular up-and-down pattern each year. How should a student describe this graph on the AP exam?

A complete time series description identifies the overall trend (increasing), any seasonal/cyclical patterns, and mentions the context (monthly sales).

Example: A cumulative relative frequency plot (ogive) for test scores shows that the 50th percentile falls at approximately 74 points. What does this tell us?

The 50th percentile on a cumulative relative frequency plot corresponds to the median, the value at which half the data falls below.

Example: A student is given parallel boxplots of daily commute times for three cities. City A has the smallest IQR but a high outlier. City B has the largest IQR. City C has a symmetric box with no outliers. Which city shows the most consistency in commute times?

Consistency is best measured by spread, and the IQR is a resistant measure. City A's smallest IQR means the bulk of commute times are most tightly grouped, even though one outlier exists.
