Lesson 10.5: Capstone Project: Statistical Analysis of a Dataset - Summary

Key Concepts: Capstone Project: Statistical Analysis of a Dataset

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a confounding variable?

Confounders create spurious associations or mask true effects because they are related to both the explanatory and response variables.

What is the purpose of proper sampling in a capstone project?

Representative sampling is the foundation of valid statistical inference, allowing conclusions to extend beyond the sample.

What is the role of a confidence interval in a capstone project?

CIs quantify the precision of the estimate, showing a plausible range for the true parameter value.

What is the purpose of reflection at the end of a capstone project?

Reflection deepens learning by encouraging you to consider what worked, what was challenging, and how you would improve.

In her capstone, a student surveys 120 people about their preferred exercise type (running, swimming, cycling) and age group (under 30, 30-50, over 50). She wants to know if exercise preference is associated with age group. Which test is appropriate, and what is the key condition?

Two categorical variables in a two-way table call for a chi-square test of independence. The key condition is that all expected cell counts are at least 5.

A student collects data on sleep hours and GPA for 80 students. After finding a significant positive correlation (r = 0.45, p = 0.002), she writes: 'Getting more sleep causes higher GPAs.' What is wrong with this conclusion?

Observational studies cannot establish causation because confounding variables may be responsible for the observed association.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Transparent handling of outliers includes investigating the cause, reporting results with and without the value, and discussing the impact in the limitations section.

Real-World Applications

Example: A capstone project examines whether there is a difference in average test scores between students who used a new online platform and those who did not. The student has n₁ = 45 (platform users, mean = 82, s = 10) and n₂ = 50 (non-users, mean = 77, s = 12). Which test should she use and what are the hypotheses?

Two independent groups with quantitative data require a two-sample t-test. The hypotheses compare the population means of the two groups.

Example: A student's capstone regression of daily temperature (x, in degrees F) vs. ice cream sales (y, in dollars) yields ŷ = −200 + 8.5x with r² = 0.71. The residual plot shows random scatter. The student is asked to interpret r² in the discussion section. Which interpretation is correct?

r² = 0.71 means 71% of the variability in ice cream sales is accounted for by the linear relationship with temperature. It does not imply causation.

Example: A capstone project investigates whether a new tutoring program improves math scores. The student uses a matched pairs design: 25 students take a pre-test, attend tutoring, then take a post-test. The mean difference (post − pre) is 6.2 points with s_d = 8.5. She computes t = 6.2/(8.5/√25) = 3.65 with df = 24 and p < 0.001. What should she conclude?

With t = 3.65 and p < 0.001, there is strong evidence of a significant improvement. The conclusion should be in context and note that without a control group, other factors could contribute.

Example: A student's capstone computes a 95% confidence interval of (2.1, 8.3) for the mean difference in test scores between two teaching methods. Her friend says this means there is a 95% probability the true difference is in this interval. How should the student correct this interpretation?

The true parameter is fixed. The 95% refers to the long-run success rate of the procedure: if we repeated the study many times, about 95% of the resulting intervals would capture the true difference.

Example: A student completes a capstone analyzing factors affecting home prices. She used multiple regression, checked conditions, reported r² = 0.68, found significant predictors, and discussed limitations. For the reflection, she notes her biggest takeaway. Which reflection best demonstrates comprehensive statistical thinking?

A strong reflection addresses the challenges of real data analysis, the importance of conditions and limitations, and the recognition that models are imperfect representations of reality.

During EDA for her capstone, a student creates a histogram of household income data and finds it is strongly right-skewed. She needs to report the center and spread. Which measures should she use

For skewed distributions, the median and IQR are preferred because they are resistant to the influence of extreme values in the tail.
