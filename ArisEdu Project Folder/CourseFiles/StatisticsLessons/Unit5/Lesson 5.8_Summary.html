Lesson 5.8: Case Studies in Real-World Distributions - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Real-World Distributions

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the 'memoryless property' of the exponential distribution?

The memoryless property means P(X > s + t | X > s) = P(X > t). Past waiting time does not affect future waiting probability.

What is a 'goodness-of-fit test'?

Goodness-of-fit tests compare observed frequencies with expected frequencies under a proposed distribution model.

What is a 'Monte Carlo simulation'?

Monte Carlo methods repeatedly generate random samples from distributions to estimate probabilities, averages, and other quantities.

What is the exponential distribution?

The exponential distribution is continuous and right-skewed, commonly used to model the time between successive events.

What is a 'mixture distribution' in real-world data?

Mixture distributions arise when data comes from multiple groups, such as bimodal height distributions from combining male and female populations.

A streaming service experiences server outages at a rate of 0.5 per week. What is the probability of going 2 full weeks without an outage?

For 2 weeks: lambda = 0.5 x 2 = 1. P(X = 0) = e^(-1) = 0.368. There is about a 36.8% chance of zero outages.

A medical test is given to 500 patients independently. If the true positive rate is 3%, what distribution models the number of positive results, and what is the expected number?

Fixed n = 500, binary outcome, same p = 0.03, independent = binomial. Expected = np = 500 x 0.03 = 15.

An online retailer measures time spent on their website. The data shows most visitors spend a short time with a few spending much longer. The mean is 8 minutes and the median is 4 minutes. What is the best distribution model?

When mean (8) is much larger than median (4) and most values cluster at the low end with a long right tail, an exponential or similar right-skewed model fits best.

Real-World Applications

Example: SAT scores are best modeled by:

SAT scores are approximately normally distributed -- bell-shaped and symmetric around the mean.

Example: Number of typos per page with an average rate is modeled by:

Rare, independent events per unit (page) at a constant rate follows the Poisson distribution.

Example: A city's daily 911 call volume averages 150 calls with a standard deviation of 20. Using the normal model, on what percentage of days do they receive more than 190 calls?

Z = (190 - 150)/20 = 2.0. P(Z > 2) = 0.0228, or about 2.3% of days.

Example: A streaming service experiences server outages at a rate of 0.5 per week. What is the probability of going 2 full weeks without an outage?

For 2 weeks: lambda = 0.5 x 2 = 1. P(X = 0) = e^(-1) = 0.368. There is about a 36.8% chance of zero outages.

Example: A call center receives calls at a rate that varies: 20 calls/hour during peak and 5 calls/hour off-peak. A manager uses a single Poisson model with lambda = 12.5. Why might this be problematic?

The Poisson distribution assumes a constant rate. If the rate varies between peak and off-peak, a single Poisson model is inappropriate.

Important Concepts

What does 'right-skewed' mean

Right-skewed distributions have a longer right tail. The mean is pulled toward the tail, so mean > median.

An engineer monitors the time between machine failures. The mean time is 200 hours and the data appears right-skewed. Which distribution should the engineer use

Time between failures is a waiting time that is typically right-skewed, making the exponential distribution the natural choice.
