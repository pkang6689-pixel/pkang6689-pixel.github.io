Lesson 5.5: Central Limit Theorem - Summary

Key Concepts: Central Limit Theorem

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the Central Limit Theorem?

The CLT states that for large n, the distribution of x-bar is approximately normal, even if the population itself is not normal.

What is a 'sampling distribution'?

The sampling distribution shows the pattern of a statistic's values when the sampling process is repeated many times.

What is the Law of Large Numbers?

The Law of Large Numbers says that x-bar approaches mu as n grows, which is related to but distinct from the CLT.

A bottling machine fills cans with mu = 12 oz and sigma = 0.5 oz. If a quality inspector measures 25 cans, what is the probability that the sample mean exceeds 12.2 oz?

SE = 0.5 / sqrt(25) = 0.1. Z = (12.2 - 12) / 0.1 = 2.0. P(Z > 2) = 0.0228.

A hospital records patient wait times with mu = 45 min and sigma = 15 min (right-skewed). For a sample of 100 patients, what is the probability the mean wait time is less than 42 minutes?

SE = 15 / sqrt(100) = 1.5. Z = (42 - 45) / 1.5 = -2.0. P(Z < -2) = 0.0228. The CLT applies since n = 100.

A teacher gives the same exam to 40 students. The population mean score is 72 with sigma = 12. What is the probability the class average is between 70 and 74?

SE = 12 / sqrt(40) = 1.897. Z for 70 = (70 - 72)/1.897 = -1.054. Z for 74 = (74 - 72)/1.897 = 1.054. P(-1.054 < Z < 1.054) = about 0.706 or 70.6%.

A factory produces bolts with mu = 10 cm and sigma = 0.3 cm. An inspector measures a sample of 9 bolts and finds x-bar = 10.2 cm. What is the z-score for this sample mean?

SE = 0.3 / sqrt(9) = 0.1. Z = (10.2 - 10) / 0.1 = 2.0.

An airline's luggage weights have mu = 50 lbs and sigma = 10 lbs (right-skewed). For a flight with 64 passengers, what is the probability the mean luggage weight exceeds 52 lbs?

SE = 10 / sqrt(64) = 1.25. Z = (52 - 50) / 1.25 = 1.6. P(Z > 1.6) = 0.0548. CLT applies since n = 64.

A survey measures household income in a city with mu = $55,000 and sigma = $20,000. If a random sample of 400 households is taken, what is the standard error and approximate distribution of x-bar?

SE = 20,000 / sqrt(400) = 20,000 / 20 = $1,000. With n = 400, the CLT ensures x-bar is approximately normal even though income is typically skewed.

Real-World Applications

Example: A teacher gives the same exam to 40 students. The population mean score is 72 with sigma = 12. What is the probability the class average is between 70 and 74?

SE = 12 / sqrt(40) = 1.897. Z for 70 = (70 - 72)/1.897 = -1.054. Z for 74 = (74 - 72)/1.897 = 1.054. P(-1.054 < Z < 1.054) = about 0.706 or 70.6%.

Example: A pizza delivery company claims an average delivery time of 30 minutes with sigma = 8 minutes. A customer orders 4 times and records a mean delivery time of 38 minutes. Is this sample mean unusually high?

SE = 8 / sqrt(4) = 4. Z = (38 - 30) / 4 = 2.0. A z-score of 2.0 is unusually high (P(Z > 2) = 0.023).

Example: A factory produces bolts with mu = 10 cm and sigma = 0.3 cm. An inspector measures a sample of 9 bolts and finds x-bar = 10.2 cm. What is the z-score for this sample mean?

SE = 0.3 / sqrt(9) = 0.1. Z = (10.2 - 10) / 0.1 = 2.0.

Example: An airline's luggage weights have mu = 50 lbs and sigma = 10 lbs (right-skewed). For a flight with 64 passengers, what is the probability the mean luggage weight exceeds 52 lbs?

SE = 10 / sqrt(64) = 1.25. Z = (52 - 50) / 1.25 = 1.6. P(Z > 1.6) = 0.0548. CLT applies since n = 64.

Example: To cut the standard error in half, you must multiply the sample size by:

SE = sigma / sqrt(n). To halve SE, you need sqrt(n) to double, which means n must quadruple (multiply by 4).
