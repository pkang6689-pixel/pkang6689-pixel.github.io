Lesson 2.2: Histograms & Bar Graphs - Summary

Key Concepts: Histograms & Bar Graphs

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a histogram?

A histogram uses touching bars to show how quantitative data is distributed across bins (intervals), revealing the shape and spread of the data.

What is a bar graph?

A bar graph uses separated bars to display the frequency, count, or other measure for each distinct category of qualitative data.

What is a 'Pareto chart'?

A Pareto chart is a special bar graph that arranges categories from most frequent to least frequent, often including a cumulative line.

Important Concepts

A frequency histogram where each bar represents exactly one value (e.g., number of siblings: 0, 1, 2, 3) is used for:

When each bar represents a single countable value, the histogram displays discrete quantitative data.

What does 'skewed right' mean for a distribution

A right-skewed (positively skewed) distribution has most data concentrated on the left with a long tail stretching toward the higher values on the right.

What does 'skewed left' mean for a distribution

A left-skewed (negatively skewed) distribution has most data concentrated on the right with a long tail stretching toward the lower values on the left.

What does 'bimodal distribution' mean

A bimodal distribution has two noticeable peaks, suggesting the data may come from two different groups or processes.

What does 'relative frequency histogram' mean

A relative frequency histogram has bars whose heights represent the proportion (or percentage) of the total data in each bin.
