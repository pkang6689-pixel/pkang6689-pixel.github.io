Lesson 2.4: Boxplots & Quartiles - Summary

Key Concepts: Boxplots & Quartiles

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a boxplot (box-and-whisker plot)?

A boxplot visually represents the five-number summary (min, Q1, median, Q3, max), shows the spread through the box and whiskers, and identifies potential outliers.

What is a quartile?

Quartiles are the three values (Q1, Q2, Q3) that divide a sorted dataset into four approximately equal groups.

What is the interquartile range (IQR)?

The IQR measures the spread of the central half of the data, calculated by subtracting Q1 from Q3.

What is an outlier in the context of boxplots?

In boxplots, an outlier is a value that lies beyond the fences, which are set at Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and Q3 + 1.5(IQR).

A dataset of 12 values is: 3, 5, 7, 8, 10, 12, 14, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25. What is Q1?

For 12 values, Q1 is the median of the lower half (3,5,7,8,10,12). The median of these 6 values is (7+8)/2 = 7.5.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Outliers are data points that fall more than 1.5 times the IQR below Q1 or above Q3.

In boxplots, an outlier is a value that lies beyond the fences, which are set at Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and Q3 + 1.5(IQR).

The IQR is resistant because it is based on Q1 and Q3, which are not influenced by extreme values at the tails of the distribution.

Q1 = 20, Q3 = 55, IQR = 35. Upper fence = 55 + 1.5(35) = 107.5. Since 100 < 107.5, it is actually within the fence. However, 100 is the most extreme and the value most likely flagged in context.

Lower fence = Q1 - 1.5(IQR) = 20 - 1.5(20) = 20 - 30 = -10. Any value below -10 would be a lower outlier.

Outliers represent properties with prices far from the bulk of the data, warranting investigation but not automatic removal.

Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5(IQR) = 8.3 + 1.5(1.1) = 8.3 + 1.65 = 9.95. Since 12.0 > 9.95, it is classified as an outlier.

Real-World Applications

Example: A gym compares workout durations (in minutes) for two age groups using side-by-side boxplots. The 20-30 age group has a median of 60 minutes and IQR of 20, while the 50-60 age group has a median of 40 minutes and IQR of 30. Which group has more variability in workout duration?

The 50-60 age group has a larger IQR (30 vs. 20), indicating greater spread in the middle 50% of their workout durations.

Example: Three classes take the same final exam. Their boxplots show: Class A median = 72, IQR = 15; Class B median = 78, IQR = 8; Class C median = 75, IQR = 25. Which class has the most consistent scores?

A smaller IQR means less spread in the middle 50% of scores. Class B's IQR of 8 indicates the most consistent performance.
