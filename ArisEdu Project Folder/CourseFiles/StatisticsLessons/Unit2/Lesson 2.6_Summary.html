Lesson 2.6: Pie Charts & Circle Graphs - Summary

Key Concepts: Pie Charts & Circle Graphs

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a pie chart?

A pie chart is a circular graph where each slice represents a category's proportion of the total, making it easy to see how a whole is divided.

What is a 'donut chart'?

A donut chart is a variation of a pie chart with a blank center, sometimes used to show a total or label in the middle.

What is a 'legend' on a pie chart?

A legend maps each color or pattern to its corresponding category name, helping readers identify what each slice represents.

A survey of 400 students asks their favorite school subject: Math (100), Science (80), English (90), History (70), Art (60). What is the central angle for the Math slice?

Math proportion = 100/400 = 0.25. Central angle = 0.25 x 360 = 90 degrees.

Real-World Applications

Example: A disadvantage of pie charts compared to bar graphs is that pie charts:

Humans find it harder to compare angles and areas in pie charts than bar heights, making precise comparisons more difficult.

Example: What does 'percentage label' on a pie chart communicate?

Percentage labels show readers the exact share each category represents, since estimating proportions visually from slice size alone can be inaccurate.

Example: A company's budget pie chart shows: Salaries 45%, Rent 20%, Marketing 15%, Supplies 10%, Other 10%. An employee notices the percentages sum to 100%. If the total budget is $500,000, how much is spent on Marketing?

Marketing = 15% of $500,000 = 0.15 x $500,000 = $75,000.

Example: A researcher creates a pie chart for a survey where respondents could select multiple favorite sports (football, basketball, baseball, soccer). The percentages add up to 180%. This is problematic because:

When respondents can choose multiple categories, the responses do not form parts of a single whole, making a pie chart inappropriate.

Example: A magazine displays transportation methods of commuters using a pie chart with 12 tiny slices (bike, bus, car, carpool, e-scooter, ferry, motorcycle, rail, rideshare, subway, taxi, walk). This chart would be improved by:

With 12 categories, many slices will be too small to distinguish. Aggregating minor categories or using a bar graph would improve readability.

Example: A city's annual water usage report shows: Residential 55%, Agricultural 25%, Industrial 15%, Commercial 5%. The pie chart omits the 'Commercial' label because the slice is very small. A council member, seeing only three labeled slices summing to 95%, might incorrectly think:

Omitting a label makes the visible percentages appear not to sum to 100%, which can confuse readers. All categories should be labeled or grouped into 'Other.'
