Lesson 2.3: Stem-and-Leaf Plots - Summary

Key Concepts: Stem-and-Leaf Plots

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a stem-and-leaf plot?

A stem-and-leaf plot separates each data value into a stem (leading digits) and a leaf (last digit), displaying all individual values while showing the distribution shape.

What is a 'back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot'?

A back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot places the stems in the center with one dataset's leaves on the left and the other on the right for easy comparison.

What is the purpose of a 'key' or 'legend' on a stem-and-leaf plot?

The key provides an example showing how to combine a stem and leaf to reconstruct the original data value.

What is the main advantage of a stem-and-leaf plot over a frequency table?

A stem-and-leaf plot simultaneously shows the distribution shape (like a histogram) and retains all original data values (unlike a grouped frequency table).

Given the stem-and-leaf plot below (key: 3|2 = 32): Stem 3: 2, 5, 7; Stem 4: 0, 1, 3, 6, 8; Stem 5: 2, 5, 9; Stem 6: 1, 4. What is the median of this dataset?

There are 14 data values. The median is the average of the 7th and 8th values. Counting: 32,35,37,40,41,43,46,48,52,55,59,61,64. The 7th value is 46 and the 8th is 48. Median = (46+48)/2 = 47. However, 46 is the closest option and the 7th value.

A stem-and-leaf plot of daily high temperatures in a city over 30 days shows stem 6 with 2 leaves, stem 7 with 12 leaves, stem 8 with 14 leaves, and stem 9 with 2 leaves. What is the approximate shape of this distribution?

With few values at the extremes (60s and 90s) and most concentrated in the middle (70s and 80s), the distribution is roughly bell-shaped.

Real-World Applications

Example: An advantage of stem-and-leaf plots over histograms is that they:

Unlike histograms which group data into bins, stem-and-leaf plots retain every individual data value.

Example: What is the main advantage of a stem-and-leaf plot over a frequency table?

A stem-and-leaf plot simultaneously shows the distribution shape (like a histogram) and retains all original data values (unlike a grouped frequency table).

Example: A class of 20 students received the following test scores: 62, 65, 67, 71, 73, 74, 75, 78, 79, 80, 81, 82, 84, 85, 87, 88, 90, 92, 95, 98. In a stem-and-leaf plot, which stem has the most leaves?

Stem 8 has leaves 0, 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8 (seven values), which is more than stem 7 (six values: 1, 3, 4, 5, 8, 9), stem 6 (three values), or stem 9 (four values).

Example: Two sections of a statistics class take the same quiz. Section A scores: 55, 60, 65, 70, 72, 75, 78, 80, 85. Section B scores: 68, 70, 75, 78, 80, 82, 85, 88, 92. Using a back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot, which section generally scored higher?

Section B's scores are concentrated in the 70s-90s range, while Section A has scores starting from the 50s, indicating Section B generally performed better.

Example: A teacher uses a stem-and-leaf plot to display scores on a 100-point exam. One student scored 100. With stems representing tens digits, the teacher should handle 100 by:

For three-digit numbers, the stem becomes the first two digits (10) and the leaf is the last digit (0), giving stem 10, leaf 0.

Example: A researcher wants to compare the ages of volunteers at two different nonprofits. She creates a back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot. Nonprofit A has leaves clustered around stems 2 and 3, while Nonprofit B has leaves clustered around stems 5 and 6. What can she conclude?

Leaves clustered at stems 2-3 indicate ages mostly in the 20s-30s (younger), while leaves at stems 5-6 indicate ages in the 50s-60s (older).
