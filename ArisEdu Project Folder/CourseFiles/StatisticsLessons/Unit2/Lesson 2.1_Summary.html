Lesson 2.1: Frequency Tables - Summary

Key Concepts: Frequency Tables

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a frequency table?

A frequency table organizes data by listing each distinct value or category alongside its count (frequency) in the dataset.

What is a 'class interval' in a grouped frequency table?

A class interval defines the lower and upper boundaries of a bin, grouping all data values that fall within that range.

What is an 'ungrouped frequency table'?

An ungrouped frequency table lists every distinct value separately with its frequency, without combining values into intervals.

A teacher records quiz scores for 30 students: five scored 70, eight scored 80, twelve scored 90, and five scored 100. What is the relative frequency of students who scored 90?

Relative frequency = 12/30 = 0.40, which is 40%. Twelve out of thirty students scored 90.

In a survey of 500 people about their favorite fruit, the frequency table shows: Apples 150, Bananas 120, Oranges 100, Grapes 80, Other 50. What is the cumulative frequency up to and including Oranges (listed in order)?

Cumulative frequency up to Oranges = 150 + 120 + 100 = 370.

A school surveys 200 students about study hours per week and builds a frequency table: 0-4 hrs (30), 5-9 hrs (55), 10-14 hrs (70), 15-19 hrs (35), 20-24 hrs (10). What is the class midpoint for the 10-14 hour class?

Class midpoint = (10 + 14) / 2 = 12 hours.

Real-World Applications

Example: Which type of frequency table would best organize the ages of 500 survey respondents (ages 18-85)?

With a wide range of ages and many data points, grouping ages into intervals makes the table more readable and informative.

Example: A teacher records quiz scores for 30 students: five scored 70, eight scored 80, twelve scored 90, and five scored 100. What is the relative frequency of students who scored 90?

Relative frequency = 12/30 = 0.40, which is 40%. Twelve out of thirty students scored 90.

Example: A hospital emergency room records patient wait times. The grouped frequency table shows: 0-14 min: 20 patients, 15-29 min: 45 patients, 30-44 min: 30 patients, 45-59 min: 5 patients. What percentage of patients waited less than 30 minutes?

Patients waiting less than 30 minutes: 20 + 45 = 65 out of 100 total patients, which is 65%.

Example: A researcher collects data on the ages of 60 participants. The frequency table with 5-year intervals shows: 20-24 (8), 25-29 (15), 30-34 (20), 35-39 (12), 40-44 (5). She wants to quickly determine what percentage of participants are under 35. Using cumulative relative frequency, the answer is:

Participants under 35 = 8 + 15 + 20 = 43 out of 60. Cumulative relative frequency = 43/60 = 0.717, or about 72%.

Example: A manager looks at a frequency table of employee salaries and notices that the relative frequencies are: Under $40K: 0.15, $40K-$59K: 0.35, $60K-$79K: 0.30, $80K-$99K: 0.12, $100K+: 0.08. If the company has 400 employees, how many earn between $40K and $59K?

Frequency = relative frequency x total = 0.35 x 400 = 140 employees.

Example: A city planner examines a frequency table of daily traffic counts at an intersection: 0-99 cars (2 days), 100-199 (8 days), 200-299 (12 days), 300-399 (6 days), 400-499 (2 days). Over the 30-day period, what percentage of days had fewer than 300 cars?

Days with fewer than 300 cars: 2 + 8 + 12 = 22 days. Percentage = 22/30 = 0.733, or about 73%.

Relative frequency equals the frequency of a category divided by the total number of data points, often expressed as a proportion or percentage.

Cumulative frequency sums all frequencies from the beginning up to and including the current value or class.

Since relative frequencies represent proportions of the whole, they must add up to 1 (or 100%).

A class interval (or bin) is a range of values that are grouped together in a frequency table to simplify large datasets.

Adding all frequencies gives the total count of observations in the dataset.

Classes should be mutually exclusive (non-overlapping) and exhaustive (covering all data values) to avoid ambiguity.

What is a frequency table

A frequency table organizes data by listing each distinct value or category alongside its count (frequency) in the dataset.

What does 'relative frequency' mean

Relative frequency is the fraction or percentage of the total observations that fall into a particular category or class.

What does 'cumulative frequency' mean

Cumulative frequency is the sum of all frequencies up to and including a given class, showing how many data points fall at or below that value.

What is a 'class interval' in a grouped frequency table

A class interval defines the lower and upper boundaries of a bin, grouping all data values that fall within that range.

What does 'mutually exclusive classes' mean in a frequency table

Mutually exclusive classes have no overlap, ensuring every data value belongs to one and only one class.

What does 'frequency distribution' mean

A frequency distribution shows how observations are spread across different values or ranges, revealing the shape and pattern of the data.

What is an 'ungrouped frequency table'

An ungrouped frequency table lists every distinct value separately with its frequency, without combining values into intervals.

Class 40-49 contains three values (42, 48, 40), which is the most of any class. 10-19 has 2 (15, 10); 20-29 has 2 (22, 20); 30-39 has 2 (35, 30); 50-59 has 1 (50).

A company records the number of customer complaints per day for 20 days. The data ranges from 0 to 12. An analyst wants to create a grouped frequency table with 4 classes of equal width. What should the class width be

Class width = range / number of classes = (12 - 0) / 4 = 3. The classes would be 0-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-11 (with 12 included in the last class or an adjusted upper boundary).
