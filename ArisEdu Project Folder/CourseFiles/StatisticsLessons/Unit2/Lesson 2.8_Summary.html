Lesson 2.8: Case Studies in Data Visualization - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Data Visualization

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a polar area chart?

A polar area chart uses wedge-shaped sectors whose area (not just radius) represents data values. Nightingale's Rose Diagram is the most famous example.

What is the data-ink ratio?

The data-ink ratio, introduced by Tufte, measures the share of ink in a graphic devoted to data rather than non-data elements. A higher ratio indicates a cleaner graphic.

What is a choropleth map?

A choropleth map shades geographic regions (states, countries) according to a data variable, such as population density or election results.

What is meant by 'context' in data visualization?

Context helps the audience understand what the data represent and how to interpret them correctly.

What is an interactive dashboard?

Interactive dashboards are modern data visualization tools that allow users to manipulate views, apply filters, and explore underlying data dynamically.

What is meant by 'data storytelling'?

Data storytelling combines data, visuals, and narrative to convey a message, highlight trends, and engage the audience.

What is a heat map?

A heat map uses color gradients to represent data intensity, making it easy to spot patterns, clusters, and outliers across a matrix or map.

A public health department creates a choropleth map showing COVID-19 cases by county using raw case counts rather than per-capita rates. A large urban county with 2 million people and 10,000 cases appears bright red, while a rural county with 5,000 people and 800 cases appears pale. What is the primary issue?

The rural county actually has a 16% case rate versus the urban county's 0.5% rate. Using per-capita rates would more accurately represent relative impact.

Real-World Applications

Showing only a favorable time period while hiding the broader trend is a misleading visualization practice.

Example: A hospital administrator creates a bar chart comparing patient wait times across five departments. She notices the y-axis starts at 25 minutes instead of 0, making the emergency department's 30-minute average look dramatically higher than the 28-minute average of the outpatient clinic. What should she do to present the data honestly?

Starting at 0 or adding a clear axis break notation prevents exaggerating small differences, which is essential for honest data communication.

Example: A marketing team builds a 3D pie chart showing quarterly sales. The quarter closest to the viewer (Q4) appears visually larger than Q2, even though both represent 25% of sales. An intern suggests switching to a flat 2D pie chart or a bar chart. Why is the intern correct?

3D perspective causes foreshortening, making slices closer to the viewer look disproportionately large. A flat chart or bar chart avoids this distortion.

Example: A researcher studying commute times creates a histogram for 500 employees. She uses 50 bins for data ranging from 5 to 65 minutes. The histogram looks extremely jagged and hard to interpret. A colleague suggests using 10-12 bins. Why would fewer bins improve the visualization?

With 50 bins over a 60-minute range, each bin spans only about 1.2 minutes, creating jagged noise. Using 10-12 bins reveals the true shape of the distribution.

Example: A school district publishes an infographic claiming test scores 'doubled' by showing a cartoon pencil that is twice as tall and twice as wide for the current year compared to last year. If scores rose from 60 to 72, what TWO problems exist?

The actual increase is (72-60)/60 = 20%, not 100%. Furthermore, doubling both dimensions of a 2D icon quadruples the visual area, creating a lie factor well above 1.

Example: A pharmaceutical company presents clinical trial results using a dual y-axis chart. The left axis shows percentage of patients improved (0-100%) and the right axis shows side effect count (0-500). A critic argues this is misleading. Why?

Dual y-axes allow the creator to manipulate which scale aligns with which, potentially making a small effect look large or implying a spurious relationship between the two measures.
