Lesson 6.5: Expansion of the Universe - Summary

Key Concepts: Expansion of the Universe

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

If the universe is expanding, what is NOT expanding?

Local gravity overcomes the expansion; galaxies, the solar system, and atoms do not expand.

What is Hubble's Law?

Hubble's Law is the observational foundation of expanding universe cosmology.

What is the Hubble constant (H₀)?

H₀ quantifies how fast distant galaxies are receding: ~70 km/s/Mpc means a galaxy at 1 Mpc recedes at ~70 km/s.

What is cosmological redshift?

Cosmological redshift differs from the Doppler effect: it's caused by space expanding, not objects moving through space.

What is the Big Bang?

The Big Bang describes the beginning of space, time, matter, and energy — not an explosion within space.

What is cosmic inflation?

Inflation (proposed by Alan Guth) explains the universe's uniformity and flatness — space expanded faster than light for a tiny fraction of a second.

What is the observable universe?

The observable universe is finite because light travels at finite speed; beyond the horizon, there may be more universe we can't yet see.

What is the cosmic microwave background (CMB)?

The CMB was emitted when the universe cooled enough for electrons and protons to combine, releasing photons.

What is redshift (z)?

Higher redshift means greater distance and look-back time; z=1 means the wavelength has doubled.

What is the Hubble tension?

CMB-based measurements give ~67 km/s/Mpc while local distance ladder measurements give ~73 km/s/Mpc — a significant disagreement.

What is the scale factor?

The scale factor a(t) is normalized to 1 today; at the CMB epoch, a ≈ 1/1100.

A student asks: 'Where is the center of the Big Bang expansion?' What is the correct answer?

The expansion of space is uniform; every galaxy sees all others receding, as if each point is the center.

If the Hubble constant is 70 km/s/Mpc, what is the approximate recession velocity of a galaxy 100 Mpc away?

v = H₀d = 70 × 100 = 7,000 km/s.

Real-World Applications

Example: The age of the universe is estimated at:

Multiple independent measurements (CMB, stellar ages, nucleosynthesis) converge on ~13.8 billion years.
