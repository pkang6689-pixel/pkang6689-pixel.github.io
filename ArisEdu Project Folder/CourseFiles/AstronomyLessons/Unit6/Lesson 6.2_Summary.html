Lesson 6.2: Milky Way Structure - Summary

Key Concepts: Milky Way Structure

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the galactic disk?

The disk is where spiral arms, star formation, and most of the visible galaxy resides.

What is the galactic bulge?

The bulge contains a mix of old and intermediate-age stars and surrounds Sagittarius A*.

What is the galactic halo?

The halo extends hundreds of thousands of light-years and is mostly dark matter.

What is the galactic center?

The galactic center region is extremely dense with stars and hosts the 4-million solar mass black hole Sgr A*.

What is the Orion Arm?

Also called the Orion Spur, it sits between the larger Perseus and Sagittarius arms.

What is a globular cluster?

Globular clusters are ancient (10-13 Gyr) and orbit in the galactic halo; the Milky Way has about 150-200.

What is the rotation curve?

Milky Way rotation curves remain flat at large radii, providing key evidence for dark matter.

What is interstellar medium (ISM)?

The ISM includes molecular clouds, atomic hydrogen, ionized gas, and dust — the raw material for star formation.

What is the zone of avoidance?

The zone of avoidance makes it difficult to survey galaxies behind the Milky Way's dusty disk in optical wavelengths.
