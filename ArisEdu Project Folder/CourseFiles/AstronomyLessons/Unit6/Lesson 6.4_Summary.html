Lesson 6.4: Dark Matter & Dark Energy - Summary

Key Concepts: Dark Matter & Dark Energy

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is dark matter?

Dark matter makes up ~27% of the universe and is detected only through its gravitational effects.

What is dark energy?

Dark energy is the dominant component of the universe (~68%), but its nature remains one of physics' greatest mysteries.

What is the cosmological constant (Λ)?

Einstein originally introduced Λ for a static universe; it was later reinterpreted as dark energy.

What is a galaxy rotation curve?

Flat rotation curves at large radii are among the strongest evidence for dark matter halos around galaxies.

What is gravitational lensing?

Gravitational lensing maps the total mass distribution, including invisible dark matter.

What is baryonic matter?

Baryonic matter includes all visible and familiar matter — stars, planets, gas, animals, humans.

What is the cosmic microwave background (CMB)?

The CMB is a nearly uniform glow at 2.725 K, with tiny fluctuations revealing early universe structure.

What is modified Newtonian dynamics (MOND)?

MOND can explain some galaxy rotation curves but struggles with galaxy clusters and the CMB.

What is the fate of the universe if dark energy continues to dominate?

With dark energy driving acceleration, distant galaxies will become unreachable and the universe will cool eternally.
