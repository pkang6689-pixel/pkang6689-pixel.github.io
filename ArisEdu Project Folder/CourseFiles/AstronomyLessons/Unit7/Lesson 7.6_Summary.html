Lesson 7.6: Case Studies in Space Technology - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Space Technology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a CubeSat?

A CubeSat is a type of miniaturized satellite built to a standardized size (one unit is a 10 cm cube weighing about 1.3 kg), which has democratized access to space by allowing universities, small companies, and developing nations to build and launch satellites at a fraction of traditional costs.

What is transit spectroscopy as used by JWST?

Transit spectroscopy works by analyzing the starlight that passes through an exoplanet's atmosphere as the planet crosses in front of its host star. Different molecules like water vapor, CO2, and methane absorb specific wavelengths, creating a chemical fingerprint that JWST can detect.

What is the definition of 'sunshield' as used on JWST?

JWST's sunshield is a five-layer structure made of Kapton, roughly the size of a tennis court, that blocks and dissipates heat from the Sun, Earth, and Moon. It reduces the temperature on the instrument side to below -233 degrees Celsius, which is essential for detecting faint infrared signals.

What is Rocket Lab's Electron?

Rocket Lab's Electron is a small, two-stage launch vehicle specifically designed to provide affordable, dedicated launches for small satellites and CubeSats, addressing the growing demand for flexible small-satellite deployment without requiring rideshare on larger rockets.

What is meant by 'technology readiness level' (TRL) in space technology development?

Technology Readiness Level (TRL) is a standardized scale from 1 (basic principles observed) to 9 (proven through successful mission operations) used by NASA and other agencies to evaluate how mature a technology is before incorporating it into a mission, helping manage risk in space system development.

What is the definition of 'propulsive landing' as demonstrated by Falcon 9?

Propulsive landing is a technique where a rocket booster reignites its engines during descent to decelerate and land vertically under its own power, as demonstrated by SpaceX's Falcon 9 boosters landing on ground pads and autonomous drone ships at sea.

What is 'Kapton' in the context of JWST's sunshield?

Kapton is a polyimide film developed by DuPont that remains stable across extreme temperatures and resists radiation degradation. JWST's sunshield uses five layers of aluminized and silicon-coated Kapton to progressively reduce the temperature from the sun-facing side to the instrument side.

A satellite company needs to launch a single 50 kg Earth-observation CubeSat into a specific sun-synchronous orbit. They could book a rideshare on a large Falcon 9 launch or a dedicated launch on Rocket Lab's Electron. What is the main advantage of choosing the dedicated Electron launch?

While rideshare launches on larger rockets are cheaper per kilogram, a dedicated launch on Electron allows the customer to choose the exact orbital parameters, inclination, and launch date, rather than compromising to match the primary payload's requirements. This flexibility is critical for missions needing specific sun-synchronous or other specialized orbits.

An astronomer using JWST detects water vapor, carbon dioxide, and methane in the atmosphere of an exoplanet orbiting a nearby star. A colleague asks why JWST's infrared capability is essential for this detection. What is the best explanation?

Molecules like water, CO2, and methane have characteristic absorption bands in the infrared spectrum. During a transit, starlight passing through the exoplanet's atmosphere is partially absorbed at these specific wavelengths, and JWST's sensitive infrared spectrographs can measure these tiny absorption features to identify the atmospheric composition.

A SpaceX engineer is analyzing the economics of Falcon 9 reuse. If a new Falcon 9 booster costs $30 million to build and can be reflown 15 times with $2 million in refurbishment per flight, what is the approximate booster cost per launch compared to an expendable booster?

By spreading the $30 million construction cost across 15 flights ($2M per flight) and adding $2M refurbishment per flight, the booster cost drops to roughly $4 million per launch. Compared to the $30 million expendable cost, this represents approximately a 7-8 times reduction, illustrating why reusability is revolutionizing launch economics.

A startup is designing a new rocket and must choose between traditional machining and 3D printing (additive manufacturing) for their engine combustion chamber. The 3D-printed version has 3 parts compared to 300 parts in the traditionally machined version. What is the primary engineering advantage of fewer parts?

Reducing a combustion chamber from 300 individually machined and welded parts to 3 printed components eliminates hundreds of welds and joints, each of which is a potential failure point. This dramatically improves reliability, reduces manufacturing time from months to days, and lowers costs, which is why companies like Relativity Space and SpaceX are adopting this approach.

A materials scientist notices that JWST's mirror segments are made of beryllium coated in gold. A student asks why gold is used instead of aluminum, which is the standard coating for most telescope mirrors. What is the best explanation?

Gold is an excellent reflector of infrared light, reflecting over 98% of infrared radiation, which makes it the ideal coating for JWST's mirrors since the telescope is optimized for infrared observations. Aluminum, while excellent for visible and ultraviolet light, has lower infrared reflectivity than gold.

A hospital administrator learns that a new water purification system installed in the building was originally developed by NASA for the International Space Station. She is skeptical that space technology is relevant to healthcare. What is the strongest argument that space technology spin-offs benefit fields like medicine and public health?

The demanding constraints of space missions (extreme reliability requirements, severe weight and power limitations, and the need for self-contained systems) push engineers to develop technologies that, once adapted for terrestrial use, offer superior performance in water purification, portable medical diagnostics, advanced prosthetics, and remote telemedicine systems.
