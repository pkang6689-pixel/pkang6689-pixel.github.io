Lesson 7.3: Human Spaceflight - Summary

Key Concepts: Human Spaceflight

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is microgravity?

Microgravity refers to the condition of apparent weightlessness experienced in orbit, where gravity is not truly absent but where everything (the spacecraft and its occupants) is in continuous free fall around Earth. This creates a near-weightless environment that profoundly affects the human body, fluid behavior, and physical processes.

What is SANS (Spaceflight Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome)?

SANS is a medical condition affecting many astronauts where the absence of gravity causes body fluids to shift toward the head, increasing intracranial pressure that leads to swelling of the optic nerves and flattening of the eyeballs. It remains an active area of research because the resulting vision changes can persist even after returning to Earth.

What is In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)?

In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) is the strategy of harvesting and using materials found at the mission destination rather than transporting everything from Earth. For example, on Mars this could mean extracting oxygen from the CO2 atmosphere and obtaining water from subsurface ice, which would dramatically reduce the mass and cost of future crewed missions.

What is an EMU (Extravehicular Mobility Unit)?

The EMU is essentially a miniature spacecraft worn by astronauts during spacewalks, providing all the life support functions needed to survive in the vacuum of space. It supplies pressurized breathable air, regulates temperature, shields against micrometeoroid impacts and radiation, and includes communication equipment and a jet-pack-like system for emergency propulsion.

What is artificial gravity in the context of spacecraft design?

Artificial gravity is a concept in which a rotating spacecraft creates centrifugal force that simulates the effect of gravity, pushing occupants outward toward the hull. While this idea has been studied extensively as a way to counteract the harmful effects of long-term weightlessness, it has not yet been implemented on any crewed spacecraft.

What is the definition of space adaptation syndrome?

Space adaptation syndrome is a type of motion sickness that affects roughly 60-80% of astronauts during their first 2-3 days in microgravity, causing nausea, disorientation, and headaches. It occurs because the vestibular system in the inner ear sends conflicting signals to the brain when gravity is no longer providing a consistent reference for 'up' and 'down.'

What is the meaning of 'deep space' as used in human spaceflight planning?

In the context of human spaceflight, deep space refers to destinations beyond low Earth orbit (LEO), including cislunar space, the Moon, Mars, and beyond. Deep-space missions present far greater challenges than LEO missions because astronauts are beyond the protection of Earth's magnetosphere and cannot be quickly returned home in an emergency.

A flight surgeon reviewing astronaut health data notices that a crew member returning from a 6-month ISS mission has lost 8% of bone density in the hip despite exercising daily. What is the most likely explanation?

Even with rigorous daily exercise, astronauts in microgravity still experience some bone loss because their weight-bearing bones are not loaded the way they would be under Earth's gravity. Exercise is a critical countermeasure that significantly reduces the rate of loss, but it cannot eliminate it entirely, which is why some bone density reduction is expected after a 6-month mission.

An engineer working on a Mars habitat design must decide how the crew will obtain water during a 500-day surface stay. Launching all the needed water from Earth would be prohibitively expensive. What ISRU approach could solve this problem?

Mars has confirmed subsurface ice deposits, particularly at higher latitudes, which could be mined and melted to provide water for drinking, hygiene, agriculture, and even rocket propellant production. Combined with highly efficient water recycling systems similar to those on the ISS, this ISRU approach would dramatically reduce the mass of supplies that must be launched from Earth, making long-duration surface missions feasible.

A space agency is planning a crewed lunar surface mission lasting 14 days. The crew will need to perform multiple EVAs to collect geological samples. During planning, the spacesuit engineering team must ensure their new suits address a hazard unique to the lunar surface that was reported by Apollo astronauts. What is this hazard?

Apollo astronauts reported that fine lunar dust (regolith) was one of the most troublesome hazards they encountered: the electrostatically charged particles clung to suits, scratched helmet visors, jammed equipment, and irritated their eyes and lungs when tracked into the lunar module. New spacesuit designs for Artemis missions must include improved dust-resistant seals, coatings, and airlock systems to mitigate this pervasive and damaging hazard.

Real-World Applications

Example: A space agency is planning a crewed Mars mission expected to last 2.5 years round trip. During the mission planning meeting, the psychology team raises concerns about crew mental health. Which combination of factors makes this a uniquely challenging psychological environment?

A Mars mission presents an unprecedented combination of psychological stressors including extreme isolation from friends and family on Earth, confinement in a small habitat with the same few people for years, communication delays of up to 24 minutes preventing real-time conversations, disrupted circadian rhythms, and the knowledge that emergency return is impossible. Mission planners must carefully select compatible crews and develop robust psychological support strategies.

Example: An optometrist examining an astronaut after a year-long ISS mission finds that the astronaut's eyesight has worsened, with signs of optic disc swelling and globe flattening. Based on current research, what condition is this most consistent with, and what causes it?

These symptoms are characteristic of Spaceflight Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome (SANS), which affects a significant percentage of astronauts on long-duration missions. In microgravity, the normal downward drainage of body fluids does not occur, causing fluid to accumulate in the head and increase pressure around the brain and optic nerves, leading to structural changes in the eyes that can impair vision.
