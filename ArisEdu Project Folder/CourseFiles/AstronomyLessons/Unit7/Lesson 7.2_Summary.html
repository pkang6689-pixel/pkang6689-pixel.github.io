Lesson 7.2: Satellites & Space Probes - Summary

Key Concepts: Satellites & Space Probes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a gravity assist?

A gravity assist (also called a gravitational slingshot) is a spaceflight maneuver where a probe flies close to a planet and uses its gravitational field to gain or lose speed and change direction, effectively getting a free momentum boost without expending fuel.

What is the Kessler syndrome?

The Kessler syndrome, proposed by NASA scientist Donald Kessler in 1978, describes a scenario in which the density of objects in Low Earth Orbit is high enough that collisions between them generate debris that triggers further collisions in a runaway cascade, potentially rendering entire orbital regions unusable for generations.

What is a CubeSat?

A CubeSat is a miniaturized satellite built in standardized 10 cm x 10 cm x 10 cm units (called 1U), which can be combined into larger configurations like 3U or 6U. Their low cost and standardized form factor have democratized space access for universities and small organizations.

What is a Lagrange point?

Lagrange points are five positions in a two-body gravitational system (such as the Sun-Earth system) where the combined gravitational pull of the two large masses provides exactly the centripetal force needed for a small object to orbit with them. The James Webb Space Telescope operates at the Sun-Earth L2 point.

What is ion propulsion?

Ion propulsion (also called ion drive) works by ionizing a propellant such as xenon gas and accelerating the ions using electric fields to produce thrust. While the thrust is very small, it is extremely fuel-efficient and can operate continuously for months or years, as demonstrated by NASA's Dawn spacecraft.

What is a solar sail?

A solar sail is a spacecraft propulsion technology that uses a large, lightweight, reflective surface to harness the momentum of photons from sunlight. Though the force is tiny, it is continuous and requires no fuel, making it ideal for long-duration deep-space missions.

What is the Deep Space Network (DSN)?

The Deep Space Network is NASA's international network of large radio antennas located at three sites spaced roughly 120 degrees apart around the globe (Goldstone in California, Madrid in Spain, and Canberra in Australia), ensuring continuous communication with spacecraft anywhere in the solar system.

What is the definition of 'space debris'?

Space debris (also called orbital debris or space junk) consists of all human-made objects in Earth orbit that no longer serve a function, including defunct satellites, discarded rocket stages, paint flakes, and fragments from collisions or explosions. There are currently millions of tracked and untracked pieces of debris posing collision risks.

Real-World Applications

Example: Voyager 2 used gravity assists from which planets during its grand tour?

Voyager 2, launched in 1977, exploited a rare planetary alignment that occurs once every 175 years to perform gravity assists at Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune, becoming the only spacecraft to visit all four outer planets.

Example: A telecommunications company wants to provide continuous TV broadcast coverage to customers in Brazil. The company needs its satellite to appear stationary over a fixed point above the equator so that customers' dish antennas never need to move. Which orbit should the company select for its satellite?

Geostationary orbit (GEO) at about 35,786 km is the correct choice because a satellite there has a 24-hour orbital period matching Earth's rotation, causing it to appear stationary over a fixed equatorial point so ground-based dishes can maintain a constant, uninterrupted signal.

Example: An environmental scientist wants to monitor deforestation in the Amazon rainforest with high-resolution satellite imagery updated every few weeks. She needs a satellite that flies low enough for detailed images and passes over the same areas with consistent lighting. Which type of orbit best meets her requirements?

A sun-synchronous polar orbit in LEO is ideal for Earth observation because the satellite passes over every part of the Earth's surface at the same local solar time, ensuring consistent lighting conditions for comparative imaging, and the low altitude provides high-resolution imagery.

Example: A space agency is concerned about the growing amount of orbital debris threatening its operational satellites. An analyst warns that if a collision between two large defunct objects occurs, the resulting fragments could trigger a chain reaction of further collisions. What phenomenon is the analyst describing?

The analyst is describing the Kessler syndrome, in which one collision generates debris that causes additional collisions in a cascading chain reaction, potentially rendering heavily used orbital zones like LEO too hazardous for satellites and crewed spacecraft.

Example: A space agency is planning a mission to fly a lightweight probe to a nearby star system over several decades. The probe will be too far from the Sun for solar panels to generate meaningful power, and chemical fuel would be far too heavy for such a long journey. A mission designer proposes using a large reflective membrane deployed in space to accelerate the probe using sunlight before it leaves the inner solar system. What propulsion technology is the designer proposing?

The designer is proposing a solar sail, which uses the radiation pressure of sunlight pushing on a large, thin, reflective membrane to accelerate a spacecraft. Since solar sails require no fuel, they are being studied as a potential technology for interstellar precursor missions, such as the Breakthrough Starshot concept.
