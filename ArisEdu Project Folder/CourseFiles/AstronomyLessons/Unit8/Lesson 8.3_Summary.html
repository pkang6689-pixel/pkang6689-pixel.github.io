Lesson 8.3: Search for Extraterrestrial Life - Summary

Key Concepts: Search for Extraterrestrial Life

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is unique about the Perseverance rover's mission compared to previous Mars rovers?

Perseverance's unique contribution is collecting and caching carefully selected rock and soil samples in sealed tubes that a future Mars Sample Return mission will retrieve and bring back to Earth, allowing scientists to analyze them with advanced laboratory instruments.

What type of vehicle will the Dragonfly mission send to Titan, and what is its purpose?

Dragonfly is a rotorcraft lander (drone) that will fly to multiple locations across Titan's surface, sampling and analyzing the moon's organic-rich terrain to study prebiotic chemistry and assess the potential for life in Titan's unique methane-cycle environment.

What is the Mars Sample Return (MSR) mission?

Mars Sample Return is an ambitious planned campaign involving NASA and ESA that would collect the sample tubes cached by the Perseverance rover on Mars, launch them into Mars orbit, and transport them back to Earth where they can be studied with laboratory instruments far more sophisticated than any that can fit on a rover.

What is transit spectroscopy?

Transit spectroscopy works by measuring how starlight is selectively absorbed at specific wavelengths as it passes through the thin atmospheric layer of an exoplanet crossing in front of its star, creating a spectral fingerprint that reveals which molecules are present in the atmosphere.

What is an atmospheric biosignature?

An atmospheric biosignature is a detectable gas or combination of gases in a planet's atmosphere that is best explained by biological processes, such as the simultaneous presence of oxygen and methane, which would normally react and disappear without a biological source continuously replenishing them.

What is the 'follow the water' strategy in planetary exploration?

The 'follow the water' strategy is NASA's guiding principle for astrobiological exploration, directing missions to prioritize sites where liquid water existed in the past or may exist today, because all known life on Earth requires liquid water.

What is astrobiology?

Astrobiology is a broad interdisciplinary field that combines biology, chemistry, geology, physics, and astronomy to investigate questions about the origin of life, the possibility of life beyond Earth, and the future of life in the universe.

What is planetary protection?

Planetary protection encompasses policies and procedures designed to prevent Earth organisms from contaminating other worlds (forward contamination) and to prevent any potential extraterrestrial organisms from reaching Earth (backward contamination), ensuring the scientific integrity of life-detection missions.

What is the Viking biology experiment?

The Viking landers carried three biology experiments that tested Martian soil for signs of microbial metabolism in 1976. The Labeled Release experiment produced a positive-like result, but the absence of organic molecules detected by another instrument led most scientists to conclude the results were caused by chemical rather than biological reactions, though the debate continues.

NASA's Perseverance rover has collected and sealed dozens of rock and soil samples in tubes deposited on the Martian surface. A future Mars Sample Return mission is being planned to bring these tubes back to Earth. A student asks why scientists cannot simply analyze the samples on Mars using the rover's own instruments. What is the best explanation?

While Perseverance carries advanced instruments, they are constrained by the size, weight, and power limitations of a rover. Earth-based laboratories contain room-sized instruments like synchrotrons and mass spectrometers capable of far more sensitive and detailed analysis that could conclusively determine whether Martian samples contain biosignatures.

A spacecraft mission to Enceladus is proposed that would fly through the moon's water vapor plumes and analyze the ejected material with onboard instruments. During a review, an engineer asks why Enceladus is preferred over drilling through Europa's thicker ice shell. What is the strongest scientific advantage of the Enceladus plume flythrough approach?

Enceladus's geysers continuously eject water, ice particles, salts, and organic molecules from its subsurface ocean into space, providing a natural delivery system that allows a spacecraft to sample ocean material simply by flying through the plumes, avoiding the enormous engineering challenge of landing on and drilling through kilometers of ice.

A planetary protection officer is reviewing a proposed mission to collect samples from Enceladus's water plumes and return them to Earth. She raises concerns about backward contamination. What is she worried about, and why is this a legitimate concern?

Backward contamination is the risk that extraterrestrial organisms brought to Earth could interact with and potentially harm our biosphere in unpredictable ways. Planetary protection protocols require that any sample return mission from a potentially habitable world include rigorous containment systems to prevent any extraterrestrial material from escaping into Earth's environment.

If microbial life were confirmed on Mars -- even as fossilized remains in ancient rock -- scientists say it would be one of the most significant discoveries in human history. A student asks why finding mere microbes would matter so much when we have trillions of microbes on Earth. What is the most compelling reason this discovery would be transformative?

Finding even simple microbial life on Mars would be revolutionary because it would prove that the origin of life is not a singular, unrepeatable event unique to Earth. If life arose independently on two planets in the same solar system, it would imply that life may be common throughout the universe.

A mission proposal suggests sending a submarine to explore the subsurface ocean of Europa by first landing on the surface, melting through the ice shell, and descending into the ocean below. While this is beyond current technology, the concept is being studied. What is the primary scientific motivation for such an ambitious mission?

A Europa submarine mission would allow direct exploration of one of the most promising habitable environments in the solar system, where a liquid water ocean meets a rocky seafloor possibly hosting hydrothermal vents -- conditions analogous to those where life thrives on Earth's deep ocean floor.
