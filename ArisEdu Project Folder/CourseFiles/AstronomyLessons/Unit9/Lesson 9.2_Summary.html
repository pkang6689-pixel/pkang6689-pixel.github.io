Lesson 9.2: Space Telescopes (Hubble, James Webb) - Summary

Key Concepts: Space Telescopes (Hubble & JWST)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the diameter of Hubble's primary mirror?

Hubble has a 2.4-meter primary mirror, which is relatively modest compared to ground-based telescopes but achieves outstanding resolution because it operates above Earth's atmosphere without any atmospheric blurring.

What is a Lagrange point?

A Lagrange point is one of five positions in an orbital system (such as the Sun-Earth system) where gravitational and centrifugal forces balance, allowing a spacecraft to orbit in a fixed position relative to both bodies. JWST orbits at L2, about 1.5 million km from Earth.

What is spherical aberration?

Spherical aberration occurs when a mirror or lens is not shaped perfectly, causing light from the edges to focus at a different point than light from the center. Hubble's initial mirror had this flaw, which was corrected by the COSTAR instrument installed in 1993.

What is transit spectroscopy?

Transit spectroscopy is performed when an exoplanet crosses in front of its host star. A small fraction of the starlight passes through the planet's atmosphere, and by analyzing which wavelengths are absorbed, astronomers can identify molecules like water vapor, CO2, and methane.

What is the Hubble Deep Field?

The Hubble Deep Field was a groundbreaking observation in 1995 where Hubble stared at a tiny, apparently blank patch of sky for ten consecutive days. The resulting image revealed nearly 3,000 galaxies at various distances and ages, fundamentally changing our understanding of the universe's structure.

What is a sunshield in the context of JWST?

JWST's sunshield is a five-layer structure roughly the size of a tennis court, made of Kapton with aluminum and silicon coatings. It provides a temperature difference of about 300 Kelvin between its sun-facing and shade sides, keeping the instruments cold enough to detect faint infrared light.

What is COSTAR?

COSTAR (Corrective Optics Space Telescope Axial Replacement) was a set of small corrective mirrors installed during Servicing Mission 1 in December 1993 that compensated for the flaw in Hubble's primary mirror, restoring the telescope to its intended sharp focus.

What is the cosmic distance ladder?

The cosmic distance ladder is a succession of methods by which astronomers determine distances, starting with parallax for nearby stars, then using Cepheid variables and Type Ia supernovae for progressively greater distances. Hubble's observations of Cepheids in distant galaxies were critical to calibrating this ladder.

A news headline claims that JWST has detected galaxies that formed only 300 million years after the Big Bang, far earlier than previously thought possible. A student asks how a telescope can look back in time. What is the correct explanation?

Because light travels at a finite speed (about 300,000 km/s), the light from a galaxy 13 billion light-years away took 13 billion years to reach us. We therefore see that galaxy as it appeared 13 billion years ago, allowing astronomers to study the universe's history by observing progressively more distant objects.

Real-World Applications

Example: A spacecraft engineer is designing a new infrared space telescope and must decide where to place it. The telescope's detectors must be kept extremely cold to avoid self-generated infrared noise. A colleague suggests placing it at the Sun-Earth L2 point. Why is L2 advantageous for infrared observatories?

At the Sun-Earth L2 point, the Sun, Earth, and Moon are all in roughly the same direction from the telescope, meaning a single sunshield can block all major heat sources simultaneously. The opposite side faces the cold of deep space, allowing passive cooling to the extremely low temperatures infrared detectors require.

Example: A student compares images of the same nebula taken by Hubble (visible light) and JWST (infrared). The Hubble image shows glowing gas with dark opaque regions, while the JWST image reveals newborn stars hidden within those dark regions. Why can JWST see what Hubble cannot in this case?

Cosmic dust strongly absorbs and scatters visible light but is largely transparent to infrared radiation. Since JWST observes in the infrared, it can peer through dense dust clouds to reveal star-forming regions, protoplanetary disks, and galactic centers that are completely obscured at visible wavelengths.

Example: NASA is planning the next generation of space telescopes after JWST. One proposal, the Habitable Worlds Observatory (HWO), aims to directly image Earth-like exoplanets and search for biosignature gases in their atmospheres. Based on what Hubble and JWST have demonstrated, why is a space-based platform essential for this goal?

Directly imaging an Earth-like exoplanet next to its star requires blocking the star's light to a contrast ratio of about one to ten billion, which atmospheric turbulence makes extremely difficult from the ground. Additionally, key biosignature gases like ozone and water have absorption features at wavelengths partially or fully blocked by Earth's atmosphere.
