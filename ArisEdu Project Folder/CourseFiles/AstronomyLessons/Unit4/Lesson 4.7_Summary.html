Lesson 4.7: AP Prep: Energy Calculations - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Energy Calculations

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is luminosity?

Luminosity is the intrinsic total power output, independent of distance, measured in watts or solar luminosities.

What is the Stefan-Boltzmann law?

The Stefan-Boltzmann law connects a star's luminosity to its surface area and the fourth power of its temperature.

What is Wien's displacement law?

Wien's law explains why hotter stars are blue and cooler stars are red — higher temperature shifts peak emission to shorter wavelengths.

What is the inverse square law?

The inverse square law: I = L/(4πr²), applies to light, gravity, and other phenomena radiating from a point source.

What is a blackbody?

A blackbody is a theoretical perfect emitter/absorber; stars approximate blackbodies, and their spectra follow Planck's law.

What is flux?

Flux (F = L/4πr²) measures how much energy from a star reaches each square meter at a given distance.

What is the mass defect?

The mass defect represents the binding energy: the 'missing' mass has been converted to energy holding the nucleus together.

What is the Kelvin temperature scale?

Kelvin is the SI unit of temperature, essential in astrophysics because many laws use absolute temperature.

What is energy conservation?

In stellar physics, energy conservation means fusion energy equals the sum of radiation, neutrino energy, and kinetic energy.

What is a joule?

One joule = 1 kg⋅m²/s². The Sun's luminosity is about 3.8 × 10²⁶ joules per second.

Using Wien's law (b = 2.898 × 10⁻³ m·K), what is the peak wavelength of the Sun's emission at 5,800 K?

λmax = 2.898 × 10⁻³ / 5,800 = 4.996 × 10⁻⁷ m ≈ 500 nm, in the green-yellow part of the visible spectrum.

A star has radius 2R☉ and temperature 2T☉. What is its luminosity relative to the Sun?

L = σ(4πR²)T⁴. Relative: (2)² × (2)⁴ = 4 × 16 = 64 times solar luminosity.
