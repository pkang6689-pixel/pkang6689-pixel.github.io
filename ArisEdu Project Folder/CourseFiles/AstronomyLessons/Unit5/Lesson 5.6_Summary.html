Lesson 5.6: Supernovae & Neutron Stars - Summary

Key Concepts: Supernovae & Neutron Stars

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a supernova?

Supernovae can outshine entire galaxies for weeks, releasing enormous energy.

What is a supernova remnant?

Supernova remnants like the Crab Nebula expand for thousands of years, enriching the interstellar medium.

What is core collapse?

Core collapse happens in seconds when the iron core exceeds ~1.4 M☉ and electron degeneracy fails.

What is a pulsar?

Pulsars were first discovered in 1967; their incredibly regular pulses initially puzzled astronomers.

What is neutron degeneracy pressure?

Like electron degeneracy in white dwarfs, neutron degeneracy arises from the Pauli exclusion principle applied to neutrons.

What is the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit?

The TOV limit is the neutron star equivalent of the Chandrasekhar limit for white dwarfs.

What is nucleosynthesis?

Stellar nucleosynthesis creates elements up to iron through fusion; supernovae create heavier elements.

What is a magnetar?

Magnetars have the strongest magnetic fields known, capable of producing intense bursts of X-rays and gamma rays.

What is the r-process?

The r-process produces about half of all elements heavier than iron, including gold, platinum, and uranium.

What is a supernova light curve?

Light curves show the rise, peak, and decline of a supernova's brightness, revealing its type and energy.

What is a neutron star merger?

Neutron star mergers (kilonovae) were first observed via gravitational waves in 2017 (GW170817).

Astronomers detect regular radio pulses every 0.033 seconds from a source in the Crab Nebula. What is it?

The Crab Pulsar rotates ~30 times per second, producing millisecond-period pulses.

An astronomer notices that a pulsar's period is gradually increasing. What is this called and what causes it?

Isolated pulsars gradually slow as they radiate energy via their magnetic field (`pulsar braking`).
