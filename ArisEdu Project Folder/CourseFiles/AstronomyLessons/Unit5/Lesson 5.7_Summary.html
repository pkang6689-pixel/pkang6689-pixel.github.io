Lesson 5.7: Black Holes - Summary

Key Concepts: Black Holes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the event horizon?

The event horizon defines the point of no return; outside it, escape is possible — inside, it is not.

What is the singularity?

Classical general relativity predicts a singularity, but quantum gravity (not yet developed) may modify this picture.

What is an accretion disk?

Accretion disks convert gravitational energy to thermal energy, making them among the most luminous objects in the universe.

What is gravitational lensing?

Predicted by general relativity, gravitational lensing can magnify, distort, or multiply images of background objects.

What is spaghettification?

Near a black hole, the difference in gravitational pull between near and far sides stretches objects into thin strands.

What is the Schwarzschild radius?

For a 3 M☉ black hole, R_s ≈ 9 km; for a supermassive black hole of 4 million M☉, R_s ≈ 12 million km.

What is a quasar?

Quasars can outshine their entire host galaxy, visible across billions of light-years.

What is an active galactic nucleus (AGN)?

AGN encompass quasars, Seyfert galaxies, and blazars — all powered by supermassive black hole accretion.

What is time dilation near a black hole?

A clock near a black hole ticks more slowly relative to a distant observer — an effect of gravitational time dilation.

An astronomer observes a star orbiting an invisible massive object with a period of 16 years near the Milky Way's center. What is the object?

The star S2 orbits Sgr A* with a 16-year period, providing one of the best measurements of the central black hole's mass.

The mass of Sgr A* is approximately 4 million solar masses. What is its Schwarzschild radius in solar radii (1 R☉ ≈ 700,000 km)?

R_s = 2GM/c² ≈ 3 km × 4 × 10⁶ = 12 × 10⁶ km ≈ 17 R☉. The event horizon would fit within Mercury's orbit.
