Lesson 5.8: Case Studies in Stellar Observation - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Stellar Observation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a red supergiant?

Red supergiants like Betelgeuse have radii hundreds of times the Sun's and are nearing the ends of their lives.

What is spectroscopy?

Spectroscopy is fundamental to astrophysics, revealing chemical composition, temperature, density, and velocity of stars.

What is a binary star system?

Many stars exist in binary or multiple systems; studying their orbits allows mass determination.

What is the transit method?

The transit method has discovered the majority of known exoplanets, providing planetary radii and orbital periods.

What is an eclipsing binary?

Eclipsing binaries allow precise measurement of stellar radii, masses, and temperatures.

What is a light curve?

Light curves reveal variability patterns: pulsation periods, eclipsing binary orbits, exoplanet transits, and supernova evolution.

What is proper motion?

Proper motion is measured in arcseconds per year and indicates how a star physically moves through space.

What is radial velocity?

Radial velocity is measured through blueshifts (approaching) or redshifts (receding) of spectral lines.

What is a stellar wind?

All stars produce stellar winds; massive and evolved stars can lose significant mass through strong winds.

What is astrometry?

Astrometry has been revolutionized by Gaia, providing positions accurate to microarcseconds for over a billion stars.

An astronomer finds a star with a slight periodic wobble in its radial velocity. What is the most likely cause?

The radial velocity method detects exoplanets by measuring the star's reflex motion caused by the planet's gravity.
