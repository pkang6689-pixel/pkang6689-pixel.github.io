Lesson 3.6: Planetary Atmospheres - Summary

Key Concepts: Planetary Atmospheres

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the 'troposphere'?

The troposphere extends from the surface up to about 12 km on Earth and is where nearly all weather phenomena occur.

What is the 'stratosphere'?

The stratosphere extends from ~12-50 km and contains the ozone layer; temperature increases with altitude due to UV absorption.

What is 'atmospheric escape'?

Atmospheric escape occurs through thermal escape, solar wind stripping, and other processes — Mars lost much of its atmosphere this way.

What is 'escape velocity' in the context of atmospheres?

If gas molecules gain enough thermal speed to exceed escape velocity, they leave the planet permanently — lighter molecules escape more easily.

What is 'atmospheric pressure'?

Atmospheric pressure at Earth's surface averages ~101.3 kPa (1 atm), created by the weight of the atmosphere above.

What is an 'exosphere'?

The exosphere is where the atmosphere is so thin that molecules can travel without collisions and potentially escape.

What is 'adiabatic lapse rate'?

The adiabatic lapse rate is typically ~6.5°C/km on Earth — rising air expands and cools at this predictable rate.

What is 'thermal inversion'?

Temperature inversions occur when warm air sits above cooler air, preventing vertical mixing — common in the stratosphere.

What is 'sputtering' in atmospheric science?

Sputtering occurs when high-energy solar wind particles impact atmospheric atoms, giving them enough energy to escape.

What is 'Rayleigh scattering'?

Rayleigh scattering preferentially scatters shorter (blue) wavelengths of light, making Earth's sky appear blue.
