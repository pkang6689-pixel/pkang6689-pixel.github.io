Lesson 3.1: Formation of the Solar System - Summary

Key Concepts: Formation of the Solar System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'solar nebula'?

The solar nebula was the primordial cloud that collapsed under gravity to form our solar system ~4.6 billion years ago.

What is 'accretion'?

Accretion is how dust grains → pebbles → boulders → planetesimals → planets gradually built up through collisions.

What is a 'protoplanetary disk'?

A protoplanetary disk is the flattened disk of material orbiting a newborn star, where planets eventually coalesce.

What is a 'planetesimal'?

Planetesimals are intermediate-sized objects that formed early and merged to create planets.

What is the 'frost line'?

The frost line (~3 AU from the Sun) marks where temperatures were cool enough for ices to form in the solar nebula.

What is 'differentiation' in planetary science?

Differentiation occurs when a planet is hot enough for dense materials to sink to the center and lighter materials to rise, forming layers.

What is a 'protoplanet'?

Protoplanets are large embryonic planets — bigger than planetesimals but not yet full-sized planets.

What is 'angular momentum'?

Angular momentum (L = mvr for orbits) is conserved, which is why the solar nebula spun faster as it contracted.

All planets orbit the Sun in the same direction and roughly the same plane. A student asks why. What is the explanation?

The uniform direction and near-planar orbits reflect the original rotation and flattened shape of the protoplanetary disk.
