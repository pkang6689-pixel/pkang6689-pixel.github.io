Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Space Exploration - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Space Exploration

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'flyby' mission?

Flyby missions use gravity assists and pass through a target's vicinity, collecting data during the brief encounter.

What is an 'orbiter'?

Orbiters like Cassini and Juno circle their targets for extended periods, providing long-term study.

What is a 'lander'?

Landers (like Viking, InSight, Huygens) are designed to survive touchdown and operate on a body's surface.

What is a 'rover'?

Rovers (Curiosity, Perseverance, Zhurong) can traverse terrain, reaching multiple study sites.

What is a 'gravity assist'?

Gravity assists (slingshots) allow spacecraft to gain or lose speed by flying close to a planet, conserving fuel.

What is 'aerobraking'?

Aerobraking passes through the upper atmosphere to create drag, gradually lowering and circularizing an orbit.

What is a 'sample return mission'?

Sample return missions (e.g., OSIRIS-REx, Stardust, Hayabusa) bring extraterrestrial material to Earth's laboratories.

What is 'telemetry'?

Telemetry provides mission controllers with spacecraft status, science data, and health information via radio signals.

What is the 'Deep Space Network' (DSN)?

The DSN has three complexes (Goldstone, Madrid, Canberra) spaced ~120° apart to maintain 24/7 contact with distant missions.

What is 'in situ' analysis?

In situ (Latin: 'in place') analysis means studying materials right where they are, rather than returning samples.

Real-World Applications

Example: The Voyager missions revealed active volcanoes on which moon?

Voyager 1's 1979 flyby of Jupiter discovered active volcanic eruptions on Io — the first active extraterrestrial volcanoes found.

Example: Voyager 1 and 2 carry golden records with sounds and images of Earth. Why was this included?

Carl Sagan led the effort to include a cultural snapshot of Earth, understanding the craft might travel for millions of years.
