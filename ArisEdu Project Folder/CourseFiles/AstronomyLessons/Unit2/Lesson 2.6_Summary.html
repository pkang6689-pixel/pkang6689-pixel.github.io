Lesson 2.6: Case Studies in Earth-Moon-Sun Interactions - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Earth-Moon-Sun Interactions

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the 'Giant Impact Hypothesis'?

The Giant Impact Hypothesis (Theia impact) is the leading explanation for the Moon's origin.

What is 'laser ranging'?

Lunar laser ranging bounces laser pulses off Moon retroreflectors to measure the Earth-Moon distance to centimeter precision.

What is a 'retroreflector'?

Retroreflectors (corner-cube reflectors) bounce light precisely back toward its source, enabling accurate distance measurements.

What is the 'Theia' hypothesis about?

The hypothetical planetary embryo that struck Earth is named Theia, after the Greek goddess who was the mother of Selene (the Moon).

What is 'tidal evolution'?

Tidal evolution describes how tidal forces slowly modify the orbits and spin rates of interacting bodies over geological time.

What is 'gravitational lensing'?

Gravitational lensing occurs when a massive object bends the path of light passing near it, confirmed during the 1919 eclipse.

What is an 'eclipse season'?

Eclipse seasons occur about every 6 months when the Sun is near one of the Moon's orbital nodes.

What is 'obliquity stabilization'?

The Moon acts as a gravitational anchor, keeping Earth's axial tilt relatively stable at ~23.5° over long timescales.

What is a 'synchronous orbit'?

The Moon is in synchronous rotation — its rotation period equals its orbital period, so the same face always points at Earth.

A student asks: 'If the Moon keeps moving away, will it eventually escape Earth's gravity?' What is the answer?

The Moon's recession rate slows as it moves farther out, and the Sun's evolution into a red giant (~5 billion years) will intervene first.
