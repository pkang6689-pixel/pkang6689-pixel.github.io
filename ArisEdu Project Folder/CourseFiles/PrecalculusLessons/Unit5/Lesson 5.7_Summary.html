Lesson 5.7: Graphing Exponential & Logarithmic Functions - Summary

Key Concepts: Graphing Exponential & Logarithmic Functions

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the horizontal asymptote of y = 2ˣ?

As x → −∞, 2ˣ → 0. The x-axis (y = 0) is the horizontal asymptote.

What is the horizontal asymptote of y = 3ˣ − 2?

The graph of 3ˣ is shifted down 2 units, so the horizontal asymptote moves from y = 0 to y = −2.

What is the vertical asymptote of y = log(x)?

The logarithm is undefined for x ≤ 0. As x → 0⁺, log(x) → −∞, creating a vertical asymptote at x = 0.

What is the domain of y = ln(x − 3)?

The argument must be positive: x − 3 > 0, so x > 3.

What is the y-intercept of y = 5 · 2ˣ?

At x = 0: y = 5 · 2⁰ = 5 · 1 = 5.

What is the range of y = log₂(x)?

Logarithmic functions can output any real number. As x → 0⁺, y → −∞, and as x → ∞, y → ∞. The range is (−∞, ∞).

What is the relationship between the graphs of y = log(x) and y = 10ˣ?

log₁₀ and 10ˣ are inverse functions. Their graphs are reflections of each other across the line y = x.

What is the range of y = 3ˣ?

Since 3ˣ is always positive and never equals zero, the range is (0, ∞), or y > 0.

A teacher asks students to identify the VA of y = ln(x − 3). A student says x = 0. What is the correct VA?

The VA occurs where the argument equals zero: x − 3 = 0, so x = 3. The shift moves the VA from x = 0 to x = 3.

A statistics student needs to determine the domain of y = log₂(5 − x) for a data transformation. What is the domain?

The argument must be positive: 5 − x > 0, so x < 5. The domain is (−∞, 5) and the VA is at x = 5.
