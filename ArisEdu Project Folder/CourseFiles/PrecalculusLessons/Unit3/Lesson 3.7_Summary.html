Lesson 3.7: Rational Root Theorem - Summary

Key Concepts: Rational Root Theorem

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the relationship between the Rational Root Theorem and irrational zeros?

The Rational Root Theorem only generates rational candidates. Irrational zeros (such as √2 or √3) will not appear on the list and must be found by other methods.

What is the 'upper bound' rule in the context of testing zeros?

The upper bound theorem states that if you perform synthetic division with a positive c and all resulting coefficients are non-negative, then no real zero is greater than c.

An engineer models the load capacity of a beam as L(x) = 2x³ − 3x² − 11x + 6. After testing candidates from the Rational Root Theorem, she finds L(3) = 0. What is the reduced polynomial after dividing by (x − 3)?

Synthetic division with c = 3: [2, −3, −11, 6] gives quotient 2x² + 3x − 2 with remainder 0.

An economist models GDP growth as G(x) = 4x³ + 8x² − x − 2, where x is in years. She applies the Rational Root Theorem with p dividing −2 and q dividing 4. After testing x = 1/2, she finds G(1/2) = 4(1/8) + 8(1/4) − 1/2 − 2 = 0.5 + 2 − 0.5 − 2 = 0. What is the complete factorization?

After dividing by (x − 1/2), or equivalently factoring out (2x − 1), we get 4x³ + 8x² − x − 2 = (2x − 1)(2x² + 5x + 2) = (2x − 1)(2x + 1)(x + 2).

Real-World Applications

Example: A pharmaceutical company models drug effectiveness as E(d) = 6d³ − 11d² − 3d + 2, where d is dosage in mg. Using the Rational Root Theorem, the possible rational dosages where effectiveness is zero include p/q where p divides 2 and q divides 6. Which is an actual zero?

E(2) = 6(8) − 11(4) − 3(2) + 2 = 48 − 44 − 6 + 2 = 0. So d = 2 mg is a zero.
