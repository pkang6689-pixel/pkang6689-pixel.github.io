Lesson 3.2: End Behavior & Graph Shapes - Summary

Key Concepts: End Behavior & Graph Shapes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the meaning of 'even-degree polynomial' with respect to end behavior?

An even-degree polynomial has matching end behavior on both sides: both ends rise if the leading coefficient is positive, or both fall if negative.

What is a 'quintic' polynomial?

A quintic polynomial is one of degree 5, meaning the highest power of the variable is 5.

A meteorologist models temperature over a 24-hour period as T(t) = −0.01t⁴ + 0.5t³ − 7t² + 30t + 50, where t is hours after midnight and T is in degrees Fahrenheit. Based on the leading term, what is the end behavior as t grows large?

The leading term is −0.01t⁴ (even degree, negative coefficient), so both ends fall to −∞.

A physics student launches a model rocket whose altitude profile is well-approximated by h(t) = −0.5t⁴ + 8t³ − 36t² + 48t for 0 ≤ t ≤ 6. Based on the degree, what is the maximum number of times the rocket can be at ground level (h = 0)?

A degree-4 polynomial can have at most 4 real zeros, meaning the rocket can be at ground level at most 4 times.

A marine biologist notices that a dolphin pod's population over 10 years follows a pattern: increases, decreases, then increases again. What is the minimum degree polynomial needed to model this behavior?

The pattern increase-decrease-increase requires 2 turning points. A polynomial needs degree at least n + 1 = 3 to have n = 2 turning points.

A civil engineer observes that a suspension bridge cable follows a curve with both ends rising and three turning points. What is the minimum degree polynomial that could model this cable?

Three turning points requires at least degree 4 (since at most n − 1 turning points). Both ends rising requires an even degree with positive leading coefficient, and degree 4 fits.

A theme park ride designer needs a polynomial track profile that falls on the left end, has 4 turning points, and rises on the right end. What is the minimum degree polynomial that could satisfy all three conditions?

Falls left and rises right means opposite end behaviors, requiring odd degree with positive leading coefficient. Four turning points requires at least degree 5 (since at most n − 1 turning points). Degree 5 satisfies all conditions.
