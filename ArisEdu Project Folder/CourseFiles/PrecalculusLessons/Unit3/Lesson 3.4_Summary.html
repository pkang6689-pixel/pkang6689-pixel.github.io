Lesson 3.4: Roots, Zeros, and Multiplicity - Summary

Key Concepts: Roots, Zeros, and Multiplicity

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the multiplicity of x = 3 in f(x) = (x − 3)²(x + 1)?

The factor (x − 3) appears squared, so x = 3 has multiplicity 2.

What is the degree of f(x) = x(x − 1)³(x + 2)²?

Sum the multiplicities: 1 + 3 + 2 = 6.

What is a 'zero' of a polynomial?

A zero (also called a root) of a polynomial f(x) is any value c such that f(c) = 0.

What is a 'repeated root' or 'repeated zero'?

A repeated root is a zero whose corresponding factor appears more than once, giving it a multiplicity of 2 or more.

What is the meaning of 'distinct zeros' versus 'zeros counted with multiplicity'?

Distinct zeros counts each unique zero value once. Counting with multiplicity counts a zero as many times as its multiplicity. For example, (x − 1)²(x + 2) has 2 distinct zeros but 3 zeros counted with multiplicity.

A data analyst models website traffic as T(d) = d²(d − 7)²(d − 14) for days d = 0 through 14. What is the total degree of this polynomial?

The degree is the sum of all multiplicities: 2 + 2 + 1 = 5.

A quality engineer determines that a production defect polynomial is f(x) = x³ − 3x² + 3x − 1. After factoring, all defects occur at a single production rate. What is the zero and its multiplicity?

x³ − 3x² + 3x − 1 = (x − 1)³. There is a single zero at x = 1 with multiplicity 3.

A financial model for bond prices is P(r) = (r − 0.02)²(r − 0.05)(r − 0.08)² where r is the interest rate. How many distinct interest rates give a price of zero, and what is the sum of all multiplicities?

There are 3 distinct zeros: r = 0.02 (mult 2), r = 0.05 (mult 1), and r = 0.08 (mult 2). The sum of multiplicities is 2 + 1 + 2 = 5, which equals the degree.
