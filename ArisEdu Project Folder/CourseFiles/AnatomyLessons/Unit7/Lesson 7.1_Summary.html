Lesson 7.1: Anatomy of the Respiratory System - Summary

Key Concepts: Anatomy of the Respiratory System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the respiratory membrane?

The respiratory membrane is the extremely thin barrier (approximately 0.5 µm) separating alveolar air from pulmonary capillary blood, consisting of the alveolar epithelium, fused basement membranes, and capillary endothelium.

What is surfactant?

Surfactant is a phospholipid-rich substance secreted by Type II alveolar cells that lowers alveolar surface tension, preventing the small alveoli from collapsing during expiration and improving lung compliance.

What is the pleura?

The pleura consists of two layers — the visceral pleura adhering to the lung surface and the parietal pleura lining the thoracic wall — with a thin film of serous fluid between them that reduces friction during breathing.

What is the hilum of the lung?

The hilum (root of the lung) is an indentation on the mediastinal surface of each lung through which the primary bronchus, pulmonary arteries and veins, bronchial vessels, lymphatics, and nerves pass.

What is the mucociliary escalator?

The mucociliary escalator is the defense mechanism in which cilia on respiratory epithelial cells beat in coordinated waves to propel the mucus blanket — along with trapped particles and microorganisms — upward to the pharynx, where it is swallowed or expectorated.

What is the cardiac notch?

The cardiac notch is a concavity on the left lung's anterior-medial border that makes room for the heart, which is why the left lung is slightly smaller and has only two lobes compared to the right lung's three.

An anatomy student notices on a cadaver that the right primary bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left. A classmate asks why aspirated foreign objects more commonly lodge in the right lung. What is the best anatomical explanation?

The right primary bronchus is anatomically wider, shorter, and more vertical compared to the left, creating a more direct pathway from the trachea. This is why aspirated foreign bodies, food particles, and fluids preferentially enter the right lung.

A medical student is studying a cross-section of the trachea and notices the posterior wall is soft and flexible, lacking cartilage. A classmate asks why this design is advantageous. What is the best explanation?

The C-shaped cartilage rings are open posteriorly, where the trachealis smooth muscle forms a flexible wall. This allows the esophagus, which lies directly behind the trachea, to expand anteriorly when a bolus of food is swallowed.

A first-year medical student is examining a cadaver and notices that when she cuts the pleural membrane, the lung immediately retracts away from the chest wall. She asks the instructor to explain this observation. What is the anatomical basis?

The lungs are elastic organs that naturally tend to recoil inward. In life, negative pressure in the sealed pleural space counterbalances this recoil, keeping the lungs inflated against the chest wall. Once the pleural seal is broken, the elastic recoil causes the lung to collapse.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

In conditions like emphysema, destruction of alveolar walls and loss of elastic recoil in the distal airways (bronchioles and alveoli) trap air during expiration. Over time, chronic hyperinflation produces the characteristic barrel chest.
