Lesson 7.8: AP Prep: Respiratory Physiology - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Respiratory Physiology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is Fick's law of diffusion?

Fick's law of diffusion states: Diffusion rate = (Surface area x Partial pressure gradient x Solubility) / (Membrane thickness x Molecular weight). This equation explains why conditions that reduce surface area (emphysema), increase thickness (fibrosis), or decrease the pressure gradient (altitude) impair gas exchange.

What is the Haldane effect?

The Haldane effect is the complement of the Bohr effect: deoxygenated hemoglobin (at the tissues) binds CO₂ more readily, while oxygenated hemoglobin (at the lungs) releases CO₂ more easily. This facilitates CO₂ pickup at the tissues and CO₂ release at the lungs.

What is alveolar ventilation?

Alveolar ventilation (VA) represents the truly effective ventilation — the fresh air reaching the alveoli for gas exchange. It is calculated as VA = (TV - VD) x RR. For example: (500 mL - 150 mL) x 12 = 4,200 mL/min. This is the most physiologically meaningful ventilation measurement.

What is the ventilatory response to metabolic acidosis?

When metabolic acidosis lowers blood pH, peripheral and central chemoreceptors stimulate the respiratory center to increase ventilation. This hyperventilation (often appearing as deep, rapid Kussmaul breathing) rapidly lowers PaCO₂, reducing carbonic acid and partially compensating for the metabolic acid excess.

What is the 'alveolar gas equation' used for?

The alveolar gas equation (PAO₂ = FiO₂ x (Patm - PH₂O) - PaCO₂/R) calculates the expected partial pressure of oxygen in the alveoli. Comparing this to the measured arterial PaO₂ gives the A-a gradient, which helps distinguish between causes of hypoxemia.

An AP exam question presents a patient with the following ABG: pH 7.25, PaCO₂ 68 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 30 mEq/L. The student must determine the acid-base disturbance and whether compensation is occurring. What is the correct interpretation?

Step-by-step ABG interpretation: (1) pH 7.25 = acidosis; (2) PaCO₂ 68 = elevated, respiratory cause; (3) HCO₃⁻ 30 = elevated above normal (22-26), indicating the kidneys are retaining bicarbonate to compensate. Since pH is still abnormal, compensation is partial, not complete.

An AP exam question presents a patient with an ABG: pH 7.52, PaCO₂ 24 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 18 mEq/L. The student must identify the primary disorder and determine if compensation is occurring. What is the correct interpretation?

Interpretation: (1) pH 7.52 = alkalosis; (2) PaCO₂ 24 = low (respiratory cause — hyperventilation); (3) HCO₃⁻ 18 = below normal (22-26), indicating the kidneys are excreting bicarbonate to compensate. Since pH has not returned to normal range, this is partially compensated respiratory alkalosis.

An AP exam question asks the student to calculate alveolar ventilation for a patient with: tidal volume 450 mL, dead space 150 mL, respiratory rate 16/min. The student then must explain why this is more clinically relevant than minute ventilation. What is the alveolar ventilation and why does it matter more?

Alveolar ventilation = (TV - VD) x RR = (450 - 150) x 16 = 4,800 mL/min. While minute ventilation (450 x 16 = 7,200 mL/min) measures total air moved, alveolar ventilation is more clinically meaningful because it represents the air that actually participates in gas exchange — the air that determines PaO₂ and PaCO₂.

An AP exam scenario presents a trauma patient who develops fat embolism syndrome 48 hours after long bone fractures. The patient's PaO₂ drops to 50 mmHg despite 60% FiO₂, and the A-a gradient is significantly widened. The student must explain the pathophysiology. What is happening?

Fat embolism syndrome occurs when fat globules from fractured long bones enter the venous circulation and become trapped in pulmonary capillaries. They trigger inflammation and capillary endothelial damage, causing alveolar edema, V/Q mismatch, and intrapulmonary shunting. The widened A-a gradient confirms a gas exchange defect rather than simple hypoventilation.

Real-World Applications

Example: On the AP exam, a question shows two O₂-Hb dissociation curves — one shifted right relative to the other. The student is asked what conditions produce the right shift and its physiological significance. A patient exercising vigorously would demonstrate this shift because:

The right shift is a physiological optimization: during exercise, active muscles produce CO₂ (which lowers pH via the Bohr effect) and generate heat, both of which reduce hemoglobin's O₂ affinity. Additionally, increased glycolysis in RBCs elevates 2,3-BPG. The net effect is enhanced O₂ delivery precisely where metabolic demand is highest.

Example: A spirometry question on the AP exam presents a patient with: FVC 3.0 L (60% predicted) and FEV₁ 2.6 L (FEV₁/FVC = 87%). The student must identify the pattern. What does this spirometry pattern indicate?

In restrictive disease, lung volumes are reduced (FVC 60% of predicted) because the stiff lungs or limited chest wall cannot expand fully. However, the airways themselves are normal, so the patient can exhale what little air is present at a normal rate, producing a preserved or even elevated FEV₁/FVC ratio (87%).

Example: An AP exam clinical scenario describes a patient on a ventilator whose PaCO₂ is 60 mmHg (respiratory acidosis). The physician wants to correct the CO₂ retention. What ventilator adjustment would most directly lower PaCO₂?

PaCO₂ is directly determined by the ratio of CO₂ production to alveolar ventilation. To lower PaCO₂, the physician must increase alveolar ventilation. On a ventilator, this is achieved by increasing either the respiratory rate or the tidal volume (or both), which increases total minute ventilation and CO₂ elimination.

Example: An AP exam question asks about a patient at 3,000 meters altitude who has adapted over several weeks. Her blood work shows increased hemoglobin concentration and elevated 2,3-BPG levels. These adaptations help maintain oxygen delivery by:

At altitude, chronic hypoxia stimulates erythropoietin (EPO) release from the kidneys, increasing red blood cell production and hemoglobin concentration (polycythemia). Simultaneously, elevated 2,3-BPG reduces hemoglobin's O₂ affinity, ensuring more O₂ is released to tissues despite the lower PaO₂.

Example: An AP exam question presents a newborn with respiratory distress syndrome. The student must explain why the infant's lungs are difficult to inflate. Applying Fick's law and knowledge of surfactant, the best explanation is:

LaPlace's law states that the pressure needed to keep a small sphere open is proportional to surface tension and inversely proportional to radius. Without surfactant to reduce surface tension, small alveoli require enormous opening pressures and tend to collapse into larger ones. This reduces total gas exchange surface area (Fick's law) and causes severe hypoxemia.

Example: For the AP exam, a comprehensive question asks the student to explain why respiratory physiology cannot be studied in isolation from other organ systems. A patient with heart failure develops pulmonary edema and respiratory acidosis. How does this case illustrate the integration of cardiovascular, respiratory, and renal systems?

This case demonstrates systems integration: left heart failure increases pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure, causing fluid transudation into the alveoli (cardiovascular-respiratory link). The edema impairs gas exchange, causing hypoxemia and hypercapnia. The resulting respiratory acidosis triggers renal compensation through bicarbonate retention (respiratory-renal link). No organ system operates independently.
