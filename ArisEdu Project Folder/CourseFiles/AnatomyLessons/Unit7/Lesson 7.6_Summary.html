Lesson 7.6: Disorders of the Respiratory System - Summary

Key Concepts: Disorders of the Respiratory System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is COPD?

COPD is an umbrella term for chronic bronchitis and emphysema, both characterized by progressive, poorly reversible airflow limitation. The disease is usually caused by long-term exposure to lung irritants, most commonly cigarette smoke.

What is ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome)?

ARDS is a severe, acute inflammatory response in the lungs triggered by conditions such as sepsis, pneumonia, trauma, or aspiration. Massive capillary leak floods the alveoli with protein-rich fluid, producing refractory hypoxemia that requires ICU-level care and mechanical ventilation.

What is a pleural effusion?

A pleural effusion is the pathological accumulation of fluid in the pleural space. It can be transudative (from pressure changes, as in heart failure) or exudative (from inflammation, as in infection or malignancy), and large effusions can compress the lung and impair breathing.

What is atelectasis?

Atelectasis refers to the partial or complete collapse of lung tissue. It can be caused by airway obstruction (obstructive atelectasis), external compression (compressive atelectasis from pleural effusion or tumor), or loss of surfactant. It is common after surgery.

What is pulmonary fibrosis?

Pulmonary fibrosis involves the progressive replacement of normal lung tissue with scar tissue (collagen). The fibrotic lung becomes stiff (low compliance) and the thickened interstitium increases diffusion distance, resulting in a restrictive ventilatory pattern and impaired gas exchange.

What is consolidation on a chest X-ray?

Consolidation refers to lung tissue that has become solidified because the normally air-filled alveoli are filled with material (inflammatory exudate, pus, or fluid). On a chest X-ray, consolidated areas appear as dense white (opaque) regions against the normally dark, air-filled lung.

A patient is diagnosed with community-acquired pneumonia. His chest X-ray shows right lower lobe consolidation. Blood cultures and sputum cultures are sent, and the physician starts empiric antibiotics immediately without waiting for results. A medical student asks why treatment begins before the causative organism is identified. What is the rationale?

Empiric antibiotic therapy is started immediately because culture results take 24-72 hours. The initial antibiotic choice is guided by the most probable pathogens (e.g., S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae) based on clinical setting. Once culture and sensitivity data return, therapy can be narrowed to target the specific organism.

A physician tells a long-term smoker that quitting now, even after 30 years, would still provide significant health benefits. The patient is skeptical and asks if it is 'too late.' Based on respiratory disease prevention, what is the evidence-based response?

Evidence consistently shows that smoking cessation at any age provides measurable health benefits. The rate of lung function decline slows, cancer risk gradually decreases (though it remains elevated compared to never-smokers), and cardiovascular risk drops significantly within the first year of quitting.

A 40-year-old woman presents with gradually worsening shortness of breath. She has no history of smoking. Her spirometry shows FVC of 55% predicted with FEV₁/FVC of 85%. Her physician orders additional testing and finds significantly elevated alpha-1 antitrypsin levels are low. What is the connection between alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency and lung disease?

Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) is a protease inhibitor that normally protects lung tissue from neutrophil elastase. When AAT is deficient (genetic condition), unopposed elastase activity progressively destroys alveolar walls, producing panacinar emphysema at a much younger age than smoking-related emphysema, even in people who have never smoked.
