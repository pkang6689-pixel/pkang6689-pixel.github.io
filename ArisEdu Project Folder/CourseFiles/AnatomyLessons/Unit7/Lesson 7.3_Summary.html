Lesson 7.3: Gas Exchange in Alveoli - Summary

Key Concepts: Gas Exchange in the Alveoli

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is Dalton's law of partial pressures?

Dalton's law states that the total pressure exerted by a mixture of gases equals the sum of the pressures each gas would exert independently. This principle explains how individual gas partial pressures drive diffusion during gas exchange.

What is Henry's law?

Henry's law states that at a given temperature, the concentration of a gas dissolved in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid, multiplied by the gas's solubility coefficient. This explains why CO₂ dissolves more readily than O₂ in blood.

What is a V/Q mismatch known as 'dead space ventilation'?

Alveolar dead space occurs when alveoli are ventilated but not perfused, as happens with a pulmonary embolism blocking blood flow to a lung region. The air reaching those alveoli is 'wasted' because there is no blood to exchange gases with.

What is a 'shunt' in the context of V/Q mismatch?

A pulmonary shunt occurs when blood flows past alveoli that receive no ventilation (e.g., collapsed or fluid-filled alveoli). The blood passes through without being oxygenated, mixing with oxygenated blood and lowering overall arterial PO₂.

What is hypoxemia?

Hypoxemia is defined as an abnormally low partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood, typically PaO₂ below 60 mmHg. It can result from impaired gas exchange, hypoventilation, V/Q mismatch, shunting, or low inspired oxygen.

What is hypercapnia?

Hypercapnia is an abnormally high partial pressure of carbon dioxide in arterial blood (PaCO₂ > 45 mmHg), typically resulting from hypoventilation or severe V/Q mismatch. It leads to respiratory acidosis if uncompensated.

What is an arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis?

An ABG is a blood test drawn from an artery (usually the radial artery) that provides precise measurements of pH, partial pressures of oxygen and carbon dioxide, bicarbonate concentration, and oxygen saturation — giving a comprehensive picture of gas exchange and acid-base balance.

A nurse is caring for a patient with COPD who is receiving supplemental oxygen at 2 L/min via nasal cannula. The patient's SpO₂ improves from 85% to 92%. The nurse explains to a student that supplemental O₂ works by a specific physiological mechanism. What is that mechanism?

Supplemental oxygen increases the fraction of inspired oxygen (FiO₂) above the normal 21%, which raises the partial pressure of O₂ in the alveoli. This steeper gradient drives more O₂ across the respiratory membrane into the blood, improving oxygenation.
