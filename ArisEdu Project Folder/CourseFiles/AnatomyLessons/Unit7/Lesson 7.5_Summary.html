Lesson 7.5: Regulation of Breathing - Summary

Key Concepts: Regulation of Breathing

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is apnea?

Apnea literally means 'without breathing' (a- = without, -pnea = breathing). It refers to a temporary cessation of respiratory effort, which can occur during sleep (sleep apnea), after hyperventilation, or from central nervous system depression.

What is sleep apnea?

Sleep apnea involves repeated episodes where breathing stops for 10 seconds or more during sleep. Obstructive sleep apnea (the most common form) results from pharyngeal collapse, while central sleep apnea results from the brain failing to send appropriate signals to the respiratory muscles.

What is Cheyne-Stokes respiration?

Cheyne-Stokes respiration is a periodic breathing pattern seen in conditions like heart failure, stroke, and approaching death. It results from delayed feedback between CO₂ levels in the blood and the respiratory center, causing oscillating cycles of hyperventilation and apnea.

What is respiratory acidosis?

Respiratory acidosis occurs when hypoventilation causes CO₂ to accumulate in the blood. The excess CO₂ is converted to carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which lowers pH below 7.35. Common causes include COPD, respiratory depression from opioids, and neuromuscular disorders.

What is respiratory alkalosis?

Respiratory alkalosis results from hyperventilation causing excessive CO₂ loss. With less CO₂ available to form carbonic acid, H⁺ concentration drops and pH rises above 7.45. Common causes include anxiety, pain, fever, and high altitude.

What is Kussmaul breathing?

Kussmaul breathing is a pattern of deep, rapid respiration that represents the respiratory system's attempt to compensate for metabolic acidosis. By hyperventilating, the body blows off excess CO₂ to reduce carbonic acid and raise blood pH back toward normal.

A patient with severe COPD is brought to the emergency department in respiratory distress. A well-meaning paramedic placed the patient on high-flow oxygen (15 L/min) during transport. Upon arrival, the patient becomes increasingly drowsy and his breathing slows. An ABG shows PaCO₂ of 80 mmHg. What is the most likely explanation?

In chronic CO₂ retainers, central chemoreceptors are desensitized to high CO₂, so low O₂ (hypoxic drive) becomes the primary breathing stimulus. High-flow oxygen corrects the hypoxemia, but in doing so removes the hypoxic drive, causing ventilation to decrease and CO₂ to accumulate further — potentially to lethal levels.

A 22-year-old woman is hyperventilating during a severe panic attack. She feels dizzy, her fingers are tingling, and she feels like she cannot get enough air. Her ABG shows pH 7.52 and PaCO₂ of 25 mmHg. Based on breathing regulation, what is causing her symptoms?

Hyperventilation reduces PaCO₂ well below normal, causing respiratory alkalosis (pH > 7.45). The alkalosis causes cerebral vasoconstriction (dizziness) and increases protein binding of ionized calcium, reducing free calcium levels and causing paresthesias (tingling) and potentially carpopedal spasms.

A patient is found unconscious after an opioid overdose. His respiratory rate is 4 breaths per minute. An ABG reveals pH 7.18, PaCO₂ 78 mmHg, and PaO₂ 45 mmHg. Based on breathing regulation, what is the mechanism of his respiratory failure?

Opioids (morphine, heroin, fentanyl) depress the brainstem respiratory centers in the medulla, reducing both the rate and depth of breathing. The resulting severe hypoventilation leads to CO₂ accumulation (respiratory acidosis) and inadequate oxygenation, which can be fatal without intervention.

A patient with heart failure exhibits Cheyne-Stokes breathing — her respirations wax and wane in depth with periods of apnea. A cardiology resident asks why this pattern occurs in heart failure. What is the best physiological explanation?

In heart failure, reduced cardiac output prolongs the time for blood to circulate from the lungs to the brain. This delay in chemoreceptor feedback causes the respiratory center to overshoot (hyperventilation → hypocapnia → apnea) and then undershoot (apnea → hypercapnia → hyperventilation) in a repeating cycle.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is sleep apnea?

Sleep apnea involves repeated episodes where breathing stops for 10 seconds or more during sleep. Obstructive sleep apnea (the most common form) results from pharyngeal collapse, while central sleep apnea results from the brain failing to send appropriate signals to the respiratory muscles.

Example: A patient with obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) undergoes a sleep study that reveals 45 apneic episodes per hour, with oxygen desaturations to 72%. The sleep medicine physician prescribes CPAP therapy. Based on breathing regulation, why does CPAP help this patient?

In obstructive sleep apnea, the pharyngeal muscles relax during sleep, allowing the soft tissues to collapse and block the airway. CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) delivers pressurized air that acts as a pneumatic splint, keeping the upper airway open so the patient can breathe normally.

Example: A newborn infant occasionally stops breathing for 15-20 seconds during sleep, then resumes without intervention. The NICU nurse reassures the parents but explains that prolonged episodes (>20 seconds with color change) would be concerning. What distinguishes normal periodic breathing in a newborn from pathological apnea?

Newborn respiratory centers are immature, making brief breathing pauses (periodic breathing) common and generally benign. Pathological apnea of infancy is defined as cessation of breathing for 20 seconds or more, or a shorter pause accompanied by oxygen desaturation, cyanosis, or bradycardia, requiring medical evaluation.
