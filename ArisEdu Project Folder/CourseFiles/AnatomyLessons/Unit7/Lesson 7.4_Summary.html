Lesson 7.4: Transport of Oxygen & Carbon Dioxide - Summary

Key Concepts: Transport of O₂ & CO₂

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the Bohr effect?

The Bohr effect describes how elevated CO₂ and hydrogen ions (lower pH) in metabolically active tissues cause a conformational change in hemoglobin that decreases its O₂ affinity, facilitating O₂ unloading precisely where it is needed most.

What is the chloride shift?

When HCO₃⁻ exits the red blood cell into the plasma, Cl⁻ ions enter through the band 3 anion exchanger to maintain electrical balance across the cell membrane. This process, called the chloride shift (or Hamburger phenomenon), accompanies CO₂ loading in the tissues.

What is 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG)?

2,3-BPG is a byproduct of red blood cell glycolysis that inserts into the central cavity of deoxyhemoglobin, stabilizing its low-affinity state and promoting further O₂ unloading. Its levels increase in conditions of chronic hypoxia, such as living at high altitude.

What is oxyhemoglobin?

Oxyhemoglobin (HbO₂) is the oxygenated form of hemoglobin, with one or more O₂ molecules bound to its heme iron atoms. It appears bright red and is found predominantly in arterial blood leaving the lungs.

What is cyanosis?

Cyanosis occurs when there is 5 g/dL or more of deoxyhemoglobin in the capillary blood. Deoxyhemoglobin has a dark blue-purple color, which shows through the skin and is most easily visible in the lips, nail beds, and earlobes.

What is fetal hemoglobin (HbF)?

Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) contains gamma globin chains instead of the beta chains found in adult hemoglobin (HbA). HbF has a higher oxygen affinity (left-shifted curve) because it does not bind 2,3-BPG as effectively, allowing the fetus to extract O₂ from maternal blood at the placenta.

What is pulse oximetry?

Pulse oximetry uses two wavelengths of light (red and infrared) passing through a pulsating vascular bed to measure the ratio of oxyhemoglobin to total hemoglobin. It provides a continuous, noninvasive estimate of arterial oxygen saturation.

Real-World Applications

Example: A blood bank technician is explaining to a nursing student why stored blood becomes less effective at delivering oxygen over time. The technician notes that 2,3-BPG levels decrease during storage. How does this affect the stored blood's ability to deliver oxygen?

During storage, red blood cells gradually lose 2,3-BPG. Without adequate 2,3-BPG, the O₂-Hb curve shifts leftward, meaning hemoglobin binds O₂ tightly and releases it poorly to tissues. It takes approximately 24 hours after transfusion for 2,3-BPG levels to normalize.
