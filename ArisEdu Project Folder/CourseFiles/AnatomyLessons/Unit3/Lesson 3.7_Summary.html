Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Sports Medicine - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Sports Medicine

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the PCL (posterior cruciate ligament)?

The posterior cruciate ligament is a strong ligament inside the knee that prevents the tibia from sliding backward relative to the femur, working as the functional counterpart to the ACL; it is most commonly injured by a direct blow to the front of the tibia (dashboard injury).

What is a meniscus in the knee joint?

The knee contains two menisci (medial and lateral), which are C-shaped pads of fibrocartilage that sit between the femoral condyles and tibial plateau, distributing weight, absorbing shock, improving joint congruence, and providing lubrication during movement.

What is shin splints (medial tibial stress syndrome)?

Shin splints (medial tibial stress syndrome) is an overuse injury causing pain along the inner edge of the tibia, resulting from repetitive stress on the shinbone and the connective tissue attaching muscles to it, commonly seen in runners, dancers, and military recruits.

What is the 'Thompson test' used to diagnose?

The Thompson test (calf squeeze test) is a clinical examination where the examiner squeezes the patient's calf while the patient lies prone; if the Achilles tendon is intact, the foot will plantar flex, but if it is ruptured, the foot will not move, indicating a positive test.

What is the SITS mnemonic?

SITS is a widely used mnemonic to remember the four muscles of the rotator cuff: Supraspinatus (abduction), Infraspinatus (external rotation), Teres minor (external rotation), and Subscapularis (internal rotation), all of which stabilize the glenohumeral joint.

A recreational basketball player in his 40s suddenly feels a sharp pain in the back of his lower leg while sprinting, describing it as being 'kicked in the calf.' He cannot push off with that foot. The Thompson test is positive. What is the most likely diagnosis and what anatomical structure is damaged?

The classic presentation of an Achilles tendon rupture includes a sudden 'pop' or sensation of being kicked in the calf during explosive push-off, inability to plantar flex, and a positive Thompson test; this injury is common in middle-aged recreational athletes whose tendon has undergone age-related degeneration.

A retired professional boxer, now age 55, is experiencing memory loss, mood swings, depression, and difficulty with impulse control. His neurologist suspects CTE. The family asks if there is a definitive test to diagnose CTE while the patient is alive. What is the current status of CTE diagnosis?

CTE can currently only be definitively confirmed post-mortem by neuropathological examination showing characteristic patterns of abnormal tau protein deposition; while research into biomarkers and PET imaging is advancing, clinical diagnosis in living patients is based on history of repetitive head trauma, progressive neurological symptoms, and exclusion of other conditions.

A weekend tennis player develops pain on the outside of her elbow that worsens when she grips objects or shakes hands. Her orthopedist diagnoses lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow). She asks why this happened even though she only plays tennis once a week. What is the pathophysiology?

Lateral epicondylitis results from repetitive micro-tears at the origin of the wrist extensor muscles on the lateral epicondyle; factors like poor backhand technique, improper grip size, sudden increases in playing time, and insufficient recovery can overwhelm the tendon's repair capacity even with relatively infrequent play.

Real-World Applications

Example: Concussions in sports should be managed by:

Current concussion management protocols require immediate removal from play, cognitive and physical rest, medical evaluation, and a stepwise return-to-play progression where each stage must be symptom-free before advancing, to prevent second impact syndrome and long-term brain damage.
