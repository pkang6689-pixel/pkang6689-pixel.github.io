Lesson 3.2: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology - Summary

Key Concepts: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the sarcolemma?

The sarcolemma is the specialized plasma membrane of a muscle fiber (cell), which receives action potentials at the motor end plate and transmits them along the surface and into T-tubules to initiate contraction.

What is a myofibril?

A myofibril is a cylindrical organelle running the length of a muscle fiber, composed of thousands of sarcomeres arranged end-to-end; each muscle fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils that produce the actual force of contraction.

What is the neuromuscular junction (NMJ)?

The neuromuscular junction is the specialized chemical synapse where a motor neuron's axon terminal communicates with a skeletal muscle fiber via the release of acetylcholine, converting a neural signal into a muscular response.

What is the motor end plate?

The motor end plate is the folded region of the muscle fiber's sarcolemma directly opposite the motor neuron terminal, densely packed with nicotinic acetylcholine receptors that allow the muscle to respond rapidly to neural signals.

What is acetylcholinesterase?

Acetylcholinesterase is an enzyme bound to the basal lamina in the synaptic cleft that rapidly hydrolyzes acetylcholine into acetate and choline, ensuring that each nerve impulse produces only one brief muscle response and preventing continuous stimulation.

What is the A band of a sarcomere?

The A band is the dark-staining region of a sarcomere that corresponds to the full length of the thick myosin filaments, including the zones where thin actin filaments overlap with myosin; importantly, the A band does not change in length during contraction.

What is the I band?

The I band is the light-staining region of a sarcomere containing only thin (actin) filaments with no overlap by thick (myosin) filaments; it spans two adjacent sarcomeres and is bisected by the Z line.

What is the H zone?

The H zone is the lighter central portion of the A band where only thick myosin filaments are present and no thin actin filaments reach; during contraction, the H zone narrows and can disappear completely.

Real-World Applications

Example: During a laboratory practical, a student examines a sarcomere diagram and notices that after contraction, the I band and H zone have narrowed but the A band remains the same width. Which principle of muscle contraction does this observation support?

The sliding filament theory predicts that the A band (length of myosin) stays constant while the I band and H zone narrow because actin slides inward over myosin, bringing Z lines closer together without any filament changing its own length.

Example: A surgeon is repairing a severed nerve to a patient's quadriceps muscle. She explains that without nerve supply, the muscle will waste away. Which component of the motor unit is damaged, and what condition will develop?

When the motor neuron component of a motor unit is severed, the muscle fibers it innervates lose their neural stimulation and undergo denervation atrophy, progressively shrinking in size and losing function until the nerve is repaired or reinnervation occurs.

Example: A pharmacology student is learning about nerve agent poisoning. The instructor explains that nerve agents inhibit acetylcholinesterase, causing ACh to accumulate at the NMJ. Based on knowledge of NMJ physiology, what cascade of events would occur in the victim's muscles?

Inhibition of acetylcholinesterase causes acetylcholine to accumulate in the synaptic cleft, leading to continuous stimulation of the motor end plate, initial muscle fasciculations and spasms, followed by depolarization block and flaccid paralysis as receptors become desensitized.
