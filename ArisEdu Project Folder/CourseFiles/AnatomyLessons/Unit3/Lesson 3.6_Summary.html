Key Concepts: Disorders of the Muscular System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is muscular dystrophy?

Muscular dystrophy encompasses a group of more than 30 inherited genetic disorders in which mutations in genes encoding structural muscle proteins (like dystrophin) lead to progressive skeletal muscle weakness, degeneration, and replacement with fibrotic and fatty tissue.

What is atrophy in the context of muscular disorders?

Atrophy is the wasting or reduction in muscle size that occurs when muscle fibers shrink due to decreased protein synthesis and increased protein degradation, caused by factors such as prolonged immobilization, nerve damage, aging (sarcopenia), or chronic disease.

What is hypertrophy?

Hypertrophy is the increase in skeletal muscle size that results from enlargement of existing muscle fibers (not creation of new ones) through increased synthesis of myofilaments and myofibrils, typically stimulated by resistance training and adequate nutrition.

What is compartment syndrome?

Compartment syndrome occurs when swelling or bleeding within a closed fascial compartment raises the pressure to a level that compromises blood flow and nerve function, leading to ischemia and potential permanent tissue damage if not treated with emergency fasciotomy.

What is dystrophin?

Dystrophin is a large, rod-shaped cytoskeletal protein that forms a crucial link between the intracellular actin cytoskeleton and the extracellular matrix through the dystrophin-associated glycoprotein complex, protecting the sarcolemma from mechanical stress during contraction; its absence causes Duchenne muscular dystrophy.

What is a muscle spasm?

A muscle spasm (or cramp) is an involuntary, sustained, and often painful contraction that occurs without conscious control, commonly caused by electrolyte imbalances, dehydration, muscle fatigue, or nerve irritation.

A nurse is caring for an ICU patient who develops sudden, severe pain and swelling in his right lower leg after a tibial fracture was casted. The limb appears tense, and the patient reports numbness in the foot. The surgeon is urgently called. What is the likely diagnosis, and why is emergency surgery needed?

Acute compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency where swelling within a rigid fascial compartment raises pressure above capillary perfusion pressure, cutting off blood flow to muscles and nerves; without fasciotomy (surgical opening of the compartment) within 6 hours, irreversible muscle necrosis and nerve damage occur.

A dermatologist is using Botox injections to treat a patient with chronic migraines. The patient asks how a toxin can be a medicine. Based on knowledge of muscular disorders, what is the therapeutic mechanism of Botox?

Therapeutic Botox uses tiny, precisely targeted doses of botulinum toxin to block ACh release at selected neuromuscular junctions, causing localized, temporary muscle paralysis that reduces the excessive muscle tension contributing to chronic migraines, cervical dystonia, and spasticity.

A physical therapist is treating a patient with multiple sclerosis (MS) who is experiencing increasing weakness and spasticity in her legs. The patient asks why her muscles are getting weaker even though the disease affects nerves, not muscles. What is the best explanation?

In MS, the autoimmune destruction of myelin in the central nervous system slows or blocks motor signals traveling from the brain and spinal cord to skeletal muscles; although the muscles themselves are initially structurally normal, impaired innervation causes weakness, and damaged inhibitory pathways lead to spasticity.

An elderly patient has been hospitalized for pneumonia and confined to bed for 3 weeks. Upon discharge, she is unable to walk unassisted. Her doctor explains that her leg muscles have atrophied. The patient asks if the muscle loss is permanent. What is the most accurate response?

Disuse atrophy is generally reversible because the muscle fibers shrink but are not destroyed; with progressive resistance exercise and physical therapy, protein synthesis increases, myofibrils are rebuilt, and muscle size and strength can be largely restored, though recovery may take longer in elderly patients.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Duchenne MD is X-linked recessive; female carriers have two X chromosomes, and the normal copy on one X produces sufficient dystrophin through random X-inactivation to maintain muscle function, while males have only one X chromosome and therefore express the full disease phenotype when the dystrophin gene is mutated.

Real-World Applications

Example: A muscle strain involves damage to:

A muscle strain is an injury to muscle fibers or their associated tendons caused by overstretching or excessive force, ranging from mild microscopic fiber damage (Grade I) to complete rupture (Grade III) of the muscle-tendon unit.

Example: What does 'demyelination' mean in the context of multiple sclerosis and muscle weakness?

Demyelination is the loss or destruction of the myelin sheath that normally insulates nerve fibers and speeds up signal conduction; in multiple sclerosis, the immune system attacks myelin on central nervous system neurons, slowing or blocking motor signals and causing muscle weakness, spasticity, and coordination problems.
