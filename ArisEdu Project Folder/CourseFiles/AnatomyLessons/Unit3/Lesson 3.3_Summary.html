Lesson 3.3: Sliding Filament Theory - Summary

Key Concepts: Sliding Filament Theory

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a cross-bridge in muscle contraction?

A cross-bridge refers to the temporary bond formed when the globular head of a myosin molecule attaches to a binding site on an actin filament, creating the structural link through which force is transmitted to pull actin inward during the power stroke.

What is the power stroke?

The power stroke is the force-generating step of the cross-bridge cycle in which the myosin head pivots from its cocked position to a bent position, pulling the attached actin filament approximately 10 nanometers toward the center of the sarcomere.

What is the role of tropomyosin in muscle contraction?

Tropomyosin is a long, rod-shaped regulatory protein that winds along the actin filament groove, physically blocking the myosin-binding sites when the muscle is relaxed and shifting position when calcium binds to troponin to permit cross-bridge formation.

What is the role of troponin in the contraction cycle?

Troponin is a three-subunit regulatory complex (troponin C binds calcium, troponin T binds tropomyosin, troponin I inhibits actin-myosin interaction) that acts as the calcium sensor on the thin filament, initiating contraction when calcium binds to troponin C.

What is excitation-contraction coupling?

Excitation-contraction coupling is the physiological process that converts an electrical signal (action potential on the sarcolemma and T-tubules) into a mechanical response (muscle contraction) through the release of calcium from the sarcoplasmic reticulum.

What is the Ca2+-ATPase pump?

The Ca2+-ATPase (SERCA) pump is an active transport protein embedded in the sarcoplasmic reticulum membrane that uses ATP to transport calcium ions from the sarcoplasm back into the SR lumen, lowering cytoplasmic calcium and allowing tropomyosin to re-cover actin binding sites for relaxation.

During a physiology exam, a student is asked: 'If a myosin head could not hydrolyze ATP, what would happen to the cross-bridge cycle?' What is the correct answer?

ATP hydrolysis by the myosin ATPase is essential for re-energizing the myosin head; if hydrolysis cannot occur, the myosin head cannot return to its cocked (high-energy) position after detachment, meaning it cannot perform another power stroke and the cross-bridge cycle would stall.

Real-World Applications

Example: A forensic scientist examining a body found in a warm apartment notices the muscles are very stiff. The estimated time of death is 8 hours prior. Using knowledge of the sliding filament theory, which molecular event best explains the stiffness?

After death, cellular metabolism stops and ATP is rapidly depleted; without ATP to bind myosin and break the actin-myosin cross-bridges, the myosin heads remain locked to actin, causing the characteristic stiffness of rigor mortis that typically begins 2-6 hours after death.
