Lesson 3.5: Major Muscle Groups - Summary

Key Concepts: Major Muscle Groups

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an agonist (prime mover)?

An agonist (or prime mover) is the muscle that bears the primary responsibility for a particular movement, such as the biceps brachii being the agonist for elbow flexion or the quadriceps being the agonist for knee extension.

What is an antagonist muscle?

An antagonist is a muscle that opposes the action of the agonist, providing controlled resistance and smooth movement; for example, the triceps brachii is the antagonist to the biceps brachii during elbow flexion, relaxing to allow flexion and contracting to produce extension.

What is a synergist muscle?

A synergist is a muscle that assists the prime mover by contributing to the same movement, stabilizing joints to prevent unwanted motions, or neutralizing undesired actions of the agonist, such as the brachialis assisting the biceps during elbow flexion.

What is the latissimus dorsi?

The latissimus dorsi ('lat') is the broadest muscle of the back, extending from the lower spine and iliac crest to the humerus, responsible for powerful arm extension, adduction, and medial rotation, as used in pull-ups, swimming, and rowing.

What is the pectoralis major?

The pectoralis major is a large, fan-shaped muscle covering the upper anterior chest, originating from the clavicle, sternum, and ribs and inserting on the humerus; it is the primary muscle for chest press movements, push-ups, and bringing the arms across the body.

What is the erector spinae?

The erector spinae is a group of three paired muscle columns (iliocostalis, longissimus, and spinalis) running along the length of the vertebral column that are the primary extensors of the spine, maintaining upright posture and controlling trunk flexion.

What is the sternocleidomastoid (SCM)?

The sternocleidomastoid is a large, strap-like neck muscle named for its attachments (sternum, clavicle, and mastoid process of the temporal bone) that, when contracting unilaterally, rotates the head to the opposite side, and bilaterally, flexes the neck.

A bodybuilder is performing a bench press. During the upward pressing phase, which major muscle group acts as the primary agonist, and what is the type of contraction?

During the pressing (upward) phase of the bench press, the pectoralis major is the primary agonist performing a concentric contraction, shortening to push the barbell upward through horizontal adduction and flexion of the shoulder, assisted by the anterior deltoid and triceps.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

The deltoid is the primary abductor of the shoulder, and damage to the motor cortex area controlling it would prevent the patient from raising her arm laterally, making overhead activities like hair brushing, reaching shelves, and dressing extremely difficult.

Real-World Applications

Example: A patient who had a stroke has difficulty raising her left arm above her head to brush her hair. The neurologist explains that the stroke damaged the motor cortex area controlling the left shoulder. Which muscle is primarily affected, preventing arm abduction?

The deltoid is the primary abductor of the shoulder, and damage to the motor cortex area controlling it would prevent the patient from raising her arm laterally, making overhead activities like hair brushing, reaching shelves, and dressing extremely difficult.

Example: A respiratory therapist is treating a patient with a spinal cord injury at the C3-C5 level. The therapist explains that the patient cannot breathe independently because the phrenic nerve is damaged. Which muscle is paralyzed, and why is it so critical?

The diaphragm is the primary muscle of inspiration, responsible for about 75% of air movement during quiet breathing, and it is innervated by the phrenic nerve originating from C3-C5; damage to these spinal cord segments paralyzes the diaphragm and requires mechanical ventilation.

Example: A patient complains of a stiff neck after sleeping in an awkward position. The doctor diagnoses a strained sternocleidomastoid muscle. The patient notices she can only turn her head to the right comfortably. On which side is the SCM most likely strained?

The sternocleidomastoid rotates the head to the opposite side when contracting unilaterally; if the left SCM is strained, turning the head to the right (which requires left SCM contraction) would be more comfortable since the strained muscle is already shortened, while turning left would painfully stretch it.
