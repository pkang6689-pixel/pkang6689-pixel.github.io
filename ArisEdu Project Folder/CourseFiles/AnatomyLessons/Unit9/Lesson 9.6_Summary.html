Lesson 9.6: Hormonal Regulation of Reproduction - Summary

Key Concepts: Hormonal Regulation of Reproduction

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone)?

GnRH is a decapeptide hormone produced by the hypothalamus and released in a pulsatile fashion into the hypophyseal portal blood system. It is the master regulator of the reproductive axis, stimulating gonadotroph cells in the anterior pituitary to synthesize and secrete FSH and LH.

What is the HPG axis?

The HPG (hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal) axis is the hierarchical hormonal system that controls reproduction. The hypothalamus releases GnRH, the anterior pituitary releases FSH and LH, and the gonads produce sex hormones, with multiple negative (and one positive) feedback loops maintaining hormonal homeostasis.

What is precocious puberty?

Precocious puberty is the abnormally early onset of sexual maturation, defined as before age 8 in girls or before age 9 in boys. It can be central (GnRH-dependent, from early HPG axis activation) or peripheral (GnRH-independent, from autonomous sex steroid production by gonads or adrenals).

What is hypogonadism?

Hypogonadism refers to inadequate gonadal function resulting in low sex hormone production. Primary hypogonadism (hypergonadotropic) involves gonadal failure with elevated FSH/LH, while secondary hypogonadism (hypogonadotropic) involves hypothalamic or pituitary dysfunction with low FSH/LH.

What is clomiphene?

Clomiphene (Clomid) is a selective estrogen receptor modulator that blocks estrogen receptors at the hypothalamus and pituitary, tricking the brain into perceiving low estrogen. This removes negative feedback, causing increased GnRH, FSH, and LH secretion, which stimulates follicular development and ovulation in anovulatory women.

What is testosterone replacement therapy (TRT)?

Testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) is the medical administration of exogenous testosterone to men diagnosed with hypogonadism who have both low serum testosterone levels and clinical symptoms such as decreased libido, fatigue, reduced muscle mass, and depressed mood. It is a prescribed treatment that requires monitoring of hematocrit and PSA levels.

An 8-year-old girl develops breast buds and pubic hair. Blood work shows elevated estradiol, LH, and FSH. A brain MRI is normal. Her pediatric endocrinologist diagnoses central precocious puberty. What is the underlying mechanism?

Central (true) precocious puberty occurs when the hypothalamic GnRH pulse generator activates prematurely, driving the entire HPG axis to function as it would during normal puberty, but at an inappropriately early age. Elevated LH and FSH confirm central activation, distinguishing it from peripheral precocious puberty where gonadotropins would be suppressed.

Real-World Applications

Example: A patient with endometriosis is prescribed a continuous combined oral contraceptive (without a placebo week) to manage her symptoms. The doctor explains this creates a state of pseudo-pregnancy. How does this hormonal therapy reduce endometriosis symptoms?

Continuous combined oral contraceptives suppress the HPG axis through negative feedback, preventing ovulation and the cyclical rise and fall of estrogen and progesterone. Without these hormonal fluctuations, the ectopic endometrial implants receive less stimulation, reducing inflammation, pain, and the progression of endometriosis.
