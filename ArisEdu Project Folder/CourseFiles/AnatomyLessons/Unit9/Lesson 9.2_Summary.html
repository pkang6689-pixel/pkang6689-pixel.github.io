Lesson 9.2: Kidney Function & Filtration - Summary

Key Concepts: Kidney Function & Filtration

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is glomerular filtration rate (GFR)?

Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) is the volume of plasma filtered through the glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule per unit time, normally about 125 mL/min, and it serves as the primary indicator of overall kidney function.

What is the renal threshold?

The renal threshold is the plasma concentration at which a substance (such as glucose) exceeds the capacity of its tubular transport proteins to reabsorb it, causing the excess to spill into the urine, as occurs with glucose in uncontrolled diabetes.

What is oliguria?

Oliguria is defined as abnormally low urine output of less than 400 mL per day (or less than 0.5 mL/kg/hr), and it is an important clinical sign of kidney dysfunction, dehydration, or reduced renal perfusion.

What is the countercurrent multiplier?

The countercurrent multiplier is a mechanism involving the opposing flow of fluid in the descending and ascending limbs of the loop of Henle that progressively builds a concentration gradient in the renal medulla, essential for producing concentrated urine.

What is net filtration pressure (NFP)?

Net filtration pressure is the sum of forces that determine glomerular filtration, calculated as glomerular hydrostatic pressure minus both the capsular hydrostatic pressure and the glomerular osmotic pressure, normally resulting in about 10 mmHg favoring filtration.

What is creatinine clearance?

Creatinine clearance is a clinical measure used to estimate GFR by comparing the concentration of creatinine in the blood to the amount excreted in the urine over 24 hours, since creatinine is freely filtered and minimally reabsorbed or secreted.

A 65-year-old patient with heart failure is prescribed furosemide, a loop diuretic. The medication targets a specific segment of the nephron. Which segment does furosemide act upon, and what is the expected outcome?

Furosemide blocks the Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransporter in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle, preventing NaCl reabsorption and disrupting the medullary concentration gradient, resulting in significant water and salt excretion that reduces blood volume and relieves fluid overload.

During a physiology exam, a student is asked why the kidneys autoregulate blood flow when systemic blood pressure fluctuates between 80-180 mmHg. What is the primary purpose of renal autoregulation?

Renal autoregulation uses myogenic mechanisms and tubuloglomerular feedback to adjust afferent arteriole diameter, maintaining a relatively constant GFR across a wide range of systemic blood pressures (80-180 mmHg) to ensure stable filtration and urine production.

Real-World Applications

Example: The proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) reabsorbs approximately what percentage of the filtrate?

The PCT is the primary site of reabsorption, recovering approximately 65% of filtered water, sodium, glucose, amino acids, and other solutes through both active transport and passive mechanisms.

Example: Overall, more than what percentage of the glomerular filtrate is reabsorbed back into the blood?

More than 99% of the approximately 180 liters of filtrate produced daily is reabsorbed back into the blood by the nephron tubules, resulting in only about 1-2 liters of final urine output per day.
