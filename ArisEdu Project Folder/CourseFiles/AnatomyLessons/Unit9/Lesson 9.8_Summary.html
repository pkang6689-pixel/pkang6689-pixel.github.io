Lesson 9.8: AP Prep: Renal Physiology - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Renal Physiology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is PAH (para-aminohippuric acid) clearance used to estimate?

PAH clearance estimates effective renal plasma flow (ERPF) because PAH is both freely filtered at the glomerulus AND actively secreted by the proximal tubule, so approximately 90% of PAH is removed from plasma in a single pass through the kidney. This makes PAH clearance the best clinical estimate of renal blood flow.

What is the transport maximum (Tm)?

Transport maximum (Tm) is the maximum rate at which a substance can be transported across the tubular epithelium, limited by the number and capacity of carrier proteins. When plasma concentrations exceed the Tm (as with glucose in diabetes), the excess substance cannot be reabsorbed and appears in the urine.

What is the vasa recta?

The vasa recta are hairpin-shaped capillaries that descend into the medulla alongside the loops of Henle of juxtamedullary nephrons. They function as countercurrent exchangers, allowing blood to supply the medulla with oxygen and nutrients while preserving the medullary osmotic gradient essential for urine concentration.

What is the BUN-to-creatinine ratio used to assess?

The BUN-to-creatinine ratio helps differentiate the cause of kidney dysfunction. A ratio greater than 20:1 suggests prerenal azotemia (dehydration, heart failure) where urea is preferentially reabsorbed. A normal ratio of 10-20:1 with elevated values suggests intrinsic renal disease, and the clinical context helps identify postrenal causes.

What is the normal range for urine specific gravity?

Normal urine specific gravity ranges from 1.001 to 1.035, reflecting the concentration of dissolved solutes relative to pure water (1.000). A low specific gravity indicates dilute urine (as in diabetes insipidus or excessive fluid intake), while a high specific gravity indicates concentrated urine (as in dehydration or SIADH).

What is the tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF) mechanism?

Tubuloglomerular feedback is a local autoregulatory mechanism in which the macula densa cells of the JGA sense NaCl concentration in the tubular fluid at the DCT. When NaCl is high (indicating high GFR), the macula densa signals the afferent arteriole to constrict, reducing GFR. When NaCl is low, the arteriole dilates to increase GFR, maintaining stable filtration.

A patient presents with the following arterial blood gas: pH 7.28, pCO2 25 mmHg, HCO3- 12 mEq/L. On an AP exam, you are asked to identify the primary disorder and the compensation. What is your analysis?

The low pH (7.28) confirms acidemia. The low HCO3- (12 mEq/L, normal 22-26) indicates the primary problem is metabolic acidosis (loss of bicarbonate or gain of acid). The low pCO2 (25 mmHg, normal 35-45) represents respiratory compensation through hyperventilation, as the lungs attempt to blow off CO2 to raise the pH toward normal.

On an AP exam, a student is presented with the following scenario: A patient has glycosuria with a normal blood glucose of 85 mg/dL. The student must explain this finding. What is the most likely cause?

Glycosuria with normal blood glucose levels indicates a defect in the proximal tubule's glucose reabsorption machinery (renal glucosuria). This can be caused by mutations in the SGLT2 transporter, reducing the transport maximum for glucose so that even normal filtered glucose loads exceed the tubule's diminished reabsorptive capacity. This is distinct from diabetic glucosuria, where the glucose load simply exceeds normal transport capacity.

Real-World Applications

Example: The countercurrent multiplier system operates in which part of the nephron?

The countercurrent multiplier operates in the loop of Henle, where the opposing flow directions of the descending limb (permeable to water) and ascending limb (pumps out NaCl) work together to establish the medullary osmotic gradient essential for producing concentrated urine.

Example: During an AP exam, a student is presented with the following scenario: A substance X has a clearance higher than inulin clearance. What can be concluded about how substance X is handled by the kidney?

Since inulin clearance equals GFR (inulin is freely filtered but neither reabsorbed nor secreted), a substance with clearance greater than inulin must undergo net tubular secretion in addition to filtration. This means the tubules are actively adding the substance from peritubular blood into the tubular fluid, increasing its excretion rate beyond what filtration alone would produce (e.g., PAH, penicillin).

Example: A patient with SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion) is retaining excess water. On an AP exam, you are asked to predict the changes in serum sodium, urine osmolality, and urine volume. What would you expect?

In SIADH, excess ADH causes inappropriate water reabsorption in the collecting duct, producing inappropriately concentrated urine (high osmolality) with low volume. The retained water dilutes the serum sodium, causing hyponatremia. This is a classic AP exam topic requiring understanding of ADH physiology and its pathological excess.

Example: During an AP exam review, a student is asked: Why is plasma creatinine a better clinical marker of GFR than BUN, even though both are elevated in kidney failure?

Creatinine is produced at a relatively constant rate from muscle creatine phosphate metabolism and is freely filtered without significant reabsorption, making its plasma concentration inversely proportional to GFR. BUN, while also useful, is influenced by many non-renal factors including dietary protein intake, GI bleeding, dehydration, liver function, and catabolic states, making it less specific for kidney function.
