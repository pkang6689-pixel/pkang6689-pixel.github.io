Lesson 9.3: Fluid & Electrolyte Balance - Summary

Key Concepts: Fluid & Electrolyte Balance

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is hypernatremia?

Hypernatremia is defined as a serum sodium concentration above 145 mEq/L, most commonly caused by water deficit (dehydration) rather than sodium excess. It leads to cellular dehydration as water shifts out of cells into the hypertonic ECF.

What is hyponatremia?

Hyponatremia is a serum sodium concentration below 135 mEq/L, which can result from excess water intake, SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion), or sodium loss. Severe cases can cause cerebral edema, confusion, seizures, and coma.

What is hyperkalemia?

Hyperkalemia is an elevated serum potassium above 5.0 mEq/L, and it is a medical emergency when K+ exceeds 6.0 mEq/L because it can cause tall peaked T waves on ECG, widened QRS complexes, and potentially fatal cardiac arrest.

What is metabolic acidosis?

Metabolic acidosis is a condition in which blood pH falls below 7.35 due to an accumulation of metabolic acids (as in diabetic ketoacidosis or lactic acidosis) or a loss of bicarbonate (as in severe diarrhea or renal failure).

What is metabolic alkalosis?

Metabolic alkalosis is a condition in which blood pH rises above 7.45 due to loss of hydrogen ions (as in prolonged vomiting of gastric acid) or gain of bicarbonate (as in excessive antacid use), resulting in an alkaline shift in blood chemistry.

What is edema?

Edema is the abnormal accumulation of fluid in the interstitial spaces of tissues, causing visible swelling. It can result from decreased plasma oncotic pressure (low albumin), increased capillary hydrostatic pressure (heart failure), lymphatic obstruction, or increased capillary permeability.

What is the bicarbonate buffer system?

The bicarbonate buffer system (CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> H+ + HCO3-) is the most important buffer in the extracellular fluid, providing immediate resistance to pH changes by shifting the equilibrium to absorb or release hydrogen ions as needed.

A marathon runner collapses after a race and is found to have a serum sodium of 118 mEq/L. She reports drinking large amounts of plain water during the event. What condition has developed, and what is the underlying mechanism?

This runner developed exercise-associated hyponatremia (dilutional hyponatremia) from drinking excessive amounts of hypotonic fluid (plain water) during the race, which diluted the serum sodium to dangerously low levels. This condition can cause cerebral edema, seizures, and death if untreated.

A patient with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) presents with rapid, deep breathing known as Kussmaul respiration. Their blood gas shows pH 7.15, low HCO3-, and low pCO2. What type of acid-base disorder does this represent, and what is the body's compensatory mechanism?

DKA causes metabolic acidosis due to accumulation of ketoacids, lowering blood pH and consuming bicarbonate. The body compensates through respiratory mechanisms (Kussmaul breathing), hyperventilating to blow off CO2 and raise pH, which is why pCO2 is also low.

A post-surgical patient on a diuretic develops muscle cramps, fatigue, and an ECG showing flattened T waves and U waves. The serum potassium level is 2.8 mEq/L. What electrolyte imbalance is present, and what is the most likely cause?

This patient has hypokalemia (K+ < 3.5 mEq/L), likely caused by the diuretic increasing potassium excretion in the urine. The characteristic ECG findings of flattened T waves and U waves, along with muscle cramps and fatigue, are classic manifestations of low potassium affecting cardiac and skeletal muscle function.

Real-World Applications

Example: Total body water is approximately what percentage of body weight?

Total body water constitutes approximately 60% of body weight in an average adult, often referred to as the '60% rule,' though this percentage varies with age, sex, and body fat content.

Example: A patient who recently had thyroid surgery develops tingling around her mouth and fingertips, followed by muscle spasms in her hands. The physician taps the facial nerve and observes twitching of the facial muscles (positive Chvostek's sign). What electrolyte imbalance is most likely responsible?

Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia occurs when the parathyroid glands are accidentally damaged or removed during surgery, causing a rapid drop in PTH and serum calcium. Low calcium increases neuromuscular excitability, producing tetany (muscle spasms), perioral tingling, and positive Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs.
