Lesson 1.3: Homeostasis & Feedback Mechanisms - Summary

Key Concepts: Homeostasis & Feedback Mechanisms

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is negative feedback?

Negative feedback is a regulatory mechanism in which the output of a process opposes or reverses the initial stimulus, effectively shutting down the response once the set point is reached, such as thermoregulation or blood glucose regulation.

What is positive feedback?

Positive feedback enhances or amplifies the original change rather than reversing it. It is less common than negative feedback and typically drives a process to completion, such as during childbirth or blood clotting.

What is a receptor in a feedback loop?

A receptor (sensor) is the component of a feedback loop that detects a change in a physiological variable, such as thermoreceptors in the skin sensing temperature changes or baroreceptors sensing blood pressure changes.

What is a set point?

A set point is the ideal or normal value that the body works to maintain for a given physiological variable, such as 37 degrees C for body temperature or approximately 90 mg/dL for fasting blood glucose.

What is the role of glucagon in blood glucose regulation?

Glucagon is a hormone released by alpha cells of the pancreas when blood glucose drops too low. It stimulates the liver to break down glycogen into glucose and release it into the blood, raising blood glucose levels.

What is a fever in terms of homeostatic regulation?

During an infection, pyrogens cause the hypothalamus to raise the temperature set point, so the body actively generates heat (shivering, vasoconstriction) until the new, higher set point is reached. This is a regulated adjustment, not a loss of homeostasis.

What is the stimulus in a feedback loop?

A stimulus is any change in a controlled variable (such as a rise in body temperature or a drop in blood glucose) that is detected by a receptor and initiates the feedback loop to restore homeostasis.

Real-World Applications

Example: Body temperature regulation is an example of:

When body temperature rises above 37 degrees C, sweating and vasodilation are triggered to cool the body down; when it drops, shivering and vasoconstriction warm it up. This reversal of the change is classic negative feedback.

Example: Blood clotting is an example of:

Blood clotting is a positive feedback mechanism because each clotting factor activates more clotting factors, amplifying the response until the wound is completely sealed.

Example: A medical student is studying a case in which a pregnant woman's labor contractions become progressively stronger. The student recognizes that oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions, which push the baby against the cervix, causing more oxytocin release. This is an example of:

Childbirth is a classic example of positive feedback: oxytocin causes contractions, contractions push the baby against the cervix, the cervix signals for more oxytocin, and the cycle amplifies until the baby is delivered and the stimulus is removed.
