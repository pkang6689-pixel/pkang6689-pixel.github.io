Lesson 1.5: Medical Imaging Techniques - Summary

Key Concepts: Medical Imaging Techniques

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is computed tomography (CT)?

Computed tomography (CT) is an advanced X-ray-based imaging technique in which an X-ray source rotates around the patient while detectors capture data from multiple angles, and a computer reconstructs these data into detailed cross-sectional (slice) images of the body.

What is magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)?

MRI uses a powerful magnet to align hydrogen atoms in the body, then radio wave pulses disturb this alignment. As the atoms realign, they emit signals that are processed into highly detailed images, especially of soft tissues like the brain, joints, and muscles.

What is positron emission tomography (PET)?

PET is a nuclear medicine technique in which a radioactive tracer (usually fluorodeoxyglucose) is injected into the patient. The tracer accumulates in metabolically active areas, and the scanner detects emitted positrons to create images showing functional activity rather than just anatomy.

What is fluoroscopy?

Fluoroscopy is a specialized X-ray technique that produces continuous real-time moving images on a screen, commonly used during procedures like barium swallows, catheter placement, and orthopedic surgeries to visualize structures in motion.

What is Doppler ultrasound?

Doppler ultrasound uses the Doppler effect (change in frequency of reflected sound waves) to measure the velocity and direction of blood flow through vessels, making it valuable for detecting blood clots, vessel blockages, and heart valve abnormalities.

What is a mammogram?

A mammogram is a specialized X-ray of the breast that uses low-dose radiation to detect tumors, calcifications, and other abnormalities in breast tissue, serving as the primary screening tool for early detection of breast cancer.

What is the meaning of 'contraindication' when a physician says MRI is contraindicated for a patient?

A contraindication is a condition or circumstance that makes a particular treatment or procedure inadvisable. For MRI, contraindications include certain metal implants (pacemakers, metal clips) because the powerful magnet can move or heat them, posing a serious safety risk.

What is sonography?

Sonography is the technical term for ultrasound imaging (sono- = sound, -graphy = recording). It uses a transducer to emit high-frequency sound waves that reflect off internal structures, creating real-time images without radiation.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Doppler ultrasound is the first-line imaging technique for suspected deep vein thrombosis because it can visualize blood flow in real time, detect blockages or reduced flow, and is non-invasive with no radiation exposure.

Real-World Applications

Example: CT scans produce images by using:

CT (computed tomography) scans use X-rays taken from many different angles around the body, which a computer then processes to create detailed cross-sectional images, providing far more detail than a standard X-ray.

Example: MRI uses which form of energy to create images?

MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) uses powerful magnetic fields and radio wave pulses to align and then detect hydrogen atoms in body tissues, creating detailed images without using any ionizing radiation.

Example: Ultrasound creates images using:

Ultrasound (sonography) uses high-frequency sound waves that bounce off internal structures and return as echoes, which a computer converts into real-time images of organs and tissues.

Example: A neurologist suspects a patient has a brain tumor and needs detailed images of the brain's soft tissue to plan treatment. Which imaging technique would provide the best soft-tissue contrast for this purpose?

MRI provides superior soft-tissue contrast compared to all other imaging modalities, making it ideal for detecting and characterizing brain tumors, assessing their size and location, and planning surgical approaches.

Example: A patient with a pacemaker needs brain imaging. The radiologist explains that MRI cannot be used because the strong magnetic field could damage the pacemaker. What alternative imaging technique could provide cross-sectional brain images?

A CT scan is the best alternative for patients who cannot undergo MRI (due to pacemakers, metal implants, or claustrophobia). While CT provides somewhat less soft-tissue contrast than MRI, it still produces detailed cross-sectional images and is safe for patients with metal implants.
