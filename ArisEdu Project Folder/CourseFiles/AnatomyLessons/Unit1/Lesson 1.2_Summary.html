Lesson 1.2: Anatomical Terminology (Planes, Directions, Cavities) - Summary

Key Concepts: Anatomical Terminology (Planes, Directions, Cavities)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the anatomical position?

The anatomical position is the standard reference posture: standing erect, facing forward, feet slightly apart, arms at the sides with palms facing anteriorly. All directional terms are based on this position.

What is the dorsal body cavity?

The dorsal body cavity is located along the posterior (back) side of the body and consists of two subdivisions: the cranial cavity (enclosing the brain) and the vertebral canal (enclosing the spinal cord).

What is the ventral body cavity?

The ventral (anterior) body cavity is the larger of the two main body cavities, subdivided into the thoracic cavity (above the diaphragm) and the abdominopelvic cavity (below the diaphragm).

What is the abdominopelvic cavity?

The abdominopelvic cavity lies below the diaphragm and is subdivided into the abdominal cavity (containing the stomach, liver, intestines, and other organs) and the pelvic cavity (containing the bladder, reproductive organs, and rectum).

Real-World Applications

Example: A medical student examining a cadaver is asked to make a cut that divides the body into front and back halves. Which plane should the student use?

The frontal (coronal) plane runs vertically from side to side, dividing the body into anterior (front) and posterior (back) portions, which is exactly what the student needs for this cut.

Example: During a physical examination, a physician notes that a patient's heart sounds are best heard on the anterior chest wall. The physician describes the stethoscope placement as on the _____ surface of the thorax.

The front of the chest is the ventral (anterior) surface. In humans, anterior and ventral are interchangeable terms meaning toward the front of the body.

Example: A radiologist is reviewing a CT scan that shows cross-sectional images of the abdomen. These images were produced by scanning in which anatomical plane?

Standard CT scans produce images in the transverse (horizontal) plane, creating cross-sectional slices that divide the body into superior and inferior portions, though modern scanners can reconstruct images in any plane.
