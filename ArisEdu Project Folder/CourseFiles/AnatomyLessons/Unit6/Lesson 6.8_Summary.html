Lesson 6.8: AP Prep: ECG Interpretation - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: ECG Interpretation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the QT interval?

The QT interval spans from the beginning of the QRS complex to the end of the T wave, representing the total time for ventricular depolarization and repolarization; a prolonged QT interval increases the risk of dangerous ventricular arrhythmias like torsades de pointes.

What is a 12-lead ECG?

A 12-lead ECG uses 10 electrode placements to create 12 different electrical viewpoints of the heart (6 limb leads and 6 precordial leads), allowing clinicians to localize areas of ischemia, injury, or conduction abnormalities to specific regions of the myocardium.

What is ventricular tachycardia (VT)?

Ventricular tachycardia is a dangerous arrhythmia originating in the ventricles, characterized by wide QRS complexes (>0.12s) at a rapid rate (>100 bpm); it can compromise cardiac output and may degenerate into ventricular fibrillation and cardiac arrest.

What is ventricular fibrillation (VFib)?

Ventricular fibrillation produces a completely chaotic ECG tracing with no recognizable P waves, QRS complexes, or T waves; the ventricles quiver ineffectively, producing zero cardiac output, and without immediate defibrillation, death occurs within minutes.

What is the PR interval?

The PR interval measures the time from the beginning of atrial depolarization to the beginning of ventricular depolarization, encompassing atrial conduction and the physiological delay at the AV node; abnormalities in this interval indicate conduction problems.

A patient presents to the ER with palpitations. The ECG shows no visible P waves and the R-R intervals are completely irregular with a ventricular rate of 140 bpm. The QRS complexes are narrow. What is the most likely rhythm?

The combination of absent P waves, irregularly irregular R-R intervals, and narrow QRS complexes at a rapid rate is pathognomonic (uniquely characteristic) of atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response, the most common sustained arrhythmia encountered in clinical practice.

A medical student is interpreting an ECG and notices the PR interval is 0.28 seconds (normal: 0.12-0.20s) but every P wave is followed by a QRS complex. What is the rhythm, and how clinically significant is it?

First-degree AV block shows a prolonged but constant PR interval (>0.20s) with every P wave followed by a QRS; it is typically benign and often an incidental finding, but can be seen with medications (beta-blockers, digoxin) or may indicate early AV conduction disease.

An ECG shows wide, bizarre QRS complexes at a regular rate of 180 bpm with no discernible P waves. The patient is conscious but hypotensive with BP 80/50. What is the rhythm and the appropriate next step?

Wide, regular QRS complexes at 180 bpm without P waves is ventricular tachycardia; because the patient is hemodynamically unstable (hypotensive), immediate synchronized cardioversion is indicated to convert the rhythm before it degenerates into VFib.

A patient on a cardiac monitor shows a rhythm where every few beats the PR interval gets progressively longer, and then one QRS is missing entirely. After the dropped beat, the PR interval resets to a shorter length and the pattern repeats. A nursing student asks what is happening at the cellular level. What is occurring in the conduction system?

In Wenckebach (Type I second-degree AV block), the AV node's refractory period progressively lengthens with each conducted impulse (progressive PR prolongation) until one impulse arrives when the AV node is still refractory and fails to conduct (dropped QRS); the subsequent rest period allows the AV node to recover.

A cardiology fellow is teaching medical students to interpret a 12-lead ECG. She shows an ECG with ST elevation in leads I, aVL, V5, and V6, with reciprocal ST depression in leads III and aVF. She asks what region of the heart is affected. What is the correct answer?

ST elevation in leads I, aVL, V5, and V6 indicates a lateral wall STEMI, with the left circumflex artery being the most common culprit; reciprocal ST depression in the inferior leads (III, aVF) confirms the lateral localization and supports the diagnosis of an acute lateral MI.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

ST elevation in leads V1-V4 (the precordial leads overlying the anterior chest) localizes the infarction to the anterior wall of the left ventricle, which is supplied by the left anterior descending artery; LAD occlusion is often called the 'widow maker' due to the large area of myocardium at risk.
