Lesson 6.5: Blood Pressure & Homeostasis - Summary

Key Concepts: Blood Pressure & Homeostasis

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is diastolic pressure?

Diastolic pressure represents the lowest pressure in the arteries when the ventricles are relaxed and refilling; it reflects the baseline resistance and elasticity of the arterial walls between heartbeats.

What is mean arterial pressure (MAP)?

MAP represents the average arterial pressure during one complete cardiac cycle, weighted toward diastole because the heart spends more time in diastole; it must remain above approximately 60 mmHg to adequately perfuse vital organs.

What is orthostatic hypotension?

Orthostatic hypotension is a drop of at least 20 mmHg systolic or 10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing; gravity causes blood to pool in the lower extremities, and if compensatory mechanisms fail, cerebral perfusion drops causing dizziness or fainting.

What is an ACE inhibitor?

ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) inhibitors block the enzyme that converts angiotensin I to the potent vasoconstrictor angiotensin II, resulting in vasodilation, reduced aldosterone secretion, and lower blood pressure.

What is hypotension?

Hypotension occurs when blood pressure is too low to adequately perfuse organs, potentially causing dizziness, fainting (syncope), confusion, and in severe cases, shock with organ failure due to insufficient blood flow.

What is syncope?

Syncope is a transient loss of consciousness and postural tone due to reduced cerebral blood flow, often caused by hypotension, and typically resolves quickly once the person is in a recumbent position and blood flow to the brain is restored.

An elderly patient on blood pressure medication stands up quickly from bed and feels extremely dizzy, nearly fainting. The nurse checks blood pressure lying down (140/85) and standing (95/60). What is this condition and why did it occur?

Antihypertensive medications can impair the baroreceptor reflex and reduce the compensatory vasoconstriction needed when standing; combined with age-related blunting of baroreceptors, this causes a significant BP drop upon standing, reducing cerebral perfusion.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does the DASH diet mean in the context of hypertension management?

The DASH diet is an evidence-based dietary plan specifically designed to reduce blood pressure through reduced sodium intake and emphasis on potassium-rich fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy products.

Example: A 55-year-old patient with no symptoms has a blood pressure reading of 155/95 mmHg on three separate visits. The physician explains that untreated hypertension can damage multiple organs. Which organs are most at risk from chronic hypertension?

Chronic hypertension causes end-organ damage to the heart (hypertrophy, heart failure), brain (stroke), kidneys (nephrosclerosis, renal failure), and eyes (hypertensive retinopathy), making it a leading risk factor for cardiovascular morbidity and mortality.
