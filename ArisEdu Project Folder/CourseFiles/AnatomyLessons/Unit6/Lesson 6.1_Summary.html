Lesson 6.1: Structure of the Heart - Summary

Key Concepts: Structure of the Heart

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the myocardium?

The myocardium is the middle and thickest layer of the heart wall, composed of cardiac muscle tissue that contracts to pump blood through the chambers.

What is the endocardium?

The endocardium is the thin, smooth endothelial layer that lines the interior of the heart chambers and covers the heart valves, reducing friction as blood flows through.

What is the pericardium?

The pericardium consists of a fibrous outer layer and a serous inner layer, forming a protective sac that encloses the heart and contains a small amount of lubricating pericardial fluid.

What is the interventricular septum?

The interventricular septum is a thick muscular wall dividing the left and right ventricles, ensuring that oxygenated and deoxygenated blood do not mix.

What is the fossa ovalis?

The fossa ovalis is a shallow depression in the interatrial septum marking where the foramen ovale existed during fetal development, allowing blood to bypass the non-functioning fetal lungs.

What is the coronary sinus?

The coronary sinus is the main venous drainage vessel of the myocardium, collecting blood from cardiac veins and emptying it into the right atrium so it can be reoxygenated.

What is a heart murmur?

A heart murmur is an extra or unusual sound during the heartbeat cycle, typically caused by turbulent blood flow through stenotic or regurgitant valves, though some murmurs are benign.

A 72-year-old woman presents with fatigue and shortness of breath. An echocardiogram shows severe aortic stenosis. Which valve is narrowed, and what is the immediate hemodynamic consequence?

Aortic stenosis narrows the aortic semilunar valve at the exit of the left ventricle, forcing the LV to generate much higher pressures to push blood into the aorta, eventually leading to left ventricular hypertrophy and heart failure.

Real-World Applications

Example: A 58-year-old man experiences crushing chest pain during exercise. An angiogram reveals a 90% blockage in his left anterior descending artery. Which structure's blood supply is most directly compromised?

The left anterior descending artery is the main branch of the left coronary artery and supplies blood to the anterior wall and interventricular septum of the left ventricle, making blockage here particularly dangerous.
