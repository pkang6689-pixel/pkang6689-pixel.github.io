Lesson 6.6: Disorders of the Circulatory System - Summary

Key Concepts: Disorders of the Circulatory System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is valvular stenosis?

Valvular stenosis occurs when a heart valve becomes stiffened, thickened, or calcified, narrowing the opening and restricting blood flow, which forces the upstream chamber to work harder to push blood through the constricted orifice.

What is valvular regurgitation?

Valvular regurgitation (insufficiency) occurs when a valve fails to close completely, allowing blood to leak backward; this reduces forward cardiac output and increases the workload on the affected chamber, potentially leading to heart failure.

What is an aortic aneurysm?

An aortic aneurysm is a pathological dilation of the aortic wall caused by degenerative weakening, and it poses a serious risk of rupture, especially when it exceeds 5.5 cm in the abdominal aorta, which would cause massive internal bleeding.

What is cardiac catheterization?

Cardiac catheterization is both a diagnostic and therapeutic procedure where a thin catheter is inserted into a peripheral artery and guided to the heart to inject contrast dye for imaging coronary arteries and, if needed, perform balloon angioplasty and stent placement.

What is a pacemaker (artificial)?

An artificial pacemaker is a small battery-powered device implanted under the skin with leads attached to the heart that delivers precisely timed electrical impulses to maintain an adequate heart rate when the natural conduction system fails.

What is myocardial ischemia?

Myocardial ischemia is a reversible condition in which reduced coronary blood flow deprives the heart muscle of adequate oxygen, causing chest pain (angina) but not permanent tissue death; if prolonged, ischemia progresses to infarction.

What is reperfusion therapy?

Reperfusion therapy aims to quickly restore blood flow to ischemic heart muscle during a myocardial infarction, either mechanically through percutaneous coronary intervention (angioplasty/stenting) or pharmacologically with thrombolytic drugs that dissolve the occluding clot.

What is coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG)?

CABG is a major surgical procedure in which a healthy blood vessel (often from the leg or chest wall) is grafted to bypass a severely blocked coronary artery, restoring blood flow to the ischemic myocardium downstream of the blockage.

An ER physician tells a heart failure patient that their legs are swollen because of right-sided heart failure. The patient asks why a weak right ventricle causes leg swelling rather than breathing problems. What is the correct explanation?

In right-sided heart failure, the right ventricle cannot adequately pump blood to the lungs, so blood backs up into the systemic venous system; increased venous hydrostatic pressure forces fluid out of capillaries into the interstitial spaces, causing dependent edema in the legs, ankles, and abdomen.

A 55-year-old patient with a mechanical aortic valve replacement must take warfarin (a blood thinner) for the rest of their life. The patient asks why this is necessary. What is the primary reason?

Mechanical heart valves have artificial surfaces that activate the clotting cascade when blood contacts them; without lifelong anticoagulation, thrombi can form on the valve, potentially causing valve obstruction, systemic embolization, or stroke.

Real-World Applications

Example: A 50-year-old man undergoes cardiac catheterization revealing a 95% blockage of his left main coronary artery. The cardiologist recommends CABG rather than a stent. Why would surgery be preferred over stenting for this particular blockage?

Left main coronary artery disease is considered the most critical location because it supplies the majority of the left ventricle; CABG is often preferred because it provides multiple bypass routes and has been shown to have better long-term outcomes than stenting for this severe, high-risk anatomy.

Example: A 28-year-old woman presents with a new heart murmur after a recent strep throat infection. The physician suspects rheumatic heart disease. How does a strep infection lead to valve damage?

Rheumatic heart disease occurs when antibodies produced against group A streptococcus antigens cross-react with similar proteins in heart valve tissue (molecular mimicry), triggering an autoimmune inflammatory response that damages and scars the valves, most commonly the mitral valve.
