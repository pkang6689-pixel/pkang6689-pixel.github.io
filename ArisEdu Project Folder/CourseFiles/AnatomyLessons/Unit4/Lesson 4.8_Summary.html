Lesson 4.8: AP Prep: Neurophysiology - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Neurophysiology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a graded potential?

A graded potential is a localized, variable-amplitude change in membrane potential (either depolarizing or hyperpolarizing) that is proportional to stimulus intensity and decays over short distances, serving as the input signal that may or may not reach threshold to trigger an action potential.

What is the axon hillock?

The axon hillock is the cone-shaped region at the junction of the cell body and axon, containing the highest density of voltage-gated Na+ channels, making it the trigger zone where EPSPs and IPSPs are integrated and where action potentials are initiated if threshold is reached.

What is an electroencephalogram (EEG)?

An EEG records the summed electrical activity of millions of cortical neurons through scalp electrodes, producing waveforms (alpha, beta, theta, delta waves) that are essential for diagnosing epilepsy, sleep disorders, brain death, and monitoring anesthesia depth.

What is the equilibrium potential of an ion?

The equilibrium potential (calculated by the Nernst equation) is the membrane voltage at which the electrical gradient exactly balances the concentration gradient for a specific ion, resulting in no net flux of that ion across the membrane (e.g., approximately -90 mV for K+ and +60 mV for Na+).

What is a neuromuscular junction (NMJ)?

The neuromuscular junction is the specialized chemical synapse where a motor neuron's axon terminal meets the motor end plate of a skeletal muscle fiber; ACh released here binds to nicotinic receptors, generating an end-plate potential that triggers muscle contraction.

A physiology student is given a graph showing a neuron's membrane potential over time. The trace shows the potential rising from -70 mV to -55 mV, then rapidly spiking to +30 mV, dropping past -70 mV to -80 mV, and finally returning to -70 mV. The student is asked to identify each phase. What is the correct sequence of events?

The action potential phases are: resting potential (-70 mV) rising to threshold (-55 mV) via graded potentials, rapid depolarization to +30 mV (Na+ channel opening), repolarization back toward -70 mV (K+ channel opening, Na+ inactivation), hyperpolarization undershoot to ~-80 mV (excess K+ efflux), and gradual return to resting potential.

An AP exam question asks: 'If the extracellular K+ concentration is experimentally increased from 5 mM to 15 mM, what happens to the resting membrane potential and neuronal excitability?' What is the correct answer?

Increasing extracellular K+ reduces the K+ concentration gradient across the membrane, meaning less K+ flows out through leak channels, resulting in a less negative (depolarized) resting potential. This brings the membrane closer to threshold, making the neuron hyperexcitable. This is clinically relevant in hyperkalemia.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does 'voltage-gated channel' mean?

Voltage-gated channels are membrane proteins that change conformation (open or close) in response to changes in the electrical potential across the membrane, such as the voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels critical for action potential generation and propagation.

Example: A researcher applies tetrodotoxin (TTX, from puffer fish) to a neuron in a laboratory experiment. TTX specifically blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels. What would the researcher observe on an oscilloscope when attempting to stimulate this neuron?

TTX blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels, which are essential for the depolarization phase of the action potential. Without Na+ influx, the membrane cannot depolarize to generate an action potential, resulting in complete nerve block, similar to how local anesthetics work.

Increasing extracellular K+ reduces the K+ concentration gradient across the membrane, meaning less K+ flows out through leak channels, resulting in a less negative (depolarized) resting potential. This brings the membrane closer to threshold, making the neuron hyperexcitable. This is clinically relevant in hyperkalemia.

Example: An AP exam scenario describes a neuron receiving 10 EPSPs and 8 IPSPs simultaneously. The net effect is a small depolarization that does not reach threshold. What will happen at the axon hillock?

The axon hillock integrates all incoming EPSPs (depolarizing) and IPSPs (hyperpolarizing) through summation. Although the EPSPs numerically outnumber the IPSPs, the net depolarization is subthreshold, so no action potential fires. The all-or-none principle means no 'partial' AP is possible.

Example: A clinical neuroscience exam asks why general anesthesia works to render patients unconscious and unable to feel pain during surgery. Based on your understanding of neurophysiology, which mechanism best explains general anesthesia?

Most general anesthetics work through dual mechanisms: enhancing GABA-mediated inhibition (increasing Cl- influx to hyperpolarize neurons) and blocking glutamate/NMDA-mediated excitation, resulting in widespread suppression of CNS activity that produces unconsciousness, amnesia, analgesia, and muscle relaxation.

Example: An AP exam question presents two patients: Patient A has damage to the sympathetic chain ganglia, and Patient B has damage to the vagus nerve. When both patients exercise, which one would have a more dangerous cardiac response, and why?

Patient A (sympathetic damage) cannot adequately increase heart rate, contractility, and cardiac output during exercise because the sympathetic system drives the exercise cardiac response. Patient B (vagal damage) would actually have a higher resting heart rate but could still increase it somewhat through circulating catecholamines.
