Lesson 4.1: Neurons & Neuroglia - Summary

Key Concepts: Neurons & Neuroglia

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a neuron?

A neuron is the fundamental functional unit of the nervous system, specialized for generating and propagating electrical signals (action potentials) and communicating with other cells via neurotransmitters.

What is neuroglia?

Neuroglia (glial cells) are the non-neuronal cells of the nervous system that provide structural support, metabolic assistance, insulation (myelination), and immune defense without directly transmitting electrical signals.

What is an astrocyte?

Astrocytes are the most abundant glial cells in the CNS, performing critical functions including forming the blood-brain barrier with their end-feet, regulating ion concentrations, recycling neurotransmitters, and providing metabolic support to neurons.

What is the function of microglia?

Microglia are small, motile glial cells that act as the resident macrophages of the central nervous system, constantly surveilling brain tissue and responding to injury or infection by engulfing debris and pathogens.

What is an interneuron?

Interneurons (also called association neurons) are found entirely within the CNS and serve as the integrating link between sensory input and motor output, making up the vast majority of neurons in the body.

What is the blood-brain barrier?

The blood-brain barrier is a highly selective semipermeable structure formed by specialized endothelial tight junctions and astrocyte end-feet that protects the brain by preventing most toxins, pathogens, and large molecules from crossing from the blood into brain tissue.

A medical student is examining a patient who suffered a spinal cord injury at the thoracic level. The attending physician explains that unlike peripheral nerves, the damaged CNS neurons are unlikely to regenerate. What is the primary reason CNS neurons have such limited regenerative capacity?

CNS regeneration is severely limited because oligodendrocytes and other CNS components produce growth-inhibiting molecules, and glial scarring (by astrocytes) forms a physical barrier, unlike the PNS where Schwann cells actively promote and guide axon regrowth.

Real-World Applications

Example: A 45-year-old woman presents to her neurologist with progressive numbness, tingling, and vision problems. An MRI reveals multiple areas of demyelination in her brain and spinal cord. Which cells are being damaged by the autoimmune attack in this condition?

This presentation is consistent with multiple sclerosis (MS), an autoimmune disease in which the immune system attacks oligodendrocytes and the myelin they produce in the CNS, leading to impaired nerve signal conduction.

Example: A pediatrician explains to worried parents that their newborn's nerve conduction is slower than an adult's but will improve with age. What developmental process is primarily responsible for this speed increase?

Myelination is an ongoing developmental process that continues into the mid-20s, with progressive wrapping of myelin around axons enabling faster saltatory conduction and more efficient neural processing as the child matures.

Example: A neurosurgeon is operating near the brain's ventricles and must be careful not to damage the cells lining these cavities. Which type of glial cell lines the ventricles and helps produce and circulate cerebrospinal fluid?

Ependymal cells are ciliated glial cells that line the ventricles of the brain and the central canal of the spinal cord, playing a key role in producing, circulating, and monitoring cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
