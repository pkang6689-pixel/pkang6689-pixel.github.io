Key Concepts: Action Potentials & Synaptic Transmission

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an action potential?

An action potential is a brief, self-propagating electrical signal in which the membrane potential rapidly reverses from negative to positive and back, traveling along the axon to transmit information over long distances.

What is an excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP)?

An EPSP is a small, graded depolarization in the postsynaptic neuron caused by excitatory neurotransmitter binding, which pushes the membrane potential toward threshold and increases the probability of firing an action potential.

What is an inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP)?

An IPSP is a graded hyperpolarization that makes the postsynaptic membrane more negative (farther from threshold), typically caused by Cl- influx or K+ efflux, reducing the likelihood of an action potential.

What is a synapse?

A synapse is the specialized junction between two neurons (or a neuron and its target cell) consisting of the presynaptic terminal, synaptic cleft, and postsynaptic membrane, where communication occurs primarily through neurotransmitter release.

What is a neurotransmitter?

Neurotransmitters are chemical signaling molecules (such as acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, GABA, and glutamate) stored in synaptic vesicles and released into the synaptic cleft to transmit signals between neurons or to effector cells.

A neuroscience researcher is studying a nerve fiber that conducts impulses at 120 m/s, while another fiber in the same nerve bundle conducts at only 2 m/s. What is the most likely difference between these two fibers?

Conduction velocity depends on both axon diameter and myelination; large-diameter myelinated fibers (like A-alpha motor neurons) conduct at up to 120 m/s via saltatory conduction, while small unmyelinated C fibers conduct as slowly as 0.5-2 m/s.
