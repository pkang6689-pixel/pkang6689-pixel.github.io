Key Concepts: Autonomic Nervous System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the autonomic nervous system (ANS)?

The autonomic nervous system is the involuntary motor division of the PNS that controls visceral functions through its sympathetic and parasympathetic branches, regulating heart rate, digestion, respiration, and other functions without conscious effort.

What is a beta-blocker?

Beta-blockers are drugs (such as metoprolol and atenolol) that block beta-adrenergic receptors on the heart and blood vessels, reducing the effects of sympathetic stimulation and thereby lowering heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure.

What is a nicotinic receptor?

Nicotinic receptors are ligand-gated ion channels activated by acetylcholine (and nicotine) found at all autonomic ganglia (both sympathetic and parasympathetic) and at the skeletal muscle neuromuscular junction.

A trauma surgeon notes that a patient in hemorrhagic shock has a rapid heart rate, pale and cool skin, and dilated pupils. These signs are all due to a massive sympathetic response. What is the physiological purpose of each of these responses?

In hemorrhagic shock, the sympathetic system activates compensatory mechanisms: tachycardia maintains cardiac output with reduced blood volume, peripheral vasoconstriction (causing pale, cool skin) shunts blood to vital organs, and pupil dilation enhances visual awareness of threats.

A physician explains to a patient that the autonomic nervous system uses a 'two-neuron chain' to reach target organs, unlike the somatic system which uses a single motor neuron. A student asks why this two-neuron design is beneficial. What is the advantage?

The two-neuron chain (preganglionic to postganglionic) provides an additional synapse in the ganglion where signals can be modulated, integrated, and distributed to multiple postganglionic neurons, allowing a single preganglionic neuron to coordinate widespread, simultaneous responses across multiple target organs.
