Lesson 4.6: Reflex Arcs - Summary

Key Concepts: Reflex Arcs

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a reflex arc?

A reflex arc is the complete neural pathway from stimulus detection to response execution, consisting of five components: a receptor that detects the stimulus, a sensory neuron, an integration center in the CNS, a motor neuron, and an effector that produces the response.

What is the Babinski reflex?

The Babinski sign is an upward fanning of the toes when the sole is stroked, which is normal in infants (whose corticospinal tracts are not yet fully myelinated) but abnormal in adults, indicating upper motor neuron damage to the corticospinal tract.

What is the crossed extensor reflex?

The crossed extensor reflex is a paired response that accompanies the withdrawal reflex: while the stimulated limb flexes away from the painful stimulus, the contralateral limb extends to bear the body's weight and maintain balance, such as when stepping on a sharp object.

What is a stretch reflex?

The stretch reflex (myotatic reflex) is a monosynaptic response in which stretching a muscle stimulates its muscle spindles, which send afferent signals to the spinal cord, triggering the same muscle to contract and resist the stretch; the patellar reflex is the classic example.

What is an autonomic reflex?

Autonomic (visceral) reflexes are involuntary reflex responses that regulate internal organ functions, such as the baroreceptor reflex for blood pressure regulation, the pupillary light reflex, and the micturition (urination) reflex.

Real-World Applications

Example: A neurologist examines a patient who suffered a stroke and finds that the reflexes in both legs are abnormally brisk (hyperreflexic) with a positive Babinski sign bilaterally. What does this combination of findings indicate about the level of nervous system damage?

Hyperreflexia combined with a positive Babinski sign indicates an upper motor neuron (UMN) lesion, meaning the descending inhibitory pathways from the brain (particularly the corticospinal tract) are damaged, releasing spinal reflexes from cortical control and producing exaggerated responses.

Example: During a routine physical exam, a physician tests a patient's deep tendon reflexes and grades them on a 0-4 scale. The patient's biceps and triceps reflexes are both graded as 3+ (hyperactive) with clonus. What does this clinical finding suggest?

Hyperactive reflexes (3+ to 4+) with clonus (rhythmic, involuntary muscle contractions) indicate upper motor neuron pathology where descending inhibitory signals from the brain are disrupted, leaving spinal reflexes uninhibited and exaggerated.

Example: A nursing student observes a physician performing the Achilles tendon reflex on a patient with diabetes. The reflex is absent bilaterally. The physician explains this is due to diabetic peripheral neuropathy. Which component of the reflex arc has been damaged by the neuropathy?

Diabetic peripheral neuropathy damages the peripheral sensory and motor nerve fibers (lower motor neuron component), disrupting the reflex arc at the peripheral nerve level. Without functional sensory input or motor output, the reflex cannot be completed, resulting in areflexia.
