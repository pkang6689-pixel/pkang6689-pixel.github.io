Lesson 4.4: Peripheral Nervous System - Summary

Key Concepts: Peripheral Nervous System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an afferent neuron?

An afferent neuron is a sensory neuron that transmits information from sensory receptors (such as those detecting touch, pain, or temperature) toward the central nervous system for processing; 'afferent' means 'arriving at' the CNS.

What is an efferent neuron?

An efferent neuron is a motor neuron that transmits signals from the central nervous system outward to effector organs (skeletal muscles, smooth muscles, cardiac muscle, or glands) to produce a response; 'efferent' means 'exiting' the CNS.

What is a dermatome?

A dermatome is a specific region of skin supplied by sensory fibers from a single spinal nerve root; dermatome maps are used clinically to assess the level of spinal cord injury or nerve root compression based on areas of sensory loss.

What is a nociceptor?

Nociceptors are free nerve endings that function as pain receptors, detecting potentially harmful stimuli such as extreme temperature, intense pressure, or tissue-damaging chemicals; the term 'noci-' comes from Latin meaning 'harmful' or 'injurious.'

What is a nerve plexus?

A nerve plexus is a branching network where ventral rami of spinal nerves merge, exchange fibers, and redistribute to form named peripheral nerves; the major plexuses include the cervical, brachial, lumbar, and sacral plexuses.

What is a mechanoreceptor?

Mechanoreceptors are sensory receptors that detect mechanical stimuli including touch (Meissner's corpuscles), deep pressure (Pacinian corpuscles), stretch (muscle spindles), and vibration, converting physical deformation into electrical nerve signals.

What is Bell's palsy?

Bell's palsy is an acute condition involving inflammation or compression of the facial nerve (CN VII), resulting in sudden one-sided facial paralysis with inability to close the eye, smile, or control facial muscles on the affected side.

A patient presents to the emergency department with left arm and jaw pain during a heart attack. The ER physician explains this is 'referred pain.' A medical student asks why cardiac pain is felt in the arm and jaw rather than the chest. What is the underlying mechanism?

Referred pain occurs because visceral afferent fibers from the heart and somatic afferent fibers from the left arm and jaw converge on the same dorsal horn neurons in the spinal cord (T1-T5), and the brain misinterprets the visceral pain as coming from the more commonly stimulated somatic areas.

A medical student is learning to perform a cranial nerve examination. When testing CN V (trigeminal), the student lightly touches the patient's forehead, cheek, and jaw with a cotton wisp, and also asks the patient to clench their teeth. What is the student testing with each maneuver?

The trigeminal nerve has both sensory and motor components; light touch on the forehead (V1), cheek (V2), and jaw (V3) tests the sensory divisions, while asking the patient to clench evaluates the motor branch that innervates the muscles of mastication (temporalis and masseter).

Real-World Applications

Example: A construction worker falls from scaffolding and injures his brachial plexus on the right side. During the neurological exam, he cannot raise his right arm, flex his elbow, or grip with his right hand, but his legs and left arm function normally. Why is the deficit limited to his right upper limb?

The brachial plexus is formed from spinal nerves C5-T1 and is the sole nerve supply to the upper extremity; unilateral damage affects only the ipsilateral arm because each side has its own separate brachial plexus.

Example: A surgeon is performing an operation near the parotid gland and accidentally damages the facial nerve (CN VII). What postoperative deficit would the patient most likely experience?

The facial nerve (CN VII) passes through the parotid gland and provides motor innervation to all muscles of facial expression; surgical damage results in ipsilateral facial paralysis affecting the ability to smile, close the eye, raise the eyebrow, and control lip movements.
