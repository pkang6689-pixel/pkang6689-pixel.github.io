Lesson 4.3: Central Nervous System (Brain & Spinal Cord) - Summary

Key Concepts: Central Nervous System (Brain & Spinal Cord)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the cerebral cortex?

The cerebral cortex is the highly folded outer layer of gray matter (2-4 mm thick) covering the cerebral hemispheres, containing billions of neuron cell bodies responsible for consciousness, thought, language, memory, and voluntary movement.

What is cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)?

Cerebrospinal fluid is a clear fluid produced mainly by the choroid plexuses in the brain ventricles that circulates through the ventricles and subarachnoid space, cushioning the CNS, removing metabolic waste, and maintaining chemical homeostasis.

What is Broca's area?

Broca's area is located in the posterior part of the left frontal lobe (inferior frontal gyrus) and is responsible for speech production and language processing; damage leads to Broca's aphasia, where patients understand language but struggle to produce fluent speech.

What is Wernicke's area?

Wernicke's area is located at the junction of the temporal and parietal lobes (usually left hemisphere) and is critical for understanding spoken and written language; damage results in fluent but nonsensical speech and impaired comprehension.

What is the limbic system?

The limbic system is a collection of deep brain structures including the amygdala (fear and emotion), hippocampus (memory formation), and cingulate gyrus that together regulate emotional responses, memory consolidation, and motivational behaviors.

What is the hippocampus?

The hippocampus is a seahorse-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe that plays a critical role in converting short-term memories into long-term memories and in spatial navigation; bilateral damage results in the inability to form new declarative memories.

What is a ventricle in the context of the brain?

The brain contains four ventricles (two lateral, the third, and the fourth) that are interconnected fluid-filled cavities where CSF is produced by the choroid plexus and circulated to cushion and nourish the CNS.

A child is diagnosed with hydrocephalus, a condition in which cerebrospinal fluid accumulates excessively within the brain ventricles. If left untreated, what is the primary danger of this condition?

Hydrocephalus occurs when CSF drainage is blocked or absorption is impaired, causing fluid buildup in the ventricles that increases intracranial pressure, compresses delicate brain tissue, and can lead to permanent brain damage if not treated with a shunt to drain excess fluid.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

The hippocampus is essential for converting short-term memories into long-term memories; injury to this structure causes both retrograde amnesia (loss of recent pre-injury memories) and anterograde amnesia (inability to form new memories) until the structure recovers.

The postcentral gyrus is the primary somatosensory cortex, and because sensory pathways cross over (decussate), the left parietal cortex receives somatosensory input from the right side of the body, so surgery in this area could impair touch, pain, and temperature perception on the contralateral (right) side.

Real-World Applications

Example: A 68-year-old man is brought to the emergency room with sudden-onset slurred speech, right-sided facial drooping, and weakness in his right arm. A CT scan reveals a blood clot in his left middle cerebral artery. Which type of stroke is this, and why does the left-sided blockage cause right-sided symptoms?

This is an ischemic stroke (caused by a clot blocking blood flow). Motor pathways decussate (cross over) at the medullary pyramids in the brainstem, so damage to the left motor cortex produces contralateral (right-sided) weakness and facial drooping.

Example: A patient with damage to Broca's area can understand everything said to them but speaks in broken, effortful phrases like 'want...water...please.' A different patient with damage to Wernicke's area speaks fluently but says nonsensical sentences and cannot understand questions. Which lobe houses each of these language areas?

Broca's area in the left frontal lobe controls motor speech production (damage causes non-fluent aphasia), while Wernicke's area at the left temporal-parietal junction handles language comprehension (damage causes fluent but meaningless speech with poor comprehension).

Example: A neurologist examines a patient who suffered a traumatic brain injury to the cerebellum. Which of the following deficits would you expect the physician to observe during the examination?

Cerebellar damage results in ataxia (uncoordinated movement), dysmetria (inability to judge distances accurately), intention tremor, impaired balance, and difficulty with smooth, coordinated motor tasks like walking or touching the nose with a finger.

Example: A researcher is studying a patient whose corpus callosum was surgically severed to treat severe epilepsy (split-brain patient). When an image is shown only to the patient's left visual field, the patient cannot verbally name the object. Why?

In a split-brain patient, the left visual field is processed by the right hemisphere, but since the corpus callosum (the bridge between hemispheres) is severed, visual information cannot reach the left hemisphere where language centers (Broca's and Wernicke's areas) are located, making verbal naming impossible.

Example: A patient in the ICU has suffered severe damage to the medulla oblongata from a brainstem stroke. The medical team places the patient on a ventilator immediately. Why is damage to this specific brain region life-threatening?

The medulla oblongata houses the cardiac center, vasomotor center, and respiratory center that control heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing. Damage to these vital autonomic centers can result in cessation of breathing and cardiac function, making it immediately life-threatening.
