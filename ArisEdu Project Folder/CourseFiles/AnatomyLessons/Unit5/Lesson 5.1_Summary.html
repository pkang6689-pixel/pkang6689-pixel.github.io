Lesson 5.1: Hormones & Chemical Signaling - Summary

Key Concepts: Hormones & Chemical Signaling

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an endocrine gland?

An endocrine gland is a ductless organ that synthesizes hormones and releases them directly into the blood, in contrast to exocrine glands which use ducts to deliver secretions to epithelial surfaces.

What is up-regulation?

Up-regulation occurs when a cell increases the number of receptors for a particular hormone, typically in response to prolonged low hormone levels, making the cell more sensitive to even small amounts of that hormone.

What is a paracrine signal?

Paracrine signals are chemical messengers like prostaglandins and histamine that act locally on neighboring cells, diffusing through the interstitial fluid rather than traveling through the bloodstream.

What is an autocrine signal?

An autocrine signal is a chemical messenger released by a cell that binds to receptors on that same cell, allowing the cell to regulate its own activity, as seen with certain growth factors and immune cell cytokines.

What is a prostaglandin?

Prostaglandins are eicosanoids derived from arachidonic acid (a fatty acid) that act as local paracrine signals involved in inflammation, pain sensation, blood clotting, and regulation of smooth muscle contraction.

A nurse notices that a patient with severe chronic illness has become less responsive to a hormone therapy that initially worked well. Blood levels of the hormone are actually higher than normal. What is the most likely explanation for the decreased response?

Prolonged exposure to elevated hormone levels commonly leads to down-regulation, where target cells internalize and degrade receptors, reducing their total number and making the cells less responsive despite adequate or excessive hormone concentrations.

A patient with a thyroid condition is prescribed a hormone that is lipid-soluble. The patient asks why the medication must be taken consistently for weeks before full effects are noticed, unlike a fast-acting pain reliever. What is the best explanation?

Lipid-soluble hormones like thyroid hormones enter cells and modify gene transcription, leading to new protein synthesis; this genomic mechanism produces longer-lasting but slower-onset effects compared to the rapid second messenger cascades used by water-soluble hormones.

Real-World Applications

Example: cAMP is an example of a:

Cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) is one of the most common second messengers, relaying and amplifying the hormone's signal inside the cell after the hormone binds its surface receptor.

Example: A researcher notices that when she adds a specific hormone to a cell culture, no intracellular response occurs even though the hormone is present in high concentrations. When she examines the cells, she finds they lack the receptor for that hormone. Which principle of endocrine signaling does this best demonstrate?

This scenario illustrates the lock-and-key principle of hormone specificity: a hormone can only exert its effect on cells that express the appropriate receptor, and without that receptor the cell cannot respond regardless of hormone concentration.

Example: A student is comparing the response times of the nervous and endocrine systems for an exam. She notes that when a person touches a hot stove, the hand withdraws in milliseconds, but when blood glucose rises, insulin takes minutes to noticeably lower it. What fundamental difference explains this?

The nervous system transmits electrochemical impulses along dedicated neural pathways at high speed for rapid responses, while the endocrine system relies on hormones traveling through the bloodstream, resulting in slower onset but more sustained, widespread effects.
