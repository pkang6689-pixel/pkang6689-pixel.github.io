Lesson 5.4: Feedback Loops in Endocrine Control - Summary

Key Concepts: Feedback Loops in the Endocrine System

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a negative feedback loop?

A negative feedback loop is a homeostatic mechanism in which the end product of a pathway inhibits the initial stimulus or earlier steps, preventing overproduction and maintaining the regulated variable within a narrow range around its set point.

What is a positive feedback loop?

A positive feedback loop amplifies the initiating stimulus rather than opposing it, creating an escalating cycle that continues until an external event terminates the loop, as seen in childbirth (oxytocin) and the LH surge during ovulation.

What is TRH?

TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) is a tripeptide hormone secreted by the hypothalamus into the hypophyseal portal system, stimulating thyrotroph cells of the anterior pituitary to produce and secrete TSH, which then acts on the thyroid gland.

What is CRH?

CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone) is a 41-amino-acid peptide secreted by the hypothalamus in response to stress, stimulating the anterior pituitary to release ACTH, which then stimulates the adrenal cortex to produce cortisol.

What is GnRH?

GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) is a decapeptide released in a pulsatile manner from the hypothalamus, stimulating the anterior pituitary gonadotrophs to secrete FSH and LH, which then regulate reproductive function.

What is a releasing hormone?

Releasing hormones are peptides produced by hypothalamic neurons and secreted into the hypophyseal portal system; they stimulate specific anterior pituitary cell types to synthesize and release their respective hormones, such as TRH stimulating TSH release.

A patient has been taking prednisone (a synthetic cortisol) daily for six months to manage an autoimmune condition. The physician insists the medication must be tapered slowly rather than stopped abruptly. Based on your knowledge of HPA axis feedback, what is the danger of sudden withdrawal?

Long-term exogenous cortisol suppresses the HPA axis through negative feedback, causing CRH and ACTH to decrease and the adrenal cortex to atrophy; sudden withdrawal means the atrophied adrenals cannot produce sufficient cortisol, potentially causing a fatal adrenal crisis with hypotension and shock.

A medical student examines lab results for a patient: TSH is very high, and T4 is very low. The attending physician asks the student to determine whether this is a primary or secondary thyroid problem. What is the correct interpretation?

High TSH with low T4 indicates primary hypothyroidism: the thyroid gland itself is failing to produce adequate hormones, so the loss of negative feedback causes the pituitary to compensate by increasing TSH output in an attempt to stimulate the failing gland.

A couple undergoing fertility treatment learns that the woman's estrogen levels have reached a critical threshold mid-cycle. The reproductive endocrinologist explains that estrogen is now having the opposite effect on LH compared to earlier in the cycle. What is happening?

During most of the follicular phase, moderate estrogen levels exert negative feedback on LH; however, when estrogen from the dominant follicle reaches a critical threshold, it triggers a switch to positive feedback, causing a rapid surge in LH that triggers ovulation.

A medical student notices that a patient's cortisol levels are highest at 8 AM and lowest at midnight. The student asks why cortisol is not constant throughout the day. What is the best explanation?

Cortisol secretion follows a circadian rhythm controlled by the suprachiasmatic nucleus and the HPA axis, with peak levels in the early morning hours to prepare the body for waking activity and a gradual decline throughout the day to its nadir around midnight.

Real-World Applications

Example: An example of positive feedback in the endocrine system is:

Oxytocin during childbirth is a classic positive feedback example: uterine contractions stimulate oxytocin release, which increases contraction strength, which stimulates even more oxytocin, escalating until delivery occurs.

Example: The LH surge that triggers ovulation is an example of:

During the menstrual cycle, when estrogen levels reach a critical threshold, they switch from negative to positive feedback on the anterior pituitary, causing a massive surge in LH secretion that triggers ovulation.

Example: A patient with chronic unmanaged stress presents with weight gain, elevated blood sugar, immune suppression, and anxiety. The physician explains that the patient's stress response has been chronically activated. Which axis is most directly responsible for these symptoms?

Chronic stress persistently activates the HPA axis, leading to sustained high cortisol levels that override normal negative feedback; chronically elevated cortisol causes weight gain (especially central), hyperglycemia, immune suppression, and mood disturbances.

Example: An endocrinologist is evaluating a patient with suspected Cushing's disease (an ACTH-secreting pituitary tumor). The patient has high cortisol but does not respond to a dexamethasone suppression test. What does this tell you about the feedback loop?

In Cushing's disease, a pituitary adenoma autonomously secretes ACTH regardless of cortisol levels; the dexamethasone suppression test provides exogenous cortisol-like negative feedback, but the tumor does not respond because it operates independently of normal feedback control.
