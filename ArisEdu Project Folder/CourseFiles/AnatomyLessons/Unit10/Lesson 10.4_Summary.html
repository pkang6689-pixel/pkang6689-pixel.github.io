Lesson 10.4: Review of Key Anatomy & Physiology Concepts - Summary

Key Concepts: Review of Key Concepts

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is hematopoiesis?

Hematopoiesis is the process by which all blood cells (red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets) are produced from multipotent hematopoietic stem cells in the red bone marrow. This continuous process replaces the billions of blood cells that are destroyed daily throughout a person's lifetime.

What is a sarcomere?

A sarcomere is the fundamental contractile unit of striated muscle, bounded by Z-lines (Z-discs) on each end. It contains organized arrays of thick myosin filaments and thin actin filaments whose overlapping interaction produces the force of contraction as described by the sliding filament theory.

What is the glomerular filtration rate (GFR)?

The glomerular filtration rate (GFR) measures the volume of plasma filtered through the glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule per minute, normally about 125 mL/min or 180 L/day. It is considered the best overall indicator of kidney function and is used to stage chronic kidney disease.

What is hemoglobin?

Hemoglobin is a metalloprotein found in red blood cells that consists of four subunits, each containing an iron-containing heme group. Each heme group can reversibly bind one oxygen molecule, allowing each hemoglobin molecule to carry up to four O2 molecules from the lungs to the tissues.

What is the meaning of 'antagonistic hormones'?

Antagonistic hormones have opposite effects on the same physiological variable, allowing precise homeostatic control. The classic example is insulin (lowers blood glucose) and glucagon (raises blood glucose), which work in opposition to maintain blood sugar within a narrow normal range.

What is the menstrual cycle?

The menstrual cycle is an approximately 28-day reproductive cycle regulated by the interplay of four key hormones: FSH and LH from the anterior pituitary, and estrogen and progesterone from the ovaries. These hormones coordinate follicular development, ovulation, and endometrial preparation for potential embryo implantation.

What is the lymphatic system's dual role in the body?

The lymphatic system serves two critical functions: maintaining fluid balance by collecting excess interstitial fluid and returning it to the venous blood supply, and providing immune surveillance through lymph nodes where immune cells monitor lymph for pathogens and mount immune responses when threats are detected.

A physical therapy student is working with a patient who has a torn ACL (anterior cruciate ligament). The patient asks why a torn ligament does not heal as quickly as a muscle strain. Using knowledge of tissue types, what is the best explanation?

Ligaments are composed of dense regular connective tissue with relatively poor blood supply compared to skeletal muscle, which is highly vascularized. Since healing depends on blood delivering oxygen, nutrients, and repair cells to the injury site, the limited vascularity of ligaments results in significantly slower and often incomplete healing.

During a lab practical, a student examines a histology slide showing tissue with closely packed cells, no blood vessels, and a basement membrane. The instructor asks the student to identify the tissue type and explain where it would be found. What is the correct identification?

The three key features described (closely packed cells, avascular, basement membrane) are hallmarks of epithelial tissue. Epithelial tissue lines and covers body surfaces and cavities, receives nutrients by diffusion from underlying connective tissue, and sits on a basement membrane that anchors it to the tissue below.

An anatomy instructor presents a case of a patient with myasthenia gravis who experiences progressive muscle weakness that worsens throughout the day. The instructor asks students to connect this disease to their knowledge of the neuromuscular junction. What is the underlying pathophysiology?

Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder in which antibodies target and destroy acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. With fewer functional receptors, acetylcholine released by motor neurons cannot effectively trigger muscle contraction, causing progressive weakness that worsens with repetitive use as remaining receptors become increasingly depleted.

A medical student is reviewing a case where a patient's blood pH dropped to 7.25 after a severe asthma attack. The student must explain why prolonged bronchospasm leads to respiratory acidosis. What is the correct pathophysiological chain?

During a severe asthma attack, bronchospasm severely restricts airflow, preventing adequate CO2 exhalation. The retained CO2 dissolves in blood to form carbonic acid (CO2 + H2O -> H2CO3 -> H+ + HCO3-), increasing hydrogen ion concentration and causing respiratory acidosis with a pH below the normal 7.35-7.45 range.

A patient with liver cirrhosis develops severe bleeding from a minor cut that will not stop. Lab results show prolonged prothrombin time (PT) and low levels of clotting factors. A student is asked to connect liver function to the coagulation system. What is the best explanation?

The liver is the primary site of synthesis for most coagulation factors, including fibrinogen and vitamin K-dependent factors II, VII, IX, and X. When cirrhosis destroys liver tissue, the organ can no longer produce adequate clotting factors, resulting in coagulopathy evidenced by prolonged PT and inability to form stable blood clots at injury sites.

An AP exam question describes a patient who takes a diuretic medication for high blood pressure and then develops muscle cramps and an irregular heartbeat. A student must identify the connection between the urinary, muscular, and cardiovascular systems in this scenario. What is the best answer?

This scenario demonstrates urinary-muscular-cardiovascular integration. Many diuretics increase renal potassium excretion, causing hypokalemia. Potassium is critical for repolarization during action potentials in both skeletal and cardiac muscle, so deficiency causes skeletal muscle cramps and can lead to dangerous cardiac arrhythmias such as ventricular tachycardia.

Real-World Applications

Example: A student is reviewing for the AP exam and encounters a question about a patient whose blood calcium is dangerously low after thyroid surgery. The patient develops muscle twitching and tingling in the fingers. Which concept from the course explains why accidental removal of the parathyroid glands during thyroid surgery leads to these neuromuscular symptoms?

This scenario connects the endocrine and musculoskeletal systems. PTH from the parathyroid glands is essential for maintaining blood calcium by stimulating bone resorption and renal calcium reabsorption. Without PTH, calcium levels plummet, increasing the excitability of neurons and muscle fibers, causing involuntary muscle contractions (tetany) and paresthesias.
