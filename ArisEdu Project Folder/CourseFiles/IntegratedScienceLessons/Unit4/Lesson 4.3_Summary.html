Lesson 4.3: Radiometric Dating & Half-Life - Summary

Key Concepts: Radiometric Dating & Half-Life

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the half-life of a radioactive isotope?

A half-life is the time required for exactly half of the parent radioactive atoms in a sample to undergo radioactive decay and transform into daughter atoms. Each radioactive isotope has a unique, constant half-life that is unaffected by external conditions.

In radioactive decay, what is the 'parent' isotope?

The parent isotope is the original unstable radioactive atom that undergoes decay. Over time, parent atoms transform into daughter atoms through radioactive decay processes. The ratio of parent to daughter isotopes in a sample is used to calculate the sample's age.

What is radiometric dating?

Radiometric dating is an absolute dating technique that determines the age of a sample by measuring the ratio of remaining parent radioactive isotopes to the daughter products they decay into. Because decay rates are constant and known, this ratio reveals how much time has elapsed since the mineral formed.

What is a daughter isotope?

A daughter isotope is the stable (or sometimes less radioactive) product formed when a parent radioactive isotope undergoes decay. For example, Lead-206 is the daughter isotope of Uranium-238. The accumulation of daughter isotopes over time is what allows scientists to calculate the age of a sample.

What is radioactive decay?

Radioactive decay is the spontaneous process by which an unstable atomic nucleus loses energy by emitting radiation in the form of alpha particles, beta particles, or gamma rays, transforming the parent atom into a different element (daughter isotope). This process occurs at a statistically predictable rate for each isotope.

What is the decay constant?

The decay constant (lambda) is a fixed probability per unit time that describes the likelihood of a given radioactive atom decaying. It is mathematically related to the half-life and is unique to each radioactive isotope. The decay constant remains unchanged by temperature, pressure, or chemical conditions.

What is the meaning of 'closure temperature' in radiometric dating?

The closure temperature is the temperature below which a mineral's crystal structure becomes a closed system that no longer allows parent or daughter isotopes to escape or enter. This effectively starts the radiometric clock, and the age measured reflects the time since the mineral cooled below this temperature.

What is potassium-argon dating used for?

Potassium-argon (K-Ar) dating measures the decay of Potassium-40 to Argon-40 (half-life ~1.25 billion years) in volcanic rocks and minerals. It is especially useful for dating volcanic ash layers and lava flows, which can in turn bracket the ages of sedimentary and fossil-bearing layers between them.

What is a mass spectrometer used for in radiometric dating?

A mass spectrometer is an analytical instrument that separates atoms by their mass-to-charge ratio, allowing scientists to precisely measure the amounts of parent and daughter isotopes in a sample. This accurate measurement of isotope ratios is essential for calculating radiometric ages.

What is the parent-daughter ratio in a sample that has undergone exactly one half-life of decay?

After exactly one half-life, 50% of the original parent atoms have decayed into daughter atoms, while 50% remain as parent atoms. This gives a 1:1 ratio of parent to daughter isotopes, which is a key reference point used in radiometric age calculations.

Real-World Applications

Example: What type of dating provides an actual numerical age in years?

Absolute dating, most commonly accomplished through radiometric methods, provides a numerical age in years for rocks and minerals. This contrasts with relative dating, which only determines the order of events without assigning specific ages.

Example: What does 'absolute age' mean in geological terms?

Absolute age refers to the specific numerical age of a rock, mineral, or geological event, typically expressed in years (or millions/billions of years). It is determined through radiometric dating techniques that measure the decay of radioactive isotopes, providing a precise time measurement rather than just a relative sequence.

Example: A scientist wants to determine the age of a dinosaur bone found between two volcanic ash layers. The ash layer below the bone dates to 72 million years ago, and the ash layer above dates to 68 million years ago. Why can't the scientist directly radiometrically date the bone itself, and what can be concluded about the bone's age?

Most radiometric dating methods work on igneous or volcanic minerals, not directly on fossils or sedimentary rock. However, by dating the volcanic ash layers above and below the bone, the scientist can bracket the bone's age to between 68 and 72 million years old. This technique of using datable layers to constrain the age of intervening strata is commonly used in paleontology.

Example: A paleontologist finds a fossil shell and wants to know its exact age. She sends a piece to a lab, but the lab returns the sample saying Carbon-14 dating is not appropriate because the shell is made of calcium carbonate that likely exchanged carbon with groundwater over time. What problem does this illustrate about radiometric dating?

Radiometric dating requires a closed system where no parent or daughter isotopes have been gained or lost since formation. If the shell exchanged carbon with groundwater, its original Carbon-14 ratio was altered (contaminated), violating the closed-system assumption and potentially yielding an inaccurate age.

Example: An oceanographer cores a sediment sample from the deep sea floor. She wants to determine the age of a shell found 2 meters below the surface. Carbon-14 dating returns an age of 60,000 years, but she suspects this is near the method's limit of detection. How should she evaluate the reliability of this age, and what alternative approaches might she consider?

Carbon-14 dating becomes unreliable beyond approximately 50,000 years because the remaining Carbon-14 is too scarce to measure accurately. At 60,000 years, the result should be treated with caution. Alternative methods such as uranium-series dating (useful for carbonates up to ~500,000 years) or oxygen isotope stratigraphy can provide independent age constraints for deep-sea samples.
