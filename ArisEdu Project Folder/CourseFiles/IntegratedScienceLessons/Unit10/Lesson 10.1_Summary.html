Lesson 10.1: Review of Key Earth Science Concepts - Summary

Key Concepts: Review of Key Earth Science Concepts

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary source of energy that drives the water cycle?

Solar energy is the primary driver of the water cycle. It heats surface water causing evaporation, drives atmospheric circulation that transports water vapor, and powers the convection that leads to cloud formation and precipitation.

What is the Coriolis effect?

The Coriolis effect is the apparent deflection of freely moving objects (including air and water masses) caused by Earth's rotation. It deflects movement to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere, influencing wind patterns and ocean currents.

What is the lithosphere?

The lithosphere is Earth's rigid outer shell, composed of the crust and the brittle uppermost portion of the upper mantle. It is broken into tectonic plates that float on the more ductile asthenosphere beneath.

What is the asthenosphere?

The asthenosphere is a zone in the upper mantle, roughly 100-660 km below the surface, that behaves in a ductile manner over geological timescales. Its plasticity allows the rigid lithospheric plates above it to move slowly across Earth's surface.

What is an aquifer?

An aquifer is a body of permeable rock or unconsolidated material (such as gravel or sand) that can store and transmit significant quantities of groundwater. The Ogallala Aquifer in the central United States is one of the world's largest.

What is the greenhouse effect?

The greenhouse effect occurs when atmospheric gases such as carbon dioxide, water vapor, and methane absorb infrared radiation emitted by Earth's surface and re-radiate it in all directions, including back toward the surface, warming the planet to habitable temperatures.

What is 'subduction' in plate tectonics?

Subduction occurs at convergent plate boundaries where a denser plate (typically oceanic) descends beneath a less dense plate and sinks into the mantle. This process creates deep ocean trenches, volcanic arcs, and is responsible for recycling oceanic crust.

What is 'isostasy' in geology?

Isostasy describes the buoyant equilibrium of the lithosphere floating on the denser asthenosphere, similar to how icebergs float in water. Thicker or less dense crust rides higher, explaining why continental crust stands higher than oceanic crust and why mountain ranges have deep crustal roots.

A student observes that the Hawaiian Islands form a chain of progressively older islands stretching to the northwest, with active volcanism only at the southeastern end (Big Island). Using the concept of plate tectonics, what is the best explanation for this pattern?

The Hawaiian Island chain provides classic evidence for plate tectonics and mantle hotspots. As the Pacific Plate moves northwest over a fixed hotspot in the mantle, each island forms over the hotspot and then is carried away, creating a chain of progressively older and more eroded islands.

Scientists discover a layer of iridium-rich clay in rock formations worldwide that dates to 66 million years ago, at the boundary between the Cretaceous and Paleogene periods. Above this layer, dinosaur fossils are absent while mammal fossils become more diverse. What is the widely accepted explanation for this evidence?

The iridium anomaly at the K-Pg boundary is strong evidence for the Chicxulub asteroid impact. Iridium is rare in Earth's crust but abundant in asteroids. The impact caused widespread wildfires, an impact winter, and acid rain that triggered the mass extinction of about 75% of species, including non-avian dinosaurs.

A coastal community notices that their beach has been losing sand steadily over the past decade. Aerial photographs show that a jetty was built at a nearby harbor entrance 12 years ago. An Earth science consultant explains the connection. What is the most likely explanation?

Jetties and groins interrupt longshore drift, the natural transport of sediment along the coast by wave action. Sand accumulates on the updrift side of the structure while the downdrift beach is deprived of its sediment supply, leading to erosion. This is a well-documented example of unintended consequences of coastal engineering.

Real-World Applications

Example: What percentage of Earth's water is freshwater available for human use (not locked in ice caps)?

Although about 3% of Earth's water is freshwater, most of it is locked in glaciers and ice caps. Less than 1% of all Earth's water is readily accessible freshwater in lakes, rivers, and shallow groundwater available for human use.

Example: A geologist examining a road cut notices a layer of light-colored sandstone sitting above a layer of dark shale, with a thin layer of conglomerate between them. The conglomerate contains rounded pebbles of various rock types. What does this sequence of layers most likely indicate about the depositional environment?

Different sedimentary rock types indicate different depositional environments. Dark shale forms in quiet, deep water; conglomerate with rounded pebbles indicates high-energy transport like a river; and sandstone suggests a moderate-energy environment like a beach or shallow sea, recording a changing depositional history.
