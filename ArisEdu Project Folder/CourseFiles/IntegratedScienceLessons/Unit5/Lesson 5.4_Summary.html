Lesson 5.4: Resource Extraction Methods (Mining, Drilling) - Summary

Key Concepts: Resource Extraction Methods (Mining, Drilling)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary difference between surface mining and subsurface mining?

Surface mining removes soil and rock (overburden) to access mineral deposits near Earth's surface, while subsurface mining involves creating underground tunnels, shafts, and chambers to reach deposits that are too deep for surface extraction. The choice depends on the depth, shape, and location of the ore body.

What is the purpose of drilling mud used during oil and gas drilling operations?

Drilling mud (drilling fluid) serves multiple critical functions: it cools and lubricates the drill bit, carries rock cuttings from the bottom of the hole to the surface, maintains hydrostatic pressure to prevent well blowouts, and stabilizes the walls of the borehole to prevent collapse.

What is the typical first step before any mining operation begins at a new site?

Before mining begins, geological surveys and exploration are conducted to locate mineral deposits, determine their size and grade, assess the feasibility of extraction, and evaluate environmental conditions. This typically involves geological mapping, core drilling, geophysical surveys, and geochemical analysis.

In open-pit mining, what is the term for the waste rock and soil removed to access the ore beneath?

Overburden is the rock, soil, and other material that lies above a mineral deposit and must be removed during surface mining to access the ore beneath. The volume of overburden relative to the volume of ore is called the stripping ratio and is a key factor in determining whether surface mining is economically viable.

What is hydraulic fracturing (fracking)?

Hydraulic fracturing (fracking) is a well-stimulation technique in which a mixture of water, sand, and chemicals is injected at high pressure into tight rock formations such as shale to create small fractures. These fractures allow trapped oil or natural gas to flow more freely to the wellbore for extraction.

What is strip mining?

Strip mining is a surface mining method used for relatively flat-lying deposits near the surface, such as coal seams. Overburden is removed in long strips, the mineral is extracted, and then the overburden from the next strip is placed into the previously mined area. This creates a characteristic terraced landscape.

What is an open-pit mine?

An open-pit mine is a large surface excavation that extracts minerals by digging progressively deeper, creating a terraced, cone-shaped pit. Heavy machinery removes rock in benches (steps), and the mine expands both downward and outward. Some open-pit mines, like the Bingham Canyon copper mine in Utah, are over a kilometer deep.

What is solution mining?

Solution mining (also called in-situ leaching) involves injecting a solvent, such as water or a dilute acid, through wells into an underground mineral deposit to dissolve the target mineral. The mineral-rich solution is then pumped to the surface for processing. This method is commonly used for salt, potash, and uranium extraction.

What is reclamation in the context of mining?

Mine reclamation is the process of restoring land that has been mined to a productive or environmentally stable condition. This includes reshaping the terrain, replacing topsoil, replanting native vegetation, and managing water drainage. Many countries require mining companies to develop reclamation plans before mining permits are issued.

What is a blowout in oil drilling?

A blowout is an uncontrolled release of crude oil, natural gas, or drilling fluids from a well when the formation pressure exceeds the pressure exerted by the column of drilling fluid. Blowout preventers (BOPs) are safety devices installed at the wellhead to seal the well and prevent blowouts, but their failure can lead to catastrophic events like the 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster.

What is the stripping ratio in surface mining?

The stripping ratio is a key economic measure in surface mining that compares the amount of overburden (waste rock and soil) that must be removed to the amount of ore that can be extracted. A high stripping ratio means more waste must be moved per unit of ore, increasing costs and making the mine less economically attractive.

What is a wellbore?

A wellbore is the actual hole drilled into Earth's subsurface during oil, gas, or water well drilling. It includes both the uncased (open) sections and the sections lined with steel casing and cement. The wellbore connects the surface to the target reservoir and serves as the conduit through which oil or gas flows to the surface.

What is acid mine drainage?

Acid mine drainage (AMD) occurs when sulfide minerals, particularly pyrite (iron sulfide), are exposed to air and water during mining. Bacteria accelerate the oxidation of these minerals, producing sulfuric acid that can contaminate streams, rivers, and groundwater with toxic heavy metals, sometimes persisting for decades or centuries after mining stops.

A mining company proposes building a new open-pit copper mine in a region that serves as the watershed for a nearby city's drinking water supply. Environmental regulators must decide whether to approve the permit. What is the primary water-related risk they should evaluate?

The primary water-related risk from copper mining in a watershed area is acid mine drainage and heavy metal contamination. When sulfide minerals associated with copper ore are exposed to air and water, they can generate acidic runoff containing dissolved copper, arsenic, lead, and other toxic metals that could contaminate the city's drinking water source.

Following the 2010 Deepwater Horizon oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico, regulators implemented new safety requirements for offshore drilling. An oil company engineer must explain to executives why the new blowout preventer requirements increase costs. What is the core safety principle behind requiring improved blowout preventers?

Blowout preventers (BOPs) are the critical last line of defense in well control, designed to seal the well if formation pressures exceed the drilling fluid's ability to contain them. The Deepwater Horizon disaster occurred partly because the BOP failed to seal the well, leading to an uncontrolled blowout that killed 11 workers and spilled millions of barrels of oil.

A community in West Virginia has experienced mountaintop removal mining for two decades. A health researcher finds that residents in the area have higher rates of respiratory illness, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers compared to residents of unmined areas. What is the most likely explanation for these health disparities?

Research has shown associations between mountaintop removal mining and negative health outcomes. The blasting and heavy equipment generate airborne dust containing silica and other particulates, while mine runoff can contaminate local water supplies. Multiple peer-reviewed studies have documented elevated rates of respiratory disease, cancer, and cardiovascular problems in communities near mountaintop removal sites.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is acid mine drainage?

Acid mine drainage (AMD) occurs when sulfide minerals, particularly pyrite (iron sulfide), are exposed to air and water during mining. Bacteria accelerate the oxidation of these minerals, producing sulfuric acid that can contaminate streams, rivers, and groundwater with toxic heavy metals, sometimes persisting for decades or centuries after mining stops.

Example: A geologist discovers that a valuable mineral deposit lies beneath a national forest with endangered species habitat. The mining company proposes using solution mining (in-situ leaching) instead of open-pit mining to reduce surface disturbance. What advantage does solution mining offer in this environmentally sensitive area?

Solution mining requires only wellheads and surface processing facilities, causing far less surface disturbance than open-pit mining. This preserves most of the forest cover and wildlife habitat. However, it still carries environmental risks including potential groundwater contamination from the leaching chemicals, so careful monitoring and management are essential.

Example: A gold mining operation in South America uses cyanide heap leaching to extract gold from low-grade ore. Environmental groups are concerned about the use of cyanide near a major river system. Which safety measure is most critical for preventing environmental damage from this extraction method?

Impermeable liners beneath cyanide heap leach pads are the most critical containment measure, preventing cyanide solution from seeping into groundwater and eventually reaching surface water systems. Proper liner installation, leak detection systems, and solution collection ponds are essential components of responsible cyanide leaching operations.
