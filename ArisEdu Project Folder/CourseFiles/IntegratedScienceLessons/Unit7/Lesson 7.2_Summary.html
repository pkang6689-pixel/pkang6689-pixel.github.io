Lesson 7.2: Earth Science & Chemistry (Atmospheric Composition, Reactions) - Summary

Key Concepts: Earth Science & Chemistry (Atmospheric Composition, Reactions)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the chemical formula for ozone, and how does it form in the stratosphere?

Ozone (O3) forms in the stratosphere when UV-C radiation splits an O2 molecule into two oxygen atoms. Each freed atom then combines with an intact O2 molecule to form O3. This process, part of the Chapman cycle, creates and maintains the protective ozone layer.

What is atmospheric composition?

Atmospheric composition refers to the specific gases and their relative abundances in Earth's atmosphere. The current composition is about 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.9% argon, and trace amounts of CO2, methane, water vapor, and other gases.

What is chemical weathering?

Chemical weathering transforms the mineral composition of rocks through reactions with water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and acids. Common types include dissolution (limestone in acid), oxidation (rusting), and hydrolysis (feldspar breaking down to clay).

What is ocean acidification?

Ocean acidification occurs when the ocean absorbs excess atmospheric CO2. The CO2 reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid (H2CO3), which dissociates and releases hydrogen ions, lowering pH. Since the industrial revolution, ocean pH has decreased by about 0.1 units.

What is a trace gas?

Trace gases make up less than 1% of the atmosphere but can have enormous impacts. CO2 (about 0.04%), methane, ozone, and nitrous oxide are all trace gases that play critical roles in the greenhouse effect, atmospheric chemistry, and climate regulation.

What is the pH scale?

The pH scale measures hydrogen ion (H+) concentration on a logarithmic scale. A pH of 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, and above 7 is basic (alkaline). Each unit change represents a 10-fold change in H+ concentration, making it relevant to water chemistry and acid rain studies.

What is the carbon-silicate weathering cycle?

The carbon-silicate weathering cycle is a long-term feedback that regulates atmospheric CO2. CO2 dissolved in rain forms carbonic acid, which weathers silicate rocks, and the dissolved products are carried to oceans where they form carbonate sediments, removing CO2 from the atmosphere over millions of years.

What is a 'photochemical reaction'?

Photochemical reactions require light energy to initiate or sustain them. In the atmosphere, photochemical reactions include ozone formation (UV splitting O2), smog formation (sunlight driving reactions between NOx and VOCs), and stratospheric ozone destruction by CFCs.

Real-World Applications

Example: A city downwind of a coal-burning power plant discovers that its marble statues and limestone buildings are dissolving and eroding faster than similar structures in cleaner areas. Chemical analysis of local rainwater shows a pH of 4.2 (normal rain is about 5.6). What atmospheric chemistry explains this damage?

Coal combustion releases sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides that react with water vapor to form sulfuric acid (H2SO4) and nitric acid (HNO3). These strong acids in precipitation react vigorously with calcium carbonate in marble and limestone, dissolving the stone surface over time.

Example: An environmental chemist measures that ocean water at a CO2 seep (where volcanic CO2 bubbles up from the seafloor) has a pH of 7.5, while surrounding water has a pH of 8.1. Near the seep, coral coverage is minimal and most organisms have thin or no calcium carbonate shells. What does this natural laboratory reveal about the future of ocean ecosystems under rising CO2?

Natural CO2 seeps serve as real-world laboratories for studying ocean acidification effects. The reduced pH and lower carbonate saturation around these seeps show that calcifying organisms like corals and shellfish are severely impacted, giving a preview of ocean ecosystems under continued CO2 rise.
