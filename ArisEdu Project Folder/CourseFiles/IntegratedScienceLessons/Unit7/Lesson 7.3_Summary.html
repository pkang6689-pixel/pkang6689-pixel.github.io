Lesson 7.3: Earth Science & Physics (Energy Transfer, Waves, Forces) - Summary

Key Concepts: Earth Science & Physics (Energy Transfer, Waves, Forces)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the relationship between wavelength and energy in electromagnetic radiation?

Electromagnetic wave energy is inversely proportional to wavelength (E = hc/wavelength). Shorter wavelengths like UV, X-rays, and gamma rays carry more energy per photon than longer wavelengths like visible light and infrared. This is why UV radiation can cause DNA damage.

What is convection?

Convection is heat transfer through fluid motion. When a fluid is heated from below, it expands, becomes less dense, and rises, while cooler, denser fluid sinks to replace it. This creates convection cells that drive atmospheric circulation, ocean currents, and mantle movement.

What is electromagnetic radiation?

Electromagnetic radiation consists of self-propagating waves of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that travel at the speed of light. The full spectrum ranges from low-energy radio waves to high-energy gamma rays, with visible light occupying a narrow band in between.

What is a seismic wave?

Seismic waves are mechanical waves that propagate through Earth after a sudden release of energy. P-waves (primary, compressional) travel fastest through all media, S-waves (secondary, shear) travel through solids only, and surface waves travel along Earth's surface causing the most damage.

What is the Coriolis effect?

The Coriolis effect is an apparent force that deflects moving objects (air, water, projectiles) on a rotating planet. It causes deflection to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere, shaping wind patterns and ocean circulation.

What is conduction?

Conduction is heat transfer through direct physical contact between molecules. Faster-vibrating (hotter) molecules transfer kinetic energy to slower-vibrating (cooler) neighbors. In Earth science, conduction warms the lowest layer of atmosphere from heated ground and transfers heat through rock.

What is isostasy?

Isostasy is the principle that Earth's crust floats on the denser mantle like an iceberg in water. Thicker, lighter continental crust rises higher, while thinner, denser oceanic crust sits lower. When ice sheets melt, the crust slowly rebounds upward as the weight is removed.

What is a P-wave (primary wave)?

P-waves are compressional waves that push and pull material in the same direction as the wave travels, like sound waves. They are the fastest seismic waves and can travel through solids, liquids, and gases, making them the first to be detected by seismographs.

Real-World Applications

Example: A geophysicist measures that since the last ice age ended, the Scandinavian Peninsula has been rising by about 1 cm per year. The thick ice sheet that covered the region melted about 10,000 years ago. What force relationship explains why the land is still rising today?

The heavy ice sheet depressed the crust into the mantle. After melting, buoyancy forces drive the crust back toward its equilibrium position (isostasy). Because the mantle is highly viscous, this rebound is extremely slow and continues thousands of years after the ice melted.

Example: A climate scientist explains that without the greenhouse effect, Earth's average surface temperature would be about -18 degrees Celsius instead of the current +15 degrees Celsius. This 33-degree warming is caused by atmospheric gases absorbing and re-emitting infrared radiation. What fundamental physics law governs how much infrared radiation Earth emits?

The Stefan-Boltzmann Law (E = sigma*T^4) governs thermal radiation. It explains why hotter objects emit vastly more radiation and how Earth's temperature adjusts to maintain energy balance. As greenhouse gases trap more outgoing infrared radiation, Earth's surface must warm to increase emissions enough to restore equilibrium.
