Lesson 7.1: Earth Science & Biology (Ecosystems, Biogeochemical Cycles) - Summary

Key Concepts: Earth Science & Biology (Ecosystems, Biogeochemical Cycles)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the role of decomposers in biogeochemical cycling?

Decomposers like bacteria and fungi break down dead organisms and waste products, releasing carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and other nutrients back into the environment. Without decomposition, nutrients would remain locked in dead matter and unavailable for new growth.

What is the primary source of energy that drives most ecosystems on Earth?

Solar radiation provides the energy that powers photosynthesis, the process by which plants, algae, and cyanobacteria convert light energy into chemical energy. This energy flows through food webs and drives the vast majority of Earth's ecosystems.

What is a biogeochemical cycle?

A biogeochemical cycle traces how chemical elements move between living organisms (bio-), Earth's crust and soil (geo-), and the atmosphere and water (chemical). These cycles connect all of Earth's systems and are essential for sustaining life.

What is an ecosystem?

An ecosystem encompasses all living organisms (biotic factors) in an area along with the nonliving physical and chemical components (abiotic factors) they interact with. Energy flows and nutrient cycles connect the biotic and abiotic components into a functional unit.

What is primary productivity?

Primary productivity measures the rate of energy capture by producers. Gross primary productivity is the total energy fixed, while net primary productivity subtracts the energy producers use for their own respiration, representing the energy available to the rest of the food web.

What is denitrification?

Denitrification is a microbial process in which anaerobic bacteria convert soil nitrates (NO3-) back into nitrogen gas (N2) or nitrous oxide (N2O), returning nitrogen to the atmosphere and completing the nitrogen cycle.

What is a trophic level?

Trophic levels categorize organisms by their position in the food chain. Producers (plants) occupy the first level, primary consumers (herbivores) the second, secondary consumers (carnivores eating herbivores) the third, and so on.

What is the water table?

The water table marks the boundary below which all pore spaces in soil and rock are saturated with groundwater. Its depth fluctuates with precipitation, drought, and human water use, and it connects the water cycle to both geological and biological systems.

What is a biome?

A biome is a major ecological community type defined primarily by climate (temperature and precipitation patterns). Examples include tropical rainforest, desert, tundra, and temperate grassland. Biomes demonstrate how Earth science factors like climate shape biological communities.

After a glacier retreats, ecologists observe a sequence of ecosystem development on the newly exposed rock: first lichens, then mosses, then small plants, then shrubs, and eventually trees. This process takes centuries. What is the role of geological weathering in this biological succession?

This is primary succession, where biological and geological processes work together. Lichens produce acids that chemically weather rock, creating tiny mineral particles. Combined with dead organic matter, these particles form primitive soil that supports increasingly complex plant communities.

Real-World Applications

Example: Marine sediment cores reveal that during past ice ages, atmospheric CO2 was about 180 ppm, while during warm interglacial periods it was about 280 ppm. These changes correlate with ocean temperature and biological productivity records in the same cores. What does this suggest about the relationship between Earth science and biology in regulating atmospheric CO2?

Ocean temperature affects CO2 solubility (colder water absorbs more), and ocean circulation determines nutrient supply to surface waters, which drives biological productivity. Phytoplankton draw down CO2 through photosynthesis. These interconnected physical and biological processes regulate the ocean's role as a carbon sink.
