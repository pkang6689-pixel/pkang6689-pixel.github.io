Lesson 7.4: Earth Science & Technology (Satellites, Climate Modeling) - Summary

Key Concepts: Earth Science & Technology (Satellites, Climate Modeling)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary purpose of the Landsat satellite program?

The Landsat program, operational since 1972, provides the longest continuous space-based record of Earth's land surface, enabling scientists to monitor deforestation, urban growth, agriculture, and natural disasters over decades.

What is the primary role of Doppler weather radar?

Doppler weather radar sends out microwave pulses and analyzes the frequency shift of returned signals to determine both the location and intensity of precipitation as well as the velocity and direction of wind within storm systems.

What is remote sensing?

Remote sensing is the science of acquiring information about Earth's surface and atmosphere from a distance, usually using electromagnetic radiation detected by sensors on satellites or aircraft, without making physical contact with the target.

What is a climate model?

A climate model is a computer-based mathematical simulation that represents the interactions among the atmosphere, oceans, land surface, and ice using physical equations to project how climate may change under different conditions.

What is spatial resolution in satellite imagery?

Spatial resolution refers to the area on the ground represented by each pixel in a satellite image. Higher spatial resolution means smaller pixel sizes, allowing finer details such as individual buildings or trees to be distinguished.

What is a Geographic Information System (GIS)?

A Geographic Information System (GIS) is a framework for gathering, managing, and analyzing spatial and geographic data. It integrates many types of data layers to help users understand patterns, relationships, and geographic context.

What is LIDAR?

LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) emits rapid laser pulses toward the ground and measures the time for each pulse to return, creating precise three-dimensional maps of terrain, vegetation canopy, buildings, and other surface features.

What is parameterization in the context of climate modeling?

Parameterization is a technique in climate modeling where small-scale processes like cloud formation, turbulence, or convection that cannot be explicitly resolved at the model's grid scale are approximated using simplified equations based on larger-scale variables.

What is spectral resolution in remote sensing?

Spectral resolution describes the number and width of wavelength bands a sensor can detect. Higher spectral resolution allows discrimination of more materials and features, such as distinguishing between different vegetation types or mineral compositions.

What is an emission scenario in climate modeling?

An emission scenario is a plausible representation of future greenhouse gas and aerosol emissions based on assumptions about socioeconomic development, energy use, land use, and policy. Climate models use these scenarios to project a range of possible future climate conditions.

What is a polar-orbiting satellite?

A polar-orbiting satellite travels in a north-south orbit passing near both poles while Earth rotates beneath it, allowing the satellite to observe virtually every part of the planet over the course of several days, typically from low Earth orbit at 700-800 km altitude.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Change detection analysis compares pre-disaster and post-disaster satellite images to rapidly identify areas where buildings have collapsed, roads are blocked, or terrain has shifted. This technique provides critical information to emergency responders within hours of a disaster.

Real-World Applications

Example: Which resolution type describes how frequently a satellite revisits and images the same location on Earth?

Temporal resolution refers to the time interval between successive observations of the same area on Earth, which determines how frequently changes can be detected. Higher temporal resolution means more frequent revisits.

Example: What is spatial resolution in satellite imagery?

Spatial resolution refers to the area on the ground represented by each pixel in a satellite image. Higher spatial resolution means smaller pixel sizes, allowing finer details such as individual buildings or trees to be distinguished.

Example: A city planning department wants to identify areas most vulnerable to flooding after a hurricane. They have satellite imagery, elevation data, and population density maps. Which technology would best allow them to overlay and analyze all these data layers together?

A GIS is specifically designed to integrate, layer, and analyze multiple types of geospatial data simultaneously. By overlaying satellite imagery, elevation models, and demographic data, planners can identify flood-prone areas with vulnerable populations.

Example: Researchers studying deforestation in the Amazon need imagery every few days to track rapid clearing events, but they also need to see through frequent cloud cover. Which satellite sensor type would best meet both requirements?

SAR uses microwave energy that penetrates clouds and works regardless of daylight conditions. A polar-orbiting SAR satellite with frequent revisit capability can track rapid deforestation events in the Amazon even during the persistently cloudy rainy season.

Example: After a major earthquake, emergency responders need to quickly assess which buildings have collapsed in an affected city. Satellite imagery from before the earthquake is available. Which approach would be most effective?

Change detection analysis compares pre-disaster and post-disaster satellite images to rapidly identify areas where buildings have collapsed, roads are blocked, or terrain has shifted. This technique provides critical information to emergency responders within hours of a disaster.

Example: An environmental agency uses LIDAR to survey a forested watershed before and after a wildfire. The post-fire LIDAR data shows a dramatic reduction in canopy height and increased bare ground. What downstream environmental impact should the agency plan for based on this data?

Loss of forest canopy and ground vegetation after wildfire, as revealed by LIDAR, dramatically increases surface runoff and erosion. Without root systems and leaf litter to hold soil in place, rainfall washes sediment into streams, degrading water quality and increasing flood risk.
