Lesson 2.8: Weather Prediction Models - Summary

Key Concepts: Weather Prediction Models

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the 'butterfly effect'?

The butterfly effect, coined by meteorologist Edward Lorenz, illustrates that in chaotic systems like the atmosphere, even tiny differences in starting conditions (metaphorically, a butterfly flapping its wings) can lead to dramatically different outcomes over time, fundamentally limiting long-range weather prediction.

What is data assimilation in weather forecasting?

Data assimilation is a mathematical technique that combines millions of observations (from radiosondes, satellites, radar, aircraft, and surface stations) with a short-range model forecast to produce the best possible estimate of current atmospheric conditions, which then serves as the starting point for new forecasts.

What is nowcasting?

Nowcasting focuses on very short-range forecasts from 0 to 6 hours ahead, relying heavily on extrapolating current radar and satellite observations forward in time rather than relying on NWP models, and is especially useful for predicting thunderstorm development and movement.

What is a radiosonde?

A radiosonde is a small, expendable instrument package attached to a weather balloon that ascends through the atmosphere, measuring temperature, humidity, and pressure at various altitudes while transmitting data via radio to a ground receiver.

What is the difference between weather models and climate models?

Weather models focus on predicting specific day-to-day conditions over periods of hours to about two weeks, while climate models simulate the long-term behavior of the climate system over decades to centuries, including responses to changes in greenhouse gas concentrations.

What is AMDAR (Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay)?

AMDAR uses sensors on commercial aircraft to automatically measure and transmit temperature, wind, and sometimes humidity data during flights, providing hundreds of thousands of observations daily that are particularly valuable for upper-atmosphere data where radiosondes are sparse.

What is model parameterization?

Parameterization involves representing physical processes that occur at scales smaller than the model's grid cells (such as individual cumulus clouds, turbulence, or radiation transfer) using simplified mathematical approximations, since directly simulating these processes would require impossibly fine resolution.

What is the role of artificial intelligence in modern weather forecasting?

AI and machine learning are increasingly used to improve weather forecasting by identifying patterns in massive datasets, correcting systematic model errors, and even producing full forecasts that in some cases rival traditional physics-based models for certain prediction tasks.

What is forecast verification?

Forecast verification is the process of systematically comparing forecasts against what actually occurred, using statistical measures to quantify accuracy, identify biases, and track improvements, which is essential for developing better models and improving forecast skill.

What is a sounding in meteorology?

An atmospheric sounding is a vertical profile showing how temperature, humidity, wind speed, and wind direction change with altitude at a specific location, typically plotted on a special diagram (Skew-T) that meteorologists use to assess atmospheric stability and severe weather potential.

Real-World Applications

Example: Ensemble forecasting runs multiple simulations to:

Ensemble forecasting runs the same model multiple times with slightly different initial conditions to assess forecast confidence: when most runs produce similar results, confidence is high, but when runs diverge widely, the forecast is more uncertain.

Example: Polar-orbiting satellites provide global coverage by:

Polar-orbiting satellites travel north to south and south to north over the poles at about 800 km altitude, and because Earth rotates beneath them, they observe a different strip of the surface on each pass, achieving complete global coverage twice daily.
