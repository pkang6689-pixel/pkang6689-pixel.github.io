Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a primary pollutant?

A primary pollutant is one that is emitted directly into the atmosphere from an identifiable source, such as carbon monoxide from vehicle exhaust, sulfur dioxide from coal-burning power plants, or particulate matter from industrial smokestacks.

What is a secondary pollutant?

Secondary pollutants are not emitted directly but form through chemical reactions in the atmosphere; for example, ground-level ozone forms when nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds react in sunlight, and photochemical smog is composed of secondary pollutants.

What is the carbon footprint?

A carbon footprint measures the total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by a person, organization, or product, expressed as carbon dioxide equivalents, helping quantify the climate impact of activities like driving, flying, or manufacturing.

What is carbon capture and storage (CCS)?

Carbon capture and storage is a technology that captures CO2 emissions from large point sources like power plants and industrial facilities, then transports and stores the CO2 in deep underground geological formations to prevent it from entering the atmosphere.

What is the enhanced greenhouse effect?

The enhanced greenhouse effect refers to the additional warming of Earth's surface caused by human activities that have increased atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases (especially CO2, methane, and nitrous oxide) beyond their natural levels, intensifying the natural greenhouse effect.

What is thermal expansion in the context of sea level rise?

Thermal expansion is the process by which ocean water increases in volume as it warms, and it has been responsible for roughly one-third to one-half of observed sea level rise, with the remainder caused by melting land ice.

What is a carbon sink?

Carbon sinks are natural or artificial reservoirs that absorb more carbon than they release, including forests (which absorb CO2 through photosynthesis), oceans (which dissolve CO2), and soil, and they play a crucial role in regulating atmospheric CO2 levels.

What is photochemical smog?

Photochemical smog is a type of air pollution that forms when sunlight triggers chemical reactions between nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds (primarily from vehicle exhaust), producing a mixture of ground-level ozone and other harmful secondary pollutants.

What is ocean acidification?

Ocean acidification occurs as the ocean absorbs about 25-30% of human-emitted CO2, which reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid, lowering the pH and making it harder for marine organisms like corals, shellfish, and plankton to build their calcium carbonate shells and skeletons.

Real-World Applications

Example: The global average temperature has risen approximately _____ since pre-industrial times.

Global average surface temperature has increased by approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius since the pre-industrial period (1850-1900), with the rate of warming accelerating in recent decades.

Example: A farmer in the American Midwest switches to reduced-tillage farming practices. In addition to improving soil health, this change benefits the atmosphere because:

Reduced-tillage farming benefits the atmosphere by both reducing fossil fuel use from less machinery operation and preserving soil carbon that would otherwise be released as CO2 when soil is disturbed, making soil a more effective carbon sink.
