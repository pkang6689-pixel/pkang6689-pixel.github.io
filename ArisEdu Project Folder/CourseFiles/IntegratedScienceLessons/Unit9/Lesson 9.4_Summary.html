Lesson 9.4: Modeling Geological Processes - Summary

Key Concepts: Modeling Geological Processes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is plate tectonics?

Plate tectonics is the unifying theory of geology stating that Earth's lithosphere is divided into rigid plates that float on the semi-fluid asthenosphere and move relative to each other, causing earthquakes, volcanism, and mountain building.

What is the rock cycle?

The rock cycle describes the continuous transformation of rocks among three types: igneous (from cooling magma), sedimentary (from compacted sediments), and metamorphic (from heat/pressure changes). This cycle is driven by plate tectonics, weathering, and erosion.

What is erosion?

Erosion is the transport of weathered rock fragments, soil, and dissolved materials from one location to another by agents such as water, wind, glaciers, or gravity. It is a key process shaping Earth's surface and redistributing materials.

What is a subduction zone?

A subduction zone is a convergent plate boundary where one plate (usually oceanic) dives beneath another into the mantle. These zones produce the deepest ocean trenches, the strongest earthquakes, and explosive volcanic arcs.

What is the lithosphere?

The lithosphere is Earth's rigid outermost layer, about 100 km thick, consisting of the crust and the upper portion of the mantle. It is broken into tectonic plates that float and move on the softer asthenosphere below.

What is the asthenosphere?

The asthenosphere is the weak, semi-plastic layer of the upper mantle beneath the lithosphere. Its partial melting and ductile behavior allow tectonic plates to float and move across it.

What is weathering?

Weathering is the in-place breakdown of rocks and minerals at Earth's surface through physical processes (frost wedging, root growth) and chemical processes (dissolution, oxidation). It produces sediment and dissolved minerals that enter the rock cycle.

What is seismology?

Seismology is the branch of geoscience that studies earthquakes and seismic waves. Seismologists use seismographs to detect and measure ground vibrations, and analyze wave propagation to understand Earth's internal structure and fault behavior.

What is the Richter scale?

The Richter scale (now largely replaced by the moment magnitude scale) is a logarithmic scale where each whole number increase represents approximately 32 times more energy released. A magnitude 7 earthquake releases about 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 5.

What is a geological model?

A geological model is a scientific representation (mathematical, computer-based, or physical) of geological processes such as plate tectonics, erosion, volcanic activity, or sedimentation, used to understand past events and predict future behavior.

What is a mid-ocean ridge?

A mid-ocean ridge is a continuous underwater mountain system formed at divergent plate boundaries where magma rises to create new oceanic crust. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge, stretching over 16,000 km, is the longest mountain range on Earth.

What is sedimentation?

Sedimentation is the process of settling and accumulation of particles (sediment) that have been transported by water, wind, or ice. Over time, accumulated sediments compact and cement together to form sedimentary rocks.

What is the Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI)?

The Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI) is a logarithmic scale from 0 to 8 that measures the explosiveness of volcanic eruptions based on ejected volume, eruption column height, and duration. Each step represents a tenfold increase in ejected material.

Real-World Applications

Example: A city near a tectonic plate boundary experiences a magnitude 6.0 earthquake. Engineers want to model the potential damage from a magnitude 7.0 earthquake in the same location. How much more energy would a 7.0 release compared to a 6.0?

The earthquake magnitude scale is logarithmic -- each whole number increase represents approximately 32 times more energy released. A magnitude 7.0 earthquake releases about 32 times the energy of a 6.0, making proper engineering preparation critical.
