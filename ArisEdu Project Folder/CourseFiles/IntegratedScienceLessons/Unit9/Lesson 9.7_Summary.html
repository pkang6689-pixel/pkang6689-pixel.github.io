Lesson 9.7: Applications in Environmental Engineering - Summary

Key Concepts: Applications in Environmental Engineering

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary goal of environmental engineering?

Environmental engineering applies scientific and engineering principles to protect and improve environmental quality, including the design of systems for water treatment, air pollution control, waste management, and remediation of contaminated sites.

What is the purpose of an activated sludge process in wastewater treatment?

The activated sludge process uses aerated tanks where communities of microorganisms consume and break down dissolved organic pollutants in wastewater. The microbial biomass (sludge) is then settled out, with some recycled to maintain the active microbial population.

What is bioremediation?

Bioremediation harnesses the metabolic capabilities of microorganisms, fungi, or plants to degrade, transform, or sequester environmental contaminants such as petroleum hydrocarbons, heavy metals, and pesticides in soil and groundwater.

What is the primary advantage of membrane bioreactor (MBR) technology over conventional wastewater treatment?

Membrane bioreactors combine the activated sludge biological process with membrane filtration, eliminating the need for secondary clarifiers. This produces higher quality effluent suitable for reuse, in a more compact system than conventional treatment plants.

What is the purpose of an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)?

An Environmental Impact Assessment is a systematic process for identifying and evaluating the potential environmental effects of a proposed project or development, allowing decision-makers and the public to understand the consequences before the project proceeds.

What is 'phytoremediation'?

Phytoremediation uses living plants to remove, degrade, or stabilize contaminants in soil and water. Different plant species can accumulate heavy metals, break down organic pollutants, or stabilize contaminated sediments through their root systems.

What is a 'constructed wetland' in environmental engineering?

Constructed wetlands are engineered systems designed to replicate the water-purifying functions of natural wetlands. They use wetland vegetation, soils, and associated microbial communities to remove pollutants from wastewater through biological, physical, and chemical processes.

What is 'eutrophication' in the context of water quality?

Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients, primarily nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff and wastewater, stimulate excessive algal growth. When the algae die, their decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic conditions that can kill aquatic life.

What is 'sustainable development' in the context of environmental engineering?

Sustainable development, as defined by the Brundtland Commission, balances economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection to ensure that current resource use does not deplete or degrade conditions for future generations.

What is meant by 'point source pollution'?

Point source pollution originates from a specific, identifiable location such as a factory discharge pipe, sewage treatment plant outfall, or smokestack. It is generally easier to regulate and control than non-point source pollution, which comes from diffuse sources.

What is a 'Superfund site'?

Superfund sites are locations contaminated with hazardous substances that the EPA has identified as priorities for long-term cleanup under CERCLA, commonly known as the Superfund law. There are over 1,300 sites on the National Priorities List.

A city's water treatment plant detects elevated levels of nitrate in the raw water supply drawn from a river. Testing reveals that the contamination increases significantly after rainfall events and is highest during the spring planting season. What is the most likely source of the nitrate contamination, and what engineering solution could help?

The seasonal pattern coinciding with spring fertilizer application and rainfall-driven increases strongly indicate agricultural non-point source pollution. Riparian buffers and constructed wetlands can intercept nutrient-laden runoff and remove nitrogen through plant uptake and denitrification before it reaches the river.

An environmental engineer is assessing indoor air quality in a newly constructed school building. Teachers report headaches and irritation. Monitoring reveals elevated levels of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from new building materials, furniture, and paint. What is the most effective engineering solution?

Addressing indoor air quality involves both immediate and long-term strategies. Increasing ventilation dilutes indoor pollutants, activated carbon filtration adsorbs VOCs, and specifying low-VOC materials for future use prevents the problem at its source, following the engineering hierarchy of controls.

Real-World Applications

Example: A coastal community experiences frequent flooding from combined sewer overflows (CSOs) during heavy rainstorms. Their aging sewer system carries both stormwater and sewage in the same pipes. During major storms, the system capacity is exceeded and untreated sewage-stormwater mixture is discharged directly into the harbor. What comprehensive engineering approach would best address this problem?

Addressing CSOs requires a multi-pronged approach: green infrastructure reduces stormwater volumes entering the system, deep storage tunnels hold excess combined flow for later treatment, and sewer separation in key areas prevents stormwater from overwhelming the sanitary sewer capacity.

Example: A developing country is planning a water treatment system for a rural village of 500 people. The village has no electricity grid, limited technical expertise, and the water source is a turbid river. Which treatment approach is most appropriate given these constraints?

For rural communities without electricity or technical expertise, appropriate technology is essential. Slow sand filtration effectively removes turbidity and pathogens without requiring electricity or complex operation, while solar disinfection or chlorine tablets provide additional pathogen inactivation as a second barrier.

Example: An environmental engineering firm is evaluating two landfill designs for a new municipal solid waste facility. Design A is a traditional landfill with a clay liner and leachate collection system. Design B adds a geomembrane liner below the clay, a stormwater management system, and a methane gas collection system for energy recovery. Which design better represents modern environmental engineering principles and why?

Modern sanitary landfill design uses multiple engineered barriers. The composite liner (geomembrane plus clay) provides redundant groundwater protection. Methane capture prevents a potent greenhouse gas from reaching the atmosphere while generating renewable energy. Stormwater diversion reduces leachate volume requiring treatment.

Example: A community near a former mining site discovers that acid mine drainage (AMD) is contaminating a local stream with heavy metals and low pH water. The orange-colored water kills aquatic organisms for several miles downstream. An environmental engineer proposes a passive treatment system. Which of the following best describes this approach?

Passive AMD treatment uses natural processes to remediate contaminated water. Anoxic limestone drains add alkalinity to neutralize acidity, and constructed wetlands provide conditions for metal precipitation and biological treatment, all without the ongoing operational costs of active chemical treatment.
