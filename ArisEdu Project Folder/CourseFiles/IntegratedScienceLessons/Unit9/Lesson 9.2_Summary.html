Lesson 9.2: Feedback Loops (Positive & Negative) - Summary

Key Concepts: Feedback Loops (Positive & Negative)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a feedback loop in Earth science?

A feedback loop occurs when the output of a process cycles back to influence the input, creating a circular chain of cause and effect that is fundamental to understanding how Earth systems maintain stability or undergo rapid change.

What is a positive feedback loop?

A positive feedback loop is a self-reinforcing cycle in which an initial perturbation triggers a response that further amplifies it. 'Positive' refers to the direction of reinforcement, not whether the outcome is beneficial.

What is a negative feedback loop?

A negative feedback loop is a self-correcting mechanism where a perturbation triggers responses that oppose and diminish the initial change, promoting stability. 'Negative' refers to the opposing direction, not harmful consequences.

What is homeostasis?

Homeostasis is the tendency of a system to maintain stable, relatively constant conditions through self-regulating processes, primarily negative feedback loops, whether in biology (body temperature) or Earth science (climate stability).

What is the ice-albedo feedback?

The ice-albedo feedback is a positive feedback: as ice and snow melt, they expose darker surfaces that absorb more solar energy, raising temperatures further and accelerating melting.

What is a tipping point?

A tipping point is a critical threshold beyond which positive feedbacks take over and the system moves rapidly to a new state. Once crossed, these transitions may be difficult or impossible to reverse.

What is the weathering-CO2 feedback (silicate weathering thermostat)?

The silicate weathering thermostat is a crucial negative feedback: higher temperatures accelerate chemical weathering of rocks, consuming atmospheric CO2 and reducing the greenhouse effect over geological timescales.

What is the cloud feedback in climate science?

Cloud feedback is complex: low thick clouds cool Earth by reflecting sunlight (negative feedback), while high thin clouds warm it by trapping infrared radiation (positive feedback). The net effect remains an active research area.

A student claims natural negative feedbacks will prevent human-caused climate change. What is the flaw in this reasoning?

Natural negative feedbacks like the silicate weathering thermostat operate over hundreds of thousands to millions of years. Human emissions are changing the atmosphere far faster than these geological processes can compensate.

Real-World Applications

Example: The ice-albedo feedback is an example of which type?

The ice-albedo feedback is a positive feedback loop: warming melts ice, exposing dark water that absorbs more solar energy, causing further warming and more ice melt in a self-reinforcing cycle.

A tipping point is a critical threshold beyond which positive feedbacks take over and the system moves rapidly to a new state. Once crossed, these transitions may be difficult or impossible to reverse.
