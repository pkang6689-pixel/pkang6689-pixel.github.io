Lesson 1.5: Earthquakes (Seismic Waves, Measurement, Prediction) - Summary

Key Concepts: Earthquakes (Seismic Waves, Measurement, Prediction)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the epicenter of an earthquake?

The epicenter is the point on Earth's surface located directly above the focus (hypocenter) where the earthquake originated underground. Damage and shaking intensity are typically greatest near the epicenter.

What is liquefaction?

Liquefaction occurs when water-saturated, loosely packed sediment loses its strength during seismic shaking and temporarily behaves like a liquid. This can cause buildings to sink, tilt, or collapse, and is a major hazard in areas built on unconsolidated fill or alluvial deposits.

What is a seismograph?

A seismograph is an instrument that records ground vibrations produced by seismic waves. It works on the principle of inertia, with a suspended mass remaining relatively stationary while the ground and frame move beneath it, producing a seismogram.

What is a tsunami?

A tsunami is a series of powerful ocean waves caused by a sudden displacement of a large volume of water, most commonly triggered by an underwater earthquake. Tsunamis can cross entire ocean basins and reach heights of over 30 meters when they strike shore.

What is a fault in geology?

A fault is a fracture or zone of fractures in Earth's crust along which there has been displacement of the sides relative to each other. Faults are classified by their motion: normal (tension), reverse (compression), and strike-slip (shear).

What is an aftershock?

Aftershocks are smaller earthquakes that follow the main shock as the crust adjusts to the stress changes caused by the main rupture. They can continue for weeks, months, or even years and can cause additional damage to already-weakened structures.

What is a P-wave?

P-waves (primary waves) are compressional waves where particles move back and forth in the direction of wave travel, like a Slinky being pushed and pulled. They are the fastest seismic waves and can propagate through all states of matter.

What is an S-wave?

S-waves (secondary or shear waves) cause the ground to move side-to-side, perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. They are slower than P-waves and critically cannot travel through liquids, which revealed that Earth's outer core is liquid.

What is a seismic hazard map?

Seismic hazard maps show the probability and expected intensity of ground shaking for different areas, based on historical seismicity, fault locations, and soil conditions. They are essential tools for building codes, land-use planning, and emergency preparedness.

A homeowner in a seismically active region is deciding between building on a lot with solid granite bedrock and a lot with deep, sandy soil near a river. A geologist advises choosing the bedrock site. What is the scientific basis for this recommendation?

Sandy, water-saturated soil near rivers is highly susceptible to liquefaction and amplifies seismic waves, increasing both shaking intensity and ground failure risk. Solid bedrock is far more stable, transmitting seismic waves without amplifying them and providing firm support for foundations.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

The Tohoku disaster shows that earthquake preparedness must be comprehensive. While earthquake-resistant buildings saved many lives during the shaking, the tsunami was a secondary hazard that proved far more deadly. Effective preparedness requires addressing multiple hazard types simultaneously.

Real-World Applications

Example: After a major earthquake, a city built on soft, water-saturated sediment experiences far more damage than a nearby city built on solid bedrock, even though both are the same distance from the epicenter. What phenomenon explains this difference?

Soft, unconsolidated sediment amplifies seismic waves, causing stronger and longer shaking compared to solid bedrock. Additionally, saturated sediments may liquefy during shaking, causing the ground to behave like a fluid and buildings to sink or tilt.

Example: A news report states that a magnitude 5.0 earthquake was felt over a wide area but caused minimal damage, while a magnitude 4.5 earthquake in a different region collapsed several buildings and caused deaths. How is this possible if the first earthquake had a higher magnitude?

Magnitude measures energy released, but damage depends on many factors: depth (shallow earthquakes cause more damage), soil conditions (soft sediment amplifies shaking), building construction quality, and population density. A smaller earthquake in a vulnerable area can be far more destructive.
