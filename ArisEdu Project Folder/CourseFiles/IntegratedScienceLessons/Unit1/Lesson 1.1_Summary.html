Lesson 1.1: Layers of the Earth (Crust, Mantle, Core) - Summary

Key Concepts: Layers of the Earth (Crust, Mantle, Core)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the asthenosphere?

The asthenosphere is a partially molten, ductile region of the upper mantle located beneath the lithosphere. It behaves plastically over long time scales, allowing tectonic plates to glide over it.

What is the Mohorovičić discontinuity (Moho)?

The Moho is the boundary between Earth's crust and mantle, identified by a sharp change in seismic wave velocity. It was discovered by Croatian seismologist Andrija Mohorovičić in 1909.

What is the geodynamo?

The geodynamo is the mechanism by which Earth's magnetic field is generated through the motion of electrically conductive liquid iron in the outer core. This convective motion converts kinetic energy into magnetic energy.

What is isostasy?

Isostasy is the principle of gravitational balance where less dense continental crust floats higher on the denser mantle material, similar to how an iceberg floats in water. This explains why continents sit higher than ocean floors.

What is a seismic shadow zone?

Seismic shadow zones are regions on Earth's surface where seismographs detect no direct seismic waves from an earthquake. The S-wave shadow zone (beyond 104°) revealed the liquid outer core, while P-wave shadow zones helped map the core's boundaries.

What is the mesosphere in Earth science?

In Earth science, the mesosphere refers to the lower mantle extending from about 660 km to 2,900 km depth. It is stronger and more rigid than the asthenosphere above it but can still flow very slowly over geological time.

What is the D'' (D double-prime) layer?

The D'' layer is a zone roughly 200-300 km thick at the very base of the mantle. It is significant because it is where the rocky mantle directly contacts the metallic core, creating complex interactions that influence mantle plumes and hotspot volcanism.

What is the inner core?

The inner core is a solid sphere with a radius of about 1,220 km at Earth's center. Although temperatures exceed 5,000°C, the immense pressure (over 3 million atmospheres) raises the melting point of iron-nickel above the actual temperature, keeping it solid.

A seismologist at a station 150 degrees from an earthquake's epicenter records P-waves arriving but notices they are delayed and refracted compared to the expected arrival time. What is the most likely explanation?

At distances beyond about 104 degrees, P-waves that travel through the liquid outer core are refracted (bent) due to the change in density and material state. This refraction changes their path and delays their arrival, which helped scientists map the core's boundaries.

A city is built on thick continental crust in a region far from any plate boundaries. A geologist assures residents that the area is relatively safe from major earthquakes and volcanic eruptions. What is the scientific basis for this assessment?

About 90% of earthquakes and most volcanic eruptions occur at or near tectonic plate boundaries. Locations in the middle of a large continental plate, far from any plate boundary, are generally much less prone to these hazards, though intraplate earthquakes can still occur rarely.

Real-World Applications

Example: The mantle makes up approximately what percentage of Earth's volume?

The mantle is by far the largest layer by volume, making up about 84% of Earth's total volume. It extends from the base of the crust to a depth of about 2,900 km.
