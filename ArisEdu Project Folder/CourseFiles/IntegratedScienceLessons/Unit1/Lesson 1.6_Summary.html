Key Definitions

What is a composite volcano (stratovolcano)?

Composite volcanoes (stratovolcanoes) are large, steep-sided volcanoes built from alternating layers of lava flows, volcanic ash, and pyroclastic material. They are among the most dangerous volcanoes and include Mt. Fuji, Mt. St. Helens, and Mt. Vesuvius.

What is viscosity in the context of volcanic eruptions?

Viscosity is a measure of how much a fluid resists flowing. In volcanology, magma viscosity is controlled primarily by silica content and temperature. High-silica magma is very viscous (thick and sticky), while low-silica basaltic magma flows easily.

What is a caldera?

A caldera is a large volcanic depression, typically circular, that forms when the roof of a magma chamber collapses after a massive eruption empties the chamber. Crater Lake in Oregon fills a caldera, and Yellowstone sits atop one of the world's largest calderas.

What is tephra?

Tephra is a general term for all fragments of rock ejected into the air during a volcanic eruption. It is classified by size: ash (less than 2 mm), lapilli (2-64 mm), and volcanic bombs or blocks (greater than 64 mm).

What is the Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI)?

The VEI is a relative scale that ranges from 0 (non-explosive) to 8 (mega-colossal). Each step represents roughly a tenfold increase in ejected material. VEI-8 eruptions, like Yellowstone's ancient super-eruptions, eject over 1,000 cubic kilometers of material.

What is a cinder cone volcano?

Cinder cone volcanoes are the smallest and simplest type, built from piles of volcanic cinders and bombs ejected from a single vent. They have steep sides and are typically short-lived. Paricutin in Mexico, which grew in a farmer's field in 1943, is a famous example.

What is a shield volcano?

Shield volcanoes are the largest volcanoes by volume, with broad, gently sloping profiles that resemble a warrior's shield. They are built almost entirely from numerous thin flows of fluid basaltic lava. Mauna Loa in Hawaii is the world's largest active shield volcano.

What is a pyroclastic flow?

A pyroclastic flow is a ground-hugging avalanche of hot gas, ash, pumice, and rock fragments that can travel at speeds exceeding 700 km/h with temperatures above 700 degrees C. They are the most lethal volcanic hazard and the flow that destroyed Pompeii in 79 AD.

What is volcanic monitoring?

Volcanic monitoring uses multiple tools to track changes that precede eruptions: increased seismicity, ground deformation (swelling), changes in gas emissions, and thermal anomalies. While exact eruption timing cannot be predicted, monitoring provides crucial early warnings.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Soufriere Hills is a stratovolcano that erupts viscous, gas-rich andesitic to dacitic magma. This produces explosive eruptions, dome collapses that generate pyroclastic flows, and lahars. Its location on a small, densely populated island amplifies the impact.

Ground uplift, increased seismicity, and elevated gas emissions are classic precursory signals of magma movement. While these signs do not necessarily mean an eruption is imminent, they indicate that magma is actively moving, requiring heightened monitoring and public communication.
