Key Definitions

What is a natural hazard?

A natural hazard is a naturally occurring physical event that can cause harm to humans, infrastructure, or the environment. Hazards become disasters when they significantly impact human communities. The distinction between hazard and disaster depends on human vulnerability and exposure.

What is disaster preparedness?

Disaster preparedness encompasses all measures taken before a hazard event to reduce its impact, including building codes, early warning systems, emergency plans, public education, evacuation routes, and resource stockpiling. It focuses on reducing vulnerability since hazards themselves cannot be prevented.

What is vulnerability in the context of natural hazards?

Vulnerability refers to how susceptible a community is to harm from a natural hazard. It depends on social, economic, and infrastructure factors: building construction quality, poverty levels, population density, government preparedness, and community awareness. High vulnerability can make a moderate hazard into a major disaster.

What is a pyroclastic surge?

A pyroclastic surge is a dilute, turbulent cloud of hot gas and fine volcanic particles that moves at extremely high speeds. Unlike denser pyroclastic flows that follow valleys, surges can overtop ridges and barriers, making them particularly dangerous.

What is ecological succession after a volcanic eruption?

Ecological succession is the natural process of ecosystem recovery after disturbance. After a volcanic eruption, pioneer species (mosses, lichens, and hardy plants) colonize the barren landscape first, gradually building soil that supports progressively more complex plant and animal communities over decades to centuries.

What is a seismic gap?

A seismic gap is a segment of an active fault that has not produced a major earthquake in a period longer than the average recurrence interval. Such gaps may represent areas of accumulated strain that could release in a future earthquake, making them targets for heightened monitoring.

What is base isolation in earthquake engineering?

Base isolation involves placing a building on flexible pads or bearings that allow the ground to move beneath it during an earthquake while the building remains relatively stationary. This dramatically reduces the seismic forces transmitted to the structure and its occupants.

What is a DART buoy?

DART (Deep-ocean Assessment and Reporting of Tsunamis) buoys are seafloor pressure sensors connected to surface buoys that detect the subtle pressure changes caused by tsunami waves in deep water. They are critical components of tsunami warning systems worldwide.

What is resilience in the context of natural disaster management?

Resilience encompasses a community's full capacity to deal with natural hazards: preparation before events, survival during events, rapid recovery afterward, and adaptation to reduce future impacts. Building resilience involves infrastructure, governance, social networks, and economic resources.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is resilience in the context of natural disaster management?

Resilience encompasses a community's full capacity to deal with natural hazards: preparation before events, survival during events, rapid recovery afterward, and adaptation to reduce future impacts. Building resilience involves infrastructure, governance, social networks, and economic resources.

Example: After the devastating 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, the Indonesian village of Simeulue, located very close to the epicenter, had remarkably few casualties compared to more distant communities. Investigation revealed that village elders had taught younger generations to flee to high ground after feeling strong earthquake shaking. What does this demonstrate about disaster preparedness?

Simeulue's survival demonstrates that cultural memory and education are powerful disaster mitigation tools. Elders who remembered stories of a 1907 tsunami taught the community to recognize natural warning signs and respond immediately, saving nearly all residents without any modern technology.

Example: After Hurricane Katrina devastated New Orleans in 2005, investigations revealed that the failure of levees (flood walls) was a major factor in the flooding. How does this case study apply to natural hazard management beyond hurricanes?

Katrina demonstrated that engineered defenses can create a false sense of security. When levees fail, the consequences are catastrophic because communities behind them are unprepared. This lesson applies to all hazard engineering: seawalls, earthquake-resistant buildings, and volcanic barriers all require proper design, maintenance, and backup plans.

Example: The Great East Japan Earthquake of 2011 caused an estimated 235 billion dollars in damage, making it the costliest natural disaster in history. Yet the death toll (approximately 20,000) was far lower than the 2010 Haiti earthquake (over 200,000 deaths, 8 billion dollars in damage). What does this comparison reveal about the economics of natural disasters?

This comparison reveals the divergence between economic and human costs. Wealthy nations have more valuable infrastructure (higher economic loss) but better building codes and preparedness (lower death toll). Poor nations have less to lose economically but far more vulnerable populations. Investment in preparedness is ultimately the most cost-effective strategy.

Important Concepts

Climate change is predicted to increase the frequency and intensity of certain natural hazards. A coastal city is planning for the next 50 years. Which combination of climate-related hazard changes should their plan address

Climate change amplifies multiple natural hazards for coastal cities: rising sea levels increase the baseline for storm surge and tsunami inundation, warmer atmosphere holds more moisture leading to more intense rainfall and flooding, and melting glaciers can trigger outburst floods and landslides.
