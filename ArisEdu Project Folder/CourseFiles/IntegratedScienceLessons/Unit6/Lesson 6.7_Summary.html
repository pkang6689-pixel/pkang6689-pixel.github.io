Key Definitions

What is a cap-and-trade system for pollution control?

Cap-and-trade sets an overall limit (cap) on emissions and distributes permits to polluters. Companies that reduce emissions below their allowance can sell excess permits to those who exceed theirs, creating financial incentives for pollution reduction.

What is an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)?

An EIA evaluates the likely environmental consequences of a proposed development, including effects on air, water, soil, ecosystems, and communities. It is required by law in many countries before major projects can be approved.

What is environmental policy?

Environmental policy encompasses the laws, regulations, and guidelines that governments and organizations implement to manage human interactions with the natural environment. These policies aim to protect ecosystems, public health, and natural resources.

What is a carbon tax?

A carbon tax sets a direct price on carbon emissions by charging a fee per ton of CO2 emitted. It creates a financial incentive for businesses and individuals to reduce fossil fuel consumption and invest in cleaner alternatives.

What is the 'polluter pays' principle?

The polluter pays principle holds that those responsible for producing pollution should bear the costs of cleaning it up and preventing future harm. It shifts the financial burden from the public to the parties responsible for contamination.

What is CITES?

CITES is an international agreement among governments that ensures international trade in wild animal and plant specimens does not threaten the survival of species. It regulates trade in over 38,000 species through a permit system.

What is an emissions trading scheme (ETS)?

An ETS creates a market for carbon emissions by issuing a limited number of emission permits. Companies that emit less than their allocation can sell surplus permits, while those exceeding their limit must purchase additional ones, creating economic pressure to reduce emissions.

The Flint crisis is a textbook case of environmental injustice. Cost-cutting decisions made without adequate community input or safety testing exposed a vulnerable, predominantly minority population to serious health risks. It highlighted systemic failures in protecting marginalized communities.

Real-World Applications

Example: What was the primary environmental issue that led to the creation of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in 1970?

The EPA was established in response to growing public concern over severe pollution events, including the Cuyahoga River catching fire and deadly smog incidents. It consolidated federal environmental regulations under one agency.

Example: The Cuyahoga River in Ohio caught fire multiple times due to industrial pollution, most famously in 1969. This event helped catalyze the creation of the Clean Water Act and the EPA. What does this case study illustrate about the relationship between environmental disasters and policy change?

The Cuyahoga River fire became a powerful symbol of environmental neglect that outraged the public. It demonstrated how visible environmental disasters can shift public opinion and create the political will necessary to enact transformative environmental legislation.

Example: The Great Barrier Reef Marine Park in Australia was established in 1975 to protect the world's largest coral reef system. Despite protections, the reef has suffered multiple mass bleaching events linked to rising ocean temperatures. What does this case study reveal about the limitations of local conservation policy?

Despite being one of the best-managed marine protected areas in the world, the Great Barrier Reef cannot be protected from warming oceans caused by global emissions. This demonstrates that local conservation policy must be complemented by global climate action.
