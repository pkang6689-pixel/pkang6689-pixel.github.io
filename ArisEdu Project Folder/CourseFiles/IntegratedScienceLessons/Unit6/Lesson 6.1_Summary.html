Lesson 6.1: Deforestation & Land Use Changes - Summary

Key Concepts: Deforestation & Land Use Changes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary cause of tropical deforestation worldwide?

Agricultural expansion, including cattle ranching and soy or palm oil cultivation, is the leading driver of tropical deforestation, accounting for roughly 80% of forest loss worldwide.

What is slash-and-burn agriculture?

Slash-and-burn agriculture involves cutting down trees and burning the debris to create nutrient-rich ash for short-term farming. Once the soil is depleted, farmers move on and clear new forest areas.

What is deforestation?

Deforestation is the large-scale, permanent clearing of forest land, typically for agriculture, logging, or development. It differs from natural disturbances because the forest is not allowed to regenerate.

What is a carbon sink?

A carbon sink is any reservoir that absorbs more carbon from the atmosphere than it releases. Forests are major carbon sinks because trees sequester CO2 through photosynthesis and store it in their wood, leaves, and roots.

What is reforestation?

Reforestation involves planting trees on land where forests have been removed or degraded. It is an important strategy for restoring ecosystems, rebuilding carbon sinks, and preventing further soil erosion.

What is biodiversity?

Biodiversity encompasses the variety of all living organisms at the genetic, species, and ecosystem levels. High biodiversity indicates a healthy, resilient ecosystem with many different forms of life interacting together.

What is afforestation?

Afforestation is the establishment of a forest on land that was not previously forested, such as former grasslands or degraded agricultural areas. It differs from reforestation, which replants trees where forests once existed.

What is habitat fragmentation?

Habitat fragmentation occurs when large areas of habitat are divided into smaller, disconnected patches by roads, farms, or development. This isolates wildlife populations and reduces genetic diversity.

What is desertification?

Desertification is the degradation of dryland ecosystems caused by overgrazing, deforestation, poor irrigation, and climate change. It transforms productive land into barren, desert-like conditions.

A satellite image shows that a large area of the Amazon has been converted to cattle pasture. Scientists observe that nearby rivers now carry much more sediment than before. What is the most likely explanation?

Without the forest canopy to intercept rain and tree roots to stabilize soil, rainwater runs directly over exposed ground and carries loose soil particles into streams and rivers, dramatically increasing sediment loads.

An island nation depends on its mangrove forests to protect coastal villages from storm surges. A developer proposes clearing mangroves for a shrimp farming operation. What is the most significant environmental risk of this land use change?

Mangrove forests serve as critical natural barriers against storm surges, tsunamis, and coastal erosion. Removing them for aquaculture exposes shorelines and settlements to severe storm damage and accelerated land loss.

Real-World Applications

Example: A government proposes building a new highway through a national forest to improve transportation access. An environmental scientist warns this could cause more damage than just the area paved. What additional impact is the scientist most concerned about?

Roads through forests do far more damage than the land they cover. They fragment habitat, block animal migration routes, and serve as pathways for loggers, settlers, and hunters to access previously remote forest areas.

Example: After a massive wildfire, a forestry agency debates whether to replant the area immediately or allow natural regeneration. A scientist argues for assisted natural regeneration. In this scenario, what would be the strongest argument for allowing some natural regrowth rather than exclusively replanting?

Natural regeneration allows a diverse mix of native species adapted to local conditions to reestablish, creating a more complex and resilient ecosystem than the monoculture tree plantations that are typically used in replanting efforts.
