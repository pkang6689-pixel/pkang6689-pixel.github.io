Lesson 8.2: Seasons & Earth's Tilt - Summary

Key Concepts: Seasons & Earth's Tilt

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the approximate tilt of Earth's axis?

Earth's rotational axis is tilted at approximately 23.5 degrees relative to the plane of its orbit around the Sun, and this tilt is the primary cause of the seasons.

What is an equinox?

An equinox occurs when the Sun crosses Earth's equatorial plane, meaning neither hemisphere is tilted toward or away from the Sun. This results in approximately 12 hours of daylight and 12 hours of darkness worldwide.

What is a solstice?

A solstice is an astronomical event that occurs when Earth's axial tilt is most inclined toward or away from the Sun, resulting in the longest or shortest day of the year for each hemisphere.

What is the Tropic of Cancer?

The Tropic of Cancer is the circle of latitude at approximately 23.5 degrees north of the equator, marking the northernmost latitude where the Sun can appear directly overhead, which occurs on the June solstice.

What is the angle of incidence in relation to sunlight?

The angle of incidence refers to the angle at which sunlight strikes Earth's surface. A higher angle of incidence (more direct sunlight) delivers more concentrated energy per unit area, while a lower angle spreads the energy over a larger area.

What is the Tropic of Capricorn?

The Tropic of Capricorn is the circle of latitude at approximately 23.5 degrees south of the equator, marking the southernmost latitude where the Sun appears directly overhead, which occurs during the December solstice.

What is the Arctic Circle?

The Arctic Circle is the circle of latitude at approximately 66.5 degrees north, above which the Sun remains above the horizon for 24 continuous hours at least once a year (June solstice) and below the horizon for 24 hours at least once (December solstice).

A student argues that summers are warmer because Earth is closer to the Sun during summer months. What is the best scientific response to this claim?

This is a common misconception. Earth reaches perihelion (closest to the Sun) around January 3, during Northern Hemisphere winter. Seasons are caused by axial tilt, which determines the directness and duration of sunlight each hemisphere receives.
