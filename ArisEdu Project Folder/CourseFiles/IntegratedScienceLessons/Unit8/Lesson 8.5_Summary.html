Lesson 8.5: Gravity & Orbital Motion - Summary

Key Concepts: Gravity & Orbital Motion

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the relationship between a planet's distance from the Sun and its orbital period?

Kepler's third law states that the square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its average distance from the Sun. Farther planets travel longer paths at slower speeds, resulting in significantly longer orbital periods.

What is gravitational force?

Gravitational force is a fundamental force of nature that attracts any two objects with mass toward each other. Its strength is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

What is orbital velocity?

Orbital velocity is the minimum speed required for an object to maintain a stable orbit around a gravitational body without falling into it or escaping into space. For low Earth orbit, this is approximately 7.8 km/s.

What is centripetal force?

Centripetal force is any force that acts perpendicular to an object's motion, directed toward the center of its curved path. In orbital motion, gravity provides the centripetal force that continuously changes the direction of an orbiting object.

What is a geosynchronous orbit?

A geosynchronous orbit has an orbital period of exactly 24 hours, matching Earth's rotation. A geostationary orbit is a special case where the satellite orbits above the equator, appearing to hover over the same point on Earth's surface.

What is Kepler's first law of planetary motion?

Kepler's first law (the law of ellipses) states that every planet's orbit around the Sun is an ellipse, with the Sun positioned at one of the two focal points. This replaced the earlier assumption that orbits were perfectly circular.

What is the eccentricity of an orbit?

Eccentricity is a numerical measure of how elongated an orbit is, ranging from 0 (a perfect circle) to just under 1 (a very elongated ellipse). Earth's orbital eccentricity is about 0.017, making it nearly circular.

During the Apollo missions, astronauts experienced weightlessness during their transit from Earth to the Moon. A student asks if there is a point between Earth and the Moon where the gravitational pulls cancel out. What is the correct answer?

The L1 Lagrange point between Earth and the Moon is a location where the gravitational pull from Earth and the Moon, combined with the centrifugal effect from the orbital motion, balance out. This point is about 326,000 km from Earth.

An oceanside restaurant owner notices that flooding is especially severe during a full moon in January. A meteorologist explains this is because the Moon is at perigee (closest to Earth). What is this combined tidal event called?

When a spring tide (Sun-Moon alignment at full or new moon) coincides with the Moon being at perigee (closest to Earth), the result is an exceptionally high tide called a king tide or perigean spring tide. The closer Moon exerts even stronger gravitational pull, amplifying the tidal range.

Real-World Applications

Example: Neptune takes about 165 Earth years to complete one orbit around the Sun, while Earth takes 1 year. Using Kepler's third law, what can you infer about Neptune's average distance from the Sun compared to Earth's?

Kepler's third law states that the orbital period squared is proportional to the semi-major axis cubed (T2 = a3). With T = 165 years, a3 = 1652 = 27,225, so a = cube root of 27,225, which is approximately 30 AU.
