AP Statistics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Experimental Design establishes cause-and-effect relationships by deliberately imposing treatments on experimental units and comparing outcomes. The three principles of experimental design are control (using comparison groups), randomization (assigning treatments randomly to reduce bias), and replication (using enough subjects to detect real effects). A well-designed experiment with random assignment allows researchers to attribute differences in response to the treatment rather than confounding variables.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Experiment: A study in which the researcher deliberately imposes one or more treatments on subjects to observe the effect on a response variable
  • Random assignment: Allocating experimental units to treatment groups using a chance mechanism to balance confounding variables across groups
  • Control group: A group that receives no treatment or a standard treatment, providing a baseline for comparison with the treatment group
  • Placebo: An inactive treatment (e.g., sugar pill) given to the control group to eliminate the psychological effect of receiving treatment
  • Double-blind experiment: Neither the subjects nor the researchers measuring outcomes know which treatment each subject received, minimizing both placebo effect and experimenter bias

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Pharmaceutical clinical trials using double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomized designs to test new drug efficacy while meeting FDA requirements
  • Agricultural experiments using randomized block designs to compare fertilizer effectiveness across different soil types and field conditions
  • A/B testing in technology companies randomly assigning users to different website designs to determine which produces higher engagement
