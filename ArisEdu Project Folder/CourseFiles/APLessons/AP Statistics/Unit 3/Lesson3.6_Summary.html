AP Statistics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.6 - Summary

Key Concepts

Controlling Variables involves design strategies to isolate the effect of the treatment variable by holding other variables constant, matching subjects, or using statistical adjustments. Effective control ensures that observed differences in the response are attributable to the treatment rather than extraneous factors. Controlling includes keeping conditions identical across groups, standardizing procedures, and using control groups as baselines for comparison.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Control (in experiments): Holding all variables constant across treatment groups except the variable being tested, so differences can be attributed to the treatment
  • Extraneous variable: Any variable other than the explanatory variable that might affect the response and should be controlled in the experiment
  • Standardized conditions: Keeping environmental and procedural factors identical across all treatment groups to prevent them from influencing results
  • Replication: Repeating the experiment on many subjects to increase the reliability of results and enable estimation of natural variation
  • Statistical control: Using statistical techniques (stratification, regression) to adjust for confounding variables when physical control is not possible

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Pharmaceutical companies standardizing dosage timing, diet, and monitoring schedules across all treatment groups in a clinical trial
  • Controlling for socioeconomic status in educational studies by matching treatment and control schools on demographics before comparing outcomes
  • Laboratory experiments controlling temperature, humidity, and light exposure when testing plant growth under different fertilizer treatments
