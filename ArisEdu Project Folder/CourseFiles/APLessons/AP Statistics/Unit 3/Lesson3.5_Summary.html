AP Statistics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Bias and Confounding Variables threaten the validity of statistical studies by systematically distorting results. Bias is a systematic tendency to over- or underestimate the true value and cannot be reduced by increasing sample size. The three major types—selection bias, response bias, and nonresponse bias—must be addressed through careful study design, while confounding variables create false associations that mask or mimic true causal relationships.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Bias: A systematic error in data collection or analysis that consistently skews results in one direction; not reducible by increasing sample size
  • Selection bias (undercoverage): Systematic exclusion of certain population members from the sampling frame, producing a sample that doesn't represent the population
  • Response bias: Systematic error caused by the wording of questions, interviewer influence, or respondent dishonesty leading to inaccurate answers
  • Nonresponse bias: Occurs when individuals selected for a sample cannot be contacted or refuse to participate, and non-respondents differ systematically from respondents
  • Confounding: When the effects of two variables on the response cannot be distinguished from each other because they are intertwined

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Literary Digest's 1936 presidential poll failure—selection bias from using telephone directories and car registrations in the Depression era, yielding a wealthy, unrepresentative sample
  • Online survey nonresponse bias where only highly motivated or opinionated individuals choose to respond, skewing results toward extreme views
  • Confounding in studies linking coffee consumption to heart disease, where smoking (a confounder associated with both coffee drinking and heart disease) inflated the apparent risk
