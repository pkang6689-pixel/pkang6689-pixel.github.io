AP Statistics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Sampling Methods are systematic procedures for selecting a subset of individuals from a population to draw conclusions about the whole. Simple random sampling (SRS) gives every possible sample of size n an equal chance of selection, while stratified, cluster, and systematic sampling offer advantages in specific contexts. The method of sampling directly affects the validity of statistical inferences—poorly designed samples produce biased results regardless of sample size.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Simple random sample (SRS): A sample selected so that every possible sample of size n from the population has an equal probability of being chosen
  • Stratified random sample: Dividing the population into homogeneous subgroups (strata) and taking an SRS from each stratum to ensure representation
  • Cluster sample: Randomly selecting entire groups (clusters) and surveying all individuals within chosen clusters; practical when a population list is unavailable
  • Systematic sample: Selecting every kth individual from a list after a random starting point between 1 and k
  • Sampling frame: The list of all individuals in the population from which the sample is actually drawn; ideally matches the target population

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Political polling organizations using stratified sampling by demographic groups (age, region, party) to produce accurate election forecasts
  • Healthcare studies using cluster sampling of hospitals rather than individual patients when a complete patient list is impractical
  • Quality assurance using systematic sampling (every 50th item on an assembly line) to efficiently monitor production consistency
