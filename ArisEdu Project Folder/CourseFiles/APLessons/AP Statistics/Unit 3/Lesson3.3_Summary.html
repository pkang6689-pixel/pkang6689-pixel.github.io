AP Statistics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Blocking and Randomization are complementary strategies for reducing variability in experiments. Blocking groups experimental units by a known source of variability (such as age, gender, or location) before random assignment within each block, increasing the experiment's ability to detect treatment effects. A randomized block design combines the benefits of both techniques: blocking controls for known confounders while randomization balances unknown confounders across treatment groups.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Block: A group of experimental units that are similar on some characteristic expected to affect the response variable
  • Randomized block design: An experimental design where units are grouped into blocks by a confounding variable, then treatments are randomly assigned within each block
  • Matched pairs design: A special case of blocking where each block has exactly two units (or one unit receiving both treatments in random order)
  • Within-block variability: The variation among experimental units within the same block; reduced by effective blocking
  • Randomization within blocks: After forming blocks, using a chance mechanism to assign treatments within each block to balance remaining confounders

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Medical research blocking patients by disease severity before randomly assigning drug vs. placebo within each severity block
  • Tire wear studies using matched-pairs design: placing competing tire brands on opposite sides of the same car to control for driving conditions
  • Agricultural experiments blocking by field section (soil quality, sunlight exposure) before randomly assigning crop varieties within each block
