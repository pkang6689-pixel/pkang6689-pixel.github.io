AP Statistics - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Observational Studies vs Experiments differ fundamentally in whether the researcher imposes a treatment. Observational studies simply observe and record data without intervening, making them unable to establish causation due to lurking and confounding variables. Experiments use random assignment to eliminate confounding, allowing causal conclusions. Understanding this distinction is critical—many important questions (smoking's effect on health, educational interventions) rely on observational data when experiments are unethical or impractical.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Observational study: A study in which the researcher observes and records data without imposing a treatment or intervention on the subjects
  • Confounding variable: A variable associated with both the explanatory variable and the response variable that distorts the apparent relationship between them
  • Lurking variable: A variable not included in the study that may influence the relationship between the variables being studied
  • Causation vs. association: Association means two variables are related; causation requires that changing one variable directly produces a change in the other
  • Retrospective vs. prospective study: Retrospective studies look backward at existing data; prospective studies follow subjects forward in time from the start

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Tobacco litigation using decades of observational evidence (cohort studies, dose-response relationships) to establish smoking as a cause of lung cancer when experiments were unethical
  • Evaluating whether a new teaching method causes improved test scores requires an experiment with random assignment—simply comparing schools using different methods is observational
  • Epidemiological prospective cohort studies (like the Framingham Heart Study) tracking thousands of people over decades to identify cardiovascular risk factors
