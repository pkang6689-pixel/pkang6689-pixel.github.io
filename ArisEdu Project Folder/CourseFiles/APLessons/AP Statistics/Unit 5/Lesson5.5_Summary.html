AP Statistics - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

t-Distributions replace the normal distribution when the population standard deviation σ is unknown and must be estimated by the sample standard deviation s. The t-distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom has thicker tails than the standard normal, reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating σ. As degrees of freedom increase, the t-distribution approaches the standard normal, explaining why large-sample procedures using z and small-sample procedures using t give similar results for large n.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • t-distribution: A symmetric, bell-shaped distribution with heavier tails than the normal, parameterized by degrees of freedom (df = n-1 for one sample)
  • Degrees of freedom (df): df = n - 1 for a single sample; controls the shape of the t-distribution—more df means closer to normal
  • t-statistic: t = (x̄ - μ₀)/(s/√n); replaces the z-statistic when σ is unknown, using sample standard deviation s in the denominator
  • Heavy tails: t-distributions have more probability in the tails than the normal, producing wider confidence intervals and larger critical values for small samples
  • Robustness: t-procedures are robust to moderate non-normality for large samples but can be misleading for small samples from highly skewed distributions

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Environmental monitoring using t-procedures to test whether mean pollutant concentrations exceed safety standards when only sample data (not population σ) are available
  • Clinical trials with small patient groups using t-distribution critical values for confidence intervals, which are wider than z-intervals to account for imprecise σ estimates
  • Student performance analysis in a classroom of 25 students using t-distribution (df = 24) rather than z-distribution because the true population standard deviation is unknown
