AP Statistics - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that regardless of the population's shape, the sampling distribution of x̄ approaches a normal distribution as sample size n increases (generally n ≥ 30 is sufficient). This is the most important theorem in statistics because it justifies using normal-based procedures (z-intervals, z-tests) for inference about means even when the underlying population is not normal. The CLT also applies to sample proportions and sums.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Central Limit Theorem (CLT): For large samples, the sampling distribution of x̄ is approximately N(μ, σ/√n) regardless of the population distribution shape
  • Normal approximation: Using a normal distribution to approximate the sampling distribution of a statistic when the CLT conditions are satisfied
  • Sample size condition: General guideline: n ≥ 30 for the CLT to produce a good normal approximation; smaller n may suffice for populations already near normal
  • Shape of sampling distribution: Approaches normality as n increases; the more skewed or irregular the population, the larger n must be for good approximation
  • CLT for proportions: The sampling distribution of p̂ is approximately N(p, √(p(1-p)/n)) when np ≥ 10 and n(1-p) ≥ 10

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Demonstrating the CLT with simulations: rolling dice (uniform distribution) and plotting the mean of 30 rolls—the distribution of means is approximately normal despite the uniform population
  • Election polling using CLT to justify normal-based margin of error calculations even though individual voter responses (yes/no) are binary, not normal
  • Quality control using CLT to construct control charts for process means even when individual measurements follow a skewed distribution
