AP Statistics - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Sampling Distribution of Sample Proportions describes the distribution of p̂ = X/n across all possible samples, where X is the count of successes in n independent trials. The sampling distribution of p̂ has mean p and standard deviation √(p(1-p)/n), and by the CLT is approximately normal when np ≥ 10 and n(1-p) ≥ 10. This distribution forms the basis for inference about population proportions using z-procedures.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Sample proportion (p̂): The fraction of successes in a sample: p̂ = X/n, where X is the number of successes
  • Mean of p̂: μp̂ = p; the sample proportion is an unbiased estimator of the population proportion
  • Standard deviation of p̂: σp̂ = √(p(1-p)/n); decreases as n increases, meaning larger samples give more precise estimates
  • Normal approximation conditions: The sampling distribution of p̂ is approximately normal when np ≥ 10 AND n(1-p) ≥ 10 (Large Counts condition)
  • 10% condition: When sampling without replacement, n should be less than 10% of the population to treat observations as approximately independent

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Pre-election polls using the sampling distribution of p̂ to calculate the margin of error for estimating a candidate's true support level
  • Pharmaceutical trials determining sample size needed so the sampling distribution of the cure rate is narrow enough to detect a clinically meaningful effect
  • Social media analysis estimating the proportion of posts containing misinformation and computing a confidence interval using the normal approximation to p̂
