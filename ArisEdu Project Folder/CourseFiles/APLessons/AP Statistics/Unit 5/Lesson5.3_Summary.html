AP Statistics - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Rules for Means and Variances of combined random variables enable calculation of summary statistics for sums, differences, and linear combinations. For any random variables: E(X ± Y) = E(X) ± E(Y), always. For independent random variables: Var(X ± Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)—variances always add, even for differences. These rules are essential for combining sampling distributions and for calculating standard errors of differences in means or proportions.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Addition rule for means: E(X + Y) = E(X) + E(Y) and E(X - Y) = E(X) - E(Y); applies regardless of independence
  • Addition rule for variances: Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) and Var(X - Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y); ONLY valid when X and Y are independent
  • Standard deviation of a sum/difference: SD(X ± Y) = √(Var(X) + Var(Y)) for independent random variables; note that SDs do NOT simply add
  • Linear combination: T = aX + bY: E(T) = aE(X) + bE(Y) and Var(T) = a²Var(X) + b²Var(Y) for independent X, Y
  • Independence requirement: Variance addition rules require independence; for dependent variables, covariance terms must be included

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Combining manufacturing tolerances: if a bolt and nut are produced independently, the play in the assembled joint has variance equal to the sum of their individual variances
  • Portfolio diversification: the variance of a portfolio return equals the weighted sum of individual stock variances (plus covariance terms for non-independent stocks)
  • Computing the standard error of the difference in sample means (x̄₁ - x̄₂) when comparing two independent groups in a two-sample t-test
