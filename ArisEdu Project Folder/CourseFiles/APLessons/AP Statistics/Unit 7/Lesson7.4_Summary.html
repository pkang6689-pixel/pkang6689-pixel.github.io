AP Statistics - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Paired t-Tests compare two related measurements on the same subjects by analyzing the differences d = x₁ - x₂ within each pair. The test statistic t = d̄/(sd/√n) with df = n-1 (where n is the number of pairs) tests H₀: μd = 0. Paired designs are more powerful than independent two-sample designs because they eliminate subject-to-subject variability, isolating the treatment effect.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Paired data: Two measurements on the same subject or matched subjects, creating natural pairs whose differences are analyzed
  • Paired t-test: One-sample t-test applied to the differences: t = d̄/(sd/√n) with df = n-1, testing H₀: μd = 0
  • Mean difference (d̄): The average of the paired differences; estimates the true mean difference μd between the two conditions
  • Paired t-interval: d̄ ± t*·(sd/√n); estimates the true mean difference between two paired conditions
  • Advantage of pairing: Reduces variability by eliminating between-subject variation, making it easier to detect true treatment effects

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Before-and-after weight loss studies measuring each participant's weight before and after a diet program, testing whether the mean weight change differs from 0
  • Taste tests where each consumer rates two products, analyzing the paired differences to determine if there is a preference for one product over the other
  • Vision research comparing visual acuity readings from the left and right eyes of the same patients to test for systematic differences
