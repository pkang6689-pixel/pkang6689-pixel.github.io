AP Statistics - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Confidence Intervals for Means estimate an unknown population mean μ using x̄ ± t*(s/√n), where t* comes from the t-distribution with df = n-1. The width of the interval is determined by the confidence level (higher → wider), sample size (larger → narrower), and sample variability (larger s → wider). Correct interpretation states: "We are C% confident that the true population mean lies within this interval," meaning C% of all such intervals capture μ.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Confidence interval for μ: x̄ ± t*·(s/√n); gives a range of plausible values for the population mean based on sample data
  • Margin of error (for means): t*·(s/√n); the maximum likely difference between x̄ and μ at the stated confidence level
  • Interpretation of confidence level: If we repeated the sampling process many times, C% of the resulting intervals would capture the true population mean
  • Effect of sample size: Increasing n decreases the margin of error proportionally to 1/√n, producing a narrower (more precise) interval
  • Required sample size: n = (t*·s/ME)²; the sample size needed to achieve a desired margin of error, often requiring an initial pilot study to estimate s

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Hospital administrators constructing a 95% confidence interval for mean patient wait time to evaluate whether current staffing levels meet service standards
  • Consumer Reports testing batteries and reporting the mean lifetime as a confidence interval: '425 to 475 hours at 95% confidence'
  • Agricultural researchers estimating average crop yield per acre using field trial data to determine whether a new variety outperforms the current standard
