AP Statistics - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Significance Tests for Proportions test whether a population proportion p equals a hypothesized value p₀ using the test statistic z = (p̂ - p₀)/√(p₀(1-p₀)/n). The four-step process involves stating hypotheses, checking conditions (random sample, 10% condition, np₀ ≥ 10 and n(1-p₀) ≥ 10), calculating the test statistic and p-value, and stating a conclusion in context. One-sided tests (Hₐ: p > p₀ or p < p₀) and two-sided tests (Hₐ: p ≠ p₀) require different p-value calculations.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • One-proportion z-test: Tests H₀: p = p₀ using z = (p̂ - p₀)/√(p₀(1-p₀)/n) and the standard normal distribution
  • Test statistic: z = (p̂ - p₀)/√(p₀(1-p₀)/n); measures how many standard errors the sample proportion falls from the hypothesized value
  • One-sided vs. two-sided test: One-sided: Hₐ: p > p₀ or p < p₀ (p-value is area in one tail); Two-sided: Hₐ: p ≠ p₀ (p-value is area in both tails)
  • Conditions for z-test: Random sample or random assignment, n < 10% of population, np₀ ≥ 10 and n(1-p₀) ≥ 10
  • Conclusion in context: State the decision (reject/fail to reject H₀) and interpret in the real-world context of the problem, including the p-value

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Testing whether a coin is fair (H₀: p = 0.5) by flipping it 100 times and computing the z-statistic from the observed proportion of heads
  • A manufacturer testing whether the defect rate exceeds the 3% industry standard (H₀: p = 0.03, Hₐ: p > 0.03) to trigger quality interventions
  • Public health officials testing whether the proportion of residents with elevated lead levels differs from the national rate to justify environmental remediation
