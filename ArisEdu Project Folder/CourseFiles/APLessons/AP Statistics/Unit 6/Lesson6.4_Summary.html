AP Statistics - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Comparing Two Proportions tests whether two population proportions differ using the test statistic z = (p̂₁ - p̂₂)/√(p̂c(1-p̂c)(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)), where p̂c is the pooled proportion under H₀: p₁ = p₂. Confidence intervals for p₁ - p₂ use the formula (p̂₁ - p̂₂) ± z*√(p̂₁(1-p̂₁)/n₁ + p̂₂(1-p̂₂)/n₂). Comparing proportions is essential in clinical trials, A/B testing, and any study comparing success rates between groups.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Two-proportion z-test: Tests H₀: p₁ = p₂ using pooled proportion p̂c and z = (p̂₁ - p̂₂)/SE_pooled
  • Pooled proportion: p̂c = (X₁ + X₂)/(n₁ + n₂); combined success rate across both samples, used in the test statistic under H₀
  • CI for p₁ - p₂: (p̂₁ - p̂₂) ± z*·√(p̂₁(1-p̂₁)/n₁ + p̂₂(1-p̂₂)/n₂); uses unpooled standard error since we don't assume p₁ = p₂
  • Large Counts condition (two samples): n₁p̂c ≥ 10, n₁(1-p̂c) ≥ 10, n₂p̂c ≥ 10, and n₂(1-p̂c) ≥ 10 for the z-test
  • Independent samples: Observations in one sample must not influence observations in the other; required for valid two-proportion inference

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Clinical trials comparing cure rates between drug and placebo groups to determine whether the drug is more effective than no treatment
  • Political campaigns comparing voter support proportions between two demographic groups to target messaging strategies
  • Education research comparing graduation rates between students enrolled in a mentoring program vs. those who were not
