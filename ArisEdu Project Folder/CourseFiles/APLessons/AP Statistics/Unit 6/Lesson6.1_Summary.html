AP Statistics - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Confidence Intervals for Proportions estimate an unknown population proportion p using the sample proportion p̂ ± z*√(p̂(1-p̂)/n), where z* is the critical value for the desired confidence level. The interval gives a range of plausible values for p, with the margin of error z*√(p̂(1-p̂)/n) reflecting both the confidence level and sample size. Interpretation is crucial: a 95% CI means that 95% of all intervals constructed this way capture the true p.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Confidence interval for p: p̂ ± z*·√(p̂(1-p̂)/n); a range of plausible values for the population proportion based on sample data
  • Confidence level: The long-run proportion of intervals that capture the true parameter; e.g., 95% means 95 out of 100 intervals would contain p
  • Margin of error: z*·√(p̂(1-p̂)/n); the maximum likely difference between p̂ and the true p at the given confidence level
  • Critical value (z*): The z-score corresponding to the desired confidence level: z* = 1.645 for 90%, 1.96 for 95%, 2.576 for 99%
  • Sample size determination: n = (z*/ME)²·p̂(1-p̂); formula for choosing n to achieve a desired margin of error; use p̂ = 0.5 for conservative estimate

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • News organizations reporting poll results with margin of error ('52% favor the measure ± 3 percentage points') derived from the confidence interval formula
  • Market research firms determining sample sizes needed to estimate the proportion of consumers preferring a product within ±2% with 95% confidence
  • Public health surveys constructing confidence intervals for vaccination rates in a community to guide immunization campaign decisions
