AP Statistics - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Correlation and Causation is a critical distinction in statistics: correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship, but it does not imply that one variable causes changes in the other. Observational data can reveal associations but not causation, because confounding variables may drive the apparent relationship. Establishing causation requires controlled experiments with random assignment, ruling out confounders and reverse causality.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Correlation (r): Measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two quantitative variables; -1 ≤ r ≤ +1
  • Causation: A change in x directly produces a change in y; requires ruling out confounding variables and establishing a mechanism
  • Confounding in regression: A third variable associated with both x and y that creates a spurious apparent relationship between them
  • Lurking variable: An unobserved variable that influences both the explanatory and response variables, creating a false impression of a direct relationship
  • Establishing causation: Requires: (1) association, (2) temporal precedence (cause before effect), (3) elimination of confounders (typically through random assignment in experiments)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • The classic ice cream and drowning correlation: both increase in summer due to the lurking variable of temperature, not because ice cream causes drowning
  • Debunking the claim that countries with higher chocolate consumption produce more Nobel laureates by identifying wealth as the confounding variable
  • Medical researchers using randomized controlled trials rather than observational studies to establish whether a drug causes improvement, controlling for the placebo effect and confounders
