AP Statistics - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Transformations and Non-Linear Models in regression address data where the relationship between x and y is not linear. When residual plots reveal a pattern (curve, fan shape), transformations such as log(y), √y, 1/y, or log(x) can linearize the relationship. The choice of transformation depends on the curvature pattern, and predictions must be back-transformed to the original scale. Model comparison using r² and residual plots helps determine which model (linear, exponential, power) best fits the data.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Residual pattern: A curved or systematic pattern in the residual plot indicates the linear model is inadequate and a transformation or different model is needed
  • Log transformation: Taking log(y) to linearize exponential growth; taking log(x) to linearize logarithmic relationships; taking both for power models
  • Exponential regression: Fits y = ab^x by regressing log(y) against x; the slope of the transformed line gives log(b)
  • Power regression: Fits y = ax^b by regressing log(y) against log(x); the slope of the transformed line gives b
  • Model selection: Compare R² values, residual plots, and prediction accuracy across candidate models to choose the best-fitting transformation

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Modeling world population growth (exponential) by plotting log(population) vs. year to achieve linearity and project future population
  • Astronomy: the power relationship between a planet's orbital period and distance from the sun (Kepler's Third Law: T² ∝ r³) linearized using log-log transformation
  • Economics: linearizing the diminishing returns relationship between advertising spending and sales using a logarithmic transformation of spending
