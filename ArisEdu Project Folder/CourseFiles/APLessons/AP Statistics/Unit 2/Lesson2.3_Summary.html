AP Statistics - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Residuals and Least-Squares Regression fit a linear model ŷ = a + bx to bivariate data by minimizing the sum of squared residuals (Σ(y - ŷ)²). The slope b = r(sy/sx) represents the predicted change in y for a one-unit increase in x, while the y-intercept a gives the predicted y when x = 0. Residual plots—graphs of residuals versus x—are essential diagnostic tools: a random scatter indicates the linear model is appropriate, while patterns suggest non-linearity.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Least-squares regression line (LSRL): The line ŷ = a + bx that minimizes the sum of squared residuals; passes through the point (x̄, ȳ)
  • Residual: The difference between observed and predicted values: residual = y - ŷ; positive means the model underpredicted
  • Coefficient of determination (r²): The proportion of variation in y explained by the linear relationship with x; ranges from 0 to 1
  • Correlation coefficient (r): A measure of the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two quantitative variables; ranges from -1 to +1
  • Residual plot: A scatterplot of residuals versus the explanatory variable; used to check whether a linear model is appropriate for the data

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Predicting home sale prices from square footage using LSRL, then examining residual plots to verify the linear model's adequacy
  • Analyzing the relationship between study hours and exam scores to determine what proportion (r²) of score variation is explained by study time
  • Identifying non-linear trends in economic data (GDP growth vs. time) when residual plots show curved patterns requiring transformation
