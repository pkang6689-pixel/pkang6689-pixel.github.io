AP Human Geography - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.6 - Summary

Key Concepts

Cultural Landscapes and Identity synthesizes how identities—ethnic, national, religious, and cultural—are expressed and reinforced through landscapes. This lesson examines how groups contest spaces, assert identity claims through place-making, and how landscapes embody power relationships. Understanding identity landscapes reveals social hierarchies and cultural politics.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Contested Space: A place where different groups claim identity or territorial rights, often resulting in conflict
  • Symbolic Landscape: Places imbued with particular meaning and significance for specific groups
  • Imagined Community: An ethnic or national group bound together by shared imagination rather than face-to-face interaction
  • Place-Making: The process through which groups construct identity by investing meaning in particular locations
  • Heritage Tourism: Tourism based on cultural, historical, or ethnic heritage, commodifying identity and landscaped

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Explains ethnic neighborhoods, monument conflicts, and competing claims to holy sites
  • Critical for understanding diaspora communities, immigrant identity, and belonging
  • Essential for analyzing cultural preservation, heritage tourism, and commodification of culture
