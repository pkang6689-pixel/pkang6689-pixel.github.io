AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Conservative Forces and Potential Energy connect force to potential energy through F = -dU/dr (in one dimension) or F = -∇U (in three dimensions). A force is conservative if the work it does depends only on initial and final positions, not the path. The potential energy function U(x) stores energy that can be recovered as kinetic energy. Force-potential energy diagrams reveal equilibrium positions (where F = -dU/dx = 0) and their stability (slope determines restoring or repelling character).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Conservative force: A force whose work is path-independent; equivalently, the work around any closed path is zero; examples: gravity, spring force
  • Potential energy function: U(x) such that F = -dU/dx; the negative derivative of potential energy gives the force
  • Gravitational PE (near Earth): U = mgh; the energy stored when an object is raised height h above a reference level
  • Elastic PE: U = ½kx²; the energy stored in a spring displaced x from equilibrium
  • Equilibrium points: Locations where F = -dU/dx = 0; stable if U is a minimum (restoring force), unstable if U is a maximum

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Molecular chemistry modeling interatomic bonds as potential energy curves (Lennard-Jones potential) to predict bond length and bond energy
  • Civil engineering analyzing dam stability using gravitational potential energy and identifying stable vs. unstable equilibrium configurations
  • Roller coaster design using potential energy diagrams to verify sufficient kinetic energy at all points along the track without external power input
