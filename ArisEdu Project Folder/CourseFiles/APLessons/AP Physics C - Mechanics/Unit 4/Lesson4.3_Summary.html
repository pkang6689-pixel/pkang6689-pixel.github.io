AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Conservation of Energy states that in an isolated system, total mechanical energy (KE + PE) remains constant when only conservative forces do work: KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂. When non-conservative forces (friction, air resistance) are present, the generalized equation becomes KE₁ + PE₁ + W_nc = KE₂ + PE₂, where W_nc represents energy converted to or from thermal energy. Energy conservation is the most broadly applicable principle in physics.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Conservation of mechanical energy: KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂; total mechanical energy is constant when only conservative forces do work
  • Mechanical energy: E_mech = KE + PE; the sum of kinetic and potential energies of a system
  • Non-conservative work: W_nc = ΔKE + ΔPE = E_final - E_initial; work by friction or applied forces that changes the total mechanical energy
  • Energy dissipation: Conversion of mechanical energy to thermal energy by friction: ΔE_thermal = f_k × d
  • Energy bar charts: Visual representations showing the distribution of energy (KE, PE, thermal) at different points in a process

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Hydroelectric dam design converting gravitational PE of water (mgh) to kinetic energy of turbines to electrical energy, analyzing efficiency at each conversion stage
  • Forensic physics reconstructing car accident speeds from skid mark lengths using W_friction = ΔKE to calculate the initial velocity
  • Pole vaulting biomechanics analyzing the conversion of kinetic energy → elastic PE in the pole → gravitational PE at peak height
