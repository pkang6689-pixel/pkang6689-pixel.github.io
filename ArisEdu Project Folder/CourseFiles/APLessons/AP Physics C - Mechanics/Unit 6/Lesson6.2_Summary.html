AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Torque and Rotational Dynamics extend Newton's Second Law to rotation: τ_net = Iα, where torque τ = r × F (magnitude rF sinθ) is the rotational analog of force, and I (moment of inertia) is the rotational analog of mass. Torque about a pivot determines whether an object rotates and in which direction. Static equilibrium requires both ΣF = 0 and Στ = 0, providing the basis for analyzing bridges, seesaws, and structural systems.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Torque: τ = r × F; magnitude τ = rF sinθ; the rotational effectiveness of a force, measured in N·m
  • Newton's Second Law for rotation: τ_net = Iα; net torque equals moment of inertia times angular acceleration
  • Moment arm (lever arm): r⊥ = r sinθ; the perpendicular distance from the pivot point to the line of action of the force
  • Static equilibrium: ΣF = 0 and Στ = 0; conditions for an object to remain at rest with no linear or rotational acceleration
  • Sign convention for torque: Counterclockwise torques are positive; clockwise torques are negative (right-hand rule convention)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Wrench design and usage: longer handles provide greater torque for the same applied force, following τ = rF
  • Bridge engineering calculating torques about supports to determine forces on beams and ensure structural equilibrium
  • Door handle placement: handles are at the edge (maximum r) to maximize torque for a given applied force, making doors easy to open
