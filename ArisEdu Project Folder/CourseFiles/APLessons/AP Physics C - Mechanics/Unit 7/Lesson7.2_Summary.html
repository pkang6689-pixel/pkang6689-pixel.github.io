AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Pendulums are oscillating systems where gravity provides the restoring torque. The simple pendulum (point mass on a massless string) has period T = 2π√(L/g) for small angles, independent of mass and amplitude. The physical (compound) pendulum accounts for distributed mass: T = 2π√(I/(mgd)), where I is the moment of inertia about the pivot and d is the distance from pivot to center of mass. Both approximate SHM for small oscillations.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Simple pendulum period: T = 2π√(L/g); depends only on length and gravitational acceleration for small-angle oscillations (θ < ~15°)
  • Small-angle approximation: sinθ ≈ θ (in radians) for small angles; this linearizes the restoring torque and produces SHM
  • Physical pendulum: T = 2π√(I/(mgd)); a real extended body oscillating about a pivot, where I is the moment of inertia about the pivot
  • Restoring torque: τ = -mgd sinθ for a pendulum; for small θ, τ ≈ -mgdθ, giving angular SHM with ω = √(mgd/I)
  • Energy in pendulum: E = KE + PE = ½mv² + mgh; maximum KE at bottom, maximum PE at maximum angle

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Foucault pendulum demonstration proving Earth's rotation by observing the slow precession of a long pendulum's swing plane over hours
  • Grandfather clock design using the precise period T = 2π√(L/g) of a pendulum to regulate timekeeping mechanisms
  • Seismometer design using physical pendulums with long periods to detect and record slow ground motion during earthquakes
