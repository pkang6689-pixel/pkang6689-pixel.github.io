AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Collisions are classified by whether kinetic energy is conserved. In elastic collisions, both momentum and KE are conserved, yielding two equations to solve for two unknowns. In perfectly inelastic collisions, the objects stick together and maximum KE is lost (converted to thermal/sound energy). Real collisions are partially inelastic. The coefficient of restitution e = (v₂' - v₁')/(v₁ - v₂) quantifies the elasticity of a collision (e = 1 elastic, e = 0 perfectly inelastic).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Elastic collision: A collision in which both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved; objects bounce apart with no KE loss
  • Perfectly inelastic collision: A collision in which the objects stick together; maximum kinetic energy is lost while momentum is still conserved
  • Coefficient of restitution: e = relative speed after / relative speed before collision; e = 1 (elastic), 0 < e < 1 (partially inelastic), e = 0 (perfectly inelastic)
  • 1D elastic collision formulas: v₁' = ((m₁-m₂)/(m₁+m₂))v₁ + (2m₂/(m₁+m₂))v₂; derived from simultaneous momentum and KE conservation
  • Energy loss in collisions: ΔKE = KE_f - KE_i; negative for inelastic collisions; equals -½μv_rel² where μ = m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂) is the reduced mass

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Vehicle accident reconstruction using partially inelastic collision models and coefficient of restitution to determine pre-collision speeds
  • Neutron moderation in nuclear reactors: elastic collisions between neutrons and hydrogen atoms (equal mass) transfer maximum kinetic energy in a single collision
  • Sports equipment design (golf clubs, tennis rackets) optimizing mass ratios and coefficients of restitution for maximum energy transfer to the ball
