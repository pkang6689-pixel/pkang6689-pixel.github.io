AP Physics C - Mechanics - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Conservation of Momentum states that when no external forces act on a system, the total momentum is constant: Σp_initial = Σp_final. This principle is derived directly from Newton's Third Law—internal forces between objects cancel in pairs. Conservation of momentum applies to collisions, explosions, and recoil in one and two dimensions, and is particularly powerful when forces are impulsive (large and brief) or when the system is isolated.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Conservation of momentum: p_total,initial = p_total,final; total momentum of an isolated system remains constant
  • Isolated system: A system on which no net external force acts; internal forces cancel by Newton's Third Law
  • Internal vs. external forces: Internal forces (between system objects) don't change total momentum; external forces (from outside the system) do
  • Two-dimensional momentum conservation: Momentum is conserved independently in the x and y directions: Σp_x,i = Σp_x,f and Σp_y,i = Σp_y,f
  • Recoil: When an object ejects mass (gun firing, rocket exhaust), the remaining object gains momentum in the opposite direction

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Firearms forensics using conservation of momentum to calculate bullet speed from the recoil velocity of a ballistic pendulum
  • Nuclear physics analyzing particle collision products using conservation of momentum in two dimensions to identify unknown particles
  • Space debris collision modeling using momentum conservation to predict trajectories of fragments for satellite safety planning
