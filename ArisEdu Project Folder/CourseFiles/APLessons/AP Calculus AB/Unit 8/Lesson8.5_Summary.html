AP Calculus AB - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Applications to Physics uses integration to solve problems in mechanics and other physics domains. Work done by a variable force: W = ∫ₐᵇ F(x)dx. Hooke's Law spring work: W = ∫₀ˣ kx dx = kx²/2. Center of mass: x̄ = (∫ₐᵇ x·ρ(x)dx)/(∫ₐᵇ ρ(x)dx). These applications demonstrate how integration computes total effects of continuously varying quantities.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Work by variable force: W = ∫ₐᵇ F(x)dx; the integral of force over displacement gives total work done (in joules or foot-pounds)
  • Hooke's Law: F = kx for a spring; work to stretch from 0 to x: W = ∫₀ˣ kx dx = ½kx²
  • Pumping work: W = ∫₀ᴴ ρgA(y)·y dy; the work to pump water from a tank, where A(y) is the cross-sectional area at height y
  • Center of mass (1D): x̄ = (Σmᵢxᵢ)/(Σmᵢ) for discrete; x̄ = ∫x·dm / ∫dm for continuous mass distributions
  • Hydrostatic force: F = ∫₀ᴴ ρg·w(y)·y dy; force on a submerged vertical plate, where w(y) is width at depth y

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Dam engineering: computing hydrostatic pressure and total force on a dam face using integration of pressure over the submerged area
  • Aerospace: calculating the work needed to launch a satellite by integrating the gravitational force F = GMm/r² from Earth's surface to orbit
  • Automotive suspension design: computing the work done compressing a non-linear spring (where F(x) is not simply kx) for ride comfort optimization
