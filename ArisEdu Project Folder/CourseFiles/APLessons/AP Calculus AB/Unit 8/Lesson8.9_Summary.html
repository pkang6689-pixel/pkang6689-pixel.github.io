AP Calculus AB - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.9 - Summary

Key Concepts

More Applications of Integration surveys additional real-world uses. Lorenz curves and Gini coefficients measure income inequality using the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of equality. Consumer and producer surplus in economics use area between curves. Cardiac output is estimated by integrating dye dilution curves. These diverse applications demonstrate that integration is a universal tool for accumulating continuously varying quantities.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Lorenz curve: L(x) plots the cumulative fraction of total income earned by the bottom x fraction of earners; perfectly equal distribution gives L(x) = x
  • Gini coefficient: G = 2∫₀¹ [x - L(x)]dx; ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality); measures income distribution
  • Consumer surplus: ∫₀^Q* [D(q) - p*]dq; the area between the demand curve and market price, representing consumer benefit
  • Producer surplus: ∫₀^Q* [p* - S(q)]dq; the area between market price and supply curve, representing producer benefit
  • Cardiac output: CO = amount of dye / ∫₀ᵀ c(t)dt; using the Stewart-Hamilton principle to measure blood flow from dye concentration curves

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Government policy: Gini coefficients computed from income data guide tax policy and social welfare program design
  • Market analysis: consumer and producer surplus calculations inform pricing strategy and measure economic efficiency
  • Medical diagnostics: cardiac output measurement via thermodilution uses integration of temperature-change curves over time
