AP Calculus AB - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.8 - Summary

Key Concepts

The Chain Rule is the most powerful and frequently used derivative rule, essential for differentiating composite functions. If y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f'(g(x)) · g'(x), or equivalently dy/dx = (dy/du)(du/dx) where u = g(x). The chain rule 'peels off layers' of composition: differentiate the outer function (leaving inner unchanged), then multiply by the derivative of the inner. Multiple compositions require applying the chain rule iteratively.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Chain rule formula: d/dx[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) · g'(x); outer derivative evaluated at inner, times inner derivative
  • Leibniz notation: dy/dx = (dy/du)(du/dx) where u = g(x); the 'du' terms cancel suggestively (though they are not literals fractions)
  • General power rule: d/dx[(g(x))ⁿ] = n(g(x))ⁿ⁻¹ · g'(x); the chain rule applied to a power of a function
  • General exponential rule: d/dx[e^(g(x))] = e^(g(x)) · g'(x); the chain rule applied to exponentials of functions
  • Multiple chain rule: For y = f(g(h(x))): dy/dx = f'(g(h(x))) · g'(h(x)) · h'(x); each layer is differentiated in turn

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • GPS velocity computation: position is a composite function of time through multiple coordinate transformations, requiring iterated chain rule applications
  • Compound interest with variable rates: dA/dt for A = P·e^(∫r(t)dt) requires the chain rule combined with the FTC for variable-rate financial modeling
  • Neural networks in AI: backpropagation training uses the chain rule repeatedly to compute gradients through layers of composed activation functions
