AP Calculus AB - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Introduction to Integrals presents integration as the reverse of differentiation and as the accumulation of quantities. The definite integral ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx represents the signed area between f(x) and the x-axis from a to b. Riemann sums approximate this area using rectangles: left, right, and midpoint sums with n subintervals. As n → ∞, Riemann sums converge to the exact integral. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects differentiation and integration: d/dx[∫ₐˣ f(t)dt] = f(x).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Definite integral: ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx; the signed area between f(x) and the x-axis from x = a to x = b
  • Riemann sum: An approximation of the definite integral using n rectangles: Σf(xᵢ*)Δx, where xᵢ* is the sample point in each subinterval
  • Left/Right/Midpoint sum: Left: sample at left endpoint; Right: sample at right endpoint; Midpoint: sample at center of each subinterval
  • Fundamental Theorem (Part 1): If F(x) = ∫ₐˣ f(t)dt, then F'(x) = f(x); differentiation undoes integration
  • Antiderivative: A function F(x) whose derivative is f(x): F'(x) = f(x); the general antiderivative includes + C

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Calculating distance traveled from a velocity-time graph: the area under the curve equals total displacement, used in trip planning and vehicle testing
  • Computing total energy consumed over time: integrating power (watts) over time gives energy (joules), essential for electrical grid management
  • Estimating total rainfall from rate-of-rainfall data: ∫(rainfall rate)dt gives total accumulated precipitation over a storm event
