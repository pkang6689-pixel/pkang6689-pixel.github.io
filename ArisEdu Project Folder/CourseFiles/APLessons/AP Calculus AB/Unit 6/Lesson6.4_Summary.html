AP Calculus AB - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

U-Substitution (Part 1) is the integration counterpart of the chain rule. If the integrand has the form f(g(x))·g'(x), let u = g(x), then du = g'(x)dx, transforming the integral to ∫f(u)du, which is often simpler. After integrating in terms of u, substitute back: u = g(x). For definite integrals, change the limits to u-values: when x = a, u = g(a); when x = b, u = g(b). U-substitution is the most important integration technique.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • U-substitution method: Let u = g(x), du = g'(x)dx; transform ∫f(g(x))g'(x)dx into ∫f(u)du; substitute back after integrating
  • Choosing u: Look for an inner function g(x) whose derivative g'(x) appears (or nearly appears) elsewhere in the integrand
  • Adjusting constants: If du differs from the available dx by a constant factor, adjust: if u = 3x, then du = 3dx, so dx = du/3
  • Changing limits: For definite integrals: convert limits from x to u (u_lower = g(a), u_upper = g(b)); no need to substitute back
  • Common u-substitutions: ∫2x·e^(x²)dx: u = x²; ∫sin(3x)dx: u = 3x; ∫x/(1+x²)dx: u = 1+x²

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Computing the total charge delivered by a time-varying current I(t) = I₀sin(ωt): Q = ∫I(t)dt requires u-substitution with u = ωt
  • Probability: evaluating normal distribution probabilities ∫e^(-x²/2)dx numerically requires u-substitution to standardize the variable
  • Heat transfer calculations: integrating temperature-dependent thermal conductivity over a material thickness using u-substitution for the temperature variable
