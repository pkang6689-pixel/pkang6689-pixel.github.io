AP Calculus AB - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.9 - Summary

Key Concepts

Accumulation Functions are functions defined by integrals: F(x) = ∫ₐˣ f(t)dt. F(x) represents the accumulated (signed) area under f from a to x. By FTC Part 1, F'(x) = f(x). The graph of F reveals crucial information: F is increasing where f > 0, decreasing where f < 0, and has local extrema where f = 0 and changes sign. The concavity of F corresponds to whether f is increasing (F concave up) or decreasing (F concave down).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Accumulation function: F(x) = ∫ₐˣ f(t)dt; the running total of the signed area under f from a constant a to the variable x
  • F'(x) = f(x): FTC Part 1: the derivative of the accumulation function equals the integrand; connects the graph of f to properties of F
  • F increasing/decreasing: F increases where f > 0 (positive area accumulating); F decreases where f < 0 (negative area subtracting)
  • Extrema of F: F has local maxima/minima where f = 0 and changes sign (f goes from + to - = max; - to + = min)
  • Concavity of F: F is concave up where f is increasing (F'' = f' > 0); concave down where f is decreasing (F'' = f' < 0)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Total rainfall accumulation: if r(t) is the rainfall rate at time t, then total accumulated rainfall by time x is R(x) = ∫₀ˣ r(t)dt
  • Battery charge level: if I(t) is the current flow, the charge Q(x) = ∫₀ˣ I(t)dt represents the accumulated charge at time x
  • Distance odometers: accumulate distance from speed readings: D(x) = ∫₀ˣ |v(t)|dt tracks total distance traveled regardless of direction
