AP Calculus AB - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Riemann Sums and Definite Integrals approximate and compute the exact area under a curve. A Riemann sum divides [a,b] into n subintervals and approximates the area using rectangles: Σᵢf(xᵢ*)Δx. Left, right, and midpoint sums use different sample points. As n→∞, Riemann sums converge to the definite integral: ∫ₐᵇf(x)dx = lim(n→∞) Σf(xᵢ*)Δx. The Trapezoidal Rule uses trapezoids for better approximation: T = (Δx/2)[f(x₀) + 2f(x₁) + ... + 2f(xₙ₋₁) + f(xₙ)].

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Left Riemann sum: Uses the left endpoint of each subinterval: L_n = Σᵢ₌₀ⁿ⁻¹ f(xᵢ)Δx; underestimates if f is increasing
  • Right Riemann sum: Uses the right endpoint: R_n = Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ f(xᵢ)Δx; overestimates if f is increasing
  • Midpoint sum: Uses the midpoint of each subinterval: M_n = Σf((xᵢ + xᵢ₊₁)/2)Δx; generally more accurate than left/right
  • Trapezoidal Rule: Averages left and right sums: T_n = (L_n + R_n)/2 = (Δx/2)[f(x₀) + 2f(x₁) + ... + 2f(xₙ₋₁) + f(xₙ)]
  • Definite integral as limit: ∫ₐᵇf(x)dx = lim(n→∞) Σf(xᵢ*)Δx; the exact area equals the limit of Riemann sums as partition gets infinitely fine

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Estimating distance from velocity data: using a table of speedometer readings at time intervals and Riemann sums to calculate total distance traveled
  • Numerical integration in computer science: trapezoidal and Simpson's rules are programmed into calculators and software for approximating integrals when antiderivatives don't exist in closed form
  • Estimating total water consumption from flow rate measurements taken at regular intervals—a practical Riemann sum application in resource management
