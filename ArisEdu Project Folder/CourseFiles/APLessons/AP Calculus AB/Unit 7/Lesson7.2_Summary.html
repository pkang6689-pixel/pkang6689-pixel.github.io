AP Calculus AB - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Separation of Variables is the primary method for solving first-order DEs of the form dy/dx = g(x)h(y). The technique: (1) separate variables: dy/h(y) = g(x)dx, (2) integrate both sides: ∫dy/h(y) = ∫g(x)dx, (3) solve for y if possible, (4) apply initial condition. This method works only when the right side factors into a product of a function of x alone and a function of y alone. Many important physical models yield separable DEs.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Separable DE: A DE of the form dy/dx = g(x)·h(y); the x and y terms can be separated to opposite sides
  • Separation process: Rewrite as dy/h(y) = g(x)dx, then integrate both sides independently
  • Implicit vs. explicit solution: Sometimes the result ∫dy/h(y) = ∫g(x)dx remains implicit (y not isolated); both forms are valid solutions
  • Domain restrictions: Division by h(y) requires h(y) ≠ 0; check if constant solutions y = c where h(c) = 0 are also solutions
  • Initial condition application: After integrating both sides (with one constant C), substitute (x₀, y₀) to find C

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Radioactive decay: dN/dt = -λN separates to dN/N = -λdt, giving N = N₀e^(-λt)—the exponential decay model
  • Cooling/heating: Newton's law of cooling dT/dt = -k(T-Tₛ) is separable, giving T(t) = Tₛ + (T₀-Tₛ)e^(-kt)
  • Chemical kinetics: first-order reaction rate d[A]/dt = -k[A] separates to give [A] = [A]₀e^(-kt), modeling reactant concentration over time
