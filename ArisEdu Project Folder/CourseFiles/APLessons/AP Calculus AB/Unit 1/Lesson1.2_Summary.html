AP Calculus AB - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Introduction to Derivatives defines the derivative as the limit of the difference quotient: f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h. Geometrically, the derivative gives the slope of the tangent line to f(x) at a point. The derivative function f'(x) represents the instantaneous rate of change of f at every point in its domain. A function is differentiable at a point only if it is continuous there (but continuity alone does not guarantee differentiability—corners, cusps, and vertical tangents are non-differentiable).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Derivative definition: f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h; the instantaneous rate of change of f with respect to x
  • Difference quotient: [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h; the average rate of change over an interval of width h; becomes the derivative as h→0
  • Tangent line: The line that touches the curve at exactly one point locally and has slope equal to the derivative: y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a)
  • Differentiability: A function is differentiable at a point if the derivative exists there; requires continuity and a smooth curve (no corners, cusps, or vertical tangents)
  • Notation: f'(x), dy/dx, d/dx[f(x)], Df(x), ẏ are all common notations for the derivative

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Velocity as the derivative of position: v(t) = s'(t) tells you the instantaneous speed and direction of a moving object at any moment
  • Marginal cost in economics: the derivative of the cost function C'(x) gives the cost of producing one additional unit, guiding optimal production decisions
  • Medical dosing: the rate of drug absorption into the bloodstream is modeled as the derivative of drug concentration with respect to time
