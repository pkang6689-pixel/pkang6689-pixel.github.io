AP Calculus AB - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.6 - Summary

Key Concepts

Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes describe function behavior near points where values grow without bound. An infinite limit lim(x→c) f(x) = ±∞ means f(x) increases or decreases without bound as x approaches c. Vertical asymptotes occur at x = c where at least one one-sided limit is ±∞. For rational functions, vertical asymptotes occur where the denominator is zero but the numerator is not (after simplification). The sign of the infinite limit depends on the signs of the factors near c.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Infinite limit: lim(x→c) f(x) = ∞ means f(x) grows without bound as x→c; the limit does not exist as a real number but describes the behavior
  • Vertical asymptote: A vertical line x = c where f(x) → ±∞ as x → c from at least one side
  • Finding vertical asymptotes: For f(x) = p(x)/q(x) in simplified form, vertical asymptotes occur where q(x) = 0 and p(x) ≠ 0
  • Sign analysis: Examining the signs of factors near x = c to determine whether f(x) → +∞ or -∞ from each side
  • Infinite limit vs. limit at infinity: Infinite limit: behavior as x→c (vertical asymptote); limit at infinity: behavior as x→±∞ (horizontal asymptote)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Coulomb's law: electric force F = kq₁q₂/r² → ∞ as distance r → 0, creating a vertical asymptote that reflects the impossibility of zero separation between charges
  • Resonance in physics: when driving frequency approaches natural frequency, amplitude of oscillation approaches infinity (vertical asymptote in the response function)
  • Economics: marginal utility approaching a vertical asymptote at zero consumption, reflecting the extremely high value of the first unit of a scarce resource
