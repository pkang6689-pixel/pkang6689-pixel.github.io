AP Calculus AB - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Instantaneous Rate of Change is the central concept connecting derivatives to real-world measurement. While the average rate of change Δf/Δx gives the slope of a secant line, the instantaneous rate f'(a) = lim(Δx→0) Δf/Δx gives the slope of the tangent line—the rate at a specific instant. This distinction is crucial: a car's speedometer shows instantaneous velocity, not average velocity. Derivatives provide exact instantaneous rates for any differentiable function.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Instantaneous vs. average rate: Average: Δf/Δx over [a,b]; instantaneous: f'(a) = lim(Δx→0) Δf/Δx at a single point
  • Tangent line slope: f'(a) equals the slope of the tangent to y = f(x) at x = a; the best linear approximation at that point
  • Units of derivative: The derivative f'(x) has units of [f-units]/[x-units]; velocity = m/s, marginal cost = $/unit
  • Interpreting sign: f'(a) > 0: f is increasing at x = a; f'(a) < 0: f is decreasing; f'(a) = 0: f has a horizontal tangent
  • Interpreting magnitude: |f'(a)| measures how rapidly f is changing: large |f'(a)| = steep curve, small |f'(a)| = nearly flat

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Speedometers measure instantaneous velocity v(t) = s'(t), not average velocity—critical for traffic law enforcement and vehicle safety systems
  • Heart rate monitors: the instantaneous rate of heartbeats per minute (derivative of cumulative beat count) triggers medical alerts when it exceeds thresholds
  • Stock trading algorithms measuring the instantaneous rate of price change to trigger buy/sell orders in millisecond timeframes
