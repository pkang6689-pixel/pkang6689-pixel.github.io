AP Calculus AB - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Mean Value Theorem (MVT) states that if f is continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b), then there exists at least one c in (a,b) where f'(c) = [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a). Geometrically, the tangent line at c is parallel to the secant line from (a,f(a)) to (b,f(b)). MVT guarantees that a function must achieve its average rate of change as an instantaneous rate at some interior point. It is the theoretical foundation for many calculus results.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Mean Value Theorem: If f is continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b), then ∃c ∈ (a,b) such that f'(c) = [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a)
  • Geometric interpretation: There exists a point where the tangent line is parallel to the secant line connecting the endpoints
  • Hypotheses required: Continuity on the closed interval AND differentiability on the open interval; both conditions must hold
  • Average rate equals instantaneous rate: MVT guarantees the instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change at some interior point
  • Consequence for constant functions: If f'(x) = 0 for all x in (a,b), then f is constant on [a,b]; proven using MVT

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Speed trap reasoning: if a car travels 120 miles in 1 hour, the MVT guarantees the car was going exactly 120 mph at some point (average speed = instantaneous speed)
  • Proving uniqueness of solutions: MVT implies that if two differentiable functions have equal derivatives everywhere, they differ by a constant
  • Financial analysis: if an investment grows from $1000 to $1200 over a year, MVT guarantees the instantaneous growth rate was exactly 20% at some moment
