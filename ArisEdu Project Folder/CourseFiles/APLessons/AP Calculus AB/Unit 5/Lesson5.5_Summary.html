AP Calculus AB - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Concavity and Inflection Points uses the second derivative to analyze the curvature of a function's graph. When f''(x) > 0, the graph is concave up (opening upward, like a cup); when f''(x) < 0, it is concave down (opening downward). Inflection points are locations where concavity changes—found where f''(x) = 0 or f''(x) is undefined, provided the sign of f'' actually changes. Inflection points represent where the rate of change itself changes from increasing to decreasing or vice versa.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Concave up: f''(x) > 0; the graph bends upward; tangent lines lie below the graph; f' is increasing
  • Concave down: f''(x) < 0; the graph bends downward; tangent lines lie above the graph; f' is decreasing
  • Inflection point: A point where the concavity of f changes; requires f''(x) = 0 or undefined AND a sign change in f''
  • Sign chart of f'': Shows intervals of concavity (up/down) separated by potential inflection points where f'' = 0 or undefined
  • Relation to f': Inflection points of f correspond to local extrema of f'; where f' has a max/min, f has an inflection point

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Epidemic inflection point: when the infection curve changes from concave up (accelerating spread) to concave down (decelerating spread), the outbreak is peaking
  • Economic growth: an inflection point in GDP indicates the economy is switching from accelerating to decelerating growth (or vice versa)
  • Understanding S-curves in product adoption: inflection point marks the transition from rapid early adoption growth to market saturation deceleration
