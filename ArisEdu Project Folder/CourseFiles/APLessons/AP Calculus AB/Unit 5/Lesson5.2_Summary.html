AP Calculus AB - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Extreme Values and Critical Points identifies where functions achieve their highest and lowest values. The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that a continuous function on [a,b] has both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum. Critical points (where f'(x) = 0 or f'(x) is undefined) are candidates for local extrema. To find absolute extrema on [a,b]: evaluate f at all critical points in (a,b) and at both endpoints; the largest value is the absolute max, the smallest is the absolute min.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Extreme Value Theorem: A continuous function on a closed interval [a,b] must attain both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum
  • Critical point: An interior point c where f'(c) = 0 (horizontal tangent) or f'(c) does not exist (corner, cusp, or vertical tangent)
  • Absolute (global) extrema: The largest and smallest values of f on the entire domain; on [a,b], compare f at critical points and endpoints
  • Local (relative) extrema: f(c) is a local max if f(c) ≥ f(x) for all x near c; local min if f(c) ≤ f(x) for all x near c
  • Closed interval method: To find absolute extrema on [a,b]: 1) find critical points, 2) evaluate f at critical points and endpoints, 3) compare values

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Mountain hiking: the highest point on a trail (absolute max) and the lowest point (absolute min) are found by checking peaks, valleys, and the trail endpoints
  • Manufacturing optimization: finding the production level that yields the absolute maximum profit on a feasible interval [0, capacity]
  • Structural engineering: identifying the point of maximum stress on a beam (critical point of the stress function) to prevent failure
