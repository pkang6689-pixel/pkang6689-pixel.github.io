AP Environmental Science - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Population Regulation describes self-limiting mechanisms that maintain populations below carrying capacity through negative feedback, preventing unlimited growth and ensuring ecosystem stability. Density-dependent regulation occurs through increased mortality, decreased reproduction, or emigration as populations increase, creating stable equilibrium populations. Understanding regulation mechanisms is essential for predicting population stability and managing populations facing simultaneous density-dependent and density-independent stressors.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Negative feedback: A regulatory mechanism where population increase triggers factors reducing growth, maintaining population stability
  • Equilibrium population: A population maintaining relatively constant size around carrying capacity through density-dependent regulation
  • Range of fluctuation: The limits within which a regulated population oscillates around carrying capacity, determined by regulatory mechanisms
  • Overcompensation: A response where population decline is excessive relative to environmental change, potentially causing extinction
  • Territorial behavior: Competition for exclusive use of space, a density-dependent mechanism regulating population density through space limitation

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Understanding snowshoe hare-lynx population cycles where predator-prey dynamics create oscillations through predator lagging and prey recovery
  • Managing rangeland stocking rates to prevent overgrazing and allow vegetation recovery, maintaining stable pastoral agriculture productivity
  • Controlling invasive carp populations in North American lakes through manipulation of recruitment drivers and commercial harvesting
