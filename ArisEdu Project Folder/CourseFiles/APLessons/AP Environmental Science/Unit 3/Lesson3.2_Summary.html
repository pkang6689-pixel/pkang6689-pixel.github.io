AP Environmental Science - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Population Limiting Factors are environmental constraints that restrict population growth, either operating through density-dependent mechanisms that intensify as populations increase or density-independent forces affecting all population sizes. Competition, predation, parasitism, and disease are primary density-dependent factors, while weather, natural disasters, and habitat destruction act density-independently. Identifying limiting factors is essential for explaining spatial distribution patterns and predicting population responses to environmental change.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Limiting factor: An environmental condition or resource that restricts population growth and prevents populations from reaching theoretical carrying capacity
  • Resource limitation: Scarcity of required resources (food, water, nesting sites) preventing population increase and causing intraspecific competition
  • Predation: Consumption of one organism by another, operating as a density-dependent factor reducing prey populations
  • Parasitism: A symbiotic relationship where a parasite benefits by reducing host fitness; can be a significant limiting factor
  • Tolerance range: The range of environmental conditions within which organisms can survive and reproduce, defining habitat suitability

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Identifying phosphorus as the limiting nutrient in freshwater lakes and managing agricultural runoff to prevent excessive algal growth
  • Analyzing how wolf predation limits elk population growth in Yellowstone, affecting vegetation recovery and demonstrating top-down control
  • Determining how disease (plague) limits prairie dog colonies and designing conservation strategies accounting for disease as a critical factor
