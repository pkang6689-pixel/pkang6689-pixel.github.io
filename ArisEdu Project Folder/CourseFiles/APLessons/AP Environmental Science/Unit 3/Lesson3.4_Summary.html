AP Environmental Science - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Population Dynamics and Conservation integrates age structure, reproduction, mortality, migration, and genetic factors to predict population trajectories and develop conservation strategies for endangered species. Conservation applications include population viability analysis (PVA) to assess extinction risk, minimum viable population (MVP) determination, and management strategy evaluation using structured decision-making. Effective conservation requires understanding complex population interactions and managing multiple threatening processes simultaneously.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Population viability analysis (PVA): Quantitative assessment of extinction probability incorporating demographic stochasticity and environmental variation
  • Minimum viable population (MVP): The smallest population size that can persist long-term with acceptable extinction probability, typically defined as <5% over 100 years
  • Extinction vortex: A process where small populations experience genetic drift, inbreeding, and declining fitness, accelerating extinction risk
  • Reproductive strategy: Species-specific allocation of energy to reproduction, ranging from high-fecundity/low-investment to low-fecundity/high-investment approaches
  • Source-sink metapopulation: A system where source populations produce surplus individuals dispersing to sink populations unable to maintain themselves

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Using PVA models for Florida panther recovery to determine required population growth rates and habitat connectivity for species recovery
  • Managing California condor recovery through captive breeding while expanding wild population and addressing lead ammunition poisoning
  • Protecting sea turtle populations using structured decision models to balance olive ridley sea turtle harvest needs with population viability
