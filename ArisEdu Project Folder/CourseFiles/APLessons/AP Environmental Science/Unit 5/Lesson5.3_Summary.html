AP Environmental Science - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Fisheries and Marine Resources provide protein for billions of people but face overexploitation, with approximately 90% of fish stocks fully exploited or overfished due to industrial fishing pressure, inadequate management, and climate-driven ecological changes. Overfishing disrupts food webs, reduces genetic diversity, damages seafloor habitats, and leads to fishery collapse through depletion of economically important species. Sustainable fisheries management requires assessing stock status, setting science-based catch limits, and protecting critical habitats.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Overfishing: Harvesting fish faster than populations can reproduce, exceeding sustainable yield and causing population decline
  • Maximum sustainable yield (MSY): The largest harvest possible indefinitely without reducing stock below level supporting maximum reproduction
  • Bycatch: Unintended capture of non-target species in fishing operations, including marine mammals, sea turtles, and juvenile fish
  • Fishing down the food chain: Successively harvesting lower trophic-level fish as higher trophic-level stocks are depleted, indicating ecosystem disruption
  • Marine protected area (MPA): Ocean areas with fishing restrictions to protect biodiversity and allow population recovery

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Using harvest control rules to adjust catch limits in response to fish stock assessments, preventing predictable collapses of groundfish fisheries
  • Protecting coral reef ecosystems through MPAs that prevent fishing-related habitat destruction and allow reef fish recovery
  • Reducing bycatch through selective fishing gear development and monitoring to protect threatened sea turtle and seabird populations
