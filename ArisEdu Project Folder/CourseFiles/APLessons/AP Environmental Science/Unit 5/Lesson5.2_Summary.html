AP Environmental Science - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Deforestation and Habitat Loss represents the largest driver of terrestrial habitat loss through conversion of forest to non-forest land, eliminating biodiversity, triggering erosion and flooding, releasing carbon stores, and displacing indigenous communities. Deforestation occurs through logging, agricultural expansion (especially cattle ranching and soy cultivation), infrastructure development, and human settlement, occurring at rates of approximately 10 million hectares annually. Tropical deforestation is particularly critical given exceptional biodiversity and carbon storage, requiring protection and restoration strategies.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Deforestation: The conversion of forest to non-forest land through clearing, burning, or harvesting without adequate natural regeneration
  • Old-growth forest: Mature forests with large trees, complex structure, and high biodiversity, representing centuries of uninterrupted growth
  • Habitat fragmentation: Division of continuous habitat into isolated patches through deforestation, reducing species connectivity and population viability
  • Reforestation: Deliberate replanting of forest on cleared land, potentially restoring productivity but not full ecosystem complexity
  • Slash-and-burn: Agricultural clearing method where forest is cut and burned to create cultivated fields, a major deforestation driver in tropics

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Protecting Amazon tropical rainforest through enforcement of logging regulations and indigenous land rights to preserve carbon storage and biodiversity
  • Implementing reduced-impact logging that selectively harvests high-value trees while maintaining forest structure and ecosystem function
  • Establishing payment for ecosystem services programs paying forest-dependent communities to maintain standing forests instead of clearing
