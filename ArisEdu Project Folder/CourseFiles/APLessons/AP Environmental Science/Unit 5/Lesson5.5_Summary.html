AP Environmental Science - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Mining and Land Degradation involve extraction of mineral and energy resources essential for modern society while causing severe environmental degradation including water pollution, habitat destruction, acid drainage, and waste accumulation covering vast landscapes. Mineral extraction generates massive waste streams—tailings and overburden—creating long-term contamination risks and habitat loss. Sustainable mining requires environmental impact assessment, restoration planning, and community engagement over extraction and closure phases.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Mining: Extraction of mineral resources from earth, including surface mining (open pit, strip mining) and underground mining
  • Tailings: Finely ground waste rock remaining after mineral extraction, often containing toxic metals and sulfide minerals
  • Acid mine drainage: Oxidation of exposed sulfide minerals producing acidic runoff that leaches metals, contaminating streams and groundwater
  • Spoil: Overburden removed to expose mineral seams, creating large waste piles that alter topography and trigger erosion
  • Reclamation: Post-mining restoration attempting to return mined land to productive use through regrading, vegetation establishment, and monitoring

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Managing copper mining impacts in Chile's Atacama Desert through water treatment systems addressing acid mine drainage and water depletion
  • Restoring mountaintop removal mining sites in Appalachia through wildlife habitat creation and carbon sequestration on reclaimed land
  • Regulating rare earth element mining in China to reduce water pollution and acid drainage threatening downstream agricultural and urban water supplies
