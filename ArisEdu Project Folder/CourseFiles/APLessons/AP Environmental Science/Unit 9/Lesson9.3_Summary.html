AP Environmental Science - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Urbanization and Sprawl describe the concentration of population in cities (now 55% of humanity) and low-density expansion into surrounding land consuming agricultural land, fragmenting habitats, increasing infrastructure costs, and generating emissions from automobile dependence. Sprawling metropolitan areas have lower density than compact cities, require longer commute distances, and generate greater per capita emissions despite sometimes lower overall carbon intensity. Sustainable urbanization requires compact development, transit-oriented growth, and agricultural protection through greenbelts and development boundaries.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Urban sprawl: Low-density, geographically expansive development pattern characterized by sprawling neighborhoods, shopping centers, and dispersed employment
  • Brownfield: Previously developed property potentially contaminated by hazardous waste, suitable for redevelopment within urban areas
  • Greenfield: Undeveloped land, typically agricultural or natural, developed for urban expansion
  • Transit-oriented development (TOD): High-density residential and mixed-use development within walking distance of transit (bus, rail)
  • Urban heat island: Phenomenon where cities experience higher temperatures than surrounding rural areas from reduced vegetation and heat-absorbing pavement

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Implementing urban growth boundaries (Portland, Oregon) to contain sprawl and preserve agricultural land while increasing core city density
  • Developing bus rapid transit (BRT) systems providing rapid, affordable urban transportation reducing automobile dependence and emissions
  • Rehabilitating brownfield sites for mixed-use development (residential, commercial, recreational) reconnecting urban areas and increasing density
