AP Environmental Science - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Soil Fertility and Erosion describe soil's capacity to provide available nutrients for plant growth and the detachment and transport of soil by water and wind, which threatens global food security by removing productive topsoil accumulated over centuries. Soil fertility depends on nutrient cycling, organic matter content, pH, and microbial communities. Balancing soil fertility maintenance with erosion prevention through conservation practices is critical for long-term agricultural sustainability.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Soil fertility: The capacity of soil to provide adequate available nutrients and favorable chemical environment for plant growth
  • Erosion: The physical removal of soil by water, wind, or ice, separating and transporting particles away from the original soil location
  • Nutrient availability: The proportion of soil nutrients in chemical forms accessible to plant roots through absorption
  • Leaching: The downward movement of dissolved nutrients through soil with percolating water, potentially removing them from root zone
  • Conservation tillage: Farming practices minimizing soil disturbance to reduce erosion, improve water infiltration, and preserve soil structure

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Implementing terracing on hillslopes to reduce water runoff velocity and erosion while increasing water infiltration and crop productivity
  • Using cover crops in rotational systems to prevent erosion during fallow periods while adding nitrogen through legume fixation
  • Monitoring topsoil loss in agricultural regions using aerial surveys to guide implementation of windbreaks, contour plowing, and buffers
