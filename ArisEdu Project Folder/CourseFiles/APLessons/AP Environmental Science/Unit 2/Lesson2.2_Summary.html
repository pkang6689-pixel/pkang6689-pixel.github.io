AP Environmental Science - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Genetic Diversity refers to the variety of genetic information within a species, which determines a population's ability to adapt to environmental change such as disease, climate shifts, and habitat loss. In environmental science, genetic diversity matters because it underpins ecosystem resilience—populations with greater variation are more likely to include individuals that can survive new stressors. Loss of genetic diversity through habitat fragmentation, population bottlenecks, and inbreeding weakens species' long-term survival prospects and reduces the stability of entire ecosystems.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Genetic diversity: The total variety of genes and alleles within a population; higher diversity increases resilience to environmental change
  • Bottleneck effect: Drastic reduction in genetic diversity when a population's size is severely reduced by a catastrophic event (fire, disease, habitat destruction)
  • Minimum viable population (MVP): The smallest population size that can sustain itself long-term without losing genetic diversity to drift or inbreeding
  • Inbreeding depression: Reduced fitness in a population due to breeding among close relatives, increasing susceptibility to disease and environmental stress
  • Founder effect: Loss of genetic variation when a small group colonizes a new habitat, creating a population genetically different from the original

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Managing breeding programs for endangered California condors by carefully tracking genetic diversity and preventing inbreeding depression
  • Genetic testing of wolf populations in northern Rocky Mountains to monitor genetic connectivity and guide wildlife corridor management
  • Preserving crop genetic diversity in seed banks to maintain disease resistance alleles and adaptability for future climate scenarios
