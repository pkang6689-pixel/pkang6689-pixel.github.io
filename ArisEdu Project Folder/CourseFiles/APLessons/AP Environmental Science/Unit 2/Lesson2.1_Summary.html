AP Environmental Science - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Population Ecology studies the dynamics of groups of interbreeding individuals of the same species, examining how populations grow, decline, and interact with their environment and other species. Populations are fundamentally characterized by their size, density, dispersion pattern, and genetic composition, which determine their resilience and susceptibility to extinction. Understanding population ecology is essential for predicting species persistence, managing endangered populations, and controlling pest species.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Population: A group of organisms of the same species living in the same geographic area and capable of interbreeding
  • Population density: The number of individuals per unit area or volume, a key measure of how crowded a population is
  • Carrying capacity (K): The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely given available resources
  • Density-dependent factor: Environmental resistance whose intensity increases with population density (disease, competition, predation)
  • Density-independent factor: Environmental resistance affecting populations regardless of density (weather, natural disasters, habitat destruction)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Monitoring wolf population recovery in Yellowstone using capture-recapture methods to estimate population size and assess reintroduction success
  • Managing white-tailed deer populations in eastern forests through regulated hunting to prevent overbrowsing and maintain forest regeneration
  • Tracking endangered sea turtle populations using tagging studies to determine migration routes and identify critical habitat protection areas
