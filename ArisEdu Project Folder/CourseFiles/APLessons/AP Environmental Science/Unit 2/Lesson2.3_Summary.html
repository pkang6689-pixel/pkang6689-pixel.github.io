AP Environmental Science - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Speciation and Extinction describe how new species arise through reproductive isolation leading to speciation, while extinction represents the permanent loss of a species, fundamentally shaping biodiversity. Speciation occurs through geographic isolation (allopatric speciation), ecological specialization (sympatric speciation), and polyploidy in plants, each requiring reproductive barriers that prevent gene flow between diverging populations. Current extinction rates are 100–1000 times background levels due to human activity, driving the sixth mass extinction.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Speciation: The evolutionary process by which new species arise from ancestral species through reproductive isolation and genetic divergence
  • Allopatric speciation: Species formation when populations are geographically isolated and accumulate different mutations and adaptations
  • Reproductive isolation: Mechanisms preventing mating and gene flow between populations, essential for speciation
  • Background extinction rate: The natural rate at which species go extinct based on fossil record, approximately one species per million per year
  • Mass extinction: The rapid loss of a large proportion of Earth's biodiversity over a short geologic timescale; five major ones have occurred in Earth's history, with a sixth currently underway due to human activity

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Understanding Darwin's finches in the Galápagos Islands as a model for speciation through character displacement and ecological differentiation
  • Managing threatened species using genetic analysis to identify cryptic species and ensure each species receives adequate conservation attention
  • Tracking pandemic disease emergence through studying host-pathogen speciation as viruses jump between species and evolve in new hosts
