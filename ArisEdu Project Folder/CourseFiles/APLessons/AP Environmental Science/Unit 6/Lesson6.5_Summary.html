AP Environmental Science - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Energy Efficiency and Conservation represent the fastest, cheapest climate mitigation strategies, achieving required services with reduced energy input through lower-energy buildings, vehicles, and industrial processes. Energy efficiency improvements are constrained by rebound effects where lower energy costs increase demand, partially offsetting efficiency gains. Combining efficiency and conservation with renewable energy transition is essential for achieving climate targets and energy security.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Energy efficiency: Delivering the same energy services with less energy input, through improved technology or behavior change
  • Rebound effect: Increased energy consumption offsetting efficiency gains as lower energy costs reduce energy price barriers
  • Embodied energy: Energy required in extraction, manufacturing, transportation, and assembly of a material or product
  • Smart grid: Modern electricity system with digital communication, monitoring, and control enabling demand management and renewable integration
  • Passive house: Building design minimizing heating and cooling demands through insulation, air-tightness, and passive solar/ventilation strategies

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Retrofitting existing buildings with insulation, efficient HVAC, and LED lighting to reduce energy consumption by 30–50%
  • Designing vehicle fuel economy standards and incentivizing adoption of efficient vehicles to reduce transportation energy demand
  • Implementing industrial process improvements and waste heat recovery systems to reduce energy intensity in cement, steel, and chemical manufacturing
