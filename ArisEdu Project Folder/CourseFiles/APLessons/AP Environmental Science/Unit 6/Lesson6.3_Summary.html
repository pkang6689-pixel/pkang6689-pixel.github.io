AP Environmental Science - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Nuclear Energy generates electricity through controlled fission of uranium-235 or plutonium-239, providing low-carbon, high-density energy but creating long-lived radioactive waste requiring secure isolation for millennia. Nuclear plants produce 10% of global electricity with high reliability despite public concerns about catastrophic accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima, Three Mile Island). Nuclear energy's role in climate mitigation is disputed—supporters emphasize low carbon emissions, critics emphasize waste hazards and long construction timelines.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Nuclear fission: Splitting of heavy atomic nuclei (uranium-235, plutonium-239) releasing enormous energy, the basis of nuclear power
  • Radioactive decay: Spontaneous transformation of unstable isotopes into more stable forms, emitting radiation (alpha, beta, gamma particles)
  • Half-life: Time required for a radioactive isotope's activity to decrease to half its initial value, ranging from fractions of seconds to billions of years
  • Nuclear waste: Radioactive byproducts of nuclear power generation and weapons production, requiring permanent disposal
  • Reactor meltdown: Loss of core cooling resulting in extreme temperatures, potential fuel melting, and radioactive release

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Evaluating long-term nuclear waste storage in geological repositories like Yucca Mountain to ensure isolation for 10,000+ years
  • Decommissioning aging nuclear plants through radioactive material removal, facility decontamination, and site remediation
  • Assessing accident risk at coastal nuclear plants under climate change sea-level rise and increased storm surge threats
