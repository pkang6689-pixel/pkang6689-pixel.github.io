AP Environmental Science - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Succession describes the predictable change in community composition following disturbance or on newly available substrates, progressing from pioneer species to a relatively stable climax community. Primary succession occurs on bare substrate (lava flows, glacial till) where soil must develop, while secondary succession follows disturbance to established ecosystems with existing soil. Understanding succession is essential for predicting ecosystem recovery, managing disturbed lands, and restoring degraded habitats.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Pioneer species: Early colonizers of disturbed or bare areas, typically fast-growing, stress-tolerant organisms adapted to harsh conditions
  • Climax community: The final, relatively stable community in ecological succession for a given climate and soil type
  • Sere: The complete sequence of successional stages from pioneer community through intermediate stages to climax community in a given area
  • Facilitation: The process where early colonizers modify the environment to make conditions more suitable for later successional species
  • Inhibition: The process where established species prevent colonization by other species through competition or allelopathy

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Restoring mined lands by deliberately introducing pioneer species and managing succession toward productive ecosystems or wildlife habitat
  • Managing forest successional pathways after logging or fire by controlling which species regenerate to achieve desired forest composition
  • Using succession principles in invasive species management by understanding how native species can reestablish and outcompete invasives
