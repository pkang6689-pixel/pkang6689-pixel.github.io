AP Environmental Science - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Nutrient Cycles move essential elements including carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur between the abiotic environment and living organisms in indefinitely recycling biogeochemical pathways. Unlike energy, which flows through ecosystems and dissipates as heat, nutrients recycle locally and globally through atmospheric, terrestrial, and aquatic reservoirs. Disruptions to nutrient cycles through industrial processes, agriculture, and combustion have fundamentally altered planetary biogeochemistry and created significant environmental challenges.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Biogeochemical cycle: The pathway of a chemical element or nutrient through the biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem
  • Nitrogen fixation: The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) to ammonia (NH₃) by certain bacteria, making nitrogen available to organisms
  • Denitrification: The reduction of nitrates to nitrogen gas by soil bacteria, returning nitrogen to the atmosphere
  • Eutrophication: The process of nutrient enrichment leading to excessive algal growth and oxygen depletion in aquatic systems
  • Phosphate: The chemical form (PO₄³⁻) in which phosphorus is available to organisms; has no atmospheric reservoir

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Implementing cover crops and crop rotation to enhance nitrogen fixation and reduce synthetic fertilizer requirements in agricultural systems
  • Treating municipal wastewater to remove excess nitrogen and phosphorus before discharge to prevent eutrophication downstream
  • Installing constructed wetlands and riparian buffers to remove nitrates and phosphates from agricultural runoff before entering water bodies
