AP Environmental Science - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Energy Flow Through Ecosystems involves the one-directional movement of energy from the sun through producers, consumers, and decomposers, with approximately 10% of energy transferred between each trophic level. This unidirectional flow, combined with the second law of thermodynamics, explains why energy pyramids are always larger at the base and why ecosystems require constant solar input to maintain organization. Understanding energy transfer efficiency is crucial for predicting ecosystem productivity, sustainability, and the impact of human harvesting on food webs.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Trophic level: The feeding position of organisms in an ecosystem (producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, etc.)
  • Primary productivity: The rate at which producers capture solar energy and convert it to chemical energy through photosynthesis
  • Biomass: The total dry mass of organic matter in organisms at a particular trophic level
  • Gross primary productivity (GPP): Total energy captured by producers before respiration losses
  • Net primary productivity (NPP): Energy available for consumers after producers use energy for their own respiration

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Calculating sustainable fishing quotas by determining NPP of marine ecosystems and acceptable harvest rates at different trophic levels
  • Designing agricultural systems with crop rotation to maximize energy capture and soil productivity across growing seasons
  • Assessing the carrying capacity of rangelands by measuring primary productivity and determining sustainable livestock stocking rates
