AP Environmental Science - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Soil Contamination results from industrial operations, mining, improper waste disposal, pesticide/fuel spills, and historical pollution creating hazardous exposure pathways through crop uptake, groundwater pollution, inhalation, and direct contact. Contaminated sites ("brownfields") number in millions globally, concentrated around historical industrial areas, creating human health risks and limiting land reuse for residential/commercial development. Remediation technologies range from excavation and landfill disposal to in-situ treatments reducing mobility and toxicity without excavation.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Contamination: Introduction of harmful substances into soil exceeding background levels and regulatory standards
  • Bioavailability: Fraction of soil contamination capable of being absorbed by organisms, depending on contaminant form and soil properties
  • Phytoremediation: Use of plants and their associated microorganisms to remove or stabilize soil contaminants through uptake or degradation
  • Soil remediation: Treatment technologies reducing pollutant concentration or toxicity below regulatory standards
  • Superfund: U.S. program (CERCLA, 1980) designating and remediating hazardous waste sites, establishing liability and cleanup funding mechanisms

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Remediating petroleum-contaminated soils using excavation and land-farming to promote bioremediation through aerobic microbial degradation
  • Using phytostabilization to immobilize heavy metals in soil (reducing bioavailability without plant uptake), or phytoextraction using hyperaccumulator plants that absorb and concentrate metals in harvestable biomass
  • Implementing vapor extraction systems removing volatile contaminants from soil, particularly addressing chlorinated solvents from dry cleaning operations
