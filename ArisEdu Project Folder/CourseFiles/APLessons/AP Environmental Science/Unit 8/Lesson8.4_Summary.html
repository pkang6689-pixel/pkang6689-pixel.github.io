AP Environmental Science - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Solid Waste Management addresses annual solid waste generation exceeding 2 billion tons, with approximately 90% of waste in low-income countries lacking adequate disposal infrastructure, filling landfills, incinerators, and informal dumpsites contaminating groundwater and creating methane emissions. Waste composition varies regionally but overwhelmingly includes paper (35%), plastics (12%), food (8%), and metals (8%), with most waste ending in landfills. Waste hierarchy emphasizes reduction, reuse, and recycling over landfilling and incineration, requiring collection infrastructure and market development for recovered materials.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Landfill: Site designed for solid waste disposal with clay or synthetic liners, daily cover, and leachate collection systems
  • Leachate: Liquid percolating through waste containing dissolved and suspended contaminants leaching to groundwater
  • Incinerator: Facility burning waste at high temperature achieving energy recovery (waste-to-energy) while producing ash and air emissions
  • Material recovery facility (MRF): Sorting and processing facility separating recyclable materials (paper, plastics, metals, glass) from mixed waste
  • Circular economy: Economic model maximizing material reuse and recycling, minimizing waste extraction and disposal

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Establishing extended producer responsibility (EPR) programs making manufacturers responsible for product end-of-life management, incentivizing design for recycling
  • Implementing waste-to-energy facilities converting municipal solid waste to electricity while reducing landfill disposal volumes
  • Reducing single-use plastics through bans and charges, promoting reusable containers and improving plastic collection and recycling
