AP Environmental Science - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Bioaccumulation and Biomagnification describe the accumulation of persistent toxins in organism tissues over time and their increase in concentration across trophic levels, with top predators accumulating to levels 10,000–1,000,000 times water concentration. Persistent organic pollutants (POPs)—including DDT, PCBs, dioxins—resist degradation and bioaccumulate through food web transfer, devastating top predators (eagles, falcons) through eggshell thinning and reproductive failure. Bioaccumulation explains why mercury, heavy metals, and plastic persist in ecosystems despite relatively small direct releases.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Bioaccumulation: Accumulation of toxic substances in organism tissues exceeding concentration in surrounding environment or diet
  • Biomagnification: Increasing concentration of toxins at successive trophic levels due to predation on contaminated prey
  • Persistent organic pollutant (POP): Chemical (DDT, PCBs, dioxins, furans) resistant to degradation, bioaccumulating through food webs and contaminating even remote areas
  • Eggshell thinning: Reproductive failure where eggshells become fragile and break during incubation, caused by DDE (DDT metabolite) disrupting calcium metabolism
  • Stockholm Convention: International treaty (2001) regulating 12 initial POPs (expanding to 32), phasing out production and use

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Measuring mercury levels in fish tissue to establish consumption advisories protecting human health from mercury neurological impacts
  • Tracking PCB concentrations in Great Lakes fish showing post-ban decline despite sediment pollution from historical discharges
  • Establishing predator population recovery following DDT ban in the 1970s, allowing bald eagles and peregrine falcons to rebound
