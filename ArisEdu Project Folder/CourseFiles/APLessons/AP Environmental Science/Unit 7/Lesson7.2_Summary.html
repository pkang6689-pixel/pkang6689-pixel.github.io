AP Environmental Science - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Acid Rain is precipitation with pH <5.6 (below natural pH of 5.6 due to carbonic acid) resulting from sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides emissions forming sulfuric and nitric acids, degrading forests, acidifying lakes, and damaging infrastructure. Acid rain peaked in developed countries during the 1950s–1980s before emission controls, but remains a serious problem downwind of major industrial regions in Asia and eastern Europe. Ecosystem impacts include forest decline, aquatic acidification, and soil nutrient leaching affecting plant nutrition.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Soil acidification: Lowering of soil pH through acid deposition and organic matter decomposition, reducing nutrient availability and plant growth
  • Lake acidification: Decrease in freshwater pH from acid deposition, reducing fish populations as calcium availability declines critical for fish development
  • Nitric oxide (NO): Colorless gas produced from high-temperature combustion, later oxidized to nitrogen dioxide (NO₂)
  • Wet deposition: Transport of pollutants in precipitation, including acid rain and cloud water deposition
  • Dry deposition: Gravitational settling and direct atmospheric absorption of gaseous and particulate pollutants

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Reducing power plant SO₂ emissions through fuel switching to low-sulfur coal, coal desulfurization, or renewable energy transition
  • Liming acidified lakes to neutralize acidity and restore fish populations, demonstrating direct ecosystem restoration
  • Establishing transboundary pollution control agreements (like the Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution) to reduce regional acid rain
