AP Environmental Science - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Ozone Depletion is primarily caused by chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and related chemicals, creating an Antarctic ozone hole allowing excess ultraviolet B (UV-B) radiation to reach the surface, increasing skin cancer and cataracts while damaging phytoplankton and crops. The Montreal Protocol (1987), one of history's most successful environmental treaties, phased out ozone-depleting substances, allowing ozone layer recovery projected by 2070. Ozone depletion is distinct from ground-level ozone pollution but equally important for stratospheric protection.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Ozone layer: Stratospheric ozone concentration (15–35 km altitude) shielding earth from ultraviolet radiation
  • Chlorofluorocarbon (CFC): Synthetic chemical (CFCs, primarily CFC-11 and CFC-12) released from refrigerants and aerosols, destroying ozone molecules
  • Ultraviolet radiation: Electromagnetic radiation (UV-A, UV-B, UV-C) with wavelengths 10–400 nm, with shorter wavelengths having greater biological damage
  • Ozone hole: Seasonal reduction of stratospheric ozone (>50%) over Antarctica during spring, depleting UV protection
  • Halons: Bromine-containing chemicals used in fire suppression systems, 40–50 times more destructive per molecule than CFCs

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Phasing out CFC-based refrigerants in cooling systems, replacing with approved alternatives (hydrofluoroolefins, hydrofluoroolefin alternatives, hydrofluorocarbons)
  • Monitoring stratospheric ozone with satellites and ground stations to track recovery and detect anomalies from continued illegal CFC emissions
  • Limiting UV exposure through tanning ban regulation and sun safety education as ozone layer recovery proceeds
