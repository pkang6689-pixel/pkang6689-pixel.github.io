AP Environmental Science - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Indoor Air Quality impacts human health through exposure to particulates, volatile organic compounds, carbon monoxide, radon, biological pollutants (mold, allergens), and secondhand smoke in poorly ventilated spaces. Indoor pollutant concentrations often exceed outdoor levels due to concentrated sources (tobacco, appliances, building materials) and insufficient ventilation, particularly in energy-efficient buildings. Poor indoor air quality contributes to respiratory disease, asthma, allergies, and sick building syndrome, creating significant health and economic costs.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Volatile organic compound (VOC): Carbon-based chemical (formaldehyde, toluene, benzene) emitted from building materials, furnishings, and products
  • Sick building syndrome: Collection of symptoms (headaches, respiratory irritation, fatigue) affecting building occupants without identifiable specific illness
  • Radon: Naturally occurring radioactive gas from soil and rock, accumulating in basements and creating lung cancer risk
  • Ventilation rate: Outdoor air flow rate expressed as air changes per hour (ACH), insufficient in sealed energy-efficient buildings
  • Biological pollutant: Allergens and pathogens (mold spores, dust mites, pet dander, pollen) affecting indoor air quality and health

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Installing mechanical ventilation with heat recovery in energy-efficient buildings to maintain air quality without energy losses
  • Testing buildings for radon accumulation and installing soil depressurization systems (active soil depressurization) to reduce indoor radon
  • Specifying low-VOC paints, adhesives, and furnishings in building design to minimize indoor chemical exposure and sick building syndrome
