AP Environmental Science - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Global Warming and Greenhouse Gases result from accumulation of greenhouse gases (primarily carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide) trapping infrared radiation and warming the atmosphere at approximately 0.18°C per decade since 1981. Anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions from fossil fuel combustion (75%), agriculture (14%), and land use (11%) have increased atmospheric CO₂ from 315 ppm (1958) to 425 ppm (2024), exceeding pre-industrial levels by 50%. Climate impacts include temperature extremes, precipitation changes, sea-level rise, and ecosystem disruptions affecting food, water, and health security.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Greenhouse gas: Atmospheric gas (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, fluorine gases) absorbing infrared radiation and warming earth's surface
  • Radiative forcing: Change in energy balance at top of atmosphere from human and natural factors, measured in watts per square meter (W/m²)
  • Global warming potential (GWP): Relative warming power of greenhouse gas compared to CO₂ over 100-year time horizon (CH₄: 28–34, N₂O: 265)
  • Climate sensitivity: Temperature increase from doubling atmospheric CO₂, estimated at 2.5–4°C based on paleoclimate and model evidence
  • Tipping point: Threshold in climate system beyond which change becomes irreversible or self-reinforcing (ice sheet collapse, Amazon dieback)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Measuring atmospheric CO₂ at observation stations (Mauna Loa, South Pole) to track accumulation and verify emission reduction policies
  • Calculating corporate carbon footprints across supply chains to identify reduction opportunities and guide sustainability initiatives
  • Projecting future climate scenarios using Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP1-2.6 through SSP5-8.5) from IPCC AR6 to inform national climate policies and adaptation planning
