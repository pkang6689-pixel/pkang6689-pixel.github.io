AP Chemistry - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Enthalpy and Calorimetry measure the heat of reactions. Enthalpy (H) is a state function; ΔH = qp (heat at constant pressure). Calorimetry measures ΔH experimentally: in coffee-cup calorimetry (constant pressure), q = mcΔT; in bomb calorimetry (constant volume), q = CcalΔT. Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔH°f) is the enthalpy change when 1 mole of compound forms from elements in their standard states. ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) - ΣΔH°f(reactants).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Enthalpy (H): A thermodynamic state function equal to internal energy plus PV; ΔH = heat exchanged at constant pressure
  • Calorimetry: The experimental measurement of heat changes using an insulated container; q = mcΔT for solution calorimetry
  • Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔH°f): The enthalpy change for forming 1 mole of compound from elements in standard states (25°C, 1 atm); ΔH°f = 0 for elements
  • State function: A property that depends only on the current state (T, P, composition), not on the path taken; examples: H, S, G, E
  • Bomb calorimeter: A sealed, constant-volume calorimeter for measuring heat of combustion: q = CcalΔT, where Ccal is the calorimeter constant

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Food calorie content measured by bomb calorimetry: burning food samples in pure O₂ and measuring the temperature rise of surrounding water
  • Designing energy-efficient buildings using materials with known specific heat capacities and enthalpies for optimal thermal insulation
  • Quality control in fuel production: measuring heat of combustion per gram to verify fuel grade and energy content
