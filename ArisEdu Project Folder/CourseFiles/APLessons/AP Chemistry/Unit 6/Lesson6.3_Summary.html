AP Chemistry - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is the sum of ΔH values for individual steps, regardless of the pathway. This allows calculation of ΔH for reactions that are difficult to measure directly, by combining known reactions. If a reaction is reversed, ΔH changes sign; if multiplied by a factor n, ΔH is multiplied by n. Hess's Law is a consequence of enthalpy being a state function.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Hess's Law: ΔH for any reaction equals the sum of ΔH values for the steps into which the reaction can be divided, regardless of path
  • Enthalpy diagram: A visual representation showing the relative enthalpies of reactants, products, and intermediates; illustrates exothermic/endothermic steps
  • Standard conditions: 25°C (298 K), 1 atm pressure, 1 M concentration for solutions; designated by the ° symbol
  • Reaction pathway: The sequence of intermediate steps connecting reactants to products; ΔH is path-independent (state function) so any path gives the same total ΔH
  • Thermochemical equation: A balanced equation that includes the enthalpy change (ΔH); coefficients represent moles, and the physical state of each species must be specified

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Calculating the enthalpy of formation of CO (difficult to isolate from CO₂ formation) using Hess's Law with C + O₂ → CO₂ and CO + ½O₂ → CO₂
  • Industrial process design using Hess's Law to predict energy requirements for multi-step chemical syntheses without performing every reaction experimentally
  • Environmental chemistry: calculating the enthalpy of acid rain formation from SO₂ emissions using a series of known thermochemical equations
