AP Chemistry - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Molecular Geometry is predicted by VSEPR theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion): electron groups around a central atom arrange to minimize repulsion. The number of bonding pairs and lone pairs determines geometry: 2 groups = linear (180°), 3 = trigonal planar (120°), 4 = tetrahedral (109.5°), 5 = trigonal bipyramidal, 6 = octahedral. Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs, distorting bond angles. Molecular polarity depends on both bond polarity and geometry.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • VSEPR theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion: electron groups around a central atom repel each other and arrange to maximize angular separation
  • Electron geometry: The arrangement of ALL electron groups (bonding + lone pairs) around the central atom (e.g., tetrahedral for 4 groups)
  • Molecular geometry: The arrangement of only the atoms (bonding pairs) around the central atom (e.g., bent for 2 bonding + 2 lone pairs)
  • Hybridization: The mixing of atomic orbitals to form hybrid orbitals: sp (linear, 180°), sp² (trigonal planar, 120°), sp³ (tetrahedral, 109.5°)
  • Dipole moment: A measure of molecular polarity; vector sum of all bond dipoles; symmetric molecules (CO₂, CCl₄) can have polar bonds but no net dipole

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Drug design depending on precise molecular geometry: the 3D shape of a drug molecule determines how it fits into an enzyme active site (lock-and-key model)
  • Greenhouse gas behavior: CO₂ (linear, no permanent dipole) still absorbs IR radiation through asymmetric stretching, while symmetric stretching is IR-inactive
  • Ammonia's pyramidal geometry (sp³, one lone pair) making it a strong Lewis base and critical industrial chemical in fertilizer production (Haber process)
