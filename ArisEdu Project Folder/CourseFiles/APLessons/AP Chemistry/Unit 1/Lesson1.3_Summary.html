AP Chemistry - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Periodic Table Trends arise from systematic changes in electron configuration across periods and groups. Atomic radius decreases left to right (increasing Zeff) and increases top to bottom (more electron shells). Ionization energy and electronegativity generally increase left to right and bottom to top. These trends explain reactivity patterns: alkali metals are highly reactive (low IE, lose e⁻ easily), while noble gases are inert (full valence shells, high IE).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Effective nuclear charge (Zeff): The net positive charge experienced by valence electrons: Zeff = Z - S (shielding); increases across a period as protons increase but shielding stays roughly constant
  • Ionization energy (IE): The energy required to remove the highest-energy electron from a gaseous atom; increases across a period and up a group
  • Electronegativity: A measure of an atom's ability to attract shared electrons in a covalent bond; Pauling scale—F is the highest at 4.0
  • Electron affinity: The energy change when a gaseous atom gains an electron; halogens have the most negative (exothermic) electron affinities
  • Atomic radius: The distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron shell; decreases across a period (more Zeff), increases down a group (more shells)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Lithium-ion batteries exploiting Li's small atomic radius and low ionization energy for high energy density in lightweight portable electronics
  • Fluorine's extreme electronegativity making it the most reactive nonmetal, used in Teflon (PTFE) coatings and uranium enrichment (UF₆)
  • Noble gas applications: helium in MRI superconducting magnets, argon as inert shielding gas in welding, neon in illuminated signs
