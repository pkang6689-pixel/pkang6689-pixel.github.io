AP Chemistry - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electron Configuration describes how electrons are arranged in atoms following the Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule. Orbitals are filled in order of increasing energy: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p... Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins. Electron configurations explain periodic trends, chemical reactivity, and spectral properties. Noble gas core notation (e.g., [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² for Zn) simplifies writing configurations.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Aufbau principle: Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, from lowest to highest
  • Pauli exclusion principle: No two electrons in the same atom can have identical sets of four quantum numbers; each orbital holds at most 2 electrons with opposite spins
  • Hund's rule: Electrons occupy degenerate (equal-energy) orbitals singly with parallel spins before pairing up
  • Valence electrons: Electrons in the outermost energy level (highest principal quantum number n); determine chemical bonding and reactivity
  • Orbital: A region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron; types: s (spherical), p (dumbbell), d (cloverleaf), f (complex)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Flame tests and fireworks colors: excited electrons emit characteristic wavelengths when returning to ground state (Na = yellow, Cu = green/blue, Sr = red)
  • Semiconductor doping in electronics: adding atoms with different electron configurations (P for n-type, B for p-type) to silicon to control conductivity
  • Transition metal colors in compounds arising from d-orbital splitting: Cu²⁺ = blue, Fe³⁺ = yellow, Cr³⁺ = green
