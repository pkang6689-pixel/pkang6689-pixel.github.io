AP Chemistry - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Chemical Bonding connects atoms through ionic, covalent, and metallic bonds. Ionic bonds form when electrons transfer from metals to nonmetals (large electronegativity difference > 1.7), creating ions held by electrostatic attraction. Covalent bonds share electron pairs between nonmetals, with bond polarity determined by electronegativity difference. Lewis structures show bonding and lone pairs, with formal charge minimization guiding the best structure. Bond order, length, and energy are inversely/directly related.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Ionic bond: Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by electron transfer; typically between metals and nonmetals (ΔEN > 1.7)
  • Covalent bond: A bond formed by sharing electron pairs between atoms; can be single (2e⁻), double (4e⁻), or triple (6e⁻)
  • Lewis structure: A diagram showing bonding pairs and lone pairs of electrons; follows the octet rule (8e⁻ for most atoms, 2e⁻ for H)
  • Formal charge: FC = valence e⁻ - (lone pair e⁻ + ½ bonding e⁻); the best Lewis structure minimizes formal charges
  • Bond polarity: Unequal sharing of electrons in a covalent bond due to electronegativity difference; creates a dipole moment (δ⁺ and δ⁻)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Table salt (NaCl) as a classic ionic compound: Na donates one electron to Cl, forming Na⁺Cl⁻ with a crystal lattice structure and high melting point (801°C)
  • Diamond vs. graphite: same element (carbon) with different covalent bonding networks—diamond (4 bonds, 3D tetrahedral) is the hardest material, graphite (3 bonds, layered sheets) is a lubricant
  • Polar covalent bonds in water enabling hydrogen bonding, which gives water its uniquely high boiling point (100°C vs. H₂S at -60°C)
