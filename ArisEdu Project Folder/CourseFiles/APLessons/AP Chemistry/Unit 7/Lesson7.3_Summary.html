AP Chemistry - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Le Chatelier's Principle predicts how an equilibrium system responds to disturbances: the system shifts to partially counteract the change. Adding reactant or removing product shifts equilibrium forward (toward products); adding product or removing reactant shifts it in reverse. Increasing pressure shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas. Temperature changes alter K: increasing T shifts endothermic reactions forward (K increases) and exothermic reactions in reverse (K decreases). Catalysts do NOT shift equilibrium.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Le Chatelier's Principle: When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts in the direction that partially counteracts the disturbance
  • Concentration change: Adding a species shifts equilibrium away from it; removing a species shifts equilibrium toward it
  • Pressure/volume change: Increasing pressure (decreasing volume) shifts equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas; inert gas at constant V has no effect
  • Temperature change: The ONLY disturbance that changes K; increasing T favors the endothermic direction; decreasing T favors the exothermic direction
  • Catalyst effect: A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally, reaching equilibrium faster but NOT changing its position or K

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Haber process optimization: high pressure (fewer gas moles on product side, Δn = -2) and low temperature (exothermic) favor NH₃, but moderate T used for acceptable rate
  • Breathing regulation: increased CO₂ in blood shifts CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ rightward, lowering pH and signaling the brain to increase breathing rate
  • Industrial removal of products: in esterification (alcohol + acid ⇌ ester + water), removing water with molecular sieves shifts equilibrium toward ester, increasing yield
