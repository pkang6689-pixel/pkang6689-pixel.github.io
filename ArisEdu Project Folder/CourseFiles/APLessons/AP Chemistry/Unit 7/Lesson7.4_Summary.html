AP Chemistry - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Solubility Equilibria describe the dissolution of sparingly soluble ionic compounds: MₓAᵧ(s) ⇌ xM^(n+)(aq) + yA^(m-)(aq). The solubility product constant Ksp = [M^(n+)]ˣ[A^(m-)]ʸ. Comparing Qsp to Ksp predicts precipitation: if Qsp > Ksp, a precipitate forms. The common-ion effect reduces solubility when a solution already contains one of the ions. Selective precipitation exploits different Ksp values to separate ions from solution.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Solubility product (Ksp): The equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble ionic compound; Ksp = product of ion concentrations raised to stoichiometric powers
  • Molar solubility: The number of moles of compound that dissolve per liter of saturated solution; calculated from Ksp and stoichiometry
  • Common-ion effect: The decrease in solubility of an ionic compound when a solution already contains one of its ions (Le Chatelier's principle applied to dissolution)
  • Selective precipitation: Using differences in Ksp values to selectively precipitate specific ions from a solution containing multiple ions
  • Qsp vs. Ksp: If Qsp < Ksp: unsaturated, more dissolves; Qsp = Ksp: saturated at equilibrium; Qsp > Ksp: supersaturated, precipitation occurs

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Kidney stone formation: when [Ca²⁺][C₂O₄²⁻] exceeds Ksp of calcium oxalate (2.3 × 10⁻⁹), crystals precipitate in the kidney
  • Water treatment using selective precipitation: adding Na₂S to remove toxic heavy metals (Pb²⁺, Hg²⁺, Cu²⁺) as insoluble sulfide precipitates (very low Ksp values)
  • Hard water treatment: Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions forming insoluble CaCO₃ scale in pipes and water heaters when Qsp exceeds Ksp upon heating
