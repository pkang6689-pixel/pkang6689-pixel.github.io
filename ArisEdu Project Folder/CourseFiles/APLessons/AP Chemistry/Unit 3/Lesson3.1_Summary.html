AP Chemistry - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

States of Matter (solid, liquid, gas) are determined by the balance between kinetic energy of particles and the strength of intermolecular forces. Gases have high KE and weak IMFs (particles far apart, random motion); liquids have moderate KE and significant IMFs; solids have low KE and strong IMFs (fixed positions). Phase changes (melting, boiling, sublimation, condensation, freezing, deposition) occur at specific temperatures where energy input/removal overcomes or establishes IMFs.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Kinetic molecular theory: Gas particles are in constant random motion, collisions are elastic, average KE is proportional to absolute temperature (KE = ³⁄₂kT)
  • Phase diagram: A graph showing the stable phase of a substance as a function of temperature and pressure; includes triple point and critical point
  • Triple point: The temperature and pressure at which all three phases coexist in equilibrium (water: 0.01°C, 611.7 Pa)
  • Critical point: The temperature and pressure above which the liquid and gas phases are indistinguishable (supercritical fluid)
  • Heat of vaporization: The energy required to convert 1 mole of liquid to gas at the boiling point; reflects the strength of IMFs being overcome

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Freeze-drying (lyophilization) of food and pharmaceuticals using sublimation under reduced pressure to remove water while preserving structure and nutrients
  • Pressure cookers raising the boiling point of water by increasing pressure, cooking food faster at higher temperatures
  • Supercritical CO₂ used as a green solvent in decaffeination of coffee and dry cleaning, replacing toxic organic solvents
