AP Chemistry - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Solids and Liquids exhibit distinct properties based on their intermolecular forces and particle arrangements. Crystalline solids have ordered lattice structures (ionic, molecular, covalent network, metallic), while amorphous solids (glass) lack long-range order. Liquids display surface tension (minimizing surface area due to unbalanced IMFs), viscosity (resistance to flow), and capillary action. Properties like melting point, hardness, and conductivity correlate with the type and strength of bonding.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Crystalline solid: A solid with a regular, repeating 3D lattice structure; types include ionic (NaCl), molecular (ice), covalent network (diamond), and metallic (Cu)
  • Amorphous solid: A solid lacking long-range ordered structure; molecules are randomly arranged (e.g., glass, rubber, many plastics)
  • Surface tension: The energy per unit area at a liquid's surface; caused by net inward pull on surface molecules from unbalanced IMFs
  • Viscosity: A liquid's resistance to flow; increases with stronger IMFs, larger molecules, and lower temperature
  • Unit cell: The smallest repeating unit of a crystal lattice; common types include simple cubic, body-centered cubic (BCC), and face-centered cubic (FCC)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Diamond (covalent network solid) as the hardest natural material: each carbon forms 4 strong covalent bonds in a 3D tetrahedral network, with mp > 3500°C
  • Liquid crystal displays (LCDs) exploiting the properties of materials that flow like liquids but maintain ordered molecular arrangements like crystals
  • Tempered safety glass as an amorphous solid that, when broken, shatters into small granular pieces rather than sharp shards due to balanced internal stresses from thermal treatment (distinct from laminated glass, which holds together via a plastic interlayer)
