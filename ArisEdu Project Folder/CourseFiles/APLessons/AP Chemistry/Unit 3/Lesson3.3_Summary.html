AP Chemistry - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Solutions and Concentration describes how solutes dissolve in solvents through solvation. 'Like dissolves like': polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes, nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes. Concentration expressions include molarity (M = mol/L), molality (m = mol/kg solvent), mole fraction, and mass percent. Colligative properties (boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, osmotic pressure, vapor pressure lowering) depend on solute particle concentration, not identity.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Molarity (M): Concentration in moles of solute per liter of solution (mol/L); the most common concentration unit in chemistry
  • Colligative properties: Properties that depend on the number of dissolved particles, not their chemical identity: ΔTb, ΔTf, π, ΔP
  • Boiling point elevation: ΔTb = iKbm; solute particles raise the boiling point by lowering the vapor pressure; i = van't Hoff factor
  • Freezing point depression: ΔTf = iKfm; solute particles lower the freezing point by disrupting crystal formation
  • Osmotic pressure: π = iMRT; the pressure needed to prevent osmosis across a semipermeable membrane; used to determine molar mass of large molecules

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Road salt (NaCl) lowering the freezing point of water to prevent ice formation on roads—effective down to about -9°C (ΔTf = iKfm, i = 2 for NaCl)
  • Antifreeze (ethylene glycol) in car radiators both lowering freezing point and raising boiling point of the coolant solution
  • Kidney dialysis using osmotic pressure principles to remove waste from blood across a semipermeable membrane
