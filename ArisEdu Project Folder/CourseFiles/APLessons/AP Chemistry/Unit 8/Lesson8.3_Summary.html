AP Chemistry - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Titration is a quantitative analytical technique where a solution of known concentration (titrant) is added to a solution of unknown concentration (analyte) until the equivalence point is reached. Strong acid–strong base titrations have equivalence point at pH 7. Weak acid–strong base titrations have equivalence points at pH > 7 (due to conjugate base hydrolysis). The half-equivalence point of a weak acid titration is where pH = pKa, useful for buffer preparation.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Equivalence point: The point where moles of titrant exactly equal moles of analyte; stoichiometrically complete reaction
  • End point: The point where the indicator changes color; ideally matches the equivalence point; slightly different in practice
  • Titration curve: A plot of pH vs. volume of titrant added; shape reveals acid/base strength and equivalence point pH
  • Half-equivalence point: The point where half the analyte has reacted: [HA] = [A⁻], so pH = pKa; useful for determining Ka
  • Indicator: A weak acid or base that changes color at a specific pH range; chosen so its color change range includes the equivalence point pH (e.g., phenolphthalein, pH 8.2–10, ideal for weak acid–strong base titrations; bromothymol blue, pH 6.0–7.6, for strong acid–strong base)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Quality control in pharmaceutical manufacturing: acid-base titrations determining the exact concentration of active ingredients in drug formulations
  • Environmental monitoring: titrating water samples to determine alkalinity (buffering capacity against acid rain) and acidity of industrial effluent
  • Vinegar concentration determination: titrating with standardized NaOH; the acetic acid content must be 4-8% by mass for commercial vinegar
