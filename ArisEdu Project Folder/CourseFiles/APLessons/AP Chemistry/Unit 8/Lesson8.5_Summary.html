AP Chemistry - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Polyprotic Acids can donate more than one proton per molecule, with successive Ka values decreasing (Ka1 >> Ka2 >> Ka3). Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) has a strong first dissociation and weak second (Ka2 = 0.012). Phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) has three dissociations. For most polyprotic acid calculations, the first dissociation dominates the pH. Amino acids are polyprotic with both acidic (COOH) and basic (NH₂) groups, existing as zwitterions at physiological pH.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Polyprotic acid: An acid that can donate more than one proton: diprotic (H₂SO₄, H₂CO₃) or triprotic (H₃PO₄)
  • Successive Ka values: Ka1 >> Ka2 >> Ka3; each successive proton is harder to remove because it leaves a more negatively charged anion
  • Diprotic acid: An acid with two ionizable protons (e.g., H₂SO₄, H₂CO₃, oxalic acid H₂C₂O₄)
  • Zwitterion: A molecule with both positive and negative charges; amino acids exist as zwitterions at their isoelectric point (pH = ½(pKa1 + pKa2))
  • Isoelectric point (pI): The pH at which a molecule has zero net charge; for amino acids, pI = ½(pKa1 + pKa2)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Carbonated beverages: dissolved CO₂ forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), a diprotic acid giving soft drinks their tangy taste (pH ~3-4)
  • Amino acid behavior in gel electrophoresis: proteins migrate based on charge at a given pH relative to their isoelectric points
  • Phosphoric acid in food industry (E338): used in cola drinks (pH ~2.5) and as a rust remover due to its triprotic acid chemistry
