AP Chemistry - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electrochemistry and Redox connects chemical reactions to electrical energy. Redox reactions involve electron transfer: oxidation (loss of e⁻, OIL) at the anode and reduction (gain of e⁻, RIG) at the cathode. Standard reduction potentials (E°) from the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE = 0.00 V) predict reaction spontaneity: E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode; positive E°cell → spontaneous. The Nernst equation adjusts E for non-standard conditions: E = E° - (RT/nF)ln Q.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons; occurs at the anode; oxidation state increases
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons; occurs at the cathode; oxidation state decreases
  • Standard reduction potential (E°): The voltage of a half-reaction compared to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE); more positive E° = stronger oxidizing agent
  • E°cell: E°cathode - E°anode; positive E°cell = spontaneous galvanic cell; negative = nonspontaneous (requires electrolysis)
  • Nernst equation: E = E° - (RT/nF)ln Q; adjusts cell potential for non-standard concentrations; at equilibrium E = 0 and Q = K

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Breathalyzer devices using electrochemical oxidation of ethanol to acetic acid, generating a current proportional to blood alcohol concentration
  • Corrosion prevention through galvanic protection: attaching zinc (sacrificial anode, more negative E°) to iron structures so Zn oxidizes instead of Fe
  • pH meters measuring voltage differences between a glass electrode and reference electrode, converted to pH via the Nernst equation
