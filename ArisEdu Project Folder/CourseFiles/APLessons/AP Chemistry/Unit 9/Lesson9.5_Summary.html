AP Chemistry - Unit 9 - Lesson 9.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Batteries and Corrosion applies electrochemistry to practical applications. Primary batteries (alkaline: Zn/MnO₂, 1.5V) are single-use. Secondary batteries (lead-acid: Pb/PbO₂, 2V; Li-ion: LiCoO₂/C, 3.7V) are rechargeable by reversing the cell reaction with applied voltage. Fuel cells (H₂/O₂ → H₂O) convert chemical energy directly to electrical energy with high efficiency. Corrosion is an unwanted galvanic process where metals are oxidized; prevention methods include galvanizing, cathodic protection, and coating.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Primary battery: A non-rechargeable battery; the cell reaction is irreversible or impractical to reverse (e.g., alkaline, zinc-carbon)
  • Secondary battery: A rechargeable battery; the cell reaction is reversible by applying an external voltage (e.g., lead-acid, Li-ion, NiMH)
  • Fuel cell: An electrochemical cell that converts fuel (typically H₂) and oxygen directly to electricity and water; high efficiency, no combustion
  • Corrosion: The electrochemical oxidation of metals by environmental exposure (water, O₂, salt); iron rusting: Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻
  • Cathodic protection: Preventing corrosion by making the metal structure the cathode; achieved with a sacrificial anode (Zn, Mg) or impressed current

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Electric vehicle batteries: Tesla uses lithium-ion cells (LiNiCoAlO₂ cathode) delivering ~250 Wh/kg, enabling 300+ mile ranges
  • Hydrogen fuel cell vehicles (Toyota Mirai): H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O generating electricity with only water as byproduct; zero emissions at point of use
  • Ship hull protection: zinc sacrificial anodes bolted to steel hulls corrode preferentially, protecting the more expensive steel from saltwater corrosion
