AP Chemistry - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Acids and Bases are defined by three models: Arrhenius (H⁺/OH⁻ producers in water), Brønsted-Lowry (H⁺ donors/acceptors), and Lewis (electron pair acceptors/donors). Strong acids (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) and strong bases (NaOH, KOH) dissociate completely; weak acids/bases partially dissociate with Ka or Kb values. Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one H⁺. The relative strength of an acid is inversely related to the strength of its conjugate base.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Brønsted-Lowry acid: A proton (H⁺) donor; the dominant definition in aqueous chemistry
  • Brønsted-Lowry base: A proton (H⁺) acceptor
  • Conjugate acid-base pair: Two species that differ by one proton: HA/A⁻ or B/BH⁺; the conjugate base of a strong acid is very weak
  • Ka (acid dissociation constant): Equilibrium constant for weak acid dissociation: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻; larger Ka = stronger acid
  • Lewis acid/base: Lewis acid: electron pair acceptor (often metal ions like Fe³⁺, Al³⁺); Lewis base: electron pair donor (has lone pairs, like NH₃, OH⁻)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Stomach acid (HCl, pH ~1.5-3.5) breaking down food proteins; antacids (CaCO₃, Mg(OH)₂) neutralizing excess acid for heartburn relief
  • Acid rain (pH < 5.6) formed when SO₂ and NOₓ from fossil fuel combustion react with water to form H₂SO₄ and HNO₃, damaging ecosystems and infrastructure
  • Industrial production of sulfuric acid (Contact Process: 2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃ → H₂SO₄), the most widely produced chemical globally (~200 million tons/year)
