AP Chemistry - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Stoichiometry is the quantitative study of reactants and products in chemical reactions based on the mole concept. One mole (6.022 × 10²³ particles, Avogadro's number) provides the bridge between atomic-scale masses and laboratory-scale quantities. Molar mass (g/mol) equals the atomic or molecular mass in amu. Balanced equations provide mole ratios for calculating how much product forms from given reactants, or how much reactant is needed.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Mole: The SI unit for amount of substance; 1 mol = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)
  • Molar mass: The mass of one mole of a substance in grams per mole (g/mol); numerically equal to the atomic/molecular mass in amu
  • Avogadro's number: 6.022 × 10²³; the number of particles (atoms, molecules, ions) in one mole of substance
  • Mole ratio: The ratio of moles of any two species in a balanced chemical equation; used as a conversion factor in stoichiometric calculations
  • Empirical vs. molecular formula: Empirical formula: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (e.g., CH₂O); molecular formula: actual number of atoms (e.g., C₆H₁₂O₆)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Pharmaceutical dosing calculations using molar mass conversions to determine the exact amount of active ingredient per tablet or solution
  • Industrial chemical production (ammonia synthesis: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃) using stoichiometry to optimize raw material usage and minimize waste
  • Forensic chemistry using stoichiometric analysis to determine the composition of unknown substances from mass spectral data
