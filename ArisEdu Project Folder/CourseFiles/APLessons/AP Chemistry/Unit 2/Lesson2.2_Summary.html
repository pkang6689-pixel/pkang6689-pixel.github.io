AP Chemistry - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Chemical Equilibrium occurs when a reversible reaction's forward and reverse rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations of reactants and products. Equilibrium is dynamic (reactions continue but net change is zero). The equilibrium constant expression K relates product and reactant concentrations: for aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, K = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ/[A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ. K > 1 favors products; K < 1 favors reactants. Q (reaction quotient) compared to K predicts the direction a reaction will shift.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Equilibrium constant (K): The ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to their stoichiometric coefficients; temperature-dependent
  • Reaction quotient (Q): Calculated the same way as K but with current concentrations; Q < K: forward reaction favored; Q > K: reverse favored; Q = K: at equilibrium
  • Dynamic equilibrium: A state where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; concentrations remain constant but reactions continue at the molecular level
  • Kp vs. Kc: Kp uses partial pressures for gas-phase equilibria; Kc uses molar concentrations; related by Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn
  • ICE table: A method for calculating equilibrium concentrations: Initial concentration, Change in concentration, Equilibrium concentration

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Haber process optimization (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃) using high pressure and moderate temperature to shift equilibrium toward ammonia production for fertilizers
  • Blood oxygen transport: hemoglobin-oxygen equilibrium (Hb + O₂ ⇌ HbO₂) shifts based on O₂ partial pressure in lungs vs. tissues (Bohr effect)
  • Carbon dioxide equilibrium in oceans (CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺) affected by rising atmospheric CO₂, causing ocean acidification
