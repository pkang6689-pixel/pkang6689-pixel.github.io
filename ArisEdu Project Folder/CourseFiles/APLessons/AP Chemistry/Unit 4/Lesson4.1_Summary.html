AP Chemistry - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Types of Chemical Reactions are classified into several categories: synthesis (A + B → AB), decomposition (AB → A + B), single replacement (A + BC → AC + B), double replacement (AB + CD → AD + CB), and combustion (fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O). Oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions involve electron transfer; oxidation is loss of electrons (OIL), reduction is gain (RIG). The activity series predicts whether single replacement reactions will occur.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Synthesis reaction: Two or more reactants combine to form one product: A + B → AB (e.g., 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl)
  • Decomposition reaction: One compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances: AB → A + B (e.g., 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂)
  • Oxidation-reduction (redox): Reactions involving electron transfer; oxidation = loss of electrons (OIL), reduction = gain of electrons (RIG)
  • Oxidation state: The apparent charge on an atom in a compound, determined by electronegativity rules; tracks electron transfer in redox reactions
  • Activity series: A ranking of metals by reactivity (tendency to lose electrons); a more active metal displaces a less active metal from solution

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Combustion of hydrocarbons in engines: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O releasing energy to power vehicles; incomplete combustion produces toxic CO
  • Corrosion of iron (rusting): 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ → 2Fe₂O₃·3H₂O; prevented by galvanizing (Zn coating) or painting
  • Displacement reactions in metallurgy: extracting copper by placing iron in CuSO₄ solution (Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu, since Fe is more active than Cu)
