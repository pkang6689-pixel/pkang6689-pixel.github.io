AP Chemistry - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Activation Energy and Catalysts are central to understanding and controlling reaction rates. Activation energy (Ea) is the energy barrier that must be overcome for a reaction to proceed. The Arrhenius equation k = Ae^(-Ea/RT) quantifies how k depends on temperature and Ea. Catalysts provide an alternative reaction pathway with lower Ea, increasing the rate without being consumed. Catalysts do not change ΔG, ΔH, or the equilibrium constant K.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for reactants to reach the transition state and form products; determines the rate's temperature sensitivity
  • Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT); relates the rate constant to temperature, activation energy, and the frequency/orientation factor A
  • Catalyst: A substance that increases reaction rate by providing an alternative pathway with lower Ea; regenerated and not consumed
  • Homogeneous catalyst: A catalyst in the same phase as the reactants (e.g., H⁺ in aqueous acid catalysis)
  • Heterogeneous catalyst: A catalyst in a different phase from the reactants (e.g., solid Pt surface catalyzing gas-phase reactions)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Catalytic converters using Pt, Pd, and Rh heterogeneous catalysts to lower Ea for converting exhaust pollutants (CO, NOₓ) to harmless products
  • Enzyme inhibitors as drugs: statins lower cholesterol by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase, effectively raising the Ea for cholesterol synthesis
  • Industrial Haber process using iron catalyst to lower Ea for N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ from 230 kJ/mol (uncatalyzed) to ~60 kJ/mol, making ammonia synthesis economically viable
