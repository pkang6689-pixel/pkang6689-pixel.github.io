AP Chemistry - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Reaction Rates measure how fast reactant concentrations decrease or product concentrations increase over time. Rate = -Δ[reactant]/Δt = Δ[product]/Δt (adjusted by stoichiometric coefficients). Rates depend on concentration, temperature, surface area, and catalysts. The initial rate method measures rate at t = 0 to avoid complications from reverse reactions. Rates generally decrease over time as reactants are consumed.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Reaction rate: The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time: rate = -Δ[A]/Δt; typically in mol/(L·s) or M/s
  • Average rate: The change in concentration over a finite time interval: Δ[A]/Δt; decreases over time as reactants are consumed
  • Instantaneous rate: The rate at a specific moment, determined by the slope of the tangent to the concentration-vs.-time curve
  • Initial rate: The instantaneous rate at t = 0; used to determine rate laws because reverse reactions are negligible at the start
  • Factors affecting rate: Concentration (more collisions), temperature (faster molecules), surface area (more exposed reactant), catalysts (lower Ea)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Food preservation through refrigeration: lowering temperature slows the rate of bacterial growth and enzymatic decomposition reactions
  • Catalytic converters in cars: platinum/palladium catalysts speed up the conversion of toxic CO, NOₓ, and unburned hydrocarbons to CO₂, N₂, and H₂O
  • Antacid tablet surface area experiments: crushed tablets dissolve faster than whole tablets because increased surface area increases reaction rate
