AP Chemistry - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Rate Laws and Reaction Order describe the mathematical relationship between reaction rate and reactant concentrations. For aA + bB → products, rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ, where m and n are reaction orders (determined experimentally, NOT from coefficients). The overall order = m + n. Zero-order: rate is independent of concentration; first-order: rate doubles when concentration doubles; second-order: rate quadruples when concentration doubles. The rate constant k varies with temperature.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Rate law: An equation relating reaction rate to reactant concentrations: rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ; exponents determined experimentally
  • Reaction order: The exponent of a concentration term in the rate law; indicates how rate responds to concentration changes (0th, 1st, 2nd)
  • Rate constant (k): The proportionality constant in the rate law; units depend on overall order; increases exponentially with temperature
  • Integrated rate law: Relates concentration to time: zero-order [A] = -kt + [A]₀; first-order ln[A] = -kt + ln[A]₀; second-order 1/[A] = kt + 1/[A]₀
  • Half-life: Time for [A] to decrease to half its initial value: t₁/₂ = [A]₀/2k (zero), 0.693/k (first, constant), 1/k[A]₀ (second)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Radioactive decay following first-order kinetics with constant half-life: carbon-14 (t₁/₂ = 5,730 years) used for archaeological dating
  • Drug elimination from the body often following first-order kinetics: dosing intervals based on half-life to maintain therapeutic blood levels
  • Shelf life determination of food and pharmaceuticals using rate laws to predict how quickly products degrade at different storage temperatures
