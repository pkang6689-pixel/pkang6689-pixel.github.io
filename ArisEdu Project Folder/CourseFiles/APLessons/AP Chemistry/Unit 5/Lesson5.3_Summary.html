AP Chemistry - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Collision Theory explains reaction rates at the molecular level: for a reaction to occur, molecules must (1) collide with sufficient energy (≥ activation energy, Ea) and (2) have proper orientation. Only a small fraction of collisions are effective. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shows the range of molecular kinetic energies at a given temperature; increasing temperature shifts the distribution so more molecules exceed Ea.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Collision theory: A model stating reactions occur when molecules collide with sufficient energy (≥ Ea) and proper orientation
  • Effective collision: A collision with enough energy (≥ Ea) and correct molecular orientation to break bonds and form products
  • Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution: The distribution of molecular kinetic energies at a given temperature; a higher temperature shifts the peak and increases the fraction of molecules above Ea
  • Steric factor: The fraction of collisions with the correct orientation for reaction; determines the pre-exponential factor A in the Arrhenius equation
  • Transition state (activated complex): The high-energy, unstable intermediate configuration at the top of the energy barrier between reactants and products

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Match striking requires friction to provide activation energy, initiating the exothermic combustion reaction of KClO₃ and sulfur in the match head
  • Temperature's exponential effect on reaction rates: a 10°C increase approximately doubles the rate because of the exponential increase in molecules above Ea
  • Enzyme catalysis lowering Ea for biochemical reactions, allowing them to proceed rapidly at body temperature (37°C) rather than requiring extreme heating
