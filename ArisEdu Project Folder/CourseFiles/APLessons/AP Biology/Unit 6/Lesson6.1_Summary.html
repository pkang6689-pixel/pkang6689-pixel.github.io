AP Biology - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

DNA Replication and Transcription describes how genetic information stored in DNA is copied (replication) and read into mRNA (transcription). Transcription is catalyzed by RNA polymerase, which reads the template strand 3'→5' and synthesizes mRNA 5'→3'. In eukaryotes, pre-mRNA undergoes processing: 5' cap addition, 3' poly-A tail, and splicing to remove introns and join exons. Alternative splicing enables one gene to produce multiple protein variants.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Transcription: RNA polymerase reads the DNA template strand 3'→5' and synthesizes complementary mRNA 5'→3'; occurs in the nucleus in eukaryotes
  • Promoter: A DNA sequence upstream of a gene (e.g., TATA box) where RNA polymerase and transcription factors bind to initiate transcription
  • Pre-mRNA processing: 5' cap (7-methylguanosine) and 3' poly-A tail added for stability and export; introns spliced out by spliceosomes
  • Introns and exons: Introns are non-coding sequences removed by splicing; exons are coding sequences that are joined together in mature mRNA
  • Alternative splicing: Different combinations of exons are joined to produce multiple mRNA variants (and proteins) from a single gene

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Alternative splicing producing over 100,000 different proteins from only ~20,000 human genes, explaining how gene count doesn't correlate with organism complexity
  • α-amanitin (death cap mushroom toxin) inhibiting RNA polymerase II, blocking transcription and causing fatal liver failure within days
  • RT-PCR diagnostic tests detecting viral RNA (COVID-19) by reverse-transcribing RNA to DNA and amplifying specific sequences
