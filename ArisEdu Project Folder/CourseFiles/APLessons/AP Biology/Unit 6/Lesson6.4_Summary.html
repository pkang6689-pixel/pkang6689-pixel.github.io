AP Biology - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Mutation and Cancer explains how accumulated mutations in proto-oncogenes (gain-of-function to oncogenes) and tumor suppressor genes (loss-of-function) drive the multistep development of cancer. The Knudson two-hit hypothesis explains that both copies of a tumor suppressor must be inactivated for cancer to develop. Cancer hallmarks include sustained proliferative signaling, evasion of growth suppressors, resistance to cell death, unlimited replication, angiogenesis, and metastasis.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Oncogene: A mutated proto-oncogene that promotes uncontrolled cell growth; typically a gain-of-function mutation (one copy sufficient)
  • Tumor suppressor gene: A gene whose normal function is to inhibit cell division or promote apoptosis; both copies must be lost for cancer to develop (two-hit hypothesis)
  • Metastasis: The spread of cancer cells from the primary tumor to distant sites via the bloodstream or lymphatic system
  • Angiogenesis: Formation of new blood vessels that supply tumors with oxygen and nutrients, enabling growth beyond a few millimeters
  • Carcinogen: An agent that causes cancer by inducing DNA mutations or promoting cell proliferation (e.g., tobacco smoke, UV radiation, asbestos)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Targeted cancer therapy: Herceptin (trastuzumab) blocking the HER2 receptor in breast cancers that overexpress this oncogene protein
  • Colon cancer progression following the multistep model: APC loss → K-ras activation → DCC loss → p53 loss → metastatic cancer over 10-15 years
  • HPV vaccines (Gardasil) preventing cervical cancer by protecting against viral proteins (E6, E7) that inactivate tumor suppressors p53 and Rb
