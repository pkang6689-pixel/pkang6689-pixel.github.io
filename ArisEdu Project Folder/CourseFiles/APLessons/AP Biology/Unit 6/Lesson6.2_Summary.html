AP Biology - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Translation is the process by which ribosomes synthesize proteins from mRNA templates. Transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules carry amino acids and match their anticodons to mRNA codons. Translation occurs in three stages: initiation (ribosome assembles at start codon AUG), elongation (amino acids added one by one as ribosome moves along mRNA), and termination (release factor binds stop codon). The genetic code is degenerate (multiple codons per amino acid), nearly universal, and read in non-overlapping triplets.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Codon: A three-nucleotide sequence in mRNA that codes for a specific amino acid or stop signal; 64 total codons, 61 code for amino acids
  • tRNA (transfer RNA): An adapter molecule that carries a specific amino acid and has an anticodon complementary to an mRNA codon
  • Ribosome: The molecular machine for translation; composed of large and small subunits containing rRNA and proteins; has A, P, and E sites
  • Start codon (AUG): The codon that initiates translation and codes for methionine; sets the reading frame for the entire mRNA
  • Degeneracy of genetic code: Most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons (e.g., leucine has 6 codons); provides some protection against point mutations

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Antibiotic targeting of bacterial ribosomes: tetracycline blocks the A-site, chloramphenicol inhibits peptidyl transferase—both exploit differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic ribosomes
  • Genetic engineering using the universal genetic code to express human insulin in E. coli bacteria, producing recombinant insulin for diabetes treatment
  • Nonsense suppressor tRNA mutations in research organisms that read through stop codons, used as tools in molecular biology research
