AP Biology - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Cellular Respiration: Krebs Cycle (citric acid cycle) occurs in the mitochondrial matrix when pyruvate is converted to acetyl-CoA (producing CO₂ and NADH), which then enters the cycle. Each turn of the cycle produces 1 ATP, 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 2 CO₂, oxidizing the acetyl group completely. Two turns per glucose yield 2 ATP, 6 NADH, and 2 FADH₂. The cycle is regulated by substrate availability and allosteric inhibition by ATP, NADH, and citrate.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Pyruvate oxidation: Pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix and is converted to acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH by pyruvate dehydrogenase
  • Acetyl-CoA: A 2-carbon acetyl group attached to coenzyme A; enters the Krebs cycle by combining with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C)
  • Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle): An 8-step cyclic pathway that completely oxidizes acetyl-CoA, producing CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, and ATP per turn
  • FADH₂: An electron carrier that donates electrons to Complex II of the electron transport chain at a lower energy level than NADH
  • Oxaloacetate: The 4-carbon molecule that accepts acetyl-CoA to begin each turn of the Krebs cycle; regenerated at the end of each turn

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Exercise physiology: the Krebs cycle's rate increases during aerobic exercise as increased ATP consumption lowers ATP/ADP ratio, relieving allosteric inhibition
  • Metabolic interconnections: Krebs cycle intermediates serve as precursors for amino acid synthesis, fatty acid synthesis, and gluconeogenesis
  • Citrate as a food additive and preservative, derived from the first intermediate of the Krebs cycle, produced industrially by Aspergillus niger fermentation
