AP Biology - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Photosynthesis: Calvin Cycle occurs in the chloroplast stroma and fixes atmospheric CO₂ into organic molecules using ATP and NADPH from the light reactions. The enzyme RuBisCO catalyzes carbon fixation (CO₂ + RuBP → 2 molecules of 3-PGA), making it the most abundant protein on Earth. Three turns of the Calvin cycle fix 3 CO₂ to produce one net G3P molecule, consuming 9 ATP and 6 NADPH. G3P is used to synthesize glucose and other organic molecules.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Carbon fixation: The incorporation of CO₂ into an organic molecule (RuBP → 2 molecules of 3-PGA); catalyzed by RuBisCO
  • RuBisCO: Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase; the most abundant enzyme on Earth; fixes CO₂ but also has oxygenase activity (photorespiration)
  • G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate): A 3-carbon sugar produced by the Calvin cycle; the direct product used to build glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids
  • Regeneration of RuBP: The phase of the Calvin cycle using 3 ATP to regenerate the 5-carbon RuBP acceptor from remaining G3P molecules
  • Photorespiration: RuBisCO fixes O₂ instead of CO₂, producing a 2-carbon compound that wastes energy; more common at high temperatures

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • C4 and CAM photosynthesis as evolutionary adaptations to minimize photorespiration in hot, dry environments (corn, sugarcane, cacti, pineapple)
  • Rising atmospheric CO₂ levels increasing carbon fixation rates in some plants (CO₂ fertilization effect) but potentially decreasing nutritional quality of crops
  • Biofuel production engineering algae with enhanced Calvin cycle efficiency to convert CO₂ into lipids for biodiesel
