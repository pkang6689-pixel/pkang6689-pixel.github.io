AP Biology - Unit 2 - Lesson 2.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Photosynthesis: Light Reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes and convert light energy into chemical energy (ATP and NADPH). Photosystems II and I absorb photons, exciting electrons that pass through electron transport chains. Water is split (photolysis) at PSII, releasing O₂. The electron transport chain pumps H⁺ into the thylakoid lumen, and ATP synthase uses this proton gradient (chemiosmosis) to produce ATP. NADP⁺ is reduced to NADPH at PSI.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Photosystem II (P680): The first photosystem in the light reactions; absorbs 680nm light, splits water, and passes excited electrons to the electron transport chain
  • Photosystem I (P700): Absorbs 700nm light and re-energizes electrons for reduction of NADP⁺ to NADPH by ferredoxin and NADP⁺ reductase
  • Photolysis: The splitting of water molecules (2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂) at PSII, providing electrons and releasing oxygen as a byproduct
  • Chemiosmosis: ATP production driven by the proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane; H⁺ flows through ATP synthase from lumen to stroma
  • Electron transport chain (photosynthetic): A series of protein complexes (plastoquinone, cytochrome b6f, plastocyanin) that transfer electrons and pump H⁺ into the thylakoid lumen

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Artificial photosynthesis research mimicking PSII's water-splitting capability to generate hydrogen fuel using sunlight
  • Crop yield optimization through understanding light reaction efficiency and the effects of light intensity, wavelength, and duration on photosynthetic rates
  • Coral bleaching when heat stress damages photosystem proteins in symbiotic zooxanthellae, disrupting photosynthesis and causing coral death
