AP Biology - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.6 - Summary

Key Concepts

Conservation Biology integrates ecology, genetics, and evolutionary biology to protect and restore biological diversity. Population viability analysis (PVA) estimates extinction probability for small populations considering demographic and environmental stochasticity. Minimum viable population (MVP), effective population size (Ne), and genetic rescue inform conservation management. Landscape ecology connects habitat patches through corridors, and ecosystem services valuation provides economic arguments for conservation.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Population viability analysis (PVA): A mathematical model estimating a population's probability of persistence over a specified time period, incorporating demographic and environmental variability
  • Minimum viable population (MVP): The smallest population size that can sustain itself over a long period (typically 50/500 rule: 50 for short-term inbreeding avoidance, 500 for long-term genetic diversity)
  • Effective population size (Ne): The number of individuals contributing genetically to the next generation; usually much smaller than census size due to unequal sex ratios, variance in reproductive success
  • Wildlife corridor: A connected strip of habitat linking isolated patches, enabling gene flow, migration, and recolonization between fragmented populations
  • Ecosystem services: Benefits humans receive from ecosystems: provisioning (food, water), regulating (climate, flood control), cultural (recreation), and supporting (nutrient cycling, soil formation)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Yellowstone-to-Yukon Conservation Initiative creating wildlife corridors spanning 3,200 km to connect fragmented habitats for grizzly bears, wolves, and elk migration
  • Genetic rescue of the Florida panther: introducing 8 female Texas pumas in 1995 restored genetic diversity and tripled the population from ~25 to ~200 individuals
  • Ecosystem services valuation estimating that pollination by insects is worth $235-577 billion annually globally, providing economic justification for pollinator conservation
