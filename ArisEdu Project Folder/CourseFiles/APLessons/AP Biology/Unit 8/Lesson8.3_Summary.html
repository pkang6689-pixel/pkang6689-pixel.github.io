AP Biology - Unit 8 - Lesson 8.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Ecosystem Ecology studies energy flow and nutrient cycling through ecosystems. Energy flows unidirectionally through trophic levels with ~10% efficiency (10% rule): producers → primary consumers → secondary consumers → tertiary consumers. Nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water) cycle between biotic and abiotic components. Net primary productivity (NPP) measures the rate of energy storage by producers and varies by biome, with tropical rainforests and estuaries among the most productive.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Trophic level: A feeding level in a food chain: producers (autotrophs), primary consumers (herbivores), secondary consumers (carnivores), decomposers
  • 10% rule: Only ~10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next; the rest is lost as heat through metabolic processes
  • Net primary productivity (NPP): The rate at which producers store energy as biomass minus their own respiratory losses; NPP = GPP - respiration
  • Biogeochemical cycle: The pathway by which a chemical element (C, N, P, H₂O) moves through the biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem
  • Nitrogen fixation: The conversion of atmospheric N₂ to biologically usable NH₃/NH₄⁺ by bacteria (Rhizobium) or lightning; the only natural source of usable nitrogen

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Biomagnification of mercury and DDT through food chains: top predators (eagles, tuna) accumulate the highest concentrations, explaining why large predatory fish have mercury advisories
  • Carbon cycle disruption: fossil fuel combustion releasing 36+ billion tons of CO₂ annually, faster than natural sinks can absorb, driving climate change
  • Sustainable agriculture using nitrogen-fixing cover crops (legumes) to reduce dependence on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers that contribute to water pollution
