AP Biology - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Energy and Metabolism describes how cells manage energy through coupled reactions and metabolic pathways. The laws of thermodynamics govern biological systems: energy is conserved (1st law) and entropy increases in spontaneous processes (2nd law). Free energy change (ΔG) determines reaction spontaneity: exergonic reactions (ΔG < 0) release energy, while endergonic reactions (ΔG > 0) require energy input. ATP hydrolysis (ΔG = -30.5 kJ/mol) couples to endergonic reactions to drive cellular work.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Free energy (ΔG): The energy available to do work; ΔG < 0 (exergonic, spontaneous), ΔG > 0 (endergonic, requires energy input), ΔG = 0 (equilibrium)
  • ATP (adenosine triphosphate): The cell's primary energy currency; hydrolysis of the terminal phosphate releases -30.5 kJ/mol used to power endergonic reactions
  • Energy coupling: Using the exergonic hydrolysis of ATP to drive an endergonic reaction, often through phosphorylation of a substrate
  • Entropy: A measure of disorder or randomness; the 2nd law of thermodynamics states that entropy of the universe increases in spontaneous processes
  • Metabolism: The totality of an organism's chemical reactions; catabolism (breakdown, releases energy) and anabolism (synthesis, requires energy)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Firefly bioluminescence using ATP to power the oxidation of luciferin, converting chemical energy directly to light with remarkable efficiency
  • Muscle contraction powered by ATP hydrolysis changing the conformation of myosin heads along actin filaments (cross-bridge cycling)
  • Active transport of ions (Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase) using ATP to pump 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle, maintaining the resting membrane potential essential for nerve impulses
