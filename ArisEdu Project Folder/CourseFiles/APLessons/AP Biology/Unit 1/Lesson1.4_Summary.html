AP Biology - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Proteins are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds, with structure and function determined by the sequence (primary structure) and folding (secondary, tertiary, quaternary structures). The 20 amino acids differ in their R-groups, which determine polarity, charge, and chemical reactivity. Protein denaturation (loss of 3D shape from heat, pH, or chemical changes) destroys function, demonstrating that structure determines function—a central theme in AP Biology.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Amino acid: The monomer of proteins: a central carbon bonded to an amino group (-NH₂), carboxyl group (-COOH), hydrogen, and variable R-group
  • Peptide bond: A covalent bond between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another, formed by dehydration synthesis
  • Primary structure: The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, determined by the gene encoding the protein
  • Tertiary structure: The overall 3D shape of a single polypeptide, resulting from interactions between R-groups (hydrophobic, ionic, disulfide, hydrogen bonds)
  • Denaturation: Loss of a protein's 3D structure (and function) due to disruption of non-covalent bonds by heat, extreme pH, or chemicals; primary structure remains intact

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Sickle cell disease caused by a single amino acid change (Glu→Val at position 6 of β-globin) that alters hemoglobin's tertiary/quaternary structure and red blood cell shape
  • Enzyme-based laundry detergents using proteases and lipases that remain active at moderate temperatures but denature at high heat
  • Prion diseases (mad cow, CJD) where misfolded proteins induce normal proteins to refold incorrectly, demonstrating the critical importance of protein conformation
