AP Biology - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.6 - Summary

Key Concepts

Cell Structure covers the organization of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells. Eukaryotic cells compartmentalize functions in membrane-bound organelles: the nucleus (DNA storage), endoplasmic reticulum (protein/lipid synthesis), Golgi apparatus (modification and sorting), mitochondria (ATP production), and chloroplasts (photosynthesis). The endomembrane system connects these organelles through vesicle trafficking. Structure-function relationships are evident at every level of cellular organization.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Plasma membrane: A phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins that controls what enters and exits the cell; selectively permeable
  • Nucleus: Membrane-bound organelle containing chromatin (DNA + histones); site of DNA replication, transcription, and ribosome assembly (nucleolus)
  • Endoplasmic reticulum: Rough ER (with ribosomes): protein synthesis and modification; Smooth ER: lipid synthesis, detoxification, calcium storage
  • Mitochondria: Double-membrane organelle where cellular respiration produces ATP; has own circular DNA, supporting the endosymbiotic theory
  • Endomembrane system: The network of ER, Golgi, lysosomes, vesicles, and plasma membrane connected by vesicle trafficking for protein processing and transport

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Antibiotic targeting of prokaryotic-specific structures (70S ribosomes, cell wall synthesis) that differ from eukaryotic cells, enabling selective bacterial killing
  • Cancer cell organelle abnormalities: enlarged nuclei, increased rough ER (elevated protein synthesis), and altered mitochondrial metabolism (Warburg effect)
  • Endosymbiotic theory evidence: mitochondria and chloroplasts have double membranes, own circular DNA, 70S ribosomes, and replicate independently
