AP Biology - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Lipids are hydrophobic or amphipathic molecules including fats, phospholipids, and steroids. Triglycerides (fats and oils) store energy (9 kcal/g) through ester bonds between glycerol and three fatty acids. Phospholipids form the basis of cell membrane bilayers due to their amphipathic nature (hydrophilic head, hydrophobic tails). Saturated vs. unsaturated fatty acids differ in the presence of C=C double bonds, affecting fluidity and melting point.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Triglyceride: Three fatty acids linked to glycerol via ester bonds; fats (saturated, solid at room temp) and oils (unsaturated, liquid at room temp)
  • Phospholipid: Amphipathic lipid with a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails; forms the cell membrane bilayer
  • Saturated fatty acid: A fatty acid with no C=C double bonds; straight chain allows tight packing, resulting in solid fats at room temperature
  • Unsaturated fatty acid: A fatty acid with one or more C=C double bonds creating kinks; prevents tight packing, liquid at room temperature
  • Steroid: A lipid with four fused carbon rings; includes cholesterol (membrane fluidity), testosterone, estrogen, and cortisol

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils) increasing cardiovascular disease risk by raising LDL and lowering HDL cholesterol levels
  • Cell membrane fluidity regulation through cholesterol insertion between phospholipids and adjustment of saturated vs. unsaturated fatty acid ratios
  • Myelin sheaths (lipid-rich) insulating nerve axons to increase signal transmission speed from 2 m/s to over 100 m/s
