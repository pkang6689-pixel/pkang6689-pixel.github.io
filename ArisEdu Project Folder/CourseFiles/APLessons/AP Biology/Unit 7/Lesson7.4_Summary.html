AP Biology - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Macroevolution encompasses large-scale evolutionary patterns above the species level, including speciation, adaptive radiation, mass extinctions, and major transitions in body plan. Speciation occurs through geographic isolation (allopatric) or reproductive isolation within the same area (sympatric). Adaptive radiation (rapid diversification from a single ancestor into multiple ecological niches) often follows mass extinctions or colonization of new environments.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Speciation: The formation of new species through accumulation of reproductive isolation barriers; requires genetic divergence between populations
  • Allopatric speciation: Speciation driven by geographic isolation that prevents gene flow between populations, allowing independent evolution
  • Sympatric speciation: Speciation occurring within the same geographic area, often through polyploidy (plants) or habitat/behavioral specialization
  • Reproductive isolation: Barriers preventing gene flow: prezygotic (habitat, temporal, behavioral, mechanical, gametic) and postzygotic (hybrid inviability, sterility)
  • Adaptive radiation: Rapid diversification of a lineage into many species filling different ecological niches, often following a mass extinction or colonization of a new habitat

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Galápagos finch adaptive radiation: a single ancestor species diversifying into 14 species with specialized beak shapes for different food sources within ~1-2 million years
  • Mass extinctions (e.g., K-Pg, 66 MYA) eliminating 75% of species but opening ecological niches that enabled mammalian diversification and eventual human evolution
  • Ring species around geographic barriers (e.g., Ensatina salamanders around Central Valley of California) demonstrating speciation as a gradual geographic process
