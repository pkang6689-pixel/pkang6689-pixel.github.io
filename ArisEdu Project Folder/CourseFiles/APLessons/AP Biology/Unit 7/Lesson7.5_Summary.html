AP Biology - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Phylogenetics reconstructs evolutionary relationships using shared derived characters (synapomorphies) organized in cladograms or phylogenetic trees. Molecular phylogenetics compares DNA, RNA, or protein sequences to build trees based on sequence similarity. Parsimony (fewest evolutionary changes) and maximum likelihood methods are used to determine the most probable tree topology. Phylogenies reveal the nested hierarchy of life and enable predictions about uncharacterized organisms.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Cladogram: A branching diagram showing evolutionary relationships based on shared derived characters (synapomorphies); branch lengths may or may not represent time
  • Monophyletic group (clade): A group consisting of a common ancestor and ALL of its descendants; the fundamental unit in modern classification
  • Synapomorphy: A shared derived character that defines a clade; evidence of common ancestry (e.g., feathers in birds)
  • Outgroup: A species or group outside the clade being studied, used to root the phylogenetic tree and determine which characters are ancestral vs. derived
  • Molecular clock: The concept that DNA sequences accumulate mutations at a roughly constant rate, allowing estimation of divergence times between lineages

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Using molecular phylogenetics to trace the origin and spread of HIV, determining it crossed from chimpanzees to humans around 1920 in central Africa
  • Phylogenomic analysis revealing that birds are living dinosaurs (nested within theropod dinosaurs), fundamentally changing our classification of vertebrates
  • Virus tracking during COVID-19 pandemic using SARS-CoV-2 genome sequencing to build phylogenetic trees tracing transmission chains and identifying new variants
