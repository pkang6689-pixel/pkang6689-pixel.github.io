AP Biology - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Population Genetics provides the mathematical framework for studying evolution using allele and genotype frequencies. The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (p² + 2pq + q² = 1, p + q = 1) serves as a null hypothesis: populations are in equilibrium only when there is no mutation, migration, selection, drift, or non-random mating. Deviations from H-W equilibrium indicate evolution is occurring. Calculating allele frequencies from phenotype data allows estimation of carrier frequencies for genetic diseases.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: A mathematical model predicting allele and genotype frequencies remain constant across generations in the absence of evolutionary forces
  • Allele frequency: The proportion of a specific allele in a population: p = frequency of dominant allele, q = frequency of recessive allele; p + q = 1
  • Genotype frequency: The proportion of each genotype in a population: p² (AA) + 2pq (Aa) + q² (aa) = 1 under Hardy-Weinberg conditions
  • Hardy-Weinberg conditions: No mutation, no migration, random mating, no selection, large population size; violations drive evolution
  • Carrier frequency: 2pq; the proportion of heterozygous carriers of a recessive allele in a population, estimable from disease frequency (q²)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Estimating cystic fibrosis carrier frequency: disease frequency q² ≈ 1/2500, so q ≈ 1/50, and carrier frequency 2pq ≈ 1/25 in European populations
  • Detecting natural selection by comparing observed genotype frequencies to Hardy-Weinberg predictions and identifying significant deviations using chi-square tests
  • Conservation biology using Hardy-Weinberg analysis to assess genetic health of endangered species populations and plan genetic rescue interventions
