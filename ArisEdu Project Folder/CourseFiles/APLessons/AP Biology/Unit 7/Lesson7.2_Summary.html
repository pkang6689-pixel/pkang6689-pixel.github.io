AP Biology - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Mechanisms of Evolution include natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and mutation. Natural selection acts on phenotypic variation, favoring traits that increase fitness (reproductive success) in a specific environment. The three modes of natural selection are directional (shifts the mean), stabilizing (reduces variation around the mean), and disruptive (favors extremes). Sexual selection and artificial selection are special cases of selection that shape traits beyond survival.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Natural selection: Differential survival and reproduction of individuals with favorable heritable traits in a given environment; the primary mechanism of adaptive evolution
  • Fitness: An organism's reproductive success relative to others in the population; measured by the number of viable, fertile offspring produced
  • Genetic drift: Random changes in allele frequencies due to chance events; most significant in small populations (bottleneck effect, founder effect)
  • Gene flow: The transfer of alleles between populations through migration and interbreeding; tends to reduce genetic differences between populations
  • Directional selection: Natural selection that favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population mean in that direction over time

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Darwin's finch beak evolution on Daphne Major Island: drought years selected for larger beaks (to crack harder seeds), directly observed by Peter and Rosemary Grant over 40 years
  • Bottleneck effect in northern elephant seals: hunted to ~20 individuals by 1890s, population recovered to 100,000+ but retains extremely low genetic diversity
  • Artificial selection in agriculture: selective breeding transforming teosinte (small, hard kernels) into modern corn over thousands of years of human-directed selection
