AP Biology - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

DNA Damage and Repair Mechanisms protect genomic integrity from various sources of damage including UV radiation (thymine dimers), reactive oxygen species (oxidized bases), and replication errors. Repair mechanisms include base excision repair (BER) for damaged bases, nucleotide excision repair (NER) for bulky lesions, and mismatch repair for replication errors. When damage is too severe, p53 activates apoptosis to prevent propagation of mutations that could lead to cancer.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Thymine dimer: A UV-induced covalent bond between adjacent thymine bases on the same strand; distorts the double helix and blocks replication
  • Base excision repair (BER): Repair of a single damaged base: DNA glycosylase removes the damaged base, AP endonuclease cuts the backbone, and polymerase fills the gap
  • Nucleotide excision repair (NER): Removal of a bulky DNA lesion (e.g., thymine dimer) by cutting a ~30-nucleotide segment and resynthesizing from the template strand
  • Mismatch repair: Post-replication correction of mismatched bases that escaped proofreading; involves enzymes that distinguish parent from daughter strand
  • Mutagen: An agent that causes DNA mutations: chemical mutagens (base analogs, alkylating agents), radiation (UV, X-rays), or biological (transposons, viruses)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Sunscreen effectiveness in preventing UV-induced thymine dimers, reducing the risk of skin cancer by protecting DNA from photodamage
  • Lynch syndrome (hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer) caused by inherited mutations in mismatch repair genes, increasing cancer risk
  • Antioxidant research examining whether dietary antioxidants (vitamins C, E) can reduce oxidative DNA damage from reactive oxygen species
