AP Biology - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Cell Division (Mitosis and Meiosis) are the two types of nuclear division. Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid (2n) daughter cells for growth and repair, while meiosis produces four genetically unique haploid (n) gametes for sexual reproduction. Meiosis involves two rounds of division with crossing over and independent assortment generating genetic variation. Both processes require precise chromosome segregation to prevent aneuploidy.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Mitosis: Nuclear division producing two genetically identical daughter cells (2n → 2n); stages: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase + cytokinesis
  • Meiosis: Two rounds of nuclear division producing four haploid gametes (2n → n); includes crossing over and independent assortment for genetic diversity
  • Crossing over: Exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis; increases genetic variation
  • Independent assortment: Random orientation of homologous pairs at metaphase I, producing 2ⁿ possible gamete combinations (n = haploid number)
  • Nondisjunction: Failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, resulting in gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers (aneuploidy)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Down syndrome (trisomy 21) caused by nondisjunction during meiosis, resulting in an extra copy of chromosome 21 in all cells
  • Cancer as uncontrolled mitosis: mutations in proto-oncogenes and tumor suppressors enable cells to bypass cell cycle checkpoints
  • IVF and preimplantation genetic testing screening embryos for chromosomal abnormalities (aneuploidy) before implantation
