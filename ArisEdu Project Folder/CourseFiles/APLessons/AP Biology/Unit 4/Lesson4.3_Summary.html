AP Biology - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Regulation of Cell Cycle ensures orderly progression through G1, S, G2, and M phases via internal checkpoints and external signals. Cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) activated by cyclins drive progression past checkpoints. The G1 checkpoint (restriction point) commits the cell to division, the G2 checkpoint verifies complete DNA replication, and the M checkpoint (spindle assembly checkpoint) ensures proper chromosome attachment. Tumor suppressors (p53, Rb) and proto-oncogenes regulate these checkpoints.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Cell cycle checkpoints: Control points at G1, G2, and M where the cell evaluates conditions before proceeding; can halt the cycle if conditions are unfavorable
  • Cyclin-CDK complexes: Cyclins accumulate and activate CDKs at specific cell cycle stages, driving progression through phosphorylation of target proteins
  • Tumor suppressor gene: A gene encoding a protein that inhibits cell division (e.g., p53 triggers apoptosis in damaged cells, Rb blocks G1/S transition)
  • Proto-oncogene: A normal gene that promotes cell growth; mutations can convert it to an oncogene, causing uncontrolled cell division
  • Density-dependent inhibition: Normal cells stop dividing when they contact neighboring cells; cancer cells lose this control (contact inhibition)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • p53 ('guardian of the genome') mutations found in over 50% of human cancers, allowing damaged cells to continue dividing rather than undergoing apoptosis
  • HPV (human papillomavirus) causing cervical cancer by producing proteins that inactivate p53 and Rb tumor suppressors, disabling cell cycle checkpoints
  • Cancer immunotherapy using checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-CTLA-4) to re-enable the immune system's ability to attack cancer cells
