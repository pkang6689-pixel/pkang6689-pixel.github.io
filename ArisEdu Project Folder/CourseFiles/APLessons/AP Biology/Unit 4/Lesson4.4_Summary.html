AP Biology - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

DNA Replication and Repair describes the semiconservative process by which DNA is copied before cell division. Helicase unwinds the double helix, primase synthesizes RNA primers, and DNA polymerase III synthesizes new strands in the 5'→3' direction. The leading strand is synthesized continuously, while the lagging strand is synthesized in Okazaki fragments joined by DNA ligase. DNA polymerase's proofreading (3'→5' exonuclease) and mismatch repair maintain a remarkably low error rate (~1 error per 10⁹ bases).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Semiconservative replication: Each new DNA molecule consists of one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand; demonstrated by Meselson-Stahl experiment
  • DNA polymerase III: The main replication enzyme in E. coli; synthesizes DNA 5'→3' by adding nucleotides complementary to the template strand
  • Leading strand: The strand synthesized continuously in the 5'→3' direction toward the replication fork
  • Lagging strand: The strand synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments in the 5'→3' direction away from the replication fork
  • Proofreading: DNA polymerase's 3'→5' exonuclease activity that detects and corrects mismatched bases during replication

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Telomere shortening with each cell division limiting cell lifespan; telomerase activation in cancer cells enables unlimited replication (immortality)
  • Cancer chemotherapy drugs (e.g., cisplatin) damaging DNA or blocking replication to kill rapidly dividing cancer cells
  • Xeroderma pigmentosum caused by defective nucleotide excision repair, making patients extremely sensitive to UV radiation-induced DNA damage
