AP Biology - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Mendelian Genetics describes inheritance patterns based on Gregor Mendel's laws of segregation (each organism carries two alleles that separate during gamete formation) and independent assortment (genes on different chromosomes are inherited independently). Dominant alleles mask recessive alleles in heterozygotes. Monohybrid crosses (Aa × Aa → 3:1 phenotypic ratio) and dihybrid crosses (AaBb × AaBb → 9:3:3:1 ratio) predict offspring ratios, testable with chi-square analysis.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Law of Segregation: Each diploid organism has two alleles for each gene; these separate during meiosis so each gamete carries only one allele
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes on different (non-homologous) chromosomes sort independently into gametes during meiosis
  • Dominant and recessive: A dominant allele is expressed in heterozygotes; a recessive allele is expressed only in homozygotes (aa)
  • Punnett square: A grid used to predict genotype and phenotype ratios in offspring from a particular cross
  • Test cross: Crossing an individual of unknown genotype (dominant phenotype) with a homozygous recessive (aa) to determine whether the unknown is AA or Aa

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Genetic counseling using Punnett squares and pedigree analysis to calculate the probability of offspring inheriting genetic diseases like cystic fibrosis
  • Agricultural selective breeding applying Mendelian genetics to develop crop varieties with desired traits (disease resistance, yield) over multiple generations
  • Forensic genetics using Mendelian inheritance patterns to establish paternity or identify family relationships from DNA evidence
