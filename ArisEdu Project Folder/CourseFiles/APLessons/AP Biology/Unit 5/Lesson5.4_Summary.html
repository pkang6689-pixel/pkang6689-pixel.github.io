AP Biology - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Mutations and Genetic Variation are changes in DNA sequence that provide the raw material for evolution. Point mutations include silent, missense, nonsense, and frameshift types, with varying effects on protein function. Mutations can be spontaneous (replication errors) or induced by mutagens. While most mutations are neutral or harmful, some confer advantages (e.g., sickle cell heterozygote advantage against malaria). Mutations in somatic cells affect only the individual; germline mutations are heritable.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Point mutation: A change in a single nucleotide base pair; includes substitutions (transitions, transversions) and single base insertions/deletions
  • Missense mutation: A nucleotide change that alters one amino acid in the protein; effects range from none to severe loss of function
  • Nonsense mutation: A nucleotide change that creates a premature stop codon, producing a truncated, usually nonfunctional protein
  • Frameshift mutation: Insertion or deletion of nucleotides (not in multiples of 3) that shifts the reading frame, altering all downstream amino acids
  • Germline vs. somatic mutation: Germline mutations (in eggs/sperm) are passed to offspring; somatic mutations affect only the individual and are not inherited

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Sickle cell trait (HbAS heterozygotes) conferring malaria resistance while homozygotes (HbSS) suffer sickle cell disease—a classic example of heterozygote advantage
  • BRCA1/2 germline mutations increasing breast cancer risk to 60-80% lifetime probability, enabling preventive screening and treatment decisions
  • Antibiotic resistance emerging through bacterial mutations that survive selection pressure, creating resistant strains like MRSA
