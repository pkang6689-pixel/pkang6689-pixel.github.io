AP Biology - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Genetic Linkage and Chromosome Mapping describe how genes located on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together (linkage), violating Mendel's law of independent assortment. Recombination frequency between linked genes (calculated from testcross offspring) indicates the distance between genes on a chromosome, measured in centimorgans (cM) or map units. Genetic mapping constructs a linear order of genes along chromosomes based on recombination frequencies.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Linked genes: Genes located on the same chromosome that tend to be inherited together; do not assort independently
  • Recombination frequency: The percentage of offspring showing recombinant phenotypes; used to calculate the distance between linked genes
  • Centimorgan (cM): A unit of genetic distance: 1 cM = 1% recombination frequency between two genes on the same chromosome
  • Genetic map: A diagram showing the relative positions and distances of genes on a chromosome based on recombination frequencies
  • Parental vs. recombinant: Parental types show the same allele combination as the parents; recombinant types show new combinations from crossing over

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Thomas Hunt Morgan's Drosophila experiments discovering sex-linked inheritance and gene linkage, proving genes are carried on chromosomes
  • The Human Genome Project using genetic and physical mapping to determine the positions of all ~20,000 human genes across 23 chromosome pairs
  • Agricultural breeding programs using genetic maps to identify and select for markers linked to desirable traits like drought tolerance
