AP Physics 2 - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.3 - Summary

Key Concepts

Energy Levels in Atoms explains the discrete energy structure of atoms. In hydrogen: E_n = -13.6 eV/n². Photon emission occurs when an electron transitions from a higher to a lower energy level: ΔE = E_final - E_initial = hf. Absorption is the reverse: a photon with exactly the right energy promotes an electron to a higher level. The energy level diagram shows allowed states; arrows between levels represent possible transitions. Multi-electron atoms have more complex energy levels due to electron-electron interactions, but the quantized nature persists.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Energy levels: Discrete allowed energies for electrons: E_n = -13.6/n² eV for hydrogen; increasingly closer together for higher n
  • Photon emission: Electron drops from n_high to n_low: emitted photon has E_photon = |E_high - E_low| = hf; energy is conserved
  • Photon absorption: Electron absorbs a photon with exactly E = E_upper - E_lower and jumps to a higher level; must match exactly
  • Ionization energy: Energy needed to completely remove an electron: for hydrogen, E_ionization = 13.6 eV (from n=1 to n=∞)
  • Spectral series: Lyman (to n=1, UV), Balmer (to n=2, visible), Paschen (to n=3, IR); named groups of transitions

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Neon signs: specific gases glow with characteristic colors because their unique energy level spacings produce photons at specific visible wavelengths
  • Lasers: stimulated emission occurs when photons trigger excited electrons to release identical photons—producing coherent, monochromatic, intense light
  • Fluorescent lighting: UV photons from mercury vapor excite phosphor coating electrons, which emit visible light as they return to lower energy levels
