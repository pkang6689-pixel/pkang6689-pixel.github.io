AP Physics 2 - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Emission and Absorption Spectra are spectroscopic fingerprints of elements. An emission spectrum consists of bright lines at specific wavelengths produced when excited gas atoms emit photons during electron transitions downward. An absorption spectrum shows dark lines at the same wavelengths when continuous light passes through a cool gas—the gas absorbs photons matching its transition energies. Each element has a unique spectrum. The Rydberg formula for hydrogen: 1/λ = R_H(1/n_f² - 1/n_i²), where R_H = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Emission spectrum: Bright colored lines on a dark background; produced by excited atoms emitting photons at characteristic frequencies
  • Absorption spectrum: Dark lines on a continuous bright background; produced when cool gas absorbs specific wavelengths from white light
  • Spectral fingerprint: Each element has a unique set of spectral lines; used for identification from streetlights to distant stars
  • Rydberg formula: 1/λ = R_H(1/n_f² - 1/n_i²); predicts hydrogen spectral line wavelengths; R_H ≈ 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹
  • Continuous spectrum: Produced by hot dense matter (solids, liquids, dense gases); all wavelengths present; intensity varies with temperature (blackbody)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Stellar composition: absorption lines in starlight reveal which elements are in the star's atmosphere; helium was first discovered in the Sun's spectrum before being found on Earth
  • Gas identification: each gas produces unique spectral lines—used to identify unknown gases in chemistry labs, environmental monitoring, and forensics
  • LED color selection: the semiconductor band gap determines the emitted photon wavelength; engineers tune the gap to produce specific colors (red, blue, green, white)
