AP Physics 2 - Unit 6 - Lesson 6.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Photons and Quantum Mechanics introduces the quantized nature of light and energy. Planck proposed that energy is emitted/absorbed in discrete quanta: E = nhf. Einstein explained the photoelectric effect: photons with E = hf eject electrons only if hf ≥ φ (work function); the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons is KE_max = hf - φ. The photon model explains why increasing intensity (more photons) increases current but not electron energy, while increasing frequency increases individual electron energy. De Broglie extended wave-particle duality: all matter has a wavelength λ = h/p.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Photoelectric effect: Light ejects electrons only if hf ≥ φ (threshold frequency); KE_max = hf - φ; explained by photon model, not wave model
  • Photon: A quantum of electromagnetic radiation; energy E = hf; momentum p = h/λ = hf/c; no rest mass
  • Work function (φ): The minimum energy needed to eject an electron from a material's surface; material-dependent; determines threshold frequency f₀ = φ/h
  • De Broglie wavelength: λ = h/p = h/(mv); all particles have wave-like properties; significant only for very small masses (electrons, atoms, not baseballs)
  • Wave-particle duality: Particles exhibit wave behavior (diffraction, interference) and waves exhibit particle behavior (photoelectric effect); fundamental principle of quantum mechanics

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Solar cells convert photon energy to electrical energy using the photoelectric effect: photons above the bandgap energy create electron-hole pairs that generate current
  • Electron diffraction microscopy: electrons with de Broglie wavelength ~0.01 nm reveal atomic-scale structures in crystals—confirming matter's wave nature
  • Night vision devices use the photoelectric effect: photons from dim scenes eject electrons from a photocathode, which are then amplified to produce a visible image
