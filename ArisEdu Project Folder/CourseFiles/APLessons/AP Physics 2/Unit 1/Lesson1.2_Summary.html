AP Physics 2 - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Waves are disturbances that transfer energy without transferring matter. Mechanical waves (sound, water, seismic) require a medium; electromagnetic waves (light, radio) do not. Key properties: wavelength (λ), frequency (f), amplitude (A), and wave speed (v = fλ). Transverse waves oscillate perpendicular to propagation (light, string waves); longitudinal waves oscillate parallel (sound). Superposition: when waves overlap, their displacements add algebraically, producing constructive or destructive interference.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Wave equation: v = fλ; wave speed equals frequency times wavelength; speed depends on the medium, not the wave itself
  • Transverse wave: Oscillation is perpendicular to the direction of propagation; examples: light waves, waves on a string, S-waves
  • Longitudinal wave: Oscillation is parallel to the direction of propagation; compressions and rarefactions; examples: sound waves, P-waves
  • Superposition principle: When two waves meet, the resultant displacement is the sum of individual displacements; leads to interference patterns
  • Standing waves: Formed by superposition of two identical waves traveling in opposite directions; nodes (zero displacement) and antinodes (maximum displacement)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Seismology: P-waves (longitudinal) and S-waves (transverse) travel through Earth at different speeds, allowing scientists to locate earthquake epicenters and map Earth's interior structure
  • Musical instruments: standing waves on strings and in air columns produce specific frequencies (harmonics); the length, tension, and density determine the pitch
  • Ultrasound imaging: high-frequency sound waves reflect off internal structures; the time delay and intensity of reflected waves produce medical images of organs and fetuses
