AP Physics 2 - Unit 1 - Lesson 1.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Entropy and the Second Law of Thermodynamics governs the direction of natural processes. Entropy (S) quantifies disorder: ΔS ≥ 0 for any isolated system. The second law states that natural processes increase the total entropy of the universe; heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never the reverse, without external work. Heat engines convert thermal energy to work with efficiency η = W/Q_H = 1 - Q_C/Q_H. The Carnot efficiency η_Carnot = 1 - T_C/T_H sets the theoretical maximum efficiency.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Entropy: A measure of disorder or the number of microstates: S = k ln Ω; always increases in irreversible processes
  • Second Law of Thermodynamics: The total entropy of an isolated system never decreases; natural processes are irreversible
  • Heat engine efficiency: η = W/Q_H = 1 - Q_C/Q_H; no engine can convert all heat to work (η < 1 always)
  • Carnot efficiency: η_Carnot = 1 - T_C/T_H; the maximum possible efficiency for an engine operating between temperatures T_H and T_C (in Kelvin)
  • Reversible vs. irreversible: Reversible: idealized, infinitely slow, maximum work; irreversible: real, finite speed, always produces less work and more entropy

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Power plants: coal and nuclear plants operate as heat engines; even the best achieve ~40% efficiency due to thermodynamic limits (Carnot bound)
  • Refrigerators work as reverse heat engines, moving heat from cold interior to warm exterior—they require work input, consistent with the second law
  • Arrow of time: entropy increase explains why ice melts in warm rooms but warm rooms don't spontaneously freeze—the second law governs the direction of all natural processes
