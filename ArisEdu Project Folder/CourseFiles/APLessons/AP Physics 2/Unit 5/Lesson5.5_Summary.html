AP Physics 2 - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Light as a Wave examines the wave nature of light in detail. Electromagnetic waves consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. Polarization proves light is a transverse wave. Malus's law gives the intensity of polarized light through an analyzer: I = I₀ cos²θ. Brewster's angle: reflected light is completely polarized when tan θ_B = n₂/n₁. Light exhibits wave-particle duality, behaving as a wave in interference/diffraction experiments and as particles (photons) in the photoelectric effect.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Electromagnetic wave structure: Oscillating E and B fields, perpendicular to each other and to the propagation direction; E × B points in the direction of travel
  • Polarization: Restricting E field oscillations to one direction; only possible for transverse waves; confirms light's transverse nature
  • Malus's law: I = I₀ cos²θ; intensity of polarized light transmitted through an analyzer at angle θ to the polarization direction
  • Brewster's angle: θ_B = arctan(n₂/n₁); at this angle of incidence, reflected light is 100% polarized; reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular
  • Wave-particle duality: Light behaves as waves (interference, diffraction) and particles (photoelectric effect, Compton scattering); complementary descriptions

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • 3D movies use two differently polarized images projected simultaneously; polarized glasses filter one image to each eye, creating stereoscopic depth perception
  • LCD displays: liquid crystal pixels rotate polarized light by varying amounts controlled by voltage; combined with polarizing filters, this modulates light transmission to create images
  • Photoelectric solar cells: photon energy (E = hf) must exceed the work function of the material to free electrons and generate current—the particle nature of light in action
