AP Physics 2 - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Electromagnetic Spectrum encompasses all electromagnetic radiation, from radio waves (λ ~ km) to gamma rays (λ ~ pm). All EM waves travel at c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum and satisfy c = fλ. The spectrum includes (increasing frequency): radio, microwave, infrared, visible (red 700nm to violet 400nm), ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma ray. EM waves are produced by accelerating charges and carry energy: E = hf (photon energy). The intensity and spectrum of thermal radiation depend on temperature (Wien's law: λ_max = b/T; Stefan-Boltzmann: P = σAT⁴).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • EM wave speed: c = fλ = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum; all EM waves travel at the same speed regardless of frequency
  • Visible spectrum: Red (~700 nm) to violet (~400 nm); the narrow band detectable by human eyes; different wavelengths = different colors
  • Photon energy: E = hf = hc/λ; h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (Planck's constant); higher frequency = higher energy per photon
  • Wien's displacement law: λ_max = b/T; b ≈ 2.898 × 10⁻³ m·K; hotter objects emit peak radiation at shorter wavelengths (bluer light)
  • Stefan-Boltzmann law: P = εσAT⁴; total power radiated by a body; P ∝ T⁴ means doubling temperature increases radiation 16-fold

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Microwave ovens: 2.45 GHz radiation excites water molecule rotations, efficiently heating food while being absorbed less by plastics and ceramics
  • Thermal imaging cameras detect infrared radiation emitted by objects: used by firefighters to see through smoke and by engineers to find heat leaks in buildings
  • Medical X-rays: high-energy photons penetrate soft tissue but are absorbed by dense bone, creating shadow images for diagnosing fractures and other conditions
