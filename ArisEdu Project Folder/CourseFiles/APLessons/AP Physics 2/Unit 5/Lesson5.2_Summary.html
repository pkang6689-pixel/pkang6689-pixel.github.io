AP Physics 2 - Unit 5 - Lesson 5.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Sound and Acoustics analyzes sound wave behavior in detail. Standing waves in tubes: open tube has antinodes at both ends (harmonics: f_n = nv/2L); closed tube has a node at the closed end and antinode at the open end (odd harmonics only: f_n = nv/4L, n = 1,3,5,...). Sound intensity follows the inverse-square law: I = P/(4πr²). The speed of sound depends on the medium's elastic and inertial properties: v = √(B/ρ) for fluids and v ≈ 331 + 0.6T m/s in air (T in °C).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Open tube harmonics: Both ends are antinodes: f_n = nv/(2L), n = 1,2,3,...; all harmonics present; e.g., flute, open organ pipe
  • Closed tube harmonics: Node at closed end, antinode at open: f_n = nv/(4L), n = 1,3,5,... (odd harmonics only); e.g., clarinet, stopped pipe
  • Inverse-square law: I = P/(4πr²); doubling the distance reduces intensity by a factor of 4; explains why sound fades rapidly with distance
  • Resonance in tubes: When a driving frequency matches a natural frequency of the tube, standing waves form and the sound is amplified dramatically
  • Sound level addition: Two identical sources: I_total = 2I → increase of 10 log₁₀(2) ≈ 3 dB; doubling the number of sources adds ≈3 dB

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Concert hall acoustics: architects design room dimensions, wall shapes, and absorption materials to optimize reverberation time (1.5-2.5 seconds ideal for orchestras)
  • Musical instrument design: the length and shape of brass instruments determine which harmonics are produced; a bugle plays only natural harmonics of its tube length
  • Ultrasound imaging: frequencies of 2-18 MHz (very short wavelength) provide high-resolution images of internal body structures while being safe (non-ionizing)
