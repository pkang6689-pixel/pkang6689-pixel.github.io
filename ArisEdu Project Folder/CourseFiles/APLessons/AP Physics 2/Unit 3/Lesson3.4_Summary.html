AP Physics 2 - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Magnetic Properties of Materials classifies materials by their response to external magnetic fields. Diamagnetic materials (copper, bismuth) are weakly repelled by fields—induced dipoles oppose the external field. Paramagnetic materials (aluminum, platinum) are weakly attracted—atomic dipoles partially align with the field. Ferromagnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt) are strongly attracted—magnetic domains align cooperatively, creating permanent magnets. Curie temperature: above this temperature, ferromagnets lose their permanent magnetism and become paramagnetic.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Diamagnetism: Weak repulsion from B fields; present in all materials; caused by induced atomic dipole moments opposing the applied field
  • Paramagnetism: Weak attraction to B fields; atoms have permanent magnetic dipoles that partially align with the field; effect disappears when field is removed
  • Ferromagnetism: Strong attraction and permanent magnetization; magnetic domains align cooperatively; responsible for permanent magnets
  • Magnetic domains: Microscopic regions within ferromagnets where all atomic dipoles are aligned; applying a field causes domains to grow or rotate
  • Curie temperature: The temperature above which a ferromagnet becomes paramagnetic; iron: 770°C; nickel: 358°C; irreversible heating can demagnetize

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Hard disk drives store data using ferromagnetic domains: a write head magnetizes tiny regions in specific orientations, representing binary 0s and 1s
  • MRI contrast agents: paramagnetic gadolinium compounds enhance MRI images by altering the magnetic relaxation times of nearby water molecules
  • Magnetic levitation: diamagnetic materials (like pyrolytic graphite) can levitate in strong magnetic fields—demonstrated in physics labs and proposed for frictionless bearings
