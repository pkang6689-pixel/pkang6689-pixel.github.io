AP Physics 2 - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.5 - Summary

Key Concepts

Alternating Current (AC) periodically reverses direction. AC voltage is described by V(t) = V₀ sin(ωt), where V₀ is peak voltage and ω = 2πf. The root-mean-square (RMS) values are V_rms = V₀/√2 and I_rms = I₀/√2, which give the equivalent DC values for power calculations: P_avg = I_rms · V_rms. Transformers use electromagnetic induction to change voltage levels: V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁. Step-up transformers increase voltage (for transmission), step-down transformers decrease it (for household use).

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • AC voltage: V(t) = V₀ sin(ωt); alternates between +V₀ and -V₀; ω = 2πf; US standard: 120V RMS at 60 Hz
  • RMS values: V_rms = V₀/√2 ≈ 0.707V₀; I_rms = I₀/√2; the effective DC-equivalent values for computing average power
  • Average power: P_avg = I_rms · V_rms = ½V₀I₀ for resistive loads; P = I²R uses I_rms for AC circuits
  • Transformer equation: V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁; ideal transformers conserve power: V₁I₁ = V₂I₂; stepping up voltage steps down current
  • Power transmission: High-voltage transmission minimizes I²R losses: P_loss = I²R = P²R/V²; higher V → lower I → much lower losses

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • The electrical grid: power plants generate at ~20 kV, transformers step up to 110-765 kV for long-distance transmission, then step down to 120/240V for homes—reducing losses by factors of thousands
  • Phone chargers contain transformers (and rectifiers) that convert 120V AC to 5-20V DC for safe device charging
  • Induction motors: AC current creates rotating magnetic fields that induce currents in the rotor, producing torque—powering most industrial machinery and household appliances
