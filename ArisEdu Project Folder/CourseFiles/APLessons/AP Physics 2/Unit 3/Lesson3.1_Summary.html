AP Physics 2 - Unit 3 - Lesson 3.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Magnetic Fields and Forces explores the interaction between magnetic fields and charged particles. A charged particle moving through a magnetic field follows a circular path: the magnetic force provides centripetal acceleration, so qvB = mv²/r, giving r = mv/(qB). This allows mass spectrometers to separate ions by mass-to-charge ratio. The force on a current loop creates torque: τ = nIAB sin θ, the basis for electric motors. Magnetic flux Φ_B = B·A·cos θ measures the amount of field passing through a surface.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Circular motion in B field: r = mv/(qB); radius of circular path depends on mass, speed, and charge-to-field ratio; the basis for particle accelerators
  • Mass spectrometer: Uses r = mv/(qB) to separate ions by mass: heavier ions curve less (larger r), lighter ions curve more (smaller r)
  • Torque on a current loop: τ = nIAB sin θ; n = number of turns, I = current, A = loop area, B = field strength, θ = angle between area vector and B
  • Magnetic flux: Φ_B = B·A·cos θ; measured in webers (Wb = T·m²); the 'amount' of magnetic field passing through a surface
  • Hall effect: A current-carrying conductor in a transverse B field develops a voltage perpendicular to both I and B; used to measure magnetic field strength

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Mass spectrometry in forensics and drug testing: separating and identifying molecules by mass from blood, urine, or environmental samples
  • Cyclotrons and synchrotrons accelerate charged particles using magnetic fields to bend their paths, used in cancer radiation therapy and particle physics research
  • Hall effect sensors are used as position detectors in smartphones, automotive systems (crankshaft position), and brushless DC motor commutation
