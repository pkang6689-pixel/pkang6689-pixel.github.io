AP Physics 2 - Unit 7 - Lesson 7.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Nuclear Physics explores the structure and properties of atomic nuclei. The nucleus contains protons (Z) and neutrons (N), collectively called nucleons (A = Z + N). The strong nuclear force binds nucleons together, overcoming electrostatic repulsion between protons. This force is short-range (~1 fm), extremely strong, and charge-independent. Nuclear stability depends on the neutron-to-proton ratio: light stable nuclei have N ≈ Z; heavier nuclei need more neutrons (N > Z). The nuclear binding energy represents the energy needed to disassemble the nucleus completely.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Nuclear notation: ᴬ_Z X: A = mass number (p + n), Z = atomic number (protons), N = A - Z = neutrons; isotopes differ in N
  • Strong nuclear force: Binds nucleons; range ≈ 1 fm; much stronger than electromagnetic force at nuclear distances; charge-independent (p-p = p-n = n-n)
  • Nuclear binding energy: Energy required to separate all nucleons; comes from mass defect via E = mc²; higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable
  • Mass defect: Δm = (Z·m_p + N·m_n) - m_nucleus; the 'missing mass' converted to binding energy; Δm > 0 for all stable nuclei
  • Nuclear stability: Stable nuclei lie along the valley of stability in the N vs. Z chart; too many or too few neutrons leads to radioactive decay

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Nuclear medicine: technetium-99m (a metastable nuclear isomer) is the most widely used medical radioisotope, emitting gamma rays for diagnostic imaging of organs
  • Carbon dating: the ratio of ¹⁴C to ¹²C in organic material decreases over time due to ¹⁴C decay; measuring this ratio determines the age of archaeological artifacts
  • Smoke detectors: americium-241 emits alpha particles that ionize air, creating a current; smoke disrupts the current, triggering the alarm
