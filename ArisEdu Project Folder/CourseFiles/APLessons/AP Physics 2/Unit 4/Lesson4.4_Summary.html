AP Physics 2 - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.4 - Summary

Key Concepts

Optical Instruments combine lenses and mirrors to extend human vision. Compound microscopes use two converging lenses: the objective (short f) creates a magnified real image, and the eyepiece (longer f) further magnifies this as a virtual image. Telescopes use an objective lens/mirror to collect light from distant objects and an eyepiece to magnify the image. Total magnification = M_objective × M_eyepiece. Aberrations (chromatic, spherical) limit image quality and are corrected using compound lens systems.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Compound microscope: Two converging lenses (objective + eyepiece); total magnification M = m_objective × M_eyepiece; enlarges nearby small objects
  • Refracting telescope: Objective lens collects light from distant objects; eyepiece magnifies; M = f_objective / f_eyepiece; image is inverted
  • Reflecting telescope: Uses a concave mirror as objective; avoids chromatic aberration; Newtonian, Cassegrain, and Schmidt-Cassegrain designs
  • Chromatic aberration: Different wavelengths focus at different points (dispersion in lenses); corrected by achromatic doublets (crown + flint glass)
  • Angular magnification: M = θ_image / θ_object; how much larger objects appear through the instrument compared to the naked eye

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Electron microscopes achieve resolutions of ~0.1 nm by using electron 'lenses' (magnetic fields) instead of optical lenses—visualizing individual atoms
  • The James Webb Space Telescope uses a 6.5m segmented gold-coated mirror to collect infrared light from the earliest galaxies in the universe
  • Endoscopes use fiber optic bundles and tiny lens systems to allow doctors to view inside the body without surgery—used in colonoscopy, arthroscopy, and laparoscopy
