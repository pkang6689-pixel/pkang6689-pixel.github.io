AP Physics 2 - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.2 - Summary

Key Concepts

Refraction and Snell's Law describes how light bends when crossing boundaries between media of different optical densities. Snell's law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂, where n is the index of refraction (n = c/v). Light bends toward the normal when entering a denser medium (higher n) and away when entering a less dense medium. Total internal reflection occurs when light hits the boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle: θ_c = arcsin(n₂/n₁), and only when going from high-n to low-n medium.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Index of refraction: n = c/v; the ratio of light speed in vacuum to light speed in the medium; n > 1 for all materials (n = 1.0 for vacuum, 1.33 for water, 1.5 for glass)
  • Snell's law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂; relates angles of incidence and refraction to the indices of refraction of two media
  • Total internal reflection: Occurs when light travels from high-n to low-n medium at θ > θ_c = arcsin(n₂/n₁); all light is reflected, none transmitted
  • Dispersion: Different wavelengths refract by different amounts (n varies with λ); produces spectrum separation in prisms and rainbows
  • Apparent depth: Objects underwater appear shallower than they are due to refraction: apparent depth = actual depth × (n₂/n₁)

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Fiber optics: light pulses travel through thin glass fibers via total internal reflection, carrying internet data at near-light speed over thousands of kilometers
  • Rainbows: sunlight refracts upon entering water droplets, reflects internally, and refracts again upon exiting—dispersion separates colors at different angles
  • Eyeglasses and contact lenses correct vision by refracting light to focus images correctly on the retina, compensating for the eye's refractive errors
