AP Physics 2 - Unit 4 - Lesson 4.1 - Summary

Key Concepts

Reflection and Mirrors covers how light bounces off surfaces. The law of reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection (θᵢ = θᵣ), measured from the normal. Plane mirrors produce virtual, upright, same-size images located behind the mirror. Concave mirrors (converging) can produce real or virtual images depending on object distance: objects beyond the focal point produce inverted real images; objects inside the focal point produce upright virtual images. Convex mirrors (diverging) always produce virtual, upright, reduced images.

Key Concepts and Vocabulary

Essential concepts in this lesson include:

  • Law of reflection: θ_incident = θ_reflected; both angles measured from the normal to the surface
  • Concave mirror: Converging mirror: f > 0; produces real images when object is beyond F; virtual images when object is inside F
  • Convex mirror: Diverging mirror: f < 0; always produces virtual, upright, reduced images; field of view is wider than flat mirrors
  • Mirror equation: 1/d_o + 1/d_i = 1/f; d_o = object distance, d_i = image distance, f = focal length; sign conventions determine image type
  • Magnification: m = -d_i/d_o = h_i/h_o; m > 0: upright image; m < 0: inverted; |m| > 1: enlarged; |m| < 1: reduced

Real-World Applications and Examples

This topic has significant real-world importance:

  • Car side mirrors are convex ('objects in mirror are closer than they appear') to provide a wider field of view, reducing blind spots
  • Telescopes use concave mirrors to collect and focus starlight: the Hubble Space Telescope has a 2.4-meter primary concave mirror
  • Solar concentrators use parabolic concave mirrors to focus sunlight to a point, generating temperatures exceeding 3000°C for solar thermal power
