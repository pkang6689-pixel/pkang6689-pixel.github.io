Lesson 3.6: Case Studies in Marine Geology - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies in Marine Geology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a 'megathrust earthquake'?

A megathrust earthquake occurs along the interface (megathrust fault) between the subducting and overriding plates at a convergent boundary. These are the most powerful earthquakes on Earth, capable of exceeding magnitude 9.0. They can cause massive tsunamis by vertically displacing the ocean floor.

What is a 'mantle plume' in the context of hotspot geology?

A mantle plume is a column of exceptionally hot rock that rises from deep within the Earth's mantle, possibly from near the core-mantle boundary. When a plume reaches the base of the lithosphere, it causes melting and volcanic activity, creating hotspot volcanoes like those forming the Hawaiian Island chain.

What is the geological term for thick salt deposits formed by evaporation, like those found under the Mediterranean?

Evaporites are chemical sedimentary deposits formed when bodies of water evaporate, leaving behind dissolved minerals like halite (rock salt) and gypsum. The Mediterranean evaporite deposits from the Messinian Salinity Crisis are among the largest on Earth, with salt layers up to 3 km thick in places.

What is 'offshore drilling' in marine geology?

Offshore drilling involves using specialized ships, platforms, or rigs to drill wells into the ocean floor. This technology is used both commercially (to extract oil and natural gas) and scientifically (such as the Ocean Drilling Program) to recover sediment and rock cores for geological research.

What is a 'shield volcano'?

A shield volcano is a broad, dome-shaped volcano with gently sloping sides, built mainly by the flow of low-viscosity basaltic lava. Hawaiian volcanoes like Mauna Loa are classic examples of shield volcanoes, which are the largest volcanoes on Earth by volume.

What is the 'Zanclean flood' in the context of Mediterranean geology?

The Zanclean flood (about 5.33 million years ago) was the dramatic refilling of the Mediterranean basin when the Strait of Gibraltar reopened, ending the Messinian Salinity Crisis. Atlantic waters may have poured in as a massive waterfall, refilling the basin in as little as a few months to a few thousand years.

During the Messinian Salinity Crisis, the Mediterranean was cut off from the Atlantic. What tectonic process closed the connection at what is now the Strait of Gibraltar?

The closure of the Strait of Gibraltar was caused by the convergence of the African and Eurasian tectonic plates, which uplifted the land between the Atlantic and Mediterranean basins. This tectonic collision also formed mountain ranges in southern Europe and North Africa.

A team of scientists compares the ages of seamounts in the Hawaiian-Emperor chain using radiometric dating. They find that seamounts increase in age by approximately 8.6 million years for every 1,000 km of distance from the current hotspot. What is the approximate rate of Pacific Plate movement over the hotspot?

Rate = distance / time = 1,000 km / 8.6 million years = 116.3 km per million years ≈ 11.6 cm per year. This calculation demonstrates how hotspot tracks can be used to determine the speed and direction of tectonic plate motion over geological time.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami affected coastlines in 14 countries around the Indian Ocean, including Indonesia, Sri Lanka, India, Thailand, and Somalia. It traveled across the entire Indian Ocean basin, with waves reaching as far as East Africa, demonstrating the immense energy released by the underwater earthquake.

Real-World Applications

Example: A geologist studying ice cores and Mediterranean sediment cores finds evidence that the Mediterranean evaporated and refilled multiple times during the Messinian Salinity Crisis. What would be the geological evidence for these cycles?

Multiple evaporation-refilling cycles would produce alternating layers of evaporite minerals (deposited during evaporation) and normal marine sediments (deposited when the sea refilled). This cyclical pattern in the sediment record is exactly what geologists observe in Mediterranean drill cores.
