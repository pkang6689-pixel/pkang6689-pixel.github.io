Lesson 5.3: Energy Flow in Marine Systems - Summary

Key Concepts: Energy Flow in Marine Systems

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary source of energy that drives most marine ecosystems?

Sunlight is the primary energy source for most marine ecosystems, driving photosynthesis by phytoplankton and other marine plants. Solar energy is converted into chemical energy that flows through the food web. The exception is deep-sea hydrothermal vent communities, which rely on chemical energy through chemosynthesis.

What is "gross primary productivity" (GPP)?

Gross primary productivity (GPP) is the total rate of photosynthesis, representing all the organic matter (energy) produced by autotrophs in a given area over a given time. It includes the energy used by the producers themselves for their own cellular respiration. GPP minus the producers' respiration equals net primary productivity.

What is "net primary productivity" (NPP)?

Net primary productivity (NPP) equals gross primary productivity minus the energy producers use for their own cellular respiration (GPP - R = NPP). NPP represents the energy actually available to consumers in the food web. It is the organic matter that accumulates as plant growth and can be consumed by herbivores.

What is the "biological pump" in the ocean?

The biological pump is the process by which carbon fixed by photosynthesis in surface waters is transported to the deep ocean through sinking organic particles, fecal pellets, and the vertical migration of organisms. It plays a crucial role in the global carbon cycle by sequestering carbon in deep water for hundreds to thousands of years.

What is an "energy budget" for a marine organism?

An energy budget describes how an organism partitions its ingested energy among different life functions: respiration (metabolic maintenance), growth (new tissue), reproduction (gametes, offspring), and excretion (waste). Understanding energy budgets helps ecologists predict how much energy passes to the next trophic level versus how much is lost.

What is "new production" in ocean productivity?

New production refers to primary productivity that is driven by nutrients newly introduced into the photic zone from external sources like upwelling, rivers, or atmospheric deposition. It contrasts with "regenerated production," which uses nutrients recycled within the photic zone. New production is especially important because it represents the net addition of energy and carbon to the surface ecosystem.

What is a "dead zone" in the ocean?

Dead zones (hypoxic zones) are areas in the ocean where dissolved oxygen levels drop so low that most marine organisms cannot survive. They typically result from eutrophication, where excess nutrients cause algal blooms that consume oxygen as they decompose. The Gulf of Mexico dead zone, caused by agricultural runoff from the Mississippi River, is one of the most well-known examples.

A marine ecologist measures that a patch of ocean phytoplankton produces 5,000 kcal of energy per day. She estimates the phytoplankton use 2,000 kcal for their own respiration. What is the net primary productivity available to consumers?

Net primary productivity equals gross primary productivity minus the producers' own respiration: 5,000 - 2,000 = 3,000 kcal per day. This 3,000 kcal is the energy available for herbivores and the rest of the food web. Understanding this calculation is essential for estimating how much life an ecosystem can support.

Two ocean regions have equal rates of primary productivity, but Region A has much lower standing crop of phytoplankton than Region B. What is the most likely explanation?

When productivity is equal but standing crop differs, the difference is due to consumption rates. In Region A, zooplankton and other grazers consume phytoplankton nearly as fast as they are produced, keeping the standing crop low despite high productivity. This illustrates the important distinction between productivity (a rate) and standing crop (an amount at one time).

Real-World Applications

Example: What percentage of energy is typically transferred from one trophic level to the next?

On average, only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed to the next level, known as the 10% rule. The remaining 90% is lost as heat through cellular respiration and metabolic processes. This fundamental principle of ecology limits the number of trophic levels an ecosystem can support.

Example: A marine sanctuary manager notices that a kelp forest supports many more species and higher biomass than the adjacent open water. Using energy flow principles, what best explains this difference?

Kelp forests are highly productive ecosystems where the kelp itself is a major producer, adding substantial energy to the base of the food web beyond what phytoplankton alone provide. The structural complexity of kelp also creates diverse microhabitats, allowing more species and trophic interactions. More energy and more habitat structure together support greater biodiversity and biomass.
