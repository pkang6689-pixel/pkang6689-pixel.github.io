Lesson 5.5: Biodiversity & Conservation - Summary

Key Concepts: Biodiversity & Conservation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary energy source for communities living near deep-sea hydrothermal vents?

Deep-sea hydrothermal vent communities are powered by chemosynthetic bacteria that convert hydrogen sulfide and other chemicals from the vents into energy. This process is fundamentally different from photosynthesis and allows life to thrive without sunlight.

What is endemism in the context of marine biology?

Endemism refers to a species being native and restricted to a particular geographic location. Endemic marine species are found nowhere else on Earth, which makes them especially vulnerable to local threats like habitat destruction or pollution.

What is a biodiversity hotspot?

A biodiversity hotspot is a biogeographic region that contains exceptionally high levels of species diversity (especially endemic species) and is experiencing significant habitat loss. The Coral Triangle in Southeast Asia is one of the most recognized marine biodiversity hotspots.

What is the Shannon Diversity Index used to measure?

The Shannon Diversity Index (H') quantifies species diversity by accounting for both the number of species (richness) and how evenly individuals are distributed among those species (evenness). A higher H' value indicates greater diversity in the community.

What is a keystone species?

A keystone species has an outsized influence on its ecosystem relative to its population size. Removing a keystone species causes dramatic changes in ecosystem structure and function. The concept was first described by Robert Paine through his studies of the sea star Pisaster ochraceus.

What is captive breeding in the context of marine conservation?

Captive breeding programs raise endangered species in controlled environments like aquariums or hatcheries, with the goal of increasing population numbers and eventually reintroducing individuals into the wild. This strategy has been used for species like white abalone and some coral species.

What is habitat restoration in marine conservation?

Habitat restoration involves actively assisting the recovery of a degraded, damaged, or destroyed ecosystem. In marine environments, this can include replanting mangroves, transplanting corals, restoring oyster reefs, and removing sources of pollution.

What is a no-take zone in the context of Marine Protected Areas?

A no-take zone is the strictest type of Marine Protected Area, where all extractive activities—including fishing, mining, and collecting—are prohibited. Research shows that no-take zones are the most effective type of MPA for increasing fish biomass and biodiversity.

Real-World Applications

Example: Which of the following is an example of a keystone species in a marine ecosystem?

Sea otters are a classic example of a marine keystone species. By preying on sea urchins, they prevent urchin populations from overgrazing kelp forests. Without sea otters, sea urchin populations explode and create barren ocean floors devoid of kelp.

Example: A conservation agency is deciding where to establish a new Marine Protected Area. They have data showing Region X has 200 endemic species and Region Y has 500 total species but only 10 endemics. Focusing solely on protecting species found nowhere else, which region should receive priority?

Endemic species exist nowhere else, so if their habitat is lost, they go extinct globally. Region X with 200 endemics represents far greater irreplaceable biodiversity than Region Y's 10 endemics. Conservation prioritization often emphasizes endemism because those species cannot be protected elsewhere.

Example: An island nation discovers that a cargo ship has accidentally introduced a non-native species of green crab to its harbor. The green crabs are rapidly consuming native shellfish. Which management strategy would most directly address this invasive species threat?

Invasive species management requires both controlling the existing population (through trapping and removal) and preventing future introductions (through ballast water management regulations). Ballast water from ships is one of the primary vectors for marine invasive species worldwide.

Example: A coastal community wants to restore a degraded mangrove forest to protect their shoreline and increase marine biodiversity. Which approach would best maximize both goals?

Effective mangrove restoration uses native species and restores natural hydrological conditions. Mangroves provide critical nursery habitat for fish and crustaceans, buffer shorelines from storms, and sequester carbon. Using native species ensures the restoration supports local biodiversity rather than introducing potential invasives.

Example: A government proposes opening a previously protected coral reef area to limited fishing to support local livelihoods. Environmental scientists argue that any fishing will reduce biodiversity. Using conservation principles, what compromise strategy could balance both economic and ecological goals?

Zoned management is a widely used conservation compromise. A core no-take zone preserves the most biodiverse area and acts as a source of spillover for surrounding waters, while buffer zones with regulated fishing provide economic benefits. This approach balances conservation with sustainable use.
