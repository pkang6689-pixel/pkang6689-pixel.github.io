Lesson 2.4: Coastal Processes (Erosion, Deposition) - Summary

Key Concepts: Coastal Processes (Erosion, Deposition)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary agent of coastal erosion?

Wave action is the primary agent of coastal erosion. Waves carry enormous energy that breaks apart rock and sediment through hydraulic action, abrasion, and chemical weathering. Over time, this relentless wave energy reshapes coastlines significantly.

What is a barrier island?

Barrier islands are long, narrow sandy islands that run parallel to the mainland coastline, separated from it by a lagoon or bay. They form through wave deposition and longshore drift and serve as natural buffers that protect the mainland from storm waves and erosion.

What is a sea arch?

A sea arch forms when wave erosion cuts through a narrow headland from both sides, creating a natural rock bridge. Waves exploit weaknesses such as cracks and joints in the rock. When the top of the arch eventually collapses, the remaining pillar is called a sea stack.

What is beach nourishment?

Beach nourishment is the process of artificially adding sand (usually dredged from offshore or transported from elsewhere) to an eroded beach to restore its width and volume. It is a common "soft" engineering approach to combat coastal erosion and protect shoreline property.

What is a spit in coastal geography?

A spit is an elongated deposit of sand or gravel that extends from the shore into the water, often across the mouth of a bay or inlet. Spits form by longshore drift depositing sediment beyond the coastline where the shore changes direction.

What is the difference between erosion and deposition along a coast?

Erosion is the wearing away and removal of rock and sediment from a location by waves, currents, and wind. Deposition is the process by which transported sediment is dropped or settled in a new location when energy decreases. They are complementary processes that together shape coastlines.

What is a "wave-cut platform"?

A wave-cut platform is a gently sloping, flat rock surface at the base of an eroding cliff. As waves undercut the cliff base, the cliff face retreats, leaving behind the platform. This feature widens over time as the cliff continues to erode landward.

What is a "sea stack"?

A sea stack is a vertical column of rock left standing in the ocean after a sea arch collapses. Sea stacks represent the last remnants of a headland that has been progressively eroded by waves. They will eventually be eroded down to a stump and finally disappear.

What is a "groin" (groyne) in coastal engineering?

A groin (or groyne) is a hard engineering structure built perpendicular to the shoreline, extending into the water. It interrupts longshore drift and traps sand on its updrift side, building up the beach in that area. However, groins often cause increased erosion on the downdrift side.

What is a "jetty" in the context of coastal structures?

A jetty is a structure (usually made of rock or concrete) built at the sides of a harbor entrance, river mouth, or inlet to prevent sediment from filling the navigation channel. Jetties direct outflowing water and interrupt longshore drift to maintain a clear passage for boats.

What is a "tombolo"?

A tombolo is a sand or gravel bar that forms between an island and the mainland (or between two islands), connecting them. It forms through wave refraction and deposition in the wave shadow behind the island, where reduced wave energy allows sediment to accumulate.

What is a "rip current"?

A rip current is a narrow, fast-moving channel of water that flows from the beach back out to sea through the surf zone. Rip currents form when water pushed ashore by waves finds a channel to flow back out. They are the leading cause of drowning at beaches and can be very dangerous for swimmers.

A coastal community notices that since a dam was built 50 km upriver, their beach has been eroding much faster. What is the connection between the dam and the beach erosion?

Rivers are a major source of sand and sediment for coastal beaches. When a dam is built, it traps sediment behind it, dramatically reducing the supply reaching the coast. Without this natural replenishment, waves continue to erode the beach but less new sand arrives to replace it, accelerating net erosion.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Rocky cliffs made of hard igneous rock like granite are much more resistant to erosion than soft sedimentary coastlines. Granite is extremely hard and chemically resistant, so it erodes very slowly compared to softer materials like sandstone, clay, or unconsolidated sand.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is the primary agent of coastal erosion?

Wave action is the primary agent of coastal erosion. Waves carry enormous energy that breaks apart rock and sediment through hydraulic action, abrasion, and chemical weathering. Over time, this relentless wave energy reshapes coastlines significantly.

Example: After a major hurricane, a barrier island has been breached by a new inlet. A government agency must decide whether to close the inlet or leave it open. What ecological argument supports leaving it open?

New tidal inlets allow seawater exchange between the open ocean and the lagoon behind a barrier island. This tidal flushing improves water quality, brings in nutrients and oxygen, and helps maintain healthy estuarine habitats. These ecological benefits must be weighed against human development concerns.
