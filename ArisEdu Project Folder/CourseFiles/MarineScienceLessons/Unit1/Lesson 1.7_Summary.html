Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Scientific Method in Marine Biology - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Scientific Method in Marine Biology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the first step of the scientific method?

The scientific method begins with making an observation about the natural world. From that observation, a scientist develops a question that leads to further investigation. Without an initial observation, there would be no basis for inquiry.

In an experiment testing how salinity affects sea urchin development, what is the independent variable?

The independent variable is the factor that the researcher deliberately changes or manipulates. In this experiment, the scientist is adjusting salinity levels to observe the effect on sea urchin development. The dependent variable would be the rate of sea urchin development.

What is the purpose of peer review in the scientific process?

Peer review is a critical quality-control step in which independent experts evaluate a study's methodology, analysis, and conclusions before publication. It helps identify errors, biases, and unsupported claims. This process strengthens the reliability and credibility of scientific literature.

What is a hypothesis?

A hypothesis is a testable and falsifiable explanation for a phenomenon, based on prior knowledge and observation. It is not a random guess but an educated prediction that can be investigated through experimentation. Hypotheses guide the design of experiments.

What is a controlled variable (constant)?

Controlled variables, also called constants, are factors that are deliberately kept the same across all groups in an experiment to ensure a fair test. They prevent confounding variables from affecting the results. For example, keeping water temperature constant while testing the effect of salinity on organisms.

What is a scientific theory?

A scientific theory is a broad, well-tested explanation for a natural phenomenon, supported by extensive and repeated evidence. Unlike the everyday use of "theory" (meaning a guess), a scientific theory represents one of the highest levels of scientific understanding. Theories can be refined as new evidence emerges.

What is a transect in marine field research?

A transect is a sampling method commonly used in ecology and marine science where researchers record data along a fixed line or path. Scientists count or measure organisms at regular intervals along the transect to study distribution patterns and community structure in marine habitats like coral reefs or tide pools.

What is a quadrat used for in marine ecology?

A quadrat is a square frame (often 1m x 1m) placed on a substrate to define a specific area for counting and identifying organisms. It is widely used in intertidal zones, coral reefs, and seagrass beds to estimate population density and species diversity within a habitat.

What is the mark-recapture method used for in marine science?

The mark-recapture method involves capturing organisms, marking them in a harmless way, releasing them, and later recapturing a new sample. The ratio of marked to unmarked individuals in the second sample is used to estimate total population size. It is commonly used for fish, sea turtles, and other mobile marine species.

A marine scientist wants to test whether elevated CO2 levels cause decreased shell thickness in oysters. She sets up two tanks: one with normal seawater CO2 levels and one with elevated CO2. Both tanks have the same temperature, salinity, light, and food supply. She measures shell thickness after 8 weeks. What is the control group in this experiment?

The control group is the tank with normal CO2 levels because it is not exposed to the experimental treatment (elevated CO2). It serves as the baseline for comparison. The controlled variables are the factors kept the same across both tanks, such as temperature, salinity, light, and food.

A student is designing an experiment to test whether different light wavelengths affect photosynthesis rates in seagrass. She places seagrass samples under red, blue, green, and white light, keeping all other conditions identical. She measures oxygen production as an indicator of photosynthesis. What is the dependent variable?

The dependent variable is the factor being measured as a response to changes in the independent variable. Here, oxygen production is measured to determine how different light wavelengths (the independent variable) affect photosynthesis. Water temperature and seagrass species are controlled variables.

A marine ecologist uses the mark-recapture method to estimate a population of blue crabs in a bay. She captures and tags 200 crabs, then releases them. One week later, she captures 150 crabs and finds that 30 are tagged. What is the estimated population size?

Using the Lincoln-Petersen formula: N = (number first captured x number second captured) / number recaptured with marks. So N = (200 x 150) / 30 = 1,000. This method assumes that marked individuals mix randomly with the population and that no significant immigration, emigration, births, or deaths occur between sampling events.

A group of students designs an experiment to test whether fertilizer runoff affects algal growth in coastal waters. Group 1 uses natural seawater (control), Group 2 adds low fertilizer concentration, and Group 3 adds high fertilizer concentration. However, the students use different sized containers for each group, place them in different locations with varying light exposure, and use seawater collected on different days. What is the biggest flaw in their experimental design?

The most critical flaw is the failure to control variables. By using different container sizes, light conditions, and water sources across groups, any differences in algal growth could be caused by these confounding factors rather than the fertilizer treatment. A valid experiment requires that all conditions except the independent variable remain constant across groups.

Real-World Applications

Example: Which of the following is an example of quantitative data collected during a marine survey?

Quantitative data involves numerical measurements or counts that can be analyzed statistically. Counting 47 fish is a specific numerical measurement. The other options describe qualitative observations, which are descriptive and subjective rather than numerical.

Example: Researchers studying a kelp forest ecosystem observe that sea otter populations have declined, and at the same time sea urchin populations have exploded and kelp coverage is decreasing. A researcher hypothesizes that sea urchin overgrazing is destroying the kelp. Which type of study would best establish a causal relationship?

A controlled experiment with exclusion plots directly tests the cause-and-effect relationship between sea urchin grazing and kelp decline. By removing urchins from some plots and comparing kelp growth to unprotected plots, the researcher can isolate the effect of grazing. Observational studies alone can only show correlation.
