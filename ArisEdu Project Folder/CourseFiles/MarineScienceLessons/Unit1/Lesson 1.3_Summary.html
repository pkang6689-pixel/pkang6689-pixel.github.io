Lesson 1.3: Tools of Marine Science (Submersibles, Sonar, Satellites) - Summary

Key Concepts: Tools of Marine Science (Submersibles, Sonar, Satellites)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary advantage of using SCUBA gear for ocean research?

SCUBA allows scientists to directly observe and interact with marine organisms and habitats in shallow water, typically down to about 40 meters.

What is a major limitation of using satellites for ocean research?

While satellites can monitor vast areas of ocean, their sensors can only detect conditions at or near the surface. Deep-ocean data requires submersibles, floats, or other in-situ instruments.

What is an ROV?

ROV stands for Remotely Operated Vehicle. It is an unmanned underwater robot connected to a ship by cables, allowing operators to control it and view live video from the deep ocean.

What is 'echo sounding'?

Echo sounding is a sonar technique that sends a pulse of sound downward from a ship and measures how long it takes for the echo to return. The travel time is used to calculate water depth.

What is a 'submersible' in the context of ocean exploration?

A submersible is a small underwater vessel built to dive beneath the ocean surface. Submersibles may be human-occupied (HOVs), remotely operated (ROVs), or autonomous (AUVs).

What is the Argo float program?

The Argo program deploys thousands of autonomous floats worldwide. These floats cycle between the surface and 2,000 meters depth, collecting temperature and salinity data that is transmitted via satellite.

What is 'multibeam sonar'?

Multibeam sonar sends out many sound beams simultaneously in a fan-shaped pattern beneath a ship. This allows scientists to map a wide strip of the ocean floor in a single pass, creating detailed bathymetric maps.
