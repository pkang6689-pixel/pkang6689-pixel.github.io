Lesson 6.1: Sponges & Cnidarians - Summary

Key Concepts: Sponges & Cnidarians

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the lifestyle of an adult sponge?

Adult sponges are sessile, meaning they are permanently attached to a surface and do not move. They spend their entire adult lives anchored to rocks, coral, or the ocean floor.

What is a nematocyst?

A nematocyst is the stinging organelle housed within a cnidocyte. It contains a coiled, barbed thread that rapidly uncoils upon contact, piercing prey or threats and delivering venom. It is one of the fastest biological mechanisms known.

What is spongin?

Spongin is a modified collagen protein that forms a flexible, fibrous skeleton in many sponge species. Bath sponges, for example, have skeletons made primarily of spongin, which gives them their soft and compressible texture.

What is the polyp body form in cnidarians?

The polyp is the sessile body form of cnidarians, shaped like a tube or cylinder with a mouth and tentacles pointing upward. Corals and sea anemones spend their adult lives in the polyp form.

What is coral bleaching?

Coral bleaching occurs when stressed corals expel their symbiotic zooxanthellae algae, causing the coral to turn white. Common stressors include elevated water temperatures, pollution, and ocean acidification. Without the algae, coral can starve and die.

A marine biologist observes that a reef organism is attached to a rock, has no organs or true tissues, and water flows through tiny pores in its body. What is this organism most likely?

The lack of true tissues and organs, sessile attachment, and porous body are all characteristic of sponges (Phylum Porifera). Unlike cnidarians, sponges have no specialized tissue layers and rely entirely on water flow through pores for feeding.

A diver notices that a coral reef has turned completely white after a summer of record-high ocean temperatures. What is the most likely explanation?

Elevated ocean temperatures cause coral to become stressed and expel their symbiotic zooxanthellae. Since the algae provide both nutrition and color, their loss turns the coral white—a process called coral bleaching. Prolonged bleaching can lead to coral death.

Real-World Applications

Example: A student is examining a preserved marine specimen and finds tiny, sharp, needle-like structures made of silica within its body. These structures most likely serve what function in which organism?

Silica-based needle-like structures are spicules, which provide structural support and protection for sponges. Different sponge species produce spicules of varying shapes and materials, including silica (glass) and calcium carbonate.

Example: Researchers discover a new deep-sea cnidarian species. In the lab, they observe it alternating between a stage that is attached to rocks and a stage that swims freely. What are these two stages called?

The attached stage is the polyp and the free-swimming stage is the medusa. Many cnidarians exhibit this alternation of generations, with the polyp reproducing asexually and the medusa reproducing sexually.
