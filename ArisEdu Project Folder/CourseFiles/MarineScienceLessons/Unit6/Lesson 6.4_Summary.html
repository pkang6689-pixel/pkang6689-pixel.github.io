Lesson 6.4: Echinoderms (Starfish, Sea Urchins) - Summary

Key Concepts: Echinoderms (Starfish, Sea Urchins)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the internal skeleton of echinoderms made of?

Echinoderms have an internal skeleton (endoskeleton) made of calcium carbonate plates called ossicles. In sea urchins, these plates are fused into a rigid shell called a test, while in sea stars they are more loosely connected.

What is the primary defense mechanism of sea urchins?

Sea urchins are covered in sharp, sometimes venomous spines that deter predators. The spines can move independently and are used for defense, locomotion, and wedging into crevices for protection.

What is the water vascular system?

The water vascular system is a unique hydraulic network found only in echinoderms. Seawater enters through the madreporite, flows through canals, and fills tube feet. By controlling water pressure, echinoderms extend and retract their tube feet for movement and feeding.

What is the madreporite?

The madreporite is a small, sieve-like plate on the aboral (top) surface of echinoderms that serves as the entrance to the water vascular system. It filters seawater as it enters the system, regulating water pressure for tube foot operation.

What is Aristotle's lantern?

Aristotle's lantern is the complex jaw structure found in sea urchins, consisting of five calcium carbonate teeth arranged in a circular pattern. Named by Aristotle, it is used to scrape algae from hard surfaces and can even erode rock.

What is pentaradial symmetry?

Pentaradial symmetry is a five-part radial body plan characteristic of adult echinoderms. The body is arranged around a central axis with five equal sections, which is why sea stars typically have five arms and sand dollars show a five-petaled pattern.

What is a 'test' in echinoderm terminology?

In echinoderm biology, a test is the rigid, globular or disc-shaped shell formed by fused ossicles (skeletal plates) in sea urchins and sand dollars. The test provides structural support and protection for internal organs.

A researcher finds a sea star that has four fully-grown arms and one very small arm. What is the most likely explanation?

The small arm is most likely a regenerating arm that was lost due to predation or injury. Sea stars can regenerate lost arms over weeks to months, and during regrowth the replacement arm is noticeably smaller than the original arms.

A marine ecology student observes that brittle stars are found in much higher densities on reef rubble than on living coral. What is the most likely reason?

Brittle stars are primarily nocturnal and cryptic, preferring to hide in crevices during the day. Reef rubble provides abundant interstitial spaces for concealment, which is why they are often found at higher densities in these complex microhabitats.

Real-World Applications

Example: A student places a sea star in an aquarium with several mussels. Over several hours, the sea star pries open a mussel, everts its stomach, and digests the mussel externally. What advantage does this external digestion provide?

External digestion allows sea stars to consume prey much larger than their small mouths by digesting the soft tissue inside the prey's shell directly. The liquefied nutrients are then absorbed by the everted stomach and retracted back into the body.
