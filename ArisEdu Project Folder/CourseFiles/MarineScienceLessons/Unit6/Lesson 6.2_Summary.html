Lesson 6.2: Mollusks (Clams, Squid, Octopus) - Summary

Key Concepts: Mollusks (Clams, Squid, Octopus)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary function of the mantle in mollusks?

The mantle is a thin tissue layer that covers the body of a mollusk. It secretes calcium carbonate to build and maintain the shell and also encloses the mantle cavity, which houses the gills.

What is the shell of a mollusk primarily composed of?

Mollusk shells are primarily made of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) secreted by the mantle. The shell typically has multiple layers, including an outer organic periostracum, a middle prismatic layer, and an inner nacreous (mother-of-pearl) layer.

What is the function of chromatophores in cephalopods?

Chromatophores are specialized pigment-containing cells in the skin of cephalopods. By expanding or contracting these cells through muscular action, cephalopods can rapidly change color and pattern for camouflage, signaling, and predator avoidance.

What is nacre?

Nacre, commonly known as mother-of-pearl, is the smooth, lustrous inner layer of many mollusk shells. It is composed of alternating layers of aragonite (a form of calcium carbonate) and an organic protein called conchiolin, which gives it its characteristic iridescence.

What is a siphon in the context of mollusk anatomy?

A siphon is a tube-like structure found in many mollusks. In bivalves, paired siphons direct water over the gills for filter feeding and gas exchange. In cephalopods, the funnel (hyponome) acts as a siphon to expel water for jet propulsion.

What is the visceral mass in a mollusk?

The visceral mass is one of the three main body regions of a mollusk. It is a concentrated area that houses the major internal organs including the heart, stomach, intestines, kidneys, and gonads, all covered by the mantle.

What is the periostracum of a mollusk shell?

The periostracum is the outermost layer of a mollusk shell, composed of a tough organic protein called conchiolin. It serves as a protective coating that helps prevent the calcium carbonate layers beneath from dissolving in acidic water.

A diver photographs a cuttlefish that rapidly changes from a sandy brown to bright red with pulsating patterns when approached. What is the most likely purpose of this color change?

Cuttlefish use chromatophores—pigment-filled sacs controlled by tiny muscles—to create rapid, dramatic color changes. When approached by a potential threat, they may display warning colors or startling patterns (deimatic displays) to deter predators. This is a neurologically controlled defense behavior.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Ocean acidification increases the concentration of hydrogen ions in seawater, which reacts with carbonate ions and reduces their availability. Since mollusks depend on calcium carbonate to build and repair their shells, lower carbonate saturation makes shell formation more energetically costly and can even dissolve existing shells.

Real-World Applications

Example: A student examines a gastropod shell and notices it has been heavily eroded on the outside while the inside remains smooth and shiny. Which layer of the shell is most responsible for resisting this internal erosion?

The nacreous (mother-of-pearl) layer is the innermost layer of the shell and is extremely smooth and resistant to abrasion. Its structure of stacked aragonite platelets bonded with organic protein makes it both strong and lustrous, providing long-lasting internal protection even when outer layers erode.
