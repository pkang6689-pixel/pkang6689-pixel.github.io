Lesson 7.7: AP Prep: Evolutionary Adaptations - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Evolutionary Adaptations

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary mechanism by which organisms with favorable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce in a given marine environment?

Natural selection is the process by which organisms with traits better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and pass those traits to offspring. In marine environments, selective pressures such as predation, temperature, and food availability drive this process.

Bioluminescence has evolved independently in many deep-sea organisms. What is the most likely selective advantage of this trait?

In the aphotic zone where no sunlight penetrates, bioluminescence provides critical survival advantages. Organisms use light production to lure prey (anglerfish), communicate with potential mates, startle or confuse predators, and provide counter-illumination camouflage.

What is the term for the evolutionary process in which one species closely resembles another species or its surroundings to gain a survival advantage?

Mimicry is an evolved resemblance between one organism and another organism or its environment. In marine settings, examples include the mimic octopus, which can imitate the appearance of lionfish, flatfish, and sea snakes to deter predators.

What is a phylogenetic tree used to represent in evolutionary biology?

A phylogenetic tree is a branching diagram that depicts the inferred evolutionary relationships among species or other taxa. Each branch point (node) represents a common ancestor, and the pattern of branching shows how lineages diverged over evolutionary time.

What is the term for the type of natural selection in which individuals with extreme forms of a trait have an advantage over those with intermediate forms?

Disruptive selection favors individuals at both extremes of a trait distribution while selecting against intermediate phenotypes. In marine environments, this can occur when a population occupies multiple distinct microhabitats, each favoring a different extreme of a trait such as body size or coloration.

Real-World Applications

Example: Sharks, dolphins, and ichthyosaurs all evolved streamlined body shapes independently. This is an example of which evolutionary process?

Convergent evolution occurs when unrelated organisms independently evolve similar traits due to similar environmental pressures. Sharks (fish), dolphins (mammals), and ichthyosaurs (reptiles) all developed streamlined bodies because this shape reduces drag and is advantageous for swimming efficiently.

Example: Which of the following is an example of a vestigial structure found in marine mammals?

Vestigial structures are remnants of organs that had a function in an ancestor but are no longer used for that purpose. Whales retain small pelvic bones embedded in muscle that once supported hind legs in their terrestrial ancestors, providing strong evidence of their evolutionary history.

Example: A species of reef fish is found in both shallow and deep zones of a coral reef. Over many generations, the shallow-water population develops bright coloring for territory defense, while the deep-water population becomes dull-colored for camouflage. If these populations can no longer interbreed, what has occurred?

Because both populations inhabit the same general reef environment without a physical barrier, this is an example of sympatric speciation. Ecological differences between the shallow and deep zones created different selective pressures, ultimately leading to reproductive isolation and the formation of two distinct species.

Example: A population of lanternfish in the mesopelagic zone produces bioluminescent light from ventral photophores. Researchers hypothesize that this trait evolved as counter-illumination camouflage. Which experimental finding would most strongly support this hypothesis?

Counter-illumination camouflage works by producing light on the ventral surface that matches the ambient downwelling light, eliminating the animal's silhouette when viewed from below. Finding that fish matching the light intensity experience lower predation directly supports the hypothesis that this trait evolved as an anti-predator adaptation.

Example: A team of geneticists sequences the genomes of five marine mammal species and finds that all possess a non-functional gene for hind limb development. In whales, this gene has multiple inactivating mutations, while in sea lions, the gene has fewer mutations but is still non-functional. What can be inferred from these data about the evolutionary timeline of these species?

Once a gene loses its function, it is no longer maintained by natural selection and accumulates neutral mutations over time. More inactivating mutations in the whale hind limb gene suggest that it became non-functional earlier in their evolutionary history, consistent with whales adopting a fully aquatic lifestyle before sea lions did.
