Key Definitions

What is metamorphosis in amphibians?

Metamorphosis is a biological process in which an amphibian undergoes a dramatic physical transformation, such as a tadpole developing legs, absorbing its tail, and developing lungs to become an adult frog. This dual life stage gives the class Amphibia its name.

What is cutaneous respiration?

Cutaneous respiration is the process of exchanging oxygen and carbon dioxide directly through the skin. Amphibians rely heavily on this method, which requires their skin to remain moist at all times for diffusion to occur.

What is osmoregulation?

Osmoregulation is the physiological process by which organisms control the concentration of water and salts in their body fluids. For amphibians, limited osmoregulatory capacity in saltwater is the primary barrier to colonizing marine habitats.

What is a salt gland?

Salt glands are specialized excretory organs found in marine reptiles and birds that remove excess sodium chloride from the blood and excrete it as a concentrated salt solution. Amphibians notably lack salt glands, contributing to their inability to live in saltwater.

What is the definition of 'brackish water'?

Brackish water has a salinity level between that of freshwater and seawater, typically found in estuaries where rivers meet the ocean. The crab-eating frog is notable for being able to tolerate brackish and even some saltwater conditions.

What is 'salinity tolerance'?

Salinity tolerance refers to the range of salt concentrations an organism can survive in. Most amphibians have very low salinity tolerance due to their permeable skin, which makes them unable to regulate body fluids effectively in salty water.

A student asks why sea turtles can live in the ocean but frogs cannot. What is the best explanation?

Sea turtles have evolved specialized salt glands near their eyes that excrete excess salt, and their skin is covered with keratinized, waterproof scales. Frogs lack both of these adaptations, so their permeable skin leads to fatal dehydration and salt overload in marine water.

An amphibian egg is laid in a tide pool that receives regular influxes of seawater. What is the most likely outcome for the developing embryo?

Amphibian eggs lack hard shells and are surrounded by a permeable jelly coating. Exposure to saltwater causes osmotic water loss from the developing embryo, leading to dehydration and cell damage that is typically fatal.
