Lesson 7.3: Ocean Currents & Circulation - Summary

Key Concepts: Ocean Currents & Circulation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a gyre?

A gyre is a large, circular system of ocean surface currents formed by the interaction of global wind patterns, the Coriolis effect, and continental landmasses. The five major gyres (North Atlantic, South Atlantic, North Pacific, South Pacific, and Indian Ocean) dominate ocean circulation.

What is the Coriolis effect?

The Coriolis effect is the apparent deflection of moving objects, including ocean currents and winds, caused by Earth's rotation. Objects are deflected to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

What is upwelling?

Upwelling is an oceanographic process in which cold, nutrient-rich water from the deep ocean rises to the surface. It typically occurs along coastlines where winds push surface water offshore, and the resulting nutrient supply supports abundant marine life.

What is El Nino?

El Nino is a climate pattern characterized by the periodic warming of sea surface temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean. It occurs when trade winds weaken, disrupting normal ocean circulation and causing widespread changes in weather patterns around the globe.

What is La Nina?

La Nina is the counterpart to El Nino, characterized by cooler-than-normal sea surface temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean. It occurs when trade winds strengthen, enhancing upwelling of cold deep water and influencing global weather patterns.

What is a western boundary current?

Western boundary currents are narrow, fast, deep, and warm ocean currents that flow poleward along the western edges of ocean basins. Examples include the Gulf Stream in the Atlantic and the Kuroshio Current in the Pacific. They are intensified by the Coriolis effect.

Real-World Applications

Example: A satellite image shows a large patch of floating plastic debris accumulated in the center of the North Pacific Ocean. Which feature of ocean circulation best explains why debris collects there?

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch forms in the calm center of the North Pacific Gyre, where the rotating circular currents converge and trap floating debris. The inward-spiraling surface currents carry plastic and other floating waste toward the center where water movement is minimal.
