Lesson 7.5: Marine Ecosystems - Summary

Key Concepts: Marine Ecosystems

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is an estuary?

An estuary is a partially enclosed coastal water body where freshwater from rivers and streams meets and mixes with saltwater from the ocean. Estuaries include bays, lagoons, inlets, and sounds, and they are among the most biologically productive ecosystems on Earth.

What is chemosynthesis?

Chemosynthesis is the biological process by which certain organisms, mainly bacteria, produce organic molecules from inorganic chemical compounds like hydrogen sulfide, without the need for sunlight. It supports entire communities at deep-sea hydrothermal vents and cold seeps where no light reaches.

What is the aphotic zone?

The aphotic zone is the portion of the ocean that lies below the photic zone, where sunlight does not penetrate. This permanently dark region extends from about 200 meters depth to the ocean floor and makes up the vast majority of the ocean's volume.

What is coral bleaching?

Coral bleaching occurs when corals are stressed (usually by unusually warm water temperatures) and expel the colorful zooxanthellae algae living in their tissues. Without these algae, the coral turns white and loses its primary food source. If the stress persists, the coral can die.

What is the intertidal zone?

The intertidal zone is the coastal area that lies between the highest high tide mark and the lowest low tide mark. It is alternately submerged and exposed as tides rise and fall, creating a harsh but biologically diverse environment for specially adapted organisms.

What is a pelagic organism?

Pelagic organisms are those that live in the open water column of the ocean, as opposed to the seafloor. They include free-swimming species like tuna, sharks, and jellyfish, as well as drifting organisms like plankton. The pelagic zone encompasses all open ocean water.

What is an atoll?

An atoll is a ring-shaped coral reef, island, or chain of islands that surrounds a central lagoon. Atolls form when a volcanic island subsides below the ocean surface while the surrounding coral reef continues to grow upward, eventually leaving only the reef ring and lagoon.

What is a symbiotic relationship in a marine ecosystem?

A symbiotic relationship is a close, long-term interaction between two different species living together. In marine ecosystems, examples include mutualism (both benefit, like coral and zooxanthellae), commensalism (one benefits, the other is unaffected), and parasitism (one benefits at the other's expense).

A marine biologist studying a coral reef in the Caribbean notices that large sections of coral have turned completely white during an unusually warm summer. What is the most likely explanation for this change?

Coral bleaching occurs when abnormally warm water temperatures stress corals, causing them to expel the symbiotic zooxanthellae algae that give them color and provide most of their food. Without these algae, the white calcium carbonate skeleton shows through, and the coral may starve and die if conditions persist.

Scientists studying a deep-sea hydrothermal vent discover thriving communities of giant tube worms, crabs, and shrimp in complete darkness at 2,500 meters depth. What is the primary energy source supporting this ecosystem?

Deep-sea hydrothermal vent communities are powered by chemosynthetic bacteria that use chemical energy from hydrogen sulfide and other chemicals released by the vents. These bacteria convert inorganic chemicals into organic molecules, forming the base of the food chain in the absence of sunlight.

An oceanographer studying the open Pacific notices that the waters above an underwater mountain (seamount) support far more fish and marine life than the surrounding deep ocean. What is the most likely explanation?

Seamounts deflect deep ocean currents upward, bringing nutrient-rich water closer to the sunlit surface. This localized upwelling stimulates phytoplankton growth, which supports a productive food chain. Seamounts often act as biodiversity hotspots in the otherwise nutrient-poor open ocean.

A student learns that the open ocean (pelagic zone) covers the largest area of Earth's surface but has relatively low biological productivity per square meter compared to coastal areas. What is the primary reason for this lower productivity?

The open ocean has relatively low productivity because it lacks the nutrient inputs that make coastal areas so fertile. Rivers, runoff, and coastal upwelling deliver essential nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus to nearshore waters, while the open ocean relies primarily on recycling of nutrients from below, which is often limited by the thermocline barrier.

Real-World Applications

Example: A conservation team is trying to decide which marine habitat to prioritize for protection with limited funding. They compare a coral reef that supports 500 species, a deep-sea vent community with 50 unique species found nowhere else, and an open-ocean area with 100 common species. If the goal is to protect the most irreplaceable biodiversity, which habitat should they prioritize?

While the coral reef has the most total species, the deep-sea vent community contains endemic species found nowhere else on Earth. From a conservation perspective, protecting irreplaceable biodiversity is a high priority because if these unique vent species are lost, they cannot be recovered from any other location.
