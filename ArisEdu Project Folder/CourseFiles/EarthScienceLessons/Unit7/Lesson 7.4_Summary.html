Lesson 7.4: Tides & Their Causes - Summary

Key Concepts: Tides & Their Causes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a spring tide?

A spring tide is a tide with an especially large tidal range (high high tides and low low tides) that occurs when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned during new and full moon phases. The name 'spring' comes from the concept of water 'springing up,' not from the season.

What is a neap tide?

A neap tide occurs during the first and third quarter moon phases when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth. Their gravitational forces partially counteract each other, resulting in the smallest difference between high and low tide levels.

What is a tidal range?

The tidal range is the vertical difference in water level between high tide and low tide at a given location. Tidal ranges vary greatly around the world, from less than a meter in some areas to over 16 meters in the Bay of Fundy.

What is a diurnal tide?

A diurnal tide is a tidal pattern in which there is only one high tide and one low tide per tidal day. This pattern is less common than semidiurnal tides and is found in some parts of the Gulf of Mexico and Southeast Asia.

What is a tidal bore?

A tidal bore is a wave that forms when an incoming high tide pushes into a shallow, narrowing river or bay against the outflowing current. The incoming tide is funneled into an increasingly confined space, creating a visible wave front that can travel upstream for miles.

What is a mixed semidiurnal tide?

A mixed semidiurnal tide features two high tides and two low tides per day, but they have significantly different heights. One high tide is notably higher than the other, and one low tide is notably lower. This pattern is common along the Pacific coast of North America.

Real-World Applications

Example: A port engineer needs to schedule the passage of a large cargo ship through a shallow harbor channel. The ship requires at least 12 meters of water depth, but the channel is only 10 meters deep at mean sea level. When should the engineer schedule the ship's passage?

Scheduling during a spring high tide ensures the maximum possible water depth in the channel. Spring tides produce the highest high tides, adding the most water above mean sea level, which would give the ship the extra depth it needs to safely transit the shallow channel.
