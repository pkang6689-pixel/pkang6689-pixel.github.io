Lesson 5.2: Energy Transfer (Radiation, Conduction, Convection) - Summary

Key Concepts: Energy Transfer (Radiation, Conduction, Convection)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is radiation in the context of heat transfer?

Radiation is the transfer of energy via electromagnetic waves, such as visible light and infrared radiation, and is unique because it does not require a medium to travel through.

What is conduction?

Conduction transfers heat through direct contact between molecules, such as when your hand touches a hot pan handle or when air molecules contact the warm ground.

What is convection?

Convection is the transfer of heat through the bulk movement of fluids (liquids or gases), driven by density differences caused by temperature variations.

What is albedo?

Albedo measures a surface's reflectivity on a scale from 0 to 1, where fresh snow has a high albedo (about 0.8) and dark ocean water has a low albedo (about 0.06).

What is infrared radiation?

Infrared radiation is electromagnetic energy with wavelengths longer than visible light, emitted by all warm objects including Earth's surface, and is the type of radiation trapped by greenhouse gases.

What is a convection cell?

A convection cell is a circular flow pattern in a fluid where heating causes rising motion, horizontal flow at the top, cooling and sinking, and return flow at the bottom.

Satellites measure that Earth receives about 340 watts per square meter of solar energy on average, but also emits about 340 watts per square meter of infrared radiation back to space. Recently, the outgoing energy has become slightly less than the incoming energy. What is the most likely reason for this imbalance?

Rising greenhouse gas levels absorb more of the outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit it back toward the surface, creating an energy imbalance that causes global warming.

Real-World Applications

Example: About what percentage of incoming solar radiation is absorbed by Earth's surface and atmosphere?

Roughly 30% of incoming solar radiation is reflected back to space (albedo), while about 70% is absorbed by the surface and atmosphere, driving weather and climate.

Example: Without the greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be approximately:

The natural greenhouse effect warms Earth by about 33 degrees C, raising the average temperature from a frigid -18 degrees C to a habitable 15 degrees C.

Example: A farmer paints a water storage tank white instead of black. On a hot summer day, the water inside the white tank is cooler than water in a nearby black tank. Which concept best explains this difference?

White surfaces have a high albedo and reflect most incoming solar radiation, while black surfaces have low albedo and absorb most radiation, converting it to heat.
