Lesson 5.3: Air Pressure, Wind & Coriolis Effect - Summary

Key Concepts: Air Pressure, Wind & Coriolis Effect

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the Coriolis effect?

The Coriolis effect is the apparent deflection of moving objects, including air and water, caused by Earth's rotation, deflecting them right in the Northern Hemisphere and left in the Southern.

What is the pressure gradient force?

The pressure gradient force is the initial force that causes air to move from high to low pressure areas, and greater pressure differences produce stronger winds.

What is an isobar?

An isobar is a contour line drawn on a weather map connecting locations that have the same atmospheric pressure, and their spacing indicates wind speed.

What is a jet stream?

Jet streams are fast-flowing, narrow air currents at high altitudes, typically near the tropopause at about 10 km, with speeds reaching 100-400 km/h, that guide weather patterns.

What is a sea breeze?

A sea breeze develops during the day when land heats faster than water, causing warm air to rise over land and drawing cooler air in from the sea to replace it.

What is a land breeze?

At night, land cools faster than water, so air rises over the relatively warmer sea and cooler air flows from the land to replace it, creating a land breeze.

What is a global wind belt?

Global wind belts are large-scale atmospheric circulation patterns divided into three zones per hemisphere: trade winds (0-30 degrees), westerlies (30-60 degrees), and polar easterlies (60-90 degrees).

A hurricane in the Northern Hemisphere spirals counterclockwise when viewed from above. A student asks why hurricanes spin in that direction. What is the correct explanation?

As air rushes toward the low-pressure center of a hurricane, the Coriolis effect deflects it to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, resulting in counterclockwise rotation.

A meteorologist tracks a low-pressure system approaching a city and predicts worsening weather. A friend asks why low pressure means bad weather while high pressure means fair weather. What is the best explanation?

Low pressure draws air inward and upward, where it cools and water vapor condenses into clouds and precipitation; high pressure forces air downward, warming it and preventing cloud formation.
