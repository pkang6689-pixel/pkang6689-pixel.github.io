Lesson 5.4: Cloud Types & Precipitation - Summary

Key Concepts: Cloud Types & Precipitation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the dew point?

The dew point is the temperature at which air reaches 100% relative humidity and water vapor begins to condense into liquid droplets or ice crystals.

What is a condensation nucleus?

Condensation nuclei are microscopic particles suspended in the atmosphere that provide surfaces for water vapor to condense upon, which is essential for cloud formation.

What is relative humidity?

Relative humidity is the ratio of the actual water vapor content in air to the maximum it could hold at that temperature, expressed as a percentage; 100% means the air is saturated.

What is precipitation?

Precipitation encompasses all forms of water that fall from the atmosphere to the surface, driven by gravity once cloud droplets or ice crystals become large enough.

What is a nimbostratus cloud?

Nimbostratus clouds are dense, gray cloud layers that block sunlight and produce continuous, moderate-to-heavy precipitation over large areas, often lasting several hours.

What is freezing rain?

Freezing rain occurs when supercooled liquid raindrops fall through a thin cold layer and freeze on contact with cold surfaces, creating a dangerous glaze of ice.

What is the rain shadow effect?

As moist air rises over a mountain, it cools and drops its moisture on the windward side; the air descending the leeward side is dry, creating an arid rain shadow zone.

What is a cirrostratus cloud?

Cirrostratus clouds are thin, transparent ice-crystal sheets at high altitudes that often produce a halo around the Sun or Moon and may signal an approaching warm front.

Real-World Applications

Example: A car insurance company reports that ice storm damage claims spike when the surface temperature is just below freezing and a warm layer exists above. What type of precipitation creates the most dangerous driving conditions and why?

Freezing rain is especially hazardous because it falls as liquid but instantly freezes on contact with cold surfaces, creating a transparent, slippery ice glaze that is difficult to see.
