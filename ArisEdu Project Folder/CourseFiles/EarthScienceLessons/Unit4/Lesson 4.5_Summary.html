Lesson 4.5: Mass Extinctions & Evolution - Summary

Key Concepts: Mass Extinctions & Evolution

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a mass extinction?

A mass extinction is defined as a geologically rapid event in which more than 75% of all species disappear within a relatively short time frame (typically less than 2 million years), dramatically altering the course of evolution.

What is adaptive radiation?

Adaptive radiation is the rapid evolutionary diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many different species, each adapted to different ecological niches, and it typically occurs after mass extinctions open up many vacant niches simultaneously.

What is the 'background extinction rate'?

The background extinction rate is the natural, baseline rate at which species normally go extinct during periods between mass extinctions, estimated at roughly 1 to 5 species per year, driven by normal competition, predation, and environmental change.

A documentary states that if the Chicxulub asteroid had struck Earth just a few hours earlier or later, it would have hit deep ocean rather than the shallow, sulfur-rich sediments of the Yucatan, potentially producing a much less devastating extinction. A student asks why the impact location mattered. What is the scientific explanation?

The Yucatan's sulfur-rich carbonate and evaporite sediments were uniquely destructive because the impact vaporized them, injecting massive amounts of sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere that blocked sunlight and produced sulfuric acid rain, greatly amplifying the extinction beyond the impact alone.

Real-World Applications

Example: A marine biologist observes that modern coral reefs are experiencing severe bleaching events due to rising ocean temperatures, and many reef-building coral species are declining rapidly. She notes that reef ecosystems have collapsed multiple times during past mass extinctions and took millions of years to recover. What parallel is she drawing between past and present?

The geological record shows that reef-building organisms are highly sensitive to environmental change, and past reef collapses during mass extinctions took millions of years to recover, warning that current human-caused reef destruction could have similarly long-lasting consequences.
