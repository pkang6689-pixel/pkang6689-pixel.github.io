Lesson 4.3: The Geologic Time Scale - Summary

Key Concepts: The Geologic Time Scale

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the Paleozoic Era?

The Paleozoic Era (meaning 'ancient life') spans from 541 to 252 million years ago and witnessed the evolution of fish, amphibians, reptiles, insects, and the first land plants and forests.

What is the Mesozoic Era?

The Mesozoic Era (meaning 'middle life') lasted from 252 to 66 million years ago and is divided into the Triassic, Jurassic, and Cretaceous periods, during which dinosaurs dominated terrestrial ecosystems.

What is the Cenozoic Era?

The Cenozoic Era (meaning 'recent life') began 66 million years ago after the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs and continues to the present, during which mammals, birds, and flowering plants diversified to dominate global ecosystems.

What is an eon in the geologic time scale?

An eon is the largest unit of geological time, with Earth's history divided into four eons: Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, and Phanerozoic, each spanning hundreds of millions to over two billion years.

What is a geologic period?

A geologic period is a subdivision of an era, typically lasting tens of millions of years, and is defined by characteristic fossil assemblages and rock sequences, such as the Jurassic, Cretaceous, and Quaternary periods.

What is a geologic epoch?

An epoch is a subdivision of a geologic period and represents the finest commonly used division of the geologic time scale, such as the Holocene Epoch (the current epoch) or the Pleistocene Epoch (the Ice Age epoch).

A student is examining a chart of the geologic time scale and notices that the Precambrian takes up most of the chart's length, but the Cenozoic Era, which includes the present day, is a very thin sliver at the top. The student asks why so little detail is shown for the Precambrian compared to more recent eras. What is the best explanation?

Precambrian life was mostly microscopic and soft-bodied, leaving few distinctive fossils for subdivision, while Phanerozoic rocks contain abundant, diverse fossils that allow geologists to define many detailed periods and epochs.

Scientists studying Precambrian rocks in Australia discover layered mound-like structures called stromatolites, which are created by photosynthetic cyanobacteria. These are among the oldest evidence of life on Earth, dating back 3.5 billion years. What is the significance of these organisms for Earth's history?

Stromatolite-forming cyanobacteria were among Earth's earliest life forms and their photosynthesis produced oxygen as a waste product, gradually transforming the atmosphere from oxygen-free to oxygen-rich over billions of years, a prerequisite for complex animal life.

A geologist is giving a lecture about the end of the Paleozoic Era. She explains that the end-Permian extinction eliminated approximately 96% of marine species and 70% of land species, making it the worst mass extinction in Earth's history. A student asks what caused this catastrophe. What is the current scientific understanding?

The end-Permian extinction is linked to the Siberian Traps, massive volcanic eruptions that released enormous quantities of greenhouse gases and toxic compounds, triggering severe global warming, ocean acidification, and widespread anoxia.

Real-World Applications

Example: The Cenozoic Era is known as the age of:

Following the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs at the end of the Cretaceous, mammals diversified rapidly to fill vacant ecological niches, making the Cenozoic Era the Age of Mammals.

Example: During a class debate, one student argues that the Mesozoic Era should be called the 'Age of Dinosaurs,' but another points out that many other important groups also thrived during this time. Which of the following organisms also first appeared or diversified significantly during the Mesozoic?

While dinosaurs dominated, the Mesozoic also saw the first appearance of flowering plants (angiosperms), the evolution of birds from theropod dinosaurs, and the diversification of early mammals that would later rise to prominence.
