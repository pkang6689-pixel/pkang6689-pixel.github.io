Lesson 4.2: Absolute Dating & Radiometric Methods - Summary

Key Concepts: Absolute Dating & Radiometric Methods

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a half-life?

A half-life is the constant, measurable period of time required for exactly half of the atoms of a radioactive parent isotope in a sample to undergo nuclear decay and transform into stable daughter isotope atoms.

What is radioactive decay?

Radioactive decay is the natural, spontaneous process by which unstable atomic nuclei (parent isotopes) release energy and particles to transform into different, stable atoms (daughter isotopes) at a predictable, constant rate.

What is absolute dating?

Absolute dating provides specific numerical ages in years (such as 4.5 billion years old) using methods like radiometric dating, unlike relative dating which only determines the order of events.

What is a parent isotope?

A parent isotope is the original, unstable radioactive atom that spontaneously decays over time, transforming into a different element called the daughter isotope through the release of particles and energy.

What is a daughter isotope?

A daughter isotope is the stable atom produced when a radioactive parent isotope undergoes decay, and by measuring the ratio of daughter to parent isotopes, scientists can calculate how long ago the mineral crystallized.

What is Carbon-14?

Carbon-14 is a naturally occurring radioactive isotope of carbon produced in the upper atmosphere when cosmic ray neutrons interact with nitrogen-14 atoms, and it is incorporated into all living organisms through the food chain.

A forensic scientist is investigating human remains found in a peat bog. Carbon-14 analysis shows that approximately 50% of the original C-14 remains. What is the approximate age of the remains, and what does this tell the investigator?

With 50% of the C-14 remaining, exactly one half-life has passed, making the remains approximately 5,730 years old, which places them firmly in the archaeological past rather than being a modern forensic case.

A geology professor shows students a zircon crystal from Australia that has been dated at 4.4 billion years old, making it the oldest known mineral on Earth. A student asks why the crystal survived while the original rock it formed in was destroyed long ago. What is the best explanation?

Zircon is one of the most durable minerals known, resisting chemical weathering, physical abrasion, and even high-grade metamorphism while preserving its uranium-lead radiometric system intact, allowing scientists to date the very earliest periods of Earth's history.

Real-World Applications

Example: After 3 half-lives, what percentage of the original parent isotope remains?

After each half-life, half the remaining parent isotope decays: 100% becomes 50% after one half-life, 25% after two, and 12.5% after three half-lives, following an exponential decay pattern.

Example: The age of Earth (~4.54 billion years) was determined by dating:

Earth's oldest surface rocks have been recycled by plate tectonics, so scientists determined Earth's age by dating meteorites and Moon rocks that formed at the same time as our planet from the same solar nebula.

Example: A geologist wants to determine the age of a sandstone layer. She cannot use radiometric dating directly on the sandstone, but she notices a volcanic ash layer just above the sandstone and a lava flow just below it. How can she bracket the age of the sandstone?

By dating the igneous rocks above and below the sedimentary layer, the geologist can bracket the sandstone's age within a range, since it must be younger than the lava flow it rests on and older than the ash layer deposited on top of it.

Example: A forensic scientist is investigating human remains found in a peat bog. Carbon-14 analysis shows that approximately 50% of the original C-14 remains. What is the approximate age of the remains, and what does this tell the investigator?

With 50% of the C-14 remaining, exactly one half-life has passed, making the remains approximately 5,730 years old, which places them firmly in the archaeological past rather than being a modern forensic case.

Example: A planetary scientist is studying a meteorite that landed in the Sahara Desert. Uranium-Lead dating of zircon crystals in the meteorite gives an age of 4.56 billion years. Why is this significant for understanding Earth's age?

Meteorites are remnants of the original solar nebula that have remained unchanged since the solar system formed, so their radiometric age of approximately 4.56 billion years represents the formation age of the entire solar system, including Earth.

Example: A volcanologist collects a lava sample from a Hawaiian eruption that occurred in 1960. She sends it to a lab for Potassium-Argon dating, but the results show an age of 0 years (no measurable Argon-40). Why does this result actually confirm the method works correctly?

When lava erupts, extreme heat drives off all existing argon gas, resetting the K-Ar clock to zero, and since only decades have passed (geologically insignificant), not enough K-40 has decayed to produce measurable Ar-40, confirming the method's validity.

Example: An environmental scientist finds a layer of peat (compressed plant material) buried 2 meters below the surface at a construction site. Carbon-14 dating gives an age of 8,000 years. Above the peat is a layer of clay with marine shells, and above that is modern soil. The site is currently 15 meters above sea level. What geological story does this evidence tell?

The C-14 dated peat shows a freshwater environment existed 8,000 years ago, the overlying marine clay with shells records a period when sea level rose and flooded the area, and the modern soil on top shows the area later returned to dry land.
