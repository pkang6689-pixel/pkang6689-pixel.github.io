Lesson 1.2: Mineral Identification - Summary

Key Concepts: Mineral Identification

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the hardest mineral on the Mohs scale?

Diamond is rated 10 on the Mohs Hardness Scale, making it the hardest naturally occurring mineral known, capable of scratching all other minerals.

What is the most unreliable physical property for identifying minerals?

Color is the most unreliable property because many different minerals share the same color, and impurities can cause the same mineral to appear in a wide range of colors.

What is luster?

Luster describes the quality and intensity of light reflected from a mineral's surface, with major categories including metallic (like a metal) and non-metallic (glassy, waxy, earthy, silky, etc.).

What is cleavage in mineral identification?

Cleavage is the tendency of a mineral to break along flat, even surfaces that correspond to planes of weaker bonding in the crystal structure, producing smooth faces at consistent angles.

What is fracture?

Fracture describes how a mineral breaks when it does not follow cleavage planes, producing irregular surfaces such as conchoidal (curved, shell-like), splintery, or uneven breaks.

What is specific gravity?

Specific gravity is a dimensionless ratio comparing a mineral's density to that of water, helping to identify minerals by how heavy they feel relative to their size.

What is streak in mineral identification?

Streak is the color of the fine powder left behind when a mineral is rubbed across an unglazed porcelain streak plate, and it is more consistent than the mineral's external color.

What is a polymorph?

Polymorphs are minerals that share the same chemical composition but have different crystal structures, resulting in very different physical properties, as seen with diamond and graphite, both pure carbon.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is cleavage in mineral identification?

Cleavage is the tendency of a mineral to break along flat, even surfaces that correspond to planes of weaker bonding in the crystal structure, producing smooth faces at consistent angles.

Example: A geology student finds a mineral specimen that breaks into thin, flat sheets. Her instructor tells her this indicates the mineral has excellent cleavage in one direction. Which mineral is she most likely holding?

Mica minerals like muscovite have perfect basal cleavage in one direction, causing them to peel apart into thin, flat, flexible sheets, a property called perfect one-directional cleavage.
