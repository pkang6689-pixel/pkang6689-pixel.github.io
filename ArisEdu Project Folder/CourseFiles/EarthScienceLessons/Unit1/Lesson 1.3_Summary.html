Lesson 1.3: Types of Rocks (Igneous, Sedimentary, Metamorphic) - Summary

Key Concepts: Types of Rocks (Igneous, Sedimentary, Metamorphic)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the parent rock of marble?

Marble forms when limestone (calcium carbonate) is subjected to heat and pressure, causing the calcite crystals to recrystallize into larger, interlocking grains.

What is a clastic sedimentary rock?

Clastic sedimentary rocks, such as sandstone and conglomerate, are composed of broken fragments (clasts) of pre-existing rocks that have been transported, deposited, and cemented together.

What is foliation in metamorphic rocks?

Foliation is the layered or banded texture in metamorphic rocks caused by the alignment of flat or elongated minerals perpendicular to the direction of applied pressure.

What is metamorphism?

Metamorphism is the transformation of existing rocks through heat, pressure, or chemical fluids while the rock remains in the solid state; if the rock fully melts, the resulting product is igneous, not metamorphic.

What is a conglomerate?

Conglomerate is a clastic sedimentary rock composed of rounded pebbles, cobbles, or boulders cemented together in a matrix of sand or finer sediment, indicating transport by water.

A student finds a rock near a riverbed that contains smooth, rounded pebbles cemented in a sandy matrix. Her teacher asks her to identify the rock type and explain its formation. What is the correct identification?

The rounded pebbles in a sandy cement are diagnostic of conglomerate, a clastic sedimentary rock formed when gravel and pebbles are deposited by water, buried, and cemented together.

A road construction crew cuts through a hillside and exposes layers of sand-sized grains cemented together. An engineer identifies the rock as sandstone. A worker asks what process turned the loose sand into solid rock. What is the correct answer?

Lithification of sand into sandstone involves two processes: compaction from the weight of overlying sediments and cementation as dissolved minerals precipitate in the pore spaces, binding grains together.

A geologist examining a mountain outcrop finds alternating dark and light mineral bands in a coarse-grained rock. She identifies it as gneiss, a high-grade metamorphic rock. A student asks what the banding indicates. What is the best explanation?

Gneissic banding forms during high-grade metamorphism when extreme heat and pressure cause minerals to migrate and segregate into alternating light (felsic) and dark (mafic) layers.

Real-World Applications

Example: Granite is an example of which rock type?

Granite forms when magma cools slowly underground (intrusive), allowing large crystals of quartz, feldspar, and mica to grow over long periods.

Example: A geologist examining a rock sample from a volcano in Hawaii notices it has very small crystals that are barely visible. She concludes the rock cooled quickly at the surface. What type of rock is she most likely examining?

Fine-grained or small crystals indicate rapid cooling at the surface, characteristic of extrusive igneous rocks like basalt that form when lava cools quickly after eruption.
