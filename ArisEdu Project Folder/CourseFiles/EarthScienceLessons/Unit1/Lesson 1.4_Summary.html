Lesson 1.4: The Rock Cycle - Summary

Key Concepts: The Rock Cycle

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is lithification?

Lithification is the process by which loose, unconsolidated sediments are transformed into solid sedimentary rock, primarily through the combined effects of compaction and cementation.

What is crystallization in the rock cycle?

Crystallization occurs when magma or lava cools and the atoms organize into orderly mineral crystal structures, forming the interlocking grain texture characteristic of igneous rocks.

What is 'uplift' in the context of the rock cycle?

Uplift is the tectonic process that raises rocks from depth to the surface through forces like mountain building, fault movement, or isostatic rebound, exposing them to surface weathering.

A mining company extracts marble from a quarry. A geologist explains that the marble was once a coral reef limestone that was buried and transformed. A new worker asks if the marble could ever return to being limestone. What is the correct response?

In the rock cycle, marble can be weathered and dissolved, and the calcium carbonate can be redeposited in water, eventually lithifying into new limestone, demonstrating the cycle's interconnected nature.

A coastal town notices that cliff faces are rapidly eroding after a series of storms. A geologist explains that this erosion is part of the rock cycle. A town council member asks where the eroded material goes. What is the most accurate answer?

Eroded cliff material is transported as sediment by waves and currents to the ocean floor, where it accumulates in layers and may eventually be compacted and cemented into new sedimentary rock.

Real-World Applications

Example: A farmer notices that the soil on her hillside field washes away during heavy rains, exposing bare rock. A conservation agent explains that vegetation removal has accelerated erosion. In the rock cycle, what happens to the washed-away sediment next?

Eroded sediment is transported by water to lower elevations (rivers, lakes, oceans) where it is deposited and may eventually undergo compaction and cementation to become new sedimentary rock.

Example: A student argues that the rock cycle takes millions of years, so it is not relevant to human timescales. Her teacher disagrees. Which example best shows the rock cycle operating on human-observable timescales?

Volcanic eruptions produce new igneous rock in hours to days, and that rock immediately begins weathering, demonstrating that parts of the rock cycle are observable on human timescales.
