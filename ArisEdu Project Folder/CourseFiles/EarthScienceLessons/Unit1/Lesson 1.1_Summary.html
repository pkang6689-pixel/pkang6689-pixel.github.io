Lesson 1.1: Earth's Layers (Crust, Mantle, Core) - Summary

Key Concepts: Earth's Layers (Crust, Mantle, Core)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the physical state of Earth's outer core?

The outer core is liquid, composed mainly of molten iron and nickel, and its convection currents generate Earth's magnetic field.

What is the asthenosphere?

The asthenosphere is a weak, partially molten layer in the upper mantle whose ductile nature allows the rigid lithospheric plates above it to slide and move.

What is the Mohorovicic discontinuity (Moho)?

The Moho is the boundary between Earth's crust and mantle, identified by a sharp increase in seismic wave velocity due to the denser mantle rock below.

What is the geothermal gradient?

The geothermal gradient is the rate of temperature increase with depth, averaging about 25 to 30 degrees Celsius per kilometer in the crust.

What is the shadow zone in seismology?

The shadow zone is a region on Earth's surface (between about 104 and 140 degrees from an earthquake) where seismic waves are absent because the liquid outer core refracts P-waves and blocks S-waves.

What is the mesosphere in the context of Earth's interior?

In Earth's interior, the mesosphere (or lower mantle) is the strong, rigid layer of the mantle that extends from below the asthenosphere to the core-mantle boundary.

A mining company encounters increasingly hot conditions as they dig deeper into the Earth. At 3 km depth the rock temperature is 90 degrees Celsius above the surface temperature. A worker asks why. What is the best explanation?

The geothermal gradient, driven by residual heat from Earth's formation and radioactive decay, causes temperatures to rise about 25 to 30 degrees Celsius per kilometer of depth in the crust.

Real-World Applications

Example: The mantle makes up approximately what percentage of Earth's volume?

The mantle accounts for approximately 84% of Earth's total volume, extending from about 30 km below the surface to a depth of roughly 2,900 km.
