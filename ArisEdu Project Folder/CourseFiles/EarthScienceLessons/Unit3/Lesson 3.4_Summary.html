Lesson 3.4: Glaciers & Ice Ages - Summary

Key Concepts: Glaciers & Ice Ages

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a glacial erratic?

A glacial erratic is a boulder transported by glacial ice far from its original location and deposited when the ice melts, often appearing completely different from the local bedrock and looking conspicuously out of place.

What is a moraine?

A moraine is an accumulation of unsorted glacial debris (till) deposited in ridges along the sides, center, or terminus of a glacier, serving as evidence of the glacier's former size and extent.

What is firn?

Firn is partially compacted granular snow that has survived at least one summer melt season, representing an intermediate stage between freshly fallen snow and the dense, crystal-clear glacial ice that forms under continued compression.

What is a continental glacier?

Continental glaciers, or ice sheets, are vast masses of ice that spread over large portions of a continent, reaching thicknesses of several kilometers, with the Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets being the two that exist today.

What is an alpine glacier?

Alpine glaciers, also called valley glaciers, form in mountain valleys above the snowline and flow downhill like slow rivers of ice, carving distinctive features like cirques, aretes, and U-shaped valleys.

What is a cirque?

A cirque is an amphitheater-shaped hollow carved into a mountainside by the erosive action of a glacier at its source, and after the glacier melts, the cirque may contain a small lake called a tarn.

A coastal city in Bangladesh is already experiencing frequent flooding at high tide, and scientists project that melting of the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets will raise sea levels by 1 to 2 meters by 2100. What is the primary concern for this city?

When ice sheets on land melt, the meltwater flows into the oceans and raises sea level, threatening low-lying coastal cities like those in Bangladesh with permanent flooding, saltwater intrusion, and displacement of populations.

A farmer in Wisconsin finds that his soil is a mixture of clay, sand, gravel, and boulders all jumbled together with no sorting by size. A geologist tells him this soil composition is typical of the area. What is the origin of this mixed-up soil?

Much of Wisconsin's soil is derived from glacial till, the unsorted mixture of all sediment sizes deposited directly by the melting ice sheet, which unlike water or wind deposits does not sort particles by size.

A climate scientist presents data showing that global glacial ice volume has been decreasing at an accelerating rate since the 1990s. Satellite measurements show that the Greenland ice sheet alone lost approximately 280 billion tons of ice per year from 2002 to 2020. A student asks what the practical consequence of this ice loss is. What is the most accurate answer?

Melting glacial ice on land directly raises sea levels as meltwater enters the oceans, and the loss of glaciers also threatens freshwater supplies for billions of people who depend on glacial meltwater for drinking water and agriculture.

Real-World Applications

Example: Milankovitch cycles influence ice ages through changes in:

Milankovitch cycles are periodic variations in Earth's eccentricity, axial tilt (obliquity), and precession that alter how much solar energy different latitudes receive, triggering glacial and interglacial periods over tens of thousands of years.

Example: The most recent ice age ended approximately:

The last glacial period ended about 11,700 years ago, marking the beginning of the current Holocene interglacial epoch in which human civilization developed.

Example: During the peak of the last ice age about 20,000 years ago, approximately what percentage of Earth's land was covered by ice?

At the peak of the last glacial maximum, about 30% of Earth's land surface was covered by ice sheets and glaciers, compared to approximately 10% today, with massive ice sheets covering much of North America and Europe.

Example: Tourists visiting Norway are amazed by the deep, narrow waterways with steep cliffs that extend far inland from the coast. A tour guide explains these were carved during the last ice age. What geological process created these spectacular features?

Norway's fjords were carved by glaciers that eroded deep valleys well below sea level during the ice ages, and when the glaciers melted and sea levels rose, seawater flooded these over-deepened valleys to create the dramatic fjord landscapes.

Example: During a field trip to the Great Lakes region, students find parallel grooves scratched into exposed limestone bedrock all pointing in a north-to-south direction. Their teacher explains this is evidence of past glaciation. How do these grooves help scientists understand ice age glaciers?

Glacial striations are scratched into bedrock by rocks frozen into the base of a glacier, and their orientation directly indicates the direction of ice flow, allowing scientists to reconstruct the movement patterns of ancient ice sheets.
