Lesson 3.1: Weathering (Mechanical & Chemical) - Summary

Key Concepts: Weathering (Mechanical & Chemical)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the main difference between mechanical and chemical weathering?

Mechanical weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces without altering its chemical composition, while chemical weathering transforms minerals through chemical reactions, creating entirely new substances.

What is the first step in soil formation?

Weathering breaks bedrock into smaller fragments that form the mineral component of soil, making it the essential first step in soil formation before organic matter accumulates.

What is frost wedging?

Frost wedging is a mechanical weathering process in which water enters cracks in rock, freezes and expands by approximately 9%, and gradually widens the crack with each freeze-thaw cycle until the rock splits apart.

What is exfoliation in the context of weathering?

Exfoliation, also called unloading, occurs when overlying rock is eroded away, reducing confining pressure and causing the exposed rock below to expand and crack in concentric sheets, much like layers peeling off an onion.

What is oxidation in the context of rock weathering?

Oxidation is a chemical weathering process in which iron in minerals reacts with oxygen and water to produce iron oxide (rust), weakening the rock structure and giving it a characteristic reddish-brown color.

What is carbonic acid and how does it form?

Carbonic acid (H2CO3) forms naturally when carbon dioxide in the atmosphere dissolves in rainwater, creating a weak acid that is particularly effective at dissolving carbonate rocks like limestone.

What is karst topography?

Karst topography develops when carbonic acid in groundwater dissolves soluble bedrock such as limestone over long periods, creating distinctive features including caves, sinkholes, disappearing streams, and underground rivers.

What is the 'angle of repose' as it relates to weathered material?

The angle of repose is the maximum slope angle at which loose granular material such as sand, gravel, or weathered rock fragments can rest without sliding, and it varies depending on particle size, shape, and moisture content.

What is salt crystal growth (haloclasty) in weathering?

Salt crystal growth is a mechanical weathering process common in arid and coastal environments where saltwater seeps into rock pores, evaporates, and leaves behind growing salt crystals that exert enough pressure to fracture the rock.

An archaeologist discovers that ancient marble statues displayed outdoors in an industrial city have lost their facial features and fine details, while identical statues stored indoors remain perfectly preserved. What is the most likely cause?

Marble is composed of calcium carbonate, which readily dissolves when exposed to acid rain containing sulfuric and nitric acids from industrial emissions, gradually eroding fine details from outdoor statues while indoor statues remain protected.

Real-World Applications

Example: Which of the following is an example of mechanical weathering?

Root wedging is a form of mechanical (physical) weathering because the growing roots physically pry the rock apart without changing its chemical composition.

Example: A park ranger in Yosemite observes large, curved sheets of granite peeling away from the surface of Half Dome. What type of weathering is this an example of?

Half Dome's iconic shape is partly due to exfoliation, where the removal of overlying rock reduces pressure on the granite below, causing it to expand and crack in curved, concentric sheets that peel away from the surface.
