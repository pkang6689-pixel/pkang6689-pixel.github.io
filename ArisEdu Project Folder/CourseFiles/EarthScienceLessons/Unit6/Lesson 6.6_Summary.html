Key Concepts: Human Impact on Climate

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a carbon footprint?

A carbon footprint measures the total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by a person, company, or activity, helping identify areas where reductions are most effective.

What is climate mitigation?

Climate mitigation aims to reduce the causes of climate change by cutting greenhouse gas emissions (renewable energy, efficiency) and increasing carbon removal (reforestation, carbon capture).

What is climate adaptation?

Climate adaptation involves building resilience to climate impacts through measures like flood defenses, drought-resistant crops, heat action plans, and updated building codes.

What is carbon sequestration?

Carbon sequestration removes CO₂ from the atmosphere and stores it long-term in forests, soils, oceans, or underground geological formations, helping to offset emissions.

What is a fossil fuel?

Fossil fuels are energy-dense hydrocarbons formed from ancient marine organisms and plants that were buried and transformed by heat and pressure over millions of years.

What is carbon capture and storage (CCS)?

CCS technology captures CO₂ from emission sources before it enters the atmosphere and injects it deep underground into porous rock formations for long-term storage.

A corporation claims to be 'carbon neutral' by purchasing carbon offsets from tree-planting projects rather than reducing its own emissions. An environmental group criticizes this approach. What is the main concern with relying on offsets instead of direct emission reductions?

While offsets can supplement emission reductions, they should not replace them because trees may not permanently store carbon, and continued high emissions make net zero harder to achieve.

A tech company builds a direct air capture facility that removes 4,000 tons of CO₂ from the atmosphere per year and stores it underground. Critics say this is a distraction from reducing emissions. Supporters say it is a necessary tool. What is the most balanced scientific view?

Most climate pathways to limiting warming to 1.5-2 degrees C require both massive emission reductions AND carbon removal, but removal technology currently operates at a tiny fraction of needed scale.

Real-World Applications

Adaptation means adjusting to the effects of climate change that are already happening (sea walls, flood defenses), while mitigation means reducing the causes of climate change (cutting emissions).

Example: A country generates 80% of its electricity from coal and is considering transitioning to renewable energy. Economic advisors warn of job losses in the coal industry. How can policymakers address climate goals while managing the economic transition?

A just transition supports affected workers and communities through retraining, economic diversification, and investment in new industries, ensuring the shift to clean energy is socially equitable.

Example: A city in the Netherlands, much of which lies below sea level, has invested billions in flood barriers, dike systems, and water management infrastructure. Is this an example of climate mitigation or adaptation, and why?

Flood barriers and water management are adaptation because they prepare for and protect against the impacts of rising seas, rather than addressing the root cause of emissions.
