Lesson 6.3: Climate Zones & Biomes - Summary

Key Concepts: Climate Zones & Biomes

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a biome?

A biome is a major ecological community characterized by distinctive plant and animal groups adapted to the region's climate conditions, such as tropical rainforest, desert, or tundra.

What is the Koppen classification system?

The Koppen system classifies climates into five major groups (A-tropical, B-arid, C-temperate, D-continental, E-polar) using specific temperature and precipitation thresholds.

What is permafrost?

Permafrost is soil or rock that stays at or below 0 degrees C for at least two consecutive years, storing massive amounts of frozen organic carbon that can release greenhouse gases if it thaws.

What is a savanna?

Savannas are grasslands with widely spaced trees found in tropical and subtropical regions with distinct wet and dry seasons, supporting large herbivore populations like those in Africa.

What is the taiga?

The taiga (boreal forest) is Earth's largest biome, stretching across northern latitudes in Russia, Canada, and Scandinavia, dominated by spruce, fir, and pine trees adapted to harsh winters.

What is a temperate climate?

Temperate climates occupy the middle latitudes (about 23.5-66.5 degrees) and feature moderate temperatures with four distinct seasons, supporting diverse forests and grasslands.

What is a polar climate?

Polar climates are found above 66.5 degrees latitude, characterized by extremely cold temperatures year-round, minimal precipitation, and permanent ice or tundra vegetation.

A city planner in Phoenix, Arizona proposes planting a temperate deciduous forest in the city to cool it down. A botanist explains why this will not work. Based on climate zone matching, what is the fundamental problem with this plan?

Biomes are determined by climate: deciduous forests require moderate rainfall (75-150 cm/year) and cool winters, while Phoenix's arid climate cannot naturally sustain such water-demanding vegetation.
