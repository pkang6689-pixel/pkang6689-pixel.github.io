Lesson 2.4: Earthquakes & Seismic Waves - Summary

Key Concepts: Earthquakes & Seismic Waves

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the focus (hypocenter) of an earthquake?

The focus (or hypocenter) is the actual point underground where rock first fractures and seismic energy is released, and it can be classified as shallow, intermediate, or deep based on its depth.

What is the epicenter of an earthquake?

The epicenter is the point on Earth's surface directly above the focus, and it is typically the location where shaking intensity is greatest and initial damage reports originate.

What is a seismograph?

A seismograph uses the principle of inertia: a heavy mass suspended from a frame remains still while the ground and frame shake, and the relative motion between them is recorded as a seismogram.

What is the Richter scale?

The Richter scale is a logarithmic scale where each whole number increase represents 10 times greater ground motion amplitude and approximately 32 times more energy released.

What is the Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale?

The Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale rates earthquake effects from I (not felt) to XII (total destruction) based on observed damage, human perception, and structural impact at a given location.

What is liquefaction?

Liquefaction occurs when earthquake shaking causes water-saturated, loose sediment to lose its strength and temporarily behave as a liquid, causing buildings to sink, tilt, or collapse.

A city engineer in a coastal area is reviewing building codes after a recent earthquake caused extensive damage to buildings on landfill but minimal damage to buildings on bedrock. She asks a geologist to explain the difference. What is the most likely cause?

Loose, water-saturated landfill amplifies seismic waves and is prone to liquefaction, where the ground temporarily behaves like liquid, while solid bedrock transmits waves without amplification and provides a stable foundation.

A news reporter covering a magnitude 7.0 earthquake asks a seismologist how much more powerful it is than a magnitude 5.0 earthquake. The seismologist explains the logarithmic nature of the scale. What is the approximate energy difference?

Each magnitude step represents about 32 times more energy, so a two-step difference (5.0 to 7.0) represents approximately 32 times 32, or about 1,000 times more energy released.

A school science teacher conducts an earthquake drill and afterward discusses why buildings in earthquake zones use flexible steel frames rather than rigid concrete alone. A student asks why flexibility helps. What is the correct engineering explanation?

Earthquake-resistant design uses flexible steel frames that can sway and deform under seismic forces without fracturing, absorbing energy, while brittle rigid structures can crack catastrophically under the same stresses.

A research team installs seismometers on the ocean floor to study undersea earthquakes in real time. They aim to improve tsunami warning times. A funding agency asks how this ocean-bottom monitoring differs from relying on land-based stations alone. What is the key advantage?

Ocean-bottom seismometers detect submarine earthquakes earlier and with greater precision than distant land stations, reducing the time needed to assess tsunami risk and issue warnings to coastal populations.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Solid bedrock provides a stable foundation that transmits seismic waves without amplification, while loose sandy soil near water can amplify shaking and undergo liquefaction during earthquakes.
