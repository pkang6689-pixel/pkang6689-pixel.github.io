Lesson 2.5: Volcanoes & Hot Spots - Summary

Key Concepts: Volcanoes & Hot Spots

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is viscosity in the context of volcanic eruptions?

Viscosity measures a fluid's resistance to flow; high-viscosity magma (thick, like honey) traps gas and erupts explosively, while low-viscosity magma (thin, like water) allows gas to escape and flows gently.

What is a hot spot?

A hot spot is a volcanic region fed by a mantle plume, a column of abnormally hot rock rising from deep in the mantle, that remains relatively stationary while tectonic plates move over it.

What is a pyroclastic flow?

A pyroclastic flow is one of the most lethal volcanic hazards, consisting of a fast-moving current of superheated gas and volcanic debris that can travel at over 100 km/h with temperatures exceeding 700 degrees Celsius.

What is a lahar?

A lahar is a volcanic mudflow or debris flow made of a slurry of volcanic material and water that can travel rapidly down valleys, burying communities with a concrete-like mixture of ash, rock, and water.

What is a caldera?

A caldera is a large depression formed when the roof of a magma chamber collapses inward after a large volume of magma has been evacuated during an eruption, such as the Yellowstone Caldera.

A volcanologist monitoring a volcano in Indonesia notices that the magma has high silica content. She immediately raises the alert level. A colleague from Hawaii, where volcanoes typically have low-silica magma, asks why high silica is so dangerous. What is the correct explanation?

High silica content makes magma very viscous (thick), preventing dissolved gases from escaping; as the magma rises and pressure decreases, the trapped gas expands violently, causing explosive eruptions.

A tourist visiting Hawaii watches a slow lava flow from Kilauea enter the ocean, creating new land. She compares this to news footage of Mount Pinatubo's explosive eruption. A park ranger explains why the two volcanoes behave so differently. What is the key factor?

Magma composition determines eruption style: Kilauea's basaltic magma is low in silica and viscosity, allowing gas to escape easily for gentle eruptions, while Pinatubo's silica-rich magma traps gas for explosive eruptions.

A geology professor asks students to explain why Yellowstone's hot spot has created a chain of progressively older volcanic features stretching across Idaho to the northeast. A student suggests the hot spot has been moving southwest. The professor corrects her. What is actually happening?

The mantle plume beneath Yellowstone is stationary, but the North American Plate moves southwest over it, so older volcanic calderas and features are found to the northeast along the Snake River Plain in Idaho.

A volcanologist monitoring seismic activity beneath a dormant volcano detects increasing numbers of small earthquakes and ground uplift measured by GPS sensors. She warns that an eruption may be approaching. A local official asks how earthquakes beneath a volcano can predict an eruption. What is the scientific basis for this warning?

As magma rises through the crust, it forces rock apart and fractures surrounding material, generating swarms of small earthquakes and causing the ground surface to inflate, both of which are reliable precursors to volcanic eruptions.

Real-World Applications

Example: Mount St. Helens is an example of a:

Mount St. Helens is a composite volcano (stratovolcano) in the Cascade Range, known for its catastrophic 1980 explosive eruption that blew away the mountain's north face.

Example: Yellowstone is an example of a _____ hot spot.

Yellowstone is a continental hot spot where a mantle plume beneath the North American continental crust produces geysers, hot springs, and a supervolcano caldera with potential for massive eruptions.

Example: A geologist studying the Hawaiian island chain notices that the islands get progressively older from southeast to northwest. The Big Island (youngest) is currently volcanically active, while the northwestern islands are extinct and eroded. What mechanism explains this age progression?

The Pacific Plate moves northwest over the stationary Hawaiian hot spot, so each island was built while over the plume and then carried away, with the youngest island currently above the active magma source.

Example: A city emergency manager near Mount Rainier in Washington state is most concerned about lahars, not lava flows. She explains to the city council that lahars pose a greater threat to surrounding communities. Why are lahars especially dangerous for cities near snow-capped stratovolcanoes?

Mount Rainier's massive glaciers can melt during volcanic activity, mixing with ash and rock to form lahars that can travel down river valleys at high speed for tens of kilometers, reaching populated lowland areas.
