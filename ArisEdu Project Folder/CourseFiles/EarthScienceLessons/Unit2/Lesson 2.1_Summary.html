Lesson 2.1: Continental Drift & Evidence - Summary

Key Concepts: Continental Drift & Evidence

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is continental drift?

Continental drift is the hypothesis, first proposed by Alfred Wegener, that Earth's continents are not fixed but have moved slowly across the surface over hundreds of millions of years.

What is Gondwana?

Gondwana (or Gondwanaland) was the southern supercontinent formed when Pangaea split, comprising South America, Africa, India, Australia, and Antarctica, which later separated into individual continents.

What is Laurasia?

Laurasia was the northern supercontinent that formed when Pangaea split into two halves, comprising what would become North America, Europe, and Asia.

A museum exhibit shows the distribution of four fossil organisms across the southern continents: Mesosaurus, Glossopteris, Lystrosaurus, and Cynognathus. A visitor asks why finding these on multiple continents is better evidence for drift than finding modern species shared across continents. What is the best response?

Ancient freshwater and land-dwelling organisms could not have crossed vast oceans, so their distribution across now-separated continents strongly indicates past land connections, unlike modern species that can be dispersed by various means.

A documentary filmmaker wants to illustrate what Earth looked like when Pangaea existed. She asks a geologist what the climate and environment would have been like in the interior of such a massive supercontinent. What is the most accurate description?

The vast interior of Pangaea would have been extremely arid because ocean moisture could not penetrate far inland across such an enormous landmass, creating extensive desert conditions in the continental interior.

A geology professor presents a map showing that the same distinctive sequence of rock layers, including glacial tillite, coal beds, and sandstone, appears in South America, Africa, India, Australia, and Antarctica. A student asks how this pattern specifically supports continental drift rather than coincidence. What is the strongest argument?

Finding the identical sequence of distinctive rock layers in the same chronological order on five now-separated continents is extraordinarily strong evidence that they were once a single landmass with a shared geological history.

Real-World Applications

Example: A geology student mapping rock formations in Scotland discovers that the Caledonian mountain rocks have the same age, composition, and structure as Appalachian mountain rocks in eastern North America. She hypothesizes these were once the same mountain chain. What evidence supports this claim?

The Appalachian and Caledonian mountains share matching rock types, ages, and geological structures, strongly supporting that they were once a single mountain chain before the Atlantic Ocean opened between them.
