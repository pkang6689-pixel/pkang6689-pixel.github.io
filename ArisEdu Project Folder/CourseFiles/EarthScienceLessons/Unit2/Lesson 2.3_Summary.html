Lesson 2.3: Plate Boundaries (Convergent, Divergent, Transform) - Summary

Key Concepts: Plate Boundaries (Convergent, Divergent, Transform)

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a convergent boundary?

A convergent boundary is where two tectonic plates move toward each other, which can result in subduction (one plate diving under the other), mountain building, or both, depending on the types of crust involved.

What is a transform boundary?

A transform boundary is where two plates grind horizontally past each other along a fault, generating earthquakes from friction but no volcanic activity since no crust is created or destroyed.

What is the Ring of Fire?

The Ring of Fire is a 40,000 km horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean where about 75% of the world's active volcanoes are found and about 90% of earthquakes occur, associated with subduction zones.

What is an island arc?

An island arc is a curved chain of volcanic islands that forms parallel to an oceanic trench at an oceanic-oceanic convergent boundary, where melted material from the subducting plate rises to form volcanoes.

What is a rift valley?

A rift valley is a linear lowland between highlands or mountain ranges created by the action of a geologic rift, where continental crust is being pulled apart at a divergent boundary, such as the East African Rift.

A volcanologist studying the Cascades in Washington state explains that Mount Rainier is a dangerous composite volcano. A journalist asks what tectonic process creates these explosive volcanoes. What is the correct answer?

The Cascade volcanoes form at an oceanic-continental convergent boundary where the Juan de Fuca plate subducts beneath North America; water released from the sinking plate lowers the mantle's melting point, generating magma.

A geography teacher asks her class to explain why Japan experiences frequent earthquakes and has many active volcanoes, while central Africa has relatively few of either. What is the best explanation based on plate tectonics?

Japan sits at the junction of several tectonic plates with active subduction zones, producing intense seismic and volcanic activity, while central Africa lies within the stable interior of the African Plate far from plate boundaries.

A mountaineer climbing in the Himalayas finds marine fossils at 8,000 meters elevation. She asks her guide how ocean creatures ended up at the top of the world's highest mountains. What is the explanation based on plate tectonics?

Before the collision, the Tethys Sea existed between India and Asia, depositing marine sediments on its floor; when the plates collided, these sea-floor sediments were compressed and uplifted thousands of meters.

A documentary crew visits the Mariana Trench, the deepest point in the ocean at nearly 11,000 meters. They ask a geologist to explain what created such an extreme depth. What is the correct explanation?

The Mariana Trench formed at a convergent boundary where the old, dense Pacific Plate subducts steeply beneath the Philippine Plate, bending downward into the mantle and creating the deepest ocean floor on Earth.

A geologist comparing the Andes and Himalayas notes both are massive mountain ranges formed at convergent boundaries but have different characteristics. The Andes have active volcanoes while the Himalayas do not. She asks her students to explain this difference. What is the correct answer?

The Andes have active volcanoes because the oceanic Nazca Plate subducts and melts, generating magma, while at the Himalayas, two buoyant continental plates collide without subduction, so there is no mechanism to produce volcanic melting.

Real-World Applications
