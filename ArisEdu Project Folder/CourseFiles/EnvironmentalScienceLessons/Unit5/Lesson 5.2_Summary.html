Key Concepts: Nuclear Energy

Overview

Key Definitions

What is nuclear fission?

Nuclear fission occurs when a neutron strikes a heavy nucleus like U-235, causing it to split into smaller nuclei while releasing energy and additional neutrons that sustain the chain reaction.

What is radioactive half-life?

Radioactive half-life is a constant for each isotope; after each half-life period, exactly half the remaining radioactive atoms have decayed, meaning the material never truly reaches zero but becomes progressively less radioactive.

What is a nuclear meltdown?

A meltdown occurs when cooling systems fail, causing fuel rods to overheat and melt through their containment, potentially releasing radioactive material into the environment, as occurred at Chernobyl and Fukushima.

What is spent nuclear fuel?

Spent fuel has been used in a reactor until it can no longer efficiently sustain fission, but it remains intensely radioactive due to fission products and must be carefully managed for thousands of years.

What is nuclear proliferation?

Nuclear proliferation is a security concern because the same technology used to enrich uranium for reactor fuel can be further enriched for weapons, and plutonium from spent fuel can potentially be extracted for bombs.

What is a chain reaction in nuclear physics?

In a chain reaction, each fission event releases 2-3 neutrons that can split other U-235 atoms, creating a self-sustaining process controlled in reactors by absorbing excess neutrons with control rods.

What is nuclear fusion?

Fusion powers the sun by combining hydrogen nuclei into helium at temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees, releasing far more energy per unit mass than fission with minimal radioactive waste.

What is an exclusion zone?

Exclusion zones like the 30-km zone around Chernobyl are areas deemed too contaminated for human habitation, often for decades or longer, though wildlife has paradoxically thrived in the absence of human activity.

What is enrichment in the context of nuclear fuel?

Natural uranium contains only 0.7% fissile U-235, so enrichment increases this percentage to 3-5% for reactor use through processes like gas centrifugation; weapons-grade uranium requires enrichment above 90%.

What is a cooling pond (spent fuel pool)?

Spent fuel pools provide water that both absorbs the intense heat from radioactive decay in recently removed fuel rods and shields workers from radiation, typically holding spent fuel for 5-10 years before dry cask storage.

Real-World Applications

Example: A major advantage of nuclear power over fossil fuels is that:

During operation, nuclear reactors produce electricity without burning fuel or emitting CO2, making nuclear power one of the lowest-carbon electricity sources, comparable to wind and solar on a lifecycle basis.

Example: Nuclear power currently provides approximately what percentage of global electricity?

Nuclear energy supplies approximately 10% of global electricity from about 440 operating reactors in 32 countries, with France generating the highest share of its electricity (about 70%) from nuclear power.

Example: For the AP exam, nuclear energy questions require students to:

AP questions on nuclear energy require holistic analysis balancing climate benefits against safety risks, waste challenges, economic costs, and social acceptance, demonstrating the complexity of energy policy decisions.
