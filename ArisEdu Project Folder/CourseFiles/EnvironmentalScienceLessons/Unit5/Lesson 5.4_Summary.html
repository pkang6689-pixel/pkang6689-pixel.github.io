Lesson 5.4: Energy Efficiency & Conservation - Summary

Key Concepts: Energy Efficiency & Conservation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary purpose of building insulation in energy conservation?

Insulation slows the transfer of heat through walls, roofs, and floors, keeping buildings warmer in winter and cooler in summer, thereby reducing the energy needed for heating and cooling.

What is cogeneration?

Cogeneration, also called combined heat and power (CHP), captures waste heat from electricity generation to use for heating or industrial processes, significantly increasing overall fuel efficiency.

What is an R-value in the context of building insulation?

R-value quantifies a material's thermal resistance, or its ability to resist heat flow. Higher R-values indicate better insulating properties and greater energy savings.

What is phantom load (also called vampire power)?

Phantom load is the electricity drawn by devices that are switched off or in standby mode but remain plugged in, such as phone chargers, televisions, and computers, which can account for 5-10% of household electricity use.

What is a thermal envelope in building design?

The thermal envelope includes all components that separate the heated or cooled interior from the outside environment, including walls, roof, foundation, windows, and doors, and its integrity determines how much energy is needed for climate control.

What is a green building certification such as LEED?

LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is a globally recognized green building certification system that rates buildings on sustainability criteria including energy efficiency, water conservation, materials selection, and indoor air quality.

What is demand-side management?

Demand-side management encompasses utility programs designed to encourage consumers to modify their electricity usage patterns, such as offering rebates for efficient appliances or time-of-use pricing to shift demand to off-peak hours.

What is the rebound effect in energy economics?

The rebound effect occurs when improved energy efficiency lowers the cost of using a service, leading people to use it more, thereby offsetting some or all of the expected energy savings from the efficiency improvement.

What is a net-zero energy building?

A net-zero energy building generates as much renewable energy on-site as it consumes annually, typically through a combination of extreme energy efficiency measures and on-site renewable energy production like rooftop solar.

What is the meaning of 'energy audit'?

An energy audit evaluates how energy is used in a building through inspections, measurements, and analysis, identifying areas where efficiency improvements can reduce consumption and lower costs.

An office building manager notices that energy consumption increases by 15% during winter despite the building being well-insulated. A thermal imaging survey reveals significant heat loss around windows and door frames. What is the most cost-effective first step?

Weatherstripping and caulking around windows and doors is one of the most cost-effective energy conservation measures, sealing air leaks that can account for 25-30% of heating energy loss in a building.

A country introduces strict appliance efficiency standards, requiring all new refrigerators to use 50% less electricity than models sold 10 years ago. Critics argue this will raise appliance prices. What is the typical long-term economic outcome of such standards?

Historical evidence shows that appliance efficiency standards lead to modest initial price increases that are more than recovered through lifetime energy savings. U.S. refrigerator standards reduced energy use by 75% while average prices actually decreased.

Real-World Applications

Example: What percentage of energy in a typical incandescent light bulb is lost as heat rather than light?

Traditional incandescent bulbs are extremely inefficient, converting approximately 90% of the electrical energy they consume into heat and only about 10% into visible light.

Example: What is demand-side management?

Demand-side management encompasses utility programs designed to encourage consumers to modify their electricity usage patterns, such as offering rebates for efficient appliances or time-of-use pricing to shift demand to off-peak hours.

Example: A city installs smart meters in all residences and introduces time-of-use pricing, where electricity costs three times more during peak hours (2-7 PM) than off-peak hours. What behavioral change does this pricing strategy aim to encourage?

Time-of-use pricing incentivizes consumers to run dishwashers, laundry machines, and other flexible loads during off-peak hours, flattening the demand curve and reducing the need for expensive, often fossil-fuel-powered peaking plants.

Example: An office building manager notices that energy consumption increases by 15% during winter despite the building being well-insulated. A thermal imaging survey reveals significant heat loss around windows and door frames. What is the most cost-effective first step?

Weatherstripping and caulking around windows and doors is one of the most cost-effective energy conservation measures, sealing air leaks that can account for 25-30% of heating energy loss in a building.

Example: A large data center consumes 50 megawatts of electricity, with 40% used for cooling the servers. The facility manager is considering relocating the data center to a Nordic country with cold ambient temperatures. How would this relocation improve energy efficiency?

In cold climates, data centers can use outside air for free cooling instead of energy-intensive mechanical chillers. Since 40% of the facility's 50 MW (20 MW) goes to cooling, free cooling could save most of that energy.
