Key Concepts: AP Prep: Systems Thinking

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a feedback loop?

A feedback loop is a mechanism where the output of a process cycles back to affect the input, creating either a self-reinforcing (positive) or self-correcting (negative) cycle within the system.

What is homeostasis?

Homeostasis is the ability of a system to maintain internal stability through negative feedback mechanisms that counteract disturbances, keeping conditions within a viable range for the system to function.

What is a tipping point in environmental science?

An environmental tipping point is a critical threshold where a relatively small change causes a disproportionately large and often irreversible shift in system behavior, like the collapse of a fishery.

What is resilience in the context of environmental systems?

Resilience is the capacity of an ecosystem or environmental system to withstand disturbances (like fires, floods, or pollution) and return to its previous functional state rather than shifting to a new one.

What is albedo?

Albedo measures the reflectivity of a surface, expressed as a fraction from 0 (absorbs all light) to 1 (reflects all light). Snow and ice have high albedo, while dark surfaces like asphalt have low albedo.

What is an emergent property?

Emergent properties arise from interactions between system components and cannot be predicted by studying parts in isolation. For example, the ability of a forest to regulate climate emerges from the collective action of trees, soil, and water.

What is a system in environmental science?

In environmental science, a system is a set of interconnected components that work together as a unified whole, with defined boundaries, inputs (energy, matter), internal processes, and outputs.

What is a steady state?

A steady state occurs when system inputs balance outputs, maintaining overall stability even though processes continue within the system. It does not mean the system is static or unchanging internally.

What is systems thinking?

Systems thinking is an analytical approach that considers how individual components interact within a larger system, recognizing that the behavior of the whole emerges from these interconnections.

Real-World Applications

Example: The ice-albedo feedback is an example of a:

The ice-albedo feedback is a positive feedback loop: warming melts reflective ice, exposing darker ocean or land that absorbs more solar radiation, causing further warming and more melting.

Example: Body temperature regulation in humans is an example of a:

Human thermoregulation is a classic negative feedback loop: when body temperature rises, sweating cools the body; when it falls, shivering generates heat, maintaining a stable internal temperature.

Example: As Arctic temperatures rise, permafrost (permanently frozen soil) begins to thaw, releasing methane — a potent greenhouse gas. This methane causes more warming, which thaws more permafrost, releasing even more methane. This scenario is an example of:

The permafrost-methane feedback is a positive feedback loop: warming thaws permafrost, releasing methane that causes more warming, which thaws more permafrost. This self-amplifying cycle accelerates climate change.

Example: A forest is cleared for agriculture. Without tree roots holding soil, erosion increases, making it harder for trees to regrow. Without trees, more soil erodes, making regrowth even harder. This degradation cycle is an example of:

This is a positive feedback loop of degradation: tree removal causes erosion, which prevents regrowth, causing more erosion. Such cycles can turn forests into barren landscapes without intervention.

This scenario describes a regime shift where the coral reef ecosystem crosses a tipping point and transitions to a fundamentally different state (algae-dominated) that is resistant to reversal.

Example: A city builds more roads to reduce traffic congestion. However, the new roads attract more drivers (induced demand), creating even worse congestion, leading to demands for yet more roads. This urban planning problem is an example of:

Induced demand is a positive feedback loop in transportation systems: building more roads encourages more driving, which increases congestion, prompting more road-building. Systems thinking reveals why this approach fails.

Example: For the AP Environmental Science exam, a question presents a diagram showing rising global temperatures leading to increased water vapor in the atmosphere (a greenhouse gas), which causes further warming. You are asked to classify and explain this. The correct response identifies this as:

The water vapor feedback is a positive feedback loop: warming increases evaporation, adding more water vapor (a greenhouse gas) to the atmosphere, which traps more heat and amplifies the original warming.
