Lesson 4.1: Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources - Summary

Key Concepts: Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a nonrenewable resource?

Nonrenewable resources such as fossil fuels and mineral ores exist in finite amounts because they formed over geological timescales far exceeding a human lifetime.

What is sustainability in the context of resource use?

Sustainability, as defined by the Brundtland Commission in 1987, refers to using resources at a rate that allows natural systems to replenish them, ensuring long-term availability for future generations.

What is resource depletion?

Resource depletion occurs when the rate of extraction or consumption exceeds the rate of natural replenishment, ultimately reducing the total available stock of that resource.

What is the ecological footprint?

The ecological footprint is a measure of human demand on ecosystems, calculated as the area of land and water required to produce the resources consumed and absorb the wastes generated by a population.

What is an ore?

An ore is a rock that contains a sufficient concentration of a valuable mineral or metal to make mining and extraction profitable, and ore deposits are considered nonrenewable.

What is the tragedy of the commons?

Described by Garrett Hardin in 1968, the tragedy of the commons occurs when multiple users, each acting rationally in their own interest, collectively deplete or degrade a shared resource like fisheries, pastureland, or clean air.

What is biomass energy?

Biomass energy is produced by burning or converting organic materials like wood, agricultural residues, and animal waste into heat, electricity, or biofuels, and it is considered renewable when sourced sustainably.

What is a carbon footprint?

A carbon footprint measures the total greenhouse gas emissions, usually expressed in CO2 equivalents, caused by an individual, event, organization, or product throughout its lifecycle.

What is natural capital?

Natural capital includes Earth's natural resources such as minerals, water, and air, as well as ecosystem services like pollination, water purification, and climate regulation that sustain life and economic activity.

A farmer in a semi-arid region is pumping groundwater from an aquifer at twice the rate it is being recharged by rainfall. If this practice continues, what is the most likely long-term consequence?

When groundwater is extracted faster than natural recharge, the water table drops, wells go dry, and the land above can collapse in a process called subsidence, effectively making the aquifer a nonrenewable resource.

A country with large petroleum reserves decides to invest its oil revenues into developing wind and solar infrastructure. What is the most likely long-term benefit of this strategy?

By using revenue from a finite resource to build renewable energy infrastructure, the country is planning for a sustainable energy future that will continue to provide power long after its oil reserves are exhausted.

A village in sub-Saharan Africa relies on wood as its primary fuel for cooking and heating. As the population grows, surrounding forests are being cleared faster than trees can regrow. What is happening to this renewable resource?

Although trees are renewable, harvesting them faster than they can regrow depletes the forest, demonstrating that even renewable resources can become effectively nonrenewable when the rate of consumption exceeds the rate of regeneration.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is the tragedy of the commons?

Described by Garrett Hardin in 1968, the tragedy of the commons occurs when multiple users, each acting rationally in their own interest, collectively deplete or degrade a shared resource like fisheries, pastureland, or clean air.

Example: A coastal community relies heavily on fishing for its economy. Over the past decade, fish populations have declined by 60% due to overharvesting. Which approach would best help this community manage its fishery sustainably?

Sustainable fishery management combines catch limits to prevent overharvesting, marine protected areas where fish can reproduce undisturbed, and recovery periods to allow depleted populations to rebuild.

Example: A city government decides to install solar panels on all public buildings and convert its bus fleet to electric vehicles. Which principle of resource management does this best represent?

This city is reducing its dependence on finite fossil fuels by switching to solar power and electric transportation, which represents a proactive shift toward renewable energy and long-term sustainability.
