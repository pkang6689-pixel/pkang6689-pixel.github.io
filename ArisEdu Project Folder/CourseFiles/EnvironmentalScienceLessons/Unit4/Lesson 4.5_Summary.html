Lesson 4.5: Fisheries & Ocean Resources - Summary

Key Concepts: Fisheries & Ocean Resources

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is overfishing?

Overfishing occurs when fish are caught faster than they can breed and replace themselves, causing population declines that can lead to fishery collapse if the reproductive capacity drops below a critical threshold.

What is maximum sustainable yield (MSY)?

Maximum sustainable yield is a fisheries management concept representing the highest average catch that can be continuously harvested from a stock under existing conditions without depleting it.

What is aquaculture?

Aquaculture involves breeding, raising, and harvesting aquatic plants and animals in controlled environments, ranging from inland pond systems for tilapia to ocean net pens for salmon and coastal farms for shrimp.

What is a dead zone in the ocean?

Dead zones are areas of severely depleted oxygen caused by algal blooms fueled by nutrient runoff; when the algae die and decompose, bacteria consume the available oxygen, making the water uninhabitable for fish and shellfish.

What is biomagnification in the context of ocean food chains?

Biomagnification concentrates persistent toxins at each trophic level because predators consume many contaminated prey, accumulating increasingly higher toxin concentrations, which is why top predators like tuna have the highest mercury levels.

What is coral bleaching?

Coral bleaching occurs when ocean temperatures rise above normal, causing corals to expel the colorful zooxanthellae algae that provide them with food and color; prolonged bleaching leads to coral death.

What is a fishery collapse?

A fishery collapse occurs when overfishing reduces a population below its reproductive threshold, making recovery extremely slow or impossible without a complete halt to fishing, as happened with Atlantic cod.

What is sustainable fishing?

Sustainable fishing maintains catches at or below the maximum sustainable yield, uses methods that minimize bycatch and habitat damage, and ensures that fish populations remain healthy enough to reproduce for future generations.

What is an exclusive economic zone (EEZ)?

The EEZ established by the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea gives coastal nations sovereign rights over marine resources within 200 nautical miles of their shores, including fishing, mining, and energy production.

What is Illegal, Unreported, and Unregulated (IUU) fishing?

IUU fishing accounts for up to 26 million tons of fish caught annually, worth $10-23 billion, undermining fishery management, depleting stocks, harming marine ecosystems, and disadvantaging law-abiding fishers.

Real-World Applications

Example: Approximately what percentage of the world's fish stocks are currently overfished or fully exploited?

According to the FAO, about 34% of fish stocks are overfished (harvested beyond sustainable levels) and approximately 60% are fully exploited, leaving very few stocks with room for increased catch.

Example: Coral reefs support approximately what percentage of marine species?

Coral reefs are among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth, supporting roughly 25% of all marine species while occupying less than 1% of the ocean floor, earning them the nickname 'rainforests of the sea.'

Example: Individual transferable quotas (ITQs) are a fishery management tool that works by:

ITQs give each fisher a guaranteed share of the sustainable catch limit, eliminating the race-to-fish mentality and giving fishers a long-term stake in maintaining healthy fish populations.

Example: Fisheries managers set a total allowable catch (TAC) of 100,000 tons for a particular fish species. However, illegal fishing vessels harvest an additional 40,000 tons unreported. The ecological consequence of this IUU fishing is:

When illegal catch is added to legal harvest, the total exceeds the scientifically determined sustainable limit, undermining conservation measures and accelerating population decline toward potential collapse.

Example: A coastal community in Southeast Asia establishes a community-managed no-take zone covering 30% of their local reef. After five years, they notice larger and more abundant fish both inside and outside the protected area. This demonstrates:

The spillover effect occurs when protected areas allow fish to grow larger and produce more offspring that emigrate into surrounding waters, effectively increasing catches for local fishers in the long term.

Example: A government debates whether to subsidize its fishing fleet with cheaper fuel and boat-building grants. Economists argue these subsidies encourage overfishing. The environmental reasoning behind this argument is:

Harmful fishing subsidies estimated at $22 billion annually keep unprofitable fishing vessels operating by covering costs that would otherwise make overfishing uneconomical, effectively paying fishers to deplete fish stocks.
