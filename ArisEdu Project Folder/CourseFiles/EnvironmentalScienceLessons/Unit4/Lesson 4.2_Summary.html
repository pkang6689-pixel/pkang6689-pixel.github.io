Key Concepts: Water Resources & Management

Overview

Key Definitions

What is an aquifer?

An aquifer is a geological formation of porous rock, gravel, or sand beneath the surface that holds and transmits significant quantities of groundwater, serving as a critical fresh water source for wells and springs.

What is a watershed?

A watershed, also called a drainage basin, is the entire land area that channels rainfall and snowmelt into a common body of water, meaning pollution anywhere in the watershed can affect downstream water quality.

What is the water table?

The water table marks the level below which the ground is fully saturated with water, and it rises and falls with seasonal precipitation and pumping; wells must reach below it to access groundwater.

What is groundwater recharge?

Groundwater recharge occurs when rainfall, snowmelt, or surface water percolates through soil and rock to reach the saturated zone, replenishing the aquifer over periods ranging from years to centuries.

What is water stress?

Water stress occurs when a region's water demand approaches or exceeds its sustainable supply, often defined as withdrawing more than 25% of available renewable freshwater resources annually.

What is land subsidence in relation to water resources?

When excessive groundwater pumping removes water from pore spaces in underground sediments, the overlying land can sink permanently as soil compacts, causing structural damage and reduced aquifer storage capacity.

What is a water footprint?

A water footprint accounts for all fresh water consumed throughout a product's supply chain; for example, producing one pound of beef requires approximately 1,800 gallons of water for feed, drinking, and processing.

What is gray water?

Gray water is household wastewater excluding toilet waste that can be safely reused for non-potable purposes like landscape irrigation, reducing overall fresh water demand by up to 30-50% in some households.

What is virtual water?

Virtual water refers to the total water consumed during the production of a commodity; when countries import water-intensive goods like grain or cotton, they are effectively importing the water used to grow them.

What is xeriscaping?

Xeriscaping is a water-conservation approach to landscaping that uses drought-tolerant native plants, mulch, and efficient irrigation to dramatically reduce outdoor water use, which typically accounts for 30-60% of residential water consumption.

A farming community in California's Central Valley notices wells going dry and land sinking by several feet over the past decade. What is the most likely cause?

Excessive groundwater extraction for agriculture lowers the water table, causing wells to dry up, and the removal of water from underground pore spaces causes irreversible compaction and land subsidence.

Real-World Applications

Example: What percentage of Earth's water is fresh water available for human use?

While about 2.5% of Earth's water is fresh, most is locked in glaciers and ice caps, leaving less than 1% readily accessible in rivers, lakes, and groundwater for human use.

Example: Agriculture accounts for approximately what percentage of global freshwater withdrawals?

Agriculture is the largest consumer of fresh water globally, using approximately 70% of all withdrawals primarily for crop irrigation in both developed and developing nations.

Example: Dams provide hydroelectric power and water storage, but a major environmental drawback is:

Dams disrupt aquatic ecosystems by blocking migratory fish like salmon, changing water temperature and flow patterns, trapping sediment that would naturally replenish downstream habitats, and flooding upstream areas.

Tiered pricing creates an economic incentive for conservation by charging progressively higher rates for higher usage, while mandatory restrictions on non-essential uses ensure immediate demand reduction during the crisis.

Example: Israel recycles approximately 85% of its wastewater for agricultural irrigation. A water-scarce country wants to replicate this model. The main advantage of wastewater recycling is:

Treated wastewater recycling provides a consistent water source independent of rainfall, reduces pollutant discharge into natural waterways, and frees up fresh water supplies for drinking purposes.

Inequitable water distribution is an environmental justice issue because access to clean water is a basic human need, and disadvantaged communities disproportionately bear the burden of water scarcity and infrastructure failures.
