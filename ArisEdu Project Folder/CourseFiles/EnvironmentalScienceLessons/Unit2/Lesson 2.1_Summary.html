Lesson 2.1: Energy Flow in Ecosystems - Summary

Key Concepts: Energy Flow in Ecosystems

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a trophic level?

A trophic level is each step in a food chain or food web where energy is transferred. Producers are the first level, primary consumers second, secondary consumers third, and so on.

What is gross primary productivity (GPP)?

Gross primary productivity is the total amount of energy captured through photosynthesis per unit area per unit time. It represents all carbon fixed by producers before subtracting respiratory losses.

What is net primary productivity (NPP)?

Net primary productivity is the rate at which producers store energy as biomass after using some for their own metabolism. It represents the energy actually available to be consumed by herbivores.

What is the 10% rule in ecology?

The 10% rule is an ecological approximation stating that roughly 10% of energy at each trophic level is passed to the next level. The remaining 90% is used for metabolism and lost as heat.

What is an energy pyramid?

An energy pyramid (ecological pyramid of energy) shows the amount of energy at each trophic level. It is always upright because energy decreases at higher levels due to heat loss at each transfer.

What is entropy?

Entropy is a measure of disorder that always increases during energy transformations (second law of thermodynamics). In ecosystems, entropy explains why energy is progressively lost as unusable heat.

What is assimilation efficiency?

Assimilation efficiency is the fraction of ingested food that an organism actually absorbs across its gut wall for use in metabolism and growth, versus what passes through as feces.

What is ecological efficiency?

Ecological efficiency (also called food chain efficiency) is the percentage of energy at one trophic level that is incorporated into biomass at the next trophic level, typically averaging around 10%.

What is standing crop biomass?

Standing crop biomass is the total amount of living biological material at a given trophic level or in an ecosystem at a particular moment in time, usually measured as dry mass per unit area.

What is cellular respiration in the context of energy flow?

Cellular respiration is the process by which all living organisms convert organic molecules into ATP energy, releasing CO2, water, and heat. It accounts for the majority of energy loss at each trophic level.

Real-World Applications

Example: Approximately what percentage of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next?

The 10% rule states that approximately 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed to the next. The remaining 90% is lost primarily as heat through cellular respiration and metabolic processes.

Example: A conservation manager must choose between protecting a 1,000-acre tropical rainforest or a 1,000-acre tundra ecosystem. From an energy flow perspective, protecting the rainforest would preserve more biological productivity because:

Tropical rainforests produce roughly 2,200 g/m2/year of NPP compared to about 140 g/m2/year in tundra, making them far more productive per unit area and supporting far greater biodiversity.

Example: An AP exam question asks why there are far fewer lions than zebras on the African savanna. The best answer, based on energy flow principles, is:

The 10% rule limits how many predators an ecosystem can support. Since only ~10% of zebra energy transfers to lions, far fewer lions can be sustained, which is why predators are always rarer than their prey.
