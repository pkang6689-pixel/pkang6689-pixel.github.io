Lesson 2.3: Population Dynamics - Summary

Key Concepts: Population Dynamics

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is carrying capacity?

Carrying capacity represents the long-term equilibrium population size determined by resource availability. It can change over time as environmental conditions, technology, or resource availability change.

What is exponential growth?

Exponential growth occurs when a population increases by a constant proportion each generation, resulting in ever-accelerating growth that produces a characteristic J-shaped curve.

What is logistic growth?

Logistic growth models the reality that resources are limited. Growth starts fast but slows due to increasing competition and resource scarcity, leveling off at the environment's carrying capacity.

What is a density-dependent factor?

Density-dependent factors have impacts proportional to population density. Disease spreads faster in dense populations, competition intensifies, and predators focus on abundant prey.

What is a density-independent factor?

Density-independent factors impact populations regardless of their size or density. A severe drought, volcanic eruption, or flood can kill organisms whether the population is large or small.

What is a population crash (die-off)?

A population crash occurs when a population that has exceeded its carrying capacity (overshoot) rapidly declines due to resource depletion, starvation, disease, or other limiting factors.

What is biotic potential?

Biotic potential (rmax) is the maximum rate at which a population can grow when resources are unlimited and environmental conditions are ideal. It varies greatly among species.

What is environmental resistance?

Environmental resistance is the combination of all biotic and abiotic factors that prevent a population from reaching its biotic potential, including predation, disease, limited food, and habitat constraints.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Disease is a density-dependent factor because pathogens spread more easily when individuals are crowded together, increasing contact rates. Sparse populations have lower transmission rates.
