Lesson 2.2: Food Chains & Food Webs - Summary

Key Concepts: Food Chains & Food Webs

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a food chain?

A food chain is a linear pathway showing the transfer of energy and nutrients from one organism to the next, starting with a producer and moving through successive consumers at higher trophic levels.

What is a food web?

A food web is a comprehensive diagram showing all the interconnected feeding relationships in an ecosystem, representing the complex reality that most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple species.

What is a keystone species?

A keystone species has an outsized impact on ecosystem structure and function relative to its numbers. Removing it causes dramatic changes, like sea stars controlling mussel populations on rocky shores.

What is a trophic cascade?

A trophic cascade occurs when a predator's presence or absence indirectly affects organisms two or more trophic levels away. For example, wolves suppress elk, allowing willow trees to recover along rivers.

What is a detrital food chain?

A detrital food chain begins with dead organic matter and passes energy through organisms that consume and decompose it. In many forest and marine ecosystems, the detrital chain processes most of the energy.

What is an invasive species?

An invasive species is a non-native organism that, when introduced to a new environment, spreads rapidly and causes harm because it lacks the natural predators, diseases, or competitors that control it in its native range.

What is a generalist species?

Generalist species like raccoons and crows can thrive in many different environments and eat a wide variety of foods. This flexibility makes them more resilient to environmental changes than specialists.

What is a specialist species?

Specialist species depend on specific food sources or habitats, like koalas (eucalyptus leaves only) or giant pandas (bamboo). This narrow niche makes them particularly vulnerable to habitat loss.

What is bioaccumulation?

Bioaccumulation is the progressive buildup of substances like pesticides or heavy metals in an individual organism's tissues because the rate of intake exceeds the rate of excretion or metabolism.

What is biomagnification?

Biomagnification amplifies toxin concentrations up the food chain. Since predators consume many contaminated prey, they accumulate much higher concentrations, which is why DDT devastated apex predators like eagles.
