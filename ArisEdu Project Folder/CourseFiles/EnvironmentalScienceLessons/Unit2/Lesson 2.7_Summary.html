Key Concepts: Case Studies in Ecology

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a trophic cascade?

A trophic cascade occurs when changes at one trophic level cause cascading effects through the food web. For example, removing top predators allows herbivore populations to explode, which can overgraze vegetation and alter habitat structure for many other species.

What is eutrophication?

Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients, primarily nitrogen and phosphorus, enter a water body and stimulate excessive algal growth. When these algae die and decompose, the process depletes dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic or anoxic dead zones that cannot support most aquatic life.

What is bioaccumulation?

Bioaccumulation is the process by which chemicals such as pesticides, heavy metals, or other toxins accumulate in an organism's tissues faster than they can be metabolized or excreted. Over time, even low environmental concentrations can reach harmful levels within individual organisms.

What is biomagnification?

Biomagnification occurs when toxin concentrations increase at each successive level of a food chain. Top predators accumulate the highest concentrations because they consume large numbers of contaminated prey. DDT biomagnification in bald eagles causing eggshell thinning is a classic example.

What is an ecological dead zone?

An ecological dead zone is a hypoxic area in a body of water where dissolved oxygen levels are too low to support most marine life. Dead zones are typically caused by eutrophication, where excess nutrients stimulate algal blooms whose decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen.

What is coral bleaching?

Coral bleaching occurs when environmental stressors, most commonly elevated water temperatures, cause corals to expel the symbiotic zooxanthellae algae that provide them with food and color. Without these algae, the coral appears white and may die if conditions do not improve quickly.

What is the term 'overfishing' defined as in fisheries ecology?

Overfishing occurs when fish are caught faster than populations can reproduce, leading to declining stocks. When harvest rates consistently exceed the maximum sustainable yield, populations can collapse, as demonstrated by the Atlantic cod fishery off Newfoundland.

What is maximum sustainable yield (MSY)?

Maximum sustainable yield is the largest number of individuals that can be harvested from a renewable resource population without reducing the population's ability to sustain itself. Exceeding MSY over time leads to population decline and potential collapse.

What is a keystone predator?

A keystone predator is a predator whose ecological influence on a community is disproportionately large relative to its abundance. The sea star Pisaster ochraceus in Pacific intertidal zones is a classic example, as its removal allows mussels to dominate and exclude other species.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

The Exxon Valdez spill demonstrated that ecological recovery from pollution events can take decades. Oil persisted in beach sediments for years, continuing to harm organisms, and the loss of breeding adults created a demographic bottleneck that slowed population recovery long after the initial spill.

This scenario describes ecological restoration of a brownfield site, where contaminated or degraded land is actively rehabilitated to support native ecosystems. Example: A conservation group manages to restore wetlands along a river that feeds into a lake suffering from harmful algal blooms. Over the following years, algal blooms decrease significantly. Local officials ask whether the wetlands or new wastewater treatment plants deserve the credit. How would an ecologist evaluate this?

Environmental problems typically have multiple causes and benefit from multiple solutions. Wetlands naturally filter nutrients from agricultural runoff while wastewater treatment reduces point-source nutrient inputs. Evaluating the relative contribution of each requires monitoring nutrient loads from different sources.
