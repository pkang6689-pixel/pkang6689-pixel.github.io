Lesson 9.5: Case Studies in Sustainable Practices - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies: Sustainable Practices

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) as practiced in Costa Rica?

Costa Rica's PES program pays private landowners for the ecosystem services their forests provide, including carbon sequestration, watershed protection, biodiversity conservation, and scenic beauty, creating economic incentives for conservation.

What is Iceland's approach to using its geothermal resources?

Iceland leverages its volcanic geology to provide nearly all building heating through geothermal district systems, generate about 70% of total energy from geothermal and hydropower, and attract energy-intensive industries like aluminum smelting powered by clean energy.

What is the role of traditional ecological knowledge in sustainability?

Traditional ecological knowledge developed by indigenous communities over millennia represents time-tested approaches to living sustainably with natural systems, offering valuable insights into resource management, biodiversity conservation, and climate adaptation.

What is the role of technology in sustainability case studies?

Across all case studies, technology plays an important enabling role but is never sufficient on its own. Successful sustainability always combines technological solutions with supportive policies, community engagement, institutional capacity, and cultural change.

What is a Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) system?

Bus Rapid Transit systems use dedicated lanes, level boarding platforms, off-board fare collection, and high-frequency service to provide subway-quality transit at a fraction of the construction cost, pioneered by Curitiba, Brazil.

What is Gross National Happiness (GNH)?

GNH is a comprehensive development philosophy that measures nine domains of well-being, including psychological health, ecology, governance, and cultural resilience, providing a far richer picture of national progress than economic output alone.

What is NEWater in Singapore?

NEWater is Singapore's innovative water recycling system that treats used water through microfiltration, reverse osmosis, and UV disinfection to produce ultra-clean water, meeting approximately 40% of the city-state's water demand and reducing dependence on imported water.

What is the overarching lesson from sustainability case studies across different countries?

Case studies from diverse countries (wealthy and developing, large and small) demonstrate that sustainability is achievable when political will, community engagement, innovative policy, and investment align, but solutions must be adapted to local conditions.

Real-World Applications

Example: Approximately what percentage of Costa Rica's electricity comes from renewable sources?

Costa Rica generates approximately 99% of its electricity from renewable sources, primarily hydropower supplemented by wind, geothermal, and solar energy, demonstrating that near-100% renewable electricity is achievable.

Example: A mid-sized American city wants to reduce car dependence. The mayor visited Freiburg and Curitiba for inspiration. The city has limited rail infrastructure but good bus coverage, some bike lanes, and a downtown struggling with declining foot traffic. Which combination of strategies from these case studies would be most applicable?

Drawing from both case studies: Curitiba's BRT model provides high-quality transit on existing roads without rail costs, Freiburg's car-free districts revitalize downtowns, and both cities show that bike infrastructure and mixed-use development reduce car dependence effectively.

Example: A tropical developing country has extensive rainforests but faces pressure to clear them for agriculture and cattle ranching. The government wants to follow Costa Rica's example. What specific policies would be needed to replicate Costa Rica's forest recovery success?

Costa Rica's success required multiple reinforcing policies: PES provided economic incentives for conservation, protected areas safeguarded critical ecosystems, ecotourism created alternative livelihoods, and enforcement deterred illegal clearing, addressing both the push and pull factors of deforestation.

Example: Denmark achieved 50% wind energy through decades of supportive policy, community ownership, and industrial development. A country just beginning its renewable energy transition wants to reach similar goals. What key factors from Denmark's experience are most important to replicate?

Denmark's success was built on consistent long-term policy that gave investors confidence, community co-ownership that turned potential opponents into supporters, domestic manufacturing that created jobs, and careful grid planning to manage variable wind generation.

Example: A large city in a developing country wants to improve its waste management system. It has very limited budget but an active informal waste-picking sector. Looking at Rwanda's plastic bag ban and Curitiba's waste programs, what integrated approach could work in this context?

An integrated approach combines policy (plastic ban from Rwanda), innovative incentives (waste-for-food from Curitiba), social inclusion (formalizing existing waste pickers into cooperative recycling enterprises), and local solutions (community composting), achieving sustainability within budget constraints.

Example: An AP exam question asks students to evaluate whether Bhutan's sustainability model could be applied to a large industrialized nation. What are the main arguments for and against transferability?

Bhutan offers transferable principles (holistic well-being metrics, constitutional environmental guarantees) but direct replication is impractical for large industrial nations due to scale, existing industrial infrastructure, different cultural contexts, and the impossibility of achieving carbon-negative status through forestry alone.

Example: A city planner is writing a sustainability plan for a rapidly growing African city and wants to draw from multiple case studies. The city has abundant solar resources, a growing population, informal settlements, limited public transit, and water scarcity. Which combination of lessons from the case studies would be most relevant?

Effective sustainability planning draws selectively from multiple case studies: Curitiba's affordable BRT for transit, Singapore's water recycling adapted to local scale, Rwanda's policy leadership for developing-country context, solar deployment leveraging local resources, and community engagement principles from across case studies.
