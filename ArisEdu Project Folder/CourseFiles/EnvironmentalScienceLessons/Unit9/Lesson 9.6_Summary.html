Lesson 9.6: AP Prep: Sustainability Metrics - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Sustainability Metrics

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is Earth Overshoot Day?

Earth Overshoot Day marks the date when humanity's ecological resource consumption for the year exceeds what Earth can regenerate. It has been moving earlier each decade, currently falling in late July, meaning we use about 1.7 Earths' worth of resources annually.

What is the concept of decoupling in sustainability?

Decoupling refers to breaking the link between economic growth and environmental degradation. Absolute decoupling means total environmental impact decreases even as GDP increases, while relative decoupling means impact grows more slowly than GDP.

What is the Environmental Performance Index (EPI)?

The EPI ranks countries on two broad categories, environmental health (air quality, water, sanitation) and ecosystem vitality (climate, biodiversity, fisheries), using over 40 performance indicators to enable cross-country comparison of environmental governance.

What is the Gini coefficient and how does it relate to sustainability?

The Gini coefficient measures income inequality (0 = perfect equality, 1 = maximum inequality) and relates to the social pillar of sustainability because high inequality undermines social cohesion, health outcomes, and equitable access to resources.

Using the IPAT equation, a country doubles its population (P x 2), triples its per capita consumption (A x 3), but adopts technology that is 50% more efficient (T x 0.5). What is the net change in environmental impact?

Using IPAT: new I = P(x2) x A(x3) x T(x0.5) = 3 times original impact. Despite 50% efficiency improvement, doubled population and tripled consumption result in a net tripling of environmental impact, illustrating why efficiency alone is insufficient.

An AP exam question provides these data for Country Z: Population = 10 million, Per capita ecological footprint = 4 gha, Per capita biocapacity = 6 gha. The question asks whether Country Z is living sustainably. What is the correct analysis?

Country Z has an ecological surplus (biocapacity 6 > footprint 4), meaning it consumes less than its ecosystems can regenerate. However, ecological footprint does not capture all sustainability dimensions; the country could still have water scarcity, pollution, or social equity issues.

Real-World Applications

Example: An AP exam question provides these data for Country Z: Population = 10 million, Per capita ecological footprint = 4 gha, Per capita biocapacity = 6 gha. The question asks whether Country Z is living sustainably. What is the correct analysis?

Example: A student argues that since global GDP has risen while some environmental indicators have improved in wealthy countries, the world is successfully decoupling economic growth from environmental damage. A critical analysis of this claim should consider which of the following factors?

Critical evaluation requires checking: absolute vs. relative decoupling, whether pollution was offshored (consumption-based vs. production-based metrics), whether all environmental dimensions improved (not just carbon), and whether the rate of improvement is fast enough to stay within planetary boundaries.
