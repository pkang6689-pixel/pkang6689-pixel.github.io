Lesson 9.4: Green Building & Urban Planning - Summary

Key Concepts: Green Building & Urban Planning

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is transit-oriented development (TOD)?

Transit-oriented development concentrates housing, shops, offices, and services within walking distance of transit stations, creating compact, vibrant neighborhoods that reduce car dependence and associated emissions while improving quality of life.

What is a net-zero energy building?

A net-zero energy building produces at least as much renewable energy as it consumes over a year through a combination of high energy efficiency, passive design strategies, and on-site renewable energy generation like rooftop solar.

What is low-impact development (LID)?

Low-impact development uses green infrastructure techniques like permeable pavement, rain gardens, bioswales, and green roofs to manage stormwater at its source, mimicking natural hydrological processes rather than channeling runoff through pipes.

What is embodied carbon in buildings?

Embodied carbon refers to the total greenhouse gas emissions generated during the extraction, manufacturing, transportation, and installation of building materials. As operational efficiency improves, embodied carbon becomes an increasingly important share of a building's total carbon footprint.

What is the 15-minute city concept?

The 15-minute city concept envisions urban neighborhoods where residents can access all essential daily services, employment, education, healthcare, shopping, and recreation, within a 15-minute walk or bicycle ride, reducing car dependence.

What is adaptive reuse of buildings?

Adaptive reuse transforms existing structures for new purposes (warehouse to loft apartments, factory to cultural center), preserving the embodied carbon in existing materials, reducing demolition waste, and revitalizing communities while consuming fewer resources than new construction.

What is a heat island mitigation strategy?

Heat island mitigation uses multiple strategies: reflective (cool) roofs and pavements that reflect rather than absorb solar energy, urban tree planting for shade and evaporative cooling, green roofs and walls, and green infrastructure that cools through evapotranspiration.

What is the key principle of sustainable urban design?

Sustainable urban design integrates compact form, mixed uses, multimodal transportation, green infrastructure, and energy-efficient buildings to create communities that consume fewer resources, produce fewer emissions, and provide higher quality of life.

Real-World Applications

Example: How do bioswales and rain gardens manage stormwater?

Bioswales and rain gardens are green infrastructure features that capture stormwater runoff, filter pollutants through vegetation and engineered soil media, promote infiltration into the ground, and reduce the volume and velocity of water flowing to storm drains.

Example: A rapidly growing city in a hot climate is experiencing severe water shortages and urban heat. New developments on the city's edge consume farmland and require extensive car travel. The mayor wants a comprehensive sustainability plan. What integrated approach would address water, heat, land use, and transportation simultaneously?

An integrated approach addresses multiple challenges simultaneously: compact development conserves land, green roofs cool buildings and capture rainwater, permeable surfaces recharge groundwater, urban trees provide shade, transit reduces car emissions, and water recycling conserves supply.

Example: A city's complete streets program redesigns a four-lane road into a two-lane road with protected bike lanes, widened sidewalks, street trees, and a bus-only lane. Business owners initially fear losing customers due to reduced car lanes. One year later, pedestrian traffic is up 40%, cycling has tripled, bus ridership increased 25%, and retail sales have grown. What does this outcome illustrate about sustainable transportation planning?

This outcome shows that well-designed complete streets can boost local economic activity by making areas more inviting for pedestrians who browse and shop, while also reducing emissions, improving air quality, encouraging physical activity, and enhancing transit efficiency.
