Key Concepts: Sustainable Energy

Key Definitions

What is the current cost competitiveness of onshore wind energy?

Onshore wind energy has become one of the cheapest sources of new electricity generation globally, with costs that are competitive with or lower than new fossil fuel power plants in most markets.

What is the main challenge with solar and wind energy?

The primary challenge for solar and wind energy is intermittency: their output varies with weather conditions and time of day, requiring energy storage, grid flexibility, or backup generation to ensure reliable power supply.

What is the 'duck curve' in energy planning?

The duck curve describes how high midday solar generation reduces net demand to very low levels, then as the sun sets, grid operators must rapidly increase other power sources to meet the evening demand peak, creating a duck-shaped demand profile.

What is green hydrogen?

Green hydrogen is produced through water electrolysis powered by renewable electricity, splitting water (H2O) into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2) with zero carbon emissions, offering a clean fuel for hard-to-electrify sectors.

What is a smart grid?

A smart grid uses digital sensors, communication technology, and automated controls to optimize electricity distribution, seamlessly integrate variable renewable sources, minimize transmission losses, and enable consumers to adjust usage in response to grid conditions.

What is the nuclear energy debate in sustainability?

Nuclear energy presents complex trade-offs: it generates reliable, low-carbon electricity but faces concerns about long-term radioactive waste storage, accident risks (Chernobyl, Fukushima), nuclear weapons proliferation, and high construction costs.

What is the energy trilemma?

The energy trilemma describes the challenge of balancing three competing objectives: energy affordability (reasonable costs for all), reliability (consistent supply), and environmental sustainability (low emissions and ecological impact).

What is energy poverty?

Energy poverty refers to the lack of access to affordable, reliable, and modern energy services. Approximately 800 million people lack electricity and 2.6 billion lack clean cooking fuels, making energy access a critical sustainability and equity issue.

What is distributed energy generation?

Distributed energy generation refers to small-scale power production (rooftop solar, small wind turbines, fuel cells) located close to the point of use, reducing transmission losses, increasing grid resilience, and empowering communities.

What is demand response in electricity management?

Demand response programs incentivize electricity consumers to reduce or shift their energy use during peak demand periods through price signals or automated controls, helping balance grid supply and demand without building additional power plants.

What is the concept of electrification in the energy transition?

Electrification involves switching end uses from direct fossil fuel combustion to electricity, such as electric vehicles replacing gasoline cars and heat pumps replacing gas furnaces, so that these activities can be powered by clean electricity.

What is needed for the global transition to sustainable energy?

The energy transition requires a comprehensive approach: scaling up renewable generation, deploying storage, modernizing grid infrastructure, maximizing efficiency, electrifying end uses, and implementing policies that redirect investment from fossil fuels to clean energy.

Real-World Applications

Example: How do energy storage systems like batteries address the intermittency of renewables?

Battery storage systems capture surplus electricity generated during peak renewable production (midday sun, windy periods) and discharge it when generation drops or demand rises, helping balance supply and demand on the grid.

Example: What is demand response in electricity management?

Demand response programs incentivize electricity consumers to reduce or shift their energy use during peak demand periods through price signals or automated controls, helping balance grid supply and demand without building additional power plants.

Example: A developing country has vast solar resources but most of its population lacks access to electricity. International donors offer funding for either a large centralized solar farm with a transmission grid or distributed rooftop solar with battery storage for villages. Which approach better addresses both energy access and sustainability?

Distributed solar with battery storage can rapidly reach remote communities without waiting for expensive transmission infrastructure, empowers local ownership, and builds resilience. A combination approach, with some centralized generation for urban areas, may be optimal.

Example: A remote village in Sub-Saharan Africa currently uses kerosene lamps for lighting and wood fires for cooking, causing indoor air pollution that leads to respiratory disease. An NGO proposes installing a community solar microgrid with battery storage. How does this address both energy poverty and health?

A solar microgrid addresses energy poverty and health simultaneously: electric lighting eliminates toxic kerosene fumes, electric cooking reduces wood smoke exposure, and reliable electricity enables educational, communication, and economic development opportunities.
