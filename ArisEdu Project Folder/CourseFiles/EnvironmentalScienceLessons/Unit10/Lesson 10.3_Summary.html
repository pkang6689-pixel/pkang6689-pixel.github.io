Lesson 10.3: Case Studies in AP Environmental Science - Summary

Key Concepts: Case Studies: AP APES

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary cause of dead zones in the Chesapeake Bay?

The Chesapeake Bay's dead zones result from nutrient pollution originating from agricultural operations across its massive watershed spanning six states and Washington, D.C., causing algal blooms and subsequent oxygen depletion.

What is the generating capacity of the Three Gorges Dam, the world's largest hydroelectric facility?

The Three Gorges Dam on China's Yangtze River has a generating capacity of approximately 22.5 GW, making it the world's largest hydroelectric power station and producing significant clean energy despite its substantial environmental and social costs.

What is non-point source pollution?

Non-point source pollution originates from many diffuse sources across the landscape rather than a single discharge point, making it much harder to regulate and control, as illustrated by the Chesapeake Bay's ongoing nutrient pollution challenges.

What is desertification?

Desertification is the process by which productive dryland ecosystems lose biological productivity through human activities and climate factors, affecting about one-third of Earth's land surface and threatening the livelihoods of over 1 billion people.

What is a dead zone in aquatic ecosystems?

Dead zones are hypoxic regions where dissolved oxygen falls below approximately 2 mg/L, causing mobile organisms to flee and sessile organisms to die. The Gulf of Mexico dead zone, fed by Mississippi River nutrient runoff, is among the world's largest.

What is salinization in agriculture?

Salinization occurs when irrigation water evaporates in arid climates, leaving dissolved salts that accumulate in topsoil over time, eventually making the land too salty for crops. The Aral Sea region experienced severe salinization as a consequence of irrigation diversions.

What is a trophic cascade?

A trophic cascade occurs when changes at one trophic level (such as removing wolves) create cascading effects down through the food web, altering herbivore populations, vegetation, and even physical habitat structure.

What is subsidence?

Subsidence occurs when excessive groundwater pumping depletes aquifers and compacts underground sediments, causing the land surface to sink permanently. This is a growing problem in areas heavily dependent on aquifers like the Ogallala and Central Valley of California.

What is thermal stratification in a lake?

Thermal stratification creates layered conditions where the thermocline prevents mixing between warm upper and cold lower waters, which is important for understanding how dead zones form when nutrients accumulate in the hypolimnion without oxygen replenishment.

What is bioaccumulation?

Bioaccumulation occurs within individual organisms when persistent pollutants like mercury, DDT, or PCBs are ingested faster than they can be eliminated, leading to increasingly toxic concentrations over the organism's lifetime.

What is the precautionary principle?

The precautionary principle shifts the burden of proof to those proposing an action, requiring them to demonstrate safety rather than waiting for victims to prove harm, and is especially relevant to cases like Bhopal and chemical regulation.

What is an exclusion zone in the context of nuclear disasters?

Exclusion zones, such as the 30-km Chernobyl Exclusion Zone and the Fukushima restricted areas, demonstrate the long-term consequences of nuclear disasters, with Chernobyl's zone still uninhabitable decades after the 1986 accident.

Real-World Applications

Example: A government debates restarting its nuclear power program after a period of shutdown following a nearby country's reactor accident. Proponents cite zero-emission baseload electricity; opponents cite waste storage, accident risk, and high construction costs. Using evidence from Chernobyl and Fukushima, what trade-offs should the analysis consider?

The nuclear energy debate requires weighing the climate benefits of zero-emission baseload power against low-probability but catastrophic accident risks, unresolved long-term waste storage, high costs, and public risk perception informed by Chernobyl and Fukushima.
