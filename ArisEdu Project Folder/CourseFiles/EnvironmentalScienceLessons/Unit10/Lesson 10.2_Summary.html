Lesson 10.2: AP-Style Practice Problems - Summary

Key Concepts: AP-Style Practice

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

If a city of 500,000 people produces 2 kg of waste per person per day, what is the total daily waste?

The calculation is straightforward: 500,000 people multiplied by 2 kg per person equals 1,000,000 kg, which is equivalent to 1,000 metric tons of daily municipal solid waste.

What is the percent change formula used in AP calculations?

The percent change formula calculates the relative difference between two values by dividing the absolute change (new minus old) by the original value and multiplying by 100, which is commonly tested in AP quantitative problems.

If species count in a habitat drops from 50 to 35, what is the percent decline?

Applying the percent change formula: (50 minus 35) divided by 50, multiplied by 100 equals 15 divided by 50 times 100, which equals 30% decline in species richness.

What is LD50 (lethal dose 50%) in toxicology?

LD50 is a standard toxicological measure representing the dose lethal to 50% of a test population, allowing comparison of relative toxicity between chemicals and forming the basis of dose-response relationships in environmental health.

What is a dose-response curve?

A dose-response curve plots the magnitude of biological effect against the dose of a substance, revealing important information such as thresholds, LD50 values, and whether effects are linear or nonlinear.

What is an independent variable in experimental design?

The independent variable is the factor the experimenter intentionally changes to observe its effect on the dependent variable, and correctly identifying it is essential for earning points on AP 'Design an Investigation' FRQs.

What is a control group in an experiment?

A control group provides the baseline comparison necessary to determine whether observed effects are actually caused by the independent variable, and including it is required for earning full points on AP experimental design questions.

What is a confounding variable?

Confounding variables are uncontrolled factors that can create alternative explanations for results, and addressing them through proper experimental design (randomization, controls, replication) is essential for valid scientific conclusions.

What is the AP scoring rubric's approach to awarding points on FRQs?

AP FRQ rubrics use specific point-earning criteria where each scoreable element requires precise, accurate information, meaning students earn points by providing targeted answers rather than writing lengthy, vague responses.

What is an externality in environmental economics?

Externalities are costs (like pollution-related health problems) or benefits (like pollination from nearby forests) imposed on third parties who did not choose to be affected, and they represent a market failure frequently tested on the AP exam.

What is the difference between correlation and causation?

Understanding the distinction between correlation and causation is critical for AP science because observing that two variables change together does not prove one causes the other; controlled experiments or additional evidence are needed to establish causality.

What is a cost-benefit analysis (CBA) in environmental decision-making?

Cost-benefit analysis weighs all costs (including environmental externalities, health impacts, and social disruption) against all benefits (including ecosystem services, public health improvements, and economic gains) to inform environmental policy decisions.

What is statistical significance in environmental research?

Statistical significance (typically p less than 0.05) indicates the results are unlikely due to random chance, providing confidence that the observed pattern reflects a real effect rather than natural variation in the data.

During the AP exam, a student encounters a multi-part FRQ with parts (a) through (e) but only has 12 minutes remaining. What is the best time-management strategy?

Since each FRQ part is scored independently, answering the easiest parts first maximizes total points earned, and brief targeted answers earn more points than one elaborate response that leaves other parts blank.

An AP FRQ shows data from two watersheds: Watershed A (forested, low nitrogen runoff of 2 kg/ha/yr) and Watershed B (agricultural, high nitrogen runoff of 25 kg/ha/yr). Both drain into the same estuary. Students must explain why the estuary experiences seasonal dead zones. What is the strongest response?

This response earns points by using specific data (comparing the 2 vs. 25 kg/ha/yr runoff rates), explaining the eutrophication mechanism step by step, and adding the seasonal temperature-oxygen relationship to demonstrate integrated understanding.

Real-World Applications

Example: How many multiple-choice questions are on the APES exam?

Section I of the AP Environmental Science exam contains 80 multiple-choice questions, which students have 90 minutes to complete and which account for 60% of the total exam score.

Example: What are the three types of Free-Response Questions (FRQs) on the APES exam?

The APES exam includes three FRQ types that test different skills: designing controlled experiments, analyzing multi-factor environmental problems, and proposing and justifying evidence-based solutions.

Example: An AP FRQ presents this scenario: a city plans to build a new highway through wetlands to reduce commute times. Students must 'Analyze the Environmental Problem.' Which response would earn the most points?

High-scoring AP responses identify specific causes and effects, name ecosystem services, explain scientific mechanisms, and connect the project to broader environmental impacts, demonstrating systems thinking rather than superficial analysis.

Example: During the AP exam, a student encounters a multi-part FRQ with parts (a) through (e) but only has 12 minutes remaining. What is the best time-management strategy?

Since each FRQ part is scored independently, answering the easiest parts first maximizes total points earned, and brief targeted answers earn more points than one elaborate response that leaves other parts blank.

Example: An AP question provides a table showing energy consumption data for four countries and asks students to calculate which country has the highest per capita energy use. A student gets the arithmetic wrong but shows the correct formula and approach. How would this likely be scored?

AP rubrics typically award points for demonstrating correct methodology and reasoning even when arithmetic errors occur, which is why students should always show their work including formulas, setup, and units on quantitative problems.

Example: A student preparing for the APES exam focuses only on memorizing vocabulary terms and definitions. Their practice FRQ scores remain low. Based on AP scoring principles, what change in study strategy would most improve their performance?

The APES exam tests applied understanding rather than rote memorization, so the most effective preparation involves practicing with past exam questions, making cross-topic connections, working through calculations, and writing structured responses.
