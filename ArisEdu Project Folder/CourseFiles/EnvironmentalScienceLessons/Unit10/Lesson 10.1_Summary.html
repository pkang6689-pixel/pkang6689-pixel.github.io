Key Concepts: Review: Key Concepts

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the greatest threat to biodiversity globally?

Habitat loss and degradation is the number one driver of biodiversity decline worldwide, followed by overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, and climate change as additional major threats.

What is the tragedy of the commons?

The tragedy of the commons, described by Garrett Hardin in 1968, explains how shared resources like fisheries, atmosphere, and groundwater become overexploited when individuals maximize personal gain without bearing the full environmental cost.

What is a keystone species?

A keystone species exerts an outsized influence on its community relative to its population size, and its removal would cause dramatic changes to ecosystem structure, such as sea otters controlling sea urchin populations.

What is the difference between r-selected and K-selected species?

R-selected species (like insects and bacteria) reproduce rapidly with many offspring and short lifespans, while K-selected species (like elephants and whales) invest heavily in fewer offspring with longer lifespans and slower maturation.

What is an ecological footprint?

The ecological footprint measures total biocapacity demand in global hectares, and humanity's collective footprint has exceeded Earth's biocapacity since the 1970s, meaning we are consuming resources faster than they regenerate.

What is NEPA (National Environmental Policy Act)?

NEPA, signed in 1970, is a foundational U.S. environmental law that requires federal agencies to assess environmental impacts of proposed actions through Environmental Impact Statements before proceeding with major projects.

What is environmental justice?

Environmental justice addresses the disproportionate burden of pollution and environmental hazards placed on low-income communities and communities of color, advocating for equitable distribution of environmental benefits and burdens.

What is the EPA (Environmental Protection Agency)?

The EPA was established in 1970 to consolidate federal environmental responsibilities, and it sets and enforces regulations under major laws like the Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and CERCLA (Superfund).

What is carrying capacity in ecology?

Carrying capacity (K) represents the maximum number of individuals of a species that an environment can support long-term, limited by resources such as food, water, space, and other ecological constraints.

What is bioaccumulation?

Bioaccumulation occurs when organisms absorb toxins (like mercury or DDT) faster than they can metabolize or excrete them, leading to increasing concentrations over the organism's lifetime.

Real-World Applications

Example: The 10% rule states that approximately what percentage of energy transfers between trophic levels?

Only about 10% of energy at one trophic level is passed to the next because organisms use most of the energy they consume for metabolic processes, releasing the rest as heat.

Example: A coastal fishing village notices that fish populations have collapsed after decades of increasing catch sizes. Despite no pollution or habitat destruction, the fish stocks continue declining even after catch limits are imposed. Which concept best explains this situation?

This scenario illustrates the tragedy of the commons: individual fishers maximized their personal catch from a shared resource until the fish population fell below its reproductive replacement level, making recovery difficult even with regulations.

Example: A developing country experiences rapid population growth after introducing modern medicine and sanitation, but birth rates remain high. According to the demographic transition model, which stage is this country in?

Stage 2 of the demographic transition features declining death rates due to healthcare improvements while birth rates remain high, creating rapid population growth often called a 'population explosion.'
