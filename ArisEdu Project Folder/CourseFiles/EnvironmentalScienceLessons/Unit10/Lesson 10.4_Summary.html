Key Definitions

What is the purpose of an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) in real-world projects?

EIAs serve as preventive tools that identify potential environmental consequences before a project begins, allowing planners to modify designs, implement mitigation measures, and consider alternatives that minimize environmental harm.

What is bioremediation?

Bioremediation harnesses natural biological processes, using bacteria, fungi, and plants to degrade, transform, or absorb contaminants, offering a cost-effective and less disruptive alternative to traditional excavation and chemical treatment methods.

What is life cycle assessment (LCA)?

Life cycle assessment evaluates the total environmental footprint of a product 'from cradle to grave,' including resource extraction, manufacturing energy, transportation, consumer use, and end-of-life disposal, enabling informed comparisons of environmental alternatives.

What is environmental remediation?

Environmental remediation encompasses all techniques used to clean up contaminated sites, including excavation, chemical treatment, bioremediation, pump-and-treat systems, and capping, and is the core activity at Superfund and brownfield sites across the country.

What is an environmental consultant?

Environmental consultants bridge science, policy, and business by helping organizations navigate environmental regulations, conduct impact assessments, develop sustainability strategies, and achieve compliance, representing one of the fastest-growing environmental career paths.

What is ecosystem services valuation?

Ecosystem services valuation assigns monetary values to the benefits nature provides (estimated at over $100 trillion annually globally), making the economic case for conservation visible in cost-benefit analyses and policy decisions.

What is adaptive management in environmental science?

Adaptive management acknowledges scientific uncertainty by implementing management actions, systematically monitoring results, and modifying approaches based on outcomes, creating a learn-by-doing cycle that improves management over time.

What is environmental monitoring?

Environmental monitoring programs systematically track air quality, water quality, biodiversity, climate variables, and other parameters over time to establish baselines, detect trends, identify emerging problems, and evaluate whether management actions are achieving their goals.

What is a green job?

Green jobs span a wide range of sectors including renewable energy installation, energy efficiency retrofitting, sustainable agriculture, environmental consulting, conservation biology, and waste management, and the sector is one of the fastest-growing areas of employment globally.

What is systems thinking in environmental science?

Systems thinking is the most important skill in environmental science because environmental problems involve complex interactions among ecological, economic, and social systems, and effective solutions require understanding feedback loops, trade-offs, and cascading effects.

What is environmental literacy?

Environmental literacy combines scientific knowledge, critical thinking skills, and civic engagement to enable individuals to understand how human actions affect the environment and to make informed decisions as consumers, voters, and community members.

What is interdisciplinary research?

Environmental science is inherently interdisciplinary, integrating biology, chemistry, geology, physics, economics, policy, sociology, and engineering because environmental problems span multiple domains and require diverse expertise to understand and address effectively.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Validated citizen science data provides long-term baseline information that can be used as scientific evidence in Environmental Impact Assessments, demonstrating existing population trends that a proposed development could exacerbate.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology aim to accomplish?

CCS captures CO2 at emission sources (like power plants) or directly from the atmosphere and injects it into geological formations for permanent storage, offering a potential bridge technology while the energy system transitions to renewables.

Example: What is adaptive management in environmental science?

Adaptive management acknowledges scientific uncertainty by implementing management actions, systematically monitoring results, and modifying approaches based on outcomes, creating a learn-by-doing cycle that improves management over time.

Example: A lake management team uses adaptive management to address an invasive species problem. Their initial approach of mechanical harvesting reduces the invasive plant by 30%, but monitoring shows it rebounds within two seasons. What should they do next under adaptive management principles?

Adaptive management treats management actions as experiments: when monitoring reveals that mechanical harvesting alone is insufficient, the team should analyze why, modify their approach based on evidence (perhaps adding biological control), implement the change, and continue monitoring.
