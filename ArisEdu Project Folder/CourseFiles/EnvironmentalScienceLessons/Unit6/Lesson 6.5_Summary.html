Lesson 6.5: Hazardous Waste & Recycling - Summary

Key Concepts: Hazardous Waste & Recycling

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary purpose of a hazardous waste manifest?

A hazardous waste manifest is a shipping document that accompanies hazardous waste at every step of its journey, ensuring accountability and creating a paper trail from generator to transporter to treatment, storage, or disposal facility.

What is the primary danger of improper disposal of electronic waste (e-waste)?

Electronic devices contain hazardous materials including lead in solder, mercury in switches and screens, cadmium in batteries, and brominated flame retardants. When improperly disposed of, these toxins leach into soil and groundwater.

What is the Basel Convention?

The Basel Convention, adopted in 1989, aims to reduce the movement of hazardous waste between nations, specifically to prevent developed countries from dumping hazardous waste in developing countries with weaker environmental regulations.

What is the most recycled material in the United States by weight?

Steel and iron are the most recycled materials by weight in the United States, with auto recycling being the largest recycling industry. Steel can be recycled indefinitely without losing quality.

What is the approximate recycling rate for plastic in the United States?

Despite widespread collection programs, only about 5-10% of plastic waste in the United States is actually recycled. Many plastics are difficult to recycle due to the variety of resin types, contamination, and limited end markets.

What is a 'cradle-to-grave' management system?

Cradle-to-grave is a regulatory approach under RCRA that requires generators to track and manage hazardous waste from the point of creation (cradle) through transportation, treatment, storage, and final disposal (grave), ensuring accountability at every step.

What is 'downcycling'?

Downcycling occurs when recycled materials lose quality in the recycling process and can only be made into lower-grade products. For example, recycled plastic bottles are often downcycled into fiber for clothing or carpet rather than new bottles.

What is a 'materials recovery facility' (MRF)?

A MRF receives mixed recyclables collected from curbside programs, sorts them by material type using manual labor and automated equipment (magnets, optical scanners, air classifiers), and bales them for sale to manufacturers as feedstock.

What is 'contamination' in the context of recycling?

Recycling contamination occurs when non-recyclable items (plastic bags, food-soiled containers, non-recyclable plastics) are placed in recycling bins, reducing the value of recyclables and sometimes causing entire loads to be sent to landfill.

What is 'life cycle assessment' (LCA)?

LCA evaluates environmental impacts at every stage of a product's life: raw material extraction, manufacturing, transportation, use, and end-of-life disposal or recycling. It helps identify which stages have the greatest environmental footprint.

What is the meaning of 'waste characterization'?

Waste characterization involves testing and analyzing waste to identify its chemical composition, physical properties, and regulatory classification. This determines the appropriate treatment, storage, and disposal methods required by law.

Real-World Applications

Example: What federal law governs the management of hazardous waste from generation to disposal in the United States?

RCRA, enacted in 1976, establishes the framework for managing hazardous waste from 'cradle to grave,' tracking waste from generation through transportation, treatment, storage, and final disposal.

Example: What percentage of the world's e-waste is properly recycled according to global estimates?

Only about 20% of global e-waste is formally recycled through proper channels. The remainder is landfilled, incinerated, or informally processed in developing countries under dangerous conditions, exposing workers to toxic materials.

Example: What is a 'cradle-to-grave' management system?

Cradle-to-grave is a regulatory approach under RCRA that requires generators to track and manage hazardous waste from the point of creation (cradle) through transportation, treatment, storage, and final disposal (grave), ensuring accountability at every step.

Example: A chemical plant generates 1,000 tons of hazardous solvent waste per year. A process engineer proposes modifying the production process to use a water-based solvent instead, which would eliminate 80% of the hazardous waste while a closed-loop recovery system recycles the remaining 20%. What waste management hierarchy principle does this exemplify?

This approach combines source reduction (switching to a less hazardous water-based solvent eliminates 80% of hazardous waste) with recycling (recovering and reusing the remaining solvent). These are the two most preferred strategies in the hazardous waste management hierarchy.

Example: A hospital generates multiple types of waste: infectious biological waste from surgeries, pharmaceutical waste from expired medications, radioactive waste from diagnostic procedures, and regular office waste. Why does this make hospital waste management particularly complex?

Hospitals generate a complex mix of waste streams each governed by different regulations: infectious waste requires autoclaving or incineration, pharmaceutical waste follows RCRA hazardous waste rules, radioactive waste follows NRC regulations, and each requires separate collection, training, and disposal pathways.

Example: A battery recycling facility processes 50,000 lithium-ion batteries per month from electric vehicles and consumer electronics. During processing, a fire breaks out because damaged batteries undergo thermal runaway. What does this incident highlight about the challenges of recycling modern batteries?

Lithium-ion batteries present unique recycling challenges because they store significant energy that can be released violently if cells are damaged, punctured, or improperly discharged. Thermal runaway can cause fires reaching over 1,000 degrees F, requiring specialized facilities with fire suppression systems.
