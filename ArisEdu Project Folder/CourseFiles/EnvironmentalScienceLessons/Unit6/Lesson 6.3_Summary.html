Lesson 6.3: Soil Pollution & Land Degradation - Summary

Key Concepts: Soil Pollution & Land Degradation

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the primary cause of soil contamination at industrial sites?

Industrial sites contaminate soil through spills, leaks, and improper disposal of hazardous chemicals including heavy metals, solvents, petroleum products, and other toxic substances that persist in soil for decades.

What is desertification?

Desertification is the process by which fertile land becomes increasingly dry and unproductive, caused by a combination of climate variability and human activities such as overgrazing, deforestation, and poor irrigation practices.

What is soil salinization and what causes it?

Soil salinization occurs when irrigation water evaporates and leaves dissolved salts behind in the soil. Over time, salt accumulation can render soil unsuitable for agriculture, affecting an estimated 20% of irrigated land worldwide.

What is a brownfield site?

Brownfields are properties where expansion, redevelopment, or reuse may be complicated by the presence or potential presence of hazardous substances, pollutants, or contaminants from prior industrial or commercial use.

What is the Superfund program in the United States?

The Superfund program, established by CERCLA in 1980, identifies, investigates, and cleans up the most hazardous contaminated sites in the United States. The EPA maintains a National Priorities List of the worst sites requiring long-term cleanup.

What is the role of soil organic matter in maintaining soil health?

Soil organic matter is crucial for soil health: it improves water-holding capacity, provides essential plant nutrients as it decomposes, supports diverse microbial communities, and helps bind soil particles into aggregates that resist erosion.

What is the primary environmental concern with persistent organic pollutants (POPs) in soil?

POPs such as DDT, PCBs, and dioxins resist degradation, persist in soil for decades, and bioaccumulate through food chains, posing long-term health risks including cancer, reproductive disorders, and immune system damage.

What is bioremediation?

Bioremediation harnesses natural microbial processes to degrade, transform, or detoxify pollutants in contaminated soil. Bacteria, fungi, and plants can be used to break down petroleum hydrocarbons, pesticides, and other organic contaminants.

What is phytoremediation?

Phytoremediation uses plants to clean contaminated soil. Certain plants, called hyperaccumulators, can absorb heavy metals like lead, cadmium, and arsenic from soil through their roots and concentrate them in their tissues for later disposal.

What is soil horizons in soil science?

Soil horizons are the distinct layers visible in a soil profile, typically labeled O (organic), A (topsoil), B (subsoil), C (parent material), and R (bedrock). Each horizon has unique characteristics that reflect soil formation processes.

What is the meaning of 'soil permeability'?

Soil permeability measures how easily water and air move through soil. Sandy soils have high permeability allowing rapid drainage, while clay soils have low permeability, which affects irrigation, contamination transport, and plant growth.

What is 'no-till farming'?

No-till farming plants crops directly into undisturbed soil from the previous season's crop residue. This preserves soil structure, reduces erosion by up to 90%, maintains soil moisture, and increases soil organic matter and carbon sequestration.

What is meant by 'soil remediation'?

Soil remediation encompasses all techniques used to clean up contaminated soil, including excavation and disposal, bioremediation, chemical treatment, thermal treatment, and soil washing, with the goal of reducing contaminant levels to safe standards.

Outliers and Resistant Measures

Mountaintop removal permanently destroys centuries-old forest soils, buries headwater streams under millions of tons of rubble, and exposes minerals that leach selenium, sulfur, and heavy metals into waterways, causing long-lasting land degradation that cannot be fully restored.

Real-World Applications

Example: A developer wants to build affordable housing on a brownfield site that was formerly a dry cleaning facility. Soil tests show tetrachloroethylene (PCE) contamination. The state environmental agency requires cleanup to residential standards. Which remediation technology would be most appropriate for this type of contamination?

Soil vapor extraction removes volatile PCE from soil by applying a vacuum, while chemical oxidation (injecting oxidants like permanganate) breaks down remaining chlorinated solvents in place. This combination effectively addresses both vapor and dissolved-phase contamination.

Example: A vineyard owner in a Mediterranean climate notices that after years of conventional tillage, the soil between rows has become compacted and erosion has increased during winter rains. The topsoil depth has decreased from 12 inches to 6 inches over 20 years. At this rate of loss, approximately how many years until the topsoil is completely gone, and what conservation practice should be implemented immediately?

At 6 inches of loss over 20 years (0.3 inches per year), the remaining 6 inches would be gone in about 20 years. Cover crops protect soil from rain impact, reduce runoff, add organic matter, and their roots prevent compaction, while no-till preserves soil structure.
