Lesson 6.4: Solid Waste Management - Summary

Key Concepts: Solid Waste Management

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the most common method of solid waste disposal in the United States?

Landfilling remains the most common disposal method for municipal solid waste in the United States, receiving approximately 50% of all waste generated, though recycling and composting rates have increased over time.

What is the primary environmental advantage of recycling aluminum cans?

Recycling aluminum saves about 95% of the energy required to extract and process new aluminum from bauxite ore. This makes aluminum one of the most energy-efficient materials to recycle.

What is composting?

Composting is the aerobic decomposition of organic materials like food scraps, yard waste, and paper by microorganisms, producing humus-rich compost that can be used as a soil amendment, diverting waste from landfills.

What is the largest component of municipal solid waste in the United States by weight?

Paper and paperboard constitute the largest category of municipal solid waste by weight in the United States, accounting for roughly 23% of the waste stream, though this percentage has declined with digitization.

What is a sanitary landfill designed to prevent?

Modern sanitary landfills use clay and synthetic liners, leachate collection systems, and daily soil covers to prevent contaminated liquid from seeping into groundwater and to minimize environmental impacts.

What is waste-to-energy (WTE) incineration?

Waste-to-energy facilities burn municipal solid waste at high temperatures to produce steam that drives turbines for electricity generation, reducing waste volume by about 90% while recovering energy, though they require air pollution controls.

What is the concept of 'reduce, reuse, recycle' arranged in order of preference?

The waste hierarchy prioritizes reducing consumption first (preventing waste creation), then reusing items to extend their life, and finally recycling materials into new products. Reduction prevents the most environmental impact.

What is e-waste?

Electronic waste includes discarded electronic devices like computers, smartphones, TVs, and batteries. E-waste contains valuable materials (gold, copper) but also hazardous substances (lead, mercury, cadmium) that require special handling.

What is the average amount of solid waste generated per person per day in the United States?

Americans generate approximately 4.5 pounds of municipal solid waste per person per day, totaling about 292 million tons nationally per year, one of the highest per capita rates in the world.

What is a 'waste stream'?

The waste stream describes the complete flow of waste from the point of generation through collection, processing, and final disposal. Understanding the waste stream helps communities identify opportunities for reduction, reuse, and recycling.

What is 'tipping fee' in waste management?

A tipping fee is the price charged to waste haulers for dumping waste at a disposal facility. Higher tipping fees make recycling and waste reduction more economically attractive by increasing the cost of disposal.

What is a 'circular economy'?

A circular economy is an alternative to the traditional linear 'take-make-dispose' model. It designs out waste by keeping products and materials in use for as long as possible through reuse, repair, refurbishment, remanufacturing, and recycling.

What is 'single-stream recycling'?

Single-stream recycling allows consumers to mix all recyclables (paper, plastic, glass, metal) in one container, which increases participation rates but can also increase contamination, making sorting at the materials recovery facility more challenging.

What is 'leachate' in the context of landfills?

Leachate is liquid, primarily from rainfall, that percolates through landfill waste and picks up dissolved contaminants including heavy metals, organic compounds, and pathogens. Modern landfills collect and treat leachate to prevent groundwater contamination.

What is 'planned obsolescence'?

Planned obsolescence is a business strategy where products are intentionally designed to become outdated, non-repairable, or non-functional after a limited time, driving repeat purchases but generating enormous amounts of unnecessary waste.

What is 'municipal solid waste' (MSW)?

Municipal solid waste encompasses everyday trash generated by households, commercial establishments, and institutions, including food waste, packaging, clothing, furniture, appliances, and yard trimmings, but excludes industrial, hazardous, and construction waste.

Japan incinerates approximately 80% of its municipal waste, far more than most countries. Given Japan's limited land area and dense population, an environmental policy student asks whether incineration is a good model for other nations. What is the primary trade-off of high incineration rates?

Incineration reduces waste volume by about 90% and generates electricity, but produces air emissions (including dioxins if not properly controlled) and toxic bottom ash and fly ash containing concentrated heavy metals that must be disposed of in specialized landfills.

A company sells beverages in single-use plastic bottles and produces 5 billion bottles per year. Under an Extended Producer Responsibility law, the company must now pay $0.02 per bottle to fund collection and recycling infrastructure. What is the total annual cost to the company, and how might this change its packaging decisions?

5 billion bottles x $0.02 = $100 million per year. This significant cost creates a direct financial incentive for the company to reduce packaging waste through lighter bottles, increased recycled content, or transition to reusable/refillable systems.

Real-World Applications

Example: What is the primary environmental advantage of recycling aluminum cans?

Recycling aluminum saves about 95% of the energy required to extract and process new aluminum from bauxite ore. This makes aluminum one of the most energy-efficient materials to recycle.

Example: What is 'tipping fee' in waste management?

Example: What does 'source reduction' mean in waste management?

Source reduction, also called waste prevention, is the most preferred waste management strategy. It includes designing products with less packaging, making products more durable, and changing consumption habits to generate less waste at the source.

Example: What does the term 'NIMBY' mean in the context of waste management?

NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard) describes community opposition to locating undesirable facilities like landfills, incinerators, or hazardous waste treatment plants nearby, even when residents acknowledge the general need for such facilities.

Example: San Francisco set a goal of achieving zero waste by 2020 and diverted over 80% of its waste from landfills through aggressive recycling and composting programs. A neighboring city generates similar amounts of waste but only diverts 20%. What key policies enabled San Francisco's success?

San Francisco's success relied on mandatory participation (not voluntary), economic incentives through variable pricing (pay-as-you-throw), convenient collection systems, and a comprehensive organics composting program that diverts food waste, which is the largest landfill component.

Example: A city generates 500,000 tons of municipal solid waste per year. Currently, 30% is recycled, 10% is composted, and 60% goes to the landfill. The city sets a goal to divert 75% from the landfill within 5 years. How many additional tons must be diverted annually to meet this goal?

Current diversion = 30% + 10% = 40% (200,000 tons). Goal diversion = 75% (375,000 tons). Additional tons needed = 375,000 - 200,000 = 175,000 tons per year must be diverted from the landfill through expanded recycling, composting, or other programs.

Example: A coastal community discovers that its closed landfill from the 1970s, located near the shore, is eroding into the ocean due to rising sea levels and storm surges. Debris including plastics, metals, and potentially hazardous materials are washing into the marine environment. What does this scenario illustrate about legacy waste management?

Thousands of legacy landfills were sited without consideration of sea level rise or increased storm intensity. As climate change accelerates coastal erosion, these sites become secondary pollution sources, releasing decades of accumulated waste into marine environments.

Example: The Great Pacific Garbage Patch contains an estimated 80,000 tons of plastic debris spread across an area twice the size of Texas. An engineering startup proposes using ocean cleanup vessels to collect the plastic. Environmentalists argue this approach alone is insufficient. Why?

An estimated 8-12 million tons of plastic enter the ocean annually. Ocean cleanup addresses existing pollution but is futile without upstream interventions: reducing plastic production, improving waste collection in developing countries, and preventing plastic from reaching waterways.
