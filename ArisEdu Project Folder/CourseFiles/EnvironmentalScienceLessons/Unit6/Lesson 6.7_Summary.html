Lesson 6.7: AP Prep: Pollution Models - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Pollution Models

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the dose-response relationship in toxicology?

The dose-response relationship describes how the magnitude of biological effect (response) changes with increasing exposure (dose) to a substance. It is fundamental to risk assessment and establishing safe exposure limits.

What is LD50 in toxicology?

LD50 (Lethal Dose 50) is the dose of a substance required to kill 50% of a test population. It is a standard measure of acute toxicity, with lower LD50 values indicating greater toxicity.

What is the threshold model of toxicology?

The threshold model proposes that organisms can tolerate a certain level of exposure without adverse effects, and harm only occurs when the dose exceeds a specific threshold. This model is used for most non-carcinogenic pollutants.

According to the linear no-threshold model, what is the safe level of exposure to a carcinogen?

The linear no-threshold model assumes that any exposure to a carcinogen increases cancer risk linearly, with no safe dose. Even the smallest exposure carries some risk, which is why carcinogen regulations often aim for the lowest achievable levels.

What is bioaccumulation?

Bioaccumulation occurs when an organism absorbs a substance at a rate faster than it can metabolize or excrete it, leading to increasing concentrations in tissues over time. Fat-soluble pollutants like mercury and DDT are particularly prone to bioaccumulation.

What is the difference between acute and chronic toxicity?

Acute toxicity causes harmful effects from a single or short-term exposure to a high dose, while chronic toxicity results from repeated or prolonged exposure to lower doses over weeks, months, or years. Both are important in environmental health assessment.

What is a Gaussian plume model used for?

The Gaussian plume model is a mathematical formula that predicts pollutant concentration at any point downwind of a source based on emission rate, wind speed, atmospheric stability, and stack height. It assumes a bell-curve distribution of pollutants.

What is a dose-response curve?

A dose-response curve is a sigmoid (S-shaped) graph where the x-axis shows increasing dose and the y-axis shows the percentage of the population affected. It reveals the threshold, the ED50/LD50, and the maximum response at high doses.

What is 'risk assessment' in environmental science?

Environmental risk assessment follows four steps: hazard identification (what can cause harm), dose-response assessment (how much causes harm), exposure assessment (who is exposed and how much), and risk characterization (what is the overall risk level).

What is the meaning of 'parts per million' (ppm) in pollution measurement?

Parts per million is a concentration unit used to express very dilute concentrations. One ppm means one unit of substance per million units of the total mixture, equivalent to one milligram per liter in water or one milligram per kilogram in soil.

What is 'biomagnification factor'?

The biomagnification factor quantifies how much a contaminant's concentration increases between trophic levels. A factor of 10 means the concentration is 10 times higher in predators than in their prey, explaining why top predators are most vulnerable.

What is 'half-life' in the context of environmental pollutants?

Environmental half-life measures how quickly a pollutant breaks down. DDT has a soil half-life of 2-15 years, meaning it takes that long for half of the applied amount to degrade. After multiple half-lives, the concentration decreases exponentially.

What is a 'dilution model' in water pollution?

Dilution models calculate how pollutant concentrations change as contaminated water mixes with uncontaminated water downstream. The basic formula is C1V1 = C2V2, where C is concentration and V is volume, though real systems are more complex.

A factory discharges wastewater containing 500 mg/L of a pollutant into a river at a flow rate of 2 cubic meters per second. The river flow upstream of the discharge is 10 cubic meters per second with zero pollutant concentration. Using a simple dilution model, what is the pollutant concentration immediately after mixing?

Using mass balance: (500 mg/L x 2 m3/s) + (0 mg/L x 10 m3/s) = C x 12 m3/s. Therefore C = 1000/12 = approximately 83 mg/L after complete mixing of the discharge with the river flow.

Mercury concentration in water is 0.001 ppm. Phytoplankton concentrate it to 0.5 ppm. Small fish that eat phytoplankton have 2 ppm. Large predatory fish have 10 ppm. What is the biomagnification factor from phytoplankton to large predatory fish?

The biomagnification factor from phytoplankton to large predatory fish = 10 ppm / 0.5 ppm = 20. This means mercury is 20 times more concentrated in top predator fish than in phytoplankton at the base of the food chain.

A toxicologist tests a new industrial chemical on laboratory mice. At 10 mg/kg body weight, 0% of mice show adverse effects. At 50 mg/kg, 10% show effects. At 100 mg/kg, 50% show effects. At 200 mg/kg, 90% show effects. What is the approximate threshold dose and ED50?

The threshold lies between 10 mg/kg (no effect) and 50 mg/kg (10% affected), approximately in the 10-50 mg/kg range. The ED50 is 100 mg/kg, the dose at which 50% of the population shows the measured effect.

DDT was applied to a lake ecosystem at a concentration of 0.000003 ppm in the water. Zooplankton contained 0.04 ppm, small fish contained 0.5 ppm, large fish contained 2 ppm, and fish-eating birds contained 25 ppm. What is the total biomagnification factor from water to the top predator birds?

Total biomagnification factor = concentration in birds / concentration in water = 25 ppm / 0.000003 ppm = 8,333,333. DDT concentration increased over 8 million times from the water to the top predator, explaining the devastating effects on birds of prey.

A toxicologist is assessing the risk of a new pesticide that shows both a threshold effect for kidney damage (NOAEL of 5 mg/kg/day) and a linear no-threshold response for cancer. The EPA applies a safety factor of 100 to the NOAEL to set the reference dose. What is the reference dose, and why is the cancer assessment handled differently?

Reference dose = NOAEL / safety factor = 5 / 100 = 0.05 mg/kg/day for kidney effects. Cancer assessment differs because the linear no-threshold model assumes any exposure carries some cancer risk, so there is no safe dose. Instead, regulators calculate the probability of cancer at various exposure levels.

Real-World Applications

Example: An aquatic ecosystem receives thermal pollution from a power plant. Water temperature increases from 15 degrees C to 25 degrees C. Dissolved oxygen saturation at 15 degrees C is approximately 10 mg/L and at 25 degrees C is approximately 8 mg/L. What percentage reduction in dissolved oxygen capacity does this temperature increase cause?

The reduction in dissolved oxygen capacity = (10 - 8) / 10 x 100 = 20%. This 20% decrease in oxygen-carrying capacity, combined with increased metabolic demand of aquatic organisms at higher temperatures, can create oxygen stress in the ecosystem.

Example: A city's air quality monitoring station measures PM2.5 levels every hour for a week. The data shows that concentrations peak at 85 micrograms per cubic meter during morning rush hour (7-9 AM) and evening rush hour (5-7 PM), but drop to 15 micrograms per cubic meter overnight. The WHO guideline for 24-hour average PM2.5 is 15 micrograms per cubic meter. What does this temporal pattern indicate about the primary source and what policy intervention is most targeted?

The strong correlation between PM2.5 peaks and commuting hours (morning and evening rush) points clearly to vehicle emissions as the primary source. Congestion pricing, expanded public transit, and promoting alternative transportation during peak hours would directly target this source.
