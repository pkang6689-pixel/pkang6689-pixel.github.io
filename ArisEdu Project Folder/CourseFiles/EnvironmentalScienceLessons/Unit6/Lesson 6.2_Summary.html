Lesson 6.2: Water Pollution (Sources, Effects, Control) - Summary

Key Concepts: Water Pollution

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the leading cause of water pollution in agricultural regions?

Nutrient runoff, primarily nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural fertilizers, is the leading cause of water pollution in farming areas, causing eutrophication and dead zones in rivers, lakes, and coastal waters.

What is eutrophication?

Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients, especially nitrogen and phosphorus, stimulate algal blooms. When the algae die and decompose, bacteria consume dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic conditions that can kill fish and other aquatic organisms.

What is a dead zone in an aquatic ecosystem?

Dead zones are hypoxic areas where dissolved oxygen levels fall below 2 mg/L, making it impossible for most aquatic organisms to survive. The Gulf of Mexico dead zone, caused largely by Mississippi River nutrient runoff, is one of the largest.

What is the primary purpose of a wastewater treatment plant?

Wastewater treatment plants process municipal sewage and industrial effluent through primary (physical), secondary (biological), and sometimes tertiary (advanced) treatment stages to remove solids, organic matter, nutrients, and pathogens before discharging cleaned water.

What is the difference between point source and non-point source water pollution?

Point source pollution enters water from a specific, identifiable location like a factory pipe or sewage outfall. Non-point source pollution comes from diffuse sources like agricultural runoff, urban stormwater, and atmospheric deposition spread across the landscape.

What is biological oxygen demand (BOD)?

BOD measures the quantity of dissolved oxygen consumed by microorganisms as they break down organic matter in water. High BOD indicates heavy organic pollution and can lead to oxygen depletion that kills fish and other aquatic life.

What is the largest source of ocean pollution?

Approximately 80% of ocean pollution originates from land-based sources, including agricultural runoff, municipal sewage, industrial discharges, and plastic waste that enter the ocean through rivers, stormwater systems, and direct coastal discharge.

What is an algal bloom?

An algal bloom is a rapid, excessive growth of algae triggered by nutrient enrichment (particularly phosphorus and nitrogen). Some blooms produce toxins harmful to humans and wildlife, and decomposing bloom biomass depletes water oxygen levels.

What is biomagnification?

Biomagnification occurs when pollutant concentrations increase at each step up the food chain. A classic example is mercury: small fish have low levels, but top predators like tuna or eagles accumulate much higher concentrations from eating many contaminated prey.

What is a watershed?

A watershed is the entire land area that drains water, sediment, and dissolved materials to a common body of water. Understanding watershed boundaries is essential for managing water pollution because activities anywhere in the watershed affect downstream water quality.

What is an indicator species in water quality monitoring?

Indicator species, such as certain macroinvertebrates (mayfly larvae indicate clean water, tubifex worms indicate polluted water), serve as biological monitors of water quality because their sensitivity to pollution provides information about environmental conditions.

What is meant by 'impervious surface' and how does it relate to water pollution?

Impervious surfaces like roads, parking lots, and rooftops prevent rainwater from soaking into the ground, causing it to flow rapidly over surfaces collecting oil, heavy metals, fertilizers, and other pollutants that are then carried into waterways.

What is a 'coliform count' used for in water quality testing?

Coliform bacteria, especially E. coli, are used as indicators of fecal contamination because they are present in the intestines of warm-blooded animals. High coliform counts signal that water may contain pathogens from sewage or animal waste.

A farmer applies 200 pounds of nitrogen fertilizer per acre to a 500-acre corn field. Heavy rains wash approximately 30% of the nitrogen into a nearby stream that flows into a lake. The lake already has elevated nitrogen levels. What is the total nitrogen load entering the stream from this single application?

Total fertilizer = 200 lbs/acre x 500 acres = 100,000 lbs of nitrogen applied. With 30% runoff, the nitrogen entering the stream = 100,000 x 0.30 = 30,000 pounds, contributing significantly to downstream eutrophication.

A gold mining operation in a developing country uses mercury amalgamation to extract gold from ore. The mercury-laden waste is dumped into a river system. Downstream communities rely on the river for drinking water and fish. What is the most serious long-term health risk to these communities?

Mercury released into waterways is converted to methylmercury by bacteria and biomagnifies through the food chain. Communities eating contaminated fish face neurological damage, with developing fetuses and young children being most vulnerable to permanent brain damage.

A rapidly growing suburb has relied on septic systems for wastewater disposal. Many systems are failing, and groundwater testing shows elevated nitrate levels exceeding the EPA drinking water standard of 10 mg/L. What is the best long-term solution?

Connecting to a centralized sewer with advanced treatment addresses the root cause by properly processing all wastewater, including nutrient removal. In dense suburban areas, septic systems often fail due to insufficient soil drainage and high density, making centralized treatment the most reliable solution.

Real-World Applications

Example: A coastal city dumps partially treated sewage into the ocean through an outfall pipe. Nearby beaches have been closed repeatedly due to high bacterial counts. The city argues that the ocean dilutes the sewage sufficiently. An environmental scientist disagrees. What evidence would best support the scientist's position?

Monitoring data showing unsafe coliform levels at beaches and contaminant accumulation in local shellfish and fish provides concrete scientific evidence that dilution is insufficient, refuting the city's argument and supporting the need for full sewage treatment.

Example: A city is redesigning its stormwater management system after repeated flooding and water quality violations. Engineers propose replacing traditional concrete storm drains with green infrastructure. Which combination of green infrastructure would most effectively reduce polluted runoff?

Green infrastructure mimics natural hydrology by using vegetation, soil, and engineered systems to slow, filter, and absorb stormwater. Bioswales, rain gardens, permeable pavement, and constructed wetlands remove pollutants while reducing runoff volume and flooding.

Example: Microplastic particles have been found in drinking water, seafood, and even Arctic sea ice. A researcher discovers that a single load of synthetic laundry releases an average of 700,000 microfibers. If 100 million households in a country each do 5 loads of laundry per week, approximately how many microfibers enter waterways weekly?

Weekly microfibers = 100,000,000 households x 5 loads x 700,000 fibers = 350,000,000,000,000 = 350 trillion (3.5 x 10^14) microfibers. This staggering number illustrates why microplastic pollution from textiles is an emerging environmental crisis.
