Lesson 3.5: Urbanization & Land Use - Summary

Key Concepts: Urbanization & Land Use

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is urbanization?

Urbanization is the demographic and physical process by which an increasing share of a population comes to live in cities. It involves both the migration of people from rural to urban areas and the physical expansion of cities into surrounding land.

What is urban sprawl?

Urban sprawl is the outward expansion of low-density residential and commercial development into suburban and rural areas. It is characterized by car-dependent communities, strip malls, single-use zoning, and the consumption of farmland and natural habitats.

What is the urban heat island effect?

The urban heat island effect occurs because built surfaces absorb and re-radiate more heat than natural landscapes, vegetation that provides cooling through evapotranspiration is reduced, and human activities generate additional heat. This can increase energy demand, air pollution, and heat-related health risks.

What is impervious surface coverage?

Impervious surface coverage measures the proportion of land covered by materials that do not allow water to pass through. High impervious coverage in urban areas increases stormwater runoff, reduces groundwater recharge, increases flood risk, and degrades water quality in receiving waterways.

What is a brownfield site?

A brownfield is a previously developed property where reuse may be complicated by the presence or potential presence of contaminants. Redeveloping brownfields is environmentally preferable to building on greenfield sites because it reuses already-developed land and can remediate existing contamination.

What is land use change?

Land use change refers to human modification of the terrestrial surface, such as converting forests to farmland, wetlands to housing developments, or rural areas to urban zones. These changes alter ecosystems, affect biodiversity, change hydrology, and contribute to climate change.

What is smart growth in urban planning?

Smart growth is an urban planning approach that concentrates growth in compact, walkable centers with diverse housing and transportation choices. By reducing sprawl, it preserves farmland and natural areas, reduces per capita emissions, and creates more livable communities.

What is a greenway?

Greenways are linear corridors of protected open space that provide recreational trails, wildlife habitat connectivity, stormwater management, and aesthetic value within urban and suburban areas. They often follow rivers, abandoned rail lines, or other natural or built features.

What is transit-oriented development (TOD)?

Transit-oriented development concentrates housing, shops, and workplaces within walking distance of public transit stations. By making public transit convenient and reducing the need for cars, TOD reduces greenhouse gas emissions, traffic congestion, and land consumption compared to car-dependent sprawl.

What is green infrastructure?

Green infrastructure uses vegetation, soil, and natural processes to manage stormwater, reduce flooding, improve air quality, and cool urban areas. Examples include rain gardens, bioswales, green roofs, urban forests, and permeable pavement that mimic natural hydrological functions.

A city wants to redevelop an abandoned factory site (brownfield) into affordable housing. Nearby residents are concerned about soil contamination from the factory. An environmental planner is asked to evaluate the project. What is the best approach?

Brownfield redevelopment is environmentally preferable to greenfield development when contamination is properly assessed and remediated. It reuses already-developed land, prevents further sprawl, can revitalize urban neighborhoods, and addresses existing environmental contamination that might otherwise go unaddressed.

Real-World Applications

Example: What percentage of the world's population currently lives in urban areas?

As of the early 2020s, approximately 56% of the world's population lives in urban areas, and this proportion is projected to increase to nearly 70% by 2050. This represents a dramatic shift from 1950 when only about 30% of the global population was urban.

Example: A rapidly growing city in a developing country is expanding outward, converting surrounding farmland into housing developments. Farmers are being displaced, food production is declining locally, and commute times are increasing. Which urban planning approach would best address these problems?

Smart growth strategies can accommodate population growth while minimizing farmland conversion. Higher-density development, mixed-use zoning, and public transit allow more people to live within existing urban boundaries, preserving productive agricultural land and reducing the environmental footprint per resident.

Example: A suburb built in the 1960s consists entirely of single-family homes with large yards, separated from shops and workplaces by highways. Residents drive an average of 40 miles per day. A nearby mixed-use neighborhood built in 2010 has apartments, shops, offices, and a light rail station; residents drive only 8 miles per day. What does this comparison demonstrate?

This comparison demonstrates that urban design directly influences how much people drive and their associated environmental impact. Mixed-use, transit-oriented development reduces vehicle miles traveled by over 80% compared to car-dependent suburbs, cutting emissions, energy use, and land consumption.

Mangrove forests provide critical coastal protection by absorbing wave energy and reducing storm surge. Removing mangroves for development eliminates this natural buffer, increasing vulnerability to tsunamis and storms. This demonstrates the hidden costs of land use change when ecosystem services are not valued.
