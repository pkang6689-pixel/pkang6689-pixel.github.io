Lesson 3.7: AP Prep: Demographic Calculations - Summary

Key Concepts: AP Prep: Demographic Calculations

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

A country has a CBR of 25 per 1,000 and a CDR of 10 per 1,000. What is the natural rate of increase as a percentage?

NIR = (CBR - CDR) / 10 = (25 - 10) / 10 = 1.5%. The division by 10 converts from per 1,000 to a percentage. This means the population is growing at 1.5% per year from natural increase alone, which would produce a doubling time of about 47 years.

If a country has 20 million people under 15, 60 million aged 15-64, and 20 million over 64, what is the dependency ratio?

Dependency ratio = (20M + 20M) / 60M x 100 = 40M / 60M x 100 = 66.7, rounded to 67. This means there are approximately 67 dependents for every 100 working-age people, indicating a moderately high economic burden on the workforce.

What is the Rule of 70?

The Rule of 70 provides a quick estimate of doubling time: doubling time (years) = 70 / annual growth rate (%). For example, a population growing at 2% doubles in 70/2 = 35 years. The rule works because 70 is an approximation of the natural logarithm of 2 (0.693) multiplied by 100.

What is the crude birth rate (CBR)?

The crude birth rate is calculated by dividing the total number of live births in a year by the mid-year population, then multiplying by 1,000. It is termed 'crude' because it does not account for age or sex composition, which can significantly influence birth rates.

What is the crude death rate (CDR)?

The crude death rate is calculated by dividing the total number of deaths in a year by the mid-year population, then multiplying by 1,000. Like the CBR, it is 'crude' because it does not adjust for age structure, which heavily influences mortality patterns.

What is net migration rate?

The net migration rate is the difference between immigration (people entering) and emigration (people leaving) per 1,000 population per year. A positive rate means more people are entering than leaving, while a negative rate indicates net outward migration.

What is population density?

Population density measures how concentrated a population is within a given area, typically expressed as people per square kilometer or square mile. High-density areas include cities and small countries, while low-density areas include deserts, forests, and sparsely populated countries.

What is the growth rate of a population?

The population growth rate is the percentage by which a population increases or decreases per year. It combines natural increase (births minus deaths) and net migration. A growth rate of 2% means the population increases by 2% of its current size each year.

What is the age-specific fertility rate?

The age-specific fertility rate measures births per 1,000 women within a particular age group (e.g., 20-24, 25-29). Summing all age-specific fertility rates gives an estimate of the total fertility rate, providing more detailed information about reproductive patterns than the crude birth rate.

What is geometric (exponential) growth?

Geometric or exponential growth occurs when a population increases by a constant proportion (percentage) each time period. Because the base number grows, each successive increase is larger in absolute terms, producing the characteristic J-shaped curve. This is the growth pattern assumed by the Rule of 70.

A city has 500,000 residents, 8,000 births, 4,000 deaths, 3,000 immigrants, and 1,000 emigrants in one year. What is the total population growth rate for that year?

Total change = (8,000 + 3,000) - (4,000 + 1,000) = 11,000 - 5,000 = 6,000 people. Growth rate = (6,000 / 500,000) x 100 = 1.2%. This rate includes both natural increase (0.8%) and net migration (0.4%).

Two countries start with 100 million people each in 2020. Country A grows at 0.5% per year and Country B at 2.5% per year. An AP question asks approximately how many people Country B will have added by 2048 compared to Country A. What is the answer?

In 28 years: Country A at 0.5% doubles in 140 years, so grows about 15% to ~115 million (added ~15M). Country B at 2.5% doubles in 28 years to 200 million (added ~100M). Country B adds about 85 million more than Country A, demonstrating how small differences in growth rates compound dramatically.

An AP problem provides: Country with CBR = 18, CDR = 9, TFR = 2.0, IMR = 5, life expectancy = 78 years. You are asked to classify this country by DTM stage and predict future population trends. What is the correct classification?

Low CBR (18), low CDR (9), near-replacement TFR (2.0), very low IMR (5), and high life expectancy (78) are all hallmarks of a Stage 4 country. With TFR at 2.0, slightly below the 2.1 replacement level, the population may begin to slowly decline without immigration, suggesting a possible transition to Stage 5.

Real-World Applications

Example: Country X has a population of 20 million, a CBR of 35 per 1,000, and a CDR of 10 per 1,000. Calculate the natural rate of increase as a percentage and the approximate doubling time.

NIR = (CBR - CDR) / 10 = (35 - 10) / 10 = 2.5%. Doubling time = 70 / 2.5 = 28 years. This means Country X's population will grow from 20 million to 40 million in approximately 28 years if the growth rate remains constant.

Example: A country has 30 million people under 15, 50 million aged 15-64, and 20 million over 64. Calculate the total dependency ratio and identify whether the youth or elderly dependency is greater.

Total dependency ratio = (30M + 20M) / 50M x 100 = 100. This means there are 100 dependents per 100 workers. Youth dependency = 30/50 x 100 = 60, elderly dependency = 20/50 x 100 = 40. Youth dependency (60) is greater than elderly dependency (40).

Example: The world population was approximately 6 billion in 1999 and 8 billion in 2022. An AP exam asks you to calculate the average annual growth rate during this 23-year period. Which is closest?

The population grew by 2 billion out of 6 billion, or 33.3%, over 23 years. Average annual rate = approximately 33.3% / 23 = 1.45%, but using compound growth, the rate is closer to 1.25% per year. Using the Rule of 70, a 1.25% rate gives a doubling time of 56 years, consistent with the roughly one-third increase over 23 years.
