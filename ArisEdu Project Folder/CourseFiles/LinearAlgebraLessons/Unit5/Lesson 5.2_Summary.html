Lesson 5.2: Subspaces - Summary

Key Concepts: Subspaces

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is a subspace of a vector space V?

A subspace of V is a nonempty subset W of V that is itself a vector space when using the same addition and scalar multiplication operations defined on V. In practice, we verify the three conditions: contains zero, closed under addition, closed under scalar multiplication.

What is the 'trivial subspace' of a vector space V?

The trivial subspace (or zero subspace) is the set {0} containing only the zero vector. It is the smallest possible subspace of any vector space. Some texts also call V itself a trivial subspace, but the term most commonly refers to {0}.

What is the 'sum' of two subspaces W1 and W2?

The sum of two subspaces W1 + W2 is the set of all vectors that can be written as w1 + w2 where w1 is in W1 and w2 is in W2. This sum is always a subspace and is the smallest subspace containing both W1 and W2.

What is meant by 'the subspace spanned by a set S'?

The subspace spanned by S (denoted span(S)) is the intersection of all subspaces containing S, which equals the set of all possible linear combinations of vectors in S. It is the smallest subspace that contains every vector in S.

What is the relationship between a subspace and the original vector space's operations?

A subspace must use the same operations (addition and scalar multiplication) as the parent vector space. It inherits all properties from the parent space, which is why only three conditions need to be checked instead of all ten axioms.

What is a 'complementary subspace'?

A complementary subspace W' to a subspace W in V is a subspace such that V = W ⊕ W', meaning W + W' = V and W ∩ W' = {0}. Every vector in V can be uniquely written as w + w' with w in W and w' in W'.
