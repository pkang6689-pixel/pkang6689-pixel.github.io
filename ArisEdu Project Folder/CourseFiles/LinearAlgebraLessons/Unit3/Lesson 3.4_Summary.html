Lesson 3.4: Determinants of Larger Matrices - Summary

Key Concepts: Determinants of Larger Matrices

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is det of the 4x4 identity matrix?

The determinant of any nxn identity matrix is 1. The diagonal entries are all 1, and their product is 1.

For a 4x4 upper triangular matrix with diagonal [2, 3, -1, 4], what is the determinant?

For a triangular matrix, det = product of diagonal entries = 2 * 3 * (-1) * 4 = -24.

A 5x5 matrix has rank 4. What is its determinant?

If the rank is less than n (the matrix size), the rows are linearly dependent and det = 0. Since rank 4 < 5, det = 0.

A 4x4 matrix is reduced to upper triangular form using 3 row swaps and no scaling. The diagonal of the triangular form is [1, 2, 5, 3]. What is the original determinant?

Product of diagonal = 1*2*5*3 = 30. With 3 row swaps: (-1)^3 * 30 = -30.

For a 5x5 diagonal matrix with entries [2, 3, 1, 4, 5], what is the determinant?

For a diagonal matrix, det = product of diagonal entries = 2*3*1*4*5 = 120.

What is det(A) if A is a 6x6 matrix with two identical columns?

If any two columns (or rows) are identical, the determinant is zero. This follows from the alternating property since swapping those columns gives the same matrix but negates the determinant.

Using row reduction on a 4x4 matrix, you scale row 2 by 1/3 and swap rows 1 and 4, then reach upper triangular form with diagonal [1, 1, 2, 5]. What is the original determinant?

Triangular det = 1*1*2*5 = 10. Undo the scaling (multiply by 3) and undo the swap (multiply by -1): 10 * 3 * (-1) = -30.

For a block diagonal 6x6 matrix with blocks A (2x2, det=3) and B (4x4, det=-2), what is det?

For a block diagonal matrix, det = det(A)*det(B) = 3*(-2) = -6.

What is a 'permutation matrix'?

A permutation matrix is obtained by rearranging the rows (or columns) of the identity matrix. Its determinant is +1 or -1 depending on whether the permutation is even or odd.

What is 'LU decomposition' and how does it relate to determinants?

LU decomposition factors A into lower and upper triangular matrices. Since det of triangular matrices is the product of their diagonals, det(A) = det(L)*det(U) is efficient to compute.

What is the 'sign of a permutation' (sgn)?

sgn(sigma) = +1 for even permutations (even number of swaps) and -1 for odd permutations (odd number of swaps). This sign determines each term's contribution in the Leibniz formula.

What is the 'Sarrus rule'?

Sarrus' rule copies the first two columns to the right of the 3x3 matrix, then adds the products of the three downward diagonals and subtracts the products of the three upward diagonals. It only works for 3x3 matrices.

What is 'computational complexity' of determinant calculation via Gaussian elimination?

Gaussian elimination reduces the matrix to triangular form in O(n^3) operations, after which the determinant is the product of diagonal entries. This is much faster than cofactor expansion's O(n!).

What is a 'Vandermonde matrix' and what is special about its determinant?

A Vandermonde matrix has (i,j) entry x_i^(j-1). Its determinant is the product of all differences (x_j - x_i) for j > i, which is nonzero when all x_i are distinct.

A control systems engineer has a 4x4 state matrix with det(A) = 12. She computes A^2. What is det(A^2)?

det(A^2) = (det A)^2 = 12^2 = 144.

A quantum physicist works with a 4x4 Hermitian matrix. She needs det(-A) where A is 4x4 with det(A) = 5. What is det(-A)?

det(-A) = (-1)^4 * det(A) = 1 * 5 = 5. For a 4x4 matrix, (-1)^n = (-1)^4 = 1.

A climate scientist has a 6x6 model matrix and wants to check invertibility. After LU decomposition, L has diagonal [1,1,1,1,1,1] and U has diagonal [2, -3, 1, 4, -1, 2]. What is det(A)?

det(A) = det(L)*det(U). det(L) = product of L's diagonal = 1. det(U) = 2*(-3)*1*4*(-1)*2 = 48. So det(A) = 48.

A bioinformatics researcher has a 5x5 matrix where column 4 is three times column 2. Without computing, what is det?

If one column is a scalar multiple of another, the columns are linearly dependent, so det = 0 regardless of the other entries.

A mechanical engineer has a 4x4 mass matrix M with det(M) = 100 and a 4x4 stiffness matrix K with det(K) = 50. She computes det(M^(-1)K). What is it?

det(M^(-1)K) = det(M^(-1)) * det(K) = (1/100) * 50 = 0.5.
