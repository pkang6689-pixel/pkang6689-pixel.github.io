Lesson 7.2: Finding Eigenvalues - Summary

Key Concepts: Finding Eigenvalues

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

A matrix has eigenvalues 2, -3, and 5. What is its determinant?

The determinant equals the product of all eigenvalues: 2 * (-3) * 5 = -30.

If lambda = 4 is an eigenvalue of A, what is an eigenvalue of 3A?

If Av = 4v, then (3A)v = 3(Av) = 3(4v) = 12v. Scaling a matrix by c multiplies all eigenvalues by c.

If lambda = 3 is an eigenvalue of A, what is the corresponding eigenvalue of A^2?

If Av = 3v, then A^2 v = A(Av) = A(3v) = 3(Av) = 9v. The eigenvalues of A^k are lambda^k.

If A is invertible with eigenvalue lambda = 2, what is the corresponding eigenvalue of A^(-1)?

If Av = 2v, then v = A^(-1)(2v) = 2A^(-1)v, so A^(-1)v = (1/2)v. The eigenvalues of A^(-1) are the reciprocals of those of A.

What is an eigenvalue of a matrix?

An eigenvalue lambda is a scalar for which Av = lambda v has a nonzero solution v. This means A acts on v by simply scaling it.

What is meant by a 'defective eigenvalue'?

An eigenvalue is defective when its geometric multiplicity (dimension of eigenspace) is strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity (multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial).

What is a 'dominant eigenvalue' of a matrix?

The dominant eigenvalue is the eigenvalue with the largest absolute value. It determines the long-term behavior of systems like A^k v as k grows large.

What is meant by a 'similar matrix' in the context of eigenvalues?

Matrices A and B are similar if B = P^(-1)AP for some invertible P. Similar matrices share the same characteristic polynomial and therefore the same eigenvalues.

What is meant by the 'power method' for finding eigenvalues?

The power method starts with a random vector and repeatedly multiplies by A, normalizing at each step. The sequence converges to the eigenvector of the dominant eigenvalue.

What is a 'characteristic root' of a matrix?

A characteristic root is simply another term for an eigenvalue. It refers to a root of the characteristic polynomial det(A - lambda I) = 0.

A Markov chain model for customer loyalty has transition matrix T = [[0.9, 0.2], [0.1, 0.8]]. One eigenvalue is always 1 for a stochastic matrix. What is the other eigenvalue?

The trace = 0.9 + 0.8 = 1.7 = sum of eigenvalues. Since one eigenvalue is 1, the other is 1.7 - 1 = 0.7. Since |0.7| < 1, the chain converges to its steady state.

A climate scientist models heat transfer between two ocean layers with A = [[-0.4, 0.2], [0.4, -0.2]]. One eigenvalue is 0. What is the other eigenvalue, and what does it indicate?

The trace = -0.4 + (-0.2) = -0.6. Since the eigenvalues sum to the trace and one is 0, the other is -0.6. The negative eigenvalue means the non-equilibrium component decays exponentially.

A web developer uses the adjacency matrix A = [[0, 1, 1], [1, 0, 1], [1, 1, 0]] for a 3-page website. What is the largest eigenvalue of A?

This is the adjacency matrix of the complete graph K3. The eigenvalues are 2 (once) and -1 (twice). The vector (1,1,1) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue 2, since each row sums to 2.

An economist models a two-sector economy with the input-output matrix A = [[0.3, 0.4], [0.2, 0.5]]. What is the dominant eigenvalue, and does the Hawkins-Simon condition hold (all eigenvalues of A less than 1)?

det(A - lambda I) = (0.3-lambda)(0.5-lambda) - 0.08 = lambda^2 - 0.8lambda + 0.07 = (lambda - 0.7)(lambda - 0.1). The dominant eigenvalue is 0.7 < 1, confirming the Hawkins-Simon condition holds.
