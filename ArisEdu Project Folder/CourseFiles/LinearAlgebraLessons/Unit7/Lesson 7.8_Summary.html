Lesson 7.8: Applications in Economics - Summary

Key Concepts: Applications in Economics

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

A two-sector economy has input-output matrix A = [[0.2, 0.3], [0.4, 0.1]]. What is the largest eigenvalue of A?

det(A - lambda I) = (0.2-lambda)(0.1-lambda) - 0.12 = lambda^2 - 0.3lambda - 0.1. Using the quadratic formula: lambda = (0.3 +/- sqrt(0.09 + 0.4))/2 = (0.3 +/- 0.7)/2. So lambda = 0.5 and -0.2.

What is the Leontief input-output model?

The Leontief model describes how sectors of an economy depend on each other. Production x must satisfy x = Ax + d, solved as x = (I-A)^(-1)d when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.

What is the 'Hawkins-Simon condition' in economics?

The Hawkins-Simon condition ensures the economy is productive: each sector can produce more than it consumes. Mathematically, the spectral radius of A must be less than 1.

What is a 'stochastic matrix' in economic modeling?

A stochastic matrix has nonnegative entries with columns (or rows) summing to 1. It models probabilistic transitions such as consumer brand switching or economic state transitions.

What is 'dynamic input-output analysis' in economics?

Dynamic input-output analysis extends the Leontief model to include capital accumulation: x(t+1) = Ax(t) + Bx(t). Eigenvalues determine growth rates and stability of the economic trajectory.

What is the 'von Neumann growth model' in economics?

The von Neumann model finds the maximum rate at which all sectors can grow simultaneously. This balanced growth rate equals the dominant eigenvalue, and the eigenvector gives the balanced growth proportions.

What is 'convergence' in economic growth theory related to eigenvalues?

In the Solow model, the speed of convergence to steady state is -(1-alpha)*delta, where this is the eigenvalue of the linearized system. A more negative eigenvalue means faster convergence.

What is the 'multiplier effect' in the Leontief model related to eigenvalues?

The Leontief inverse (I-A)^(-1) = I + A + A^2 + ... captures all rounds of inter-industry demand. This geometric series converges precisely when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.

What is 'factor analysis' in econometrics?

Factor analysis uses eigendecomposition of the correlation matrix to identify latent factors (e.g., market risk, interest rate risk) that explain the correlations among observed economic variables.

What is a 'steady-state distribution' in an economic Markov model?

The steady-state distribution pi is the eigenvector for eigenvalue 1 of the transition matrix, normalized so entries sum to 1. It represents the long-run market shares or state probabilities.

A financial analyst has a portfolio covariance matrix C = [[0.04, 0.02], [0.02, 0.09]]. The eigenvalues are approximately 0.1 and 0.03. What is the maximum portfolio variance achievable?

The maximum variance of a unit-weight portfolio w^T C w is the largest eigenvalue (0.1), achieved when w is the corresponding eigenvector. This is the direction of maximum risk.

A real estate economist models housing market dynamics with x(t+1) = [[0.7, 0.1], [0.3, 0.9]]x(t), where x1 is urban and x2 is suburban population. What is the long-run urban-to-suburban ratio?

This is a column-stochastic matrix (columns sum to 1), so eigenvalue 1 exists. A - I = [[-0.3, 0.1], [0.3, -0.1]]. From row 1: 0.3*x1 = 0.1*x2, so x1/x2 = 1/3. The long-run ratio is 1:3, meaning the suburban population is three times the urban population.

Real-World Applications

Example: A Leslie population model for age-structured economic labor force has matrix L with dominant eigenvalue 1.03. What does this predict?

The dominant eigenvalue determines long-run growth: lambda > 1 means growth, lambda < 1 means decline. With lambda = 1.03, the population grows at 3% per period, approaching the stable age distribution given by the eigenvector.

Example: In a simple Solow growth model linearized around the steady state, the eigenvalue determines the convergence speed. If lambda = 0.95, what percentage of the gap to steady state closes each period?

If the deviation from steady state decays as 0.95^t, then each period the gap shrinks by (1 - 0.95) = 5%. The economy closes 5% of its distance to steady state each period.

The Leontief inverse (I-A)^(-1) = I + A + A^2 + ... captures all rounds of inter-industry demand. This geometric series converges precisely when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.

Example: A development economist computes the Leontief inverse for a developing country and finds the largest entry is 3.2. What does this 'multiplier' mean?

The column sum of the Leontief inverse for sector j gives the total output multiplier: how much total output across all sectors increases for a $1 increase in final demand for sector j.
