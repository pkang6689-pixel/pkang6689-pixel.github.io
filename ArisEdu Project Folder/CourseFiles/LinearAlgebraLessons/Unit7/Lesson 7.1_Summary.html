Lesson 7.1: Characteristic Polynomial - Summary

Key Concepts: Characteristic Polynomial

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

What is the characteristic polynomial of a 2x2 matrix A defined as?

The characteristic polynomial of a matrix A is defined as p(lambda) = det(A - lambda I), where I is the identity matrix of the same size.

What is the degree of the characteristic polynomial of a 3x3 matrix?

The characteristic polynomial of an n x n matrix is always a polynomial of degree n. For a 3x3 matrix, the degree is 3.

For a diagonal matrix D = diag(2, 5, -1), what is the characteristic polynomial?

For a diagonal matrix, the characteristic polynomial is the product of (d_i - lambda) for each diagonal entry d_i. So p(lambda) = (2 - lambda)(5 - lambda)(-1 - lambda).

If the characteristic polynomial of a 2x2 matrix is lambda^2 - 5lambda + 6, what is the trace of the matrix?

For a 2x2 matrix, the characteristic polynomial is lambda^2 - (trace)lambda + det. Comparing with lambda^2 - 5lambda + 6, the trace is 5.

What is the characteristic polynomial of the 2x2 identity matrix I?

For I = [[1,0],[0,1]], det(I - lambda I) = (1-lambda)^2 = (lambda-1)^2. The only eigenvalue is 1 with algebraic multiplicity 2.

What is the constant term of the characteristic polynomial of a matrix A?

Evaluating the characteristic polynomial at lambda = 0 gives det(A - 0*I) = det(A). Therefore, the constant term equals the determinant of A.

What is the characteristic polynomial of a matrix?

The characteristic polynomial is p(lambda) = det(A - lambda I). Its roots are precisely the eigenvalues of the matrix A.

What is the trace of a matrix in relation to its characteristic polynomial?

The trace of a matrix equals the sum of its eigenvalues, which are the roots of the characteristic polynomial. For an n x n matrix, the coefficient of lambda^(n-1) equals (-1) times the trace.

What is a 'monic polynomial' in the context of characteristic polynomials?

A monic polynomial has a leading coefficient of 1. Characteristic polynomials are often written in monic form as lambda^n - (trace)lambda^(n-1) + ... + (-1)^n det(A).

What is the minimal polynomial of a matrix?

The minimal polynomial is the monic polynomial of smallest degree such that p(A) = 0. It always divides the characteristic polynomial and shares the same roots.

What is the 'characteristic equation' of a matrix?

The characteristic equation is det(A - lambda I) = 0. Solving this equation yields the eigenvalues of the matrix A.

What is the 'spectrum' of a matrix?

The spectrum of a matrix is the set of all its eigenvalues. It consists of all values of lambda that are roots of the characteristic polynomial.

What is the spectral radius of a matrix?

The spectral radius rho(A) is the maximum absolute value of the eigenvalues: rho(A) = max(|lambda_i|). It plays a key role in convergence analysis of iterative methods.

A structural engineer models a two-story building's vibration modes using the stiffness matrix K = [[5, -2], [-2, 3]]. To find the natural frequencies, she needs the characteristic polynomial of K. What is it?

det(K - lambda I) = (5 - lambda)(3 - lambda) - (-2)(-2) = lambda^2 - 8lambda + 15 - 4 = lambda^2 - 8lambda + 11. The roots give the squares of the natural frequencies.

A population biologist models two competing species with the matrix A = [[1.2, -0.3], [0.1, 0.8]]. To analyze long-term behavior, she computes the characteristic polynomial. What is the sum of the eigenvalues?

The sum of the eigenvalues equals the trace of A. trace(A) = 1.2 + 0.8 = 2.0. This is the coefficient of lambda in the characteristic polynomial (with a sign change).

A data scientist computes the covariance matrix C = [[4, 2], [2, 3]] for a dataset. What is the product of the eigenvalues of C?

The product of the eigenvalues equals the determinant. det(C) = 4(3) - 2(2) = 12 - 4 = 8. This fact comes directly from the constant term of the characteristic polynomial.

A chemical engineer models a two-reactor system with the rate matrix A = [[-3, 1], [2, -4]]. She uses the Cayley-Hamilton theorem to express A^(-1) in terms of A and I. If the characteristic polynomial is lambda^2 + 7lambda + 10, what is A^(-1)?

By Cayley-Hamilton, A^2 + 7A + 10I = 0. Multiplying both sides by A^(-1): A + 7I + 10A^(-1) = 0, so A^(-1) = (-1/10)(A + 7I).

A quantum computing researcher works with the Pauli-Z matrix Z = [[1, 0], [0, -1]]. What is the characteristic polynomial of Z?

det(Z - lambda I) = (1-lambda)(-1-lambda) = -(1-lambda)(1+lambda) = lambda^2 - 1. The eigenvalues are 1 and -1, corresponding to spin-up and spin-down states.

A financial analyst models portfolio risk with the covariance matrix S = [[9, 3, 0], [3, 5, 1], [0, 1, 2]]. What is the sum of all eigenvalues of S?

The sum of eigenvalues equals the trace. trace(S) = 9 + 5 + 2 = 16. This represents the total variance across all three assets in the portfolio.

An electrical engineer designs a filter described by the transfer matrix H = [[2, -1], [1, 4]]. To determine the system's poles, she finds the characteristic polynomial. What is it?

det(H - lambda I) = (2-lambda)(4-lambda) - (-1)(1) = 8 - 6lambda + lambda^2 + 1 = lambda^2 - 6lambda + 9 = (lambda - 3)^2. The repeated eigenvalue 3 indicates a critically damped system.

A wildlife biologist uses a Leslie matrix L = [[0, 4], [0.5, 0]] to model a fish population. What is the dominant eigenvalue (largest in absolute value)?

det(L - lambda I) = (0 - lambda)(0 - lambda) - (4)(0.5) = lambda^2 - 2. So lambda = sqrt(2) and -sqrt(2). The dominant eigenvalue is sqrt(2) approximately 1.414, indicating population growth.

Real-World Applications

Example: What does the term 'algebraic multiplicity' mean for an eigenvalue?

The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue is the number of times it appears as a root of the characteristic polynomial, counting repeated roots.

Example: What does 'geometric multiplicity' of an eigenvalue refer to?

The geometric multiplicity is the dimension of the eigenspace ker(A - lambda I), i.e., the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for that eigenvalue. It is always at most equal to the algebraic multiplicity.
