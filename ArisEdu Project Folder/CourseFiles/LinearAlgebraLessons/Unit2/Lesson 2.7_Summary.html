Lesson 2.7: Applications in Economics - Summary

Key Concepts: Applications in Economics

Overview

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

Key Definitions

A simple supply-demand model has: supply S = 2p + 10 and demand D = -3p + 60. At equilibrium (S = D), what is the price p?

Setting S = D: 2p + 10 = -3p + 60, so 5p = 50 and p = 10. The equilibrium quantity is S = 2(10) + 10 = 30.

In a market with two goods, the equilibrium conditions give the system: 3p1 - p2 = 10 and -p1 + 4p2 = 20. What is p1?

From equation 1: p2 = 3p1 - 10. Substitute into equation 2: -p1 + 4(3p1 - 10) = 20, so 11p1 = 60, p1 = 60/11.

An economy has two sectors with consumption matrix C = [[0.1, 0.3], [0.4, 0.2]] and demand d = [20, 30]^T. What is (I - C)?

I - C = [[1-0.1, 0-0.3], [0-0.4, 1-0.2]] = [[0.9, -0.3], [-0.4, 0.8]].

In a two-commodity exchange model, each unit of good 1 costs $3 of labor and $2 of capital, while each unit of good 2 costs $1 of labor and $4 of capital. If total labor is 120 and total capital is 160, the system is 3x + y = 120 and 2x + 4y = 160. What is x?

From eq 2: x + 2y = 80, so x = 80 - 2y. Substitute into eq 1: 3(80-2y) + y = 120, 240 - 6y + y = 120, -5y = -120, y = 24. Then x = 80 - 48 = 32.

What is the 'Leontief input-output model'?

The Leontief input-output model uses a consumption matrix C to describe inter-industry transactions. It solves (I-C)x = d to find the total production x needed to meet external demand d.

What is a 'consumption matrix' in the Leontief model?

In the consumption matrix C, entry c_{ij} represents the amount (in dollars or units) of sector i's output that sector j needs to produce one unit of its own output.

What is an 'exchange model' in economics?

In an exchange model (closed Leontief model), all production is consumed within the economy. The goal is to find relative prices so that each sector's income equals its expenditure.

What is a 'Leontief inverse'?

The Leontief inverse (I - C)^(-1) captures both direct and indirect production requirements. Multiplying it by the final demand vector d gives the total output each sector must produce.

An economist models two markets: good A with supply S_A = 2p_A - 10 and demand D_A = -p_A + 50, and good B with supply S_B = 3p_B - 15 and demand D_B = -2p_B + 60. The goods are independent. What is the equilibrium price of good A?

Setting S_A = D_A: 2p_A - 10 = -p_A + 50, so 3p_A = 60 and p_A = 20. Since the goods are independent, each equilibrium is solved separately.

A government planner uses the Leontief model for a 3-sector economy. She finds (I-C)^(-1) and multiplies by the new demand vector to project total production. The model predicts sector 1 needs to produce $200M, sector 2 needs $150M, and sector 3 needs $300M. What is the total production of the economy?

Total production is the sum of all sector outputs: $200M + $150M + $300M = $650M.

A retail chain has 3 stores. Weekly sales revenue satisfies: Store 1 + Store 2 = $10,000, Store 2 + Store 3 = $12,000, and Store 1 + Store 3 = $14,000. What is Store 3's revenue?

Adding all three: 2(S1+S2+S3) = 36000, so S1+S2+S3 = 18000. Then S3 = 18000 - 10000 = 8000.

Example: A factory produces products P1, P2, and P3 requiring resources R1 and R2. The resource usage matrix is [[2, 1, 3], [1, 3, 2]] and available resources are [100, 120]. The constraint system is 2x1 + x2 + 3x3 = 100 and x1 + 3x2 + 2x3 = 120. How many free variables exist?

With 2 independent equations and 3 unknowns, there are 3 - 2 = 1 free variable. The factory can choose one product level freely and compute the other two from constraints.
