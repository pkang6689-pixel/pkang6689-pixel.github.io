--- Lesson 4.1: Chemical Composition of Seawater (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Chemical Composition of Seawater
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the average salinity of ocean water?
The average salinity of ocean water is approximately 35 parts per thousand (ppt), or about 3.5%. This means that for every kilogram of seawater, roughly 35 grams are dissolved salts.
What is the primary source of salts dissolved in ocean water?
The primary source of dissolved salts in the ocean is chemical weathering of rocks on land. Rivers carry dissolved minerals from weathered rocks into the ocean, where they accumulate over geological time.
What is the typical pH range of surface ocean water?
Surface ocean water is slightly basic, with a typical pH range of about 7.8 to 8.3. This pH is maintained by the ocean's carbonate buffering system, which resists rapid changes in acidity.
What is the principle of constant proportions in seawater chemistry?
The principle of constant proportions (Marcet's principle) states that while total salinity may vary, the ratios of major dissolved ions to each other remain remarkably constant throughout the world's oceans. This allows scientists to estimate total salinity by measuring just one ion.
What is a "conservative element" in ocean chemistry?
A conservative element is one whose ratio to total salinity stays constant throughout the ocean. Sodium and chloride are examples. Their concentrations are not significantly affected by biological processes, so they mix uniformly.
What is a "buffer" in the context of ocean chemistry?
In ocean chemistry, a buffer is a chemical system that resists changes in pH. The ocean's primary buffer is the carbonate system, involving dissolved CO₂, bicarbonate, and carbonate ions, which helps maintain relatively stable pH levels.
What is meant by "parts per thousand" (ppt) in measuring salinity?
Parts per thousand (ppt) expresses salinity as grams of dissolved salt per kilogram (1000 grams) of seawater. At 35 ppt, there are 35 grams of dissolved salts in every kilogram of seawater. The symbol ‰ is often used to represent ppt.
What is a "non-conservative element" in ocean chemistry?
A non-conservative element is one whose concentration in seawater is affected by biological uptake, chemical reactions, or other processes beyond simple mixing. Nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus are non-conservative because organisms consume and release them.
What is a "trace element" in seawater?
A trace element is one present in very small concentrations in seawater, typically less than 1 part per million (ppm). Examples include iron, zinc, and copper. Despite their low concentrations, trace elements can be essential for biological processes.
A marine biologist measures salinity at 28 ppt near the mouth of a large river. What is the most likely explanation?
Near river mouths, freshwater mixes with seawater, creating a zone of reduced salinity called an estuary. The river's freshwater dilutes the dissolved salts, lowering measured salinity below the ocean average of ~35 ppt.
A scientist measures the salinity at the ocean surface near the equator and finds it lower than at 25° N latitude. What is the best explanation?
The equatorial region receives heavy rainfall from the Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ), which dilutes surface salinity. The subtropical region at 25° N has less precipitation and high evaporation, resulting in higher salinity.
A coastal city is considering using seawater for agricultural irrigation. Based on your knowledge of seawater chemistry, what is the main concern?
At ~35 ppt salinity, seawater contains far too much dissolved salt for most crops. The high sodium and chloride concentrations would cause osmotic stress in plants, preventing water uptake, and would lead to salt accumulation in the soil over time.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the average salinity of ocean water?
The average salinity of ocean water is approximately 35 parts per thousand (ppt), or about 3.5%. This means that for every kilogram of seawater, roughly 35 grams are dissolved salts.
Example: A researcher finds that a sample of seawater from the Red Sea has a salinity of 40 ppt, well above average. Which factor best explains this?
The Red Sea is located in a hot, arid region with very high evaporation rates and almost no freshwater input from rivers or rainfall. As water evaporates, salts become more concentrated, leading to salinity above the global average.

--- Lesson 4.2: Gas Exchange (Oxygen, Carbon Dioxide) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gas Exchange (Oxygen, Carbon Dioxide)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary biological source of dissolved oxygen in the ocean?
Photosynthesis by phytoplankton and other marine plants produces the majority of dissolved oxygen in the ocean. These organisms use sunlight and CO₂ to make organic compounds, releasing O₂ as a byproduct.
What is the ocean's biological pump?
The biological pump is the process by which phytoplankton fix CO₂ through photosynthesis and the resulting organic carbon is transported to deeper waters when organisms die, sink, or are consumed. This moves carbon from the surface to the deep ocean.
What is the "oxygen minimum zone" (OMZ)?
The oxygen minimum zone (OMZ) is a layer typically found between 200–1000 meters depth where dissolved oxygen concentrations are at their lowest. This occurs because respiration and decomposition consume oxygen, while distance from the surface limits replenishment.
What is "Henry's Law" as it applies to ocean gas exchange?
Henry's Law states that at a constant temperature, the amount of gas dissolved in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas in the atmosphere above the liquid. Higher atmospheric CO₂ levels therefore lead to more CO₂ dissolving in the ocean.
What is "diffusion" in the context of gas exchange?
Diffusion is the net movement of molecules from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration. In ocean gas exchange, if the surface water has less dissolved CO₂ than the atmosphere, CO₂ will diffuse from air into the water.
What is "respiration" in the marine environment?
Respiration is a metabolic process where organisms consume oxygen and organic compounds to produce energy, releasing CO₂ and water as byproducts. In the ocean, respiration by all marine life forms is a major consumer of dissolved oxygen.
What is "supersaturation" of a dissolved gas?
Supersaturation occurs when the dissolved gas concentration exceeds what the water can hold at equilibrium under current temperature and pressure conditions. This can happen when water warms rapidly or during intense photosynthesis, and excess gas tends to escape to the atmosphere.
What is "equilibrium" in the context of air-sea gas exchange?
Equilibrium in air-sea gas exchange occurs when the flux of gas molecules entering the water equals the flux leaving it, resulting in no net transfer. At equilibrium, the dissolved gas concentration is determined by the gas's partial pressure and solubility.
A scientist finds that a deep ocean basin has almost no dissolved oxygen. What is the most likely cause?
In poorly ventilated deep basins, bacteria decompose sinking organic matter (dead organisms, fecal pellets) through respiration, consuming dissolved oxygen. Without adequate mixing or circulation to replenish oxygen from the surface, the water becomes anoxic.
A fish farmer notices large fish die-offs in a warm, stagnant pond during summer. What is the most likely cause?
Warm water holds less dissolved oxygen, and stagnant water has little circulation to replenish oxygen from the atmosphere. Combined with high respiration rates from bacteria and other organisms in summer, dissolved oxygen can drop to lethal levels for fish.
A coral reef is located in warm, shallow water near a nutrient-rich runoff area. The reef experiences nighttime oxygen crashes. What is the best explanation?
Nutrient-rich runoff promotes algal blooms. During the day, algae produce oxygen via photosynthesis, but at night, all organisms respire without photosynthetic oxygen production. In warm water with reduced O₂ capacity, this can lead to dangerously low oxygen levels.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
According to Henry's Law, increasing the partial pressure of CO₂ in the atmosphere increases the amount that dissolves in the ocean surface. The ocean has absorbed roughly 30% of anthropogenic CO₂ emissions, leading to measurable changes in ocean chemistry.
Real-World Applications
Example: Approximately what percentage of global oxygen production comes from marine photosynthesis?
Marine photosynthesis, primarily by phytoplankton, produces approximately 50% or more of the world's oxygen. This makes the ocean one of the most important sources of atmospheric oxygen on Earth.

  Q2. How does water temperature affect the solubility of oxygen in seawater?
    [ ] Only tropical water can dissolve oxygen
    [ ] Warmer water holds more dissolved oxygen
    [ ] Temperature has no effect on oxygen solubility
    [*] Colder water holds more dissolved oxygen
    Explanation: Cold water can hold more dissolved oxygen than warm water because gas molecules move more slowly at lower temperatures and are more easily retained in the liquid. This is why polar waters tend to be more oxygen-rich than tropical waters.

    [ ] Respiration by fish
    [*] Photosynthesis by phytoplankton and marine plants
    [ ] Chemical reactions with sea salts
    [ ] Decomposition of organic matter
    Explanation: Photosynthesis by phytoplankton and other marine plants produces the majority of dissolved oxygen in the ocean. These organisms use sunlight and CO₂ to make organic compounds, releasing O₂ as a byproduct.

  Q4. Which process removes dissolved oxygen from seawater?
    [ ] Photosynthesis
    [ ] Evaporation of water
    [ ] Freezing of sea ice
    [*] Respiration by marine organisms
    Explanation: Aerobic respiration by marine organisms, from bacteria to whales, consumes dissolved oxygen. Decomposition of dead organic matter by bacteria also uses large amounts of oxygen, particularly in deeper waters.

  Q5. What happens to carbon dioxide when it dissolves in seawater?
    [ ] It remains as dissolved CO₂ gas only

    Explanation: When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which then dissociates into bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and carbonate (CO₃²⁻) ions. This series of reactions is central to the ocean's carbon chemistry.

    [ ] The abyssal zone
    [*] The surface mixed layer
    [ ] The hadal zone
    [ ] The oxygen minimum zone
    Explanation: The surface mixed layer has the highest dissolved oxygen because it is in direct contact with the atmosphere and receives oxygen from photosynthesis. Wind and wave action help mix oxygen into this layer.

    [ ] Wind has no effect on gas exchange
    [ ] Wind only affects temperature, not gas exchange
    Explanation: Wind increases gas exchange by generating waves and turbulence that break the ocean's surface layer, increasing the area of air-water contact. Higher wind speeds lead to greater rates of gas transfer between the atmosphere and ocean.

  Q8. Approximately what percentage of global oxygen production comes from marine photosynthesis?

    Explanation: Higher salinity decreases the solubility of gases in water due to the "salting out" effect. Dissolved ions occupy space and interact with water molecules, reducing the water's capacity to hold dissolved gases like O₂ and CO₂.

    Explanation: Partial pressure is the pressure contribution of an individual gas in a mixture of gases. For example, the partial pressure of CO₂ (pCO₂) in the atmosphere determines how much CO₂ will dissolve into the ocean surface.

--- Lesson 4.3: Ocean Acidification (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ocean Acidification
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is ocean acidification?
Ocean acidification is the ongoing decrease in ocean pH resulting from the ocean absorbing excess carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, which releases hydrogen ions and lowers pH.
What is the primary human activity driving ocean acidification?
The combustion of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) is the primary driver of ocean acidification. It releases massive amounts of CO₂ into the atmosphere, and the ocean absorbs roughly 25-30% of this excess CO₂, leading to acidification.
What is "calcification" in marine organisms?
Calcification is the biological process by which marine organisms extract calcium and carbonate ions from seawater to build shells, tests, or skeletal structures made of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). Ocean acidification reduces the availability of carbonate ions, making this process more difficult.
What is "carbonic acid"?
Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) is a weak acid that forms when CO₂ dissolves in water. Although individually weak, the vast amount of CO₂ being absorbed by the ocean produces enough carbonic acid to significantly lower ocean pH over time.
What is "bicarbonate" in ocean chemistry?
Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) is the most abundant form of dissolved inorganic carbon in seawater, making up about 90% of it. It forms when carbonic acid dissociates and plays a central role in the ocean's carbonate buffer system.
What is the "lysocline"?
The lysocline is the ocean depth below which calcium carbonate begins to dissolve at an appreciable rate due to increased pressure and higher dissolved CO₂ concentrations. Below the compensation depth (CCD), virtually no CaCO₃ is preserved in sediments.
What is a "pteropod"?
Pteropods are small, free-swimming marine snails often called "sea butterflies." They build thin shells made of aragonite, making them extremely vulnerable to ocean acidification. They are an important food source for many marine species including salmon.
An oyster farmer in the Pacific Northwest notices that oyster larvae are failing to grow their initial shells. Scientists link this to local ocean acidification. What is the mechanism behind the shell failure?
Oyster larvae are particularly vulnerable in their first 48 hours when they must rapidly build an initial shell. In acidified water with fewer carbonate ions, this process requires more energy and may fail entirely, leading to massive larval die-offs in affected hatcheries.
A fishing community relies on shellfish harvesting. If ocean pH continues to decline, what is the most likely economic impact?
Shellfish like oysters, clams, and mussels are directly vulnerable to acidification. Declining shell-building capacity would reduce populations and harvests, threatening the food supply and economic livelihoods of coastal communities that depend on these fisheries.
A country proposes adding large amounts of crushed limestone to the ocean to combat acidification. What is the scientific basis and potential concern with this approach?
Dissolving limestone (CaCO₃) would release carbonate ions that could neutralize some acidity. However, the enormous quantity needed to meaningfully affect ocean pH makes this approach impractical at global scales, and introducing massive amounts of sediment could harm marine ecosystems.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
At 800 ppm CO₂, ocean pH could drop by an additional 0.3-0.4 units, driving aragonite saturation well below the threshold needed for coral growth. Reef dissolution could exceed calcification, meaning reefs would erode faster than they can grow.
Real-World Applications
Example: Pteropod shells collected in the Southern Ocean show visible pitting and thinning compared to historical specimens. What process is causing this damage?
The Southern Ocean's cold waters readily absorb CO₂, making it one of the first regions to experience critically low aragonite saturation. Pteropod shells, made of fragile aragonite, show measurable dissolution damage that correlates with decreasing pH.

--- Lesson 4.4: Nutrient Cycles (Nitrogen, Phosphorus) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nutrient Cycles (Nitrogen, Phosphorus)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary source of phosphorus entering the ocean?
Unlike nitrogen, phosphorus has no significant gaseous phase. The main source of phosphorus to the ocean is the weathering of phosphate-bearing rocks on land, with dissolved phosphate carried to the sea by rivers.
What is the Redfield ratio?
The Redfield ratio (C:N:P = 106:16:1) describes the consistent elemental composition of marine phytoplankton and dissolved nutrients. It shows that phytoplankton take up carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus in a remarkably predictable ratio.
What is denitrification?
Denitrification is a microbial process where bacteria convert nitrate (NO₃⁻) back to nitrogen gas (N₂) under low-oxygen (anoxic) conditions. This returns bioavailable nitrogen to the atmosphere and is a major pathway for nitrogen removal from the ocean.
What is eutrophication in coastal waters?
Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients (often nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff) enter coastal waters, triggering massive algal blooms. When the algae die, decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic "dead zones" where marine life cannot survive.
What is "nitrification"?
Nitrification is a two-step bacterial process: first, ammonia-oxidizing bacteria convert ammonium (NH₄⁺) to nitrite (NO₂⁻), then nitrite-oxidizing bacteria convert nitrite to nitrate (NO₃⁻). This is an aerobic process requiring dissolved oxygen.
What is "ammonification" (or mineralization)?
Ammonification is the process by which decomposing bacteria break down organic nitrogen from dead organisms and waste into ammonium (NH₄⁺). This recycled ammonium can then be used by phytoplankton or further processed through nitrification.
What is a "dead zone" in the ocean?
Dead zones are regions of hypoxic (very low oxygen) water typically caused by eutrophication. Excess nutrient runoff fuels algal blooms, whose decomposition depletes dissolved oxygen. Fish, shrimp, and other mobile organisms flee, while sessile organisms may die.
What is "phosphate" (PO₄³⁻) in marine chemistry?
Phosphate (PO₄³⁻) is the dissolved inorganic form of phosphorus that marine organisms assimilate for building DNA, RNA, ATP, and cell membranes. It is an essential nutrient and often a limiting factor in certain ocean regions.
What is "new production" in marine ecology?
New production refers to primary productivity supported by nutrients introduced from outside the surface mixed layer, such as nitrate from deep-water upwelling or nitrogen fixation. It represents the portion of ocean production that can potentially be exported to depth.
What is a "nutrient profile" in oceanography?
A nutrient profile is a vertical plot of nutrient concentration versus depth. Typically, nutrients like nitrate and phosphate are depleted at the sunlit surface (consumed by phytoplankton) and increase with depth (regenerated by decomposition of sinking organic matter).
The Gulf of Mexico has one of the largest dead zones in the world, appearing each summer. What is the primary cause?

    Explanation: Nitrogen and phosphorus are the two nutrients that most frequently limit the growth of phytoplankton in the ocean. Without adequate supplies of both, algae cannot produce the proteins and nucleic acids needed for cell growth and reproduction.

    [ ] Volcanic activity on the ocean floor
    [*] Weathering of phosphorus-containing rocks on land
    [ ] Nitrogen fixation by cyanobacteria
    [ ] Atmospheric deposition of phosphorus gas
    Explanation: Unlike nitrogen, phosphorus has no significant gaseous phase. The main source of phosphorus to the ocean is the weathering of phosphate-bearing rocks on land, with dissolved phosphate carried to the sea by rivers.

  Q4. What process converts dissolved nitrogen gas (N₂) into biologically usable forms?
    [ ] Nitrification
    [*] Nitrogen fixation
    [ ] Denitrification
    [ ] Ammonification
    Explanation: Nitrogen fixation is carried out primarily by cyanobacteria (like Trichodesmium) that convert dissolved N₂ gas into ammonium (NH₄⁺), making it available for use by other organisms. This is a critical input of bioavailable nitrogen to the ocean.

  Q5. What happens to nutrient concentrations in surface waters during a phytoplankton bloom?

    [*] Nutrient concentrations decrease as phytoplankton consume them
    [ ] Nutrients are converted to salt
    [ ] Nutrient concentrations increase
    Explanation: During a phytoplankton bloom, organisms rapidly take up dissolved nutrients like nitrate and phosphate from surface waters. This biological consumption can deplete surface nutrient concentrations to near-zero levels, eventually limiting further growth.

    [*] Upwelling brings nutrient-rich deep water to the surface
    [ ] Upwelling removes nutrients from surface water
    [ ] Upwelling has no effect on nutrients
    [ ] Upwelling only affects temperature, not nutrients
    Explanation: Upwelling brings cold, nutrient-rich water from the deep ocean to the sunlit surface layer. This replenishment of nitrogen, phosphorus, and other nutrients fuels high productivity, making upwelling zones among the most biologically productive areas in the ocean.

  Q7. What is the Redfield ratio?

    [ ] The ratio of salt to water in the ocean
    Explanation: The Redfield ratio (C:N:P = 106:16:1) describes the consistent elemental composition of marine phytoplankton and dissolved nutrients. It shows that phytoplankton take up carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus in a remarkably predictable ratio.

  Q8. Why are deep ocean waters generally richer in nutrients than surface waters?
    [*] Decomposition of sinking organic matter releases nutrients back into deep water
    [ ] Sunlight destroys nutrients at the surface
    [ ] Volcanic vents continuously release nutrients at depth
    [ ] Deep currents carry nutrients from rivers
    Explanation: When organisms die, their remains sink into deeper water where bacteria decompose the organic material. This decomposition releases nitrogen, phosphorus, and other nutrients back into the water column, enriching deep waters over time.

    [ ] The conversion of ammonium to nitrate
    [ ] The addition of nitrogen to seawater by rivers
    [*] The conversion of nitrate to nitrogen gas by bacteria in low-oxygen environments
    Explanation: Denitrification is a microbial process where bacteria convert nitrate (NO₃⁻) back to nitrogen gas (N₂) under low-oxygen (anoxic) conditions. This returns bioavailable nitrogen to the atmosphere and is a major pathway for nitrogen removal from the ocean.

  Q10. What is eutrophication in coastal waters?

    [ ] Only available to bacteria, not plants

    Explanation: Bioavailable means a nutrient is in a chemical form that can be directly taken up and used by living organisms. For example, nitrate and ammonium are bioavailable forms of nitrogen, while dissolved N₂ gas is not bioavailable for most marine life.

    [ ] The uptake of nitrogen by phytoplankton

    Explanation: Nitrification is a two-step bacterial process: first, ammonia-oxidizing bacteria convert ammonium (NH₄⁺) to nitrite (NO₂⁻), then nitrite-oxidizing bacteria convert nitrite to nitrate (NO₃⁻). This is an aerobic process requiring dissolved oxygen.

    [ ] A nutrient that is toxic at low concentrations
    [*] The nutrient in shortest supply relative to biological demand, controlling growth rates
    [ ] The most abundant nutrient in a system
    Explanation: A limiting nutrient is the essential element in shortest supply relative to what organisms require. According to Liebig's Law of the Minimum, growth is constrained by the scarcest resource. In the open ocean, nitrogen is often the limiting nutrient.

--- Lesson 4.5: Case Studies in Marine Chemistry (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Marine Chemistry
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary chemical consequence of increased atmospheric CO₂ dissolving into the ocean?
When CO₂ dissolves in seawater it forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which dissociates and releases hydrogen ions. This increase in hydrogen ion concentration lowers the pH of the ocean, a process known as ocean acidification.
What is the primary cause of the seasonal dead zone in the Gulf of Mexico?
Nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural fertilizers wash into the Mississippi River and flow into the Gulf of Mexico. These excess nutrients fuel massive algal blooms; when the algae die and decompose, bacteria consume dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic conditions that most marine life cannot survive.
What is the main environmental concern associated with brine discharge from desalination plants?
Desalination plants produce concentrated brine as a byproduct, which is often discharged back into the ocean. This hypersaline water can create localized zones of extremely high salinity and may contain chemical additives used in the desalination process, posing threats to nearby benthic communities.
What is bioaccumulation?
Bioaccumulation refers to the gradual buildup of a substance, such as mercury or a pesticide, in an organism's tissues because the substance is absorbed faster than it can be metabolized or excreted. Over time, even low environmental concentrations can result in significant tissue concentrations.
What is biomagnification?
Biomagnification is the process by which the concentration of a toxin increases at each higher trophic level in a food chain. For example, mercury concentrations in tuna are millions of times higher than in the surrounding seawater because the toxin is concentrated at each step from plankton to small fish to large predatory fish.
What is eutrophication?
Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients—primarily nitrogen and phosphorus—enter a water body and stimulate explosive algal growth. When these algal blooms die and decompose, the process depletes dissolved oxygen, potentially creating dead zones that devastate marine ecosystems.
What is a chemical dispersant?
Chemical dispersants are surfactant-based agents that reduce the surface tension between oil and water, breaking oil slicks into tiny droplets that disperse throughout the water column. While they reduce surface oil, the dispersed oil droplets may affect organisms at various depths.
What is a chemical tracer in oceanography?
Chemical tracers are substances—either naturally occurring or artificially introduced—whose concentrations and distributions in the ocean can be measured to infer water mass movement, age, and mixing. Examples include radiocarbon (C-14), tritium, and chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).
What is meant by 'microplastic chemical leaching'?
Microplastic chemical leaching refers to the release of chemical additives (such as plasticizers, flame retardants, and UV stabilizers) and pollutants adsorbed from seawater from microplastic particles. When marine organisms ingest these particles, the chemicals can leach into their tissues and potentially enter the food chain.
What is the definition of a 'dead zone' in marine science?
A dead zone is an area in a body of water where dissolved oxygen levels have dropped so low (typically below 2 mg/L) that most aquatic organisms cannot survive. These hypoxic zones are most commonly caused by nutrient pollution that drives excessive algal growth and subsequent oxygen-consuming decomposition.
Researchers find that mussels collected near an urban harbor contain high concentrations of phthalates and bisphenol A (BPA), chemicals commonly used as plastic additives. The surrounding water has abundant microplastic particles. What is the most likely pathway by which these chemicals entered the mussels?
Mussels are filter feeders that readily ingest microplastic particles suspended in seawater. Once inside the organism, chemical additives such as phthalates and BPA leach from the plastic into the surrounding tissues. This pathway—ingestion followed by chemical leaching—is a primary mechanism by which microplastic-associated pollutants enter marine food webs.
Real-World Applications
Example: Oceanographers discover that deep water in a remote Pacific basin has a radiocarbon age of 1,500 years. What can they conclude about this water mass?
Radiocarbon dating of dissolved inorganic carbon in seawater tells scientists how long the water has been out of contact with the atmosphere. A radiocarbon age of 1,500 years indicates relatively slow deep ocean circulation in that basin, meaning the water sank at the surface roughly 1,500 years prior.
Example: An iron fertilization experiment adds iron sulfate to a 100 km² patch of the Southern Ocean. Satellite imagery shows a large phytoplankton bloom two weeks later, but sediment traps at depth capture very little sinking carbon. What does this suggest about the experiment's effectiveness for carbon sequestration?
While iron fertilization successfully stimulated phytoplankton growth, effective carbon sequestration requires that the fixed carbon sinks to the deep ocean where it is isolated from the atmosphere. If zooplankton graze the phytoplankton and bacteria remineralize the organic matter near the surface, the carbon is rapidly recycled and re-released as CO₂.

--- Lesson 4.6: AP Prep: Chemical Equilibria in Oceans (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Chemical Equilibria in Oceans
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the first product formed when carbon dioxide (CO₂) dissolves in seawater?
When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it first reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). This weak acid then dissociates in two steps: first into bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and a hydrogen ion, and then bicarbonate can further dissociate into carbonate (CO₃²⁻) and another hydrogen ion.
What is the primary process by which dissolved ions from chemical weathering of continental rocks enter the ocean?
Chemical weathering of rocks on land dissolves minerals and releases ions such as calcium, magnesium, sodium, and bicarbonate into groundwater and surface runoff. Rivers then carry these dissolved ions to the coast, delivering them to the ocean as the primary input of many dissolved elements over geological time.
What is chemical equilibrium?
Chemical equilibrium is a dynamic state in which the forward and reverse reactions proceed at equal rates, resulting in no net change in the concentrations of reactants and products. Reactions continue to occur in both directions, but the macroscopic concentrations remain constant over time.
What is Le Chatelier's principle?
Le Chatelier's principle predicts that when an external stress (such as a change in concentration, temperature, or pressure) is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system will shift in a direction that partially counteracts the stress. This principle is fundamental to understanding how ocean chemistry responds to changes like increased CO₂.
What is the saturation state (Ω) of a mineral in seawater?
The saturation state (Ω) compares the actual concentrations of the ions that make up a mineral (the ion activity product) to the concentration at which the mineral would be in equilibrium (the solubility product). When Ω > 1, the water is supersaturated and mineral formation is favored; when Ω < 1, the water is undersaturated and dissolution occurs.
What is a buffer system in chemistry?
A buffer system consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid) that work together to resist changes in pH. The ocean's carbonate buffering system (CO₂/HCO₃⁻/CO₃²⁻) is the primary buffer that stabilizes seawater pH, though it has finite capacity.
What is a redox reaction?
A redox (reduction-oxidation) reaction involves the transfer of electrons between chemical species. The species that loses electrons is oxidized, and the species that gains electrons is reduced. In marine sediments, redox reactions drive critical biogeochemical cycles involving oxygen, sulfur, nitrogen, iron, and manganese.
What is the carbonate compensation depth (CCD)?
The carbonate compensation depth (CCD) is the depth in the ocean below which the rate of calcium carbonate dissolution exceeds the rate of supply, so CaCO₃ does not accumulate in sediments. Below the CCD, increased pressure and decreased temperature make seawater undersaturated with respect to CaCO₃.
What is denitrification in the marine nitrogen cycle?
Denitrification is the microbial process in which bacteria convert nitrate (NO₃⁻) to nitrogen gas (N₂) under anoxic or low-oxygen conditions. This process removes bioavailable nitrogen from the ocean, acting as a major nitrogen sink and counterbalancing nitrogen fixation inputs.
A marine chemist measures the aragonite saturation state (Ωarag) of surface water at a coral reef and finds it has dropped from 3.5 to 2.0 over the past 50 years. Based on your understanding of carbonate equilibria, what is the most likely consequence for the coral reef?
As the aragonite saturation state decreases toward 1.0, it becomes thermodynamically less favorable for organisms to precipitate aragonite. Corals must expend more metabolic energy to calcify in undersaturated or low-saturation conditions, leading to slower growth rates, weaker skeletons, and increased vulnerability to erosion.
A geologist analyzes ocean sediment cores from the abyssal plain and finds that layers from glacial periods contain almost no calcium carbonate, while interglacial layers are carbonate-rich. Using knowledge of the carbonate compensation depth and equilibria, what is the most likely explanation?
During glacial periods, changes in ocean circulation, temperature, and CO₂ levels altered the carbonate saturation state of deep water. If the CCD rose above the depth of the abyssal plain site, carbonate particles that sank from the surface dissolved before being preserved in sediments, creating carbonate-poor glacial layers.
Real-World Applications
Example: The average pH of surface ocean water has decreased from approximately 8.2 to 8.1 since the Industrial Revolution. By what factor has the hydrogen ion concentration increased?
The pH scale is logarithmic, so a decrease of 0.1 pH units corresponds to an increase in H⁺ concentration by a factor of 10^0.1 ≈ 1.26, or about 26%. This seemingly small pH change represents a significant shift in ocean chemistry that affects carbonate equilibria and marine organisms.

    [ ] Water Body B will have a lower pH because freshwater is always more acidic
    Explanation: Higher salinity means higher ionic strength, which affects the activity coefficients of dissolved ions. In thermodynamics, it is ion activities (not concentrations) that determine equilibrium positions. Therefore, the effective equilibrium constants for the carbonate system differ between the two water bodies, leading to different pH values and ion speciation even at the same CO₂ levels.

  Q28. A student is studying the nitrogen cycle in a coastal upwelling zone. She finds high nitrate (NO₃⁻) concentrations in the upwelled deep water but very low nitrate at the surface. Applying her knowledge of nutrient cycling, what best explains the low surface nitrate?
    [*] Phytoplankton rapidly assimilate the upwelled nitrate for growth, depleting it in the surface layer
    [ ] Nitrate evaporates from warm surface water
    [ ] Sunlight breaks down nitrate molecules at the surface
    [ ] Surface currents carry the nitrate to the open ocean before it can be used
    Explanation: In upwelling zones, nutrient-rich deep water rises to the sunlit surface layer. Phytoplankton, which require nitrogen for growth, rapidly take up the available nitrate through biological assimilation. This biological uptake is so efficient in productive upwelling zones that surface nitrate concentrations are quickly drawn down to near zero.

  Q29. A geologist analyzes ocean sediment cores from the abyssal plain and finds that layers from glacial periods contain almost no calcium carbonate, while interglacial layers are carbonate-rich. Using knowledge of the carbonate compensation depth and equilibria, what is the most likely explanation?
    [ ] Volcanic eruptions during glacial periods melted all the carbonate
    [*] During glacial periods, changes in ocean chemistry caused the CCD to rise, dissolving carbonate that sank to abyssal depths
    [ ] No shells were produced during glacial periods because all organisms went extinct
    [ ] Ice sheets physically scraped carbonate from the seafloor during glacial periods
    Explanation: During glacial periods, changes in ocean circulation, temperature, and CO₂ levels altered the carbonate saturation state of deep water. If the CCD rose above the depth of the abyssal plain site, carbonate particles that sank from the surface dissolved before being preserved in sediments, creating carbonate-poor glacial layers.

  Q30. A river heavily influenced by limestone (CaCO₃) bedrock delivers large amounts of dissolved calcium and bicarbonate to a semi-enclosed bay. Over geological time, what effect does this chemical weathering input have on the bay's carbonate equilibrium and its capacity to buffer against ocean acidification?
    [ ] It causes the bay's pH to drop below 7 due to excess bicarbonate
    [ ] It decreases the bay's buffering capacity by adding excess calcium
    [ ] It has no effect because rivers and oceans have separate chemical systems
    [*] It increases the bay's alkalinity and carbonate ion concentration, enhancing its capacity to neutralize added CO₂
    Explanation: Chemical weathering of limestone (CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻) delivers calcium and bicarbonate ions to the bay, increasing its total alkalinity. Higher alkalinity means a greater capacity to react with and neutralize added CO₂ (and H⁺ ions), making the bay more resistant to pH decreases from ocean acidification.
