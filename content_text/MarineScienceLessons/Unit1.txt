--- Lesson 1.1: What is Marine Science? (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: What is Marine Science?
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is oceanography?
Oceanography is a major branch of marine science that examines seawater, currents, waves, seafloor features, and marine life.
What is a marine ecosystem?
An ecosystem includes living things and the nonliving environment around them. In marine ecosystems, that includes water, light, temperature, nutrients, and habitat.
What is an estuary?
Estuaries are transition zones between river systems and the ocean. Because nutrients are plentiful there, they are among the most productive habitats on Earth.
What is the intertidal zone?
The intertidal zone is covered with water at high tide and exposed at low tide. Organisms there must survive changing moisture, temperature, and wave conditions.
What is salinity?
Salinity measures how much dissolved salt is present in water. It affects water density, circulation, and which organisms can survive in a habitat.
What is marine conservation?
Marine conservation aims to protect habitats, preserve biodiversity, and manage fisheries and coastal resources so ecosystems remain healthy over time.
After several days of heavy rain, a scientist measures lower salinity in an estuary. What is the best explanation?
Salinity is the amount of dissolved salt in water. When more river water enters an estuary, it lowers salinity by diluting the seawater already there.
Real-World Applications
Example: Animals such as whales, tuna, and sharks are examples of:
Nekton are active swimmers that can move independently of currents. Whales, tuna, squid, and sharks are common examples.
Example: A coastal town wants to manage its fisheries without causing long-term population collapse. Which marine science approach would be most responsible?
Sustainable fisheries management depends on scientific data, including population size, reproduction, and habitat condition. Marine science supports harvesting resources without destroying ecosystems.

    [ ] An alpine glacier only

  Q3. About how much of Earth's surface is covered by the ocean?
    [ ] About 50%
    [ ] About 25%
    [ ] About 10%
    [*] About 70%
    Explanation: The global ocean covers roughly 71% of Earth's surface, which is one reason marine science is so important to understanding the planet.

  Q4. Marine science is considered interdisciplinary because it combines:
    [ ] Only mathematics and art
    [ ] Only astronomy and geology
    [ ] Only biology
    [*] Biology, chemistry, geology, and physics
    Explanation: Marine science draws from several sciences at once. Scientists study marine organisms, water chemistry, seafloor geology, and physical processes like waves and currents.

  Q5. An estuary forms where:
    [*] Freshwater from rivers mixes with ocean water
    [ ] A forest becomes a grassland
    [ ] A volcano erupts under ice
    [ ] Two deserts meet
    Explanation: Estuaries are coastal areas where river freshwater mixes with saltwater from the sea, creating a brackish and highly productive habitat.

  Q6. Which group includes tiny organisms that drift with ocean currents?
    [*] Plankton
    [ ] Predators
    [ ] Benthos
    [ ] Nekton
    Explanation: Plankton drift with water movement rather than swimming strongly against it. They include both plant-like phytoplankton and animal-like zooplankton.

    [ ] Decomposers only
    [*] Nekton
    [ ] Plankton
    [ ] Benthos
    Explanation: Nekton are active swimmers that can move independently of currents. Whales, tuna, squid, and sharks are common examples.

  Q8. Organisms that live on or near the seafloor are called:
    [*] Benthos
    [ ] Nekton
    [ ] Atmospherics
    [ ] Plankton
    Explanation: Benthos live on, in, or near the ocean bottom. Crabs, sea stars, clams, and many worms are benthic organisms.

  Q9. The photic zone is the part of the ocean that:
    [*] Receives enough sunlight for photosynthesis
    [ ] Contains no living things
    [ ] Exists only in freshwater lakes
    [ ] Is completely frozen year-round
    Explanation: The photic zone is the sunlit upper layer of the ocean. Because light reaches it, photosynthetic organisms such as phytoplankton can live there.

  Q10. Why is marine science important to humans?
    [ ] It only helps people who work on ships
    [ ] It only studies extinct species
    [ ] It is unrelated to weather and climate
    [*] It helps us understand climate, food resources, and ocean ecosystems
    Explanation: Marine science matters because oceans influence weather, climate, oxygen production, food webs, transportation, and biodiversity.

  Q11. What is oceanography?
    [ ] The study of only fish anatomy
    [ ] The study of stars and galaxies
    [*] The scientific study of the ocean and its processes
    [ ] The study of mountain building
    Explanation: Oceanography is a major branch of marine science that examines seawater, currents, waves, seafloor features, and marine life.

  Q12. What is a marine ecosystem?
    [ ] A single species living alone
    [ ] Only the chemistry of seawater
    [ ] A type of submarine vehicle
    [*] A community of organisms interacting with each other and with ocean conditions
    Explanation: An ecosystem includes living things and the nonliving environment around them. In marine ecosystems, that includes water, light, temperature, nutrients, and habitat.

    [ ] A floating patch of sea ice

    [*] A coastal area where freshwater and saltwater mix

--- Lesson 1.2: History of Ocean Exploration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: History of Ocean Exploration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a bathyscaphe?
A bathyscaphe is a self-propelled deep-sea submersible consisting of a crew cabin attached to a float filled with gasoline for buoyancy. The Trieste was the most famous bathyscaphe.
What is an ROV?
An ROV (Remotely Operated Vehicle) is an uncrewed, tethered underwater robot controlled by operators at the surface. ROVs carry cameras, lights, and manipulator arms for deep-sea research.
What is a hydrothermal vent?
Hydrothermal vents are openings in the ocean floor where superheated, mineral-rich water escapes from beneath the Earth's crust. They support unique ecosystems powered by chemosynthesis rather than photosynthesis.
What is an AUV?
An AUV (Autonomous Underwater Vehicle) is a robot that travels underwater without real-time human control. It follows pre-programmed missions to collect data such as seafloor maps, water temperature, and salinity.
What is a 'sounding line' in the context of ocean exploration?
A sounding line is one of the oldest methods for measuring ocean depth. A weighted rope or wire was lowered until it hit the seafloor, and the length of line released indicated the depth.
What is a 'bathysphere'?
A bathysphere is a spherical, unpowered deep-sea observation chamber lowered by cable from a surface ship. William Beebe and Otis Barton used one in the 1930s to reach record depths.
A ship sends a sound pulse to the ocean floor and detects the echo 4 seconds later. If sound travels at approximately 1,500 m/s in seawater, what is the approximate depth of the water?
The sound travels down and back, so the one-way travel time is 2 seconds. Depth = speed × one-way time = 1,500 m/s × 2 s = 3,000 meters.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which famous expedition (1872–1876) is widely considered the first major scientific voyage dedicated to oceanography?
The HMS Challenger expedition (1872–1876) traveled nearly 70,000 nautical miles and collected data that laid the foundation for modern oceanography.
Example: A government agency wants to monitor global sea-level rise, track large ocean currents, and measure sea surface temperatures across all of Earth's oceans simultaneously. Which approach is best suited?
Satellite oceanography provides continuous, global coverage that can simultaneously monitor sea-level changes, surface temperatures, and ocean currents—something no ship-based method can match in scale.
Example: A team of oceanographers is planning an expedition modeled after the HMS Challenger voyage but using modern technology. Which combination of tools would BEST replicate and improve upon the Challenger's goals of mapping the seafloor, measuring ocean conditions, and cataloging marine life?
A modern Challenger-style expedition would combine multibeam sonar for detailed seafloor maps, CTD instruments for measuring conductivity/temperature/depth, ROVs for deep-sea biological sampling, and satellite remote sensing for broad ocean monitoring.

--- Lesson 1.3: Tools of Marine Science (Submersibles, Sonar, Satellites) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Tools of Marine Science (Submersibles, Sonar, Satellites)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary advantage of using SCUBA gear for ocean research?
SCUBA allows scientists to directly observe and interact with marine organisms and habitats in shallow water, typically down to about 40 meters.
What is a major limitation of using satellites for ocean research?
While satellites can monitor vast areas of ocean, their sensors can only detect conditions at or near the surface. Deep-ocean data requires submersibles, floats, or other in-situ instruments.
What is an ROV?
ROV stands for Remotely Operated Vehicle. It is an unmanned underwater robot connected to a ship by cables, allowing operators to control it and view live video from the deep ocean.
What is 'echo sounding'?
Echo sounding is a sonar technique that sends a pulse of sound downward from a ship and measures how long it takes for the echo to return. The travel time is used to calculate water depth.
What is a 'submersible' in the context of ocean exploration?
A submersible is a small underwater vessel built to dive beneath the ocean surface. Submersibles may be human-occupied (HOVs), remotely operated (ROVs), or autonomous (AUVs).
What is the Argo float program?
The Argo program deploys thousands of autonomous floats worldwide. These floats cycle between the surface and 2,000 meters depth, collecting temperature and salinity data that is transmitted via satellite.
What is 'multibeam sonar'?
Multibeam sonar sends out many sound beams simultaneously in a fan-shaped pattern beneath a ship. This allows scientists to map a wide strip of the ocean floor in a single pass, creating detailed bathymetric maps.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the primary advantage of using SCUBA gear for ocean research?
SCUBA allows scientists to directly observe and interact with marine organisms and habitats in shallow water, typically down to about 40 meters.

--- Lesson 1.4: Oceanographic Disciplines (Biology, Chemistry, Geology, Physics) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Oceanographic Disciplines (Biology, Chemistry, Geology, Physics)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is biological oceanography?
Biological oceanography is the branch that investigates marine organisms — from microscopic plankton to large marine mammals — and the ecological relationships that connect them.
What is geological oceanography?
Geological oceanography examines the geology of the ocean floor, including seafloor features, sediment types, and tectonic processes that shape ocean basins.
What is physical oceanography?
Physical oceanography investigates the physical characteristics and movements of ocean water, such as wave formation, tidal cycles, current systems, and temperature-density relationships.
What is ocean acidification?
Ocean acidification is a chemical process in which the ocean absorbs excess atmospheric CO2, forming carbonic acid, which lowers the water's pH. It is a key topic in chemical oceanography.
What is a mid-ocean ridge?
A mid-ocean ridge is a long underwater mountain chain where tectonic plates move apart and magma rises to form new oceanic crust. This is a central concept in geological oceanography.
What is a thermocline?
A thermocline is a distinct layer in the ocean where temperature drops sharply with depth. It is an important concept in physical oceanography because it affects water density and circulation.
What is seafloor sediment?
Seafloor sediment consists of particles that accumulate on the ocean bottom, originating from land erosion, volcanic activity, and biological remains. Studying these sediments is a key focus of geological oceanography.

--- Lesson 1.6: Case Studies in Ocean Research (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Ocean Research
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the deepest known point in the ocean, explored by the bathyscaphe Trieste in 1960?
The Challenger Deep, located in the Mariana Trench, reaches a depth of approximately 10,935 meters (about 36,000 feet). In 1960, Jacques Piccard and Don Walsh descended to the bottom aboard the bathyscaphe Trieste, making it the first crewed dive to the deepest point on Earth.
What is a hydrothermal vent?
A hydrothermal vent is an opening on the ocean floor where geothermally heated water, rich in dissolved minerals, erupts into the cold surrounding seawater. These vents often support unique ecosystems of organisms that rely on chemosynthesis rather than photosynthesis.
What is a marine protected area (MPA)?
A marine protected area (MPA) is a defined region of ocean or coast where human activities such as fishing, mining, or development are limited or prohibited to conserve marine habitats and biodiversity. MPAs range from fully no-take reserves to areas with managed-use regulations.
What is chemosynthesis?
Chemosynthesis is a biological process in which organisms produce energy by oxidizing inorganic chemicals such as hydrogen sulfide or methane, rather than relying on sunlight. This process was first observed in ecosystems around hydrothermal vents, where it forms the base of the food chain in total darkness.
What is a bathyscaphe?
A bathyscaphe is a type of deep-diving submersible consisting of a crew cabin attached to a large float filled with gasoline for buoyancy. The Trieste, the most famous bathyscaphe, carried two explorers to the Challenger Deep in 1960, reaching the greatest ocean depth ever recorded at that time.
What is 'bioremediation' in the context of oil spill response?
Bioremediation involves using naturally occurring or introduced microorganisms to degrade petroleum compounds into less harmful substances. After the Deepwater Horizon spill, bioremediation played a significant role as oil-eating bacteria helped break down hydrocarbons in the Gulf of Mexico.
What is 'satellite oceanography'?
Satellite oceanography uses instruments aboard Earth-orbiting satellites to measure sea surface temperature, ocean color (chlorophyll), sea level, wave height, and other properties. This field has revolutionized our ability to monitor global ocean conditions continuously and in near real-time.
A team of marine biologists discovers a thriving community of large tube worms, clams, and bacteria near a volcanic ridge on the deep ocean floor, far from any sunlight. What is the most likely primary energy source for this ecosystem?
The presence of tube worms, clams, and bacterial mats near a volcanic ridge strongly indicates a hydrothermal vent community. In these ecosystems, chemosynthetic bacteria use hydrogen sulfide from vent fluids as an energy source, forming the base of the food web in total darkness.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which expedition is widely considered the first major oceanographic research voyage?
The HMS Challenger expedition (1872–1876) is considered the birth of modern oceanography. The ship traveled nearly 70,000 nautical miles and collected data on ocean temperatures, currents, chemistry, and thousands of new marine species.
Example: The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is primarily composed of what type of pollution?
The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is a massive accumulation of plastic debris and microplastics concentrated by ocean currents in the North Pacific Subtropical Gyre. Studies estimate it covers an area roughly twice the size of Texas and contains trillions of plastic fragments.
Example: Which U.S. federal agency is the primary organization responsible for ocean and atmospheric research in the United States?
The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) is the leading U.S. agency for ocean and atmospheric science. NOAA conducts research, manages fisheries, monitors weather and climate, and maintains a fleet of research vessels and satellites.
Example: A satellite image shows a large area of ocean with unusually high sea surface temperatures persisting for several weeks near a coral reef. Based on ocean research case studies, what outcome would scientists most likely predict?
Prolonged elevated sea surface temperatures are the primary trigger for mass coral bleaching events. Research on the Great Barrier Reef and other reef systems has repeatedly shown that when water temperatures rise even 1–2°C above the seasonal maximum for several weeks, corals expel their zooxanthellae and bleach.
Example: A coral restoration team attaches small coral fragments to underwater frames in a damaged reef area and monitors their growth over two years. This approach is an example of which conservation strategy?
Coral gardening involves growing coral fragments in nurseries or on artificial structures and then transplanting them onto degraded reefs. This active restoration technique has been used successfully in projects around the world, including in the Caribbean and the Great Barrier Reef, to accelerate reef recovery.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Scientific Method in Marine Biology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Scientific Method in Marine Biology
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the first step of the scientific method?
The scientific method begins with making an observation about the natural world. From that observation, a scientist develops a question that leads to further investigation. Without an initial observation, there would be no basis for inquiry.
In an experiment testing how salinity affects sea urchin development, what is the independent variable?
The independent variable is the factor that the researcher deliberately changes or manipulates. In this experiment, the scientist is adjusting salinity levels to observe the effect on sea urchin development. The dependent variable would be the rate of sea urchin development.
What is the purpose of peer review in the scientific process?
Peer review is a critical quality-control step in which independent experts evaluate a study's methodology, analysis, and conclusions before publication. It helps identify errors, biases, and unsupported claims. This process strengthens the reliability and credibility of scientific literature.
What is a hypothesis?
A hypothesis is a testable and falsifiable explanation for a phenomenon, based on prior knowledge and observation. It is not a random guess but an educated prediction that can be investigated through experimentation. Hypotheses guide the design of experiments.
What is a controlled variable (constant)?
Controlled variables, also called constants, are factors that are deliberately kept the same across all groups in an experiment to ensure a fair test. They prevent confounding variables from affecting the results. For example, keeping water temperature constant while testing the effect of salinity on organisms.
What is a scientific theory?
A scientific theory is a broad, well-tested explanation for a natural phenomenon, supported by extensive and repeated evidence. Unlike the everyday use of "theory" (meaning a guess), a scientific theory represents one of the highest levels of scientific understanding. Theories can be refined as new evidence emerges.
What is a transect in marine field research?
A transect is a sampling method commonly used in ecology and marine science where researchers record data along a fixed line or path. Scientists count or measure organisms at regular intervals along the transect to study distribution patterns and community structure in marine habitats like coral reefs or tide pools.
What is a quadrat used for in marine ecology?
A quadrat is a square frame (often 1m x 1m) placed on a substrate to define a specific area for counting and identifying organisms. It is widely used in intertidal zones, coral reefs, and seagrass beds to estimate population density and species diversity within a habitat.
What is the mark-recapture method used for in marine science?
The mark-recapture method involves capturing organisms, marking them in a harmless way, releasing them, and later recapturing a new sample. The ratio of marked to unmarked individuals in the second sample is used to estimate total population size. It is commonly used for fish, sea turtles, and other mobile marine species.
A marine scientist wants to test whether elevated CO2 levels cause decreased shell thickness in oysters. She sets up two tanks: one with normal seawater CO2 levels and one with elevated CO2. Both tanks have the same temperature, salinity, light, and food supply. She measures shell thickness after 8 weeks. What is the control group in this experiment?
The control group is the tank with normal CO2 levels because it is not exposed to the experimental treatment (elevated CO2). It serves as the baseline for comparison. The controlled variables are the factors kept the same across both tanks, such as temperature, salinity, light, and food.
A student is designing an experiment to test whether different light wavelengths affect photosynthesis rates in seagrass. She places seagrass samples under red, blue, green, and white light, keeping all other conditions identical. She measures oxygen production as an indicator of photosynthesis. What is the dependent variable?
The dependent variable is the factor being measured as a response to changes in the independent variable. Here, oxygen production is measured to determine how different light wavelengths (the independent variable) affect photosynthesis. Water temperature and seagrass species are controlled variables.
A marine ecologist uses the mark-recapture method to estimate a population of blue crabs in a bay. She captures and tags 200 crabs, then releases them. One week later, she captures 150 crabs and finds that 30 are tagged. What is the estimated population size?
Using the Lincoln-Petersen formula: N = (number first captured x number second captured) / number recaptured with marks. So N = (200 x 150) / 30 = 1,000. This method assumes that marked individuals mix randomly with the population and that no significant immigration, emigration, births, or deaths occur between sampling events.
A group of students designs an experiment to test whether fertilizer runoff affects algal growth in coastal waters. Group 1 uses natural seawater (control), Group 2 adds low fertilizer concentration, and Group 3 adds high fertilizer concentration. However, the students use different sized containers for each group, place them in different locations with varying light exposure, and use seawater collected on different days. What is the biggest flaw in their experimental design?
The most critical flaw is the failure to control variables. By using different container sizes, light conditions, and water sources across groups, any differences in algal growth could be caused by these confounding factors rather than the fertilizer treatment. A valid experiment requires that all conditions except the independent variable remain constant across groups.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of quantitative data collected during a marine survey?
Quantitative data involves numerical measurements or counts that can be analyzed statistically. Counting 47 fish is a specific numerical measurement. The other options describe qualitative observations, which are descriptive and subjective rather than numerical.
Example: Researchers studying a kelp forest ecosystem observe that sea otter populations have declined, and at the same time sea urchin populations have exploded and kelp coverage is decreasing. A researcher hypothesizes that sea urchin overgrazing is destroying the kelp. Which type of study would best establish a causal relationship?
A controlled experiment with exclusion plots directly tests the cause-and-effect relationship between sea urchin grazing and kelp decline. By removing urchins from some plots and comparing kelp growth to unprotected plots, the researcher can isolate the effect of grazing. Observational studies alone can only show correlation.
