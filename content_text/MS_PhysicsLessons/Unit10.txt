--- Lesson 10.1: Electric Charge & Coulomb's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Charge & Coulomb's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the elementary charge?
Charge is quantized — comes in multiples of e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
What is Coulomb's constant k?
Coulomb's constant k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C².
What is 'electric charge' in physics?
Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field. It comes in positive and negative varieties.
What is a 'conductor' in the context of electricity?
A conductor is a material in which electric charges (usually electrons) can move freely. Metals like copper and aluminum are excellent conductors because they have free electrons.
What is an 'insulator' in electricity?
An insulator is a material that resists the flow of electric charge. Examples include rubber, glass, and plastic. Their electrons are tightly bound and cannot move freely.
What is the 'inverse square law' as it applies to Coulomb's Law?
The inverse square law means the force decreases as the square of the distance increases: F ∝ 1/r². Triple the distance and the force becomes 1/9 of the original.
What is 'electrostatic force'?
Electrostatic force is the force between stationary (static) electric charges. It can be attractive (opposite charges) or repulsive (like charges) and is described by Coulomb's Law.
What is 'charging by induction'?
Charging by induction involves bringing a charged object near a neutral conductor, causing charge separation, and then grounding one side to leave a net charge — all without direct contact.
A student has two charged spheres: one with +3 μC and one with −3 μC. They touch and separate. What is the final charge on each?
When the spheres touch, charge is conserved: +3 μC + (−3 μC) = 0. The charges neutralize each other, leaving both spheres with zero net charge.

--- Lesson 10.2: Electric Field & Potential (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Field & Potential
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an electric field?
An electric field is a region where a charged particle experiences a force. Field strength: E = kQ/r².
What is the formula for electric field?
Electric field E = F/q = kQ/r² for a point charge.
What is electric potential?
Electric potential is the energy per unit charge: V = U/q = kQ/r.
What is the unit of electric potential?
The unit of electric potential is the Volt (V), where 1 V = 1 J/C.
What is the electric field inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium?
In electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field inside a conductor is zero. If there were a field, charges would move until the field is canceled.
What is 'electric potential difference' (voltage)?
Electric potential difference (voltage) is the work done per unit charge when moving a charge between two points: ΔV = W/q.
What is an 'electric field line'?
An electric field line is an imaginary line that shows the direction of the force on a positive test charge. Lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges.
What is an 'equipotential surface'?
An equipotential surface connects all points at the same electric potential. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface, and these surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines.
What is the meaning of 'uniform electric field'?
A uniform electric field has constant magnitude and direction throughout a region. It is represented by parallel, equally spaced field lines. The field between parallel charged plates is approximately uniform.
What is a 'test charge' used for in physics?
A test charge is an idealized small positive charge used to probe an electric field. It must be small enough not to disturb the field it is measuring. E = F/q gives the field from the force on the test charge.
What is 'work done' in moving a charge through a potential difference?
The work done moving charge q through a potential difference ΔV is W = qΔV. This equals the change in electric potential energy of the charge.
What is 'superposition' in the context of electric fields?
The principle of superposition states that the total electric field at a point is the vector sum of the fields from all individual charges. This allows calculating fields from complex charge distributions.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is 'electric potential difference' (voltage)?
Electric potential difference (voltage) is the work done per unit charge when moving a charge between two points: ΔV = W/q.

    [ ] The total charge stored on a capacitor's conducting plate surface
    [ ] The rate at which charge flows through a wire in one second
    [ ] The force experienced by a unit charge at a point in the field
    Explanation: Electric potential difference (voltage) is the work done per unit charge when moving a charge between two points: ΔV = W/q.

    [*] An imaginary line showing the direction a positive charge would move
    [ ] The boundary between a positively charged and negatively charged region
    [ ] A line connecting points that have the same electric potential value

  Q13. What is an 'equipotential surface'?

    [*] A surface where every point has the same electric potential value

    Explanation: An equipotential surface connects all points at the same electric potential. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface, and these surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines.

  Q14. What does 'potential energy' mean in the context of electric fields?

    [ ] The kinetic energy a charged particle gains as it accelerates in a field
    [ ] The thermal energy produced when current flows through a resistance wire
    [ ] The energy stored in the magnetic field around a current-carrying wire
    Explanation: Electric potential energy is the energy stored by a charge due to its position in an electric field. U = qV, where q is the charge and V is the potential at that point.

  Q15. What is the meaning of 'uniform electric field'?
    [ ] A field that exists only in one small region of space around a charge
    [*] A field that has the same strength and direction everywhere in a region
    [ ] A field that changes direction randomly at every point throughout space
    [ ] A field produced by a single point charge located at the origin point
    Explanation: A uniform electric field has constant magnitude and direction throughout a region. It is represented by parallel, equally spaced field lines. The field between parallel charged plates is approximately uniform.

  Q16. What is a 'test charge' used for in physics?
    [ ] A large charge used to create the electric field being measured
    [*] A small positive charge used to measure the field without disturbing it
    [ ] A neutral particle used to detect magnetic fields around current wires
    [ ] An electron used to create sparks in a static electricity experiment
    Explanation: A test charge is an idealized small positive charge used to probe an electric field. It must be small enough not to disturb the field it is measuring. E = F/q gives the field from the force on the test charge.

  Q17. What does 'potential' mean in the phrase 'electric potential'?

    [ ] The actual kinetic energy of charges moving through the field region
    [ ] The magnetic force experienced by a moving charge in the region
    [ ] The resistance to charge flow measured in ohms at that point
    Explanation: Electric potential at a point represents the ability (potential) to do work on a charge placed there. A higher potential means more work can be done per unit charge.

  Q18. What does 'field strength' mean for an electric field?

    [ ] The voltage difference between the two plates of a parallel capacitor
    [ ] The number of field lines drawn in a standard physics diagram per area
    Explanation: Electric field strength is the force per unit positive charge at a point: E = F/q. It is measured in N/C or V/m and indicates how strong the electric influence is at that location.

  Q19. What is 'work done' in moving a charge through a potential difference?

--- Lesson 10.3: Capacitance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Capacitance
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is capacitance?
Capacitance C = Q/V measures a capacitor's ability to store charge per unit voltage.
What is the SI unit of capacitance?
The SI unit of capacitance is the Farad (F). 1 F = 1 C/V.
What is the formula for parallel plate capacitance?
For a parallel plate capacitor, C = ε₀A/d, where A is plate area, d is plate separation, and ε₀ is the permittivity of free space.
What is a 'capacitor' in electronics?
A capacitor consists of two conducting plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric). It stores electrical energy in the electric field between the plates.
What is a 'dielectric' material?
A dielectric is an insulating material placed between capacitor plates. It increases capacitance by reducing the effective electric field, allowing more charge to be stored for a given voltage.
What is 'plate separation' in a parallel plate capacitor?
Plate separation (d) is the distance between the two parallel conducting plates. C = ε₀A/d shows that decreasing plate separation increases capacitance.
What is the 'dielectric constant' of a material?
The dielectric constant (κ) is the factor by which a dielectric material increases capacitance compared to vacuum: C = κε₀A/d. For vacuum, κ = 1; for other materials, κ > 1.
A student has two 6 μF capacitors. They connect them in parallel. What is the total capacitance?
In parallel: C_total = C₁ + C₂ = 6 + 6 = 12 μF.
A student has two 6 μF capacitors. They connect them in series. What is the total capacitance?
In series: 1/C_total = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6, so C_total = 3 μF.
Real-World Applications
Example: What happens to stored energy if voltage across a capacitor is doubled?
Since U = ½CV², doubling V gives U = ½C(2V)² = ½C × 4V² = 4 × ½CV². The energy quadruples.
Example: A student inserts a plastic sheet (dielectric) between the plates of a charged, disconnected capacitor. What happens to the voltage?
For a disconnected capacitor, charge Q is fixed. The dielectric increases C. Since V = Q/C, increasing C decreases V. The voltage drops because the dielectric reduces the electric field between plates.

--- Lesson 10.4: Current, Resistance, & Ohm's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Current, Resistance, & Ohm's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is electric current?
Current is the rate of flow of charge: I = Q/t, measured in amperes.
What is the SI unit of resistance?
The SI unit of resistance is the ohm (Ω). 1 Ω = 1 V/A.
What is the formula for electrical power?
Power P = IV = I²R = V²/R.
What is a non-ohmic device?
Non-ohmic devices have resistance that changes with current or voltage, like diodes and filament bulbs.
What is the SI unit of current?
The SI unit of electric current is the ampere (A). One ampere equals one coulomb of charge flowing per second.
What is the formula for resistance in terms of resistivity?
R = ρL/A, where ρ is resistivity, L is length, and A is cross-sectional area.
What is 'resistance' in an electric circuit?
Resistance is the opposition to current flow. It depends on the material, length, and cross-sectional area of the conductor.
What is 'voltage' or 'potential difference'?
Voltage (potential difference) is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points: V = W/q. It is measured in volts (V).
What is 'electrical power' and what does it measure?
Electrical power is the rate of energy transfer: P = IV = I²R = V²/R. It is measured in watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.
What is 'conventional current' in circuit analysis?
Conventional current is the assumed direction of positive charge flow — from the positive terminal through the circuit to the negative terminal. This is opposite to actual electron flow.
What is a 'watt' (W) as a unit of power?
One watt equals one joule of energy per second: 1 W = 1 J/s. It measures the rate of energy transfer in a circuit.
What is 'Ohm's Law' in simple terms?
Ohm's Law states that the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it, provided the resistance remains constant: V = IR.
What is 'cross-sectional area' of a wire and how does it affect resistance?
Cross-sectional area is the area of the wire's circular end face. R = ρL/A shows that a larger area means lower resistance, as more electrons can flow simultaneously.
A student measures 2 A of current flowing through a 6 Ω resistor. What is the voltage across it?
V = IR = 2 × 6 = 12 V.
A student calculates the power dissipated by a 100 Ω resistor with 0.5 A flowing through it. What is the power?
P = I²R = (0.5)² × 100 = 0.25 × 100 = 25 W.
In a lab, a student connects a 12 V battery to a circuit and measures 3 A of current. What is the total resistance?
R = V/I = 12/3 = 4 Ω.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is 'voltage' or 'potential difference'?
Voltage (potential difference) is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points: V = W/q. It is measured in volts (V).
Example: A student measures 2 A of current flowing through a 6 Ω resistor. What is the voltage across it?
V = IR = 2 × 6 = 12 V.
Example: A student measures the current and voltage across a filament lamp as it heats up. They find the graph is curved, not straight. What does this tell them?
A curved I-V graph means the resistance is not constant — it changes as the filament heats up. This makes the filament lamp a non-ohmic device. As temperature rises, resistance increases.

--- Lesson 10.5: DC Circuits & Kirchhoff's Laws (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: DC Circuits & Kirchhoff's Laws
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the total resistance of two 10 Ω resistors in parallel?
1/R_total = 1/10 + 1/10 = 2/10, so R_total = 5 Ω. Two equal resistors in parallel give half the resistance.
What is a 'series circuit'?
In a series circuit, all components are connected in a single path so the same current flows through each one.
What is a 'parallel circuit'?
In a parallel circuit, components are connected across each other, providing multiple paths for current. Voltage is the same across each branch.
What is a 'junction' in a circuit?
A junction (or node) is a point in a circuit where three or more conductors meet. At a junction, current splits into different branches or combines from multiple branches.
What is a 'closed loop' in Kirchhoff's Voltage Law?
A closed loop is any complete path through a circuit that starts and returns to the same point. KVL states that the sum of all voltage changes around any such loop is zero.
What is a 'voltage divider' circuit?
A voltage divider uses two (or more) resistors in series. The voltage across each resistor is a fraction of the total: V₁ = V_total × R₁/(R₁ + R₂).
What is 'internal resistance' of a battery?
Internal resistance is the resistance within the battery itself. It causes the terminal voltage to be less than the EMF when current flows: V = EMF - Ir.
What is a 'branch' in a circuit?
A branch is any path between two junctions in a circuit. Each branch carries its own current. In parallel circuits, current divides among the branches.
What is a 'current divider' in a parallel circuit?
In a current divider, total current splits among parallel branches inversely proportional to their resistance. More current flows through the lower-resistance branch.
A student connects three 6 Ω resistors in parallel. What is the total resistance?
1/R_total = 1/6 + 1/6 + 1/6 = 3/6 = 1/2, so R_total = 2 Ω.
A student connects a 12 V battery with 1 Ω internal resistance to an external 5 Ω resistor. What is the current?
Total resistance = internal + external = 1 + 5 = 6 Ω. I = EMF/R_total = 12/6 = 2 A.

KVL states that the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop in a circuit equals zero. This is conservation of energy.

--- Lesson 10.6: Magnetic Fields & Forces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Magnetic Fields & Forces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI unit of magnetic field?
The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the Tesla (T).
What is the force on a moving charge in a B field?
F = qvB sin θ, where θ is the angle between v and B.
What is the force on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field?
F = BIL sin θ, where B is field strength, I is current, L is wire length, and θ is the angle between the wire and field.
What is a 'magnetic field'?
A magnetic field is a region of space where moving charges and magnetic materials experience a force. It is produced by moving charges and permanent magnets.
What is the 'right-hand rule' in magnetism?
The right-hand rule determines the direction of the magnetic force: point fingers along v, curl toward B, and the thumb gives the force direction on a positive charge.
What is a 'magnetic field line'?
Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines that show the direction of the magnetic field at every point. A compass needle (north pole) aligns along these lines.
What is a 'solenoid'?
A solenoid is a tightly wound coil of wire. When current flows through it, it produces a nearly uniform magnetic field inside, similar to a bar magnet.
What is an 'electromagnet'?
An electromagnet is a magnet produced by electric current flowing through a coil. Its strength can be controlled by changing the current, and it can be turned on and off.
What is 'magnetic flux density'?
Magnetic flux density (B) is another term for the magnetic field strength, measured in teslas (T). It represents the amount of magnetic flux per unit area.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student moves a positive charge to the right in a magnetic field pointing into the page. Using the right-hand rule, which direction is the force?
Right-hand rule: fingers point right (v), curl into the page (B), thumb points upward. The force on the positive charge is upward.

--- Lesson 10.7: Electromagnetic Induction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electromagnetic Induction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is magnetic flux?
Magnetic flux Φ = BA cos θ, where B is field strength, A is area, and θ is the angle between B and the area normal.
What is the SI unit of magnetic flux?
The SI unit of magnetic flux is the Weber (Wb). 1 Wb = 1 T·m².
What is 'electromagnetic induction'?
Electromagnetic induction is the generation of an EMF (and current in a closed circuit) by a changing magnetic flux, as described by Faraday's Law.
What is 'magnetic flux' in simple terms?
Magnetic flux is the total magnetic field passing through an area: Φ = BA cos θ. It depends on field strength, area size, and the angle between them.
What is an 'induced EMF'?
An induced EMF is a voltage generated by a changing magnetic flux through a conducting loop. It drives current if the loop forms a complete circuit.
What is 'Lenz's Law' in simple terms?
Lenz's Law states that the induced current creates its own magnetic field that opposes the change in flux that produced it. This is a consequence of energy conservation.
What is a 'generator' in physics?
A generator converts mechanical energy (rotation) into electrical energy by rotating a coil in a magnetic field, inducing an EMF according to Faraday's Law.
What is a 'transformer' in electrical engineering?
A transformer uses two coils (primary and secondary) wound on a shared iron core. The changing current in the primary induces an EMF in the secondary, allowing voltage to be stepped up or down.
What is 'mutual induction'?
Mutual induction occurs when a changing current in one coil creates a changing flux that induces an EMF in a nearby second coil. This is the operating principle of transformers.
What is 'self-induction'?
Self-induction occurs when a changing current in a coil produces a changing flux through the same coil, inducing an EMF (back-EMF) that opposes the current change. Inductance L measures this effect.
What is the 'Weber' (Wb) as a unit?
The Weber (Wb) is the SI unit of magnetic flux. 1 Wb = 1 T·m². A change of 1 Wb per second through a loop induces an EMF of 1 volt.
A student uses a step-down transformer with 1000 turns on the primary and 100 turns on the secondary. If the input is 120 V, what is the output?
V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁. V₂ = 120 × (100/1000) = 12 V.
Important Concepts
A student builds a simple AC generator by spinning a coil between two magnets. What determines the frequency of the output
The frequency of the AC output equals the rotation frequency of the coil. One complete rotation produces one full cycle of alternating voltage.

--- Lesson 10.8: Alternating Current (AC) Circuits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Alternating Current (AC) Circuits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is alternating current (AC)?
AC current and voltage vary sinusoidally: V = V₀ sin(ωt), I = I₀ sin(ωt).
What is impedance?
Impedance Z = √(R² + (X_L − X_C)²) is the total opposition to AC current.
What is the relationship between peak voltage and RMS voltage?
V_rms = V₀/√2 ≈ 0.707 × V₀. This is the effective DC equivalent voltage for power calculations.
What is the resonant frequency formula for an RLC circuit?
Resonance occurs when ω = 1/√(LC), giving f = 1/(2π√(LC)). At this frequency, X_L = X_C.
What is 'alternating current' (AC)?
Alternating current periodically reverses direction, typically following a sinusoidal pattern. The voltage and current oscillate between positive and negative values.
What is 'reactance' in an AC circuit?
Reactance is the opposition to AC current from capacitors (X_C = 1/(ωC)) or inductors (X_L = ωL). Unlike resistance, reactance depends on frequency and does not dissipate energy as heat.
What is 'impedance' (Z) in an AC circuit?
Impedance is the total opposition to AC current: Z = √(R² + (X_L − X_C)²). It combines resistance (energy-dissipating) with reactance (energy-storing).
What is 'frequency' in the context of AC?
Frequency (f) is the number of complete cycles per second, measured in hertz (Hz). Standard household AC in the US is 60 Hz; in many other countries it is 50 Hz.
What is the 'period' of an AC signal?
The period (T) is the time for one complete cycle: T = 1/f. For 60 Hz AC, T = 1/60 ≈ 0.0167 seconds.
What is 'phase difference' in an AC circuit?
Phase difference is the angular offset between voltage and current waveforms. In a resistor it is 0°, in a capacitor current leads by 90°, and in an inductor current lags by 90°.
What is 'capacitive reactance'?
Capacitive reactance X_C = 1/(ωC) = 1/(2πfC). It decreases as frequency increases because a capacitor passes high-frequency signals more easily.
What is 'inductive reactance'?
Inductive reactance X_L = ωL = 2πfL. It increases with frequency because the inductor opposes rapid changes in current more strongly.
A student measures the peak voltage of an AC outlet as 170 V. What is the RMS voltage?
V_rms = V₀/√2 = 170/1.414 ≈ 120 V. This is why US outlets are rated at 120 V — that is the RMS value.
A student builds an RLC circuit and measures that at 500 Hz the current is at its maximum. What is the resonant frequency of this circuit?
At resonance, the current is at maximum because impedance is at minimum (Z = R). If current peaks at 500 Hz, that is the resonant frequency.
A student wonders why household electricity uses AC instead of DC. What is a key advantage of AC?
AC can be easily transformed to different voltage levels using transformers. This allows high-voltage transmission (low losses) and step-down to safe household voltages. DC cannot be easily transformed.
Real-World Applications
Example: In a resistor, voltage and current are:
In a resistor, voltage and current are in phase — they reach their peaks at the same time.
Example: What is the relationship between peak voltage and RMS voltage?

V_rms = V₀/√2 = 170/1.414 ≈ 120 V. This is why US outlets are rated at 120 V — that is the RMS value.
Example: A student learns that power lines transmit electricity at very high voltages. Why is this more efficient?
P = IV, so for the same power, higher voltage means lower current. Power loss in wires is P_loss = I²R, so lower current dramatically reduces energy wasted as heat in the transmission lines.

Important Concepts
What is the resonant frequency formula for an RLC circuit

What is 'frequency' in the context of AC
Frequency (f) is the number of complete cycles per second, measured in hertz (Hz). Standard household AC in the US is 60 Hz; in many other countries it is 50 Hz.
A student connects only a capacitor to an AC source and increases the frequency. What happens to the current
X_C = 1/(2πfC). As frequency increases, X_C decreases, so more current flows. Capacitors pass high frequencies easily and block low frequencies.
A student connects only an inductor to an AC source and increases the frequency. What happens to the current
X_L = 2πfL. As frequency increases, X_L increases, reducing current. Inductors block high frequencies and pass low frequencies — opposite behavior to capacitors.
A student builds an RLC circuit and measures that at 500 Hz the current is at its maximum. What is the resonant frequency of this circuit
At resonance, the current is at maximum because impedance is at minimum (Z = R). If current peaks at 500 Hz, that is the resonant frequency.
A student measures the household AC frequency as 60 Hz. How many times does the current reverse direction each second
At 60 Hz, there are 60 complete cycles per second. In each cycle, the current goes positive then negative, reversing direction twice per cycle: 60 × 2 = 120 reversals per second.
A student designs a filter circuit to block high-frequency noise from a signal. Should they use a capacitor or inductor in series with the signal
An inductor's reactance X_L = 2πfL increases with frequency. Placed in series, it blocks high-frequency noise while allowing the desired low-frequency signal to pass through. This is a low-pass filter.

    [ ] Increases current
    [ ] Has no effect

    [ ] Leads voltage by 90°
    [ ] Is in phase with voltage
    [*] Lags voltage by 90°
    [ ] Lags voltage by 180°
    Explanation: In an inductor, current lags voltage by 90°. Remember: 'ELI' — EMF (E) leads current (I) in an Inductor (L).

    [*] V_rms = V₀ / √2
    [ ] V_rms = V₀ / 2
    [ ] V_rms = V₀ × 2
    Explanation: V_rms = V₀/√2 ≈ 0.707 × V₀. This is the effective DC equivalent voltage for power calculations.

    [*] f = 1/(2π√(LC))
    [ ] f = 2π√(LC)
    [ ] f = LC/(2π)
    [ ] f = 2πLC

    Explanation: Alternating current periodically reverses direction, typically following a sinusoidal pattern. The voltage and current oscillate between positive and negative values.

    [ ] The peak voltage divided by the peak current at any given moment
    Explanation: Impedance is the total opposition to AC current: Z = √(R² + (X_L − X_C)²). It combines resistance (energy-dissipating) with reactance (energy-storing).

    [ ] The frequency at which the voltage and current both drop to exactly zero

--- Lesson 10.9: Maxwell's Equations (introductory) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Maxwell's Equations (introductory)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the speed of electromagnetic waves?
Electromagnetic waves travel at c ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum.
What is light, according to Maxwell?
Maxwell showed that light is an electromagnetic wave.
What is an 'electromagnetic wave'?
An electromagnetic wave consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel. It does not need a medium to propagate.
What is 'Gauss's Law' for electricity?
Gauss's Law (Electric): ∮E·dA = Q/ε₀. The total electric flux through any closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by ε₀.
What is 'Faraday's Law' as one of Maxwell's equations?
Faraday's Law (as a Maxwell equation): ∮E·dl = −dΦ_B/dt. A changing magnetic flux induces a circulating electric field, which can drive current in a conductor.
What is a 'magnetic monopole'?
A magnetic monopole would be an isolated north or south magnetic pole. Gauss's Law for magnetism (∮B·dA = 0) tells us they do not exist — every magnet has both a north and south pole.
What is 'electric flux'?
Electric flux is the total electric field passing through a surface: Φ_E = ∮E·dA. Gauss's Law relates total flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge.
What is 'permittivity of free space' (ε₀)?
ε₀ (epsilon-naught) is the permittivity of free space, a fundamental constant that appears in Gauss's Law and determines the strength of electric fields in vacuum. c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀).
Real-World Applications
Example: A student learns that Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction but Maxwell turned it into a mathematical equation. What advantage did the mathematical form provide?
By expressing electromagnetic phenomena mathematically, Maxwell could make precise, testable predictions — most importantly, that changing electric and magnetic fields propagate as waves at the speed of light.
