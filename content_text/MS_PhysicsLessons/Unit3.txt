--- Lesson 3.1: Force & Interaction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Force & Interaction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI unit of force?
SI units: Force = Newton (N = kg·m/s²), Energy/Work = Joule (J = kg·m²/s²), Power = Watt (W = J/s), Momentum = kg·m/s. 1 horsepower ≈ 746 W. The answer is 'Newton (N)'. The other options — 'Joule (J)', 'Watt (W)', and 'Pascal (Pa)' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is a free-body diagram?
Free-Body Diagrams A free-body diagram shows all forces acting on a single object as arrows from its center of mass. The other options — 'A diagram of an object in free fall', 'A graph of force vs. time', and 'A picture of an object with no forces' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the net force?
A free body diagram shows all forces acting on an object. The net force (vector sum) determines acceleration via F_net = ma. If F_net = 0, the object is in equilibrium. The answer is 'The vector sum of all forces acting on an object'. The other options — 'The largest force acting on an object', 'The force of gravity', and 'Any single force on the object' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is a contact force?
Types of Forces Contact forces : Require physical touch — friction, tension, normal force, applied force, air resistance. The other options — 'A force acting at a distance', 'A force from gravity', and 'An electromagnetic force' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is 1 Newton in base units?
Derived units are combinations of base units. Newton (N) = kg·m/s² (force). Joule (J) = kg·m²/s² (energy). Watt (W) = J/s (power). Pascal (Pa) = N/m² (pressure). The answer is '1 kg·m/s²'. The other options — '1 kg·m/s', '1 kg·m²/s²', and '1 kg/m·s²' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the definition of a force in physics?
A force is defined as a push or pull that acts on an object. Forces can cause an object to start moving, stop moving, speed up, slow down, or change direction. Force is measured in Newtons (N).
What is the meaning of the term 'vector' in physics?
A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude (size or amount) and direction. Force, velocity, and acceleration are all vectors. By contrast, scalars such as mass, speed, and temperature have magnitude only.
What is the definition of 'gravity' as a force?
Gravity is a non-contact, attractive force that exists between any two objects with mass. On Earth's surface, gravitational acceleration is approximately 9.8 m/s². The weight of an object equals its mass times gravitational acceleration (W = mg).
What is the definition of 'air resistance'?
Air resistance (also called drag) is a contact force that opposes the motion of an object as it moves through air. It depends on the object's speed, shape, and surface area. Faster objects and larger surface areas experience more air resistance.
In a lab, two students push a cart in opposite directions. Student A pushes with 20 N to the right, and Student B pushes with 15 N to the left. What is the net force on the cart?
When forces act in opposite directions, the net force is the difference between them and points in the direction of the larger force. Here: 20 N (right) − 15 N (left) = 5 N to the right. The cart accelerates to the right.
A student pushes a box across the floor with a steady 30 N force. The box moves at a constant speed. What is the friction force on the box?
Constant speed means zero acceleration, which means the net force is zero. If the student pushes with 30 N forward, friction must be exactly 30 N backward to balance it out. The forces are balanced even though the box is moving.
A student is riding a bicycle and suddenly applies the brakes. The bike slows down. What is the main contact force responsible for slowing the bicycle?
When the student squeezes the brakes, the brake pads press against the wheel rim, creating friction. This friction force opposes the wheel's rotation and slows the bicycle down. Friction is a contact force that requires physical touch between surfaces.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a non-contact force?
Non-contact forces act at a distance without physical touch. Gravity is the classic example — it pulls objects toward each other without requiring contact. Friction, tension, and normal force all require surfaces or objects to be in direct contact.

--- Lesson 3.2: Newton's First Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Newton's First Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Newton's First Law also called?
Newton's First Law (inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. The answer is 'The Law of Inertia'. The other options — 'The Law of Acceleration', 'The Law of Action-Reaction', and 'The Law of Gravity' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is inertia?
Inertia — The resistance of an object to changes in its state of motion. Key Concepts — Newton's First Law Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia) An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction , unless acted upon by a net external force.
What is static equilibrium?
Equilibrium Static equilibrium — Object is at rest and ΣF = 0. Key Concepts — Newton's First Law Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia) An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction , unless acted upon by a net external force.
What is the definition of Newton's First Law of Motion?
Newton's First Law states that an object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force. The other options describe Newton's Second Law, Third Law, and conservation of energy.
What is the meaning of 'net external force'?
The net external force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object from outside sources. If external forces balance out, the net external force is zero and the object's motion does not change.
What is meant by the phrase 'an object in motion stays in motion'?
This phrase from Newton's First Law means that a moving object continues at the same speed and in the same direction unless a net external force changes its motion. On Earth, forces like friction and air resistance usually slow objects down, but the law describes what happens in the absence of net force.
What is the meaning of 'static equilibrium'?
Static equilibrium means an object is at rest and the net force on it is zero (ΣF = 0). The object has no acceleration and no velocity. A book sitting on a table is a common example of static equilibrium.
In a car crash test, a dummy flies forward when the car stops suddenly. A student asks why this happens. What is the best explanation?
The dummy was moving forward along with the car. When the car stopped suddenly, the dummy's inertia caused it to continue moving forward. This is exactly why seatbelts are important — they apply a force to stop the passenger along with the car.

    [ ] An object is vibrating rapidly while staying in one place
    Explanation: Dynamic equilibrium describes a moving object with zero net force. The object moves at constant velocity — it does not speed up, slow down, or change direction. This contrasts with static equilibrium, where the object is at rest with zero net force.

    [ ] The physical state of matter such as solid, liquid, or gas
    [ ] The amount of energy an object has stored inside of it
    Explanation: An object's state of motion describes whether it is at rest or in motion, and if moving, its speed and direction (velocity). Newton's First Law says an object's state of motion does not change unless a net force acts on it.

    [ ] An object naturally speeds up when it encounters any kind of friction
    Explanation: In the context of inertia, resistance to change means an object tends to keep doing what it is already doing. A stationary object resists being set in motion, and a moving object resists being stopped or redirected. Greater mass means greater resistance to change.

  Key Concepts: Newton's Second Law (F = ma)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the weight of a 70 kg person on Earth?
A 70 kg person weighs about 686 N on Earth but only ~114 N on the Moon. The other options — '70 N', '700 kg', and '7 N' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the difference between mass and weight?
Weight vs Mass Mass (kg): Amount of matter; does not change with location. Weight (N): The gravitational force on an object. The other options — 'They are the same thing', 'Mass is measured in Newtons', and 'Weight is measured in kilograms' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
A 5 kg object accelerates at 3 m/s². What is the net force?
A free body diagram shows all forces acting on an object. The net force (vector sum) determines acceleration via F_net = ma. If F_net = 0, the object is in equilibrium. The answer is '15 N'. The other options — '8 N', '1.67 N', and '150 N' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
A 10 N net force acts on a 2 kg object. What is the acceleration?
Using F = ma, we solve for a = F/m = 10 N / 2 kg = 5 m/s². The acceleration is 5 meters per second squared in the direction of the net force.
What is the value of gravitational acceleration near Earth's surface?
Near Earth's surface, the acceleration due to gravity is approximately 9.8 m/s² (often rounded to 10 m/s² for easier calculations). This means a freely falling object speeds up by about 9.8 m/s every second.
What is the definition of 'acceleration' in physics?
Acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity with respect to time (a = Δv/Δt). It is a vector quantity measured in m/s². An object accelerates when it speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.
What is the definition of 'weight' in physics?
Weight is the force of gravity acting on an object. It is calculated as W = mg, where m is the mass and g is the gravitational acceleration. Weight is measured in Newtons and changes depending on the strength of gravity at the location.
What is the definition of 'net force' in Newton's Second Law?
Net force is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on an object. In F = ma, the F refers to this net force. If forces cancel out, the net force is zero and there is no acceleration.
A student pushes a 10 kg cart with a net force of 20 N. What is the cart's acceleration?
Using F = ma, a = F/m = 20 N / 10 kg = 2 m/s². The cart accelerates at 2 meters per second squared in the direction the student pushes.
An astronaut has a mass of 80 kg. On the Moon, where g = 1.6 m/s², what is the astronaut's weight?
Weight = mass × gravitational acceleration. On the Moon: W = 80 kg × 1.6 m/s² = 128 N. The astronaut's mass stays at 80 kg everywhere, but the weight changes because gravity on the Moon is weaker than on Earth.
A student applies a constant 24 N force to push two boxes connected together. Box A has a mass of 3 kg and Box B has a mass of 5 kg. What is the acceleration of the system?
The total mass of the system is 3 kg + 5 kg = 8 kg. Using F = ma, a = F/m = 24 N / 8 kg = 3 m/s². Both boxes accelerate together at 3 m/s² since they are connected.
A student drops a 2 kg ball from a window. Ignoring air resistance, what is the net force on the ball as it falls?
The net force equals weight when air resistance is ignored: F = mg = 2 kg × 9.8 m/s² = 19.6 N downward. The ball is not in equilibrium — gravity is an unbalanced force causing it to accelerate downward at 9.8 m/s².
In a science experiment, a student pulls a 4 kg wagon with a constant force and measures an acceleration of 2.5 m/s². Friction provides a 3 N opposing force. What is the applied pulling force?
Net force = ma = 4 kg × 2.5 m/s² = 10 N. Since friction opposes with 3 N, the applied force must be 10 N + 3 N = 13 N to produce the observed acceleration.
A student weighs 490 N on Earth. What is the student's mass?
Weight = mg, so mass = Weight / g = 490 N / 9.8 m/s² = 50 kg. The student's mass is 50 kilograms. Remember that weight is in Newtons and mass is in kilograms.
Two students push a stalled car. One pushes with 300 N and the other pushes with 250 N in the same direction. If the car has a mass of 1100 kg, what is the car's acceleration?
The total force is 300 N + 250 N = 550 N. Using F = ma, a = 550 N / 1100 kg = 0.5 m/s². The car accelerates at 0.5 meters per second squared in the direction both students push.

--- Lesson 3.4: Newton's Third Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Newton's Third Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
You push a wall. What is the reaction force?
Newton's Third Law: when two objects interact, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other. When you push on the wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal magnitude in the opposite direction.
If the Earth pulls you downward with gravity, what is the reaction force?
The action-reaction pair for gravity: Earth pulls you down, and you pull Earth up with the same force. The normal force from the ground is a separate force — it is not the reaction to your weight. Action-reaction pairs involve the same type of force between two objects.
What is an 'action-reaction pair'?
An action-reaction pair consists of two forces that arise from a single interaction between two objects. They are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects. They always exist simultaneously.
A swimmer pushes against the wall of a pool to start moving. What is the reaction force in this situation?

--- Lesson 3.5: Friction & Tension (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Friction & Tension
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the normal force?
The normal force is the contact force perpendicular to a surface. On a flat surface: N = mg. On an incline: N = mg·cos(θ). It adjusts to prevent objects from passing through surfaces. The answer is 'The perpendicular contact force a surface exerts on an object'.
What is tension?
Tension Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable. In an ideal (massless, inextensible) string, tension is the same throughout. The other options — 'A compressive contact force that pushes an object from behind', 'The gravitational pull acting on an object toward Earth's center', and 'The drag force exerted by air on a moving object's surface' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
If N = 50 N and μₖ = 0.3, what is the kinetic friction force?
Kinetic friction opposes motion: f_k = μ_k·N, where μ_k is the kinetic friction coefficient and N is the normal force. The answer is '15 N'. The other options — '50 N', '150 N', and '0.006 N' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the definition of 'friction' in physics?
Friction is a contact force that opposes the relative motion (or attempted motion) between two surfaces in contact. It can be static (preventing motion) or kinetic (opposing ongoing sliding). Friction depends on the surfaces and the normal force.
What is the definition of 'normal force' on a flat surface?
On a flat horizontal surface, the normal force equals the object's weight (N = mg). The normal force is always perpendicular to the surface and prevents the object from passing through. On inclines, N = mg cos θ.
A student tries to push a heavy desk across the floor but it does not move. The student pushes with 40 N. What is the static friction force?
Since the desk does not move, it is in equilibrium and the net force is zero. Static friction adjusts to exactly match the applied force: 40 N opposing the push. Static friction can range from 0 up to its maximum value (μₛN).
A student slides a 10 kg box across a floor where μₖ = 0.2. What is the kinetic friction force? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
Normal force N = mg = 10 kg × 10 m/s² = 100 N. Kinetic friction fₖ = μₖN = 0.2 × 100 N = 20 N. The kinetic friction force on the sliding box is 20 N, opposing the direction of motion.
A student hangs a 5 kg weight from a rope attached to the ceiling. What is the tension in the rope? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
The weight hangs in equilibrium, so tension equals weight. T = mg = 5 kg × 10 m/s² = 50 N. The rope must pull upward with 50 N to balance the 50 N downward pull of gravity on the weight.
A student pulls a sled across snowy ground at constant speed with a horizontal rope. The tension in the rope is 25 N. What is the friction force on the sled?
Constant speed means zero acceleration, so the net horizontal force is zero. The 25 N tension pulls the sled forward, so friction must be exactly 25 N backward to balance it. The forces are balanced even though the sled is moving.
A student connects two blocks with a string on a frictionless table. Block A (2 kg) is on the table and Block B (3 kg) hangs off the edge. What is the acceleration of the system? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
The driving force is the weight of Block B: F = 3 kg × 10 m/s² = 30 N. The total mass of the system is 2 kg + 3 kg = 5 kg. Using F = ma: a = 30 N / 5 kg = 6 m/s². Both blocks share this acceleration.
A student observes that a hockey puck slides much farther on ice than a rubber ball rolls on a sidewalk when both are given the same initial push. What is the primary reason?
The coefficient of friction between the puck and ice is very low (~0.03), while the coefficient between rubber and concrete is much higher (~0.6-0.8). Lower friction means less opposing force, so the puck decelerates much more slowly and travels farther.

--- Lesson 3.7: Circular Motion & Centripetal Force (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Circular Motion & Centripetal Force
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is centripetal acceleration?
Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of circular motion: a_c = v²/r. The centripetal force F_c = mv²/r is provided by gravity, tension, friction, or other forces. The answer is 'Acceleration directed toward the center of circular motion: a_c = v²/r'.
What is the formula for period of circular motion?
The period T is the time for one complete revolution. The distance around the circle is the circumference 2πr, and the speed is v, so T = distance/speed = 2πr/v.
What is the relationship between period and frequency?
Period (T) and frequency (f) are reciprocals: T = 1/f and f = 1/T. If T = 0.5 s, then f = 1/0.5 = 2 Hz. The answer is 'f = 1/T'.
What is the definition of 'centripetal force'?
Centripetal force is the net inward force that causes an object to follow a circular path. It is directed toward the center of the circle. It is not a new type of force but rather a role played by existing forces (gravity, friction, tension, etc.).
A student calculates the centripetal force on a 0.5 kg ball swinging in a circle of radius 2 m at 4 m/s. What is the centripetal force?
F_c = mv²/r = 0.5 kg × (4 m/s)² / 2 m = 0.5 × 16 / 2 = 4 N. The centripetal force on the ball is 4 Newtons directed toward the center of the circular path.
An object completes 10 revolutions in 5 seconds. What is its frequency and period?
Frequency = revolutions / time = 10 / 5 = 2 Hz. Period = 1/frequency = 1/2 = 0.5 s. The object completes 2 revolutions per second and takes 0.5 seconds for each revolution.
A student swings a 1 kg ball on a 0.5 m string in a horizontal circle. The ball makes one full revolution every 1 second. What is the speed of the ball? (Use π ≈ 3.14)
Speed = circumference / period = 2πr / T = 2 × 3.14 × 0.5 m / 1 s = 3.14 m/s. The ball travels the full circumference of the circle (2πr = 3.14 m) in one second.
Important Concepts
What is the relationship between period and frequency
Period (T) and frequency (f) are reciprocals: T = 1/f and f = 1/T. If T = 0.5 s, then f = 1/0.5 = 2 Hz. The answer is 'f = 1/T'.
What does the term 'frequency' mean in circular motion
Frequency (f) is the number of complete revolutions (cycles) per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). It is the reciprocal of the period: f = 1/T. If an object completes 5 revolutions per second, its frequency is 5 Hz.
An object completes 10 revolutions in 5 seconds. What is its frequency and period
Frequency = revolutions / time = 10 / 5 = 2 Hz. Period = 1/frequency = 1/2 = 0.5 s. The object completes 2 revolutions per second and takes 0.5 seconds for each revolution.

    [ ] Acceleration away from the center
    [*] Acceleration directed toward the center of circular motion: a_c = v²/r
    Explanation: Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of circular motion: a_c = v²/r. The centripetal force F_c = mv²/r is provided by gravity, tension, friction, or other forces. The answer is 'Acceleration directed toward the center of circular motion: a_c = v²/r'.

  Q2. Is centripetal force a new type of force?
    [ ] Yes — it is a fundamental force
    [*] No — it is provided by existing forces
    [ ] Yes — it only appears in circular motion
    [ ] Yes — it is a quantum effect
    Explanation: Centripetal force is not a new type of force. It is the name given to whatever force (gravity, tension, friction, normal force) is directed toward the center of a circular path, keeping an object moving in a circle.

  Q3. What provides centripetal force for a car turning on a road?
    [ ] The engine's internal combustion
    [ ] Air resistance on the car's body
    [*] Friction between tires and road
    [ ] Gravitational pull toward the center
    Explanation: When a car turns on a flat road, static friction between the tires and the road surface provides the centripetal force that keeps the car moving in a curve. Without enough friction (e.g., on ice), the car would slide straight.

  Q4. In uniform circular motion, is speed constant?
    [ ] Yes, and velocity is also constant
    [ ] No, speed varies
    [ ] Speed is zero
    [*] Yes, but velocity changes direction
    Explanation: For uniform circular motion, the object moves at constant speed in a circle. However, velocity is a vector, and since the direction continuously changes as the object goes around the circle, velocity is not constant even though speed is.

  Q5. What is the formula for period of circular motion?
    [*] T = 2πr/v
    [ ] T = 2πv/r
    [ ] T = πr²/v
    [ ] T = v/(2πr)
    Explanation: The period T is the time for one complete revolution. The distance around the circle is the circumference 2πr, and the speed is v, so T = distance/speed = 2πr/v.

  Q6. What is the relationship between period and frequency?
    [ ] f = T
    [ ] f = T²
    [ ] f = 2T
    [*] f = 1/T
    Explanation: Period (T) and frequency (f) are reciprocals: T = 1/f and f = 1/T. If T = 0.5 s, then f = 1/0.5 = 2 Hz. The answer is 'f = 1/T'.

  Q7. Write centripetal force in terms of angular velocity.
    [*] F_c = mω²r
    [ ] F_c = mωr
    [ ] F_c = mω²/r
    [ ] F_c = mω/r²
    Explanation: Since v = ωr, substituting into F_c = mv²/r gives F_c = m(ωr)²/r = mω²r. This form is useful when angular velocity is known instead of linear velocity.

  Q8. In which direction does centripetal acceleration point?
    [ ] Away from the center of the circular path outward
    [*] Toward the center of the circular path inward
    [ ] Tangent to the circular path in the direction of motion
    [ ] Straight downward due to the force of gravity on Earth
    Explanation: Centripetal means 'center-seeking.' Centripetal acceleration always points toward the center of the circular path. This inward acceleration continuously changes the direction of velocity, keeping the object on a curved path.

  Q9. What happens to centripetal force if the speed of the object doubles (radius stays the same)?
    [ ] It doubles
    [*] It quadruples
    [ ] It stays the same
    [ ] It halves
    Explanation: Centripetal force F_c = mv²/r. Since force depends on v², doubling the speed means the force increases by 2² = 4 times. Centripetal force is proportional to the square of the speed.

  Q10. What provides the centripetal force for the Moon orbiting Earth?
    [ ] Tension in the space between them
    [*] Gravitational force between Earth and Moon
    [ ] Magnetic force from Earth's core
    [ ] Friction from solar wind particles
    Explanation: The gravitational force between Earth and the Moon provides the centripetal force that keeps the Moon in its nearly circular orbit. Without gravity, the Moon would travel in a straight line and fly off into space.

  Q11. What is the definition of 'centripetal force'?
    [*] The net force directed toward the center that keeps an object on a circular path
    [ ] A fictional outward force that pushes objects away from the circular center
    [ ] The tangential force that speeds up an object moving along a curved path
    [ ] The gravitational force that acts on all objects near the surface of Earth
    Explanation: Centripetal force is the net inward force that causes an object to follow a circular path. It is directed toward the center of the circle. It is not a new type of force but rather a role played by existing forces (gravity, friction, tension, etc.).

  Q12. What does the term 'uniform circular motion' mean?
    [*] Motion in a circle at constant speed with continuously changing direction
    [ ] Motion in a circle where the object speeds up with each revolution
    [ ] Motion in a straight line at constant speed with no change in direction
    [ ] Motion in a circle that gradually spirals inward to the center point
    Explanation: Uniform circular motion is motion along a circular path at constant speed. Although the speed does not change, the velocity vector is constantly changing direction. This means there is always a centripetal acceleration pointed toward the center.

  Q13. What does the term 'period' mean in circular motion?
    [*] The time it takes to complete one full revolution around the circle
    [ ] The number of complete revolutions made in one second of time
    [ ] The distance traveled during one complete trip around the circle
    [ ] The speed of the object at any point along the circular path
    Explanation: The period (T) is the time required for one complete revolution or cycle. It is measured in seconds. For example, if an object completes one full circle in 4 seconds, its period is T = 4 s.

  Q14. What does the term 'frequency' mean in circular motion?
    [*] The number of complete revolutions per unit of time, measured in Hertz
    [ ] The time required for the object to complete one full revolution
    [ ] The total distance the object travels around the entire circular path
    [ ] The acceleration of the object directed toward the center of the circle
    Explanation: Frequency (f) is the number of complete revolutions (cycles) per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). It is the reciprocal of the period: f = 1/T. If an object completes 5 revolutions per second, its frequency is 5 Hz.

  Q15. What does the word 'centripetal' mean?
    [*] Center-seeking or directed toward the center of a circular path
    [ ] Center-fleeing or directed away from the center of a circular path
    [ ] Along the circumference or tangent to the circular motion path
    [ ] Straight downward toward the ground due to gravitational attraction
    Explanation: The word 'centripetal' comes from Latin and means 'center-seeking.' Centripetal acceleration and centripetal force are always directed toward the center of the circular path. This is the opposite of 'centrifugal,' which means 'center-fleeing.'

  Q16. What does 'tangential velocity' mean in circular motion?
    [*] The velocity directed along the tangent to the circle at any point
    [ ] The velocity directed toward the center of the circular path
    [ ] The velocity directed away from the center of the circular path
    [ ] The velocity that decreases as the object moves around the circle
    Explanation: Tangential velocity is the instantaneous velocity of an object in circular motion. It is always directed along the tangent to the circle at the object's position. If the centripetal force suddenly disappeared, the object would fly off in the direction of its tangential velocity.

  Q17. What does the term 'angular velocity' (ω) mean?
    [*] The rate at which an object rotates, measured in radians per second
    [ ] The linear speed at which an object moves along a straight path
    [ ] The total angle an object has turned through since it started moving
    [ ] The force required to keep an object spinning at a constant rate
    Explanation: Angular velocity (ω) measures how fast an object rotates. It is the rate of change of angle with time, measured in radians per second (rad/s). It relates to linear velocity by v = ωr and to period by ω = 2π/T.

  Q18. What does the term 'radius' refer to in circular motion formulas?
    [*] The distance from the center of the circle to the object's path
    [ ] The total distance around the entire circumference of the circle
    [ ] The diameter of the circle divided by the constant pi (π)
    [ ] The speed of the object as it moves through one complete cycle
    Explanation: The radius (r) in circular motion formulas is the distance from the center of the circular path to the object. It appears in key equations: a_c = v²/r, F_c = mv²/r, and T = 2πr/v. A larger radius means a gentler curve.

  Q19. What does 'Hertz (Hz)' measure?
    [*] The number of cycles or revolutions per second (frequency)
    [ ] The time in seconds for one complete revolution (period)
    [ ] The force in Newtons directed toward the center of a circle
    [ ] The acceleration in meters per second squared of an object
    Explanation: Hertz (Hz) is the SI unit of frequency. One Hertz means one cycle per second. If a wheel spins at 10 Hz, it completes 10 full revolutions every second. Frequency in Hz is the reciprocal of the period in seconds.

  Q20. What does the term 'revolution' mean in circular motion?
    [*] One complete trip around the circular path back to the starting point
    [ ] Half of a complete trip around the circular path to the opposite side
    [ ] The change in speed that occurs as an object moves around a circle
    [ ] The centripetal acceleration at the outermost point of a circular path
    Explanation: A revolution is one complete trip around a circular path (360° or 2π radians). The time for one revolution is called the period. The number of revolutions per second is the frequency.

  Q21. A student swings a ball on a string in a horizontal circle. The string suddenly breaks. In which direction does the ball fly off?
    [ ] Straight outward away from the center of the circle it was following
    [ ] Straight inward toward the center of the circle it was following
    [*] In a straight line tangent to the circle at the point where it broke free
    [ ] In a spiral path that slowly curves outward from the center of the circle
    Explanation: When the string breaks, the centripetal force disappears. By Newton's First Law, the ball continues moving in the direction of its velocity at that instant — tangent to the circle. It does not fly outward radially; it flies off in a straight tangent line.

  Q22. A student on a merry-go-round sits at the outer edge. Another student sits halfway between the center and the edge. Both go around at the same rate. Who experiences more centripetal acceleration?
    [*] The student at the edge because the radius is larger (a_c = ω²r)
    [ ] The student halfway in because less centripetal force is wasted on them
    [ ] Both experience the same centripetal acceleration at all positions
    [ ] Neither experiences centripetal acceleration since they rotate together
    Explanation: Since both spin at the same angular velocity ω, centripetal acceleration a_c = ω²r depends on the radius. The student at the edge has a larger r and therefore greater centripetal acceleration. This is why you feel pulled outward more at the edge.

  Q23. A race car goes around a curve too fast and slides off the road. What happened in terms of centripetal force?
    [ ] The car had too much centripetal force pushing it off the road outward
    [*] Friction could not provide enough centripetal force for the speed and curve radius
    [ ] The normal force became too large and launched the car off the road surface
    [ ] Gravity temporarily stopped acting on the car during the turn on the curve
    Explanation: The required centripetal force is F_c = mv²/r. At higher speeds, more centripetal force is needed. Friction provides the centripetal force on a flat road. If the car goes too fast, the needed force exceeds the maximum friction available, and the car slides outward.

  Q24. A student spins a bucket of water in a vertical circle. At the top of the circle, the water does not fall out. What keeps the water in the bucket?
    [ ] Air pressure above the water pushes it down into the bucket firmly
    [*] The water's inertia keeps it moving in the circular path fast enough
    [ ] Gravity reverses direction at the top of the circle and pushes upward
    [ ] Surface tension of the water creates a seal at the bucket's opening
    Explanation: At the top of the circle, the water wants to continue in a straight line (tangent) due to inertia. If the bucket moves fast enough, the water's inertia keeps it pressed against the bottom of the bucket. Gravity and the bucket's normal force together provide the centripetal force.

  Q25. A student calculates the centripetal force on a 0.5 kg ball swinging in a circle of radius 2 m at 4 m/s. What is the centripetal force?
    [ ] 1 N
    [*] 4 N
    [ ] 8 N
    [ ] 16 N
    Explanation: F_c = mv²/r = 0.5 kg × (4 m/s)² / 2 m = 0.5 × 16 / 2 = 4 N. The centripetal force on the ball is 4 Newtons directed toward the center of the circular path.

  Q26. A student notices that clothes in a washing machine are pushed against the drum wall during the spin cycle. What force keeps the clothes moving in a circle?
    [ ] Gravity pulling the clothes toward the bottom of the spinning drum
    [*] The normal force from the drum wall pushing the clothes inward
    [ ] Water pressure inside the drum pushing clothes outward from center
    [ ] Electric charge between the drum and the clothing fabrics inside
    Explanation: The drum wall provides the normal force that acts as the centripetal force, pushing the clothes inward toward the center. The clothes press against the wall due to inertia, and the wall pushes them inward to keep them on the circular path.

  Q27. An object completes 10 revolutions in 5 seconds. What is its frequency and period?
    [*] Frequency = 2 Hz, Period = 0.5 s
    [ ] Frequency = 0.5 Hz, Period = 2 s
    [ ] Frequency = 50 Hz, Period = 0.02 s
    [ ] Frequency = 5 Hz, Period = 0.2 s
    Explanation: Frequency = revolutions / time = 10 / 5 = 2 Hz. Period = 1/frequency = 1/2 = 0.5 s. The object completes 2 revolutions per second and takes 0.5 seconds for each revolution.

  Q28. A student ties a ball to a string and swings it in a circle overhead. If the student makes the string shorter while keeping the speed the same, what happens to the centripetal force?
    [ ] The centripetal force decreases because the radius is smaller now
    [*] The centripetal force increases because F_c = mv²/r and r decreased
    [ ] The centripetal force stays the same because speed did not change at all
    [ ] The centripetal force becomes zero because shorter strings cannot rotate
    Explanation: F_c = mv²/r. If the radius r decreases while speed v stays the same, the fraction v²/r increases, so the centripetal force increases. A tighter circle at the same speed requires more inward force.

  Q29. A satellite orbits Earth in a circular path. If the satellite were suddenly to lose all its speed, what would happen?
    [ ] It would continue in its circular orbit at a slower pace
    [ ] It would fly off in a straight line tangent to its current orbit
    [*] It would fall straight toward Earth due to gravitational attraction
    [ ] It would float motionless in space at its current orbital altitude
    Explanation: A satellite stays in orbit because its tangential velocity keeps it falling around Earth rather than into it. If the satellite lost all speed, only gravity would act on it, pulling it straight down toward Earth.

  Q30. A student swings a 1 kg ball on a 0.5 m string in a horizontal circle. The ball makes one full revolution every 1 second. What is the speed of the ball? (Use π ≈ 3.14)
    [*] About 3.14 m/s
    [ ] About 6.28 m/s
    [ ] About 1.57 m/s
    [ ] About 0.50 m/s
    Explanation: Speed = circumference / period = 2πr / T = 2 × 3.14 × 0.5 m / 1 s = 3.14 m/s. The ball travels the full circumference of the circle (2πr = 3.14 m) in one second.

--- Lesson 3.8: Non-Inertial Frames & Pseudo-Forces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Non-Inertial Frames & Pseudo-Forces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a pseudo-force?
A pseudo-force (fictitious force) is not a real force caused by an interaction between objects. It is added when analyzing motion from a non-inertial (accelerating) reference frame so that Newton's Second Law (F = ma) still gives correct results within that frame.
What is a non-inertial reference frame?
A non-inertial reference frame is one that is accelerating (either linearly or rotationally). Newton's laws do not hold in their standard form in non-inertial frames unless pseudo-forces are introduced to account for the frame's acceleration.
What is an inertial reference frame?
An inertial reference frame is one that is not accelerating — it is either at rest or moving at constant velocity. Newton's laws work directly in inertial frames without needing to add any pseudo-forces.
What is the formula for the pseudo-force in a linearly accelerating frame?
In a linearly accelerating frame, the pseudo-force on an object of mass m is F_pseudo = -ma_frame. The negative sign indicates it points opposite to the frame's acceleration. If a train accelerates forward, passengers feel a pseudo-force backward.
What is the definition of a 'reference frame' in physics?
A reference frame is a coordinate system (viewpoint) from which an observer measures and describes the position, velocity, and acceleration of objects. Different observers in different reference frames may describe the same event differently.
A student is in a car that turns sharply to the left. The student feels pushed to the right. What is the physics explanation from an inertial frame?
From an inertial (outside) frame, no outward force exists. The student's body tends to continue in a straight line (Newton's First Law / inertia), while the car turns left beneath them. The student feels 'pushed' right, but it is really the car moving left.
A meteorologist explains that hurricanes in the Northern Hemisphere spin counterclockwise. A student asks what causes this rotation. What is the answer?
The Coriolis effect, caused by Earth's rotation, deflects moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere. As air rushes toward a low-pressure center, this rightward deflection causes the air to spiral counterclockwise around the center, forming the hurricane's characteristic rotation pattern.
Astronauts on the International Space Station float around inside. A student asks if gravity has stopped working. What is the correct explanation?
At the ISS altitude (~400 km), gravity is about 90% of its surface value. The astronauts float because the station and everyone inside are in continuous free fall (orbiting). They all accelerate toward Earth at the same rate, so there is no relative force between them — hence apparent weightlessness.

    Explanation: A pseudo-force (fictitious force) is not a real force caused by an interaction between objects. It is added when analyzing motion from a non-inertial (accelerating) reference frame so that Newton's Second Law (F = ma) still gives correct results within that frame.

  Q2. Is centrifugal force a real force?

    [ ] Yes — it acts in all frames
    [*] No — it is a fictitious force in a rotating frame
    [ ] Yes — it is a contact force
    Explanation: Centrifugal force is a pseudo-force that appears only in a rotating reference frame. It seems to push objects outward, but from an inertial frame, what actually happens is that objects tend to travel in straight lines (inertia) while the frame rotates around them.

    [ ] To the left
    [ ] Upward
    [ ] Downward
    Explanation: The Coriolis force is a pseudo-force in Earth's rotating frame. In the Northern Hemisphere, it deflects moving objects to the right. In the Southern Hemisphere, it deflects them to the left. It affects wind patterns, ocean currents, and large-scale weather systems.

  Q4. Do pseudo-forces have action-reaction pairs?
    [ ] Yes, always
    [*] No, never
    [ ] At high speeds
    [ ] In rotating frames
    Explanation: Pseudo-forces do not have action-reaction pairs because they are not real forces caused by interactions between two objects. They are mathematical tools used to apply Newton's laws in non-inertial frames. Real forces always have identifiable sources and obey Newton's Third Law.

  Q5. Why do you feel pushed backward when a car accelerates forward?

    [ ] Air resistance pushes you
    [ ] Gravity increases
    [*] Pseudo-force in the non-inertial frame of the car
    Explanation: In the car's accelerating (non-inertial) frame, you feel pushed backward. This is a pseudo-force (F_pseudo = -ma_frame). From an inertial frame outside, your body simply resists the change in motion due to inertia while the car accelerates forward beneath you.

  Q6. What is a non-inertial reference frame?
    [*] A frame that is accelerating
    [ ] Any frame at rest
    [ ] A frame attached to Earth
    [ ] Any frame moving at constant velocity
    Explanation: A non-inertial reference frame is one that is accelerating (either linearly or rotationally). Newton's laws do not hold in their standard form in non-inertial frames unless pseudo-forces are introduced to account for the frame's acceleration.

  Q7. What must you add to apply Newton's laws in an accelerating frame?
    [*] Pseudo-forces (fictitious forces)
    [ ] More mass
    [ ] Real forces
    [ ] Extra dimensions
    Explanation: To use F = ma in a non-inertial frame, we introduce pseudo-forces (fictitious forces). These compensate for the frame's own acceleration. The pseudo-force is F_pseudo = -ma_frame, opposite to the direction of the frame's acceleration.

  Q8. What is an inertial reference frame?
    [ ] A frame that is accelerating forward or rotating around an axis
    [*] A frame at rest or moving at constant velocity where Newton's laws hold
    [ ] A frame that is only valid inside a laboratory on Earth's surface
    [ ] A frame where all objects are required to be completely motionless
    Explanation: An inertial reference frame is one that is not accelerating — it is either at rest or moving at constant velocity. Newton's laws work directly in inertial frames without needing to add any pseudo-forces.

  Q9. What is the formula for the pseudo-force in a linearly accelerating frame?
    [ ] F_pseudo = ma_frame (same direction as acceleration)
    [*] F_pseudo = -ma_frame (opposite to the frame's acceleration)
    [ ] F_pseudo = mv²/r (toward the center of rotation)
    [ ] F_pseudo = mg (equal to the weight of the object)
    Explanation: In a linearly accelerating frame, the pseudo-force on an object of mass m is F_pseudo = -ma_frame. The negative sign indicates it points opposite to the frame's acceleration. If a train accelerates forward, passengers feel a pseudo-force backward.

  Q10. Does the Coriolis effect apply to small everyday objects like a draining bathtub?

    [ ] Yes — it makes all water spin clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere
    [ ] No — the Coriolis effect only exists in outer space beyond the atmosphere
    Explanation: The Coriolis effect is significant only for large-scale, long-duration motions like weather systems, ocean currents, and wind patterns. For small objects like draining bathtubs, other factors (basin shape, initial water motion) dominate; the Coriolis force is far too small to notice.

  Q11. What is the definition of a 'reference frame' in physics?
    [*] A coordinate system from which an observer measures position and motion
    [ ] A physical structure like a wall or floor that objects rest against
    [ ] A unit of measurement used to describe the speed of moving objects
    [ ] A type of force that acts on objects only when they are in motion
    Explanation: A reference frame is a coordinate system (viewpoint) from which an observer measures and describes the position, velocity, and acceleration of objects. Different observers in different reference frames may describe the same event differently.

  Q12. What does the term 'fictitious force' mean?
    [*] A force that appears in an accelerating frame but has no real physical source
    [ ] A force that is simply too small to be measured by current instruments
    [ ] A force that only exists in science fiction stories and not in real physics
    [ ] A force that acts between two objects that are not in physical contact
    Explanation: A fictitious force (pseudo-force) is an apparent force that appears when observing motion from a non-inertial (accelerating) reference frame. It has no real physical source — no object is causing it. It is a mathematical tool to make Newton's laws work in accelerating frames.

  Q13. What does the term 'centrifugal force' mean?
    [*] An apparent outward force felt by objects in a rotating reference frame
    [ ] The real inward force that keeps objects moving in a circular path
    [ ] The force of gravity pulling an object toward the center of the Earth
    [ ] The friction force between a spinning wheel and the ground beneath
    Explanation: Centrifugal force is a pseudo-force that appears in a rotating reference frame. It seems to push objects outward, away from the center of rotation. From an inertial frame, no outward force exists — the object simply tends to move in a straight line due to inertia.

  Q14. What does the term 'Coriolis force' mean?
    [*] A pseudo-force that deflects moving objects in a rotating reference frame
    [ ] A real contact force between two surfaces that causes friction effects
    [ ] The gravitational force between two planets orbiting around each other
    [ ] The tension force in a string that keeps an object spinning in circles
    Explanation: The Coriolis force is a pseudo-force that affects objects moving within a rotating reference frame (like Earth). It deflects moving objects perpendicular to their velocity. On Earth, it deflects objects to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

  Q15. What does 'non-inertial' mean in the context of reference frames?
    [*] The reference frame is accelerating, so Newton's laws need modification
    [ ] The reference frame has no mass and cannot support any physical objects
    [ ] The reference frame is stationary and all objects within it are at rest
    [ ] The reference frame only applies to objects that have zero net force
    Explanation: Non-inertial means the reference frame is accelerating — either speeding up, slowing down, or rotating. In such frames, objects appear to experience forces that have no real source. These are the pseudo-forces we add to make Newton's laws work within the frame.

  Q16. What does 'center-fleeing' mean regarding centrifugal force?
    [*] The apparent force pushes objects away from the center of rotation
    [ ] The real force pulls objects toward the center of the circular path
    [ ] The force causes objects to stop rotating and move to the center
    [ ] The force is perpendicular to both velocity and the rotation axis
    Explanation: Centrifugal means 'center-fleeing' or 'away from center.' In a rotating frame, objects appear to be pushed outward (away from the center). This contrasts with centripetal ('center-seeking'), which is the real inward force keeping objects on a circular path.

  Q17. What does the term 'rotating reference frame' describe?
    [*] A coordinate system that spins around an axis, like being on a merry-go-round
    [ ] A coordinate system that moves in a straight line at a constant velocity
    [ ] A coordinate system that is completely stationary and does not move at all
    [ ] A coordinate system that only measures the rotation of wheels or gears
    Explanation: A rotating reference frame is a coordinate system that rotates around some axis. Earth itself is a rotating frame (it spins on its axis). A merry-go-round or spinning space station are other examples. In these frames, centrifugal and Coriolis pseudo-forces appear.

  Q18. What does the term 'apparent weight' mean?
    [*] The reading on a scale, which can differ from true weight in accelerating frames
    [ ] The actual gravitational force on an object, which never changes with location
    [ ] The mass of an object measured in kilograms on a standard balance scale
    [ ] The weight of an object calculated using the formula W = mg at sea level
    Explanation: Apparent weight is what a scale reads. In an accelerating elevator, a scale may read more or less than your true weight. In an upward-accelerating elevator, apparent weight increases. In a downward-accelerating elevator, it decreases. In free fall, apparent weight is zero.

  Q19. What does 'weightlessness' mean in physics?
    [*] A condition where apparent weight is zero because everything is in free fall
    [ ] A condition where gravity does not exist at all in the surrounding area
    [ ] A condition where an object has zero mass and therefore no gravitational pull
    [ ] A condition that only occurs at infinite distance from all massive objects
    Explanation: Weightlessness (or apparent weightlessness) occurs when an object and its surroundings are all in free fall together. Astronauts on the ISS experience weightlessness not because gravity is absent, but because they and the station are both falling toward Earth at the same rate.

  Q20. What does the phrase 'frame of reference matters' mean in physics?
    [*] The description of motion depends on the observer's reference frame
    [ ] Only one correct reference frame exists for describing any physical event
    [ ] Reference frames do not affect the measurement of forces on objects
    [ ] All observers always agree on the velocity and acceleration of objects
    Explanation: Different reference frames can give different descriptions of the same event. A passenger in an accelerating car feels pushed backward (non-inertial frame), while an observer on the sidewalk sees the passenger's body resisting the change (inertial frame). Both descriptions are valid within their frames.

  Q21. A student stands in an elevator that accelerates upward. The student stands on a scale that normally reads 500 N. What does the scale read now?

    [ ] Exactly 500 N because the student's weight has not changed at all

    [ ] Zero because the elevator is accelerating and there is no contact force
    Explanation: When the elevator accelerates upward, the scale must push harder to both support the student's weight and accelerate them upward. The apparent weight is W_apparent = m(g + a), which is greater than mg. The student feels heavier.

  Q22. A student is in a car that turns sharply to the left. The student feels pushed to the right. What is the physics explanation from an inertial frame?
    [ ] A real centrifugal force pushes the student outward to the right side
    [*] The student's inertia keeps the body moving straight while the car turns left
    [ ] The car's engine creates a rightward force that acts on all passengers
    [ ] Gravity shifts direction during the turn and pulls the student sideways
    Explanation: From an inertial (outside) frame, no outward force exists. The student's body tends to continue in a straight line (Newton's First Law / inertia), while the car turns left beneath them. The student feels 'pushed' right, but it is really the car moving left.

  Q23. A student drops a ball inside an elevator that is in free fall. From the student's perspective inside the elevator, what does the ball appear to do?
    [ ] Fall to the floor of the elevator at the normal rate of 9.8 m/s²
    [*] Float in place as if there were no gravity acting on it at all
    [ ] Fly upward toward the ceiling of the elevator at a high speed
    [ ] Move sideways toward the nearest wall of the elevator car
    Explanation: In free fall, the elevator, the student, and the ball all accelerate downward at g together. From the student's (non-inertial) frame, the ball appears to float — this is apparent weightlessness. Gravity still acts, but everything falls together, so there is no relative acceleration.

    [*] The centrifugal force pushes the student outward against the wall

    [ ] The normal force from the floor launches the student toward the wall
    Explanation: In the rotating frame of the ride, the student feels the centrifugal pseudo-force pushing outward against the wall. From an inertial frame outside, the wall provides the centripetal (inward) force to keep the student moving in a circle, and the student's inertia makes them press against the wall.

    [*] The elevator is in free fall, accelerating downward at g
    [ ] The elevator is stationary on the ground floor of the building
    [ ] The elevator is accelerating upward faster than gravitational acceleration
    Explanation: A scale reading of 0 N means the apparent weight is zero — this occurs during free fall. The elevator and the student are both accelerating downward at g, so the student does not press against the scale. This is apparent weightlessness.

    [ ] Ocean currents underneath the storm push the air in a counterclockwise direction
    [ ] Temperature differences between the poles and equator spin the storm that way
    [ ] Earth's magnetic field deflects charged air particles into a spiral shape
    Explanation: The Coriolis effect, caused by Earth's rotation, deflects moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere. When the ball moves outward, the Coriolis force deflects it sideways, causing it to curve instead of traveling in a straight line. From an inertial frame, the ball actually moves in a straight line — it is the platform that rotates underneath it.

--- Lesson 3.9: Free-Body Diagrams (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Free-Body Diagrams
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a free-body diagram?
A free-body diagram is a simplified drawing that shows all the forces acting on a single object. The object is usually represented as a dot or simple shape, and arrows represent each force.
What is the normal force in a free-body diagram?
The normal force is the support force exerted by a surface on an object resting on it. It is always perpendicular (at a right angle) to the surface and pushes the object away from the surface.
What is a free-body diagram used for?
Free-body diagrams are used to visually identify and analyze all forces acting on an object. They help physicists and students determine the net force and predict how the object will move.
A dog pulls a sled with a 50 N force to the right, but friction provides 30 N to the left. What is the net horizontal force on the sled?
Net horizontal force = 50 N right - 30 N left = 20 N to the right. The pulling force is greater than friction, so the sled accelerates to the right with a 20 N net force.

    [ ] A picture showing what an object looks like in detail

    [ ] A chart comparing different objects by their masses
    Explanation: A free-body diagram is a simplified drawing that shows all the forces acting on a single object. The object is usually represented as a dot or simple shape, and arrows represent each force.

    [ ] To the left of the object
    [ ] Upward away from Earth
    [ ] To the right of the object
    [*] Downward toward Earth
    Explanation: The force of gravity always points straight downward toward the center of the Earth. In a free-body diagram, the gravity arrow always points down regardless of the object's orientation.

  Q5. What does the length of an arrow in a free-body diagram represent?
    [*] The magnitude (size) of the force
    [ ] The duration the force is applied
    [ ] The mass of the object involved
    [ ] The temperature of the object
    Explanation: The length of an arrow in a free-body diagram is proportional to the magnitude of the force. A longer arrow means a stronger force, and a shorter arrow means a weaker force.

    [ ] The force of gravity on the object
    [ ] The normal force from the floor surface
    [*] The friction force from the floor

    Explanation: Friction is the force that opposes the motion of an object sliding across a surface. In the free-body diagram, friction points in the opposite direction to the motion or applied force.

    [ ] The force pulling the object toward Earth
    [*] The support force perpendicular to a surface
    [ ] The force that makes an object move faster
    [ ] The friction that slows the object down
    Explanation: The normal force is the support force exerted by a surface on an object resting on it. It is always perpendicular (at a right angle) to the surface and pushes the object away from the surface.

    [ ] Yes, but only the largest forces it exerts on others
    [*] No, only forces acting ON the object are included
    [ ] No, free-body diagrams never include any forces
    Explanation: A free-body diagram only shows forces acting ON the object, not forces the object exerts on other things. This is important because we are analyzing the motion of that single object.

  Q9. How is the object typically represented in a free-body diagram?
    [ ] As a detailed realistic drawing of the object
    [*] As a dot or simple box shape in the center
    [ ] As a photograph of the object being studied
    [ ] The object is not shown in the diagram at all
    Explanation: In a free-body diagram, the object is simplified to a dot or simple shape like a box. The focus is on the forces, not on what the object looks like, so details are not needed.

  Q10. If an object is accelerating to the right, what can you conclude about the forces in its free-body diagram?
    [ ] All forces point to the right only
    [*] The net force points to the right
    [ ] All forces are perfectly balanced
    [ ] There are no forces on the object
    Explanation: If an object accelerates to the right, the net force must point to the right (Newton's second law). There can be forces in other directions too, but the total of all forces combined points right.

  Q11. What is a free-body diagram used for?
    [*] To visually identify and analyze all forces on an object
    [ ] To measure the exact temperature of an object precisely
    [ ] To determine the chemical composition of an object
    [ ] To calculate the volume and density of any object
    Explanation: Free-body diagrams are used to visually identify and analyze all forces acting on an object. They help physicists and students determine the net force and predict how the object will move.

  Q12. What does 'normal force' mean?
    [ ] The gravitational pull toward the center of Earth
    [*] The force a surface exerts perpendicular to itself
    [ ] The sideways friction between two surfaces
    [ ] The force applied by a person pushing an object
    Explanation: The normal force is the perpendicular contact force exerted by a surface on an object. 'Normal' in physics means perpendicular (at 90 degrees). A table pushes up on a book sitting on it with a normal force.

  Q13. What does 'applied force' mean in a free-body diagram?
    [*] A push or pull exerted on the object by something
    [ ] The force of gravity pulling the object downward
    [ ] The resistance from friction on the ground
    [ ] The upward push from the surface below it
    Explanation: An applied force is a push or pull directly exerted on an object by a person, machine, or another object. It is shown as an arrow in the direction the push or pull acts.

    [*] The pulling force transmitted through a rope or string
    [ ] The push force exerted by a compressed spring coil
    [ ] The force of gravity acting on an object near Earth
    [ ] The friction force between two surfaces in contact
    Explanation: Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or chain when it is pulled tight. In a free-body diagram, tension points along the rope away from the object.

  Q15. What does 'net force' mean?
    [ ] Only the largest single force on the object
    [ ] Only the smallest single force on the object
    [*] The combined effect of all forces on the object
    [ ] The force of gravity by itself on the object
    Explanation: The net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object. It determines the object's acceleration. If the net force is zero, the object stays at rest or moves at constant velocity.

--- Lesson 3.10: Net Force Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Force Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is net force?
Net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object. Forces in the same direction add together, while forces in opposite directions subtract. The net force determines the object's acceleration.
What is the net force when a 10 N force pushes right and a 10 N force pushes left on the same object?
Equal forces in opposite directions cancel out: 10 N - 10 N = 0 N. The net force is zero, which means the forces are balanced and the object will not accelerate.
A 20 N force pushes an object right and a 5 N friction force acts left. What is the net force?
Net force = 20 N right - 5 N left = 15 N to the right. The push is larger than friction, so the object accelerates to the right with a net force of 15 N.
Three people push a stalled car: 100 N, 150 N, and 200 N all in the same direction. What is the net force?
Forces in the same direction simply add together: 100 + 150 + 200 = 450 N. All three pushes combine to create a net force of 450 N in the direction they are all pushing.
Two students push a desk: one pushes 40 N to the right and the other pushes 25 N to the right. What is the net force?
Both forces push in the same direction (right), so they add: 40 N + 25 N = 65 N to the right. The desk accelerates to the right with a combined net force of 65 N.
A 10 kg object has a net force of 50 N acting on it. What is its acceleration?
Using F = ma, we solve for a: a = F/m = 50 N / 10 kg = 5 m/s squared. The object accelerates at 5 m/s squared in the direction of the net force.
A 5 kg ball is kicked with a net force of 25 N. What is the ball's acceleration?
Using F = ma: a = F/m = 25 N / 5 kg = 5 m/s squared. The ball accelerates at 5 m/s squared in the direction of the kick.
Two teams play tug of war. Team A pulls with 500 N to the left, Team B pulls with 450 N to the right. What is the net force?
Net force = 500 N left - 450 N right = 50 N to the left. Team A pulls harder, so the rope accelerates toward Team A with a net force of 50 N.
An object has four forces: 10 N up, 10 N down, 8 N right, and 3 N left. What is the net force?
Vertical: 10 N up - 10 N down = 0 N (balanced). Horizontal: 8 N right - 3 N left = 5 N right. The net force is 5 N to the right. The object accelerates to the right.
A parachutist weighing 800 N opens a parachute and air resistance increases to 1200 N. What is the net force and what happens?
Net force = 1200 N up (air resistance) - 800 N down (gravity) = 400 N upward. The upward net force decelerates the falling parachutist, causing them to slow down until reaching a new, lower terminal velocity.

--- Lesson 3.11: Static vs. Kinetic Friction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Static vs. Kinetic Friction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is static friction?
Static friction is the friction that prevents a stationary object from starting to move. It acts between surfaces that are not sliding relative to each other and can vary in strength up to a maximum value.
What is kinetic friction?
Kinetic friction is the friction acting on an object that is already sliding across a surface. It has a constant value for a given pair of surfaces and always opposes the direction of sliding.
What is meant by 'rolling friction'?
Rolling friction occurs when a round object rolls over a surface, like a wheel on a road. It is generally much less than sliding (kinetic) friction, which is why wheels make it easier to move heavy loads.

--- Lesson 3.12: Air Resistance & Drag (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Air Resistance & Drag
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is air resistance?
Air resistance is a friction-like force that air exerts on objects moving through it. It always opposes the direction of motion and increases as the object moves faster.
What is another common name for air resistance?
Air resistance is commonly called drag or drag force. The term 'drag' is used because the force drags back on (opposes) the motion of objects moving through air or any fluid.
What is drag force?
Drag force is the resistance force that acts on an object moving through a fluid such as air or water. It always opposes the direction of motion and depends on speed, shape, area, and fluid density.
What is meant by 'aerodynamic'?
Aerodynamic means designed to move through air efficiently with minimal resistance. Aerodynamic shapes are smooth and tapered, reducing drag. Cars, planes, and even cyclists' helmets are made aerodynamic.

    [ ] Air resistance stays the same at all speeds
    [*] Air resistance increases as speed increases
    [ ] Air resistance decreases as speed increases

    [ ] Tension force
    [ ] Normal force

--- Lesson 3.13: Gravitational Force & Weight (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gravitational Force & Weight
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is gravitational force?
Gravitational force is the attractive force between any two objects that have mass. Every object with mass attracts every other object with mass. The strength depends on the masses and the distance between them.
What is the relationship between weight and mass?
Weight is the force of gravity on an object, calculated as W = mg, where m is mass and g is gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s squared on Earth). Mass is how much matter an object has; weight is the gravitational force on it.
What is the value of gravitational acceleration on Earth's surface?
On Earth's surface, the acceleration due to gravity is approximately 9.8 m/s squared. This means a falling object (ignoring air resistance) gains 9.8 m/s of speed every second it falls.
What is the weight of a 10 kg object on Earth (g = 9.8 m/s squared)?
Weight = mass x gravitational acceleration = 10 kg x 9.8 m/s squared = 98 N. The 10 kg object weighs 98 newtons on Earth's surface.
What is weight?
Weight is the gravitational force acting on an object. It equals mass times gravitational acceleration (W = mg). Unlike mass, weight changes depending on the strength of gravity at the location.
What is mass?
Mass is the amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms. Unlike weight, mass does not change with location. A 70 kg person has 70 kg of mass whether on Earth, the Moon, or in space.
What is meant by 'weightlessness'?
Weightlessness is the sensation of having no apparent weight, experienced during free fall. Astronauts in orbit feel weightless because they are in continuous free fall around Earth, not because gravity is absent.
An astronaut has a mass of 80 kg. What is the astronaut's weight on Earth (g = 10 m/s squared for simplicity)?
Weight = mass x g = 80 kg x 10 m/s squared = 800 N. The astronaut weighs 800 newtons on Earth.
The same 80 kg astronaut goes to the Moon where g = 1.6 m/s squared. What is their weight on the Moon?
Weight = mass x g = 80 kg x 1.6 m/s squared = 128 N. The astronaut weighs only 128 N on the Moon versus 800 N on Earth, but their mass remains 80 kg in both places.
A student measures their weight as 600 N on Earth. What is their approximate mass?
Using W = mg: m = W/g = 600 N / 9.8 m/s squared is approximately 61 kg. The student has a mass of about 61 kilograms.
A bathroom scale reads 0 N while you stand on it in a falling elevator. What is happening?
In a freely falling elevator, you and the scale fall at the same rate. There is no contact force between you and the scale, so it reads zero. You still have weight, but you experience apparent weightlessness.

--- Lesson 3.14: Springs & Hooke's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Springs & Hooke's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the elastic limit of a spring?
The elastic limit is the maximum extension or compression a spring can undergo while still returning to its original shape. Beyond this point, the spring is permanently deformed and Hooke's Law no longer applies.
What is Hooke's Law?
Hooke's Law states that the force needed to extend or compress a spring is directly proportional to the distance of extension or compression. The formula F = kx expresses this relationship mathematically.
What is meant by 'equilibrium position' for a spring?
The equilibrium position is where the spring is at its natural length and exerts no force. When displaced from equilibrium, the restoring force pulls or pushes the spring back toward this position.
A bungee cord stretches 4 m when a 600 N person hangs from it at rest. What is the spring constant?
At rest, the spring force balances the weight: F = kx, so k = F/x = 600 N / 4 m = 150 N/m. The bungee cord has a spring constant of 150 newtons per meter.
A spring scale reads 30 N when a fish is hung from it, stretching the spring 0.15 m. What is the spring constant?
k = F/x = 30 N / 0.15 m = 200 N/m. The spring scale has a spring constant of 200 N/m. Spring scales work by using Hooke's Law to measure weight from extension.
A car's suspension spring is compressed 0.05 m when a 75 kg person sits in the car. What is the approximate spring constant (g = 10 m/s squared)?
Weight = mg = 75 x 10 = 750 N. Using k = F/x = 750 N / 0.05 m = 15000 N/m. Car suspension springs are very stiff (high k) to support heavy loads without compressing too much.

--- Lesson 3.15: Equilibrium of Forces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Equilibrium of Forces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the acceleration of an object in equilibrium?
In equilibrium, the net force is zero. By Newton's second law (F = ma), if F = 0, then a = 0. The object has zero acceleration, meaning its velocity does not change.
What is the difference between static and dynamic equilibrium?
Static equilibrium is when an object is at rest with balanced forces. Dynamic equilibrium is when an object moves at constant velocity with balanced forces. In both cases, net force and acceleration are zero.
What is static equilibrium?
Static equilibrium means an object is at rest and all forces acting on it are balanced (net force = 0). The object has zero velocity and zero acceleration. A book on a table is in static equilibrium.
What is dynamic equilibrium?
Dynamic equilibrium means an object moves at constant velocity with all forces balanced (net force = 0). The object is moving but not accelerating. A car cruising at steady speed is in dynamic equilibrium.
What is meant by 'Newton's first law' in relation to equilibrium?
Newton's first law states that objects maintain their state of motion unless a net external force acts on them. In equilibrium, net force is zero, so objects remain at rest or continue at constant velocity.
A bridge supports a stationary truck. The bridge pushes up with 50,000 N. What is the truck's weight?
The truck is in static equilibrium, so the upward force from the bridge must equal the truck's weight downward. If the bridge pushes up with 50,000 N, the truck weighs 50,000 N.

--- Lesson 3.16: Torque & Rotational Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Torque & Rotational Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is torque?
Torque is a twisting force that causes or tends to cause rotation around a pivot point (fulcrum). It depends on both the force applied and the distance from the pivot.
What is the formula for torque?
Torque equals the force multiplied by the lever arm (perpendicular distance from the force to the pivot point). Torque is measured in newton-meters (N-m).
What is the pivot point also called?
The pivot point is also called the fulcrum. It is the fixed point around which an object rotates. A seesaw rotates around its fulcrum in the middle.
What is rotational motion?
Rotational motion is when an object spins or rotates around a fixed axis or pivot point. Examples include a spinning top, a turning wheel, and Earth rotating on its axis.
On a balanced seesaw, what is true about the torques on each side?
For a seesaw to balance, the clockwise torque must equal the counterclockwise torque. This means force x distance on one side equals force x distance on the other side.
A force of 20 N is applied 0.5 m from a pivot. What is the torque?
Torque = force x distance = 20 N x 0.5 m = 10 N-m. The torque is 10 newton-meters about the pivot point.
What is meant by 'axis of rotation'?
The axis of rotation is the imaginary line around which an object spins. For a wheel, it is the axle. For Earth, it is the line from North Pole to South Pole.

    [*] The measure of a force's ability to cause rotation
    [ ] The speed at which an object moves along a path
    [ ] The total mass of a rotating object on an axis

    Explanation: Torque is the measure of a force's ability to cause rotation around a pivot. It depends on the force magnitude and the perpendicular distance from the pivot (lever arm).

--- Lesson 3.17: Simple Machines (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Simple Machines
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the main purpose of a simple machine?
Simple machines make work easier by multiplying force, changing the direction of force, or increasing the distance over which force is applied. They do not create energy or reduce the total work needed.
What is a simple machine?
A simple machine is a basic mechanical device that changes the magnitude or direction of force. It has few or no moving parts and makes work easier without creating new energy.
What is a lever?
A lever is a rigid bar that pivots around a fixed point called the fulcrum. By applying force at one end, you can move a load at the other end. Examples include seesaws, crowbars, and scissors.
What is an inclined plane?
An inclined plane is a flat surface tilted at an angle. It allows you to raise objects to a higher level using less force over a longer distance. Ramps, hills, and slides are inclined planes.
What is a pulley?
A pulley is a wheel with a groove around it for a rope or cable. A single pulley changes the direction of force. Multiple pulleys (block and tackle) can also multiply force.
What is a wedge?
A wedge is a simple machine shaped like two inclined planes joined together. It converts a force applied to its blunt end into forces that push outward, splitting or cutting. Axes and knives are wedges.
What is a wheel and axle?
A wheel and axle consists of a larger wheel attached to a smaller cylinder (axle) that rotate together. Force applied to the wheel is multiplied at the axle. Doorknobs and screwdrivers are examples.
What is a screw?
A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. The spiral threads convert rotational force into linear force. Screws can hold things together (wood screws) or lift things (jar lids, car jacks).
A wheelchair ramp at a building entrance is 10 m long and rises 0.5 m. What is the ideal mechanical advantage?
For an inclined plane, ideal mechanical advantage = length / height = 10 m / 0.5 m = 20. The ramp multiplies force by 20, meaning you need only 1/20 of the weight to push up the ramp.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which simple machine is a ramp an example of?
A ramp is an inclined plane, one of the six simple machines. It allows you to move objects upward using less force over a longer distance, instead of lifting straight up with more force.
Example: A seesaw is an example of which simple machine?
A seesaw is a lever with the fulcrum (pivot point) in the middle. When one side goes down, the other goes up. Levers can multiply force or change its direction.
Example: A knife blade is an example of which simple machine?
A knife blade is a wedge. It has a thin, angled edge that concentrates force into a small area, making it effective at cutting or splitting materials apart.
Example: A steering wheel is an example of which simple machine?
A steering wheel is a wheel and axle. The large wheel allows you to apply force over a greater distance (circumference), which is converted to a larger force at the smaller axle.
Example: What does 'mechanical advantage' mean?
Mechanical advantage is the ratio of output force to input force. It tells you how much the machine multiplies your force. A mechanical advantage of 3 means the machine triples your input force.
Example: A flagpole uses a pulley system. What advantage does this provide?
The flagpole pulley changes the direction of force. Instead of climbing up to push the flag up, you pull the rope down from the ground, and the pulley redirects this force upward to raise the flag.
Example: A wheelchair ramp at a building entrance is 10 m long and rises 0.5 m. What is the ideal mechanical advantage?
For an inclined plane, ideal mechanical advantage = length / height = 10 m / 0.5 m = 20. The ramp multiplies force by 20, meaning you need only 1/20 of the weight to push up the ramp.
Example: A construction worker uses a block and tackle (multiple pulleys) to lift a 1000 N beam. With 4 supporting rope sections, what force is needed?
With 4 supporting rope sections, the mechanical advantage is 4. Force = load / MA = 1000 N / 4 = 250 N. You pull with only 250 N, but you must pull 4 times as much rope.

--- Lesson 3.18: Mechanical Advantage (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mechanical Advantage
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for mechanical advantage?
Mechanical advantage (MA) is calculated as output force divided by input force: MA = F_out / F_in.
What is the trade-off when a machine increases force through mechanical advantage?
When a machine multiplies force, you must apply that force over a longer distance. This is the fundamental trade-off of simple machines.
What is the mechanical advantage of a machine that uses 20 N of input force to lift a 100 N load?
MA = output force / input force = 100 N / 20 N = 5. The machine multiplies the input force by a factor of 5.
What is the ideal mechanical advantage of an inclined plane?
The ideal mechanical advantage of an inclined plane equals the length of the slope divided by the height: IMA = length / height.
What is mechanical advantage?
Mechanical advantage is the ratio of output force to input force. It measures how much a machine multiplies the force you apply.
What is efficiency in the context of simple machines?
Efficiency is the ratio of useful output work to total input work, expressed as a percentage. It tells you how much work is lost to friction and other factors.
What is the meaning of 'output force' in simple machines?
Output force (also called resistance force or load force) is the force the machine exerts on the object it is working on, such as lifting a heavy load.
What is a compound machine?
A compound machine is a device that combines two or more simple machines. Examples include scissors (lever + wedge) and a bicycle (wheel and axle + lever + gears).
Maya uses a 3-meter ramp to push a box up to a platform that is 1 meter high. What is the ideal mechanical advantage of this ramp?
IMA of an inclined plane = length / height = 3 m / 1 m = 3. The ramp multiplies Maya's pushing force by a factor of 3.
A cyclist pedals a bicycle where the pedal gear has 48 teeth and the rear gear has 16 teeth. What is the mechanical advantage of this gear combination?
Gear MA = number of teeth on driven gear / number of teeth on driving gear = 48 / 16 = 3. The gear system provides a mechanical advantage of 3.
Aisha uses a crowbar that is 60 cm long. She places the fulcrum 10 cm from the end under a heavy rock. What is the ideal mechanical advantage?
IMA of a lever = effort arm / load arm = 50 cm / 10 cm = 5. The effort arm is 60 - 10 = 50 cm, giving an IMA of 5.
A construction worker uses a machine to lift a 500 N load, applying only 125 N of force. However, the ideal mechanical advantage is 5. What is the efficiency of this machine?
AMA = 500/125 = 4. Efficiency = (AMA/IMA) x 100 = (4/5) x 100 = 80%. The machine loses 20% of its work to friction.

--- Lesson 3.19: Pressure & Pascal's Principle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Pressure & Pascal's Principle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for calculating pressure?
Pressure equals force divided by area (P = F/A). The greater the force or the smaller the area, the greater the pressure.
What is the SI unit of pressure?
The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa). One pascal equals one newton per square meter (1 Pa = 1 N/m^2).
A 60 N force is applied over an area of 2 square meters. What is the pressure?
P = F/A = 60 N / 2 m^2 = 30 Pa. The pressure exerted is 30 pascals.
What is atmospheric pressure at sea level approximately equal to?
Standard atmospheric pressure at sea level is approximately 101,325 Pa (about 101 kPa), which is also called 1 atmosphere (1 atm).
What is pressure in physics?
Pressure is defined as the force applied per unit area (P = F/A). It measures how concentrated a force is over a given surface.
What is a pascal?
A pascal (Pa) is the SI unit of pressure. One pascal equals one newton of force spread over one square meter of area (1 Pa = 1 N/m^2).
What is a hydraulic system?
A hydraulic system uses an enclosed liquid (usually oil) to transmit and multiply force. It operates based on Pascal's principle.
What is a piston?
A piston is a movable component that fits tightly inside a cylinder. When pushed, it exerts pressure on the fluid inside, and the fluid transmits that pressure.

  Q23. A mechanic uses a hydraulic jack where the small piston has an area of 10 cm^2 and the large piston has an area of 200 cm^2. If the mechanic pushes with 50 N on the small piston, what force lifts the car?
    [ ] 500 N
    [*] 1,000 N
    [ ] 2,000 N
    [ ] 5,000 N
    Explanation: F2 = F1 x (A2/A1) = 50 N x (200/10) = 50 x 20 = 1,000 N. The hydraulic jack multiplies the mechanic's force by 20.

  Q24. A diver swims from a depth of 5 meters to 15 meters in the ocean. How does the water pressure change?

    [ ] The pressure stays the same because the water density is constant

    Explanation: Water pressure increases with depth because the weight of the water column above increases. At 15 m, there is more water pressing down than at 5 m.

  Q25. When you step on a thumbtack, it hurts even though the force is small. Why does the thumbtack cause so much pressure?

    Explanation: Since P = F/A, the extremely small area of the thumbtack's point means even a small force creates an enormous pressure, enough to puncture skin.

  Q26. A car's hydraulic braking system has a master cylinder with a small piston and brake calipers with larger pistons. When the driver presses the brake pedal, what happens according to Pascal's principle?

    Explanation: By Pascal's principle, the pressure from the master cylinder is transmitted equally through the brake fluid to all calipers, applying braking force at every wheel.

  Q27. An aquarium has three fish at different depths: Fish A at 10 cm, Fish B at 30 cm, and Fish C at 60 cm. Which fish experiences the greatest water pressure?

    [ ] Fish B, because it is at the middle depth of the aquarium
    [*] Fish C, because it is at the greatest depth in the aquarium

    Explanation: Water pressure increases with depth. Fish C at 60 cm depth has the most water above it and therefore experiences the greatest water pressure.

  Q28. A farmer fills two water towers to the same height. Tower A has a wide base and Tower B has a narrow base. How does the water pressure at the bottom of each tower compare?

    [*] The pressure at the bottom is the same because the water height is equal
    [ ] Neither tower has significant pressure because water towers are open on top
    Explanation: Hydrostatic pressure depends on depth (height of liquid), fluid density, and gravity -- not on the shape or volume of the container. Same height means same pressure at the bottom.

  Q29. A rescue team uses a hydraulic tool called the 'Jaws of Life' to cut open a wrecked car. The small piston has an area of 2 cm^2, and the large piston has an area of 100 cm^2. If a firefighter pushes with 80 N of force, what force is produced at the cutting jaws?
    [ ] 800 N
    [ ] 2,000 N
    [*] 4,000 N
    [ ] 8,000 N
    Explanation: F2 = F1 x (A2/A1) = 80 N x (100/2) = 80 x 50 = 4,000 N. The hydraulic multiplier of 50 turns 80 N into 4,000 N of cutting force.

    [ ] Because waves at the surface would damage a thick wall at the top area
    Explanation: Water pressure increases with depth. The bottom of the dam faces much greater water pressure than the top, so it needs to be thicker and stronger to withstand the force.

--- Lesson 3.20: Buoyancy & Archimedes' Principle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Buoyancy & Archimedes' Principle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is buoyancy?
Buoyancy is the upward force that a fluid exerts on an object that is submerged or floating in it. This force opposes the weight of the object.
What is the buoyant force?
The buoyant force is the net upward force exerted by a fluid on a submerged or partially submerged object. It results from the difference in pressure between the bottom and top of the object.
What is density?
Density is defined as mass per unit volume (density = mass/volume). It tells you how much matter is packed into a given space.
A block of wood with a volume of 500 cm^3 is placed in water. It floats with 300 cm^3 below the surface. What is the buoyant force if water weighs 1 g/cm^3?
The displaced water volume is 300 cm^3 = 300 g = 0.3 kg. Buoyant force = weight of displaced water = 0.3 kg x 9.8 m/s^2 = 2.94 N.
A student places a 2 kg rock in water and measures its apparent weight as 1.5 kg. What is the buoyant force acting on the rock?
Buoyant force = true weight - apparent weight = (2 kg x 9.8) - (1.5 kg x 9.8) = 19.6 N - 14.7 N = 4.9 N.

--- Lesson 3.20: Buoyancy & Archimedes' Principle (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is buoyancy?
    [*] The upward force exerted by a fluid on a submerged or floating object
    [ ] The downward force that gravity exerts on an object placed in a fluid
    [ ] The sideways force that flowing water exerts on an object in its path
    [ ] The frictional force between an object and the fluid surrounding the object
    Explanation: Buoyancy is the upward force that a fluid exerts on an object that is submerged or floating in it. This force opposes the weight of the object.

  Q2. What does Archimedes' principle state?
    [*] An object in a fluid experiences an upward force equal to the weight of fluid it displaces
    [ ] An object in a fluid will always eventually sink to the bottom of the container used
    [ ] The pressure on a submerged object is the same at every point on its entire surface
    [ ] An object will float only if it is lighter than an equal volume of surrounding fluid
    Explanation: Archimedes' principle states that the buoyant force on an object equals the weight of the fluid displaced by that object.

  Q3. What happens to an object when the buoyant force equals the object's weight?
    [ ] The object sinks quickly to the bottom
    [ ] The object rises to the surface rapidly
    [*] The object floats at a constant level
    [ ] The object dissolves into the surrounding fluid
    Explanation: When the buoyant force equals the weight, the net force is zero, so the object floats. It remains at a constant position, neither rising nor sinking.

  Q4. An object sinks in a fluid when which condition is true?
    [*] The object's density is greater than the fluid's density
    [ ] The object's density is less than the fluid's density
    [ ] The object's density is exactly equal to the fluid density
    [ ] The object's volume is smaller than the volume of the fluid
    Explanation: An object sinks when its density is greater than the fluid's density. Its weight exceeds the maximum buoyant force, causing it to go down.

  Q5. Why do you feel lighter when standing in a swimming pool?
    [ ] Because the water reduces the force of gravity on your body
    [*] Because the buoyant force pushes up on you, opposing your weight
    [ ] Because the water pressure compresses your body to be smaller
    [ ] Because the pool water absorbs some of your body mass directly
    Explanation: The water exerts an upward buoyant force on your body that partially supports your weight. This makes you feel lighter even though your actual weight has not changed.

  Q6. What determines how much buoyant force an object experiences?
    [*] The weight of the fluid displaced by the object
    [ ] The total weight of the object placed in the fluid
    [ ] The color and surface texture of the submerged object
    [ ] The temperature of the fluid that the object is placed in
    Explanation: According to Archimedes' principle, the buoyant force equals the weight of the fluid displaced. The more fluid displaced, the greater the buoyant force.

  Q7. A solid steel ball sinks in water but a steel ship floats. Why?
    [ ] Because the ship is made of a lighter type of steel material than the ball
    [*] Because the ship's hollow shape displaces enough water to support its weight
    [ ] Because the ship moves and moving objects always float on the water surface
    [ ] Because the ship's paint coating makes the steel waterproof and thus lighter
    Explanation: A ship's hollow hull shape displaces a large volume of water, creating a buoyant force equal to the ship's weight. The ball's small volume cannot displace enough water.

  Q8. What happens to the buoyant force on an object as you push it deeper into water?
    [ ] It stays the same at every depth below the surface
    [*] It increases until the object is fully submerged, then stays constant
    [ ] It continues to increase the deeper the object is pushed down
    [ ] It decreases because pressure compresses the object at greater depth
    Explanation: As you push an object deeper, more of it is submerged and displaces more water, so the buoyant force increases. Once fully submerged, the displaced volume stays constant, so the buoyant force remains the same regardless of further depth.

  Q9. Which type of fluid provides a greater buoyant force on the same object: freshwater or saltwater?
    [ ] Freshwater, because it has less dissolved material in it
    [*] Saltwater, because it is denser and the displaced fluid weighs more
    [ ] Both provide exactly the same buoyant force on the same object
    [ ] Neither, because buoyant force depends only on the object's shape
    Explanation: Saltwater is denser than freshwater. Since buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid, the same displaced volume of saltwater weighs more, giving a greater buoyant force.

  Q10. A floating object displaces a volume of water that weighs exactly how much?
    [ ] Twice as much as the object itself weighs
    [ ] Half as much as the object itself weighs
    [*] Exactly as much as the object itself weighs
    [ ] Three times as much as the object itself weighs
    Explanation: A floating object is in equilibrium, so the buoyant force equals its weight. Since buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid, the displaced fluid weighs exactly as much as the object.

  Q11. What is the buoyant force?
    [*] The upward force a fluid exerts on an object that is submerged in the fluid
    [ ] The total gravitational force that pulls an object toward the center of Earth
    [ ] The drag force that a fluid exerts on an object moving through the fluid
    [ ] The force needed to push an object completely below the surface of water
    Explanation: The buoyant force is the net upward force exerted by a fluid on a submerged or partially submerged object. It results from the difference in pressure between the bottom and top of the object.

  Q12. What does 'displace' mean in the context of Archimedes' principle?
    [*] To push fluid out of the way so the object can occupy that space in the fluid
    [ ] To absorb fluid into the material of the object that is placed in the liquid
    [ ] To heat the fluid so that it expands and rises above the surface level amount
    [ ] To mix the object's material with the fluid so that both become one substance
    Explanation: To displace means to push aside or move out of the way. When an object is placed in a fluid, it displaces a volume of fluid equal to the volume of the submerged part of the object.

  Q13. What is density?
    [*] The amount of mass contained in a given volume of a substance or material
    [ ] The total weight of an object when it is measured on a standard scale device
    [ ] The amount of space that an object occupies in three-dimensional measurements
    [ ] The strength of the gravitational force acting on an object near the surface
    Explanation: Density is defined as mass per unit volume (density = mass/volume). It tells you how much matter is packed into a given space.

  Q14. What does 'neutral buoyancy' mean?
    [*] When an object neither floats nor sinks but remains suspended within the fluid
    [ ] When an object sinks slowly to the bottom of the fluid over a period of time
    [ ] When an object floats with most of its volume above the surface of the fluid
    [ ] When the fluid has no effect on the motion of the object placed inside of it
    Explanation: Neutral buoyancy occurs when an object's average density equals the fluid's density. The buoyant force exactly equals the weight, so the object stays suspended at whatever depth it is placed.

  Q15. What does 'apparent weight' mean for an object in a fluid?
    [ ] The actual gravitational weight of the object measured outside of any fluid
    [*] The reduced weight an object seems to have when submerged due to buoyancy
    [ ] The weight of the fluid that is displaced by the volume of the object itself
    [ ] The weight of the container that holds the fluid and the submerged object both
    Explanation: Apparent weight is the weight an object seems to have when submerged. It equals the true weight minus the buoyant force (apparent weight = weight - buoyant force).

  Q16. What does 'volume of fluid displaced' mean?
    [*] The amount of fluid pushed aside by the part of an object submerged in it
    [ ] The total amount of fluid contained in the entire tank or container being used
    [ ] The volume of the object itself before it is placed into the surrounding fluid
    [ ] The amount of fluid that evaporates after an object is placed into the liquid
    Explanation: The volume of fluid displaced is the volume of fluid pushed aside by the submerged portion of an object. For a fully submerged object, this equals the object's total volume.

  Q17. What does 'positive buoyancy' mean?
    [ ] When an object sinks because its weight exceeds the upward buoyant force
    [*] When the buoyant force on an object is greater than its weight, so it floats
    [ ] When an object stays suspended at a fixed depth without rising or sinking
    [ ] When there is no buoyant force acting on the object placed in the fluid used
    Explanation: Positive buoyancy means the buoyant force exceeds the object's weight. The object floats or rises to the surface because the net upward force pushes it up.

  Q18. What does 'negative buoyancy' mean?
    [*] When the buoyant force on an object is less than the weight, so it sinks down
    [ ] When the buoyant force on an object is greater than the weight, so it rises up
    [ ] When the buoyant force is exactly equal to the weight of the submerged object
    [ ] When the fluid has no measurable effect on the object submerged within the fluid
    Explanation: Negative buoyancy means the object's weight exceeds the buoyant force. The net force is downward, so the object sinks.

  Q19. What does 'Archimedes' principle' mean?
    [*] The principle that states buoyant force equals the weight of fluid displaced by the object
    [ ] The principle that states all objects will eventually sink to the bottom of any fluid medium
    [ ] The principle that states fluid pressure is the same at every point inside a closed system
    [ ] The principle that states heavier objects always displace more fluid than lighter objects do
    Explanation: Archimedes' principle states that any object submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid that the object displaces.

  Q20. What does 'specific gravity' mean?
    [*] The ratio of an object's density to the density of water, with no units attached
    [ ] The exact value of gravitational acceleration at a specific location on the Earth
    [ ] The weight of a specific volume of fluid measured using a standard laboratory scale
    [ ] The amount of gravity that acts on an object when it is submerged under the water
    Explanation: Specific gravity is the ratio of a substance's density to the density of water. It is a dimensionless number. If specific gravity is less than 1, the substance floats in water.

  Q21. A block of wood with a volume of 500 cm^3 is placed in water. It floats with 300 cm^3 below the surface. What is the buoyant force if water weighs 1 g/cm^3?
    [ ] About 5.0 N because all 500 cm^3 of the wood displaces water
    [ ] About 2.0 N because only 200 cm^3 of the wood is above the water
    [*] About 2.94 N because 300 cm^3 of water is displaced by the block
    [ ] About 1.47 N because only half of the block is below the surface line
    Explanation: The displaced water volume is 300 cm^3 = 300 g = 0.3 kg. Buoyant force = weight of displaced water = 0.3 kg x 9.8 m/s^2 = 2.94 N.

  Q22. A submarine adjusts its buoyancy by filling or emptying ballast tanks with water. To rise to the surface, what should the submarine do?
    [ ] Fill the ballast tanks with water to become heavier overall
    [*] Pump water out of the ballast tanks to become lighter overall
    [ ] Keep the ballast tanks half full to maintain neutral buoyancy
    [ ] Open all hatches to let water flow freely through the submarine
    Explanation: Pumping water out reduces the submarine's total weight while its volume stays the same, lowering its average density. When the density drops below water's density, the sub rises.

  Q23. A student places a 2 kg rock in water and measures its apparent weight as 1.5 kg. What is the buoyant force acting on the rock?
    [ ] About 19.6 N upward on the rock in the water
    [*] About 4.9 N upward on the rock in the water
    [ ] About 14.7 N upward on the rock in the water
    [ ] About 9.8 N upward on the rock in the water
    Explanation: Buoyant force = true weight - apparent weight = (2 kg x 9.8) - (1.5 kg x 9.8) = 19.6 N - 14.7 N = 4.9 N.

  Q24. Two identical balls are placed in different liquids. Ball A floats in liquid X and Ball B sinks in liquid Y. What can you conclude about the liquids?
    [*] Liquid X is denser than liquid Y because it supports the floating ball
    [ ] Liquid Y is denser than liquid X because the ball sinks further down
    [ ] Both liquids have the same density since the balls are identical objects
    [ ] Nothing can be concluded about the density of either liquid from this test
    Explanation: Since Ball A floats in X, liquid X must be denser than the ball. Since Ball B sinks in Y, liquid Y must be less dense than the ball. Therefore, liquid X is denser than liquid Y.

  Q25. A person floats more easily in the Dead Sea than in a freshwater lake. Why?
    [ ] Because the Dead Sea is warmer and warm water creates more buoyancy always
    [*] Because the Dead Sea has very high salt content, making it much denser than freshwater
    [ ] Because the Dead Sea is at a lower elevation where gravity is significantly weaker
    [ ] Because the Dead Sea has waves that push people upward toward the water surface
    Explanation: The Dead Sea's extremely high salt concentration makes its water much denser than freshwater. Denser water provides a greater buoyant force, so people float more easily.

  Q26. An ice cube floats in a glass of water with about 90% of its volume below the surface. What does this tell you about the density of ice compared to water?
    [*] Ice is about 90% as dense as water, which is why 90% is submerged below
    [ ] Ice is exactly as dense as water because it is just frozen water molecules
    [ ] Ice is twice as dense as water because most of the cube is below the surface
    [ ] Ice has no measurable density difference from water in liquid form at all
    Explanation: The fraction submerged equals the ratio of the object's density to the fluid's density. Since 90% is submerged, ice is about 90% as dense as water (approximately 0.9 g/cm^3 vs 1.0 g/cm^3).

  Q27. A scuba diver adds lead weights to her belt before diving. Why does she need extra weight?
    [*] To make her body and equipment denser so she can descend below the surface
    [ ] To protect herself from strong underwater currents that might push her around
    [ ] To increase the water pressure around her body for safer deeper diving trips
    [ ] To reduce the drag force on her body as she swims through the water quickly
    Explanation: A diver's body and wetsuit tend to be less dense than water, creating positive buoyancy. Adding lead weights increases her overall density so she can descend and control her depth.

  Q28. A toy boat carries small metal weights. As more weights are added, the boat sinks lower. At what point will the boat start to sink?
    [*] When the boat's weight exceeds the weight of water it can possibly displace at maximum
    [ ] When the number of weights exceeds ten individual pieces placed inside the toy boat
    [ ] When the weights make the boat heavier than the total weight of water in the container
    [ ] When the metal weights rust and become heavier due to water absorption over long time
    Explanation: The boat sinks when its total weight (boat + cargo) exceeds the maximum buoyant force, which equals the weight of water displaced when the boat is fully submerged up to its rim.

  Q29. A hot air balloon rises because the air inside is heated. How does heating the air inside the balloon create buoyancy?
    [*] Heating makes the inside air expand and become less dense than the outside air
    [ ] Heating removes all air from the balloon making it completely empty and lighter
    [ ] Heating creates a vacuum inside the balloon that pulls the balloon upward above
    [ ] Heating changes the shape of the balloon so that it catches more upward wind
    Explanation: Heating air makes it expand and become less dense. The balloon filled with hot (less dense) air displaces cooler (denser) outside air, and the weight of the displaced air exceeds the balloon's weight, creating a net upward buoyant force.

  Q30. A cargo ship moves from the ocean into a freshwater river. What happens to how deep the ship sits in the water?
    [ ] The ship sits higher because freshwater provides a greater buoyant force on it
    [*] The ship sinks lower because freshwater is less dense and provides less buoyancy
    [ ] The ship stays at exactly the same depth because the ship's weight did not change
    [ ] The ship completely sinks because freshwater cannot support heavy metal ships at all
    Explanation: Freshwater is less dense than saltwater, so it provides less buoyant force per volume displaced. The ship must sink lower to displace more water and generate enough buoyant force to equal its weight.
