--- Lesson 2.1: Distance, Displacement, Speed, Velocity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Distance, Displacement, Speed, Velocity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between distance and displacement?
Distance is a scalar that measures the total path length traveled. Displacement is a vector that measures the straight-line distance from start to finish, including direction.
What is velocity?
Velocity is the rate of change of displacement with respect to time. It is a vector quantity that includes both speed and direction of motion.
What is instantaneous velocity?
Instantaneous velocity is the velocity of an object at a specific moment in time. On a position-time graph, it equals the slope of the tangent line at that point.
What is the SI unit for speed?
The SI unit for speed (and velocity) is meters per second (m/s). Other units like km/h and mph are commonly used but are not SI units.
A car travels 100 km in 2 hours. What is its average speed?
Average speed = total distance / total time = 100 km / 2 h = 50 km/h.
What is the definition of 'distance' in physics?
Distance is the total length of the path traveled, regardless of direction. It is a scalar quantity and is always positive or zero, never negative.
What is the definition of 'displacement'?
Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish, including direction. It is a vector quantity calculated as final position minus initial position.
What is the difference between 'speed' and 'velocity'?
Speed is a scalar that describes only how fast (magnitude). Velocity is a vector that includes both how fast and in which direction. Speed is always positive; velocity can be negative (indicating direction).
What is the meaning of a 'negative velocity'?
A negative velocity simply means the object is moving in the direction defined as negative by the coordinate system (e.g., to the left or downward). It does not mean the object is slowing down.
A student walks 3 blocks north to school, then 3 blocks south back home. What is the student's total distance and displacement?
Distance is the total path length: 3 + 3 = 6 blocks. Displacement is the straight line from start to finish: since the student returned home, displacement = 0.
A jogger runs 5 km in 30 minutes, rests for 10 minutes, then runs 3 km in 20 minutes. What is the jogger's average speed for the entire trip?
Total distance = 5 + 3 = 8 km. Total time = 30 + 10 + 20 = 60 minutes = 1 hour. Average speed = 8 km / 1 h = 8 km/h. Rest time is included in the total time.
A car drives 60 km east and then 80 km north. What is the magnitude of the car's displacement?
East and north are perpendicular, so use the Pythagorean theorem: displacement = sqrt(60^2 + 80^2) = sqrt(3600 + 6400) = sqrt(10000) = 100 km. The total distance is 140 km.
A student rides a bike at 10 m/s east for 5 seconds, then at 10 m/s west for 5 seconds. What is the student's average velocity?
The student goes 50 m east then 50 m west, returning to the start. Displacement = 0, so average velocity = 0/10 = 0 m/s. However, average speed = 100 m / 10 s = 10 m/s.
A race car completes one lap around a 500-meter circular track in 25 seconds. What is its average speed and average velocity?
Average speed = distance/time = 500/25 = 20 m/s. But after one complete lap, the car returns to its starting position, so displacement = 0 and average velocity = 0 m/s.
A student walks 100 m north in 50 s, then 100 m south in 50 s. What is the student's average speed for the entire trip?
Average speed = total distance / total time = (100 + 100) / (50 + 50) = 200/100 = 2 m/s. Even though displacement is zero, the average speed is not zero because distance was traveled.
A delivery truck travels 30 km at 60 km/h, then 30 km at 30 km/h. What is the truck's average speed for the whole trip?
Time for first leg: 30/60 = 0.5 h. Time for second leg: 30/30 = 1 h. Total: 60 km in 1.5 h. Average speed = 60/1.5 = 40 km/h. The average speed is not simply (60+30)/2 because the truck spent more time at the slower speed.
A swimmer crosses a 50-meter pool in 25 seconds and swims back in 30 seconds. What is the swimmer's average velocity for the round trip?
After swimming to the far end and back, the swimmer returns to the starting position. Displacement = 0, so average velocity = 0 / 55 = 0 m/s. The average speed, however, is 100/55 = about 1.8 m/s.
A student throws a ball straight up at 10 m/s. At the very top of its path, what is the ball's instantaneous speed?
At the very top of its path, the ball momentarily stops before falling back down. Its instantaneous speed is 0 m/s at that instant. However, it still has acceleration (9.8 m/s^2 downward due to gravity).
Real-World Applications
Example: Can average velocity equal zero for a moving object?
Average velocity = displacement / time. If an object returns to its starting position, displacement = 0, so average velocity = 0, even though the object was moving the entire time.
Example: How is average speed calculated?
Average speed = total distance / total time. It is a scalar quantity and is always positive. Average speed is always greater than or equal to the magnitude of average velocity.
Example: A car travels 100 km in 2 hours. What is its average speed?
Average speed = total distance / total time = 100 km / 2 h = 50 km/h.
Example: What does 'average velocity' mean?
Average velocity = total displacement / total time. It is a vector quantity. Note that total distance / total time gives average speed (a scalar), not average velocity.
Example: What does 'average speed' represent physically?
Average speed is the hypothetical constant speed that would allow you to cover the same total distance in the same total time. It equals total distance divided by total time.
Example: A jogger runs 5 km in 30 minutes, rests for 10 minutes, then runs 3 km in 20 minutes. What is the jogger's average speed for the entire trip?

--- Lesson 2.2: Acceleration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acceleration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is acceleration?
Acceleration is the rate at which velocity changes over time: a = change in velocity / change in time. It is a vector quantity with both magnitude and direction.
What is the SI unit of acceleration?
The SI unit of acceleration is meters per second squared (m/s^2). This means the velocity changes by a certain number of m/s every second.
A car goes from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 seconds. What is its acceleration?
Acceleration = change in velocity / time = (20 - 0) / 5 = 20/5 = 4 m/s^2. The car's speed increases by 4 m/s each second.
What is the acceleration of an object moving at constant velocity?
If velocity is constant (not changing), then acceleration = 0 m/s^2. Acceleration only exists when there is a change in velocity.
What is the difference between 'acceleration' and 'velocity'?
Velocity is the rate of change of position (how fast and in what direction something moves). Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity (how quickly velocity itself changes). Both are vectors.
What is the meaning of 'centripetal acceleration'?
Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of a circular path and is responsible for changing the direction of velocity during circular motion. It does not change the speed.
A car traveling at 30 m/s brakes and comes to a stop in 6 seconds. What is the car's acceleration?
Acceleration = (final velocity - initial velocity) / time = (0 - 30) / 6 = -30/6 = -5 m/s^2. The negative sign indicates the car is decelerating (slowing down).
A skateboarder accelerates from 2 m/s to 8 m/s in 3 seconds. What is the skateboarder's acceleration?
Acceleration = (8 - 2) / 3 = 6/3 = 2 m/s^2. The skateboarder's velocity increases by 2 m/s each second.
In a lab, a student records that a cart on a track has a velocity of 1.0 m/s at t = 0 s and 4.0 m/s at t = 2 s. If the acceleration is constant, what is the velocity at t = 1 s?
Acceleration = (4.0 - 1.0) / 2 = 1.5 m/s^2. At t = 1 s: v = 1.0 + 1.5(1) = 2.5 m/s. With constant acceleration, velocity increases uniformly.
A bicycle slows from 15 m/s to 5 m/s over 10 seconds. What is the bicycle's average acceleration?
Acceleration = (5 - 15) / 10 = -10/10 = -1 m/s^2. The negative sign indicates the bicycle is decelerating (slowing down).
A student drops a ball from rest. After 3 seconds of free fall (ignoring air resistance), what is the ball's velocity?
v = u + at = 0 + (9.8)(3) = 29.4 m/s downward. In free fall, velocity increases by 9.8 m/s each second.
A roller coaster car speeds up from 5 m/s to 25 m/s in 4 seconds, then maintains 25 m/s for the next 6 seconds. What is the average acceleration over the entire 10 seconds?
Average acceleration = (final velocity - initial velocity) / total time = (25 - 5) / 10 = 20/10 = 2 m/s^2. The constant-velocity phase reduces the average acceleration over the full 10 seconds.
A train moving at 20 m/s applies brakes and decelerates at 2 m/s^2. A student asks how long it takes to stop. What is the answer?
Using v = u + at: 0 = 20 + (-2)t, so 2t = 20, giving t = 10 seconds. The train loses 2 m/s of speed each second until it reaches 0 m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'average acceleration' mean?
Average acceleration = (final velocity - initial velocity) / time elapsed. It tells you the overall rate of velocity change over a given time interval.
Example: A bicycle slows from 15 m/s to 5 m/s over 10 seconds. What is the bicycle's average acceleration?
Acceleration = (5 - 15) / 10 = -10/10 = -1 m/s^2. The negative sign indicates the bicycle is decelerating (slowing down).
Example: A roller coaster car speeds up from 5 m/s to 25 m/s in 4 seconds, then maintains 25 m/s for the next 6 seconds. What is the average acceleration over the entire 10 seconds?
Average acceleration = (final velocity - initial velocity) / total time = (25 - 5) / 10 = 20/10 = 2 m/s^2. The constant-velocity phase reduces the average acceleration over the full 10 seconds.

    Explanation: Constant acceleration means the velocity changes by the same amount in every equal time interval. For example, if acceleration is 3 m/s^2, velocity increases by 3 m/s every second.

  Q13. What does 'average acceleration' mean?
    [*] The total change in velocity divided by the total time interval
    [ ] The maximum acceleration reached during a period of time
    [ ] The arithmetic mean of all forces acting on the object at once
    [ ] The acceleration measured only at the midpoint of a time interval
    Explanation: Average acceleration = (final velocity - initial velocity) / time elapsed. It tells you the overall rate of velocity change over a given time interval.

  Q14. What does 'instantaneous acceleration' mean?
    [*] The acceleration of an object at a specific moment in time
    [ ] The total acceleration accumulated over the entire trip duration
    [ ] The acceleration that occurs the instant an object comes to rest
    [ ] The initial acceleration an object has when it first starts moving
    Explanation: Instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a specific moment in time. On a velocity-time graph, it equals the slope of the tangent line at that particular point.

  Q15. What is the difference between 'acceleration' and 'velocity'?
    [*] Velocity describes how fast position changes; acceleration describes how fast velocity changes
    [ ] Velocity is a vector and acceleration is a scalar physical quantity
    [ ] They are two words that describe exactly the same physical concept
    [ ] Velocity applies only to horizontal motion; acceleration applies only to vertical
    Explanation: Velocity is the rate of change of position (how fast and in what direction something moves). Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity (how quickly velocity itself changes). Both are vectors.

  Q16. What does 'uniform acceleration' describe?
    [ ] Motion where the speed stays the same but direction keeps changing
    [*] Motion where the acceleration remains constant over time
    [ ] Motion where the object accelerates only during the first half of the trip
    [ ] Motion where all objects in a group accelerate at the same rate together
    Explanation: Uniform acceleration means the acceleration stays constant (does not change with time). Free fall near Earth's surface is an example of uniform acceleration (g = 9.8 m/s^2 = constant).

  Q17. What does 'non-uniform acceleration' mean?
    [*] Acceleration that changes in magnitude or direction over time
    [ ] Acceleration that always has a magnitude of exactly 9.8 m/s^2
    [ ] Acceleration that only occurs when objects are moving very slowly
    [ ] Acceleration that applies only to objects in circular orbital motion
    Explanation: Non-uniform acceleration means the acceleration itself changes over time. For example, a car that presses the gas pedal harder and harder has increasing (non-uniform) acceleration.

  Q18. What is the meaning of 'centripetal acceleration'?
    [*] Acceleration directed toward the center of a circular path
    [ ] Acceleration that acts in the same direction as the object's velocity
    [ ] Acceleration that pushes objects away from the center of a circle
    [ ] Acceleration that only occurs when objects move in a straight line
    Explanation: Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of a circular path and is responsible for changing the direction of velocity during circular motion. It does not change the speed.

  Q19. What does 'gravitational acceleration' (g) represent?
    [*] The acceleration caused by gravity near Earth's surface, about 9.8 m/s^2 downward
    [ ] The acceleration that only applies to objects that are heavier than 1 kilogram
    [ ] The acceleration experienced only by objects that are already in free fall
    [ ] The velocity at which all objects fall to the ground regardless of their mass
    Explanation: Gravitational acceleration (g) is the acceleration due to Earth's gravity, approximately 9.8 m/s^2 directed downward. It applies to all objects near Earth's surface, regardless of mass.

  Q20. What does the phrase 'change in velocity' represent in the acceleration formula?
    [*] Final velocity minus initial velocity, which can be positive or negative
    [ ] The total distance traveled divided by the total time of travel
    [ ] The absolute value of the velocity at any given moment in time
    [ ] The sum of the initial and final velocities added together
    Explanation: Change in velocity (delta v) = final velocity - initial velocity. It can be positive (speeding up in the positive direction) or negative (slowing down or reversing). It is the numerator in the acceleration formula.

--- Lesson 2.3: Graphical Analysis of Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphical Analysis of Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the meaning of 'negative slope' on a position-time graph?
A negative slope on a position-time graph means the position is decreasing over time, so the object has a negative velocity (moving in the negative direction).
A student looks at a v-t graph that shows a straight line from (0, 0) to (4, 20). What is the acceleration?
Acceleration = slope = rise/run = (20 - 0)/(4 - 0) = 20/4 = 5 m/s^2. The object speeds up from 0 to 20 m/s over 4 seconds at a constant rate.
A student has a v-t graph showing constant velocity of 10 m/s for 5 seconds. What is the displacement?
Displacement = area under the v-t graph = base x height = 5 s x 10 m/s = 50 m. The area forms a rectangle with base 5 and height 10.
A student sees a v-t graph that shows velocity increasing from 0 to 15 m/s linearly over 3 seconds. What is the total displacement during this time?
The area under the line is a triangle: area = 0.5 x base x height = 0.5 x 3 x 15 = 22.5 m. The displacement equals the area under the v-t graph.
In a lab, a student's v-t graph shows the velocity going from 10 m/s to -10 m/s over 4 seconds in a straight line. What is the acceleration?
Acceleration = slope = (-10 - 10)/4 = -20/4 = -5 m/s^2. The object decelerates, stops, and reverses direction during the 4-second interval.
A student plots an a-t graph that shows a constant acceleration of 2 m/s^2 for 5 seconds. Starting from rest, what is the change in velocity?
Area under a-t graph = change in velocity = base x height = 5 s x 2 m/s^2 = 10 m/s. Starting from rest, the final velocity is 10 m/s.
A student has a v-t graph showing 4 m/s for the first 3 seconds, then 8 m/s for the next 2 seconds. What is the total displacement?
Displacement = area under the v-t graph. First rectangle: 4 x 3 = 12 m. Second rectangle: 8 x 2 = 16 m. Total: 12 + 16 = 28 m.

--- Lesson 2.4: Equations of Motion (constant acceleration) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Equations of Motion (constant acceleration)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A car starts from rest. What is u?
'Starts from rest' means the initial velocity u = 0 m/s. This is a common condition in kinematic problems that simplifies the equations considerably.
What is the formula s = ut + (1/2)at^2 used for?
s = ut + (1/2)at^2 calculates displacement when you know initial velocity, acceleration, and time. It is derived from combining v = u + at with the definition of average velocity.
What is the acceleration due to gravity (g)?
The acceleration due to gravity near Earth's surface is approximately 9.8 m/s^2 directed downward. All objects in free fall experience this same acceleration regardless of mass.
What is the formula for average velocity with constant acceleration?
For constant acceleration, average velocity = (u + v) / 2 (the average of initial and final velocities). This works because velocity changes linearly with time during constant acceleration.
A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s^2 for 10 seconds. What is the displacement?
s = ut + (1/2)at^2 = 0(10) + (1/2)(2)(10^2) = 0 + (1)(100) = 100 m. Starting from rest simplifies the equation since the ut term is zero.
What is the meaning of 'initial conditions' in a kinematics problem?
Initial conditions are the known values at the beginning of a problem (t = 0), such as initial position and initial velocity. They are needed to solve kinematic equations.
What is the purpose of the equation s = (u + v)/2 * t?
s = (u + v)/2 * t uses the average of initial and final velocity multiplied by time to find displacement. It is useful when acceleration is not directly given.
A cyclist accelerates from 5 m/s to 15 m/s over a distance of 50 m. What is the acceleration?
Using v^2 = u^2 + 2as: 15^2 = 5^2 + 2a(50), so 225 = 25 + 100a, giving 100a = 200, so a = 2 m/s^2.
A car traveling at 20 m/s accelerates at 4 m/s^2 for 3 seconds. What is the final velocity?
v = u + at = 20 + (4)(3) = 20 + 12 = 32 m/s.
A lab cart starts at 2 m/s and accelerates at 1.5 m/s^2. A student needs to find its velocity after traveling 12 m. What is the velocity?
Using v^2 = u^2 + 2as = 4 + 2(1.5)(12) = 4 + 36 = 40. So v = sqrt(40) = about 6.3 m/s, closest to 6.4 m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the formula for average velocity with constant acceleration?
For constant acceleration, average velocity = (u + v) / 2 (the average of initial and final velocities). This works because velocity changes linearly with time during constant acceleration.

    Explanation: The equation v^2 = u^2 + 2as relates velocity to displacement without involving time. It is useful when time is not given or not needed in the problem.

  Q7. What is the acceleration due to gravity (g)?
    [ ] 9.8 m/s upward
    [ ] 3 x 10^8 m/s^2
    [ ] 10 N/kg
    [*] 9.8 m/s^2 downward
    Explanation: The acceleration due to gravity near Earth's surface is approximately 9.8 m/s^2 directed downward. All objects in free fall experience this same acceleration regardless of mass.

  Q8. What does 'comes to rest' mean in SUVAT problems?
    [ ] Initial velocity is zero (u = 0)
    [*] Final velocity is zero (v = 0)
    [ ] Acceleration is zero (a = 0)
    [ ] Displacement is zero (s = 0)
    Explanation: 'Comes to rest' means the object's final velocity is zero (v = 0). The object has stopped moving. This is different from 'starts from rest' where u = 0.

  Q9. What is the formula for average velocity with constant acceleration?
    [*] (u + v) / 2
    [ ] u + v
    [ ] (u - v) / 2
    [ ] v - u
    Explanation: For constant acceleration, average velocity = (u + v) / 2 (the average of initial and final velocities). This works because velocity changes linearly with time during constant acceleration.

  Q10. A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s^2 for 10 seconds. What is the displacement?
    [ ] 20 m
    [ ] 200 m
    [*] 100 m
    [ ] 50 m
    Explanation: s = ut + (1/2)at^2 = 0(10) + (1/2)(2)(10^2) = 0 + (1)(100) = 100 m. Starting from rest simplifies the equation since the ut term is zero.

  Q11. What does the variable 's' represent in the SUVAT equations?
    [*] Displacement, the change in position of the object
    [ ] Speed, how fast the object is currently moving
    [ ] Seconds, the unit used to measure time intervals
    [ ] Scalar, indicating the equation uses only magnitudes
    Explanation: In SUVAT, s stands for displacement (sometimes written as x or d). It is the change in position, not the total distance traveled.

  Q12. What does the variable 'u' represent in SUVAT equations?
    [ ] The unit of measurement used in the problem
    [*] The initial velocity of the object at time t = 0
    [ ] The universal gravitational constant used in gravity problems
    [ ] The final velocity of the object after acceleration
    Explanation: In SUVAT equations, u represents the initial velocity (the velocity at the start of the time interval). Some textbooks use v_0 instead of u.

  Q13. What does the acronym 'SUVAT' stand for?
    [*] The five kinematic variables: displacement, initial velocity, final velocity, acceleration, and time
    [ ] The five forces that act on an object: static, uniform, variable, applied, and tensile
    [ ] The five types of motion: straight, uniform, variable, angular, and translational
    [ ] The five units used in kinematics: second, unit, velocity, acceleration, and time
    Explanation: SUVAT stands for the five variables in kinematics: S (displacement), U (initial velocity), V (final velocity), A (acceleration), and T (time).

  Q14. What does 'constant acceleration' mean in the context of SUVAT?
    [ ] The velocity of the object does not change over the time interval at all
    [*] The acceleration stays the same value throughout the entire time interval
    [ ] The displacement increases by the same amount in each second of motion
    [ ] The object is not moving and has zero acceleration the whole time
    Explanation: Constant acceleration means the acceleration does not change with time. Velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals. All SUVAT equations require this condition.

  Q15. What is the meaning of 'initial conditions' in a kinematics problem?
    [*] The known values of position, velocity, and time at the start of the motion
    [ ] The forces that must be applied before any object can begin to move
    [ ] The laboratory conditions like temperature and pressure during the experiment
    [ ] The assumptions about friction that are made before solving the problem
    Explanation: Initial conditions are the known values at the beginning of a problem (t = 0), such as initial position and initial velocity. They are needed to solve kinematic equations.

  Q16. What does the phrase 'from rest' tell you about a SUVAT problem?
    [ ] The final velocity v equals zero at the end of the motion
    [*] The initial velocity u equals zero at the beginning of the motion
    [ ] The acceleration is zero throughout the entire time of motion
    [ ] The displacement is zero because the object did not travel anywhere
    Explanation: 'From rest' means the object starts with zero velocity (u = 0). This simplifies equations: v = at, s = (1/2)at^2, and v^2 = 2as.

    [ ] The velocity of the object at the start of the motion interval
    [*] The velocity of the object at the end of the motion interval
    [ ] The volume of the object being studied in the problem
    [ ] The vertical component of the displacement vector only
    Explanation: In SUVAT equations, v represents the final velocity at the end of the time interval. Some textbooks use v_f instead of v.

  Q18. What is the purpose of the equation s = (u + v)/2 * t?

    [ ] Finding the net force on an object using its mass and velocity change
    [ ] Calculating the kinetic energy of an object from its speed and position
    [ ] Determining the momentum change when a force acts for a given time
    Explanation: s = (u + v)/2 * t uses the average of initial and final velocity multiplied by time to find displacement. It is useful when acceleration is not directly given.

  Q19. What does 'uniformly accelerated motion' describe?
    [*] Motion where the acceleration remains constant throughout the interval
    [ ] Motion where the object moves at a constant speed in one direction
    [ ] Motion where all objects in a system have the same acceleration value
    [ ] Motion where the acceleration increases at a steady uniform rate
    Explanation: Uniformly accelerated motion means constant acceleration. The SUVAT equations are specifically designed for this type of motion. Free fall near Earth is a common example.

  Q20. What does the term 'braking distance' mean in kinematics?

    [ ] The maximum speed a vehicle can reach before needing to apply the brakes
    Explanation: Braking distance is the displacement from the moment brakes are applied until the vehicle comes to rest (v = 0). It can be calculated using v^2 = u^2 + 2as with v = 0.

--- Lesson 2.5: Free Fall & Projectile Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Free Fall & Projectile Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is free fall?
Free fall is motion where the only force acting is gravity (no air resistance). All objects in free fall accelerate at g = 9.8 m/s^2 downward regardless of mass.
At the top of a projectile's path, what is the vertical velocity?
At the peak of its trajectory, a projectile's vertical velocity is momentarily zero before it begins falling back down. The horizontal velocity remains constant throughout.
What is the formula for time of flight of a projectile?
For a projectile launched from level ground, the total time of flight is T = 2*v0*sin(theta)/g. This accounts for the time to reach the peak and the equal time to fall back down.
What is the horizontal velocity of a projectile throughout its flight (ignoring air resistance)?
Since there is no horizontal force (ignoring air resistance), horizontal velocity remains constant throughout the flight. Only vertical velocity changes due to gravity.
What is the vertical acceleration of a projectile throughout its flight?
Gravity acts downward at 9.8 m/s^2 throughout the entire flight, including at the peak. The vertical velocity is zero at the peak, but the acceleration is still 9.8 m/s^2 downward.
What is the definition of 'projectile motion'?
Projectile motion is the motion of an object that has been launched (projected) into the air and moves only under the influence of gravity (no thrust or air resistance). It has both horizontal and vertical components.
What is 'terminal velocity'?
Terminal velocity occurs when air resistance equals gravity, so net force is zero and the object stops accelerating. It falls at a constant speed. This is not true free fall since air resistance is present.
What is the definition of 'air resistance' in the context of free fall?
Air resistance (drag) is the force that air exerts on a moving object, opposing its direction of motion. In free fall problems, we often ignore air resistance to simplify calculations.
A ball is thrown horizontally from the top of a cliff at 10 m/s. What is its horizontal velocity 3 seconds later (ignoring air resistance)?
The horizontal velocity remains 10 m/s because there is no horizontal acceleration (gravity only acts vertically). The vertical velocity increases, but the horizontal component stays constant.
A student launches a projectile at 20 m/s at a 30-degree angle. What is the initial vertical velocity component?
Vertical component = v0*sin(30) = 20 * 0.5 = 10 m/s. The sin of 30 degrees is 0.5.
A soccer ball is kicked at 20 m/s at a 45-degree angle. What is the horizontal range? (Use g = 10 m/s^2.)
Range = v0^2 * sin(2*theta) / g = 400 * sin(90) / 10 = 400 * 1 / 10 = 40 m. At 45 degrees, sin(90) = 1, giving maximum range.

--- Lesson 2.6: Relative Motion & Reference Frames (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Relative Motion & Reference Frames
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a reference frame?
A reference frame is a coordinate system with respect to which position, velocity, and other quantities are measured. All motion is relative to a chosen reference frame.
What is an inertial frame?
An inertial frame is a reference frame that is not accelerating (moving at constant velocity or at rest). Newton's laws of motion hold directly in inertial frames without needing corrections.
Two cars approach each other, one at 50 km/h and the other at 60 km/h. What is their relative speed?
When two objects move toward each other (opposite directions), their relative speed is the sum: 50 + 60 = 110 km/h. Each car sees the other approaching at 110 km/h.
A person walks at 2 m/s on a train moving at 30 m/s in the same direction. What is the person's speed relative to the ground?
When moving in the same direction, velocities add: 30 + 2 = 32 m/s relative to the ground. The person's ground speed is the sum of the train speed and walking speed.
What is a 'non-inertial frame'?
A non-inertial frame is accelerating (speeding up, slowing down, or rotating). Newton's laws do not directly apply in non-inertial frames without adding fictitious forces like the centrifugal force.
What is the 'speed of light' and why is it important for relative motion?
The speed of light is approximately 3 x 10^8 m/s. At speeds approaching this value, Galilean velocity addition no longer works and must be replaced by Einstein's relativistic formulas.
A passenger on a train moving at 25 m/s throws a ball forward at 5 m/s relative to the train. What is the ball's speed relative to the ground?
The ball's ground velocity = train velocity + ball's velocity relative to train = 25 + 5 = 30 m/s forward. Both velocities are in the same direction, so they add.

North and east are perpendicular, so use the Pythagorean theorem: speed = sqrt(4^2 + 3^2) = sqrt(16 + 9) = sqrt(25) = 5 m/s.

--- Lesson 2.7: Velocity-Time Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Velocity-Time Graphs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a velocity-time graph?
A velocity-time graph (v-t graph) is a visual tool that plots an object's velocity on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. It shows how the speed and direction of an object change over a period of time.
What is meant by 'displacement' when reading a velocity-time graph?
Displacement is the change in position of an object. On a velocity-time graph, displacement equals the area between the line and the time axis. Areas above the axis count as positive, and areas below count as negative.
What is the meaning of 'slope' on a velocity-time graph?
The slope of a line is its steepness, calculated as rise over run. On a velocity-time graph, the slope equals the change in velocity divided by the change in time, which is the definition of acceleration.
A car starts from rest and reaches 20 m/s in 4 seconds, shown as a straight line on a v-t graph. What is its acceleration?
Acceleration equals the slope of the v-t graph: change in velocity divided by change in time. Here, acceleration = (20 - 0) / (4 - 0) = 20/4 = 5 m/s squared.
A bicycle's v-t graph shows velocity dropping from 15 m/s to 0 m/s in 5 seconds. What is the rate of deceleration?
Deceleration = change in velocity / time = (15 - 0) / 5 = 3 m/s squared. The velocity decreased by 3 m/s every second until the bicycle came to a complete stop.
A runner's v-t graph shows 8 m/s for 5 seconds, then 4 m/s for 5 seconds. What is the total displacement?
Displacement equals the total area under the v-t graph. First section: 8 m/s x 5 s = 40 m. Second section: 4 m/s x 5 s = 20 m. Total displacement = 40 + 20 = 60 meters.
A ball rolling uphill has a v-t graph that starts at 6 m/s and drops to 0 m/s in 3 seconds. What is the total displacement?
The area under the v-t graph is a triangle with base 3 s and height 6 m/s. Area of a triangle = 1/2 x base x height = 1/2 x 3 x 6 = 9 meters. The ball traveled 9 meters uphill before stopping.

--- Lesson 2.8: Position-Time Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Position-Time Graphs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a position-time graph?
A position-time graph (also called a distance-time graph) plots the position of an object on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. It visually shows where an object is located at every moment during its motion.
What is meant by 'constant velocity' on a position-time graph?
Constant velocity means the object covers equal distances in equal time intervals. On a position-time graph, this appears as a straight line with a constant slope, since position changes at a steady, unchanging rate.
What is meant by 'time interval' on a position-time graph?
A time interval is the difference between two time values on the x-axis. It tells you how much time has passed between two moments, which is needed to calculate velocity from the graph.
A dog's position-time graph shows a horizontal line at 15 meters for 10 seconds. What is the dog doing?
A horizontal line on a position-time graph means the position is not changing. The dog is staying at the same location, 15 meters from the starting point, for the entire 10 seconds. It is at rest.
A car's position-time graph shows a straight line going from 0 m to 200 m in 10 seconds. What is the car's velocity?
Velocity equals the slope of a position-time graph. Slope = (200 m - 0 m) / (10 s - 0 s) = 200/10 = 20 m/s. The car travels at a constant 20 meters per second.
A toy car's position-time graph shows a line from 30 m to 10 m over 4 seconds. What is the toy car's velocity?
Velocity = (final position - initial position) / time = (10 - 30) / 4 = -20/4 = -5 m/s. The negative sign tells us the toy car is moving backward, toward the reference point.
An ant walks from position 0 m to 3 m in 6 seconds at constant speed. What is the slope of the position-time graph?
Slope = change in position / change in time = (3 m - 0 m) / (6 s - 0 s) = 3/6 = 0.5 m/s. The ant moves at a steady velocity of 0.5 meters per second.

--- Lesson 2.9: Average vs. Instantaneous Velocity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Average vs. Instantaneous Velocity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is average velocity?
Average velocity is defined as the total displacement divided by the total time elapsed. It gives a single value that summarizes how position changed overall, regardless of what happened during the trip.
What is instantaneous velocity?
Instantaneous velocity is the velocity at one specific point in time. It captures both speed and direction at that exact moment, like a snapshot of the object's motion.
What is the meaning of 'scalar' in the context of speed?
A scalar is a quantity described by magnitude (size) alone, with no direction. Speed is a scalar because it tells you how fast but not which way. Velocity is a vector because it includes direction.
A student bikes 600 meters north in 2 minutes, then 600 meters south in 3 minutes. What is the average velocity for the whole trip?
The student ends up back at the starting point, so total displacement = 0 m. Average velocity = displacement / time = 0 / 300 s = 0 m/s. Even though the student traveled 1200 m total, the average velocity is zero.
A runner completes a 400-meter circular track in 80 seconds, ending where she started. What is her average velocity?
Since the runner returns to her starting point, her displacement is zero. Average velocity = displacement / time = 0 / 80 = 0 m/s. Her average speed would be 5 m/s (400/80), but average velocity is zero.
A hiker walks 3 km east in 1 hour, rests for 30 minutes, then walks 3 km east in 1 hour. What is the average velocity?
Total displacement = 3 + 3 = 6 km east. Total time = 1 + 0.5 + 1 = 2.5 hours. Average velocity = 6 km / 2.5 h = 2.4 km/h east. The rest period counts toward total time even though the hiker was not moving.
A drone flies 500 m east, then 300 m west, all in 40 seconds. What is its average velocity?
Displacement = 500 m east - 300 m west = 200 m east. Average velocity = 200 m / 40 s = 5 m/s east. The average velocity accounts for direction, unlike average speed which would use total distance (800 m).
Real-World Applications
Example: How is average velocity calculated?
Average velocity is calculated by dividing the total displacement (change in position) by the total time taken. It tells you the overall rate of position change for the entire trip.
Example: When an object moves at constant velocity, how do average and instantaneous velocity compare?
When velocity is constant, the velocity at every instant is the same value. Therefore, the average over any time period equals the instantaneous value at every moment. They are identical.
Example: On a position-time graph, average velocity between two points equals what?
Average velocity between two points on a position-time graph equals the slope of the straight line (called a secant line) connecting those two points. Slope = change in position / change in time.
Example: Can average velocity be zero even if the object moved during the trip?
Average velocity equals total displacement divided by time. If an object returns to its starting position, the displacement is zero, making the average velocity zero, even though the object was moving the entire time.
Example: Average velocity takes into account which of the following?
Average velocity considers the total displacement (final position minus initial position) and the total time elapsed. It does not account for what happened at each individual moment along the way.
Example: If an object speeds up and slows down during a trip, its average velocity will most likely differ from what?
When an object changes speed during a trip, its instantaneous velocity varies from moment to moment. The average velocity smooths these changes into one value, so it will differ from the instantaneous velocity at most individual moments.

--- Lesson 2.10: Uniform vs. Non-Uniform Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Uniform vs. Non-Uniform Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is uniform motion?
Uniform motion means the object moves at a constant velocity, covering equal distances in equal time intervals. There is no acceleration in uniform motion since the speed and direction do not change.
In non-uniform motion, what is changing?
In non-uniform motion, the velocity changes over time. The object may speed up, slow down, or change direction. This means the object is accelerating during non-uniform motion.
What is the acceleration of an object in uniform motion?
In uniform motion, the velocity is constant, which means it is not changing. Since acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, and velocity is not changing, the acceleration is zero.
What is non-uniform motion?
Non-uniform motion is motion in which the velocity changes. The object may speed up, slow down, or change direction. It covers unequal distances in equal time intervals.
What is meant by 'equal time intervals' in the definition of uniform motion?
Equal time intervals are segments of time that have the same duration, such as every 1 second or every 5 seconds. In uniform motion, the object covers the same distance during each of these equal time intervals.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of non-uniform motion?
A car accelerating from a red light is changing its velocity (speeding up), so it is in non-uniform motion. The other options describe objects either at rest or moving at a constant speed.
Example: A conveyor belt moves packages at a steady 2 m/s without stopping. What type of motion is this?
The conveyor belt moves at a steady 2 m/s without changing speed or direction. This is uniform motion because the velocity is constant and the belt covers equal distances in equal time intervals.

--- Lesson 2.11: Two-Dimensional Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Two-Dimensional Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
At the highest point of a projectile's path, what is the vertical velocity?
At the highest point, the projectile has momentarily stopped moving upward and has not yet started moving downward. The vertical velocity is zero at this instant, though the horizontal velocity continues unchanged.
What is two-dimensional motion?
Two-dimensional motion is movement that occurs in a plane, involving two perpendicular directions (such as horizontal and vertical). It requires two coordinates to fully describe the object's position.
What is a projectile?
A projectile is any object that is launched or thrown into the air and then moves under the influence of gravity alone (ignoring air resistance). Examples include a thrown ball, a kicked soccer ball, or a fired arrow.
What is meant by 'launch angle' in projectile motion?
The launch angle is the angle at which a projectile is launched relative to the horizontal ground. A 45-degree angle gives the maximum range for a given speed. A 0-degree angle means launched horizontally.
A water fountain shoots water at an angle. At the very top of the arc, what is happening to the water's vertical velocity?
At the top of the arc, the water has stopped going up and is about to start coming down. The vertical velocity is momentarily zero. However, the horizontal velocity continues unchanged at the top.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of two-dimensional motion?
A ball thrown through the air moves both horizontally (forward) and vertically (up then down) at the same time. This makes it two-dimensional motion. The other examples involve only one direction.
Example: A plane flying horizontally drops a supply package. A person on the ground watches the package fall. What path does the package follow?
As seen from the ground, the package moves forward (keeping the plane's horizontal velocity) while also falling downward due to gravity. The combination creates a curved parabolic path forward and downward.

    Explanation: Ignoring air resistance, the horizontal velocity of a projectile stays constant because no horizontal force acts on it after launch. Gravity only acts vertically, so it does not affect horizontal speed.

  Q7. In projectile motion, what happens to the vertical velocity as the object rises?
    [ ] It speeds up as the object goes higher
    [*] It slows down due to gravity pulling it back
    [ ] It stays constant throughout the entire flight
    [ ] It becomes horizontal at the peak of flight
    Explanation: As a projectile rises, gravity pulls it downward, which slows the upward velocity. At the highest point, the vertical velocity momentarily reaches zero before the object starts falling back down.

    Explanation: Two-dimensional motion is movement that occurs in a plane, involving two perpendicular directions (such as horizontal and vertical). It requires two coordinates to fully describe the object's position.

--- Lesson 2.12: Terminal Velocity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Terminal Velocity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is terminal velocity?
Terminal velocity is the maximum constant speed reached by a falling object when the upward force of air resistance equals the downward force of gravity. At this point, acceleration becomes zero.
What is the acceleration of an object at terminal velocity?
At terminal velocity, gravity and air resistance are equal and opposite, so the net force is zero. According to Newton's second law, if net force is zero, acceleration is also zero.
What is meant by 'balanced forces' at terminal velocity?
Balanced forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, resulting in a net force of zero. At terminal velocity, gravity pulling down and air resistance pushing up are balanced forces.

  Q1. What is terminal velocity?
    [*] The maximum constant speed a falling object reaches
    [ ] The initial speed of an object when it starts falling
    [ ] The speed at which an object is first thrown upward
    [ ] The speed of sound through the air at ground level
    Explanation: Terminal velocity is the maximum constant speed reached by a falling object when the upward force of air resistance equals the downward force of gravity. At this point, acceleration becomes zero.

  Q2. What two forces are balanced when an object reaches terminal velocity?
    [ ] Friction and the applied pushing force
    [*] Gravity and air resistance (drag force)
    [ ] Magnetism and the electric force on it
    [ ] Tension and the normal contact force
    Explanation: Terminal velocity occurs when the downward pull of gravity equals the upward push of air resistance. When these forces balance, the net force is zero and the object stops accelerating.

  Q3. What is the acceleration of an object at terminal velocity?
    [ ] It equals 9.8 meters per second squared
    [ ] It is increasing at a steady constant rate
    [*] It is zero because the forces are balanced
    [ ] It is negative and still decreasing rapidly
    Explanation: At terminal velocity, gravity and air resistance are equal and opposite, so the net force is zero. According to Newton's second law, if net force is zero, acceleration is also zero.

  Q4. As a skydiver falls, what happens to air resistance before terminal velocity is reached?
    [ ] Air resistance stays constant the whole time
    [ ] Air resistance decreases as speed increases
    [ ] Air resistance has no effect on the fall
    [*] Air resistance increases as speed increases
    Explanation: As a falling object speeds up, it pushes through more air per second, so air resistance increases. It keeps increasing until it equals gravity, at which point terminal velocity is reached.

  Q5. Which object would have a lower terminal velocity: a feather or a bowling ball?
    [*] A feather, because it has a large surface area relative to its weight
    [ ] A bowling ball, because it is much heavier than the feather
    [ ] Both have the same terminal velocity regardless of properties
    [ ] Neither reaches terminal velocity when falling in the air
    Explanation: A feather has a large surface area compared to its weight, so air resistance balances gravity at a much lower speed. A bowling ball is heavy and compact, so it must fall much faster before air resistance can balance gravity.

  Q6. How does opening a parachute affect terminal velocity?
    [ ] It increases terminal velocity significantly
    [ ] It has no effect on terminal velocity at all
    [*] It greatly decreases terminal velocity
    [ ] It eliminates gravity on the skydiver
    Explanation: A parachute greatly increases the surface area catching air, which increases air resistance. This means air resistance balances gravity at a much lower speed, greatly decreasing the terminal velocity.

  Q7. Does a heavier object generally have a higher or lower terminal velocity than a lighter one of the same shape?
    [ ] Lower, because heavier objects fall more slowly
    [*] Higher, because more air resistance is needed to balance gravity
    [ ] The same, because weight does not affect terminal velocity
    [ ] Neither, because heavier objects cannot reach terminal velocity
    Explanation: A heavier object has a greater gravitational force pulling it down. Air resistance must match this larger force, which only happens at higher speeds. So a heavier object of the same shape has a higher terminal velocity.

  Q8. What happens to a falling object's speed after it reaches terminal velocity?
    [ ] It continues to increase without any limit
    [ ] It decreases slowly until the object stops
    [*] It remains constant and does not change
    [ ] It oscillates between fast and slow speeds
    Explanation: Once terminal velocity is reached, the forces are balanced and acceleration is zero. The object continues falling at the same constant speed. It does not speed up or slow down.

  Q9. Which factor does NOT affect an object's terminal velocity?
    [ ] The object's surface area facing the wind
    [ ] The object's weight or gravitational force
    [*] The color of the object that is falling
    [ ] The density of the air the object falls through
    Explanation: Terminal velocity depends on weight, surface area, shape, and air density. The color of the object has no effect on the forces of gravity or air resistance, so it does not affect terminal velocity.

  Q10. When a skydiver first jumps from a plane, is the acceleration closer to 9.8 m/s squared or 0 m/s squared?

    [*] The acceleration starts close to 9.8 m/s squared

    [ ] The acceleration starts at negative 9.8 m/s sq
    Explanation: When first jumping, the skydiver has very low speed, so air resistance is minimal. Gravity dominates, making acceleration close to 9.8 m/s squared. As speed increases, air resistance grows and acceleration decreases toward zero.

  Q11. What is terminal velocity?
    [*] The constant speed where air resistance equals gravity during a fall
    [ ] The speed at which an object bounces back upward after hitting ground
    [ ] The maximum speed an object can ever reach in any situation at all
    [ ] The initial velocity when an object first starts falling from a height
    Explanation: Terminal velocity is the constant speed a falling object reaches when the upward air resistance force exactly equals the downward gravitational force. At this point, the net force and acceleration are both zero.

  Q12. What does 'air resistance' mean?
    [ ] The force of gravity pulling objects downward
    [*] The friction-like force air exerts on moving objects
    [ ] The weight of the air above an object on Earth
    [ ] The amount of oxygen available for breathing
    Explanation: Air resistance (also called drag) is the force that air exerts on a moving object, opposing its motion. It acts like friction and increases with the speed and surface area of the object.

  Q13. What does 'drag force' mean?
    [ ] The force that pushes an object forward
    [*] The resistance force opposing motion through a fluid
    [ ] The gravitational pull between two large masses
    [ ] The elastic force from a compressed spring
    Explanation: Drag force is the resistance force that opposes an object's motion through a fluid such as air or water. It depends on the object's speed, shape, size, and the density of the fluid.

  Q14. What does 'net force' mean in the context of terminal velocity?
    [*] The total combined force after adding all forces together
    [ ] Only the force of gravity acting on the falling object
    [ ] Only the air resistance acting on the falling object
    [ ] The mass of the object multiplied by its total volume
    Explanation: Net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object. At terminal velocity, the net force is zero because gravity (downward) and air resistance (upward) are equal and opposite.

  Q15. What does 'surface area' mean in relation to terminal velocity?

    [ ] The volume of air inside a hollow falling object
    [ ] The weight of the object in newtons or pounds
    [ ] The speed at which the object is currently falling
    Explanation: Surface area is the total outside area of an object. A larger surface area catches more air, creating greater air resistance. This is why a parachute (large surface area) slows a skydiver down.

  Q16. What does 'free fall' mean?

    [ ] Falling at terminal velocity through the atmosphere
    [ ] Falling and then bouncing back up off the ground
    [ ] Falling very slowly through a thick liquid material
    Explanation: Free fall is when an object falls under the influence of gravity alone, with no air resistance. In true free fall (like in a vacuum), all objects accelerate at the same rate regardless of mass.

  Q17. What is meant by 'balanced forces' at terminal velocity?
    [*] Forces that are equal in size but opposite in direction
    [ ] Forces that push the object in the same single direction
    [ ] Forces that change the acceleration of the object
    [ ] Forces that exist only when the object is at rest
    Explanation: Balanced forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, resulting in a net force of zero. At terminal velocity, gravity pulling down and air resistance pushing up are balanced forces.

  Q18. What does 'fluid density' mean in the context of terminal velocity?

    [*] How tightly packed the particles of a fluid are
    [ ] The color and temperature of the surrounding fluid

    Explanation: Fluid density is the mass per unit volume of the fluid. Denser fluids (like water versus air) create more resistance, which means terminal velocity is lower in denser fluids.
