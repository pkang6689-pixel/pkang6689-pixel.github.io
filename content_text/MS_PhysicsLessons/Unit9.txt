--- Lesson 9.1: Reflection & Refraction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Reflection & Refraction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the refractive index?
Refractive index — n = c/v (ratio of speed of light in vacuum to speed in the medium). The other options — 'n = v/c', 'n = c·v', and 'n = c + v' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is specular reflection?
A reflection flips a figure over a line (axis of reflection). The image is the same distance from the line as the original, on the opposite side. The answer is Reflection from a smooth surface. The other options — 'Reflection from a rough surface', 'Reflection at 90°', and 'No reflection' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the refractive index of a vacuum?
Since n = c/v, and light travels at speed c in a vacuum, the refractive index of a vacuum is c/c = 1. All other transparent materials have refractive indices greater than 1.
What is diffuse reflection?
Diffuse reflection occurs when light hits a rough surface and scatters in many directions. This is why rough surfaces like paper do not produce clear mirror images, unlike smooth surfaces that produce specular reflection.
What is the normal in the context of reflection and refraction?
The normal is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular (at 90°) to the surface at the point where light strikes. All angles in reflection and refraction are measured from this normal line.
What is the angle of incidence?
The angle of incidence is the angle between the incoming (incident) ray and the normal line at the point of contact with the surface. It is not measured from the surface itself.
What is an 'optically denser' medium?
An optically denser medium has a higher refractive index, meaning light travels more slowly through it. For example, glass (n ≈ 1.5) is optically denser than air (n ≈ 1.0).
What is meant by 'dispersion' of light?
Dispersion is the separation of white light into its component colors (spectrum) because different wavelengths refract by different amounts. A prism demonstrates this, producing a rainbow of colors.
What is an 'optical boundary' between two media?
An optical boundary is the surface or interface where two different media meet. When light strikes this boundary, it can be reflected, refracted, or both, depending on the angle and the media involved.
In a lab, a student measures the angle of incidence to be 30°. What is the angle of reflection?
According to the law of reflection, the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Since the angle of incidence is 30°, the reflected ray also makes a 30° angle with the normal.
Important Concepts
Does frequency change during refraction
Snell's Law: n₁sin(θ₁) = n₂sin(θ₂). Light bends toward the normal when entering a denser medium (higher index of refraction) and away from normal when entering a less dense medium. The answer is 'No — wavelength changes'. The other options — 'Yes — frequency changes', 'Both change', and 'Neither changes' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

--- Lesson 9.2: Lenses & Mirrors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Lenses & Mirrors
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the magnification formula?
Magnification m = −d_i/d_o gives the ratio of image height to object height. A negative m means the image is inverted. The other options are incorrect formulations of the magnification relationship.
What is the 'focal length' of a lens or mirror?
Focal length is the distance from the center of a lens or the surface of a mirror to the focal point, where parallel light rays converge (converging) or appear to diverge from (diverging).
What is a 'virtual image' in optics?
A virtual image is formed where light rays appear to diverge from but do not actually meet. It cannot be captured on a screen. Examples include images in flat mirrors and magnifying glasses.
What is a 'real image' in optics?
A real image is formed where actual light rays converge and intersect. Unlike virtual images, real images can be projected onto a screen. They are typically inverted.
What is the 'object distance' in a lens or mirror problem?
Object distance (d_o) is the distance from the object to the lens or mirror surface. It is one of the key variables in the mirror/lens equation 1/f = 1/d_o + 1/d_i.
Real-World Applications
Example: What type of image does a convex mirror always form?
Convex mirror — Diverging — always forms virtual, upright, diminished images. The other options — 'Real and inverted', 'Virtual and enlarged', and 'Real and upright' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
Example: If |m| > 1, the image is:
When the absolute value of magnification is greater than 1, the image is larger than the object. When |m| < 1, the image is diminished; when |m| = 1, they are the same size.
Example: If m is negative, the image is:
A negative magnification means the image is inverted (flipped upside down). Real images formed by converging lenses and concave mirrors are inverted when the object is beyond the focal point.
Example: A concave mirror can form which types of images?
A concave mirror can form real, inverted images when the object is beyond the focal point, or virtual, upright, magnified images when the object is between the focal point and the mirror.

Example: What does 'image distance' mean in optics?
Image distance (d_i) is the distance from the lens or mirror to the location where the image forms. A positive d_i indicates a real image on the opposite side; negative means virtual.
Example: A student holds a magnifying glass close to a page and sees enlarged text. What type of image is being formed?
When an object is placed between a converging lens and its focal point, the lens acts as a magnifying glass. The image formed is virtual (cannot be projected), upright, and magnified.
Example: A student looks into a spoon's inner (concave) surface and sees an upside-down image of their face. Where must their face be relative to the focal point?
A concave mirror forms a real, inverted image when the object is beyond the focal point. If the student sees an upside-down reflection, their face must be farther away than the focal length of the spoon.
Example: In a lab, a student places an object at the focal point of a converging lens. What happens to the image?
When an object is placed exactly at the focal point of a converging lens, the refracted rays emerge parallel to each other and never converge. No image is formed at any finite distance.
Example: In a lab, a converging lens produces a real image on a screen. A student moves the object closer to the lens. What happens to the image?
When the object moves closer to a converging lens (but remains beyond the focal point), the image distance increases and the image becomes larger. This follows from the lens equation 1/f = 1/d_o + 1/d_i.
Example: A student sets up a concave mirror and finds that the image is the same size as the object. Where is the object placed?
When an object is placed at the center of curvature (2f) of a concave mirror, the image forms at the same distance and is the same size as the object (|m| = 1), but inverted.
Example: A student uses a projector that contains a converging lens. The lens creates a large image on a distant screen. What type of image is this?
A projector uses a converging lens to produce a real, inverted, enlarged image on a screen. The slide or image source is placed slightly beyond the focal point, and the projector flips the source so the projected image appears right-side up.
Example: A student calculates magnification m = −2 for an image formed by a lens. What does this tell them about the image?
A magnification of m = −2 means the image is inverted (negative sign) and twice the size of the object (|m| = 2). The negative sign always indicates an inverted image in optics sign conventions.

--- Lesson 9.3: Total Internal Reflection (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Total Internal Reflection
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is total internal reflection (TIR)?
Total internal reflection occurs when all light is reflected back into the denser medium at angles above the critical angle, with no light passing through the boundary.
What is the formula for the critical angle?
Critical Angle sin θ_c = n₂/n₁ (where n₁ > n₂). At this angle the refracted ray travels along the boundary surface.
What is the approximate critical angle for glass (n = 1.5) to air?
Using sin θ_c = n₂/n₁ = 1/1.5 = 0.667, so θ_c = arcsin(0.667) ≈ 42°. Any light hitting the glass-air boundary at an angle greater than 42° will undergo total internal reflection.
What is the 'critical angle' in optics?
The critical angle is the specific angle of incidence at which the refracted ray travels exactly along the boundary (90° refraction). Above this angle, total internal reflection occurs.
What is an 'optical fiber' in telecommunications?
An optical fiber is a thin, flexible strand of glass or plastic that transmits light signals over long distances using total internal reflection. The core has a higher refractive index than the surrounding cladding.
What is meant by a 'denser medium' in optics?
In optics, a denser medium refers to one with a higher refractive index, where light travels more slowly. This is optical density, not physical density, though they often correlate.
What is an 'endoscope' and how does it work?
An endoscope is a medical instrument that uses bundles of optical fibers to transmit light into the body and carry images back to the doctor. It relies on total internal reflection within the fibers.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student is told that mirages on hot roads are caused by TIR. How does this work?
Hot air near the road surface has a lower density and lower refractive index than the cooler air above. Light from the sky traveling downward gradually bends and undergoes TIR near the road, making it appear as if there is water reflecting the sky.

--- Lesson 9.4: Optical Instruments (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Optical Instruments
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the near point of a normal eye?
The near point is approximately 25 cm for a normal eye — the closest distance at which objects can be focused clearly. The far point is at infinity for normal vision.
What is the total magnification of a compound microscope?
The total magnification of a compound microscope is the product of the objective magnification and the eyepiece magnification. For example, a 40× objective and a 10× eyepiece give 400× total magnification.
What is the far point of a normal eye?
The far point of a normal eye is at infinity, meaning the eye can focus on very distant objects. A person with myopia has a far point closer than infinity.
What is 'myopia' in terms of eye optics?
Myopia (nearsightedness) occurs when the eyeball is too long or the lens is too powerful, causing images of distant objects to focus in front of the retina instead of on it.
What is 'hyperopia' in terms of eye optics?
Hyperopia (farsightedness) occurs when the eyeball is too short or the lens is too weak, causing images of nearby objects to focus behind the retina. Distant objects may still be clear.
What is the 'retina' and what is its function?
The retina is the light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye. It contains photoreceptor cells (rods and cones) that convert focused light into electrical signals sent to the brain via the optic nerve.
What is the 'objective lens' in a microscope or telescope?
The objective lens is the lens closest to the object being viewed. In a telescope, it has a long focal length to collect light from distant objects. In a microscope, it has a short focal length for high magnification.
What is the 'eyepiece' of an optical instrument?
The eyepiece is the lens closest to the observer's eye. It acts like a magnifying glass, further enlarging the image produced by the objective lens.
What is the 'cornea' of the eye?
The cornea is the transparent, curved front surface of the eye. It performs about two-thirds of the eye's total refraction, bending incoming light before it reaches the adjustable lens behind it.
What is a 'compound microscope'?
A compound microscope uses two lens systems — the objective and the eyepiece — to achieve high total magnification. The total magnification is the product of both individual magnifications.
What is the 'near point' of the eye?
The near point is the closest distance at which the eye can focus clearly on an object. For a normal eye, this is approximately 25 cm. The near point increases with age.
A student uses a microscope with a 10× eyepiece and a 40× objective. What is the total magnification?
In a compound microscope, the total magnification equals the objective magnification multiplied by the eyepiece magnification: 40× × 10× = 400×.
In a lab, a student notices their microscope image is blurry. They adjust the focus knob. What is being changed physically?
The focus knob adjusts the distance between the objective lens and the specimen. This changes the object distance, which must be precisely set for the lens equation to produce a clear, focused image.
A student wonders why astronomers prefer reflecting telescopes (mirrors) over refracting telescopes (lenses) for large instruments. What is a key reason?
Large mirrors can be supported from behind, while large lenses sag under their own weight. Mirrors also avoid chromatic aberration (color fringing) that affects lenses, making reflecting telescopes preferred for professional astronomy.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student looks through a magnifying glass and sees an upright, enlarged image of an insect. Where must the insect be placed?
A magnifying glass (converging lens) produces a virtual, upright, magnified image only when the object is placed between the lens and its focal point. This is the standard way to use a magnifying glass.
Example: In a lab, a student notices their microscope image is blurry. They adjust the focus knob. What is being changed physically?
The focus knob adjusts the distance between the objective lens and the specimen. This changes the object distance, which must be precisely set for the lens equation to produce a clear, focused image.
Example: A student observes cells through a microscope. The image appears upside down and backwards. Why?
The objective lens in a compound microscope forms a real, inverted image of the specimen. The eyepiece then magnifies this already-inverted image, so the final view appears upside down and reversed.
Example: A student looks at the Moon through a telescope and sees a much larger image. What type of image is being formed by the eyepiece?
The eyepiece of a telescope acts like a magnifying glass, producing a virtual, magnified image of the real image formed by the objective lens. This virtual image is what the observer's eye sees.
Example: A student learns that cameras use converging lenses. Where does the image form in a camera?
A camera lens (converging) forms a real, inverted, diminished image on the film or digital sensor behind the lens. The sensor records this image, and software flips it right-side up for display.

--- Lesson 9.5: Diffraction & Interference (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Diffraction & Interference
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is diffraction?
Diffraction is the bending and spreading of waves around obstacles or through openings. It is most noticeable when the opening size is comparable to the wavelength.
What is a diffraction grating?
A diffraction grating has many closely spaced parallel slits. The large number of slits produces very sharp, well-defined bright maxima, useful for analyzing light spectra.
What is the formula for fringe spacing?
Fringe spacing Δy = λL/d, where λ is wavelength, L is the distance to the screen, and d is the slit separation. Wider spacing occurs with longer wavelengths or narrower slit separations.
What is 'constructive interference'?
Constructive interference occurs when two waves are in phase (crests align with crests) and their amplitudes add together, producing a combined wave with larger amplitude.
What is 'destructive interference'?
Destructive interference occurs when two waves are out of phase (crest meets trough) and their amplitudes subtract, reducing or eliminating the combined wave amplitude.
What is 'path difference' in the context of interference?
Path difference is the difference in the distance each wave travels from its source to a given point. Whether interference is constructive or destructive depends on whether this difference is a whole number of wavelengths or a half-number.
What is meant by an 'interference pattern'?
An interference pattern is the regular arrangement of bright (constructive) and dark (destructive) bands or fringes produced when two or more coherent waves overlap.
What is a 'fringe' in an interference or diffraction pattern?
A fringe is one of the alternating bright or dark bands in an interference or diffraction pattern. Bright fringes correspond to constructive interference and dark fringes to destructive interference.
What is 'superposition' of waves?
The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap, the resulting displacement at any point is the sum of the individual displacements. This is the basis of both constructive and destructive interference.

--- Lesson 9.6: Polarization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Polarization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is polarization?
Polarization restricts the oscillation of a transverse wave to a single plane. Only transverse waves can be polarized.
What is Brewster's angle?
Brewster's angle is the specific angle of incidence at which reflected light becomes completely polarized. At this angle, the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular to each other (tan θ_B = n₂/n₁).
What is a 'polarizer' in optics?
A polarizer is an optical filter that only allows light waves vibrating in one specific plane (the transmission axis) to pass through, blocking all other vibration directions.
What is an 'analyzer' in polarization experiments?
An analyzer is a second polarizing filter placed after the first polarizer. By rotating the analyzer and observing intensity changes (Malus's Law), you can determine the polarization state of the light.
What is the 'transmission axis' of a polarizer?
The transmission axis is the direction along which a polarizer allows the electric field component of light to pass through. Components perpendicular to this axis are blocked.
What is meant by 'intensity' of light?
Light intensity is the power (energy per second) delivered per unit area, measured in watts per square meter (W/m²). It corresponds to perceived brightness. Malus's Law describes how intensity changes through a polarizer.
What is meant by 'crossed polarizers'?
Crossed polarizers are two polarizing filters with their transmission axes oriented at 90° to each other. No light passes through this combination because cos²(90°) = 0.
What is 'scattering' as a method of polarization?
Scattering occurs when light interacts with small particles (like air molecules). The scattered light becomes partially polarized. This is why the sky appears polarized — it can be observed using polarizing sunglasses.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student learns that LCD screens use polarizers. What role do the polarizers play in displaying images?
LCD screens use two crossed polarizers with a liquid crystal layer between them. The liquid crystals rotate the polarization of light. By controlling this rotation electrically, each pixel can be switched between bright (light passes) and dark (light blocked).

--- Lesson 9.6: Polarization (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is polarization?
    [*] Restricting oscillation of a transverse wave to a single plane
    [ ] Increasing the speed of light
    [ ] Bending light around obstacles
    [ ] Changing the color of light
    Explanation: Polarization restricts the oscillation of a transverse wave to a single plane. Only transverse waves can be polarized.

  Q2. Can longitudinal waves be polarized?
    [ ] Yes
    [*] No — only transverse waves
    [ ] Only at high frequencies
    [ ] Only in certain media
    Explanation: Longitudinal waves cannot be polarized because their oscillations are already along a single direction (the direction of travel). Only transverse waves, which oscillate perpendicular to travel, can be restricted to one plane.

  Q3. What does polarization prove about light?
    [ ] That light has mass
    [ ] That light is a particle
    [*] That light is a transverse wave
    [ ] That light is longitudinal
    Explanation: The fact that light can be polarized proves it is a transverse wave. If light were longitudinal, polarization would be impossible.

  Q4. At θ = 90° (crossed polarizers), what fraction of light passes?
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 25%
    [*] 0%
    [ ] 100%
    Explanation: By Malus's Law, I = I₀ cos²(90°) = I₀ × 0 = 0. When two polarizers are crossed at 90°, no light passes through the second polarizer.

  Q5. How do polarizing sunglasses work?
    [ ] They block all light equally
    [*] They block horizontally polarized light (glare)
    [ ] They change light color
    [ ] They increase brightness
    Explanation: Glare from surfaces like water and roads is mostly horizontally polarized. Polarizing sunglasses have vertically oriented filters that block horizontally polarized light while allowing vertically polarized light through.

  Q6. State Malus's Law.
    [ ] I = I₀/cos² θ
    [ ] I = I₀ cos θ
    [ ] I = I₀ sin² θ
    [*] I = I₀ cos² θ
    Explanation: Malus's Law: I = I₀ cos² θ, where I₀ is the intensity of polarized light entering the analyzer and θ is the angle between the polarization direction and the analyzer axis.

  Q7. Name three methods of polarization.
    [ ] Gravity, magnetism, friction
    [ ] Refraction, diffraction, absorption
    [*] Filters, reflection, and scattering
    [ ] Only filters can polarize
    Explanation: Light can be polarized by three main methods: absorption (polarizing filters), reflection (Brewster's angle), and scattering (such as sunlight scattered by the atmosphere).

  Q8. What fraction of unpolarized light passes through a single ideal polarizer?
    [ ] All of the original light intensity
    [*] One half of the original light intensity
    [ ] One quarter of the original light intensity
    [ ] None of the original light passes through
    Explanation: When unpolarized light passes through a single ideal polarizer, exactly half (50%) of the intensity is transmitted. The polarizer blocks all components not aligned with its transmission axis.

  Q9. What is Brewster's angle?
    [*] The angle at which reflected light is completely polarized
    [ ] The angle at which all light is absorbed by a surface
    [ ] The angle at which total internal reflection begins
    [ ] The angle at which light changes color upon reflection
    Explanation: Brewster's angle is the specific angle of incidence at which reflected light becomes completely polarized. At this angle, the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular to each other (tan θ_B = n₂/n₁).

  Q10. When two polarizers are aligned (θ = 0°), what happens to polarized light?
    [ ] No light passes through the second polarizer
    [ ] Only half the light passes through the second polarizer
    [*] All the polarized light passes through the second polarizer
    [ ] The light becomes unpolarized after the second polarizer
    Explanation: By Malus's Law, I = I₀ cos²(0°) = I₀ × 1 = I₀. When the polarization direction is aligned with the analyzer, all polarized light passes through.

  Q11. What is a 'polarizer' in optics?
    [ ] A device that increases the intensity of all incoming light beams
    [*] A filter that transmits light vibrating in only one specific plane
    [ ] A lens that focuses parallel light rays to a single focal point
    [ ] A mirror that reflects light at exactly the same angle it arrived
    Explanation: A polarizer is an optical filter that only allows light waves vibrating in one specific plane (the transmission axis) to pass through, blocking all other vibration directions.

  Q12. What is an 'analyzer' in polarization experiments?
    [ ] The first polarizer that creates polarized light from unpolarized
    [*] A second polarizer used to test the polarization of the light
    [ ] A detector that measures the wavelength of the incoming light
    [ ] A prism that separates polarized light into different colors
    Explanation: An analyzer is a second polarizing filter placed after the first polarizer. By rotating the analyzer and observing intensity changes (Malus's Law), you can determine the polarization state of the light.

  Q13. What does 'unpolarized light' mean?
    [*] Light that vibrates in all directions perpendicular to its travel
    [ ] Light that has been filtered to vibrate in a single plane only
    [ ] Light that can only travel in a straight line without bending
    [ ] Light that has lost all of its energy and stopped propagating
    Explanation: Unpolarized light has electric field vibrations in all directions perpendicular to its direction of travel. Most natural light sources (the sun, light bulbs) produce unpolarized light.

  Q14. What is the 'transmission axis' of a polarizer?
    [*] The direction along which the polarizer allows light to pass through
    [ ] The direction in which the polarizer blocks all incoming light waves
    [ ] The physical thickness of the polarizer measured through its center
    [ ] The angle at which light must arrive for the polarizer to work
    Explanation: The transmission axis is the direction along which a polarizer allows the electric field component of light to pass through. Components perpendicular to this axis are blocked.

  Q15. What does the term 'glare' refer to in everyday optics?
    [ ] Any bright light that comes from an overhead light source directly
    [*] Intense reflected light, often horizontally polarized, from flat surfaces
    [ ] The colored halos seen around bright lights due to diffraction
    [ ] The dimming of light as it passes through a thick glass window
    Explanation: Glare is bright, harsh reflected light, often from flat surfaces like water, roads, or snow. This reflected light is predominantly horizontally polarized, which is why polarizing sunglasses (with vertical filters) effectively reduce it.

  Q16. What does 'transverse wave' mean?
    [ ] A wave where oscillations are parallel to the direction of travel
    [*] A wave where oscillations are perpendicular to the direction of travel
    [ ] A wave that can only travel through solid materials and not fluids
    [ ] A wave that carries no energy as it moves through a medium
    Explanation: A transverse wave has oscillations (vibrations) perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. Light is a transverse electromagnetic wave, which is why it can be polarized.

  Q17. What is meant by 'intensity' of light?
    [ ] The color of light determined by its wavelength value
    [*] The power of light per unit area, related to its brightness
    [ ] The speed at which light travels through a particular medium
    [ ] The direction in which a light wave is vibrating or oscillating
    Explanation: Light intensity is the power (energy per second) delivered per unit area, measured in watts per square meter (W/m²). It corresponds to perceived brightness. Malus's Law describes how intensity changes through a polarizer.

  Q18. What is meant by 'crossed polarizers'?
    [ ] Two polarizers placed side by side pointing in the same direction
    [*] Two polarizers with transmission axes at 90° to each other
    [ ] A single polarizer that has been rotated to a 45° angle position
    [ ] Two polarizers stacked directly on top of each other at 0° angle
    Explanation: Crossed polarizers are two polarizing filters with their transmission axes oriented at 90° to each other. No light passes through this combination because cos²(90°) = 0.

  Q19. What is 'scattering' as a method of polarization?
    [*] Light being redirected in many directions by small particles, becoming partially polarized
    [ ] Light being completely absorbed by dark objects and converted to thermal energy
    [ ] Light being focused to a single point by a curved mirror or lens surface
    [ ] Light being split into component colors by passing through a glass prism
    Explanation: Scattering occurs when light interacts with small particles (like air molecules). The scattered light becomes partially polarized. This is why the sky appears polarized — it can be observed using polarizing sunglasses.

  Q20. What does 'polarization by reflection' mean?
    [ ] Light reflecting off any surface always becomes completely unpolarized
    [*] Light reflected from a surface becomes partially or fully polarized
    [ ] Only mirrors can polarize light through the reflection of light rays
    [ ] Reflected light always has a higher intensity than the incoming beam
    Explanation: When light reflects off a non-metallic surface, the reflected beam becomes partially polarized. At Brewster's angle, the reflected light is completely polarized with the electric field parallel to the surface.

  Q21. A student looks at a lake on a sunny day and sees a bright glare. They put on polarizing sunglasses and the glare disappears. Why?
    [ ] The sunglasses darken all light equally so the glare is less noticeable
    [*] The sunglasses block the horizontally polarized reflected glare selectively
    [ ] The sunglasses change the color of the glare so it blends with surroundings
    [ ] The sunglasses reflect the glare back toward the lake surface directly
    Explanation: Light reflected from a flat water surface is predominantly horizontally polarized. Polarizing sunglasses have vertically oriented transmission axes that block this horizontal polarization, effectively eliminating the glare.

  Q22. A student places two polarizers in a line. When they rotate the second one to 45°, what fraction of the originally polarized light gets through?
    [ ] Exactly one quarter of the polarized light intensity
    [*] Exactly one half of the polarized light intensity
    [ ] Exactly three quarters of the polarized light intensity
    [ ] None of the polarized light passes through at 45 degrees
    Explanation: By Malus's Law: I = I₀ cos²(45°) = I₀ × (√2/2)² = I₀ × 0.5. So exactly half the polarized light intensity passes through when the analyzer is at 45°.

  Q23. A student tries to polarize sound waves using a polarizing filter. What happens?
    [ ] The sound becomes quieter because the filter blocks half the vibrations
    [*] Nothing happens because sound is a longitudinal wave and cannot be polarized
    [ ] The sound changes pitch because the filter alters its frequency content
    [ ] The sound becomes polarized and can only travel in one direction after
    Explanation: Sound waves are longitudinal — the particles vibrate along the direction of travel. Polarization only works on transverse waves where vibrations can be restricted to a single plane perpendicular to travel.

  Q24. A photographer uses a polarizing filter on their camera while taking a photo of a building with glass windows. What effect does the filter have?
    [ ] It makes the windows brighter and more reflective in the photograph
    [*] It reduces reflections on the windows so you can see inside the building
    [ ] It changes the color of the glass windows from clear to dark blue
    [ ] It has no effect because glass cannot produce polarized light reflections
    Explanation: Light reflected off glass windows is partially polarized. A camera polarizing filter oriented to block that polarization direction reduces the reflections, allowing the camera to capture what is behind the glass.

  Q25. A student uses three polarizers in sequence: the first vertical, the second at 45°, and the third horizontal. Does any light pass through all three?
    [ ] No, because the first and third are crossed at 90° and block all light
    [*] Yes, because the middle polarizer at 45° allows some light to reach the third
    [ ] Yes, because three polarizers always let all light pass through completely
    [ ] No, because adding more polarizers always blocks more light progressively
    Explanation: Surprisingly, inserting a 45° polarizer between two crossed polarizers allows some light through. The 45° filter rotates the polarization direction, giving a component that can pass through the third filter. The transmitted intensity is I₀ × cos²(45°) × cos²(45°) = I₀/4.

  Q26. A student notices that the sky appears darker through polarizing sunglasses when looking at 90° from the sun. Why?
    [ ] The sunglasses tint makes all parts of the sky equally darker overall
    [*] Scattered skylight at 90° from the sun is strongly polarized and gets blocked
    [ ] The atmosphere is thicker when looking at 90° from the sun direction
    [ ] The sunglasses refract skylight so that less reaches the observer's eyes
    Explanation: Sunlight scattered by atmospheric molecules at 90° from the sun is strongly polarized. When polarizing sunglasses are oriented to block this polarization, the sky at that angle appears notably darker.

  Q27. In a lab, a student passes polarized light through an analyzer. As they slowly rotate the analyzer from 0° to 90°, what do they observe?
    [ ] The light intensity stays constant throughout the entire rotation
    [*] The light gradually dims and reaches zero at 90° rotation angle
    [ ] The light color changes from red to blue as the angle increases
    [ ] The light suddenly disappears at exactly 45° rotation angle
    Explanation: According to Malus's Law (I = I₀ cos² θ), the intensity decreases smoothly as the angle increases from 0° to 90°. At 0° full intensity passes; at 90° no light passes through.

  Q28. A student learns that LCD screens use polarizers. What role do the polarizers play in displaying images?
    [ ] They increase the brightness of the screen backlight to visible levels
    [*] They control which areas of the screen appear bright or dark by rotating light
    [ ] They create the colors displayed on the screen by filtering wavelengths
    [ ] They protect the screen from scratches and external physical damage
    Explanation: LCD screens use two crossed polarizers with a liquid crystal layer between them. The liquid crystals rotate the polarization of light. By controlling this rotation electrically, each pixel can be switched between bright (light passes) and dark (light blocked).

  Q29. A student is fishing and wants to see fish below the water surface. Would polarizing sunglasses help, and why?
    [ ] No, polarizing sunglasses only work for blocking direct sunlight beams
    [*] Yes, they block the polarized surface glare allowing you to see beneath the water
    [ ] No, water does not produce any polarized light reflections from its surface
    [ ] Yes, they magnify the water and make the fish appear larger and closer
    Explanation: Light reflected off the water surface creates polarized glare that obscures what is below. Polarizing sunglasses block this horizontally polarized glare, allowing the fisher to see through the surface and spot fish.

  Q30. A student calculates that polarized light passes through an analyzer at 60°. What fraction of the light intensity gets through?
    [ ] Three quarters of the initial polarized light intensity
    [ ] One half of the initial polarized light intensity
    [*] One quarter of the initial polarized light intensity
    [ ] One eighth of the initial polarized light intensity
    Explanation: By Malus's Law: I = I₀ cos²(60°) = I₀ × (0.5)² = I₀ × 0.25 = I₀/4. So one quarter (25%) of the polarized light intensity passes through the analyzer at 60°.
