  Key Concepts: Cellular Reproduction
Key Concepts: Cellular Reproduction

This lesson covers the fundamental concepts of cellular reproduction in biology.

  • Understanding the basic principles and terminology
  • Exploring real-world applications and examples
  • Connecting to other biological concepts

Learning Objectives

  • Define key terms related to cellular reproduction
  • Explain the importance of this topic in biology
  • Apply concepts to solve problems

Why This Matters

  • Builds foundation for advanced biology topics
  • Has practical applications in medicine and research

--- Lesson 9.1:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What are the two main purposes of cell division in living organisms?
    [ ] Only to produce gametes
    [*] Growth and repair of tissues (via mitosis), and sexual reproduction (via meiosis)
    [ ] Only to increase cell size
    [ ] To create energy for the organism
    Explanation: Cell division serves two major purposes: (1) mitosis produces genetically identical cells for growth, development, and repair of tissues, and (2) meiosis produces gametes (sperm and egg) with half the chromosome number for sexual reproduction.

  Q2. What are the three stages of interphase, and what happens during each?
    [*] G₁ (cell growth and normal functions), S (DNA synthesis/replication), G₂ (final growth and preparation for mitosis)
    [ ] Prophase, metaphase, anaphase
    [ ] G₁, G₂, G₃
    [ ] S₁, S₂, S₃
    Explanation: Interphase is the longest part of the cell cycle (~90% of time). G₁ (Gap 1): the cell grows, produces proteins, and carries out normal functions. S (Synthesis): DNA is replicated so each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids. G₂ (Gap 2): the cell continues to grow and synthesizes proteins needed for mitosis (e.g., tubulin for spindle fibers).

  Q3. During which phase of interphase does DNA replication occur?
    [ ] G₁ phase
    [*] S (synthesis) phase — each chromosome is copied to form two identical sister chromatids
    [ ] G₂ phase
    [ ] M phase
    Explanation: DNA replication occurs during the S (synthesis) phase of interphase. Before S phase, each chromosome consists of a single DNA molecule. After S phase, each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. This ensures each daughter cell will receive a complete copy of the genome.

  Q4. How many chromosomes are in a typical human somatic (body) cell?
    [ ] 23
    [*] 46 chromosomes (23 pairs — 22 pairs of autosomes plus 1 pair of sex chromosomes)
    [ ] 92
    [ ] 12
    Explanation: Human somatic cells are diploid (2n = 46), containing 23 pairs of chromosomes. Of these, 22 pairs are autosomes (non-sex chromosomes) and 1 pair are sex chromosomes (XX in females, XY in males). Gametes (sperm and egg) are haploid (n = 23), containing one chromosome from each pair.

  Q5. What are sister chromatids, and how are they connected?
    [ ] Different chromosomes from different parents
    [*] Two identical copies of a single chromosome, formed during S phase and joined at the centromere
    [ ] The two strands of a DNA double helix
    [ ] Unrelated fragments of RNA
    Explanation: Sister chromatids are identical copies of a single replicated chromosome, produced during S phase of interphase. They are joined at a constricted region called the centromere, which serves as the attachment point for spindle fibers during mitosis. Sister chromatids are separated during anaphase and distributed to daughter cells.

  Q6. How does cytokinesis differ between animal cells and plant cells?
    [*] Animal cells: the cell membrane pinches inward (cleavage furrow); Plant cells: vesicles fuse to form a cell plate that becomes a new cell wall between daughter cells
    [ ] Both use a cleavage furrow
    [ ] Both form a cell plate
    [ ] Animal cells form a cell plate; plant cells use a cleavage furrow
    Explanation: In animal cells, a contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments pinches the membrane inward, forming a cleavage furrow that deepens until the cell splits. In plant cells, the rigid cell wall prevents pinching, so Golgi-derived vesicles fuse at the midline to form a cell plate that develops into a new cell wall separating the daughter cells.

  Q7. What type of cell division do prokaryotes (bacteria) use to reproduce?
    [ ] Mitosis with a spindle apparatus
    [*] Binary fission — the circular DNA replicates, the cell elongates, and a septum divides the cell into two identical daughter cells
    [ ] Meiosis producing haploid cells
    [ ] Budding exclusively
    Explanation: Prokaryotes lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, so they cannot perform mitosis. Instead, they reproduce by binary fission: the single circular chromosome replicates, the two copies attach to the plasma membrane and move apart as the cell elongates, and a septum forms at the midpoint to divide the cell into two genetically identical daughter cells.

  Q8. What is the key advantage of sexual reproduction compared to asexual reproduction?
    [ ] It is faster and requires fewer resources
    [*] It produces genetic variation among offspring, increasing a population's ability to adapt to changing environments
    [ ] It always produces more offspring
    [ ] It requires only one parent
    Explanation: Sexual reproduction combines genetic material from two parents through meiosis and fertilization, creating offspring with unique combinations of alleles. This genetic variation is the raw material for natural selection, allowing populations to adapt to new diseases, predators, and environmental changes. Asexual reproduction is faster but produces clones with no genetic diversity.

  Q9. What happens to a cell's chromosome number after one complete round of mitosis and cytokinesis?
    [ ] The chromosome number is halved
    [*] The chromosome number stays the same — each daughter cell receives the identical set of chromosomes as the parent cell
    [ ] The chromosome number doubles
    [ ] The number varies randomly
    Explanation: Mitosis is an equational division — it produces two daughter cells that are genetically identical to the parent cell. Although DNA is duplicated during S phase (creating sister chromatids), the separation of chromatids during anaphase ensures each daughter cell receives exactly the same number and type of chromosomes as the original cell (2n → 2n in humans: 46 → 46).

  Q10. What are some methods of asexual reproduction found in living organisms?
    [*] Binary fission, budding, fragmentation, and vegetative propagation — all producing genetically identical offspring
    [ ] Only meiosis and fertilization
    [ ] Only mitosis in multicellular organisms
    [ ] Crossing over and independent assortment
    Explanation: Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring (clones) from a single parent. Methods include binary fission (bacteria), budding (yeast, hydra), fragmentation (starfish, flatworms — a piece breaks off and regenerates), and vegetative propagation (plants producing new individuals from roots, stems, or leaves). All bypass meiosis and fertilization.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes the 'cell cycle'?
    [ ] The process of DNA unwinding for transcription
    [*] The ordered sequence of events from one cell division to the next, consisting of interphase (G₁, S, G₂) and M phase (mitosis and cytokinesis)
    [ ] The movement of cells through the bloodstream
    [ ] The recycling of dead cells by the immune system
    Explanation: The cell cycle is a precisely regulated sequence of events that a cell undergoes from one division to the next. It consists of interphase (G₁ for growth, S for DNA synthesis, G₂ for preparation) and M phase (mitosis — division of the nucleus — followed by cytokinesis — division of the cytoplasm). Checkpoints at G₁/S, G₂/M, and the spindle assembly checkpoint ensure accuracy.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes 'interphase'?
    [ ] The phase when the cell is actively dividing its nucleus
    [*] The longest phase of the cell cycle during which the cell grows, carries out normal functions, and replicates its DNA in preparation for division (G₁ → S → G₂)
    [ ] A phase where the cell shrinks and prepares to die
    [ ] The brief moment when chromosomes are visible under a microscope
    Explanation: Interphase occupies approximately 90% of the cell cycle. Despite once being called a 'resting phase,' the cell is highly active — synthesizing proteins, growing organelles (G₁), replicating all DNA (S phase), and producing materials needed for mitosis like tubulin (G₂). The cell is NOT dividing during interphase.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes a 'chromosome'?
    [ ] A single strand of RNA found in the cytoplasm
    [*] A tightly coiled structure of DNA wrapped around histone proteins, visible during cell division; humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) in each somatic cell
    [ ] A protein that catalyzes chemical reactions
    [ ] A membrane-bound organelle that produces energy
    Explanation: Chromosomes are structures made of DNA tightly wound around histone proteins, forming chromatin that condenses into visible chromosomes during cell division. Each human somatic cell contains 46 chromosomes (22 pairs of autosomes + 1 pair of sex chromosomes). Before S phase, each chromosome is a single chromatid; after S phase, each is two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes a 'chromatid'?
    [ ] A type of pigment molecule in chloroplasts
    [*] One copy of a duplicated chromosome; after DNA replication, two identical sister chromatids are joined at the centromere until they are separated during anaphase
    [ ] A permanent structural component of the nucleus
    [ ] A type of cell found only in bone marrow
    Explanation: After DNA replication in S phase, each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids connected at the centromere. During anaphase of mitosis, the centromere splits and the sister chromatids are pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibers. Once separated, each chromatid is considered an individual chromosome.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes the 'centromere'?
    [ ] The center of the cell where the nucleus is located
    [*] The specialized constricted region of a chromosome where the two sister chromatids are joined, and where kinetochore proteins assemble to attach spindle fibers during cell division
    [ ] The central vacuole of a plant cell
    [ ] The point where a nerve impulse jumps between neurons
    Explanation: The centromere is the constricted region of a chromosome where sister chromatids are held together by cohesin proteins. Kinetochore protein complexes assemble on the centromere and serve as the attachment site for spindle microtubules. Proper centromere–kinetochore–spindle attachment is monitored by the spindle assembly checkpoint to prevent chromosome missegregation.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes 'cytokinesis'?
    [ ] The process of DNA replication during S phase
    [*] The division of the cytoplasm following nuclear division (mitosis), resulting in two separate daughter cells — accomplished by a cleavage furrow in animal cells or a cell plate in plant cells
    [ ] The condensation of chromatin into chromosomes
    [ ] The alignment of chromosomes at the metaphase plate
    Explanation: Cytokinesis is the physical division of the cytoplasm that follows mitosis (karyokinesis). In animal cells, a contractile ring of actin and myosin creates a cleavage furrow that pinches the cell in two. In plant cells, Golgi-derived vesicles fuse at the cell equator to form a cell plate that develops into a new cell wall.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes 'binary fission'?
    [ ] A type of nuclear division that produces four haploid cells
    [*] The method of asexual reproduction used by prokaryotes, in which the circular DNA is replicated, the cell elongates, and a septum divides the cell into two genetically identical daughter cells
    [ ] The separation of sister chromatids during anaphase
    [ ] The fusion of two gametes to form a zygote
    Explanation: Binary fission is how bacteria and archaea reproduce. The single circular chromosome attaches to the plasma membrane, replicates, and the two copies are separated as the cell elongates. A septum (cross-wall) then forms to divide the cell into two identical daughter cells. This process is simpler and faster than mitosis — some bacteria can divide every 20 minutes.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes 'chromatin'?
    [ ] The tightly condensed, visible form of DNA seen during cell division
    [*] The complex of DNA wrapped around histone proteins found in the nucleus during interphase; it exists in a less condensed (extended) form that allows gene expression and DNA replication
    [ ] A pigment molecule in the chloroplast that absorbs light
    [ ] A type of RNA that carries genetic information
    Explanation: Chromatin is the DNA-histone protein complex that fills the nucleus during interphase. In its extended (euchromatin) form, genes can be accessed for transcription and the DNA can be replicated during S phase. When the cell enters mitosis, chromatin condenses into the compact, visible structures we call chromosomes, which allows safe distribution of DNA to daughter cells.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes 'spindle fibers'?
    [ ] Protein filaments that make up the cytoskeleton during interphase only
    [*] Microtubule structures that form during cell division, extending from centrioles (or MTOCs) to attach to kinetochores at chromosome centromeres and pull chromosomes to opposite poles of the cell
    [ ] Actin filaments responsible for muscle contraction
    [ ] RNA molecules that guide ribosomes during translation
    Explanation: Spindle fibers (mitotic spindle) are composed of microtubules — polymers of tubulin protein. They form from centrosomes (or MTOCs in plant cells) during prophase. Kinetochore microtubules attach to chromosome centromeres and shorten during anaphase to pull sister chromatids apart. The spindle assembly checkpoint ensures all chromosomes are properly attached before anaphase proceeds.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes the 'G₀ phase'?
    [ ] The gap between S phase and M phase where the cell prepares for division
    [*] A quiescent (resting) state in which a cell has exited the active cell cycle and is not preparing to divide — some cells remain in G₀ permanently (e.g., mature neurons, cardiac muscle cells)
    [ ] The initial phase of cell death (apoptosis)
    [ ] The phase when cells undergo DNA repair only
    Explanation: G₀ is a non-dividing state that cells enter when they leave G₁ of the cell cycle. Some cells (neurons, skeletal muscle fibers, red blood cells) are permanently in G₀. Others (hepatocytes, lymphocytes) are in G₀ but can re-enter the cell cycle in response to signals such as growth factors or tissue damage.

  Q21. A child scrapes their knee, removing a patch of skin cells. Over the following days, the wound closes and new skin cells fill the gap. What cellular process is primarily responsible for this tissue repair, and what phase of the cell cycle must the surrounding cells enter?
    [ ] Meiosis; the cells need to produce gametes to heal the wound
    [*] Mitosis; surrounding cells that were in G₀ or G₁ are stimulated by growth factors to re-enter the cell cycle and divide, producing genetically identical replacement cells
    [ ] Binary fission; skin cells split like bacteria
    [ ] Apoptosis; damaged cells die and are not replaced
    Explanation: Wound healing relies on mitosis. When skin is damaged, platelets form a clot, immune cells clear debris, and growth factors (like EGF and PDGF) stimulate neighboring epithelial cells to leave G₀/G₁ and re-enter the cell cycle. These cells undergo mitosis to produce genetically identical daughter cells that migrate to fill the wound, restoring the tissue barrier.

  Q22. A biopsy reveals that a patient's lung cells are dividing uncontrollably, ignoring signals to stop. The cells have a mutated p53 gene (a tumor suppressor) and show abnormal chromosome numbers. What has gone wrong with the cell cycle, and why does this lead to cancer?
    [ ] The cells are in G₀ phase and cannot divide
    Explanation: Cancer results from accumulated mutations in cell cycle regulatory genes. The p53 protein normally acts as the 'guardian of the genome' — at the G₁/S checkpoint, p53 halts division when DNA damage is detected so repairs can be made, or triggers apoptosis if the damage is irreparable. With p53 mutated, damaged cells skip this checkpoint and continue dividing, accumulating chromosomal abnormalities (aneuploidy). Multiple mutations in tumor suppressors and proto-oncogenes (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis) are typically required for full cancer development.

  Q23. After a patient undergoes partial hepatectomy (surgical removal of ~60% of their liver), the remaining liver regenerates to nearly its original mass within weeks. What cellular mechanism drives this remarkable regeneration, and what signals initiate it?
    [ ] Meiosis; the liver produces gametes that develop into new tissue
    [*] Mitosis; hepatocytes (normally in G₀) re-enter the cell cycle in response to growth factors like HGF (hepatocyte growth factor) and EGF (epidermal growth factor), undergoing compensatory hyperplasia
    [ ] Stem cells from bone marrow replace the lost liver tissue
    [ ] The remaining liver cells simply enlarge without dividing
    Explanation: Liver regeneration is a classic example of compensatory hyperplasia. After hepatectomy, cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) prime hepatocytes, and growth factors (HGF, EGF, TGF-α) stimulate them to leave G₀ and re-enter the cell cycle. Hepatocytes undergo one or two rounds of mitosis — enough to restore the original liver mass. Once the proper mass is reached, TGF-β and other inhibitory signals halt further division.

  Q24. A cytogeneticist adds colchicine to a blood sample to prepare a karyotype. The cells are arrested in metaphase with condensed, visible chromosomes. How does colchicine achieve this, and why is metaphase arrest useful for karyotyping?
    [ ] Colchicine speeds up cell division so more chromosomes can be photographed
    [*] Colchicine binds tubulin and prevents spindle microtubule assembly, arresting cells in metaphase when chromosomes are maximally condensed and aligned — ideal for photographing and arranging into a karyotype
    [ ] Colchicine stains chromosomes so they glow under UV light
    [ ] Colchicine causes chromosomes to replicate extra copies for easier counting
    Explanation: Colchicine blocks microtubule polymerization by binding free tubulin dimers, preventing spindle formation. Without a spindle, the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) is never satisfied, so cells remain arrested in metaphase — the stage when chromosomes are most condensed and visible. The arrested cells are then fixed, stained (e.g., Giemsa banding), and photographed to create a karyotype for detecting chromosomal abnormalities like trisomies or translocations.

  Q25. A starfish loses an arm to a predator and regenerates a complete new arm over several months. What role does mitosis play in this regeneration, and how does it differ from the way humans heal wounds?
    [ ] The starfish uses meiosis to produce new arm cells from gametes
    [*] Mitosis at the wound site produces a blastema (mass of undifferentiated cells) that then differentiates into all the tissues of a new arm — unlike humans, who can repair tissues but cannot regenerate entire limbs
    [ ] The arm regrows by cell enlargement without any cell division
    [ ] New cells arrive from other parts of the starfish's body to rebuild the arm
    Explanation: Starfish (sea stars) demonstrate epimorphic regeneration. After amputation, cells at the wound site dedifferentiate and proliferate via mitosis to form a blastema — an undifferentiated cell mass. Blastema cells then redifferentiate into muscle, nerve, skeletal, and other tissues, guided by positional signaling molecules. Humans lack this ability for complex structures; our wound healing primarily involves mitosis of existing differentiated cells to close wounds and form scar tissue.

  Q26. A cancer patient is treated with vincristine, a chemotherapy drug derived from the periwinkle plant. How does vincristine disrupt cancer cell division, and how does its mechanism compare to colchicine?
    [ ] Vincristine destroys the cell's DNA so it cannot replicate
    [*] Like colchicine, vincristine binds tubulin and prevents microtubule assembly, blocking spindle formation and arresting cells in mitosis — the spindle assembly checkpoint triggers apoptosis in the arrested cancer cells
    [ ] Vincristine stabilizes microtubules so they cannot disassemble, which is the opposite of colchicine
    [ ] Vincristine targets only the G₁ phase of the cell cycle
    Explanation: Vincristine (a vinca alkaloid) binds tubulin dimers at a different site than colchicine but with the same effect — preventing microtubule polymerization and blocking spindle assembly. Without a functional spindle, dividing cells arrest in mitosis and the prolonged SAC activation ultimately triggers apoptosis. Note: Taxol (paclitaxel) works OPPOSITELY — it stabilizes microtubules, preventing their disassembly, which also blocks mitosis. Both approaches exploit the cell's dependence on dynamic microtubules for division.

  Q27. Henrietta Lacks' cervical cancer cells (HeLa cells) have been dividing in laboratory culture continuously since 1951 — over 70 years. Normal human cells stop dividing after about 50 divisions (the Hayflick limit). What allows HeLa cells to bypass this limit?
    [ ] HeLa cells do not need nutrients to divide
    [*] HeLa cells express high levels of telomerase, an enzyme that rebuilds telomeres after each division, preventing the shortening that normally triggers cellular senescence after ~50 divisions
    [ ] The cells undergo meiosis instead of mitosis, which allows unlimited division
    [ ] HeLa cells have fewer chromosomes, making division easier
    Explanation: Normal somatic cells have low or no telomerase activity. With each mitotic division, telomeres (protective TTAGGG repeat caps on chromosome ends) shorten because DNA polymerase cannot fully replicate the 3' end. After ~50 divisions (Hayflick limit), critically short telomeres trigger p53-mediated senescence or apoptosis. Cancer cells like HeLa reactivate telomerase, which adds telomere repeats after each division, enabling unlimited proliferation — a hallmark of cancer (immortalization).

  Q28. The human body produces approximately 200 billion red blood cells every day. These cells originate from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow. What role does the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) play in ensuring these rapidly dividing cells maintain the correct chromosome number?
    [ ] The SAC speeds up cell division to meet the demand for new red blood cells
    [*] The SAC monitors kinetochore-microtubule attachments during metaphase and delays anaphase onset until every chromosome is properly attached to spindle fibers from both poles, preventing aneuploidy in rapidly dividing bone marrow cells
    [ ] The SAC determines which cells become red blood cells versus white blood cells
    [ ] The SAC removes excess chromosomes from cells that have divided too many times
    Explanation: The spindle assembly checkpoint is critical in rapidly dividing tissues like bone marrow. The SAC protein complex (Mad1, Mad2, BubR1, Bub3) monitors each kinetochore. Unattached or incorrectly attached kinetochores generate a 'wait' signal that inhibits the anaphase-promoting complex (APC/C), preventing separation of sister chromatids. Only when ALL kinetochores achieve proper bipolar attachment does the SAC signal dissipate, allowing APC/C to trigger anaphase.

  Q29. A gardener takes a cutting from a prize-winning rose bush and grows an entirely new, genetically identical plant. What type of reproduction is this, what cellular process underlies the growth, and how does the genetic outcome compare to growing roses from seeds?
    [ ] Sexual reproduction using meiosis; the new plant is genetically unique
    [*] Asexual reproduction (vegetative propagation) using mitosis; the new plant is a genetic clone of the parent, unlike seed-grown roses which are genetically unique due to meiosis and fertilization
    [ ] Asexual reproduction using meiosis; the new plant has half the chromosomes
    [ ] Sexual reproduction using mitosis; the new plant is identical to both parents
    Explanation: Vegetative propagation (cuttings, grafting, runners, tubers) is asexual reproduction — new individuals arise from mitotic divisions of somatic cells, producing genetic clones (identical genotype). Seeds result from sexual reproduction (meiosis → gametes → fertilization), producing genetically unique offspring due to crossing over and independent assortment. Cloning via cuttings preserves desirable traits but reduces genetic diversity, making clonal populations vulnerable to disease.

  Q30. A single E. coli bacterium is placed in a nutrient-rich medium at 37°C. The bacterium divides by binary fission every 20 minutes. How many bacteria will be present after 4 hours (12 generations), and why might antibiotic resistance spread rapidly through a bacterial population that reproduces this way?
    [ ] 24 bacteria; mutations cannot occur during binary fission
    [*] 2¹² = 4,096 bacteria; because binary fission produces genetically identical copies, a single mutation conferring antibiotic resistance is passed to ALL descendants, rapidly producing a resistant population
    [ ] 12 bacteria; each generation adds one new cell
    [ ] 4,096 bacteria; but resistance cannot spread because bacteria reproduce asexually
    Explanation: Binary fission doubles the population with each generation: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → ... → 2¹² = 4,096 after 12 generations (4 hours). Since binary fission is asexual (no genetic recombination), daughter cells are clones. If one bacterium acquires an antibiotic resistance mutation (via random DNA replication error), all its descendants inherit the resistance. Additionally, bacteria can share resistance genes through horizontal gene transfer (conjugation, transformation, transduction), further accelerating resistance spread.

--- Lesson 9.2:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What type of cells does mitosis produce, and how many?
    [ ] Four genetically different haploid cells
    [*] Two genetically identical daughter nuclei, each with the same chromosome number as the parent cell
    [ ] One larger cell with double the DNA
    [ ] Two cells with half the chromosome number
    Explanation: Mitosis is an equational division of the nucleus that produces two daughter nuclei genetically identical to the parent. In humans, each daughter cell has 46 chromosomes (2n = 46), the same as the original cell. Mitosis maintains chromosome number and genetic identity during growth and repair.

  Q2. What is the correct order of the four phases of mitosis?
    [ ] Metaphase, Anaphase, Prophase, Telophase
    [*] Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase (PMAT)
    [ ] Telophase, Prophase, Anaphase, Metaphase
    [ ] Anaphase, Telophase, Prophase, Metaphase
    Explanation: The mnemonic PMAT helps remember the order: Prophase (chromosomes condense, spindle forms), Metaphase (chromosomes align at the equator), Anaphase (sister chromatids separate), Telophase (nuclear envelopes reform). Cytokinesis (cytoplasm division) typically overlaps with telophase.

  Q3. What three key events occur during prophase of mitosis?
    [*] Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the nuclear envelope begins to break down, and spindle fibers start forming from the centrosomes
    [ ] DNA replicates, the cell plate forms, and chromosomes align
    [ ] Sister chromatids separate, the cell elongates, and cytokinesis begins
    [ ] Nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense, and nucleoli reappear
    Explanation: Prophase is the first stage of mitosis. Chromatin fibers coil and condense into compact, visible chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids). The centrosomes migrate to opposite poles and begin assembling the mitotic spindle from microtubules. The nuclear envelope breaks apart (prometaphase), allowing spindle fibers to access the chromosomes.

  Q4. During metaphase, where are the chromosomes positioned in the cell?
    [ ] Scattered randomly throughout the cytoplasm
    [*] Aligned along the cell's equator (metaphase plate), with kinetochore fibers attached to centromeres from both poles
    [ ] Clustered at one pole of the cell
    [ ] Inside the nucleolus
    Explanation: During metaphase, spindle fibers attach to the kinetochores on each chromosome's centromere and align all chromosomes along the metaphase plate (the imaginary equatorial plane of the cell). This precise alignment ensures that when sister chromatids separate in anaphase, each pole receives one copy of every chromosome.

  Q5. What happens during anaphase of mitosis?
    [ ] Chromosomes condense and become visible
    [*] Sister chromatids separate at the centromere and are pulled by shortening spindle fibers to opposite poles of the cell
    [ ] Nuclear envelopes reform around each chromosome set
    [ ] DNA replication occurs
    Explanation: Anaphase is the shortest and most dramatic phase of mitosis. The enzyme separase cleaves cohesin proteins holding sister chromatids together at the centromere. Motor proteins at the kinetochores walk along shortening microtubules, pulling the now-individual chromosomes to opposite poles. The cell also elongates as non-kinetochore microtubules push the poles apart.

  Q6. What happens during telophase, and what follows it?
    [ ] DNA replicates and the cell prepares for another division
    [*] Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes, chromosomes decondense back to chromatin, and nucleoli reappear — cytokinesis then divides the cytoplasm into two daughter cells
    [ ] Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles
    [ ] Chromosomes condense and the spindle forms
    Explanation: Telophase is essentially the reverse of prophase. Nuclear envelopes reassemble around each set of chromosomes using membrane fragments from the ER. Chromosomes decondense back into chromatin, nucleoli reappear, and the spindle disassembles. Cytokinesis (cleavage furrow in animals, cell plate in plants) typically overlaps with telophase to complete cell division.

  Q7. What are spindle fibers made of, and what structure organizes them in animal cells?
    [ ] Actin filaments organized by the nucleus
    [*] Microtubules (polymers of tubulin protein) organized by centrosomes containing centrioles
    [ ] Intermediate filaments organized by the Golgi apparatus
    [ ] Myosin filaments organized by ribosomes
    Explanation: Spindle fibers are microtubules assembled from tubulin protein dimers. In animal cells, centrosomes (each containing two centrioles) serve as microtubule-organizing centers (MTOCs) that nucleate and organize spindle fibers. Three types exist: kinetochore microtubules (attach to chromosomes), polar microtubules (overlap at the midzone), and astral microtubules (anchor the spindle to the cell cortex).

  Q8. After mitosis and cytokinesis, how many chromosomes does each human daughter cell contain?
    [ ] 23 chromosomes (haploid)
    [*] 46 chromosomes (diploid) — the same number as the parent cell, because mitosis is an equational division
    [ ] 92 chromosomes (tetraploid)
    [ ] The number varies between daughter cells
    Explanation: Mitosis maintains the chromosome number. During S phase, each of the 46 chromosomes is replicated to form sister chromatids. In anaphase, sister chromatids separate, ensuring each daughter cell receives exactly 46 chromosomes (2n = 46). This is fundamentally different from meiosis, which reduces the chromosome number to 23 (n = 23).

  Q9. Why is mitosis essential for multicellular organisms?
    [ ] It produces gametes for sexual reproduction
    [*] It enables growth (increasing cell number), tissue repair (replacing damaged cells), and maintenance (replacing worn-out cells like skin and blood cells)
    [ ] It is used only during embryonic development
    [ ] It decreases the number of cells in the body
    Explanation: Mitosis is critical throughout an organism's life. During development, it increases cell number from a single zygote to trillions of cells. In adults, it replaces cells that wear out (skin cells every 2-3 weeks, red blood cells every 120 days) and repairs damaged tissues after injury.

  Q10. What can happen if errors occur during mitosis?
    [ ] The cell always functions normally regardless of errors
    [*] Errors can result in daughter cells with abnormal chromosome numbers (aneuploidy), cell death (apoptosis), or uncontrolled cell division (potentially leading to cancer)
    [ ] Errors make cells stronger and more resistant to disease
    [ ] Errors are always corrected before the cell divides
    Explanation: Mitotic errors can have serious consequences. Nondisjunction creates aneuploid cells with too many or too few chromosomes. If cell cycle checkpoints fail to catch errors, daughter cells may accumulate mutations that disable growth controls — a key step in cancer development. Cells with severe errors typically undergo apoptosis (programmed cell death).

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes 'mitosis'?
    [ ] A type of cell division that produces four haploid gametes
    [*] The division of a cell's nucleus into two genetically identical daughter nuclei, consisting of prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase (followed by cytokinesis to produce two complete cells)
    [ ] The replication of DNA during S phase of interphase
    [ ] The division of cytoplasm between two daughter cells
    Explanation: Mitosis is specifically the division of the nucleus (karyokinesis). It encompasses four sequential phases: prophase (condensation), metaphase (alignment), anaphase (separation), and telophase (nuclear reformation). Cytokinesis, the division of the cytoplasm, is a separate but usually concurrent process. Together, mitosis and cytokinesis produce two genetically identical daughter cells.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes 'prophase'?
    [ ] The phase when chromosomes align at the metaphase plate
    [*] The first phase of mitosis during which chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the mitotic spindle begins to form from the centrosomes, and the nuclear envelope starts to break down
    [ ] The phase when sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles
    [ ] The phase when the cell grows and replicates its DNA
    Explanation: Prophase marks the beginning of mitosis. Three major events occur: (1) Chromatin condenses into compact chromosomes visible under a light microscope, (2) Centrosomes migrate toward opposite poles and begin nucleating spindle microtubules, and (3) The nuclear envelope fragments into vesicles (prometaphase), allowing spindle fibers to access chromosomes.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes 'metaphase'?
    [ ] The phase when chromosomes first become visible
    [*] The phase of mitosis during which all chromosomes are aligned along the cell's equator (metaphase plate), with kinetochore microtubules from both poles attached to each chromosome's centromere
    [ ] The phase when the nuclear envelope reforms
    [ ] The phase when DNA is being replicated
    Explanation: Metaphase ensures accurate chromosome distribution. Each chromosome must achieve bi-orientation — with kinetochore fibers from opposite poles attached to the two sister chromatids. The spindle assembly checkpoint verifies that all chromosomes are properly attached and aligned at the metaphase plate before allowing the cell to proceed to anaphase.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes 'anaphase'?
    [ ] The phase when chromosomes condense and become visible
    [*] The phase of mitosis during which cohesin proteins are cleaved, sister chromatids separate at the centromere, and shortening spindle fibers pull the individual chromosomes to opposite poles of the cell
    [ ] The phase when chromosomes line up at the equator
    [ ] The phase when cytokinesis is completed
    Explanation: Anaphase is the shortest and most dramatic phase of mitosis. The anaphase-promoting complex (APC/C) activates separase, which cleaves cohesin proteins holding sister chromatids together. Kinetochore microtubules shorten (depolymerize) at the kinetochore end, pulling chromatids poleward (Anaphase A). Simultaneously, polar microtubules lengthen and push the poles apart (Anaphase B).

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes 'cytokinesis'?
    [ ] The division of the nucleus into two daughter nuclei
    [*] The division of the cytoplasm into two separate daughter cells, occurring after mitosis — in animal cells via a cleavage furrow (contractile ring of actin/myosin), and in plant cells via a cell plate (fused Golgi vesicles forming a new cell wall)
    [ ] The movement of chromosomes to opposite poles during anaphase
    [ ] The replication of organelles during interphase
    Explanation: Cytokinesis physically separates one cell into two. In animal cells, a contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments assembles beneath the plasma membrane and constricts, forming a cleavage furrow that pinches the cell in two. In plant cells, Golgi-derived vesicles fuse at the cell equator to form a cell plate that develops into a new cell wall separating the daughter cells.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes 'telophase'?
    [ ] The phase when the cell first begins to divide
    [*] The final phase of mitosis during which nuclear envelopes reform around each set of separated chromosomes, chromosomes decondense back into chromatin, nucleoli reappear, and the mitotic spindle disassembles
    [ ] The phase when DNA is being replicated in preparation for division
    [ ] The phase when the cytoplasm divides
    Explanation: Telophase reverses the events of prophase. Nuclear envelope fragments reassemble around each chromosome set, chromosomes uncoil to resume gene expression, nucleoli (sites of ribosomal RNA synthesis) become visible again, and spindle microtubules depolymerize. By the end of telophase, two distinct nuclei exist within the still-shared cytoplasm.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes the 'mitotic spindle'?
    [ ] The ring of actin and myosin that forms the cleavage furrow
    [*] A structure made of microtubules organized by centrosomes that attaches to chromosome kinetochores and is responsible for separating chromosomes to opposite poles during mitosis
    [ ] A network of DNA strands connecting chromosomes
    [ ] A protein that holds sister chromatids together
    Explanation: The mitotic spindle is an assembly of microtubules that forms during prophase from centrosomes (MTOCs). It consists of three types of microtubules: kinetochore microtubules (attach to chromosomes via kinetochores and pull them), polar/interpolar microtubules (overlap at the midzone and push poles apart), and astral microtubules (anchor the spindle to the cell cortex and help position it).

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes the 'kinetochore'?
    [ ] The region of DNA that codes for centromere proteins
    [*] A multi-protein complex that assembles on the centromere of each chromatid and serves as the attachment point for spindle microtubules, enabling chromosome movement during mitosis
    [ ] An enzyme that replicates DNA during S phase
    [ ] A membrane structure that forms the cell plate in plant cells
    Explanation: The kinetochore is a complex of over 80 proteins that assembles at the centromeric region of each chromatid. It serves as the interface between the chromosome and spindle microtubules. Motor proteins in the kinetochore (including dynein and CENP-E) generate force for chromosome movement. The spindle assembly checkpoint monitors kinetochore attachment to ensure all chromosomes are properly connected before anaphase.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes a 'cell plate'?
    [ ] The cleavage furrow formed in animal cells during cytokinesis
    [*] A structure unique to plant cell cytokinesis, formed when Golgi-derived vesicles carrying cell wall materials fuse at the center of the dividing cell to create a new cell wall and membrane between the two daughter cells
    [ ] The metaphase plate where chromosomes align
    [ ] A rigid structure surrounding all cells
    Explanation: The cell plate forms in plant cells because their rigid cell wall prevents the membrane pinching that occurs in animal cells. During telophase, vesicles from the Golgi apparatus (carrying polysaccharides and glycoproteins for wall construction) are guided by remaining phragmoplast microtubules to the cell's equator, where they fuse to form the cell plate. This grows outward until it fuses with the existing cell wall, completely separating the daughter cells.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes the 'cleavage furrow'?
    [ ] A structure that forms in plant cells during cytokinesis
    [*] An indentation on the surface of an animal cell formed by a contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments that constricts progressively during cytokinesis, pinching the cell in two
    [ ] A groove on a chromosome where the centromere is located
    [ ] The space between two adjacent cells in a tissue
    Explanation: The cleavage furrow is the mechanism of cytokinesis in animal cells. A contractile ring assembles just beneath the plasma membrane at the cell equator (positioned by signals from the mitotic spindle midzone). The ring is composed of actin filaments and myosin II motor proteins that contract, pulling the membrane inward like a drawstring purse until the cell pinches into two daughter cells.

  Q21. In a biology lab, students examine an onion root tip under a microscope and observe cells in different stages. They count 20 cells in interphase, 10 in prophase, 6 in metaphase, 3 in anaphase, and 5 in telophase. What can they conclude about the relative duration of each phase?
    [ ] All phases of mitosis take equal time
    [*] The number of cells in each phase is proportional to the time spent in that phase — interphase is longest (~45%), prophase is the longest mitotic phase (~23%), and anaphase is the shortest (~7%)
    [ ] Anaphase is the longest phase because most cells were in anaphase
    [ ] The counts are random and reveal nothing about timing
    Explanation: This is a classic mitotic index experiment. In a rapidly dividing tissue like the onion root tip meristem, the proportion of cells observed in each phase reflects the duration of that phase (assuming cells are asynchronous). More cells in interphase means it takes the longest. Among mitotic phases, prophase has the most cells (longest mitotic phase), and anaphase has the fewest (shortest phase, ~3–5 minutes).

  Q22. A patient's tumor biopsy shows cells with 92 chromosomes instead of the normal 46. The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) appears to be non-functional in these cells. How does a defective SAC contribute to this abnormality?
    [ ] The SAC normally prevents DNA replication, so without it DNA is copied too many times
    [*] The SAC normally delays anaphase until all kinetochores are properly attached to spindle fibers from both poles — without it, cells enter anaphase with misattached chromosomes, causing nondisjunction and progressive chromosome accumulation (92 = near-tetraploid)
    [ ] The SAC causes chromosomes to replicate additional copies
    [ ] SAC dysfunction only affects meiosis, not mitosis
    Explanation: The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) is a surveillance mechanism that monitors kinetochore-microtubule attachment during prometaphase/metaphase. Unattached kinetochores generate a 'wait' signal (Mad2-Cdc20 complex) that inhibits the anaphase-promoting complex (APC/C). Without a functional SAC, cells proceed to anaphase despite misattached or unattached chromosomes, resulting in unequal chromosome distribution. Over many divisions, this leads to massive aneuploidy — the 92-chromosome count suggests failed cytokinesis or repeated nondisjunction events.

  Q23. A pharmacologist is comparing two anti-cancer drugs: Taxol (paclitaxel) and colchicine. Both target microtubules but through opposite mechanisms. How do their mechanisms differ, and why do both ultimately block cell division?
    [ ] Both drugs destroy microtubules by breaking them into fragments
    [*] Colchicine prevents microtubule assembly (blocks polymerization) while Taxol prevents microtubule disassembly (stabilizes polymers) — both disrupt the dynamic instability required for spindle function, arresting cells in mitosis
    [ ] Taxol prevents DNA replication while colchicine prevents cytokinesis
    [ ] Neither drug affects microtubules; they both target the cell membrane
    Explanation: Microtubule dynamic instability (constant assembly and disassembly) is essential for spindle function. Colchicine binds free tubulin dimers, preventing polymerization — no new microtubules can form. Taxol binds assembled microtubules and prevents depolymerization — existing microtubules become rigid. Both disrupt the dynamic behavior needed for chromosome capture, alignment, and segregation. The SAC detects the resulting attachment errors and arrests cells in mitosis, ultimately triggering apoptosis.

  Q24. A student views a cell under a microscope and sees that all chromosomes are lined up in a single row at the center of the cell with spindle fibers attached. She wants to identify which phase of mitosis this cell is in. What phase should she identify, and what key evidence supports this?
    [ ] Prophase — the chromosomes are visible and the spindle is forming
    [*] Metaphase — all chromosomes are aligned at the metaphase plate (cell equator) with kinetochore fibers attached from both poles, confirming bi-orientation
    [ ] Anaphase — the chromosomes are being pulled apart
    [ ] Telophase — the chromosomes are at the center before the nucleus reforms
    Explanation: Metaphase is identified by the characteristic alignment of all chromosomes at the metaphase plate (equatorial plane). The key diagnostic features are: (1) chromosomes are maximally condensed and visible, (2) they form a single row at the cell center, and (3) kinetochore microtubules from both poles are attached to each chromosome's centromere (bi-orientation). This alignment is verified by the spindle assembly checkpoint before the cell proceeds to anaphase.

  Q25. Researchers use fluorescently labeled tubulin to study spindle dynamics in living cells. They observe that fluorescence at the kinetochore end of microtubules disappears while new fluorescent subunits are added at the centrosome end. What does this 'Pacman-kinetochore' model tell us about how chromosomes move during anaphase?
    [ ] Chromosomes are pushed away from the metaphase plate by growing microtubules
    [*] Kinetochores actively depolymerize microtubules at the attachment point — the kinetochore 'eats' the microtubule like Pac-Man, pulling the chromosome poleward as the fiber shortens from the kinetochore end
    [ ] Motor proteins on the cell membrane pull the chromosomes to the poles
    [ ] Chromosomes diffuse randomly to the poles after the spindle disassembles
    Explanation: The Pacman-kinetochore model (from fluorescence studies) shows that microtubules depolymerize primarily at the kinetochore end during anaphase A. The kinetochore complex includes depolymerizing motors (like MCAK/Kif2b) and coupling proteins (Ndc80 complex, Dam1 ring in yeast) that maintain attachment to the shortening microtubule tip. Simultaneously, flux-based mechanisms add tubulin at the centrosome end. Combined with dynein motors, this generates the force that moves chromosomes poleward at ~1-2 micrometers per minute.

  Q26. A patient with mosaic Down syndrome has some cells with 47 chromosomes (trisomy 21) and other cells with the normal 46. Unlike typical Down syndrome (caused by meiotic nondisjunction), what type of error during early embryonic development could produce this mosaic pattern?
    [ ] Crossing over during all meiotic divisions
    [*] Mitotic nondisjunction in one cell during early embryonic divisions — one daughter cell gets 3 copies of chromosome 21, the other gets 1 copy, while all other cell lineages from normal divisions retain 46 chromosomes
    [ ] Deletion of chromosome 21 in some cells
    [ ] The patient inherited different chromosome numbers from each parent
    Explanation: Mosaic Down syndrome occurs when mitotic nondisjunction happens in one cell during early embryonic development after the zygote has already begun dividing. Instead of the meiotic error that causes classic trisomy 21 (present in ALL cells), one mitotic division fails to separate the sister chromatids of chromosome 21. One daughter cell lineage becomes trisomic (47 chromosomes) while cells from unaffected divisions remain normal (46). The severity of symptoms depends on when the error occurs — earlier errors produce more trisomic cells.

  Q27. A cancer research team tests a CDK1 inhibitor drug on rapidly dividing tumor cells. After 48 hours, they find that most treated cells are arrested with condensed chromosomes but have NOT begun separating sister chromatids. At what transition point has the drug blocked the cell cycle, and why?
    [ ] G₁/S checkpoint — the cells have not replicated their DNA
    [*] The G₂/M transition — CDK1 (also called Cdc2/CDK1 or MPF) is essential for triggering entry into mitosis; its inhibition arrests cells at the boundary between G₂ and M phase with condensed chromosomes but without progression to anaphase
    [ ] Metaphase/anaphase transition — the cells are stuck at the spindle checkpoint
    [ ] Cytokinesis — the cells cannot complete division
    Explanation: CDK1 (cyclin-dependent kinase 1), when bound to Cyclin B, forms the MPF (maturation-promoting factor) complex that is the master regulator of mitotic entry. CDK1 phosphorylates dozens of substrates needed for chromosome condensation, nuclear envelope breakdown, and spindle assembly. When CDK1 is inhibited, cells can still begin chromosome condensation (initiated by other kinases like Aurora B) but cannot fully commit to mitotic progression. The arrested cells accumulate at the G₂/M boundary, making CDK1 inhibitors (like RO-3306) important research tools and potential cancer therapeutics.

  Q28. Mature red blood cells (erythrocytes) in humans cannot divide or repair themselves and have a lifespan of about 120 days. A patient with severe anemia receives erythropoietin (EPO) to stimulate red blood cell production. Where are new red blood cells produced, and why can't existing ones just divide by mitosis?
    [ ] Existing red blood cells divide slowly; EPO just speeds up their mitosis
    [*] New RBCs are produced from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow — mature erythrocytes cannot undergo mitosis because they lose their nucleus (and all DNA) during differentiation, making cell division impossible
    [ ] Red blood cells are produced in the liver and released into the blood
    [ ] White blood cells transform into red blood cells when EPO signals them
    Explanation: Mature mammalian red blood cells are among the few cell types that CANNOT undergo mitosis because they are enucleated — they expel their nucleus during erythropoiesis to maximize space for hemoglobin. Without a nucleus (no DNA), they cannot replicate chromosomes or divide. New RBCs must be continuously produced from hematopoietic stem cells in the red bone marrow. EPO (a glycoprotein hormone produced mainly by the kidneys) stimulates the proliferation and differentiation of erythroid progenitor cells in the marrow, increasing red blood cell production by 5-8 fold.

  Q29. In a biology lab, students prepare slides from an onion root tip and count cells in each phase of mitosis. Out of 200 cells observed, 120 are in interphase, 40 in prophase, 20 in metaphase, 10 in anaphase, and 10 in telophase. Students then examine a slide from a fast-growing tumor and find that 100 of 200 cells are actively in mitotic phases. What does the comparison of mitotic index (percentage of cells in mitosis) tell us?
    [ ] Both tissues divide at the same rate because they have the same number of cells
    [*] The tumor has a mitotic index of 50% versus 40% in the root tip, indicating the tumor tissue has a higher proportion of actively dividing cells — consistent with the uncontrolled cell division characteristic of cancer
    [ ] The root tip is cancerous because so many cells are dividing
    [ ] Mitotic index has no relationship to cell division rate
    Explanation: The mitotic index is calculated as (cells in mitosis / total cells counted) × 100%. For the onion root tip: 80 mitotic cells / 200 = 40%. For the tumor: 100 / 200 = 50%. A higher mitotic index indicates a greater proportion of cells are actively dividing. Pathologists use the mitotic index as a diagnostic tool — a high mitotic index in a tissue biopsy can indicate cancer or other conditions with abnormal cell proliferation. Normal tissues like root tips have high mitotic indices because they are regions of active growth (meristems).

  Q30. Frog embryos undergo extremely rapid cleavage divisions after fertilization, with the first 12 divisions occurring in about 8 hours without any cell growth between divisions. Each successive cell (blastomere) gets smaller. Why can these early embryonic cells skip the G₁ and G₂ growth phases that most somatic cells require?
    [ ] Frog embryonic cells do not need DNA; they divide without replicating chromosomes
    [*] The large egg cell provides a massive stockpile of maternal mRNAs, proteins (including pre-made cyclins and CDKs), ribosomes, and nutrients — so early blastomeres can cycle rapidly through S phase and M phase without needing time to grow or synthesize new organelles
    [ ] External growth factors from the environment accelerate the divisions
    [ ] The cells do grow between divisions, but they grow so fast it appears they skip growth phases
    Explanation: Early embryonic cleavage divisions in frogs (Xenopus) are among the fastest cell cycles known (~30 minutes each). The enormous egg (~1.2 mm diameter) is pre-loaded with vast stores of maternal mRNAs, proteins, cyclins, histones, ribosomes, and yolk nutrients during oogenesis. These stockpiled resources allow the embryo to simply alternate between S phase (DNA replication) and M phase (mitosis) without G₁ or G₂ gaps. The result is rapid subdivision of the large egg into thousands of progressively smaller blastomeres, forming a hollow blastula. After about 12 divisions (~4,096 cells), the mid-blastula transition (MBT) activates the embryo's own genome, and normal G₁/G₂ phases resume.

--- Lesson 9.3:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. How many cells does meiosis produce from one parent cell, and are they genetically identical?
    [ ] Two genetically identical diploid cells
    [*] Four genetically unique haploid cells (gametes)
    [ ] Four genetically identical haploid cells
    [ ] Two genetically different diploid cells
    Explanation: Meiosis consists of two consecutive divisions (meiosis I and meiosis II) that start with one diploid cell (2n) and produce four haploid cells (n). Each of the four resulting gametes is genetically unique because crossing over during prophase I and independent assortment during metaphase I shuffle alleles into new combinations.

  Q2. How does meiosis change the chromosome number of a cell?
    [ ] It doubles the chromosome number from haploid to diploid
    [*] It reduces the chromosome number from diploid (2n) to haploid (n)
    [ ] It keeps the chromosome number the same
    [ ] It reduces the number to one-quarter of the original
    Explanation: Meiosis is called a reduction division because it halves the chromosome number. In humans, a diploid cell (2n = 46) produces haploid gametes (n = 23). This reduction is essential for sexual reproduction — when two haploid gametes fuse at fertilization, the diploid number is restored in the zygote.

  Q3. What are the two main divisions of meiosis, and what does each accomplish?
    [*] Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes (reduction division); Meiosis II separates sister chromatids (equational division)
    [ ] Meiosis I separates sister chromatids; Meiosis II separates homologs
    [ ] Both divisions separate homologous chromosomes
    [ ] Both divisions separate sister chromatids
    Explanation: Meiosis I is the reduction division — homologous chromosome pairs are separated, cutting the chromosome number in half (diploid to haploid). Meiosis II is the equational division — similar to mitosis, it separates sister chromatids. The result after both divisions is four haploid cells from one diploid cell.

  Q4. When and where does crossing over occur during meiosis?
    [ ] During metaphase II when chromosomes line up
    [*] During prophase I, when homologous chromosomes pair (synapse) and exchange DNA segments at chiasmata
    [ ] During anaphase I when homologs separate
    [ ] During S phase when DNA replicates
    Explanation: Crossing over occurs specifically during prophase I of meiosis. Homologous chromosomes pair up side by side (synapsis) to form tetrads. Non-sister chromatids of the homologous pair then exchange corresponding DNA segments at points called chiasmata. This recombination creates new allele combinations on each chromatid, greatly increasing genetic diversity.

  Q5. What is independent assortment, and how does it create genetic variation?
    [*] Homologous pair orientation is random at metaphase I, so each gamete gets a different mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes — in humans, 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million possible combinations
    [ ] All maternal chromosomes go to one pole and paternal to the other
    [ ] Genes on the same chromosome are always inherited together
    [ ] Sister chromatids separate randomly during meiosis II
    Explanation: Independent assortment occurs during metaphase I when homologous chromosome pairs line up at the metaphase plate. The orientation of each pair is random — the maternal homolog can face either pole independently of every other pair. With 23 pairs in humans, this produces 2²³ (about 8.4 million) possible gamete combinations. Combined with crossing over, the genetic diversity is essentially limitless.

  Q6. What are homologous chromosomes?
    [ ] Two identical copies of the same chromosome joined at the centromere (sister chromatids)
    [*] A matching pair of chromosomes — one from each parent — that carry the same genes at the same loci but may have different alleles
    [ ] Chromosomes found only in reproductive cells
    [ ] Chromosomes that have been broken and repaired by crossing over
    Explanation: Homologous chromosomes (homologs) are a pair of chromosomes — one inherited from the mother and one from the father — that are similar in size and shape and carry genes for the same traits at corresponding positions (loci). While they carry the same genes, they may have different versions (alleles) of those genes. Humans have 23 pairs of homologs (22 autosomal pairs + 1 sex chromosome pair).

  Q7. How many chromosomes do human gametes (sperm and egg cells) contain?
    [ ] 46 chromosomes (diploid)
    [*] 23 chromosomes (haploid) — one from each homologous pair
    [ ] 92 chromosomes
    [ ] 12 chromosomes
    Explanation: Human gametes are haploid (n = 23), meaning they contain one chromosome from each of the 23 homologous pairs (22 autosomes + 1 sex chromosome). When a sperm (23) and egg (23) fuse during fertilization, the resulting zygote has the full diploid complement of 46 chromosomes (2n = 46).

  Q8. What happens during fertilization to restore the chromosome number?
    [ ] A diploid cell undergoes another round of meiosis
    [*] A haploid sperm (n = 23) fuses with a haploid egg (n = 23) to form a diploid zygote (2n = 46)
    [ ] Chromosomes are duplicated within the egg before division
    [ ] Two diploid cells merge their chromosomes
    Explanation: Fertilization (syngamy) is the fusion of two haploid gametes to produce a diploid zygote. The sperm contributes 23 chromosomes (22 autosomes + X or Y) and the egg contributes 23 chromosomes (22 autosomes + X). The resulting zygote inherits 46 chromosomes in 23 pairs, restoring the diploid number for all subsequent mitotic divisions.

  Q9. What is nondisjunction, and what can it cause?
    [ ] Normal separation of chromosomes during cell division
    [*] The failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, producing gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers (aneuploidy)
    [ ] The exchange of DNA between homologous chromosomes
    [ ] The normal pairing of chromosomes during synapsis
    Explanation: Nondisjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes (in meiosis I) or sister chromatids (in meiosis II) to separate properly. This produces gametes with one extra chromosome (n + 1) or one missing chromosome (n − 1). If these gametes are fertilized, the resulting offspring has aneuploidy (abnormal chromosome count) — such as trisomy 21 (Down syndrome, 47 chromosomes) or monosomy X (Turner syndrome, 45 chromosomes).

  Q10. How does spermatogenesis differ from oogenesis in the number of functional gametes produced?
    [ ] Both produce four equally-sized functional gametes
    [*] Spermatogenesis produces 4 functional sperm; oogenesis produces only 1 functional egg plus 2-3 small polar bodies
    [ ] Spermatogenesis produces 1 sperm; oogenesis produces 4 eggs
    [ ] Both produce 2 functional gametes
    Explanation: In spermatogenesis, cytoplasm divides equally at each meiotic division, producing four same-sized functional sperm. In oogenesis, the cytoplasm divides unequally — one large cell retains most cytoplasm, nutrients, and organelles (the future egg) while the small polar bodies receive minimal cytoplasm and degenerate. This asymmetric division maximizes the egg's resource stockpile for early embryonic development.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes meiosis?
    [ ] Cell division that produces two identical diploid daughter cells for growth and repair
    [*] A two-stage cell division (meiosis I and II) that produces four genetically unique haploid gametes from one diploid cell
    [ ] The process by which a cell copies its DNA before division
    [ ] Programmed cell death triggered by DNA damage
    Explanation: Meiosis is a specialized form of cell division that occurs only in reproductive organs (gonads). Starting from one diploid cell (2n), it produces four haploid cells (n) through two sequential divisions. Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes (the reduction division) and meiosis II separates sister chromatids. The key distinction from mitosis is that meiosis halves the chromosome number and produces genetically unique cells through crossing over and independent assortment.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes crossing over?
    [ ] The separation of homologous chromosomes during anaphase I
    [*] The exchange of DNA segments between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during prophase I, creating new allele combinations
    [ ] The random orientation of chromosome pairs at the metaphase plate
    [ ] The duplication of chromosomes during S phase of interphase
    Explanation: Crossing over (genetic recombination) occurs during prophase I when homologous chromosomes are synapsed together as tetrads. Non-sister chromatids from each homolog physically break and rejoin at corresponding positions, swapping DNA segments. The visible points of exchange are called chiasmata. This process shuffles alleles between maternal and paternal chromosomes, producing recombinant chromatids with unique allele combinations not present in either parent.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes independent assortment?
    [ ] The exchange of genetic material between chromatids during prophase I
    [*] The random, independent orientation of each homologous chromosome pair at the metaphase I plate, producing diverse gamete chromosome combinations
    [ ] The ordered separation of sister chromatids during mitosis
    [ ] The controlled destruction of faulty chromosomes
    Explanation: Independent assortment (Mendel's Second Law) means that during metaphase I, the orientation of each homologous pair at the metaphase plate is random and independent of all other pairs. For example, whether chromosome 1's maternal copy faces the top pole has no influence on which way chromosome 2's maternal copy faces. In humans, this generates 2²³ (about 8.4 million) possible chromosome combinations per gamete — without even counting crossing over.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes haploid (n)?
    [ ] A cell containing two complete sets of chromosomes — one maternal set and one paternal set
    [*] A cell containing only one set of chromosomes (half the diploid number) — such as human gametes with 23 chromosomes
    [ ] A cell with three sets of chromosomes (triploid)
    [ ] A cell that has lost all chromosomes during division
    Explanation: Haploid (written as 'n') describes a cell with a single set of chromosomes — one member from each homologous pair. In humans, the haploid number is 23 (n = 23). Gametes (sperm and egg) are haploid cells produced by meiosis. The haploid state is essential for sexual reproduction: when two haploid gametes fuse at fertilization, they restore the diploid number (2n = 46) in the zygote.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes diploid (2n)?
    [ ] A cell containing one set of chromosomes, like a gamete
    [*] A cell containing two complete sets of chromosomes (one from each parent) — such as human somatic cells with 46 chromosomes (23 pairs)
    [ ] A cell that has undergone meiosis and lost half its chromosomes
    [ ] A cell with four sets of chromosomes (tetraploid)
    Explanation: Diploid (written as '2n') describes a cell with two complete sets of chromosomes — one set inherited from each parent. These chromosomes exist as homologous pairs. In humans, the diploid number is 46 (2n = 46), consisting of 22 pairs of autosomes and 1 pair of sex chromosomes. All somatic (body) cells are diploid. The diploid state ensures each gene has two copies (alleles), which is important for genetic diversity and masking harmful recessive mutations.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes nondisjunction?
    [ ] The normal separation of chromosomes during anaphase
    [*] The failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis (or mitosis), producing cells with abnormal chromosome numbers (aneuploidy)
    [ ] The pairing of homologous chromosomes during synapsis
    [ ] The exchange of DNA between non-sister chromatids
    Explanation: Nondisjunction is a division error where chromosomes fail to separate properly. It can occur in meiosis I (homologs fail to separate) or meiosis II (sister chromatids fail to separate). The result is gametes with an extra chromosome (n + 1) or a missing chromosome (n − 1). If these abnormal gametes participate in fertilization, the offspring has aneuploidy — for example, trisomy 21 (Down syndrome, 3 copies of chromosome 21) or monosomy X (Turner syndrome, only one X chromosome).

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes a gamete?
    [ ] Any cell in the body that can divide by mitosis
    [*] A haploid reproductive cell (sperm or egg) produced by meiosis that fuses with another gamete during fertilization to form a diploid zygote
    [ ] A diploid cell that undergoes growth and repair
    [ ] A cell that has been damaged by nondisjunction
    Explanation: A gamete is a specialized haploid (n) reproductive cell produced by meiosis in the gonads. In animals, male gametes are sperm (produced in the testes via spermatogenesis) and female gametes are eggs/ova (produced in the ovaries via oogenesis). Gametes are the only cells in the body that are haploid — all other cells (somatic cells) are diploid. The fusion of two gametes during fertilization restores the diploid state.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes synapsis?
    [ ] The separation of sister chromatids during anaphase II
    [*] The precise pairing of homologous chromosomes side by side during prophase I, held together by the synaptonemal complex, enabling crossing over
    [ ] The formation of the cleavage furrow during cytokinesis
    [ ] The random alignment of chromosomes at the metaphase plate
    Explanation: Synapsis is the process during prophase I where homologous chromosomes find each other and pair up intimately along their length. A protein structure called the synaptonemal complex (SC) zips the homologs together like a molecular zipper. This tight pairing is essential for crossing over — it aligns corresponding gene loci so that DNA exchange occurs between the correct regions of non-sister chromatids.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes a tetrad (bivalent)?
    [ ] A single chromosome composed of two sister chromatids
    [*] A group of four chromatids formed when a pair of homologous chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids) synapse during prophase I
    [ ] Four separate chromosomes that line up together at metaphase
    [ ] The four haploid cells produced at the end of meiosis
    Explanation: A tetrad (also called a bivalent) is the structure formed during prophase I when two homologous chromosomes synapse. Since each homolog has been replicated and consists of two sister chromatids, the paired structure contains four chromatids total (hence 'tetrad'). The bivalent is also called a tetrad because of these four chromatids. Crossing over occurs within tetrads, with non-sister chromatids exchanging DNA segments at chiasmata.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes genetic variation?
    [ ] All organisms in a population having identical DNA sequences
    [*] Differences in DNA sequences and allele combinations among individuals in a population, generated by crossing over, independent assortment, and random fertilization
    [ ] The process of DNA replication during S phase
    [ ] The failure of chromosomes to separate during meiosis
    Explanation: Genetic variation refers to the diversity of allele combinations in a population. Meiosis generates variation through three mechanisms: (1) crossing over shuffles alleles between homologs, (2) independent assortment randomly combines maternal and paternal chromosomes in gametes (2²³ ≈ 8.4 million combinations in humans), and (3) random fertilization pairs any sperm with any egg. This variation is the raw material for natural selection and is essential for the long-term survival and adaptation of species.

  Q21. A lily plant (2n = 24) produces pollen grains through meiosis. A genetics student wants to know how many chromosomes each pollen grain nucleus contains and whether the maternal and paternal chromosomes are mixed. What should she conclude?
    [ ] Each pollen grain has 24 chromosomes, all from the mother plant
    [*] Each pollen grain has 12 chromosomes (haploid), containing a random mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes due to independent assortment and crossing over
    [ ] Each pollen grain has 6 chromosomes because meiosis divides by four
    [ ] Each pollen grain has 24 chromosomes, but they are identical to the parent
    Explanation: Meiosis halves the chromosome number, so a lily cell with 2n = 24 produces gametes (pollen) with n = 12 chromosomes. During meiosis I, independent assortment shuffles maternal and paternal chromosomes randomly — each pollen grain receives a unique combination of 12 chromosomes from the 12 homologous pairs. Crossing over during prophase I further mixes alleles between homologs, making each pollen grain genetically unique.

  Q22. A baby is born with Klinefelter syndrome (47, XXY). The father's chromosomes are normal (46, XY) and the mother's chromosomes are normal (46, XX). A genetic counselor determines that the extra X chromosome came from the mother. During which meiotic division in the mother did nondisjunction most likely occur?
    [ ] The father's meiosis I — he failed to separate X and Y
    [*] The mother's meiosis I — the two X homologs failed to separate, producing an egg with XX; or meiosis II — the X sister chromatids failed to separate. Either could produce an XX egg that, when fertilized by a Y-bearing sperm, yields XXY
    [ ] Mitotic nondisjunction after fertilization in the embryo
    [ ] Crossing over during the father's meiosis created the extra X
    Explanation: Since the extra X came from the mother, the error was in maternal meiosis. In meiosis I, if the two X homologs failed to separate (both went to the same daughter cell), the resulting egg would have two X chromosomes (XX). In meiosis II, if the X sister chromatids failed to separate, the egg would also end up with XX. When this XX egg is fertilized by a normal Y-bearing sperm, the result is 47, XXY (Klinefelter syndrome). Genetic markers can sometimes distinguish meiosis I vs II errors.

  Q23. In a corn genetics experiment, a researcher crosses two pure-breeding parents that differ in two traits located on different chromosomes: kernel color (purple P dominant over yellow p) and kernel shape (smooth S dominant over wrinkled s). All F₁ offspring are PpSs. When the F₁ plants are crossed, the researcher expects a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio in the F₂ generation. What meiotic process produces this characteristic ratio?
    [ ] Crossing over between the two gene loci during prophase I
    [*] Independent assortment — the Pp pair and Ss pair orient randomly and independently at metaphase I, so all four gamete types (PS, Ps, pS, ps) are produced in equal frequency
    [ ] Nondisjunction of the chromosomes carrying both genes
    [ ] Linked inheritance — the genes travel together on the same chromosome
    Explanation: The classic 9:3:3:1 dihybrid ratio depends on independent assortment. Because the P/p and S/s genes are on different chromosomes, each homologous pair orients independently at metaphase I. This produces four equally frequent gamete types: PS, Ps, pS, and ps. When these gametes combine randomly in the F₂ cross, the 16-square Punnett square yields the 9:3:3:1 ratio. If the genes were on the same chromosome (linked), they would deviate from this ratio unless crossing over separated them.

  Q24. A dog breeder notices that every puppy in a litter of 8 from the same two parents looks slightly different — different coat patterns, sizes, and temperaments. She asks why siblings from the same parents are not identical. Which combination of meiotic mechanisms BEST explains this variation?
    [ ] Mutations during embryonic development cause all the differences
    [*] Crossing over (shuffles alleles between homologs), independent assortment (random chromosome combinations in each gamete), and random fertilization (each puppy comes from a different sperm-egg combination) together create unique offspring
    [ ] The puppies are identical genetically but the environment makes them look different
    [ ] Mitosis during growth causes genetic differences between puppies
    Explanation: Sibling variation is produced by three mechanisms during sexual reproduction: (1) crossing over during prophase I creates recombinant chromosomes with new allele combinations, (2) independent assortment during metaphase I randomly distributes maternal and paternal chromosomes into gametes (2³⁹ ≈ 5.5 × 10¹¹ combinations in dogs with n = 39), and (3) random fertilization means each puppy develops from a different sperm-egg pair. With these mechanisms combined, the probability of two siblings being genetically identical (without being identical twins) is astronomically small.

  Q25. A geneticist studying an organism with 2n = 8 (4 pairs of chromosomes) wants to calculate the maximum number of genetically different gamete types produced by independent assortment alone (ignoring crossing over). How many different chromosome combinations are possible in each gamete?
    [ ] 4 different combinations
    [*] 2⁴ = 16 different chromosome combinations per gamete
    [ ] 8 different combinations
    [ ] 2⁸ = 256 different combinations
    Explanation: The formula for the number of possible gamete chromosome combinations from independent assortment is 2ⁿ, where n = the haploid number (number of homologous pairs). With 4 pairs (n = 4), there are 2⁴ = 16 possible combinations. Each pair independently orients at metaphase I, with the maternal or paternal homolog facing either pole. In humans (n = 23), this gives 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million. Note: this calculation ignores crossing over, which makes the actual number of unique gametes effectively infinite.

  Q26. During an IVF (in vitro fertilization) procedure, a doctor retrieves eggs from a patient. Alongside each mature egg, small non-functional cells called polar bodies are observed. Why does oogenesis produce these polar bodies instead of four equally-sized eggs like spermatogenesis produces four equal sperm?
    [ ] Polar bodies contain extra chromosomes that need to be discarded
    [*] Asymmetric cytokinesis during oogenesis concentrates nearly all cytoplasm, organelles, mRNAs, and nutrients into one large egg — maximizing the resource stockpile for early embryonic development — while the polar bodies receive almost no cytoplasm and degenerate
    [ ] The polar bodies will later mature into additional eggs
    [ ] Oogenesis only undergoes one meiotic division instead of two
    Explanation: Oogenesis features extremely asymmetric cell division. During meiosis I and II, the cytoplasm divides unequally — one large cell receives ~95% of the cytoplasm while the polar body gets a tiny amount. This strategy evolved to concentrate all maternal resources (mRNAs, ribosomes, mitochondria, yolk proteins, nutrients) into a single large egg. The early embryo depends entirely on these stored maternal resources for its first divisions before its own genome activates. In IVF, polar body biopsy can be used for genetic testing without damaging the egg.

  Q27. Scientists compare meiosis in budding yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae, 2n = 32) with meiosis in humans (2n = 46). In yeast, meiosis produces four haploid spores enclosed in a structure called an ascus. Despite the vast evolutionary distance between yeast and humans, the core events of meiosis are remarkably conserved. Which shared feature is most fundamental?
    [ ] Both produce gametes that swim toward each other for fertilization
    [*] Both undergo homologous chromosome pairing, crossing over, and two sequential divisions that reduce the chromosome number from diploid to haploid — the core meiotic program is conserved across nearly all eukaryotes
    [ ] Both produce exactly four sperm cells from each meiosis
    [ ] Both use identical numbers of chromosomes during meiosis
    Explanation: The fundamental meiotic program — homologous chromosome pairing (synapsis), crossing over (recombination), reductional division (meiosis I separating homologs), and equational division (meiosis II separating sister chromatids) — is conserved across virtually all sexually reproducing eukaryotes, from yeast to plants to animals. Key meiotic proteins (like Spo11 for initiating recombination and Rec8 cohesin) are homologous across species. This conservation over ~1 billion years of evolution underscores the importance of meiosis for genetic diversity and species survival.

  Q28. In honeybee colonies, females (queens and workers) develop from fertilized eggs and are diploid (2n = 32), while males (drones) develop from unfertilized eggs and are haploid (n = 16). When a haploid drone produces sperm, can he undergo normal meiosis? What special process occurs instead?
    [ ] Drones undergo normal meiosis like diploid organisms
    [*] Drones cannot undergo standard meiosis (no homologous pairs to separate) — instead, they produce sperm through a modified process where the first division is abortive and the second is essentially mitotic, producing haploid sperm genetically identical to the drone's cells
    [ ] Drones do not produce sperm because they are haploid
    [ ] Drones double their chromosomes to become diploid before undergoing meiosis
    Explanation: Honeybee drones are haploid (n = 16) and lack homologous chromosome pairs. Standard meiosis I (which separates homologs) cannot occur normally. Instead, drone spermatogenesis involves a modified division: the cell undergoes a pseudo-meiosis where one division is essentially skipped or abortive, and the functional division separates sister chromatids (similar to meiosis II/mitosis). The resulting sperm are haploid (n = 16) and genetically identical to the drone's own cells. This haplodiploidy system means all sperm from one drone are genetically identical.

  Q29. Seedless watermelons are produced by creating triploid plants (3n = 33). Breeders first treat a normal diploid watermelon (2n = 22) with colchicine to produce a tetraploid (4n = 44), then cross the tetraploid with a normal diploid. Why are the resulting triploid plants unable to produce seeds?
    [ ] Triploid plants have too many chromosomes to survive
    [*] With three sets of chromosomes (3n = 33), homologous chromosomes cannot pair properly during meiosis I — trivalents form instead of bivalents, leading to uneven chromosome segregation and non-viable gametes, so no seeds develop
    [ ] Colchicine makes the plants permanently sterile by destroying their DNA
    [ ] Triploid plants cannot undergo mitosis, so they never grow flowers
    Explanation: Triploid organisms (3n) have three copies of each chromosome rather than the normal two. During meiosis I, chromosomes pair as homologs — but with three copies, they form trivalents (groups of three) instead of normal bivalents (pairs). Trivalents cannot segregate evenly into two groups, so the resulting gametes get unbalanced chromosome numbers (some chromosomes have 2 copies, others have 1). These aneuploid gametes are almost always non-viable. Since seeds require successful fertilization, the triploid watermelon is effectively seedless.

  Q30. Researchers studying the nematode C. elegans notice that crossing over events are not evenly distributed across chromosomes — some regions have many crossovers ('hotspots') while others have very few ('coldspots'). They also observe that each chromosome arm receives exactly one crossover per meiosis (crossover interference). Why is at least one crossover per chromosome pair essential for proper meiosis?
    [ ] Crossovers are needed to replicate DNA during S phase
    [*] At least one crossover per homologous pair creates a physical chiasma connection that holds the homologs together until anaphase I — without this connection, homologs may segregate randomly, causing nondisjunction and aneuploidy
    [ ] Crossovers are needed to condense chromosomes during prophase
    [ ] Crossovers provide the energy needed for chromosome movement
    Explanation: The obligate crossover (at least one per chromosome pair) serves a critical mechanical function beyond generating genetic diversity. The chiasma formed by crossing over, together with sister chromatid cohesion distal to the chiasma, physically links the two homologs. This connection ensures that homologous chromosomes orient correctly at the metaphase I plate with tension from opposing spindle poles. Without a chiasma, homologs have no physical link after the synaptonemal complex dissolves, leading to random segregation (nondisjunction). C. elegans enforces exactly one crossover per chromosome through crossover interference — a mechanism that ensures proper meiotic segregation.

--- Lesson 9.4:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What are the main phases of the cell cycle?
    [*] G₁, S, G₂ (interphase) and M phase (mitosis + cytokinesis)
    [ ] Prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase only
    [ ] Meiosis I and meiosis II
    [ ] S phase and M phase only
    Explanation: The cell cycle has two major parts: interphase and the mitotic (M) phase. Interphase includes G₁ (cell growth and normal function), S phase (DNA synthesis/replication), and G₂ (further growth and preparation for division). The M phase includes mitosis (nuclear division through PMAT stages) and cytokinesis (cytoplasm division). Most cells spend about 90% of their cycle in interphase.

  Q2. What happens during each sub-phase of interphase (G₁, S, G₂)?
    [*] G₁: cell grows and performs normal functions; S: DNA is replicated; G₂: cell continues growing and prepares organelles and proteins for mitosis
    [ ] G₁: DNA replicates; S: cell grows; G₂: chromosomes condense
    [ ] All three phases involve chromosome separation
    [ ] G₁ and G₂ are identical phases where cells rest
    Explanation: Interphase is the longest part of the cell cycle (~90%). G₁ (Gap 1): the cell grows, produces proteins, and carries out normal metabolic activities. S (Synthesis): DNA is replicated so each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. G₂ (Gap 2): the cell continues growing, synthesizes proteins needed for mitosis (like tubulin for spindle fibers), and checks for DNA replication errors before committing to division.

  Q3. What are cell cycle checkpoints, and what are the three main ones?
    [*] Control points that either allow or halt cell cycle progression: G₁ checkpoint (cell size/DNA damage), G₂ checkpoint (DNA replication complete?), and M checkpoint (spindle attachment correct?)
    [ ] Points where cells automatically speed up division
    [ ] Locations in the cell where chromosomes are stored
    [ ] The three phases of mitosis
    Explanation: Cell cycle checkpoints act as quality-control gates that check whether critical events have been completed correctly before allowing progression to the next phase. The G₁ checkpoint (restriction point) checks for cell size, available nutrients, growth factor signals, and DNA integrity. The G₂ checkpoint verifies that DNA replication is complete and error-free. The M checkpoint (spindle assembly checkpoint/SAC) ensures all chromosomes are properly attached to spindle fibers before anaphase begins.

  Q4. What is the role of p53 in the cell cycle?
    [*] p53 is a tumor suppressor protein that detects DNA damage and either halts the cell cycle for repair or triggers apoptosis (programmed cell death) if damage is irreparable
    [ ] p53 promotes rapid cell division by activating oncogenes
    [ ] p53 replicates DNA during S phase
    [ ] p53 forms the mitotic spindle during cell division
    Explanation: p53 is called the 'guardian of the genome' because it responds to DNA damage by activating repair pathways or triggering apoptosis. When DNA damage is detected, p53 activates p21, which inhibits CDK-cyclin complexes and halts the cell cycle at the G₁ checkpoint. If the damage cannot be repaired, p53 activates pro-apoptotic genes (like BAX), causing the cell to self-destruct. About 50% of all human cancers have mutated p53.

  Q5. What is the difference between a proto-oncogene and an oncogene?
    [*] A proto-oncogene is a normal gene that promotes cell growth and division; when mutated, it becomes an oncogene that drives uncontrolled cell proliferation
    [ ] Proto-oncogenes cause cancer; oncogenes prevent it
    [ ] Both terms refer to the same type of tumor suppressor gene
    [ ] Oncogenes are normal genes that only promote apoptosis
    Explanation: Proto-oncogenes are normal, essential genes that encode proteins promoting cell growth, division, and survival (like growth factor receptors, signal transduction proteins, and transcription factors). They are the 'accelerator' of the cell cycle. When a proto-oncogene is mutated (by point mutation, gene amplification, or chromosomal translocation), it becomes an oncogene — a permanently activated version that drives continuous cell proliferation regardless of normal signals. Examples include RAS (signal transduction) and HER2 (growth factor receptor).

  Q6. What do tumor suppressor genes do, and what happens when they are mutated?
    [*] They normally slow cell division, repair DNA, or trigger apoptosis; when both copies are mutated/lost, these 'brakes' fail and cells can proliferate uncontrollably
    [ ] They promote cell division; when mutated they slow growth
    [ ] They suppress the immune system's response to tumors
    [ ] They only function during embryonic development
    Explanation: Tumor suppressor genes act as the 'brakes' of the cell cycle. Their protein products inhibit cell division (like Rb blocking the G₁/S transition), repair DNA damage, or trigger apoptosis when damage is severe (like p53). Unlike oncogenes, BOTH copies must be inactivated for function to be lost (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis). Examples: p53 (guardian of the genome), Rb (retinoblastoma protein), BRCA1/BRCA2 (DNA repair), APC (colon cancer suppressor).

  Q7. What is apoptosis, and why is it important for preventing cancer?
    [*] Apoptosis is programmed cell death — a controlled, orderly process that eliminates damaged, infected, or unnecessary cells before they can become cancerous
    [ ] Apoptosis is accidental cell death from physical injury (necrosis)
    [ ] Apoptosis is cell division that produces cancer
    [ ] Apoptosis is the process of DNA replication before mitosis
    Explanation: Apoptosis is programmed cell death — a highly regulated process where the cell shrinks, its DNA is fragmented by enzymes (caspases), and it is neatly packaged into 'apoptotic bodies' that are absorbed by neighboring cells or macrophages without causing inflammation. It is essential for preventing cancer because cells with irreparable DNA damage can self-destruct before they accumulate the mutations needed for tumor formation. Cancer cells often evade apoptosis through mutations in p53 or overexpression of anti-apoptotic proteins like Bcl-2.

  Q8. What is the difference between a benign tumor and a malignant tumor?
    [*] Benign tumors remain localized and do not invade other tissues; malignant tumors invade surrounding tissue and can metastasize (spread to distant body parts)
    [ ] Benign tumors are always dangerous; malignant tumors are harmless
    [ ] Both types are identical and equally dangerous
    [ ] Malignant tumors stay in one place; benign tumors spread
    Explanation: Benign tumors are abnormal growths that remain encapsulated, grow slowly, and do not invade surrounding tissue or spread to distant sites. They are generally not life-threatening (though they can cause problems by pressing on nearby structures). Malignant tumors (cancers) are invasive — they break through tissue boundaries, stimulate blood vessel growth (angiogenesis), and can metastasize by releasing cells into the blood or lymphatic system that establish secondary tumors in distant organs.

  Q9. What is metastasis?
    [ ] The initial formation of a tumor from a single mutant cell
    [*] The spread of cancer cells from the primary tumor to distant parts of the body through the blood or lymphatic system, forming secondary tumors
    [ ] The programmed death of cancer cells during treatment
    [ ] The shrinkage of a tumor after chemotherapy
    Explanation: Metastasis is the process by which cancer cells break away from the primary tumor, enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system, travel to distant organs, and establish new (secondary) tumors. This is what makes cancer most dangerous and difficult to treat. Metastatic cells must acquire several capabilities: detachment from the primary tumor, invasion through the basement membrane, survival in circulation, and colonization of new tissue. Common metastatic sites include lungs, liver, brain, and bones.

  Q10. What are carcinogens, and how do they contribute to cancer development?
    [*] Carcinogens are agents that cause cancer by damaging DNA or disrupting cell division — examples include UV radiation, tobacco smoke chemicals, and certain viruses (like HPV)
    [ ] Carcinogens are medications used to treat cancer
    [ ] Carcinogens are proteins that prevent tumor growth
    [ ] Carcinogens are beneficial substances that strengthen DNA
    Explanation: Carcinogens are agents that increase cancer risk by causing DNA mutations or promoting uncontrolled cell division. They include chemical carcinogens (benzene, formaldehyde, tobacco tar compounds like benzo[a]pyrene), physical carcinogens (UV radiation causing thymine dimers, ionizing radiation breaking DNA strands), and biological carcinogens (HPV causing cervical cancer, Hepatitis B/C causing liver cancer, H. pylori causing stomach cancer). Carcinogens typically act over years, accumulating mutations through repeated exposure.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes a tumor suppressor gene?
    [ ] A gene that promotes rapid cell growth and division when activated
    [*] A gene whose protein product slows cell division, repairs DNA damage, or triggers apoptosis — acting as a 'brake' on the cell cycle (e.g., p53, Rb, BRCA1)
    [ ] A gene that suppresses the immune system's ability to detect tumors
    [ ] A gene found only in cancer cells
    Explanation: Tumor suppressor genes encode proteins that regulate cell growth by slowing division, repairing DNA, or inducing apoptosis. They function as protective 'brakes' on the cell cycle. Key examples: p53 detects DNA damage and triggers repair or death; Rb blocks the G₁/S transition until growth signals are confirmed; BRCA1/2 repair double-strand DNA breaks; APC regulates the Wnt signaling pathway. Cancer typically requires loss of BOTH copies of a tumor suppressor (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis), unlike oncogenes which need only one activating mutation.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes an oncogene?
    [ ] A gene that suppresses tumor formation by triggering apoptosis
    [*] A mutated or overexpressed form of a proto-oncogene that promotes uncontrolled cell growth and division, even without normal growth signals
    [ ] A gene that only activates during embryonic development
    [ ] A gene that protects cells from carcinogens
    Explanation: An oncogene is a gain-of-function mutation of a normal proto-oncogene. While proto-oncogenes help regulate normal cell growth, oncogenes are permanently 'switched on' — they continuously signal the cell to divide regardless of external growth factors or checkpoint controls. Oncogenes act like a stuck accelerator pedal. Common oncogenes include mutant RAS (found in ~30% of cancers), amplified HER2 (breast cancer), and the BCR-ABL fusion gene (chronic myelogenous leukemia). Only ONE mutated copy is sufficient to drive cancer (dominant mutation).

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes apoptosis?
    [ ] Uncontrolled, accidental cell death caused by physical injury or infection (necrosis)
    [*] Programmed cell death — a controlled, orderly self-destruction process that safely eliminates damaged, infected, or unnecessary cells without causing inflammation
    [ ] The process of cell division during the M phase
    [ ] The growth of new blood vessels toward a tumor (angiogenesis)
    Explanation: Apoptosis (programmed cell death) is a tightly regulated 'cellular suicide' mechanism. Triggered by internal signals (DNA damage, p53 activation) or external signals (death receptor ligands like FasL or TNF), it activates a cascade of caspase enzymes that systematically disassemble the cell. The cell shrinks, its DNA is fragmented, and it is packaged into membrane-bound apoptotic bodies that phagocytes cleanly engulf. Unlike necrosis (messy, inflammatory cell death), apoptosis is clean and inflammation-free. About 50-70 billion cells undergo apoptosis daily in an adult human.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes a benign tumor?
    [ ] A cancerous mass that invades surrounding tissue and metastasizes
    [*] An abnormal cell mass that remains localized, is usually encapsulated, does not invade surrounding tissue or spread to other body parts, and is generally not life-threatening
    [ ] Any type of tumor, whether cancerous or not
    [ ] A tumor that has been successfully treated with chemotherapy
    Explanation: A benign tumor is a non-cancerous growth that remains enclosed in a fibrous capsule and does not invade neighboring tissue or metastasize. Benign tumors typically grow slowly, have well-defined borders, and retain some resemblance to normal tissue. While generally not life-threatening, benign tumors can still cause problems depending on their location — for example, a benign brain tumor can create dangerous pressure on brain structures, and benign thyroid nodules can affect hormone production. Common examples include lipomas (fat tissue), fibroids (uterus), and moles.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes a malignant tumor?
    [ ] A non-cancerous growth that stays in one location
    [*] A cancerous mass of cells that can invade surrounding tissues, break through basement membranes, stimulate angiogenesis, and metastasize to distant organs via the bloodstream or lymphatic system
    [ ] A temporary swelling caused by infection
    [ ] A cluster of dead cells that the body will naturally absorb
    Explanation: A malignant tumor (cancer) is characterized by several dangerous properties: (1) uncontrolled proliferation ignoring normal growth signals, (2) invasion of surrounding tissues by breaking through the extracellular matrix and basement membrane, (3) angiogenesis — stimulating new blood vessel growth to supply the tumor, (4) metastasis — spreading through blood or lymph to colonize distant organs. Malignant cells are often poorly differentiated (look abnormal), have irregular nuclei, and show high mitotic index. Treatment typically requires surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, or targeted therapy.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes a carcinogen?
    [ ] A medication that kills cancer cells
    [*] Any agent (chemical, radiation, or biological) that can cause cancer by damaging DNA, inducing mutations, or disrupting normal cell cycle regulation
    [ ] A protein produced by tumors to suppress the immune system
    [ ] A type of benign tumor that never becomes cancerous
    Explanation: A carcinogen is any substance or agent that promotes cancer formation. Chemical carcinogens include tobacco tar compounds (benzo[a]pyrene), asbestos fibers, and benzene. Physical carcinogens include UV radiation (causes thymine dimers) and ionizing radiation (breaks DNA strands). Biological carcinogens include viruses that insert their DNA near proto-oncogenes or disable tumor suppressors — HPV (cervical cancer), Hepatitis B/C (liver cancer), EBV (Burkitt's lymphoma). Carcinogens typically require repeated exposure over time to accumulate enough mutations for cancer.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes metastasis?
    [ ] The initial formation of a tumor in one location
    [*] The process by which cancer cells break away from the primary tumor, travel through the blood or lymphatic system, and establish secondary tumors in distant organs
    [ ] The death of cancer cells through chemotherapy
    [ ] The encapsulation of a benign tumor
    Explanation: Metastasis is a multi-step process: (1) cancer cells lose cell-cell adhesion (downregulate E-cadherin), (2) they secrete enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases) to degrade the basement membrane and extracellular matrix, (3) they intravasate into blood vessels or lymphatics, (4) they survive circulation and evade immune cells, (5) they arrest at distant capillary beds, (6) they extravasate and colonize new tissue. Metastasis is the primary cause of cancer mortality, responsible for ~90% of cancer deaths.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes a proto-oncogene?
    [ ] A gene that always causes cancer when present
    [*] A normal gene that encodes proteins promoting cell growth and division — it becomes a cancer-causing oncogene when mutated, amplified, or translocated
    [ ] A gene that suppresses tumor formation
    [ ] A viral gene inserted into human DNA
    Explanation: Proto-oncogenes are essential, normal genes present in all cells. They encode growth factors, growth factor receptors, signal transduction proteins, and transcription factors that regulate cell growth and division. They become oncogenes through activating mutations: point mutations (e.g., RAS), gene amplification (extra copies, e.g., HER2 in breast cancer), or chromosomal translocation (e.g., Philadelphia chromosome creating BCR-ABL in CML). The key distinction is that proto-oncogenes are normal and necessary; oncogenes are their mutant, overactive forms.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes a cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK)?
    [ ] A protein that directly replicates DNA during S phase
    [*] An enzyme that, when activated by binding a cyclin protein, phosphorylates target proteins to drive the cell cycle forward through specific transitions (G₁→S, G₂→M)
    [ ] A structural protein that forms the mitotic spindle
    [ ] A receptor on the cell surface that detects growth factors
    Explanation: CDKs are a family of protein kinases that serve as the core molecular 'engines' driving cell cycle progression. CDKs are present throughout the cell cycle but are only active when bound to their specific cyclin partner (which rises and falls in level at specific phases). For example, CDK4/6-Cyclin D drives G₁ progression, CDK2-Cyclin E triggers the G₁/S transition, and CDK1-Cyclin B (MPF) triggers mitotic entry. CDK inhibitors (like p21, activated by p53) can halt the cycle at checkpoints.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes angiogenesis in the context of cancer?
    [ ] The programmed death of cancer cells
    [*] The formation of new blood vessels that tumors stimulate to supply themselves with oxygen and nutrients, enabling growth beyond 1-2 mm and providing routes for metastasis
    [ ] The spreading of cancer through the lymphatic system only
    [ ] The formation of a protective capsule around a benign tumor
    Explanation: Angiogenesis is the growth of new blood vessels from existing ones. Without a blood supply, tumors cannot grow beyond about 1-2 mm in diameter because cells too far from capillaries lack oxygen and nutrients. Cancer cells stimulate angiogenesis by secreting vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and other pro-angiogenic signals. The new blood vessels are abnormal (leaky, disorganized) but provide the tumor with resources and pathways for metastasis. Anti-angiogenic drugs (like bevacizumab/Avastin) target VEGF to starve tumors of their blood supply.

  Q21. A family has a history where multiple members develop different types of cancer (breast, brain, leukemia, bone) at unusually young ages. Genetic testing reveals that affected members carry a germline mutation in one copy of the TP53 gene (Li-Fraumeni syndrome). Why does losing one copy of p53 dramatically increase cancer risk across many tissue types?
    [ ] One copy of p53 is sufficient to cause cancer directly
    [*] With one inherited defective copy, only ONE additional somatic mutation in the remaining p53 allele is needed to completely lose p53 function — since p53 protects virtually all cell types by detecting DNA damage and triggering repair or apoptosis, losing it leaves every tissue vulnerable to uncontrolled growth
    [ ] The mutation causes all cells to immediately become cancerous
    [ ] p53 mutations only affect bone tissue
    Explanation: Li-Fraumeni syndrome illustrates Knudson's two-hit hypothesis for tumor suppressors. Normally, both copies of p53 must be inactivated for function to be lost. Patients with LFS inherit one defective copy in every cell, so only one somatic mutation (the 'second hit') in any cell is needed to eliminate p53 entirely. Since p53 is the 'guardian of the genome' active in all cell types, its loss removes the key checkpoint that detects DNA damage and triggers apoptosis. This explains why LFS patients develop diverse cancer types at young ages — any tissue can be affected.

  Q22. A breast cancer patient's tumor biopsy shows overexpression of the HER2 protein (a growth factor receptor). Her doctor prescribes trastuzumab (Herceptin), a monoclonal antibody that specifically targets HER2. How does this targeted therapy differ from traditional chemotherapy in its mechanism?
    [ ] Trastuzumab kills all rapidly dividing cells like chemotherapy
    [*] Trastuzumab specifically binds to HER2 receptors on cancer cells, blocking the growth signals that drive proliferation — unlike chemotherapy which targets all rapidly dividing cells (including healthy ones), targeted therapy selectively attacks cancer cells expressing the specific molecular target
    [ ] Trastuzumab repairs the mutated HER2 gene so cells return to normal
    [ ] Trastuzumab strengthens the immune system nonspecifically
    Explanation: HER2 is an oncogene — its amplification produces excess growth factor receptors that continuously signal cell proliferation. Trastuzumab (Herceptin) is a targeted therapy: a monoclonal antibody that binds specifically to the extracellular domain of HER2, blocking receptor dimerization and signaling. It also marks cancer cells for immune destruction (ADCC). Unlike chemotherapy (which poisons all rapidly dividing cells, causing side effects like hair loss, nausea, and immune suppression), targeted therapies attack specific molecular drivers of cancer with fewer off-target effects.

  Q23. A pathologist examines two tissue biopsy slides. Slide A shows a well-defined mass with cells that closely resemble normal tissue, surrounded by a fibrous capsule, with rare mitotic figures. Slide B shows irregular borders, cells with large abnormal nuclei, many mitotic figures, and cells invading through the basement membrane. How should the pathologist classify each sample?
    [ ] Both are malignant tumors with different growth rates
    [*] Slide A: benign tumor (encapsulated, well-differentiated, non-invasive, low mitotic index); Slide B: malignant tumor (poorly differentiated, invasive through basement membrane, high mitotic index)
    [ ] Slide A: malignant tumor; Slide B: benign tumor
    [ ] Neither slide shows a tumor — both are normal tissue
    Explanation: Pathologists distinguish benign from malignant tumors based on several criteria: (1) Differentiation — benign cells resemble normal tissue (well-differentiated), while malignant cells look abnormal (poorly differentiated/anaplastic). (2) Growth pattern — benign tumors are encapsulated with smooth borders; malignant tumors have irregular, invasive borders. (3) Mitotic index — benign tumors have few dividing cells; malignant tumors show many abnormal mitoses. (4) Invasion — the hallmark of malignancy is invasion through the basement membrane into surrounding tissue.

  Q24. A 30-year-long smoker develops lung cancer. His doctor explains that the cancer did not appear overnight — it resulted from decades of carcinogen exposure. Using the multi-hit model of carcinogenesis, how did accumulated mutations from tobacco smoke lead to cancer?
    [ ] A single mutation from one cigarette immediately caused cancer
    [*] Over 30 years, tobacco carcinogens (like benzo[a]pyrene) repeatedly damaged DNA — mutations gradually accumulated in proto-oncogenes (activating them) and tumor suppressor genes (inactivating both copies), until the cell acquired enough mutations (typically 3-7) to bypass all growth controls and become cancerous
    [ ] Tobacco smoke only causes lung infections, not genetic mutations
    [ ] The cancer was inherited and unrelated to smoking
    Explanation: The multi-hit (or multi-step) model of carcinogenesis explains that cancer requires the accumulation of multiple mutations over time (typically 3-7 driver mutations). Tobacco smoke contains over 70 known carcinogens that damage DNA with each exposure. Over decades, mutations accumulate: perhaps first a RAS oncogene activation, then p53 loss, then Rb inactivation, then activation of telomerase, etc. Each mutation confers a growth advantage, and the cell lineage progressively loses normal growth controls. This explains why cancer risk increases with both the duration and intensity of carcinogen exposure.

  Q25. A 25-year-old woman receives a Pap smear that detects abnormal cervical cells. Further testing reveals infection with human papillomavirus (HPV) types 16 and 18. Her doctor explains that HPV is a biological carcinogen. How do these 'high-risk' HPV strains promote cervical cancer at the molecular level?
    [ ] HPV directly destroys cervical tissue through inflammation
    [*] HPV produces viral proteins E6 and E7 that inactivate the tumor suppressors p53 (E6 targets p53 for degradation) and Rb (E7 binds and inhibits Rb) — simultaneously removing both 'brakes' on the cell cycle, allowing uncontrolled proliferation
    [ ] HPV inserts an oncogene that makes cells grow faster
    [ ] HPV prevents the immune system from reaching cervical cells
    Explanation: High-risk HPV strains (types 16 and 18 cause ~70% of cervical cancers) produce two key oncoproteins: E6 binds p53 and targets it for ubiquitin-mediated degradation, eliminating the cell's ability to detect DNA damage or trigger apoptosis. E7 binds and inactivates the Rb protein, releasing the E2F transcription factor to drive cells past the G₁/S restriction point into uncontrolled DNA replication. Together, E6 and E7 simultaneously disable two critical tumor suppressors. The HPV vaccine prevents infection by these high-risk strains, preventing cervical cancer.

  Q26. A child is diagnosed with retinoblastoma (eye cancer). Genetic testing reveals the child inherited a mutant copy of the RB1 gene from a parent. Only one additional somatic mutation in the remaining normal RB1 copy was needed for cancer to develop. This pattern is described by what hypothesis, and how does it explain inherited vs sporadic cancer?
    [ ] The oncogene hypothesis — one activating mutation in RB1 directly causes cancer
    [*] Knudson's two-hit hypothesis — inherited retinoblastoma requires only ONE somatic mutation (second hit) because the first hit is already present in every cell from birth; sporadic retinoblastoma requires TWO somatic mutations in the same cell, which is rarer and typically occurs later
    [ ] The multi-hit hypothesis — retinoblastoma requires 7+ mutations to develop
    [ ] Retinoblastoma is caused by environmental carcinogens, not genetics
    Explanation: Knudson's two-hit hypothesis (1971) was developed specifically from studying retinoblastoma. For tumor suppressors like RB1, BOTH alleles must be inactivated for function to be lost. Inherited (familial) retinoblastoma: one defective RB1 allele is present in every cell from birth (first hit inherited); only one somatic mutation is needed (second hit), so cancer develops early, often in both eyes. Sporadic retinoblastoma: both hits must occur somatically in the same retinal cell, which is extremely rare — cancer develops later, usually in only one eye.

  Q27. A patient with chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML) has the Philadelphia chromosome — a translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22 that creates the BCR-ABL fusion oncogene. The oncologist prescribes imatinib (Gleevec), which specifically inhibits the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase. Within months, the patient's white blood cell count returns to normal. What makes this a landmark example of targeted cancer therapy?
    [ ] Imatinib destroys all rapidly dividing cells like conventional chemotherapy
    [*] Imatinib was designed to specifically fit into the ATP-binding pocket of the BCR-ABL fusion kinase, blocking the exact molecular driver of CML — this precision targeting of a single oncogenic protein made it the first successful rationally designed cancer drug
    [ ] Imatinib repairs the Philadelphia chromosome translocation
    [ ] Imatinib works by stimulating the patient's immune system
    Explanation: Imatinib (Gleevec, approved 2001) is a paradigm-shifting drug in cancer treatment. The Philadelphia chromosome's BCR-ABL fusion produces a constitutively active tyrosine kinase that continuously signals cell proliferation. Imatinib was rationally designed to fit into the ATP-binding site of BCR-ABL, blocking its kinase activity and shutting down the proliferative signal. CML patients on imatinib achieve ~95% remission rates. This success demonstrated that understanding the specific molecular driver of a cancer can lead to highly effective, targeted therapies with fewer side effects than broad chemotherapy.

  Q28. A cancer patient undergoing chemotherapy experiences severe side effects: hair loss, nausea, low blood cell counts, and mouth sores. Her doctor explains that chemotherapy drugs target rapidly dividing cells. Why do these specific side effects occur, and what do the affected tissues have in common?
    [ ] Chemotherapy drugs are toxic to all cells equally regardless of division rate
    [*] Hair follicles, gut epithelium, bone marrow, and oral mucosa are among the most rapidly dividing normal tissues — chemotherapy cannot distinguish cancer cells from other fast-dividing cells, so these healthy tissues are collateral damage
    [ ] The side effects are caused by the cancer itself, not the treatment
    [ ] Only cancer cells are affected, but the immune system overreacts
    Explanation: Traditional chemotherapy drugs (like cisplatin, doxorubicin, and taxanes) target cells that divide rapidly — which includes cancer cells but also normal cells with high turnover rates. Hair follicle cells (divide every 23-72 hours → hair loss/alopecia), intestinal epithelium (replaced every 3-5 days → nausea/diarrhea), bone marrow stem cells (continuous blood cell production → anemia, low WBCs, low platelets), and oral mucosa (fast turnover → mouth sores). These side effects are the fundamental limitation of non-targeted chemotherapy and the reason targeted therapies were developed.

  Q29. A researcher discovers that a cancer cell line has a homozygous deletion of the CDKN2A gene, which encodes the p16 protein (a CDK inhibitor). She teststo see whether adding a CDK4/6 inhibitor drug (like palbociclib) can compensate for the lost p16 function. What checkpoint is she trying to restore, and why?
    [ ] The M checkpoint (spindle assembly) — p16 controls spindle attachment
    [*] The G₁/S restriction point — p16 normally inhibits CDK4/6, preventing Rb phosphorylation and blocking the G₁→S transition; without p16, CDK4/6 is constitutively active and cells uncontrollably enter S phase; the CDK4/6 inhibitor drug replaces the lost p16 braking function
    [ ] The G₂/M checkpoint — p16 prevents entry into mitosis
    [ ] The S phase checkpoint — p16 controls DNA replication
    Explanation: The p16/INK4a protein (encoded by CDKN2A) is a tumor suppressor that inhibits CDK4 and CDK6. Normally, p16 prevents CDK4/6 from phosphorylating Rb protein. When Rb is unphosphorylated, it binds and inhibits the E2F transcription factor, blocking S phase entry. When p16 is lost (one of the most commonly deleted tumor suppressors in cancer), CDK4/6 is hyperactive, Rb is continuously phosphorylated and inactivated, and cells bypass the G₁ checkpoint. CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib) are FDA-approved for breast cancer and pharmacologically restore the G₁ brake that p16 loss removed.

  Q30. A woman who tests positive for a BRCA1 mutation (which impairs double-strand DNA break repair) considers prophylactic bilateral mastectomy to reduce her breast cancer risk. Her genetic counselor explains that carrying one mutant BRCA1 copy gives her a 60-80% lifetime risk of breast cancer, versus ~12% in the general population. Why does losing DNA repair capability so dramatically increase cancer risk?
    [ ] BRCA1 mutations directly cause cells to become cancerous immediately
    [*] BRCA1 normally repairs double-strand DNA breaks through homologous recombination — with impaired repair, DNA damage from normal metabolism accumulates uncorrected, greatly accelerating the rate at which cells acquire the additional mutations (in oncogenes and other tumor suppressors) needed for cancer development
    [ ] BRCA1 mutations only affect breast tissue, so other organs are not at risk
    [ ] The BRCA1 mutation causes the immune system to attack breast tissue
    Explanation: BRCA1 is a tumor suppressor involved in the homologous recombination repair (HRR) pathway for double-strand DNA breaks — the most dangerous type of DNA damage. When BRCA1 function is lost (second hit in a BRCA1 carrier), cells must rely on error-prone backup repair pathways (like NHEJ), which introduce frequent mutations. This dramatically accelerates the accumulation of driver mutations needed for carcinogenesis (the multi-hit model). BRCA1 carriers also have elevated ovarian cancer risk. Notably, BRCA1-deficient cancers are particularly sensitive to PARP inhibitors (like olaparib) — a synthetic lethality approach where blocking the backup repair pathway kills only cells lacking BRCA1.

--- Lesson 9.5:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is a stem cell?
    [ ] A fully specialized cell that can no longer divide (like a red blood cell)
    [*] An undifferentiated cell that can both self-renew (divide to make more stem cells) and differentiate into various specialized cell types
    [ ] A cancer cell that divides uncontrollably
    [ ] A dead cell that is being recycled by the body
    Explanation: Stem cells have two defining properties: (1) self-renewal — the ability to divide and produce more stem cells, maintaining the stem cell pool, and (2) potency — the ability to differentiate into one or more specialized cell types. These properties make them fundamentally different from most body cells, which are terminally differentiated and perform specific functions but cannot produce new cell types.

  Q2. What is the difference between totipotent, pluripotent, and multipotent stem cells?
    [*] Totipotent can become ANY cell type including placenta (zygote); pluripotent can become almost any body cell type but not placenta (embryonic stem cells); multipotent can become several related cell types (adult stem cells)
    [ ] All three types have identical differentiation potential
    [ ] Multipotent has the broadest potential; totipotent has the narrowest
    [ ] These terms refer to different sizes of stem cells, not their potential
    Explanation: Stem cell potency decreases as development progresses. Totipotent cells (the zygote and first few divisions) can form an entire organism including extraembryonic tissues (placenta). Pluripotent cells (inner cell mass of blastocyst → embryonic stem cells) can become any of the ~200+ cell types in the body but not placenta. Multipotent cells (adult stem cells, like hematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow) can only differentiate into a limited range of related cell types within their tissue lineage.

  Q3. Where do embryonic stem cells come from, and why are they scientifically valuable?
    [ ] From adult bone marrow; they can only produce blood cells
    [*] From the inner cell mass of a blastocyst (early embryo, ~5 days after fertilization); they are pluripotent and can differentiate into virtually any cell type in the body
    [ ] From the placenta; they can only form skin cells
    [ ] From adult muscle tissue; they can regenerate any organ
    Explanation: Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) are derived from the inner cell mass (ICM) of a blastocyst — a hollow ball of about 100-200 cells formed ~5 days after fertilization. The ICM normally develops into the embryo proper. ESCs are pluripotent: when cultured in a lab, they can be directed to become neurons, heart muscle cells, pancreatic beta cells, blood cells, and virtually any other cell type. This makes them invaluable for research and potentially for cell replacement therapies.

  Q4. What are adult (somatic) stem cells, and where are they found?
    [*] Adult stem cells are multipotent cells found in various tissues (bone marrow, skin, intestines, brain) that maintain and repair those tissues throughout life
    [ ] Adult stem cells are only found in embryos
    [ ] Adult stem cells can become any cell type in the body (pluripotent)
    [ ] Adult stem cells exist only in the blood
    Explanation: Adult (somatic) stem cells are undifferentiated cells found in small numbers within differentiated tissues throughout the body. They are typically multipotent — limited to producing cell types within their tissue of origin. Key examples: hematopoietic stem cells (bone marrow → all blood cell types), intestinal stem cells (crypt base → absorptive and secretory gut cells), neural stem cells (brain → neurons, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes), and epidermal stem cells (skin basal layer → keratinocytes). They play essential roles in tissue maintenance and repair.

  Q5. What are induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), and how are they created?
    [ ] iPSCs are embryonic cells extracted earlier than normal ESCs
    [*] iPSCs are adult somatic cells (like skin cells) that have been reprogrammed back to a pluripotent state by introducing specific transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc — the Yamanaka factors)
    [ ] iPSCs are stem cells taken from umbilical cord blood
    [ ] iPSCs are genetically modified bacteria used for tissue engineering
    Explanation: Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) were developed by Shinya Yamanaka in 2006 (Nobel Prize 2012). By introducing four transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc) into adult somatic cells (like skin fibroblasts), the cells are reprogrammed to a pluripotent state resembling embryonic stem cells. iPSCs can differentiate into virtually any cell type. This breakthrough avoids the ethical concerns of destroying embryos and allows patient-specific pluripotent cells to be created, reducing immune rejection risk in therapies.

  Q6. What is cellular differentiation?
    [ ] The process by which cells lose their DNA and die
    [*] The process by which an unspecialized cell becomes a specialized cell type with a specific structure and function — driven by selective gene expression
    [ ] The process by which cells divide to produce identical copies
    [ ] The process of DNA replication during S phase
    Explanation: Differentiation is the developmental process where a cell progressively specializes — activating certain genes and silencing others — to acquire the specific structure and function of a mature cell type. All cells in an organism contain the same DNA, but different cell types express different subsets of genes. For example, a stem cell might differentiate into a muscle cell (expressing actin, myosin) or a neuron (expressing ion channels, neurotransmitter receptors). Differentiation is usually irreversible under normal conditions, though iPSC technology reverses it artificially.

  Q7. How do bone marrow transplants use stem cells to treat disease?
    [ ] Bone marrow transplants replace damaged bone tissue with healthy bone
    [*] Donated hematopoietic stem cells from bone marrow engraft in the patient's marrow and regenerate all blood cell types — used to treat leukemia, lymphoma, sickle cell disease, and immune disorders
    [ ] Neural stem cells from bone marrow repair brain damage
    [ ] Bone marrow transplants provide temporary pain relief only
    Explanation: Bone marrow transplants (hematopoietic stem cell transplants) are the most established stem cell therapy. The patient's diseased or damaged bone marrow is destroyed with chemotherapy/radiation, then replaced with healthy hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) from a donor's bone marrow, peripheral blood, or umbilical cord blood. The transplanted HSCs migrate to the bone marrow space, engraft, and regenerate the entire blood system — red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. This can cure leukemia, sickle cell disease, and severe immune deficiencies.

  Q8. What is regenerative medicine?
    [ ] A field focused on replacing organs with mechanical devices only
    [*] A field of medicine that aims to repair or replace damaged tissues and organs using stem cells, tissue engineering, biomaterials, and growth factors to restore normal function
    [ ] Traditional medicine using herbal remedies
    [ ] Surgery to remove damaged organs without replacing them
    Explanation: Regenerative medicine is an interdisciplinary field that combines stem cell biology, tissue engineering, biomaterials science, and molecular biology to develop therapies that repair, replace, or regenerate damaged or diseased tissues and organs. Approaches include: stem cell transplantation (injecting cells to repair tissue), tissue engineering (growing tissues on biodegradable scaffolds), 3D bioprinting (layer-by-layer construction of tissue), and gene therapy (correcting genetic defects in a patient's own cells). The ultimate goal is to restore normal tissue function.

  Q9. What ethical concerns surround embryonic stem cell research?
    [*] The primary ethical concern is that obtaining ESCs requires the destruction of human embryos (blastocysts), raising questions about the moral status of early embryos
    [ ] There are no ethical concerns — everyone agrees ESC research is acceptable
    [ ] The only concern is the financial cost of the research
    [ ] ESC research is unethical because the cells don't work
    Explanation: The central ethical debate concerns the moral status of human embryos. Obtaining ESCs from the inner cell mass of a blastocyst destroys the embryo, which some consider the end of a potential human life. Views vary widely based on religious, philosophical, and cultural beliefs about when life begins. iPSC technology (reprogramming adult cells to pluripotency) partially addresses this concern by avoiding embryo destruction. Other ethical issues include informed consent for embryo donation, commercialization risks, and equitable access to stem cell therapies.

  Q10. What is somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT), and what was its most famous application?
    [*] SCNT transfers the nucleus of a somatic (body) cell into an enucleated egg cell, creating an embryo genetically identical to the donor — famously used to create Dolly the sheep (1996), the first mammal cloned from an adult cell
    [ ] SCNT is a technique that extracts DNA from fossils to clone extinct animals
    [ ] SCNT is artificial insemination used in livestock breeding
    [ ] SCNT is gene therapy that inserts new genes into stem cells
    Explanation: Somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) involves removing the nucleus from an egg cell and replacing it with the nucleus from an adult somatic cell. The reconstructed egg is stimulated to begin dividing, creating an embryo that is a genetic clone of the nuclear donor. In 1996, Ian Wilmut used SCNT to create Dolly the sheep from a mammary gland cell — proving that differentiated adult cells retain all genetic information needed to produce a complete organism. SCNT is used in research to produce patient-matched ESCs (therapeutic cloning) without requiring fertilization.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes 'Stem Cell'?
    [ ] A fully differentiated cell that performs a single specialized function and can never divide again
    [*] An undifferentiated cell capable of self-renewal (dividing to produce more stem cells) and differentiation (becoming specialized cell types), serving as the body's internal repair system
    [ ] A cancer cell that has lost all growth control
    [ ] A cell found only in plant meristems, not in animals
    Explanation: A stem cell is defined by two key properties: (1) self-renewal — the ability to undergo cell division and produce daughter cells that remain stem cells, maintaining the stem cell population, and (2) potency — the ability to differentiate into one or more specialized cell types. Stem cells range from totipotent (can form an entire organism) to pluripotent (can form any body cell type) to multipotent (limited to related cell types within a tissue). They serve as the body's repair and regeneration system, replacing cells lost through normal wear, injury, or disease.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes 'Embryonic Stem Cell'?
    [ ] A multipotent cell found in adult tissues that can only produce cell types within its lineage
    [*] A pluripotent cell derived from the inner cell mass of a blastocyst (~5 days post-fertilization) that can differentiate into virtually any cell type in the body
    [ ] A somatic cell that has been reprogrammed using Yamanaka factors
    [ ] A cell that can only form extraembryonic tissues like the placenta
    Explanation: Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) are pluripotent cells isolated from the inner cell mass (ICM) of a blastocyst — the hollow ball of cells that forms about 5 days after fertilization. The ICM normally gives rise to the embryo proper (all three germ layers: ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm). In culture, ESCs can be maintained indefinitely while retaining pluripotency, and can be directed to differentiate into neurons, cardiomyocytes, blood cells, pancreatic beta cells, and essentially any cell type of the body. Their isolation requires destruction of the blastocyst, which raises ethical concerns.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes 'Adult Stem Cell'?
    [ ] A pluripotent cell that can form any cell type, equivalent in potency to embryonic stem cells
    [*] A multipotent, tissue-resident stem cell found in differentiated organs (bone marrow, skin, gut, brain) that maintains and repairs the tissue by producing a limited range of specialized cell types within its lineage
    [ ] A cell that exists only in adults over 18 years old
    [ ] A terminally differentiated cell that has completed its development
    Explanation: Adult (somatic) stem cells are undifferentiated cells found in small numbers within mature, differentiated tissues throughout life — even in children and infants, despite the name 'adult.' They are typically multipotent, meaning they can produce several related cell types but not all body cell types. Examples: hematopoietic stem cells (bone marrow → red/white blood cells, platelets), intestinal stem cells (gut crypts → absorptive/secretory cells), neural stem cells (brain → neurons, glia), and mesenchymal stem cells (bone marrow → bone, cartilage, fat). They are the body's built-in repair crew for tissue homeostasis.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes 'Regenerative Medicine'?
    [ ] A field of medicine focused exclusively on organ transplantation from donors
    [*] An interdisciplinary field that uses stem cells, tissue engineering, biomaterials, and growth factors to repair, replace, or regenerate damaged or diseased cells, tissues, and organs to restore normal function
    [ ] Traditional herbal medicine practices used for wound healing
    [ ] The study of how amphibians naturally regrow lost limbs, with no human application
    Explanation: Regenerative medicine is a broad interdisciplinary field at the intersection of biology, engineering, and clinical medicine. Its goal is to develop therapies that restore the structure and function of damaged tissues and organs rather than merely managing symptoms. Key approaches include: stem cell transplantation (injecting cells to replace damaged tissue), tissue engineering (growing tissues on biodegradable scaffolds seeded with cells), 3D bioprinting (precisely depositing cells and biomaterials layer by layer), and gene therapy (correcting genetic defects in patient cells). It holds promise for conditions currently treatable only by organ transplant.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes 'Differentiation'?
    [ ] The process of cell division that produces genetically identical daughter cells
    [*] The developmental process by which an unspecialized (stem) cell becomes a specialized cell type with a distinct structure and function, driven by selective activation and silencing of specific genes
    [ ] The death of a cell through programmed apoptosis
    [ ] The process of DNA replication during S phase of the cell cycle
    Explanation: Differentiation is the process by which a less specialized cell becomes a more specialized cell type. All cells in an organism contain the same DNA, but different cell types express different subsets of genes — controlled by transcription factors, epigenetic modifications, and signaling pathways. As a stem cell differentiates, it progressively activates genes needed for its target function (e.g., hemoglobin genes in red blood cells, keratin in skin cells) while silencing others. Differentiation is normally irreversible, though iPSC technology artificially reverses it by reactivating pluripotency genes.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes 'Totipotent'?
    [ ] A cell that can become several related cell types within one tissue lineage
    [*] A cell with the highest potency — capable of forming ALL cell types of the organism PLUS extraembryonic tissues (placenta), a property limited to the zygote and first few cell divisions
    [ ] A cell that can become any body cell type but not placental tissue
    [ ] A cell that has permanently lost the ability to divide
    Explanation: Totipotent (Latin: totus = whole, potens = powerful) describes cells with the broadest developmental potential — they can give rise to every cell type needed for a complete organism, including all embryonic cell types AND extraembryonic structures like the placenta, umbilical cord, and amniotic membranes. Only the zygote and cells from the first ~3-4 cell divisions (up to about the 16-cell morula stage) are truly totipotent. After that, cells begin to specialize and lose totipotency, becoming pluripotent in the inner cell mass of the blastocyst.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes 'Pluripotent'?
    [ ] A cell that can form an entire organism including the placenta
    [*] A cell capable of differentiating into virtually any cell type of the body (all three germ layers: ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) but NOT extraembryonic tissues — characteristic of embryonic stem cells and iPSCs
    [ ] A cell restricted to producing only blood cell types
    [ ] A cell that can only become one specific cell type
    Explanation: Pluripotent (Latin: pluris = many) cells can differentiate into any of the ~200+ specialized cell types derived from the three embryonic germ layers — ectoderm (skin, neurons), mesoderm (muscle, bone, blood), and endoderm (gut lining, lungs, liver) — but cannot form extraembryonic tissues (placenta). Embryonic stem cells (from the blastocyst ICM) and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs, reprogrammed adult cells) are both pluripotent. Pluripotency is one step below totipotency in the potency hierarchy.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes 'Multipotent'?
    [ ] A cell that can become any cell type in the body
    [*] A stem cell that can differentiate into several related cell types within a particular tissue lineage, but not all body cell types — characteristic of most adult stem cells
    [ ] A cell that has completely lost the ability to differentiate
    [ ] A cell that can form an entire organism plus placenta
    Explanation: Multipotent (Latin: multus = many) describes stem cells with more restricted potency than pluripotent cells — they can produce multiple cell types but only within a related lineage. Most adult stem cells are multipotent. For example, hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in bone marrow can differentiate into red blood cells, white blood cells (neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes), and platelets — all blood cell types — but cannot become neurons, muscle cells, or skin cells. Neural stem cells can produce neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes but not blood cells.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes 'Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell (iPSC)'?
    [ ] A naturally occurring pluripotent cell found in adult bone marrow
    [*] An adult somatic cell (e.g., skin fibroblast) that has been artificially reprogrammed to a pluripotent state by introducing specific transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc — the Yamanaka factors), giving it ESC-like differentiation potential without embryo destruction
    [ ] An embryonic stem cell that has been genetically modified to resist immune rejection
    [ ] A stem cell created by somatic cell nuclear transfer (cloning)
    Explanation: Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) were pioneered by Shinya Yamanaka in 2006 (Nobel Prize 2012). By delivering four transcription factors — Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc (the 'Yamanaka factors') — into adult somatic cells (typically skin fibroblasts or blood cells), the cells are reprogrammed to a pluripotent state functionally equivalent to embryonic stem cells. iPSCs can differentiate into virtually any cell type. This technology avoids the ethical issues of embryo destruction and enables patient-specific cells, reducing immune rejection risk. It opened entirely new avenues for disease modeling, drug testing, and personalized regenerative therapy.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes 'Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer (SCNT)'?
    [ ] A technique that introduces transcription factors into cells to reprogram them
    [*] A cloning technique in which the nucleus of an adult somatic cell is transferred into an enucleated egg cell, producing an embryo genetically identical to the nuclear donor — used to create Dolly the sheep (1996) and for therapeutic cloning to generate patient-matched ESCs
    [ ] The natural process of fertilization where sperm and egg nuclei fuse
    [ ] Gene therapy that corrects mutations in stem cells
    Explanation: Somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) is a laboratory technique where the nucleus (containing DNA) is removed from an egg cell and replaced with the nucleus from an adult body (somatic) cell. The reconstructed egg is activated (chemically or electrically) to begin dividing, producing an embryo that is a genetic clone of the nuclear donor. SCNT achieved worldwide fame when Ian Wilmut and Keith Campbell used it in 1996 to create Dolly the sheep — the first mammal cloned from an adult somatic cell. In medicine, therapeutic cloning uses SCNT to create blastocysts from which patient-matched ESCs can be derived, avoiding immune rejection.

  Q21. A 62-year-old patient with Parkinson's disease has progressive loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra. Researchers propose transplanting iPSC-derived dopaminergic neurons into the patient's brain. Why might iPSCs be preferred over embryonic stem cells for this patient?
    [*] iPSCs can be derived from the patient's own skin cells, making the transplanted neurons genetically identical to the patient and greatly reducing immune rejection risk — while also avoiding the ethical issues of embryo destruction
    [ ] iPSCs divide faster than ESCs
    [ ] iPSCs are totipotent while ESCs are only multipotent
    [ ] iPSCs do not require any laboratory manipulation
    Explanation: iPSCs offer two major advantages for this Parkinson's patient: (1) Autologous therapy — iPSCs can be made from the patient's own somatic cells (skin fibroblasts or blood cells), reprogrammed using Yamanaka factors, then differentiated into dopaminergic neurons. Because these neurons are genetically identical to the patient, the immune system is far less likely to reject them, potentially eliminating the need for lifelong immunosuppressive drugs. (2) Ethical advantage — no embryo destruction is required. Clinical trials (e.g., by Jun Takahashi's team in Japan) have begun transplanting iPSC-derived dopaminergic progenitors into Parkinson's patients.

  Q22. A 5-year-old child with severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID, 'bubble boy disease') receives a bone marrow transplant from an HLA-matched sibling donor. Six months later, blood tests show the child is producing normal levels of all white blood cell types. What explains this recovery?
    [*] The transplanted hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) engrafted in the patient's bone marrow and, being multipotent, differentiated into all blood cell lineages — including the functional lymphocytes (T cells, B cells, NK cells) the child's own marrow could not produce
    [ ] The transplant provided mature white blood cells that will last a lifetime
    [ ] The child's own stem cells were reprogrammed by the transplant
    [ ] The donor's pluripotent stem cells formed new organs in the child
    Explanation: Bone marrow transplant is the classic stem cell therapy. The donated HSCs migrate to and colonize the patient's bone marrow (engraftment), where they self-renew and differentiate into all blood cell lineages: erythrocytes (red blood cells), platelets, and — critically for SCID — all types of leukocytes including T lymphocytes, B lymphocytes, and NK cells. Because HSCs are multipotent and self-renewing, a single successful engraftment provides a permanent, self-sustaining source of functional immune cells. HLA matching between donor and recipient minimizes graft-versus-host disease (GvHD).

  Q23. A biotech company uses Yamanaka factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc) to reprogram adult skin fibroblasts into iPSCs, then differentiates them into cardiomyocytes (heart muscle cells) for drug toxicity testing. Why is this approach valuable for pharmaceutical development?
    [*] It allows testing drug effects on functional human heart cells in a dish — including patient-specific cells that can reveal individual variations in drug response — without the ethical and practical limitations of testing directly on human hearts or relying solely on animal models
    [ ] iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes are cheaper than any alternative testing method
    [ ] The iPSC cardiomyocytes are identical to animal heart cells
    [ ] Drug testing on iPSC cells eliminates all need for clinical trials
    Explanation: iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes have transformed drug safety testing. Many drugs fail in late-stage clinical trials because they cause cardiac toxicity (arrhythmias, QT prolongation) that animal models don't predict well. iPSC-cardiomyocytes allow pharmaceutical companies to: (1) test drugs on functional human heart cells that beat and conduct electrical signals, (2) create patient-specific cardiomyocytes to identify genetic susceptibility to drug side effects (pharmacogenomics), and (3) screen thousands of compounds efficiently. This approach has detected cardiotoxicity earlier in development, potentially saving billions in failed drug programs and protecting patient safety.

  Q24. A burn victim with 40% total body surface area burns receives a skin graft grown from their own epidermal stem cells. A small biopsy of healthy skin was taken, the stem cells were expanded in culture for 3 weeks, and sheets of new skin were applied to the burns. Why are the patient's own stem cells used rather than donor skin?
    [*] Autologous (self-derived) epidermal stem cells produce skin that is genetically identical to the patient, virtually eliminating immune rejection — whereas donor skin (allograft) would be recognized as foreign and attacked by the immune system, requiring immunosuppression
    [ ] The patient's own skin cells divide faster than donor cells
    [ ] Donor skin cannot be cultured in a laboratory
    [ ] The patient's stem cells are totipotent, while donor cells are only unipotent
    Explanation: Autologous stem cell therapy (using the patient's own cells) is preferred because it avoids the #1 challenge of transplantation medicine: immune rejection. The patient's adaptive immune system (T cells) recognizes foreign MHC/HLA molecules on donor cells and mounts an attack, destroying the graft. By culturing the patient's own epidermal stem cells (which are multipotent — producing keratinocytes, melanocytes, and other skin cell types), the resulting tissue is genetically self and is not rejected. This approach, pioneered by Howard Green, has saved lives of severe burn patients by growing enough skin from a small biopsy to cover extensive wounds.

  Q25. Scientists are using 3D bioprinting to construct a kidney scaffold seeded with patient-derived iPSC cells that have been differentiated into nephron-specific cell types. What key challenge must be solved before bioprinted kidneys can replace donor kidneys for transplantation?
    [*] The bioprinted organ must develop a functional vascular network (blood supply) that integrates with the patient's circulatory system — without vascularization, cells deeper than ~200 micrometers from a blood vessel will die from oxygen and nutrient deprivation
    [ ] 3D printers cannot print biological materials at all
    [ ] iPSCs cannot be differentiated into kidney cell types
    [ ] Kidneys are too small to benefit from bioprinting technology
    Explanation: Vascularization is the most critical unsolved challenge in bioprinting complex organs like kidneys, livers, and hearts. Simple tissues (skin, cartilage) can survive via diffusion because they are thin, but solid organs require an intricate network of blood vessels to deliver oxygen and nutrients to every cell. The kidney has a particularly complex vascular architecture (each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons with elaborate capillary networks). Current 3D bioprinting can create the structural scaffold and seed it with appropriate cells, but engineering a functional, perfusable vascular network that connects to the patient's circulation remains the primary bottleneck.

  Q26. A couple expecting a baby decides to bank their newborn's umbilical cord blood. The marketing materials claim cord blood contains valuable stem cells. What type of stem cells does cord blood contain, and what conditions can they potentially treat?
    [*] Cord blood contains hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) that can reconstitute the blood and immune system — used to treat leukemia, lymphoma, sickle cell disease, thalassemia, and immune deficiencies, similar to bone marrow transplants
    [ ] Cord blood contains totipotent stem cells that can form any cell type including organs
    [ ] Cord blood contains only red blood cells with no stem cell properties
    [ ] Cord blood stem cells can only treat the baby, never anyone else
    Explanation: Umbilical cord blood is a rich, easily accessible source of hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs). These multipotent HSCs can differentiate into all blood cell types and have been used since 1988 to treat blood cancers (leukemia, lymphoma), inherited blood disorders (sickle cell disease, thalassemia), and immune deficiencies. Cord blood HSCs are immunologically naive (less likely to cause graft-versus-host disease) and don't require perfect HLA matching, making them useful when no matched bone marrow donor is available. Both private banking (for family use) and public banking (donated for anyone) are options.

  Q27. Dolly the sheep, cloned via SCNT in 1996, developed arthritis and lung disease and was euthanized at age 6 (sheep typically live ~12 years). Some scientists speculated her premature aging was linked to the fact that the donor nucleus came from a 6-year-old ewe's mammary cell. What cellular factor could explain premature aging in Dolly?
    [*] The donor somatic cell's telomeres were already shortened due to 6 years of cell divisions — when the nucleus was transferred into the enucleated egg, incomplete telomere reprogramming may have given Dolly cells with pre-shortened telomeres, causing them to reach senescence earlier than normal
    [ ] Dolly had too many chromosomes from the cloning process
    [ ] The egg cell added harmful mutations to the donor DNA
    [ ] Cloned animals cannot produce any immune cells
    Explanation: Telomere length is a biological clock — telomeres (protective DNA caps on chromosome ends) shorten with each cell division, and when they become critically short, cells enter senescence or die. The mammary cell used to clone Dolly had already undergone 6 years of divisions, shortening its telomeres. While the egg cytoplasm can partially reset epigenetic marks (reprogramming), telomere restoration by telomerase may have been incomplete. This means Dolly's cells may have started life with the biological age of a 6-year-old sheep. This 'telomere problem' is one of several reprogramming challenges in SCNT cloning (though later studies on Dolly's cloned 'sisters' showed healthier aging).

  Q28. A clinical trial uses iPSC-derived retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells to treat age-related macular degeneration (AMD), a leading cause of blindness in the elderly. The RPE cells are transplanted beneath the patient's retina. Why is the eye considered an ideal early target for stem cell therapy compared to other organs?
    [*] The eye is an immune-privileged site (limited immune surveillance), requires relatively few transplanted cells, the target cell type (RPE) is a single monolayer, disease progression can be precisely monitored with imaging, and the other eye serves as an internal control
    [ ] Eye cells never undergo immune rejection under any circumstances
    [ ] RPE cells are the easiest cell type to differentiate from any stem cell source
    [ ] The eye does not require blood supply, so vascularization is not an issue
    Explanation: The eye has several features that make it an ideal 'proving ground' for stem cell therapies: (1) Immune privilege — the blood-retinal barrier and local immunosuppressive factors limit immune cell access, reducing rejection risk. (2) Small cell requirement — AMD involves loss of RPE, a single-cell-thick layer; only ~100,000-500,000 cells are needed per transplant. (3) Accessible anatomy — cells can be precisely delivered via subretinal injection. (4) Measurable outcomes — OCT imaging and visual acuity tests can objectively track results. (5) Built-in control — the untreated eye provides comparison. The RIKEN Center in Japan and Moorfields Eye Hospital in London have conducted pioneering iPSC-RPE trials.

  Q29. Scientists grow 'mini-brains' (cerebral organoids) in a dish by differentiating iPSCs through stages that recapitulate embryonic brain development. These organoids develop cortical layers, neurons, and glial cells. Researchers use them to study Zika virus infection during brain development. What advantage do organoids offer over traditional 2D cell cultures and animal models?
    [*] Organoids are 3D structures that self-organize to mimic the architecture and cell-cell interactions of real organs, providing a more physiologically relevant model than flat 2D cultures — while using human cells that better predict human-specific responses than animal models (e.g., Zika targets human neural progenitors differently than mouse progenitors)
    [ ] Organoids are identical to fully developed human brains
    [ ] Organoids can replace all animal testing immediately
    [ ] Organoids do not require any stem cells to create
    Explanation: Organoids bridge the gap between oversimplified 2D cell cultures and complex (but not always human-relevant) animal models. When iPSCs are cultured in 3D with appropriate growth factors, they self-organize into structures resembling miniature organs — cerebral organoids develop layered cortical tissue, intestinal organoids form crypt-villus structures, and kidney organoids form nephron-like segments. For Zika research, cerebral organoids revealed that the virus preferentially infects human neural progenitor cells, causing microcephaly-like growth reduction — a finding that was difficult to replicate in mice. Organoids also enable personalized medicine: patient-specific organoids can test drug responses for that individual.

  Q30. A research team combines CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing with iPSC technology to treat sickle cell disease. They take skin cells from a patient, reprogram them to iPSCs, use CRISPR to correct the sickle cell mutation (Glu→Val at position 6 of beta-globin), differentiate the corrected iPSCs into hematopoietic stem cells, and transplant them back. What makes this combined approach so powerful?
    [*] It combines genetic correction (CRISPR fixes the disease-causing mutation) with cellular therapy (iPSC-derived HSCs provide a self-renewing source of corrected blood cells) in an autologous system (patient's own cells, minimizing immune rejection) — potentially curing a genetic disease at its root cause rather than managing symptoms
    [ ] CRISPR alone can cure sickle cell disease without any stem cell involvement
    [ ] iPSCs naturally correct genetic mutations during reprogramming
    [ ] This approach only treats symptoms and requires lifelong treatment
    Explanation: This ex vivo gene therapy approach represents the cutting edge of regenerative medicine. The workflow exploits three converging technologies: (1) iPSC reprogramming — converting the patient's somatic cells to pluripotent stem cells that can be expanded indefinitely, providing unlimited material. (2) CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing — precisely correcting the single nucleotide mutation (A→T in codon 6 of HBB gene) that causes the Glu→Val substitution creating sickle hemoglobin (HbS). (3) Directed differentiation — converting corrected iPSCs into hematopoietic stem cells that can engraft in bone marrow and produce normal red blood cells with functional hemoglobin (HbA). Because the corrected HSCs self-renew, a single successful transplant could provide a lifelong cure. This paradigm — patient cells → iPSCs → gene correction → differentiation → autologous transplant — is being pursued for sickle cell disease, beta-thalassemia, hemophilia, and other monogenic disorders.
