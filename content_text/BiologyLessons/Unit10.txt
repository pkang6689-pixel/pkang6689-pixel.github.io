--- Lesson 10.1: Overview of Meiosis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Overview of Meiosis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the expected phenotypic ratio from a monohybrid cross between two heterozygous organisms (Tt x Tt)?
A cross of Tt x Tt produces offspring in a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio (1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt). Under complete dominance, both TT and Tt show the dominant phenotype, giving a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 dominant : 1 recessive). The 1:2:1 is the genotypic ratio, not the phenotypic ratio. A 1:1 ratio results from Tt x tt. 'All dominant' would result from TT x any genotype.
What is the purpose of a Punnett square in genetics?
A Punnett square is a grid diagram named after Reginald Punnett. One parent's possible gametes are placed across the top and the other parent's along the side. Each cell in the grid shows a possible offspring genotype. For a monohybrid cross (e.g., Tt x Tt), a 2x2 grid with 4 boxes is used. For a dihybrid cross (e.g., TtYy x TtYy), a 4x4 grid with 16 boxes is used.
Two heterozygous tall pea plants (Tt x Tt) are crossed. Using a Punnett square, what is the probability that a single offspring will be homozygous recessive (tt) and therefore short?
Setting up a Punnett square for Tt x Tt yields four equally likely outcomes: TT (25%), Tt (25%), Tt (25%), and tt (25%). Only 1 out of 4 boxes is tt, so the probability of a homozygous recessive offspring is 25%. The 50% answer confuses the probability of getting 't' from one parent with getting 'tt' from both. Both parents carry a recessive allele, so producing a tt offspring is certainly possible — just not guaranteed.
A brown-eyed man (Bb) marries a blue-eyed woman (bb). Blue eyes are recessive. What is the expected phenotypic ratio among their children?
For Bb x bb: the father can pass B or b (50% each), and the mother can only pass b. Offspring are either Bb (brown eyes, 50%) or bb (blue eyes, 50%), giving a 1:1 phenotypic ratio. The 3:1 ratio comes from a cross between two heterozygotes (Bb x Bb), not this cross. Not all children will be brown-eyed because the father passes his 'b' allele to half his children, and the mother always contributes 'b.'
In a dihybrid cross between two organisms heterozygous for both traits (RrYy x RrYy), what is the expected phenotypic ratio in the offspring?
A dihybrid cross between two double heterozygotes (RrYy x RrYy) involves a 4x4 Punnett square with 16 possible outcomes. The phenotypic ratio under complete dominance is 9:3:3:1 — 9 showing both dominant traits, 3 showing only the first dominant trait, 3 showing only the second dominant trait, and 1 showing both recessive traits. The 3:1 ratio applies only to monohybrid crosses. The 1:1:1:1 ratio would come from a dihybrid test cross (RrYy x rryy).
Two parents are both carriers of sickle cell trait (Ss x Ss), where S is the normal hemoglobin allele and s is the sickle cell allele. What is the probability their child will have sickle cell disease (ss)?
For Ss x Ss, the Punnett square yields: SS (25%), Ss (25%), Ss (25%), ss (25%). The probability of having sickle cell disease (ss) is 1/4 or 25%. Each parent has a 50% chance of passing the 's' allele, and the probability of BOTH passing 's' simultaneously is 0.5 x 0.5 = 0.25 (25%). Even though both parents appear healthy, they each silently carry one copy of the recessive allele, making affected offspring possible.
In pea plants, round seeds (R) are dominant over wrinkled (r) and yellow seeds (Y) are dominant over green (y). Using the multiplication rule, what is the probability of getting a wrinkled, green offspring (rryy) from the cross RrYy x RrYy?
The multiplication rule works because the two genes assort independently. The probability of rr from Rr x Rr is 1/4, and the probability of yy from Yy x Yy is also 1/4. Since the events are independent: 1/4 x 1/4 = 1/16. This corresponds to the '1' in the 9:3:3:1 ratio. The 1/4 answer only accounts for one gene, not both. The 9/16 represents the double dominant class (round yellow), not the double recessive.
A farmer has two pea plants: one with round, yellow seeds (genotype unknown) and one with wrinkled, green seeds (rryy). He crosses them and ALL offspring have round, yellow seeds. What is the most likely genotype of the round, yellow parent?
This is a dihybrid test cross. The wrinkled, green parent is rryy and can only contribute r and y alleles to offspring. If the round, yellow parent were RrYy, approximately 1/4 of offspring would show at least one recessive phenotype (wrinkled or green). Since ALL offspring show both dominant traits (round and yellow), the first parent must be contributing only R and Y alleles to every offspring, making it RRYY (homozygous dominant for both genes). The genotype RrYy would produce some wrinkled or green offspring in a large sample.
Real-World Applications
Example: In pea plants, round seeds (R) are dominant over wrinkled (r) and yellow seeds (Y) are dominant over green (y). Using the multiplication rule, what is the probability of getting a wrinkled, green offspring (rryy) from the cross RrYy x RrYy?
The multiplication rule works because the two genes assort independently. The probability of rr from Rr x Rr is 1/4, and the probability of yy from Yy x Yy is also 1/4. Since the events are independent: 1/4 x 1/4 = 1/16. This corresponds to the '1' in the 9:3:3:1 ratio. The 1/4 answer only accounts for one gene, not both. The 9/16 represents the double dominant class (round yellow), not the double recessive.
Example: A genetic counselor examines a pedigree chart and notices that an autosomal recessive disorder appears in grandchildren but not in the parents or grandparents (the trait seems to skip a generation). Which inheritance pattern best explains this observation?
Autosomal recessive traits characteristically 'skip' generations. Unaffected carrier parents (Aa x Aa) have a 25% chance of producing affected children (aa) in each pregnancy. The trait appears to skip because carriers (Aa) do not show the phenotype — the recessive allele is masked by the dominant allele. Autosomal dominant traits typically appear in every generation. X-linked recessive shows a specific pattern affecting mostly males. Mitochondrial inheritance follows the maternal line exclusively.

--- Lesson 10.1: Overview of Meiosis (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Who is considered the 'father of modern genetics' for his pioneering work on inheritance in the 1860s?
    [ ] Robert Hooke
    [ ] Charles Darwin
    [*] Gregor Mendel
    [ ] Louis Pasteur
    Explanation: Gregor Mendel, an Augustinian friar, conducted groundbreaking experiments on pea plants in the 1860s that established the fundamental laws of inheritance. His work on how traits are passed from parents to offspring earned him the title 'father of modern genetics.' Charles Darwin is known for the theory of evolution, Louis Pasteur for microbiology and pasteurization, and Robert Hooke for coining the term 'cell.'

  Q2. What organism did Mendel use for his famous genetics experiments?
    [ ] Fruit flies (Drosophila melanogaster)
    [ ] Bacteria (Escherichia coli)
    [*] Pea plants (Pisum sativum)
    [ ] Corn plants (Zea mays)
    Explanation: Mendel used garden pea plants (Pisum sativum) because they had clearly contrasting traits (tall/short, yellow/green seeds, round/wrinkled seeds), short generation times, large numbers of offspring, and could be easily cross-pollinated or self-pollinated. Fruit flies (Drosophila) became a model genetics organism later through Thomas Hunt Morgan's work in the early 1900s.

  Q3. In a heterozygous organism, what does the dominant allele do to the recessive allele's expression?
    [*] It masks the expression of the recessive allele so only the dominant trait is observed
    [ ] It has no effect — both alleles are expressed equally
    [ ] It blends with the recessive allele to produce an intermediate trait
    [ ] It permanently deletes the recessive allele from the DNA
    Explanation: In a heterozygous organism (e.g., Tt), the dominant allele masks the expression of the recessive allele, meaning only the dominant phenotype is observed. The recessive allele is still present in the genotype — it is not deleted or altered — it simply is not expressed when a dominant allele is present. Blending inheritance was disproven by Mendel's experiments, which showed traits remain discrete.

  Q4. When is a recessive trait expressed in an organism's phenotype?
    [*] Only when the organism is homozygous recessive (e.g., tt)
    [ ] Only in the F1 generation of a cross
    [ ] Whenever at least one recessive allele is present
    [ ] Recessive traits are never expressed in any organism
    Explanation: A recessive trait is only visible in the phenotype when the organism has two copies of the recessive allele (homozygous recessive, e.g., tt). In a heterozygous organism (Tt), the dominant allele masks the recessive one. Having just one recessive allele is not enough for expression under complete dominance. Recessive traits can appear in any generation — they famously 'skip' the F1 and reappear in the F2 in Mendel's classic crosses.

  Q5. How many alleles for a single gene does a diploid organism inherit, and from whom?
    [*] Two alleles — one from each parent
    [ ] One allele — from the dominant parent only
    [ ] Four alleles — two from each parent
    [ ] It varies depending on the trait
    Explanation: For each gene, a diploid organism inherits exactly two alleles — one from the mother and one from the father. These two alleles make up the organism's genotype for that trait (e.g., TT, Tt, or tt). This is a consequence of meiosis, where each gamete carries only one allele per gene, and fertilization restores the pair. The number of alleles per gene does not vary by trait in diploid organisms.

  Q6. What does it mean when an organism is described as 'true-breeding' for a trait?
    [ ] It can only produce one offspring per generation
    [*] It always produces offspring identical to itself for that trait when self-pollinated, because it is homozygous
    [ ] It has been genetically modified in a laboratory
    [ ] It shows a blended phenotype from both parents
    Explanation: True-breeding organisms are homozygous for a trait (e.g., TT or tt). When they self-pollinate or mate with an identical genotype, all offspring have the same phenotype as the parents. Mendel established true-breeding lines (P generation) before performing his crosses to ensure a clean experimental starting point. Heterozygous organisms (Tt) would produce mixed offspring when self-pollinated, so they are not true-breeding.

  Q7. In genetics notation, the offspring of the P (parental) generation are designated as which generation?
    [ ] G1 (first genetic generation)
    [ ] F0 (zeroth filial generation)
    [*] F1 (first filial generation)
    [ ] P2 (second parental generation)
    Explanation: In genetics notation, the P generation refers to the parental generation. Their offspring are called the F1 (first filial) generation, from the Latin 'filius' meaning son. The offspring of the F1 generation are the F2 (second filial) generation, and so on. There is no P2, F0, or G1 designation in standard genetics nomenclature.

  Q8. What tool is used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross?
    [*] A Punnett square
    [ ] A karyotype
    [ ] A pedigree chart
    [ ] A gel electrophoresis apparatus
    Explanation: A Punnett square is a grid diagram used to predict the probability of offspring genotypes and phenotypes from a genetic cross. One parent's possible gametes are placed along the top and the other's along the side, with each box representing a possible offspring genotype. A karyotype displays an organism's chromosomes, gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size, and a pedigree chart tracks inheritance patterns through family generations.

  Q9. When Mendel crossed true-breeding tall plants (TT) with true-breeding short plants (tt), what phenotype did ALL F1 offspring display?
    [ ] Short — because recessive traits are stronger
    [ ] Medium height — because the alleles blend together
    [ ] Half were tall and half were short
    [*] Tall — because the dominant allele (T) masks the recessive allele (t)
    Explanation: All F1 offspring from TT x tt are heterozygous (Tt). Because tall (T) is dominant over short (t), all F1 plants display the tall phenotype. This demonstrates the principle of dominance — one copy of the dominant allele is sufficient to produce the dominant phenotype. The recessive allele (t) is still present but not expressed. Blending inheritance was disproven by Mendel's experiments.

  Q10. What is the expected phenotypic ratio from a monohybrid cross between two heterozygous organisms (Tt x Tt)?
    [ ] 1:1 (equal dominant and recessive)
    [ ] 1:2:1 (one dominant, two intermediate, one recessive)
    [*] 3:1 (three dominant phenotype to one recessive phenotype)
    [ ] All offspring show the dominant phenotype
    Explanation: A cross of Tt x Tt produces offspring in a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio (1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt). Under complete dominance, both TT and Tt show the dominant phenotype, giving a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 dominant : 1 recessive). The 1:2:1 is the genotypic ratio, not the phenotypic ratio. A 1:1 ratio results from Tt x tt. 'All dominant' would result from TT x any genotype.

  Q11. Which of the following BEST defines an allele?
    [ ] A type of protein produced by DNA during translation
    [ ] A complete chromosome inherited from one parent
    [*] An alternative form of a gene that occupies the same position (locus) on a chromosome
    [ ] Another name for a gene — the terms are interchangeable
    Explanation: An allele is one of two or more alternative versions of a gene found at the same locus (position) on a chromosome. For example, the gene for pea plant height has two alleles: T (tall) and t (short). Each diploid organism inherits two alleles per gene — one from each parent. Alleles are not proteins, not whole chromosomes, and while related to genes, the terms are not interchangeable — a gene can have multiple alleles.

  Q12. Which of the following BEST describes what it means for a trait to be dominant?
    [ ] The trait is always the most common phenotype in a population
    [ ] The trait will eventually replace the recessive trait over many generations
    [*] The trait is expressed in the phenotype when at least one dominant allele is present
    [ ] The trait requires two copies of the allele to be expressed
    Explanation: A dominant trait is expressed whenever at least one copy of the dominant allele is present (in both homozygous dominant and heterozygous individuals). Dominance does NOT mean the trait is more common in a population — a recessive trait can be more frequent if natural selection favors it. Dominant alleles do not replace recessive ones over time. Requiring two copies for expression describes a recessive trait, not a dominant one.

  Q13. Which of the following BEST describes what it means for a trait to be recessive?
    [ ] The trait will disappear from the population over time through natural selection
    [*] The trait is only expressed when two copies of the recessive allele are present (homozygous recessive)
    [ ] The trait is always harmful or causes genetic disorders
    [ ] The trait can never be passed to offspring if a parent is unaffected
    Explanation: A recessive trait requires two copies of the recessive allele (homozygous recessive, e.g., tt) to be expressed in the phenotype. In heterozygous individuals, the recessive allele is present but masked by the dominant allele. Recessive traits do not disappear from populations — they can be carried silently by heterozygous carriers for many generations. Recessive traits are not inherently harmful (e.g., blue eyes and straight hair are recessive traits).

  Q14. Which of the following BEST defines genotype?
    [ ] The total number of chromosomes found in a cell
    [*] The specific combination of alleles an organism carries for a particular trait (e.g., TT, Tt, or tt)
    [ ] The physical appearance or observable characteristics of an organism
    [ ] The process by which genes are converted into functional proteins
    Explanation: Genotype refers to the genetic makeup of an organism for a specific trait, expressed as allele combinations like TT (homozygous dominant), Tt (heterozygous), or tt (homozygous recessive). It represents what alleles are present in the DNA. The physical appearance is the phenotype, not the genotype. The chromosome count is called ploidy, and converting genes to proteins is gene expression.

  Q15. Which of the following BEST defines phenotype?
    [ ] The specific allele combination an organism carries for a trait
    [ ] The sequence of nucleotides in a gene
    [*] The observable physical characteristics of an organism resulting from its genotype and environment
    [ ] A type of genetic mutation that alters DNA sequence
    Explanation: Phenotype refers to the observable traits of an organism — such as height, color, shape, or behavior — that result from the interaction between its genotype and environmental factors. For example, a plant with genotype Tt has a tall phenotype under complete dominance. The allele combination is the genotype, not the phenotype. Phenotype is not a mutation, and it is not the DNA sequence itself.

  Q16. What does the term 'homozygous' mean in genetics?
    [ ] Having two different alleles for a particular gene
    [ ] Having only one allele for a gene instead of two
    [ ] Having alleles located on different chromosomes
    [*] Having two identical alleles for a particular gene (e.g., TT or tt)
    Explanation: Homozygous means an organism has two identical alleles at a particular gene locus — either homozygous dominant (TT) or homozygous recessive (tt). True-breeding organisms are homozygous for the traits they breed true for. Having two different alleles (e.g., Tt) is called heterozygous. All diploid organisms have two alleles per gene, located at the same locus on homologous chromosomes.

  Q17. What does the term 'heterozygous' mean in genetics?
    [ ] Having alleles inherited from only one parent
    [ ] Having no alleles for a particular gene
    [*] Having two different alleles for a particular gene (e.g., Tt)
    [ ] Having two identical alleles for a particular gene
    Explanation: Heterozygous means an organism carries two different alleles at a gene locus — for example, Tt for the height gene, with one dominant (T) and one recessive (t) allele. In Mendel's crosses, the F1 generation from TT x tt are all heterozygous (Tt). Heterozygous individuals are sometimes called 'carriers' for recessive traits because they carry the recessive allele without expressing it. Having two identical alleles is homozygous.

  Q18. What is the purpose of a Punnett square in genetics?
    [*] To predict the genotype and phenotype probabilities of offspring from a specific genetic cross
    [ ] To measure the physical size of individual chromosomes
    [ ] To graph changes in allele frequencies in a population over time
    [ ] To display a list of all known human genetic disorders
    Explanation: A Punnett square is a grid diagram named after Reginald Punnett. One parent's possible gametes are placed across the top and the other parent's along the side. Each cell in the grid shows a possible offspring genotype. For a monohybrid cross (e.g., Tt x Tt), a 2x2 grid with 4 boxes is used. For a dihybrid cross (e.g., TtYy x TtYy), a 4x4 grid with 16 boxes is used.

  Q19. Which of the following BEST states Mendel's Law of Segregation?
    [ ] Dominant alleles segregate into different cells than recessive alleles during meiosis
    [ ] Organisms always segregate into purely dominant and purely recessive groups
    [*] During gamete formation, the two alleles for each gene separate so that each gamete carries only one allele
    [ ] Genes located on the same chromosome always stay together during cell division
    Explanation: Mendel's Law of Segregation states that during meiosis, the two alleles for each gene separate (segregate) into different gametes, so each gamete carries only one allele for each trait. When fertilization occurs, the offspring receives one allele from each parent, restoring the pair. This law explains the 3:1 phenotypic ratio observed in monohybrid crosses between heterozygotes (Tt x Tt). Alleles segregate randomly — dominant and recessive alleles do not sort into separate cells preferentially.

  Q20. Which of the following BEST states Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment?
    [*] Genes for different traits on separate chromosomes are inherited independently of one another during gamete formation
    [ ] Genes on the same chromosome always assort independently of each other
    [ ] All genes in an organism are inherited together as a single unit
    [ ] Offspring always display the same trait combinations as their parents
    Explanation: Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment states that alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation, provided they are located on different chromosomes. The inheritance of one trait does not affect the inheritance of another. This law explains the 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio in dihybrid crosses (e.g., RrYy x RrYy). An important exception is genetic linkage — genes on the SAME chromosome that are close together tend to be inherited as a unit and do NOT assort independently.

  Q21. Two heterozygous tall pea plants (Tt x Tt) are crossed. Using a Punnett square, what is the probability that a single offspring will be homozygous recessive (tt) and therefore short?
    [ ] 75% (3 out of 4 possible outcomes)
    [*] 25% (1 out of 4 possible outcomes)
    [ ] 50% (2 out of 4 possible outcomes)
    [ ] 0% — it is impossible when both parents are tall
    Explanation: Setting up a Punnett square for Tt x Tt yields four equally likely outcomes: TT (25%), Tt (25%), Tt (25%), and tt (25%). Only 1 out of 4 boxes is tt, so the probability of a homozygous recessive offspring is 25%. The 50% answer confuses the probability of getting 't' from one parent with getting 'tt' from both. Both parents carry a recessive allele, so producing a tt offspring is certainly possible — just not guaranteed.

  Q22. A brown-eyed man (Bb) marries a blue-eyed woman (bb). Blue eyes are recessive. What is the expected phenotypic ratio among their children?
    [ ] 3 brown-eyed : 1 blue-eyed
    [ ] All brown-eyed
    [*] 1 brown-eyed : 1 blue-eyed (50% each)
    [ ] All blue-eyed
    Explanation: For Bb x bb: the father can pass B or b (50% each), and the mother can only pass b. Offspring are either Bb (brown eyes, 50%) or bb (blue eyes, 50%), giving a 1:1 phenotypic ratio. The 3:1 ratio comes from a cross between two heterozygotes (Bb x Bb), not this cross. Not all children will be brown-eyed because the father passes his 'b' allele to half his children, and the mother always contributes 'b.'

  Q23. A plant breeder has a purple-flowered pea plant but does not know if it is PP or Pp. She crosses it with a white-flowered plant (pp) and all 40 offspring have purple flowers. What can she most likely conclude about the purple parent's genotype?
    [ ] The purple parent must be Pp because some offspring could still be white in the future
    [*] The purple parent is most likely homozygous dominant (PP), because no recessive phenotype appeared among many offspring
    [ ] The test is completely inconclusive regardless of how many offspring are produced
    [ ] White flowers are not actually recessive in this species
    Explanation: This is a test cross — crossing an organism of unknown genotype with a homozygous recessive (pp). If the purple parent were Pp, approximately 50% of offspring would be pp (white). With 40 offspring all showing purple flowers, the probability that the parent is Pp is astronomically low (0.5^40). The parent is almost certainly PP. While absolute proof requires genetic sequencing, 40 offspring with no recessive phenotype is very strong statistical evidence.

  Q24. In a dihybrid cross between two organisms heterozygous for both traits (RrYy x RrYy), what is the expected phenotypic ratio in the offspring?
    [*] 9:3:3:1 (9 both dominant : 3 first dominant only : 3 second dominant only : 1 both recessive)
    [ ] 1:1:1:1 (equal proportions of all four phenotypes)
    [ ] 3:1 (three dominant to one recessive)
    [ ] 1:2:1 (one dominant, two intermediate, one recessive)
    Explanation: A dihybrid cross between two double heterozygotes (RrYy x RrYy) involves a 4x4 Punnett square with 16 possible outcomes. The phenotypic ratio under complete dominance is 9:3:3:1 — 9 showing both dominant traits, 3 showing only the first dominant trait, 3 showing only the second dominant trait, and 1 showing both recessive traits. The 3:1 ratio applies only to monohybrid crosses. The 1:1:1:1 ratio would come from a dihybrid test cross (RrYy x rryy).

  Q25. Two parents are both carriers of sickle cell trait (Ss x Ss), where S is the normal hemoglobin allele and s is the sickle cell allele. What is the probability their child will have sickle cell disease (ss)?
    [ ] 75% — most children will be affected since both parents carry the allele
    [ ] 0% — because both parents are healthy, no child can be affected
    [*] 25% — there is a 1 in 4 chance the child inherits 's' from both parents
    [ ] 50% — half the children will be affected
    Explanation: For Ss x Ss, the Punnett square yields: SS (25%), Ss (25%), Ss (25%), ss (25%). The probability of having sickle cell disease (ss) is 1/4 or 25%. Each parent has a 50% chance of passing the 's' allele, and the probability of BOTH passing 's' simultaneously is 0.5 x 0.5 = 0.25 (25%). Even though both parents appear healthy, they each silently carry one copy of the recessive allele, making affected offspring possible.

  Q26. In a family, neither parent has cystic fibrosis (a recessive condition), yet their child is diagnosed with it. How is this possible according to Mendelian genetics?
    [ ] The child must have developed a new spontaneous mutation not present in either parent
    [ ] One parent must secretly have cystic fibrosis but was misdiagnosed
    [*] Both parents are heterozygous carriers (Cc) who each passed their recessive allele (c) to the child, making the child cc
    [ ] This is impossible — recessive traits cannot skip generations
    Explanation: This is a classic example of autosomal recessive inheritance. Both parents are carriers (Cc) — they each carry one copy of the recessive allele without showing symptoms because the dominant allele masks it. When both parents pass the 'c' allele to their child, the child is cc and has cystic fibrosis. This demonstrates how recessive traits can 'skip' generations — they hide in heterozygous carriers. From Cc x Cc, there is a 25% chance of an affected child in each pregnancy.

  Q27. A cross between a black rabbit and a white rabbit produces approximately half black and half white offspring. Black fur (B) is dominant over white fur (b). What are the most likely genotypes of the parents?
    [ ] Both parents are Bb (heterozygous), producing a 3:1 ratio
    [ ] Both parents are bb (homozygous recessive)
    [ ] The black parent is BB (homozygous dominant) and the white parent is bb
    [*] The black parent is Bb (heterozygous) and the white parent is bb (homozygous recessive)
    Explanation: An approximately 1:1 ratio of black to white offspring indicates a test cross: Bb x bb. The heterozygous black parent contributes B or b with equal probability, and the homozygous recessive white parent only contributes b. This gives Bb (black, 50%) and bb (white, 50%) offspring. If the black parent were BB, ALL offspring would be Bb (black). A Bb x Bb cross would produce a 3:1 ratio, not 1:1. Both parents cannot be bb because black offspring were produced.

  Q28. In pea plants, round seeds (R) are dominant over wrinkled (r) and yellow seeds (Y) are dominant over green (y). Using the multiplication rule, what is the probability of getting a wrinkled, green offspring (rryy) from the cross RrYy x RrYy?

--- Lesson 10.2: Sexual Reproduction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sexual Reproduction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In genetics, what is a carrier?
A carrier is heterozygous — they have one dominant and one recessive allele. The dominant allele masks the recessive one, so the carrier appears unaffected, but they can pass the recessive allele to offspring. This is especially important for X-linked traits, where carrier mothers can pass the allele to sons who will express it.
A woman who is a carrier for color blindness (X^C X^c) has children with a man who has normal vision (X^C Y). What is the probability that a DAUGHTER will be color blind?
Daughters receive one X from each parent. Father always gives X^C (normal). Mother gives either X^C or X^c. So daughters are either X^C X^C (normal) or X^C X^c (carrier, but pheno­typically normal). No daughter can be X^c X^c because the father cannot contribute X^c. To have a color-blind daughter, the father would also need to be color blind (X^c Y).
A color-blind man (X^c Y) marries a woman with normal vision whose father was color blind. What is the probability that their SON will be color blind?
The woman's father was color blind (X^c Y), so he must have given her X^c. Since she has normal vision, her genotype must be X^C X^c (carrier). Crossed with a color-blind man (X^c Y): sons get their X from the mother — either X^C (normal) or X^c (color blind). So 50% of sons will be color blind. The father's X^c goes only to daughters, not sons (sons get Y from dad).
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Possible offspring: X^H X^H (normal daughter), X^H X^h (carrier daughter), X^H Y (normal son), X^h Y (hemophiliac son). Only one out of four possible outcomes produces hemophilia (the affected son). So 25% of ALL children are expected to be hemophiliac. Note: 50% of SONS will be affected, but across all children it is 25%.

    [ ] Polygenic inheritance
    [ ] Pleiotropy
    [*] Epistasis
    [ ] Incomplete dominance

    [*] X chromosome

    [ ] 1:1
    [ ] 3:1

    [ ] 9:3:3:1

    [ ] Type B
    [*] Type O
    [ ] Type A
    [ ] Type AB
    Explanation: Type O blood (genotype ii) has neither A nor B antigens on its red blood cells, so it does not trigger an immune reaction in recipients with any blood type. Type AB is the universal recipient because individuals with AB blood have both antigens and produce no anti-A or anti-B antibodies.

    [ ] Males have two X chromosomes, doubling their chance of inheriting the allele
    Explanation: Males are hemizygous for X-linked genes — they have only one X (from their mother) and one Y. Because there is no second X to carry a dominant allele that could mask the recessive one, any recessive allele on the X is expressed. Females need two copies of the recessive allele (one on each X) to show the trait.

    [*] A pattern where the heterozygote shows a blended, intermediate phenotype between the two homozygotes
    [ ] A pattern where a gene on the X chromosome is only expressed in males

    [ ] A pattern where one allele completely masks the other in the heterozygote
    Explanation: Incomplete dominance produces a heterozygote phenotype that is intermediate between the two homozygotes. The key word is 'blended' — for example, red × white producing pink. Neither allele fully dominates, so the result appears to be a mixture of both parental traits.

  Q12. Which of the following best defines codominance?
    [ ] A pattern where many genes each contribute to one trait
    [*] A pattern where both alleles are fully and distinctly expressed at the same time in the heterozygote
    [ ] A pattern where the heterozygote shows a blended intermediate phenotype
    [ ] A pattern where one gene masks the expression of another gene at a different locus
    Explanation: In codominance, both alleles contribute equally and distinctly to the phenotype — they do not blend. Examples include AB blood type (both A and B antigens present) and roan cattle (separate red and white hairs side by side). The critical distinction from incomplete dominance is that both traits appear fully, not as a mix.

  Q13. Which of the following best defines polygenic inheritance?
    [*] A pattern where multiple genes each contribute small additive effects to a single trait, producing continuous variation
    [ ] A pattern where more than two alleles exist for one gene in a population
    [ ] A pattern where one gene masks the expression of another gene
    [ ] A pattern where one gene controls multiple unrelated traits
    Explanation: Polygenic inheritance means MANY genes → ONE trait. Each gene adds a small effect, and together they produce a continuous range of phenotypes (like height or skin color) that follow a bell curve. This is the opposite of pleiotropy (ONE gene → MANY traits).

  Q14. Which of the following best defines epistasis?
    [ ] A pattern where traits are linked to sex chromosomes
    [ ] A pattern where heterozygotes show a blended phenotype
    [ ] A pattern where one gene controls multiple unrelated traits
    [*] A pattern where one gene masks or modifies the phenotypic expression of a different gene
    Explanation: Epistasis is a gene-gene interaction where one gene (the epistatic gene) controls whether another gene (the hypostatic gene) is expressed. Labrador coat color is the classic example: the E gene determines whether pigment is deposited at all, overriding whatever the B gene codes for.

  Q15. Which of the following best defines pleiotropy?
    [*] A pattern where a single gene influences multiple, seemingly unrelated phenotypic traits
    [ ] A pattern where more than two alleles exist for a gene in the population
    [ ] A pattern where many genes each contribute a small amount to one trait
    [ ] A pattern where one gene masks the expression of another gene
    Explanation: Pleiotropy means ONE gene → MANY traits. The sickle cell gene is a classic example: a single mutation in the beta-globin gene causes anemia, joint pain, organ damage, and increased malaria resistance — all from one gene. Pleiotropy is the conceptual opposite of polygenic inheritance.

  Q16. Which of the following best defines the term 'multiple alleles'?

    [ ] A trait controlled by many different genes on different chromosomes
    [ ] A situation where the heterozygote shows a blended phenotype
    [ ] A mutation that creates a new gene not previously found in a species
    Explanation: Multiple alleles means more than two versions of a gene exist in the population. The ABO blood type system is the best example: three alleles (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i) exist, but any one person only has two of them. This is different from polygenic inheritance, where many genes (not many alleles of one gene) affect a trait.

    [ ] A trait that is always dominant in males and recessive in females

    [*] A trait controlled by a gene located on a sex chromosome (usually the X chromosome)
    Explanation: Sex-linked traits are determined by genes on sex chromosomes. Most sex-linked traits in humans are X-linked because the X chromosome is much larger and carries many more genes than the Y. Hemophilia and red-green color blindness are classic X-linked recessive examples.

  Q18. In genetics, what is a carrier?

--- Lesson 10.3: Meiosis I (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Meiosis I
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
DNA is composed of two strands wound around each other. What is the name of this three-dimensional shape?
Watson and Crick discovered in 1953 that DNA has a double helix structure — two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wound around each other with the sugar-phosphate backbones on the outside and the nitrogenous bases facing inward. The bases on opposite strands are held together by hydrogen bonds (A-T and G-C), forming the 'rungs' of the twisted ladder.
What is the universal start codon that signals the beginning of translation?
AUG is the universal start codon. It serves a dual purpose: it signals the ribosome to begin translation AND it codes for the amino acid methionine. Every newly synthesized protein begins with methionine (though it may be removed later by post-translational processing). The three stop codons — UAA, UAG, and UGA — signal termination of translation and do not code for any amino acid.
What is a codon?
A codon is a triplet of three consecutive nucleotides on mRNA that codes for a specific amino acid or serves as a stop signal. With four possible RNA bases (A, U, G, C) in groups of three, there are 64 possible codons: 61 specify amino acids and 3 (UAA, UAG, UGA) are stop codons. The genetic code is degenerate, meaning most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon.
What is the Central Dogma of molecular biology?
The Central Dogma, proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, states that genetic information flows in one general direction: DNA is transcribed into RNA, and RNA is translated into protein. Information does not flow backward from protein to nucleic acid. Exceptions include reverse transcriptase (RNA → DNA, found in retroviruses like HIV) and RNA replicase (RNA → RNA), but protein → nucleic acid transfer has never been observed.
What is the primary function of messenger RNA (mRNA)?
Messenger RNA (mRNA) serves as the intermediary between DNA and protein. During transcription, the information in a gene's DNA is copied into mRNA. This mRNA then exits the nucleus and travels to ribosomes in the cytoplasm, where its codon sequence is read during translation to direct the assembly of a specific protein. mRNA is a temporary molecule that is eventually degraded after being translated.
What is the primary function of transfer RNA (tRNA)?
Transfer RNA (tRNA) is the adaptor molecule that bridges the nucleotide language of mRNA and the amino acid language of proteins. Each tRNA molecule has an anticodon (complementary to an mRNA codon) at one end and carries the corresponding amino acid at its other end. Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are enzymes that 'charge' each tRNA with the correct amino acid, ensuring translation accuracy.
What is the primary function of ribosomal RNA (rRNA)?
Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is the most abundant type of RNA in cells and makes up about 60% of the ribosome's mass. rRNA provides both structural scaffolding and catalytic activity — it is the rRNA (not ribosomal proteins) that catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids, making the ribosome a ribozyme. Ribosomes have two subunits (large and small) that come together around mRNA during translation.
What is an anticodon?
An anticodon is a set of three nucleotides on the bottom loop of a tRNA molecule that is complementary to a codon on the mRNA. During translation, the anticodon base-pairs with the mRNA codon in an antiparallel fashion (e.g., mRNA codon 5′-GCA-3′ pairs with tRNA anticodon 3′-CGU-5′). This ensures the correct amino acid is added to the growing polypeptide chain.
What is the role of DNA polymerase during DNA replication?
DNA polymerase is the central enzyme of replication. It reads the template strand in the 3′→5′ direction and adds complementary deoxyribonucleotides to the new strand in the 5′→3′ direction. DNA polymerase also has 3′→5′ exonuclease (proofreading) activity, allowing it to remove and correct mismatched nucleotides. On the leading strand it works continuously; on the lagging strand it synthesizes short Okazaki fragments.
A DNA template strand reads 3′-TACGGATTC-5′. During transcription, RNA polymerase uses this strand to build an mRNA molecule. What is the resulting mRNA sequence?
To determine the mRNA sequence, apply complementary base pairing to the DNA template strand, remembering that RNA uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T). Template 3′-TACGGATTC-5′ yields mRNA 5′-AUGCCUAAG-3′: T→A, A→U, C→G, G→C, G→C, A→U, T→A, T→A, C→G. The mRNA strand is always written 5′→3′ because that is the direction of synthesis.
A researcher discovers that cells treated with a chemical inhibitor of helicase cannot complete DNA replication. What is the most direct reason for this failure?
Helicase is the first enzyme to act at the replication fork — it breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs to separate the two strands of the double helix. If helicase is inhibited, the DNA remains wound up and the single-stranded templates needed by DNA polymerase are never exposed. Joining Okazaki fragments is ligase's job, primer synthesis is primase's job, and translation is unrelated to replication.
During translation, the ribosome is positioned at the mRNA codon 5′-GCA-3′. A tRNA molecule must deliver the amino acid alanine. What is the anticodon sequence on this tRNA?
The tRNA anticodon must be complementary AND antiparallel to the mRNA codon. For mRNA codon 5′-GCA-3′: G pairs with C, C pairs with G, A pairs with U. The anticodon reads 3′-CGU-5′. Directionality is critical — mRNA and tRNA strands run antiparallel to each other during codon-anticodon base pairing, just as the two strands of DNA are antiparallel.
A point mutation changes the mRNA codon CUU (leucine) to CUC. Consulting a codon table reveals that CUC also codes for leucine. What type of mutation is this, and what is its effect on the protein?
This is a silent (synonymous) mutation — the codon changes from CUU to CUC, but both code for leucine. The protein's amino acid sequence is completely unaffected. Silent mutations are possible because the genetic code is degenerate (redundant), meaning most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons. Changes in the third (wobble) position of a codon often result in silent mutations.
A mutation changes the mRNA codon UAC (tyrosine) to the stop codon UAA. What is the most likely consequence for the protein being synthesized?
This is a nonsense mutation — a codon that normally specifies an amino acid (UAC = tyrosine) is changed to a premature stop codon (UAA). When the ribosome reaches this new stop codon, translation terminates early, producing a truncated protein that is usually nonfunctional. Nonsense mutations are typically more harmful than missense mutations because they can eliminate large portions of the protein's amino acid sequence.

    [ ] Peptide bonds — these link amino acids in proteins, not nucleotides in DNA
    [ ] Ionic bonds — these involve transfer of electrons, not found between DNA strands
    Explanation: Hydrogen bonds hold the two DNA strands together between complementary bases: two hydrogen bonds between A-T and three between G-C. These bonds are individually weak, but collectively (billions of them in a human chromosome) they provide stability while still allowing the strands to be separated for replication and transcription. Covalent phosphodiester bonds hold nucleotides together WITHIN each strand.

  Q4. During DNA replication, which enzyme unwinds the double helix by breaking hydrogen bonds between base pairs?
    [*] Helicase — it separates the two DNA strands at the replication fork
    [ ] Primase — it synthesizes short RNA primers
    [ ] DNA ligase — it joins DNA fragments together
    [ ] DNA polymerase — it adds new nucleotides but does not unwind DNA
    Explanation: Helicase is the enzyme that 'unzips' the DNA double helix at the replication fork by using ATP energy to break the hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs. This creates two single-stranded templates that DNA polymerase can then use to synthesize new complementary strands. Without helicase, the double helix cannot be opened and replication cannot proceed.

  Q5. Which enzyme is responsible for adding new nucleotides to a growing DNA strand during replication?
    [ ] Ligase — this enzyme joins DNA fragments but does not add individual nucleotides
    [ ] RNA polymerase — this enzyme synthesizes RNA during transcription, not DNA during replication
    [ ] Helicase — this enzyme unwinds DNA but does not add nucleotides
    [*] DNA polymerase — it reads the template strand and adds complementary nucleotides in the 5′ to 3′ direction
    Explanation: DNA polymerase is the main enzyme of replication. It reads the template strand in the 3′→5′ direction and synthesizes the new complementary strand in the 5′→3′ direction, adding nucleotides one at a time following base pairing rules (A with T, G with C). DNA polymerase also has proofreading ability, removing incorrectly paired nucleotides to maintain an error rate of roughly 1 in a billion bases.

  Q6. DNA replication is described as semiconservative. What does this mean?
    [ ] The original DNA molecule is completely conserved and an entirely new copy is made alongside it
    [ ] Only half of the genome is copied during each round of cell division
    [*] Each new DNA molecule contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand
    [ ] Old and new DNA segments are randomly scattered throughout both daughter molecules
    Explanation: Semiconservative replication means each daughter DNA molecule retains ('conserves') one original parental strand while the other strand is newly synthesized. This was confirmed by the Meselson-Stahl experiment (1958), which used heavy (15N) and light (14N) nitrogen isotopes to track old versus new DNA strands. After one round of replication, all DNA had intermediate density, proving each molecule was half old, half new.

  Q7. Which enzyme synthesizes mRNA from a DNA template during transcription?
    [ ] Ribosome — this reads mRNA during translation, not during transcription
    [ ] DNA polymerase — this enzyme copies DNA during replication, not transcription
    [*] RNA polymerase — it reads the DNA template strand and builds a complementary mRNA strand
    [ ] Helicase — this enzyme unwinds DNA but does not synthesize RNA
    Explanation: RNA polymerase is the enzyme responsible for transcription. It binds to the promoter region of a gene, unwinds a small section of DNA, reads the template strand 3′→5′, and synthesizes a complementary mRNA strand 5′→3′. Unlike DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase does NOT require a primer to begin synthesis. In eukaryotes, RNA polymerase II is specifically responsible for transcribing protein-coding genes into mRNA.

  Q8. Where does translation (protein synthesis) take place in a eukaryotic cell?
    [*] At ribosomes in the cytoplasm — mRNA is decoded into a polypeptide chain here
    [ ] In the mitochondria — some mitochondrial proteins are made here, but most translation occurs in the cytoplasm
    [ ] In the Golgi apparatus — this organelle modifies and packages proteins after they are made
    [ ] In the nucleus — this is where transcription occurs, not translation
    Explanation: Translation occurs at ribosomes in the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells. After mRNA is transcribed and processed in the nucleus, it is exported through nuclear pores into the cytoplasm where ribosomes (composed of rRNA and proteins) read the mRNA codons and assemble amino acids into a polypeptide chain. Ribosomes can be free in the cytoplasm or attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum.

  Q9. What is the universal start codon that signals the beginning of translation?
    [ ] UAG — this is a stop codon, not a start codon
    [ ] UGA — this is a stop codon, not a start codon
    [ ] UAA — this is a stop codon, not a start codon
    [*] AUG — it codes for methionine and is the first codon read by the ribosome
    Explanation: AUG is the universal start codon. It serves a dual purpose: it signals the ribosome to begin translation AND it codes for the amino acid methionine. Every newly synthesized protein begins with methionine (though it may be removed later by post-translational processing). The three stop codons — UAA, UAG, and UGA — signal termination of translation and do not code for any amino acid.

  Q10. Which molecule carries amino acids to the ribosome during translation?
    [ ] DNA — it stores genetic information in the nucleus and does not participate directly in translation
    [ ] rRNA (ribosomal RNA) — it is a structural component of ribosomes, not an amino acid carrier
    [ ] mRNA (messenger RNA) — it carries the genetic code but does not transport amino acids
    [*] tRNA (transfer RNA) — it has an anticodon that matches the mRNA codon and carries the corresponding amino acid
    Explanation: Transfer RNA (tRNA) is the adaptor molecule of translation. Each tRNA has two critical regions: an anticodon (three nucleotides complementary to an mRNA codon) and an amino acid attachment site at its 3′ end. Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase enzymes charge each tRNA with the correct amino acid. During translation, tRNA anticodons base-pair with mRNA codons in the ribosome, delivering amino acids in the order specified by the mRNA.

  Q11. Which of the following best defines DNA replication?
    [ ] The process of assembling amino acids into a protein using information from mRNA
    [*] The process by which a cell copies its entire DNA molecule before cell division, producing two identical double helices
    [ ] The breakdown and recycling of old DNA molecules during programmed cell death
    [ ] The process of copying a gene's DNA sequence into a messenger RNA molecule
    Explanation: DNA replication is the biological process by which a cell duplicates its entire DNA, producing two identical copies from one original molecule. This occurs during the S phase of the cell cycle, before mitosis or meiosis, ensuring each daughter cell receives a complete genome. Key enzymes include helicase (unwinds), primase (lays RNA primers), DNA polymerase (adds nucleotides), and ligase (seals fragments).

  Q12. Which of the following best defines transcription in molecular biology?
    [ ] The folding of a polypeptide chain into its three-dimensional functional shape
    [*] The synthesis of an mRNA molecule from a DNA template by RNA polymerase, occurring in the nucleus of eukaryotes
    [ ] The duplication of the entire DNA molecule before cell division
    [ ] The assembly of a polypeptide chain from mRNA instructions at the ribosome
    Explanation: Transcription is the first step of gene expression in which RNA polymerase reads a DNA template strand (3′→5′) and synthesizes a complementary mRNA strand (5′→3′). In eukaryotes this occurs in the nucleus. The resulting pre-mRNA undergoes processing (5′ cap, poly-A tail, intron splicing) to produce mature mRNA, which is then exported to the cytoplasm for translation.

  Q13. Which of the following best defines translation in molecular biology?
    [ ] The duplication of the entire genome before cell division
    [ ] The copying of DNA into a complementary mRNA strand by RNA polymerase
    [ ] The transport of mRNA from the nucleus to the cytoplasm through nuclear pores
    [*] The process by which ribosomes decode mRNA codons and assemble a specific sequence of amino acids into a polypeptide
    Explanation: Translation is the second step of gene expression where the ribosome reads mRNA in sets of three nucleotides (codons), each specifying an amino acid. Transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules deliver the correct amino acids, which are joined by peptide bonds into a growing polypeptide chain. Translation begins at the start codon AUG and continues until a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) is reached.

  Q14. What is a codon?
    [ ] A chemical bond that links amino acids together in a polypeptide chain
    [*] A sequence of three consecutive nucleotides on mRNA that specifies a particular amino acid or a stop signal
    [ ] A single nucleotide in a DNA strand that encodes one amino acid
    [ ] A protein that assists in the folding of other proteins
    Explanation: A codon is a triplet of three consecutive nucleotides on mRNA that codes for a specific amino acid or serves as a stop signal. With four possible RNA bases (A, U, G, C) in groups of three, there are 64 possible codons: 61 specify amino acids and 3 (UAA, UAG, UGA) are stop codons. The genetic code is degenerate, meaning most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon.

  Q15. What is the Central Dogma of molecular biology?
    [*] The principle that genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to protein (DNA → RNA → Protein)
    [ ] The rule that DNA must replicate before every cell division
    [ ] The concept that genetic mutations are always harmful to organisms
    [ ] The idea that all living cells arise from pre-existing cells
    Explanation: The Central Dogma, proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, states that genetic information flows in one general direction: DNA is transcribed into RNA, and RNA is translated into protein. Information does not flow backward from protein to nucleic acid. Exceptions include reverse transcriptase (RNA → DNA, found in retroviruses like HIV) and RNA replicase (RNA → RNA), but protein → nucleic acid transfer has never been observed.

  Q16. What is the primary function of messenger RNA (mRNA)?
    [ ] It carries amino acids to the ribosome and matches them to the correct mRNA codons
    [ ] It unwinds the DNA double helix during replication
    [ ] It forms the structural and catalytic core of ribosomes
    [*] It carries the genetic code copied from DNA in the nucleus to ribosomes in the cytoplasm for translation
    Explanation: Messenger RNA (mRNA) serves as the intermediary between DNA and protein. During transcription, the information in a gene's DNA is copied into mRNA. This mRNA then exits the nucleus and travels to ribosomes in the cytoplasm, where its codon sequence is read during translation to direct the assembly of a specific protein. mRNA is a temporary molecule that is eventually degraded after being translated.

  Q17. What is the primary function of transfer RNA (tRNA)?
    [ ] It carries the genetic code from DNA to the ribosome for translation
    [*] It carries specific amino acids to the ribosome and uses its anticodon to match them to the correct mRNA codon
    [ ] It forms the structural framework and catalytic center of the ribosome
    [ ] It synthesizes new DNA strands by adding nucleotides during replication
    Explanation: Transfer RNA (tRNA) is the adaptor molecule that bridges the nucleotide language of mRNA and the amino acid language of proteins. Each tRNA molecule has an anticodon (complementary to an mRNA codon) at one end and carries the corresponding amino acid at its other end. Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are enzymes that 'charge' each tRNA with the correct amino acid, ensuring translation accuracy.

  Q18. What is the primary function of ribosomal RNA (rRNA)?
    [ ] It carries the genetic message from DNA to the ribosome
    [ ] It copies the DNA molecule during replication
    [*] It forms the structural and catalytic core of the ribosome, the molecular machine where translation occurs
    [ ] It transports amino acids to the ribosome during translation
    Explanation: Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is the most abundant type of RNA in cells and makes up about 60% of the ribosome's mass. rRNA provides both structural scaffolding and catalytic activity — it is the rRNA (not ribosomal proteins) that catalyzes the formation of peptide bonds between amino acids, making the ribosome a ribozyme. Ribosomes have two subunits (large and small) that come together around mRNA during translation.

  Q19. What is an anticodon?
    [ ] An enzyme that degrades codons after translation is complete
    [*] A three-nucleotide sequence on tRNA that is complementary and antiparallel to an mRNA codon
    [ ] A three-nucleotide sequence on mRNA that codes for a specific amino acid
    [ ] A signal on mRNA that tells the ribosome to stop translating
    Explanation: An anticodon is a set of three nucleotides on the bottom loop of a tRNA molecule that is complementary to a codon on the mRNA. During translation, the anticodon base-pairs with the mRNA codon in an antiparallel fashion (e.g., mRNA codon 5′-GCA-3′ pairs with tRNA anticodon 3′-CGU-5′). This ensures the correct amino acid is added to the growing polypeptide chain.

  Q20. What is the role of DNA polymerase during DNA replication?
    [ ] It joins Okazaki fragments together on the lagging strand
    [ ] It unwinds the DNA double helix at the replication fork
    [ ] It synthesizes mRNA from a DNA template during transcription
    [*] It synthesizes new DNA strands by adding nucleotides complementary to the template strand in the 5′→3′ direction
    Explanation: DNA polymerase is the central enzyme of replication. It reads the template strand in the 3′→5′ direction and adds complementary deoxyribonucleotides to the new strand in the 5′→3′ direction. DNA polymerase also has 3′→5′ exonuclease (proofreading) activity, allowing it to remove and correct mismatched nucleotides. On the leading strand it works continuously; on the lagging strand it synthesizes short Okazaki fragments.

  Q21. A DNA template strand reads 3′-TACGGATTC-5′. During transcription, RNA polymerase uses this strand to build an mRNA molecule. What is the resulting mRNA sequence?
    [ ] 3′-AUGCCUAAG-5′ — the bases are correct but the direction is reversed; mRNA is built 5′→3′
    [ ] 5′-ATGCCTAAG-3′ — this uses thymine instead of uracil, which would be DNA, not mRNA
    [ ] 5′-TACGGATTC-3′ — this is simply a copy of the template strand, not its complement
    [*] 5′-AUGCCUAAG-3′ — each DNA base is matched with its RNA complement (T→A, A→U, C→G, G→C)
    Explanation: To determine the mRNA sequence, apply complementary base pairing to the DNA template strand, remembering that RNA uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T). Template 3′-TACGGATTC-5′ yields mRNA 5′-AUGCCUAAG-3′: T→A, A→U, C→G, G→C, G→C, A→U, T→A, T→A, C→G. The mRNA strand is always written 5′→3′ because that is the direction of synthesis.

  Q22. A mutation changes the mRNA codon GAG (which codes for glutamic acid) to GUG (which codes for valine). The resulting protein has a different amino acid at that position, altering its function. What type of mutation is this?
    [*] Missense mutation — a single nucleotide change results in a different amino acid being incorporated
    [ ] Frameshift mutation — this would require an insertion or deletion shifting the reading frame
    [ ] Nonsense mutation — this would create a premature stop codon, not substitute an amino acid
    [ ] Silent mutation — the amino acid would remain the same despite the codon change
    Explanation: This is a classic missense mutation — a single nucleotide substitution (A→U in the second position) changes the codon from GAG to GUG, replacing glutamic acid with valine. This exact mutation causes sickle cell disease in the beta-globin gene. Missense mutations alter protein function when the substituted amino acid has different chemical properties (e.g., hydrophilic glutamic acid vs. hydrophobic valine).

  Q23. A researcher discovers that cells treated with a chemical inhibitor of helicase cannot complete DNA replication. What is the most direct reason for this failure?
    [*] The DNA double helix cannot be unwound, so the template strands are not exposed for copying
    [ ] RNA primers cannot be synthesized on the template strands
    [ ] Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand cannot be joined together
    [ ] mRNA molecules cannot be translated into proteins at the ribosome
    Explanation: Helicase is the first enzyme to act at the replication fork — it breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs to separate the two strands of the double helix. If helicase is inhibited, the DNA remains wound up and the single-stranded templates needed by DNA polymerase are never exposed. Joining Okazaki fragments is ligase's job, primer synthesis is primase's job, and translation is unrelated to replication.

  Q24. An mRNA strand contains the following codons: AUG-GGC-UAC-UGA. How many amino acids will be incorporated into the polypeptide chain before translation stops?
    [ ] 2 amino acids — only AUG and GGC are translated before the ribosome stops
    [*] 3 amino acids (Met-Gly-Tyr) — AUG starts translation, GGC and UAC each add an amino acid, and UGA is a stop codon
    [ ] 4 amino acids — all four codons including UGA code for amino acids
    [ ] 1 amino acid — only the start codon AUG is translated
    Explanation: Reading the mRNA codons: AUG = methionine (start), GGC = glycine, UAC = tyrosine, UGA = stop. The ribosome translates three codons into amino acids (Met-Gly-Tyr) before reaching the stop codon UGA, which signals release factor proteins to terminate translation. The stop codon does NOT code for an amino acid — it terminates the polypeptide chain. The resulting tripeptide is methionine-glycine-tyrosine.

  Q25. During translation, the ribosome is positioned at the mRNA codon 5′-GCA-3′. A tRNA molecule must deliver the amino acid alanine. What is the anticodon sequence on this tRNA?
    [*] 3′-CGU-5′ — the anticodon is complementary and antiparallel to the mRNA codon
    [ ] 5′-CGU-3′ — these are the correct complementary bases but in the wrong orientation
    [ ] 5′-GCA-3′ — this is just a copy of the mRNA codon in the same direction
    [ ] 3′-GCA-5′ — this is identical to the codon, not complementary to it
    Explanation: The tRNA anticodon must be complementary AND antiparallel to the mRNA codon. For mRNA codon 5′-GCA-3′: G pairs with C, C pairs with G, A pairs with U. The anticodon reads 3′-CGU-5′. Directionality is critical — mRNA and tRNA strands run antiparallel to each other during codon-anticodon base pairing, just as the two strands of DNA are antiparallel.

  Q26. A point mutation changes the mRNA codon CUU (leucine) to CUC. Consulting a codon table reveals that CUC also codes for leucine. What type of mutation is this, and what is its effect on the protein?
    [ ] Frameshift mutation — the reading frame shifts, changing all downstream amino acids
    [ ] Missense mutation — a different amino acid is substituted, altering the protein
    [ ] Nonsense mutation — a premature stop codon is created, truncating the protein
    [*] Silent (synonymous) mutation — the nucleotide changes but the amino acid remains leucine, so the protein is unaffected
    Explanation: This is a silent (synonymous) mutation — the codon changes from CUU to CUC, but both code for leucine. The protein's amino acid sequence is completely unaffected. Silent mutations are possible because the genetic code is degenerate (redundant), meaning most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons. Changes in the third (wobble) position of a codon often result in silent mutations.

  Q27. A mutation changes the mRNA codon UAC (tyrosine) to the stop codon UAA. What is the most likely consequence for the protein being synthesized?
    [*] The protein will be truncated (shorter than normal) because translation terminates prematurely at the new stop codon
    [ ] The protein will be completely unaffected because the genetic code is redundant
    [ ] The protein will be longer than normal because the ribosome reads past the new codon
    [ ] The protein will have only one amino acid substitution but otherwise function normally
    Explanation: This is a nonsense mutation — a codon that normally specifies an amino acid (UAC = tyrosine) is changed to a premature stop codon (UAA). When the ribosome reaches this new stop codon, translation terminates early, producing a truncated protein that is usually nonfunctional. Nonsense mutations are typically more harmful than missense mutations because they can eliminate large portions of the protein's amino acid sequence.

  Q28. A cell is treated with a drug that specifically inhibits RNA polymerase. Which cellular process is directly blocked, and what downstream effect does this have?
    [ ] DNA replication is blocked — the cell cannot copy its genome for cell division
    [ ] Translation is directly blocked — ribosomes cannot assemble amino acids into proteins
    [ ] DNA repair is blocked — damaged nucleotides cannot be replaced
    [*] Transcription is blocked — without new mRNA, the cell eventually cannot produce new proteins via translation
    Explanation: RNA polymerase is the enzyme that performs transcription (DNA → mRNA). If it is inhibited, no new mRNA can be synthesized. Since mRNA is the template for protein synthesis, the downstream effect is that translation will also eventually halt as existing mRNA molecules are degraded and not replaced. DNA replication uses DNA polymerase, not RNA polymerase, and is not directly affected by this drug.

  Q29. A researcher studying DNA replication discovers that a cell line has a mutation causing DNA ligase to be nonfunctional. Which of the following problems would be observed during replication?
    [ ] The DNA double helix will not be unwound at the replication fork
    [ ] The leading strand will not be synthesized continuously
    [ ] RNA primers will not be synthesized on either strand
    [*] Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand will not be joined, leaving gaps in the sugar-phosphate backbone
    Explanation: DNA ligase seals the phosphodiester bonds between Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand after DNA polymerase I has replaced the RNA primers with DNA. Without functional ligase, these fragments remain disconnected, leaving nicks (breaks) in the sugar-phosphate backbone of the new lagging strand. The leading strand is synthesized continuously and requires only one primer, so it is much less affected by ligase deficiency.

  Q30. A two-nucleotide insertion occurs near the beginning of the coding region of an mRNA. How does this affect every codon downstream of the insertion?
    [ ] It changes only the single codon at the insertion site, leaving downstream codons unaffected
    [ ] It causes the ribosome to skip two codons and resume normal reading downstream
    [*] It causes a frameshift — the reading frame shifts so that every codon after the insertion is misread, likely producing a nonfunctional protein
    [ ] It has no effect because the genetic code's redundancy compensates for the extra nucleotides
    Explanation: A two-nucleotide insertion shifts the reading frame — since codons are read in non-overlapping triplets, adding two nucleotides causes every subsequent codon to be read incorrectly. This frameshift mutation typically introduces wrong amino acids and often creates a premature stop codon, producing a truncated, nonfunctional protein. Only insertions or deletions in multiples of three preserve the reading frame (though they still add or remove amino acids).

--- Lesson 10.4: Meiosis II (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Meiosis II
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the main role of a transcription factor?
Transcription factors are proteins that bind to specific DNA sequences near a gene's promoter. They can activate or repress transcription by recruiting or blocking RNA polymerase.
What is the difference between an oncogene and a tumor suppressor?
Oncogenes are mutated versions of normal growth-promoting genes (proto-oncogenes) — they act like a gas pedal stuck down. Tumor suppressors normally stop or slow the cell cycle — they act as brakes. Cancer usually involves both accelerators stuck on AND brakes disabled.
What is alternative splicing?
Alternative splicing is a post-transcriptional process where different exon combinations are included in the mature mRNA. This allows one gene to code for multiple protein variants — a key reason humans can have ~100,000 protein types from only ~20,000 genes.
What is the function of the p53 protein?
p53 is called the 'guardian of the genome.' When it detects DNA damage, it halts the cell cycle to allow repair. If the damage is too severe, p53 triggers apoptosis (programmed cell death). It is mutated in over 50% of human cancers.
What is an operon?
An operon is a unit of gene regulation in bacteria that contains a promoter, an operator, and one or more structural genes transcribed as a single mRNA. The lac operon and trp operon are classic examples.
What is a promoter?
A promoter is a specific DNA sequence located upstream of a gene that serves as the binding site for RNA polymerase and associated transcription factors. It determines when and how often a gene is transcribed.
What is an enhancer in genetics?
An enhancer is a regulatory DNA element that can be thousands of base pairs away from the gene it controls. When activator proteins bind to the enhancer, the DNA loops so the enhancer contacts the promoter, boosting transcription.
What is microRNA (miRNA)?
MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small (~22 nucleotide) non-coding RNA molecules. They bind to complementary sequences on target mRNAs, either preventing translation or triggering mRNA degradation. This is a form of post-transcriptional gene regulation.
What is RNA interference (RNAi)?
RNA interference (RNAi) is a natural defense mechanism in which double-stranded RNA is cut by the enzyme Dicer into small interfering RNAs (siRNAs). These siRNAs guide the RISC complex to find and destroy complementary mRNA, silencing the gene post-transcriptionally.
Real-World Applications
Example: Identical twins raised in different environments are studied at age 60. Twin A, who smoked for decades, has significantly different gene expression patterns compared to Twin B. Their DNA sequences are still identical. A researcher finds heavy methylation on certain genes in Twin A that are unmethylated in Twin B. This study BEST illustrates:
This is a classic example of epigenetics. Identical twins start with the same DNA, but environmental factors (diet, stress, smoking, toxins) alter methylation patterns over time. These epigenetic changes modify gene expression WITHOUT changing the DNA sequence — explaining why identical twins can develop different diseases as they age.
Example: A breast cancer patient's tumor is analyzed. Doctors find that the Ras proto-oncogene has a point mutation making it permanently active, AND both copies of the p53 tumor suppressor gene are inactivated. Why do most cancers require MULTIPLE mutations rather than just one?
Cancer is a multi-step process requiring 5-7 mutations. Cells have built-in redundancy: oncogene activation alone can be countered by functional tumor suppressors. It usually takes hits to BOTH systems — stuck accelerators AND broken brakes — for uncontrolled proliferation. This explains why cancer risk increases with age.

  Q1. Do all cells in your body contain the same DNA?
    [*] Yes — nearly all body cells have the same complete genome
    [ ] Only brain cells have DNA
    [ ] No — each cell type has different DNA
    [ ] Cells get new DNA each time they divide
    Explanation: Nearly all cells in your body contain an identical copy of your complete genome — about 20,000 genes. What makes a muscle cell different from a nerve cell is which genes are turned on or off, not which genes are present.

  Q2. What is the main role of a transcription factor?
    [ ] It transports mRNA out of the nucleus
    [ ] It builds polypeptides at the ribosome
    [ ] It copies DNA during replication
    [*] It binds to DNA and helps control whether a gene is transcribed
    Explanation: Transcription factors are proteins that bind to specific DNA sequences near a gene's promoter. They can activate or repress transcription by recruiting or blocking RNA polymerase.

  Q3. In the lac operon, what happens when lactose is present?
    [ ] The repressor binds more tightly to the operator
    [*] The repressor releases the operator, allowing transcription
    [ ] The structural genes are deleted
    [ ] The promoter is methylated
    Explanation: When lactose is present, it is converted to allolactose, which binds the lac repressor and changes its shape. The repressor can no longer attach to the operator, so RNA polymerase proceeds and the lactose-digesting enzymes are produced.

  Q4. Does histone acetylation generally turn genes ON or OFF?
    [ ] It has no effect on gene expression
    [ ] OFF — acetylation compresses chromatin tighter
    [ ] It deletes the gene permanently
    [*] ON — acetylation loosens DNA from histones, making genes accessible
    Explanation: Adding acetyl groups to histone tails neutralizes their positive charge, weakening the attraction to negatively charged DNA. The chromatin opens up (euchromatin), allowing transcription factors and RNA polymerase to access the gene.

  Q5. What does DNA methylation typically do to a gene?
    [ ] Removes the gene from the chromosome
    [ ] Changes the DNA sequence
    [ ] Activates it — methylation promotes transcription
    [*] Silences it — methyl groups block transcription factor binding
    Explanation: DNA methylation (adding -CH₃ groups to cytosine bases at CpG sites) usually silences genes by preventing transcription factors from binding the promoter region.

  Q6. What is the difference between an oncogene and a tumor suppressor?
    [ ] Both promote uncontrolled cell growth
    [ ] Tumor suppressors cause cancer; oncogenes prevent it
    [ ] They are the same thing
    [*] Oncogenes promote cell growth; tumor suppressors stop cell division
    Explanation: Oncogenes are mutated versions of normal growth-promoting genes (proto-oncogenes) — they act like a gas pedal stuck down. Tumor suppressors normally stop or slow the cell cycle — they act as brakes. Cancer usually involves both accelerators stuck on AND brakes disabled.

  Q7. What is alternative splicing?
    [*] Different combinations of exons produce different mRNAs from one gene
    [ ] DNA is cut into pieces and rearranged
    [ ] Ribosomes skip certain codons during translation
    [ ] A gene is duplicated multiple times
    Explanation: Alternative splicing is a post-transcriptional process where different exon combinations are included in the mature mRNA. This allows one gene to code for multiple protein variants — a key reason humans can have ~100,000 protein types from only ~20,000 genes.

  Q8. What is the function of the p53 protein?
    [ ] It replicates DNA
    [ ] It promotes rapid cell division
    [ ] It digests proteins in the lysosome
    [*] It pauses the cell cycle when DNA damage is detected and can trigger cell death
    Explanation: p53 is called the 'guardian of the genome.' When it detects DNA damage, it halts the cell cycle to allow repair. If the damage is too severe, p53 triggers apoptosis (programmed cell death). It is mutated in over 50% of human cancers.

  Q9. What are Hox genes?
    [ ] Genes only found in plants
    [*] Master control genes that determine body segment identity during development
    [ ] Genes that cause cancer when mutated
    [ ] Genes that code for hemoglobin
    Explanation: Hox (homeotic) genes are transcription factors that specify which body structures develop in each segment — head, thorax, abdomen, etc. They are remarkably conserved across animals from fruit flies to humans.

  Q10. At which level is gene expression most commonly regulated in eukaryotes?
    [*] Transcription — controlling whether mRNA is made in the first place
    [ ] Cell division, particularly regarding point in the
    [ ] DNA replication, particularly regarding levels when
    [ ] Protein folding only under equilibrium conditions
    Explanation: While gene expression can be regulated at many levels (epigenetic, transcriptional, post-transcriptional, translational, post-translational), transcription initiation is the primary control point — it is most energy-efficient to prevent unwanted mRNA from being made at all.

  Q11. Define 'gene expression.'
    [ ] The movement of chromosomes during mitosis
    [ ] The total number of genes in an organism
    [*] The process by which information in a gene is used to make a functional product like a protein
    [ ] The copying of DNA before cell division
    Explanation: Gene expression is the overall process of gene → mRNA → protein (or functional RNA). It involves transcription (DNA to mRNA) and translation (mRNA to protein). Not all genes are expressed in every cell.

  Q12. What is an operon?
    [*] A cluster of genes under the control of a single promoter, found in prokaryotes
    [ ] An enzyme that unwinds DNA
    [ ] A membrane-bound organelle
    [ ] A type of ribosome in eukaryotic cells
    Explanation: An operon is a unit of gene regulation in bacteria that contains a promoter, an operator, and one or more structural genes transcribed as a single mRNA. The lac operon and trp operon are classic examples.

  Q13. What does 'epigenetic' mean?
    [ ] Permanent mutations in the DNA code
    [ ] The creation of new genes through evolution
    [*] Changes in gene expression that do not alter the DNA sequence itself
    [ ] A type of genetic disease
    Explanation: Epigenetics (literally 'above genetics') refers to heritable changes in gene activity that occur without any change to the DNA sequence. Key mechanisms include DNA methylation and histone modification. These marks can be influenced by environment, diet, and age.

  Q14. Define 'cell differentiation.'
    [ ] The division of one cell into two identical cells
    [ ] The death of damaged cells
    [ ] The transfer of DNA between bacteria
    [*] The process by which a cell becomes specialized in structure and function
    Explanation: Cell differentiation is the process during development where unspecialized cells become specialized (neurons, muscle cells, blood cells, etc.). It is driven by differential gene expression — different genes being turned on and off — not by changes in DNA.

  Q15. What is a promoter?
    [ ] A type of RNA that carries amino acids
    [ ] The end signal of a gene
    [ ] A protein that degrades mRNA
    [*] A DNA sequence where RNA polymerase binds to begin transcription
    Explanation: A promoter is a specific DNA sequence located upstream of a gene that serves as the binding site for RNA polymerase and associated transcription factors. It determines when and how often a gene is transcribed.

  Q16. What is an enhancer in genetics?
    [*] A distant regulatory DNA element that increases transcription of a target gene
    [ ] A chemical that speeds up DNA replication
    [ ] An enzyme that does proofreading during translation
    [ ] A type of mutation that makes a protein work better
    Explanation: An enhancer is a regulatory DNA element that can be thousands of base pairs away from the gene it controls. When activator proteins bind to the enhancer, the DNA loops so the enhancer contacts the promoter, boosting transcription.

  Q17. What is microRNA (miRNA)?
    [ ] mRNA that codes for very small proteins
    [ ] The smallest complete genome found in bacteria
    [ ] DNA fragments left over from viral infections
    [*] Small RNA molecules that regulate gene expression by blocking or degrading mRNA
    Explanation: MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small (~22 nucleotide) non-coding RNA molecules. They bind to complementary sequences on target mRNAs, either preventing translation or triggering mRNA degradation. This is a form of post-transcriptional gene regulation.

  Q18. What does the term 'proto-oncogene' mean?
    [ ] A gene that always causes cancer
    [ ] A gene found only in cancer cells
    [*] A normal gene that promotes cell growth and can become an oncogene if mutated
    [ ] A viral gene inserted into human DNA
    Explanation: Proto-oncogenes are normal, essential genes that encode proteins promoting cell growth and division. When mutated (by point mutations, gene amplification, or chromosomal translocation), they become oncogenes — driving uncontrolled cell proliferation.

  Q19. What is RNA interference (RNAi)?
    [ ] When RNA polymerase blocks other enzymes from working
    [*] A mechanism where small RNA molecules silence genes by destroying target mRNA
    [ ] When two RNA molecules combine to make a protein
    [ ] A method of DNA sequencing
    Explanation: RNA interference (RNAi) is a natural defense mechanism in which double-stranded RNA is cut by the enzyme Dicer into small interfering RNAs (siRNAs). These siRNAs guide the RISC complex to find and destroy complementary mRNA, silencing the gene post-transcriptionally.

  Q20. Define 'euchromatin' and 'heterochromatin.'
    [ ] They are the same type of chromatin
    [*] Euchromatin is loosely packed and actively transcribed; heterochromatin is tightly packed and silenced
    [ ] Heterochromatin is loosely packed; euchromatin is tightly packed
    [ ] Euchromatin is found only in bacteria; heterochromatin in eukaryotes
    Explanation: Euchromatin ('true chromatin') is lightly packed, open chromatin where genes are accessible for transcription — associated with histone acetylation. Heterochromatin is densely packed, closed chromatin where genes are silenced — associated with histone deacetylation and DNA methylation.

  Q21. A pharmaceutical company develops a drug that inhibits histone deacetylases (HDACs) in cancer cells. HDACs normally remove acetyl groups from histones. If the drug successfully blocks HDACs, what would you predict happens to tumor suppressor genes that were epigenetically silenced?
    [ ] They become more silenced — the drug compacts chromatin further
    [ ] No change — histone modifications cannot affect gene expression
    [*] They become reactivated — blocking deacetylation keeps histones acetylated and chromatin open
    [ ] The DNA sequence of the tumor suppressors mutates
    Explanation: HDAC inhibitors prevent acetyl group removal from histones, keeping chromatin in an open (euchromatin) state. This reactivates genes that cancer cells had silenced via histone deacetylation — including tumor suppressors. Drugs like vorinostat (SAHA) are FDA-approved cancer treatments based on this epigenetic mechanism.

  Q22. Identical twins raised in different environments are studied at age 60. Twin A, who smoked for decades, has significantly different gene expression patterns compared to Twin B. Their DNA sequences are still identical. A researcher finds heavy methylation on certain genes in Twin A that are unmethylated in Twin B. This study BEST illustrates:
    [ ] Genetic mutation — smoking changed Twin A's DNA sequence
    [ ] The twins were not actually identical
    [ ] Natural selection acting on the twins differently
    [*] Epigenetic changes — environmental factors altered gene expression without changing DNA sequence
    Explanation: This is a classic example of epigenetics. Identical twins start with the same DNA, but environmental factors (diet, stress, smoking, toxins) alter methylation patterns over time. These epigenetic changes modify gene expression WITHOUT changing the DNA sequence — explaining why identical twins can develop different diseases as they age.

  Q23. E. coli bacteria are grown on a medium with both glucose and lactose. A student predicts that the lac operon will be fully active because lactose is present. However, the bacteria preferentially use glucose and the lac operon is barely transcribed. Why?

    Explanation: The lac operon has dual regulation: negative control (lac repressor, removed by lactose) and positive control (CAP protein, activated by high cAMP when glucose is absent). Even when lactose removes the repressor, the operon needs CAP for strong transcription. With glucose present, cAMP is low, CAP is inactive, and the operon remains at minimal transcription. This is catabolite repression — bacteria prioritize glucose.

  Q24. A researcher studying fruit flies discovers a mutant where legs grow out of the head where antennae should be. This bizarre phenotype is caused by a mutation in a Hox gene called Antennapedia that is expressed in the wrong body segment. What does this tell us about Hox genes?

    [*] Hox genes are master switches — expressing one in the wrong segment builds the wrong body structure for that location
    Explanation: The Antennapedia mutation is a famous homeotic transformation. The Antennapedia gene normally specifies 'build thorax/legs here,' but when misexpressed in the head segment, it overrides the antenna program and builds legs instead. Hox gene order on the chromosome mirrors body segment order (collinearity).

  Q25. A breast cancer patient's tumor is analyzed. Doctors find that the Ras proto-oncogene has a point mutation making it permanently active, AND both copies of the p53 tumor suppressor gene are inactivated. Why do most cancers require MULTIPLE mutations rather than just one?
    [ ] Cancer is caused only by environmental factors, not mutations
    [*] Cells have multiple safeguards — both growth promoters must be stuck ON and brakes must be disabled to cause uncontrolled division

    Explanation: Cancer is a multi-step process requiring 5-7 mutations. Cells have built-in redundancy: oncogene activation alone can be countered by functional tumor suppressors. It usually takes hits to BOTH systems — stuck accelerators AND broken brakes — for uncontrolled proliferation. This explains why cancer risk increases with age.

  Q26. A researcher introduces double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) matching the mRNA of a disease-causing gene into cells. The protein product drops by 90%. The mechanism involves Dicer cutting the dsRNA into small fragments that guide the RISC complex to the target mRNA. This technique is called:
    [ ] Alternative splicing — rearranging exons in the pre-mRNA

    [ ] PCR — amplifying the gene for further study
    [ ] CRISPR gene editing — cutting and replacing the DNA sequence
    Explanation: RNA interference (RNAi), discovered by Fire and Mello (Nobel Prize 2006), is a post-transcriptional gene silencing mechanism. dsRNA is processed by Dicer into ~21-nucleotide siRNAs that load into RISC to find and destroy complementary mRNA. The FDA-approved drug patisiran uses this mechanism.

  Q27. The Dscam gene in fruit flies produces over 38,000 different protein variants from a single gene — each neuron expresses a unique combination. The pre-mRNA has 115 exons that can be combined differently. This mechanism is:
    [ ] Gene amplification — the gene was copied 38,000 times

    [ ] Horizontal gene transfer — neurons pick up different genes from nearby cells
    Explanation: Dscam is the most extreme example of alternative splicing — 115 exons combined in 38,016 ways from a single gene. Each neuron expresses a unique subset for self-recognition during neural wiring. This illustrates why ~20,000 human genes can produce far more than 20,000 proteins.

  Q28. Estrogen passes through the cell membrane and binds an intracellular receptor. The estrogen-receptor complex enters the nucleus and directly binds DNA at estrogen response elements (EREs), activating target genes. At which level does estrogen regulate gene expression?

    [ ] DNA replication — estrogen controls when chromosomes are copied
    Explanation: Steroid hormones (estrogen, testosterone, cortisol) are lipid-soluble, so they cross cell membranes and bind intracellular receptors. The complex acts as a transcription factor — entering the nucleus and directly binding DNA to activate specific genes. This is transcriptional-level regulation.

  Q29. A patient with Li-Fraumeni syndrome inherits one non-functional copy of p53. She has one working copy and one broken copy. Why doesn't the one working copy fully protect her from cancer?
    [*] If the remaining good copy is lost through a second mutation (loss of heterozygosity), she loses all p53 function

    [ ] The broken copy produces a toxin that harms cells
    Explanation: This illustrates Knudson's two-hit hypothesis. Inheriting one mutant p53 allele means only one more 'hit' is needed to eliminate the gene. Over a normal lifetime (~10¹⁶ cell divisions), the probability of that second hit is very high — hence the ~90% cancer risk in Li-Fraumeni syndrome.

When Ras is permanently active (stuck 'ON'), it continuously fires growth signals regardless of external growth factors. Each cascade step amplifies the signal, making a single stuck-ON Ras especially potent in driving cancer.

--- Lesson 10.5: Crossing Over and Genetic Variation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Crossing Over and Genetic Variation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a GMO?
A GMO (Genetically Modified Organism) has had its DNA deliberately altered in a laboratory, typically by inserting genes from another species. Examples include Bt corn (insect resistance) and Golden Rice (vitamin A production).
What is biological cloning?
Cloning produces a genetically identical copy. Dolly the sheep (1996) was the first mammal cloned from an adult cell using somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT). Natural cloning occurs in bacteria (binary fission) and identical twins.
What is gel electrophoresis used for?
Gel electrophoresis uses an electric field to move DNA through a gel matrix. Since DNA is negatively charged, it migrates toward the positive electrode. Smaller fragments move faster and farther, creating a pattern of bands used for DNA fingerprinting and analysis.
What is a plasmid?
Plasmids are small, circular DNA molecules that replicate independently of the bacterial chromosome. In genetic engineering, they serve as vectors — vehicles for carrying foreign genes into host cells. The human insulin gene was first produced in bacteria using a plasmid vector.
What is the role of DNA ligase in genetic engineering?
DNA ligase forms phosphodiester bonds to seal the sugar-phosphate backbone, permanently joining DNA fragments. After restriction enzymes cut and sticky ends base-pair, ligase seals the nicks to create a continuous recombinant DNA molecule.
What is gene therapy?
Gene therapy delivers a functional copy of a gene to cells that have a defective version. Viral vectors typically carry the working gene into cells. The first FDA-approved gene therapy (Luxturna, 2017) treats inherited blindness by delivering a functional RPE65 gene.
What is the difference between reproductive cloning and therapeutic cloning?
Both use SCNT (somatic cell nuclear transfer), but the goals differ. Reproductive cloning implants the embryo to develop fully (like Dolly the sheep). Therapeutic cloning stops at the blastocyst stage to harvest stem cells that are genetically matched to the patient, avoiding immune rejection in transplants.
What is a 'vector' in genetic engineering?
A vector is a delivery vehicle for foreign DNA. Plasmid vectors are commonly used for bacterial transformation. Viral vectors (modified viruses with disease genes removed) are used in gene therapy to deliver therapeutic genes into human cells.
What is a transgenic organism?
A transgenic organism carries a transgene — a gene from another species. Examples: E. coli with the human insulin gene, Bt corn with a Bacillus thuringiensis gene, GloFish with a jellyfish fluorescence gene. The foreign gene is stably integrated and can be inherited by offspring.
What is bioinformatics?
Bioinformatics combines biology, computer science, and statistics to manage and analyze vast biological data. Tools like BLAST compare DNA sequences against databases containing billions of sequences. After the Human Genome Project generated 3.2 billion base pairs of data, bioinformatics became essential for genomics research and drug discovery.
What is somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT)?
SCNT was used to create Dolly the sheep in 1996. A somatic (body) cell's nucleus is transferred into an enucleated egg. The egg's cytoplasm reprograms the differentiated nucleus back to a totipotent state, allowing it to develop into a complete organism genetically identical to the nuclear donor.
What is pharmacogenomics?
Pharmacogenomics examines how genetic variants affect drug metabolism, efficacy, and side effects. For example, some people have genetic variants that make them metabolize codeine too quickly (dangerous) or too slowly (ineffective). This enables doctors to choose drugs and doses tailored to each patient's genetic profile.
What is a guide RNA (gRNA) in the CRISPR system?
The guide RNA is CRISPR's targeting system. Scientists design the gRNA to be complementary to their DNA target. The gRNA-Cas9 complex scans the genome until the gRNA finds its matching sequence adjacent to a PAM motif (NGG). Changing the gRNA sequence redirects Cas9 to any desired gene — this programmability is what makes CRISPR revolutionary.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Gene flow through cross-pollination is a real ecological concern with GMO crops. When GMO crop pollen fertilizes wild relatives, transgenes can transfer — creating 'superweeds' resistant to herbicides. Over 40 weed species have developed glyphosate resistance. Management strategies include buffer zones, crop rotation, alternating herbicides, and refuge areas. This is one of the most scientifically valid GMO concerns.
Real-World Applications
Example: A clinical trial uses CRISPR-Cas9 to treat sickle cell disease. Doctors remove a patient's bone marrow stem cells, use CRISPR to edit the BCL11A gene (which silences fetal hemoglobin), then return the edited cells. The patient's body begins producing fetal hemoglobin again, which doesn't sickle. This is an example of:
This is ex vivo (outside the body) somatic gene editing. Because only blood stem cells (somatic cells) are edited, the changes are NOT inherited — it's NOT germline editing. The FDA approved this approach (Casgevy) in 2023 as the first CRISPR-based therapy. By reactivating fetal hemoglobin (HbF), which has higher oxygen affinity than adult hemoglobin and doesn't polymerize like sickle hemoglobin (HbS), patients can produce enough functional hemoglobin to prevent sickling crises.
Example: A student compares two GMO crops: Golden Rice and Bt corn. Golden Rice produces beta-carotene (provitamin A) to combat vitamin A deficiency in developing countries. Bt corn produces Cry protein from Bacillus thuringiensis that kills pest larvae when eaten. These represent two DIFFERENT goals of GMO development. What are they?
GMOs serve different purposes: (1) Nutritional biofortification — Golden Rice adds vitamin A to address deficiency that blinds 250,000-500,000 children yearly. (2) Pest resistance — Bt crops produce insecticidal Cry proteins, reducing pesticide use by ~37%. (3) Herbicide tolerance — Roundup Ready crops survive glyphosate spraying. (4) Drought/salt tolerance. Each addresses a different agricultural challenge.
Example: When Dolly the sheep was cloned in 1996 using SCNT, she was born with apparently normal health but developed arthritis at age 5 and lung disease at age 6 (typically seen in much older sheep). She was euthanized at age 6 — half the normal lifespan. Scientists discovered her telomeres were shorter than expected. This suggests:
Dolly's premature aging highlights challenges of SCNT: the donor nucleus came from a 6-year-old sheep, and its telomeres (chromosome end caps that shorten with age) may not have been fully restored. Incomplete epigenetic reprogramming also means clones may have abnormal gene expression patterns. Since Dolly, cloning efficiency has improved, but health problems remain common in cloned animals.

--- Lesson 10.6: Nondisjunction and Genetic Disorders (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nondisjunction and Genetic Disorders
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a karyotype?
A karyotype displays an individual's chromosomes arranged in homologous pairs by size and centromere position. It can detect abnormalities in chromosome number (like trisomy 21/Down syndrome) or large structural changes (deletions, translocations). Normal humans have 46 chromosomes: 22 pairs of autosomes plus 2 sex chromosomes.
What is a pedigree chart?
A pedigree uses symbols (circles for females, squares for males; filled for affected, open for unaffected) to track inheritance patterns across generations. Genetic counselors analyze pedigrees to determine whether a trait is dominant/recessive, autosomal/X-linked, and to calculate risk for future offspring.
What is an X-linked recessive disorder?
X-linked recessive disorders disproportionately affect males because males have only one X chromosome (XY) — one mutant allele is enough to cause disease. Females (XX) need two mutant copies to be affected; with one they are carriers. Examples: hemophilia A, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, red-green color blindness. Carrier mothers pass the allele to 50% of sons.
What is Down syndrome?
Down syndrome results from trisomy 21 — three copies of chromosome 21 instead of two, giving 47 chromosomes total. It's the most common chromosomal abnormality in live births (~1 in 700). It is caused by nondisjunction during meiosis and risk increases with maternal age.
What is genetic counseling?
Genetic counselors are healthcare professionals who help families understand genetic risks, interpret test results, and make informed decisions. They use pedigree analysis, probability calculations, and knowledge of inheritance patterns to assess risk. Genetic counseling is recommended when there is a family history of genetic disorders, advanced maternal age, or abnormal prenatal screening results.
What is nondisjunction?
Nondisjunction occurs when homologous chromosomes (meiosis I) or sister chromatids (meiosis II) fail to separate properly. This produces gametes with too many or too few chromosomes. After fertilization, the resulting embryo may have trisomy (three copies, like Down syndrome) or monosomy (one copy, like Turner syndrome).
In genetics, what is a carrier?
Carriers are heterozygous (e.g., Cc for cystic fibrosis) — they have one normal and one mutant allele. Because the normal allele compensates, carriers are phenotypically unaffected but can pass the recessive allele to children. If two carriers have children, there's a 25% chance of an affected (homozygous recessive) child.
What is heterozygote advantage?
Heterozygote advantage (also called overdominance or balanced polymorphism) occurs when the heterozygote has greater fitness than either homozygote. The classic example is sickle cell trait: HbA HbS carriers resist malaria better than HbA HbA individuals and don't suffer sickle cell disease like HbS HbS individuals. This maintains both alleles in malaria-endemic populations.
What is amniocentesis?
Amniocentesis extracts amniotic fluid containing fetal cells that have been shed. These cells can be cultured and used for karyotyping (detecting chromosomal abnormalities) or DNA analysis (detecting specific gene mutations). It's performed at 15-20 weeks with ~0.5% miscarriage risk. Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) is an alternative performed earlier (10-13 weeks) but with slightly higher risk.
What is phenylketonuria (PKU)?
PKU is caused by mutations in the PAH gene, which encodes phenylalanine hydroxylase — the enzyme that converts phenylalanine to tyrosine. Without this enzyme, phenylalanine accumulates and damages the developing brain. All newborns in the US are screened for PKU (heel prick test). A low-phenylalanine diet started early prevents intellectual disability — making PKU one of the great success stories of newborn screening.
What is a point mutation?
A point mutation is the simplest type of mutation — a single nucleotide is substituted, inserted, or deleted. Sickle cell disease is caused by a point mutation (A→T) that changes one amino acid (glutamic acid → valine) in the hemoglobin beta chain. Point mutations can be silent, missense, nonsense, or frameshift depending on their effect on the protein.
What is genetic screening?
Genetic screening tests large groups (like all newborns) or targeted individuals (like those with family history) for specific genetic conditions. Newborn screening tests for PKU, sickle cell, and other treatable conditions. Carrier screening identifies heterozygous individuals who may pass recessive alleles to children. Prenatal screening uses blood tests, ultrasound, amniocentesis, or CVS to detect fetal genetic conditions.
What is a chromosomal translocation?
Translocations involve segments of chromosomes breaking and reattaching to non-homologous chromosomes. Reciprocal translocations swap segments between two chromosomes. Robertsonian translocations fuse two acrocentric chromosomes. The Philadelphia chromosome (translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22) creates the BCR-ABL fusion gene that causes chronic myeloid leukemia (CML). Some translocations can also cause a form of Down syndrome.
What is mitochondrial inheritance?
Mitochondria have their own circular DNA (mtDNA) inherited almost exclusively from the mother through the egg cell's cytoplasm. Sperm contribute virtually no mitochondria. Therefore, an affected mother passes mtDNA mutations to ALL children (sons and daughters), but an affected father passes it to NONE. Examples: Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (LHON), some forms of deafness, and MELAS syndrome.
A couple visits a genetic counselor because the husband has Huntington's disease (autosomal dominant, Hh). The wife is unaffected (hh). They want to know the risk for their children. Using a Punnett square, what is the probability that each child will develop Huntington's disease?
Huntington's disease is autosomal dominant — only one copy of the mutant allele (H) causes disease. From Hh × hh: 50% Hh (will develop Huntington's) and 50% hh (unaffected). Unlike recessive disorders, dominant conditions cannot 'hide' in carriers. Every person with even one H allele will eventually develop symptoms, typically in their 30s-50s. Huntington's is caused by CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the HTT gene — more repeats = earlier onset (anticipation).
A woman who is a carrier for hemophilia A (X^H X^h) marries an unaffected man (X^H Y). They are expecting a son. What is the probability this son will have hemophilia, and why are sons at higher risk than daughters for X-linked recessive disorders?
Sons receive their single X chromosome from their mother. From a carrier mother (X^H X^h): 50% of sons get X^H (unaffected) and 50% get X^h (hemophilic). Males are more vulnerable to X-linked recessive disorders because they have only one X — there's no second X to provide a working allele. The father's X goes only to daughters, so he cannot pass his X to sons or protect them. Daughters get one X from each parent and are at most carriers (X^H X^h) in this cross.
Both parents are carriers for PKU (Pp × Pp). They want to know: what is the probability that their next THREE children will ALL be unaffected? The genetic counselor calculates:
From Pp × Pp: each child has a 3/4 chance of being unaffected (1/4 PP + 2/4 Pp) and a 1/4 chance of being affected (pp). For independent events occurring together, use the multiplication rule: P(all three unaffected) = 3/4 × 3/4 × 3/4 = 27/64 ≈ 42%. The addition rule applies to 'or' scenarios. Each pregnancy is independent — previous outcomes don't affect future ones.
A patient undergoes direct-to-consumer genetic testing and learns she carries one BRCA1 mutation, significantly increasing her breast and ovarian cancer risk. She is worried about discrimination. Her genetic counselor explains that GINA protects her from discrimination in health insurance and employment, but warns about a major gap. What is the gap?
GINA (2008) protects against genetic discrimination in health insurance and employment but has significant gaps. Life insurance, disability insurance, and long-term care insurance are NOT covered — companies can legally deny coverage or increase premiums based on genetic test results. This is a real concern in genetic counseling: patients must be informed before testing that results could affect their ability to obtain these types of insurance. Some states have passed additional protections beyond GINA.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Huntington's disease is autosomal dominant — only one copy of the mutant allele (H) causes disease. From Hh × hh: 50% Hh (will develop Huntington's) and 50% hh (unaffected). Unlike recessive disorders, dominant conditions cannot 'hide' in carriers. Every person with even one H allele will eventually develop symptoms, typically in their 30s-50s. Huntington's is caused by CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the HTT gene — more repeats = earlier onset (anticipation).
This addresses the 'gambler's fallacy' — the mistaken belief that past outcomes affect future probabilities for independent events. Each pregnancy from Cc × Cc independently produces: 25% CC, 50% Cc, 25% cc. Having one cc child doesn't change the odds for the next child — just like flipping heads doesn't make tails more likely on the next flip. The 1:2:1 ratio is a statistical expectation over large numbers, not a guarantee for any individual family.
Sons receive their single X chromosome from their mother. From a carrier mother (X^H X^h): 50% of sons get X^H (unaffected) and 50% get X^h (hemophilic). Males are more vulnerable to X-linked recessive disorders because they have only one X — there's no second X to provide a working allele. The father's X goes only to daughters, so he cannot pass his X to sons or protect them. Daughters get one X from each parent and are at most carriers (X^H X^h) in this cross.
Key autosomal dominant signatures: (1) appears in every generation — doesn't skip; (2) affects males and females equally (autosomal); (3) affected individuals always have at least one affected parent; (4) two unaffected parents cannot produce affected offspring (unlike autosomal recessive, where two carrier parents can). Autosomal recessive CAN skip generations because carriers are unaffected. X-linked recessive disproportionately affects males. Mitochondrial always comes from the mother.
From Pp × Pp: each child has a 3/4 chance of being unaffected (1/4 PP + 2/4 Pp) and a 1/4 chance of being affected (pp). For independent events occurring together, use the multiplication rule: P(all three unaffected) = 3/4 × 3/4 × 3/4 = 27/64 ≈ 42%. The addition rule applies to 'or' scenarios. Each pregnancy is independent — previous outcomes don't affect future ones.
This is the classic X-linked recessive 'skip a generation' pattern: color-blind grandfather (X^n Y) → all daughters are obligate carriers (X^N X^n, getting X^n from dad and X^N from mom) → 50% of carrier daughter's sons are color-blind (getting X^n from mom). Sons CANNOT inherit X-linked traits from their fathers because fathers pass Y (not X) to sons. The trait 'skipped' the daughter because she was a heterozygous carrier shielded by her normal X allele.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is heterozygote advantage?
Heterozygote advantage (also called overdominance or balanced polymorphism) occurs when the heterozygote has greater fitness than either homozygote. The classic example is sickle cell trait: HbA HbS carriers resist malaria better than HbA HbA individuals and don't suffer sickle cell disease like HbS HbS individuals. This maintains both alleles in malaria-endemic populations.
Important Concepts
Sickle cell anemia is caused by a single point mutation (GAG→GTG) in the hemoglobin gene, changing glutamic acid to valine. The sickle allele frequency is ~15% in parts of sub-Saharan Africa despite being lethal when homozygous (HbS HbS). A student asks why natural selection hasn't eliminated this harmful allele. The answer is:
This is the classic example of balanced polymorphism. In malaria-endemic regions: HbA HbA individuals are fully susceptible to malaria (disadvantage). HbS HbS individuals have severe sickle cell disease (disadvantage). HbA HbS carriers have mild sickling that disrupts the malaria parasite's lifecycle inside red blood cells (advantage). Since the heterozygote has the highest fitness, natural selection maintains both alleles — a situation called heterozygote advantage or overdominance.

    [ ] 25% — only one quarter of offspring inherit the allele
    [ ] 100% — dominant means all children are affected
    [ ] 0% — the disease always skips a generation
    [*] 50% — a cross of Hh × hh yields half Hh (affected) and half hh (unaffected)
    Explanation: Huntington's disease is autosomal dominant — only one copy of the mutant allele (H) causes disease. From Hh × hh: 50% Hh (will develop Huntington's) and 50% hh (unaffected). Unlike recessive disorders, dominant conditions cannot 'hide' in carriers. Every person with even one H allele will eventually develop symptoms, typically in their 30s-50s. Huntington's is caused by CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the HTT gene — more repeats = earlier onset (anticipation).

  Q22. Two healthy parents discover through carrier testing that they are both carriers of cystic fibrosis (Cc × Cc). Their first child is diagnosed with CF (cc). They ask: 'Since we already have an affected child, is the next child guaranteed to be unaffected?' What should the genetic counselor explain?
    [ ] After one affected child, all future children will be carriers only
    [*] Each pregnancy is independent — every child still has a 25% chance of being affected, regardless of previous children
    [ ] The next child is guaranteed unaffected because probability must 'balance out'
    [ ] The chance increases to 50% for the next child
    Explanation: This addresses the 'gambler's fallacy' — the mistaken belief that past outcomes affect future probabilities for independent events. Each pregnancy from Cc × Cc independently produces: 25% CC, 50% Cc, 25% cc. Having one cc child doesn't change the odds for the next child — just like flipping heads doesn't make tails more likely on the next flip. The 1:2:1 ratio is a statistical expectation over large numbers, not a guarantee for any individual family.

  Q23. A woman who is a carrier for hemophilia A (X^H X^h) marries an unaffected man (X^H Y). They are expecting a son. What is the probability this son will have hemophilia, and why are sons at higher risk than daughters for X-linked recessive disorders?
    [ ] 25% — same as autosomal recessive for both sexes
    [*] 50% chance — sons get their only X from their mother and have no backup allele on the Y chromosome
    [ ] 0% — the father's normal X protects all children
    [ ] 100% — all sons of carriers are affected
    Explanation: Sons receive their single X chromosome from their mother. From a carrier mother (X^H X^h): 50% of sons get X^H (unaffected) and 50% get X^h (hemophilic). Males are more vulnerable to X-linked recessive disorders because they have only one X — there's no second X to provide a working allele. The father's X goes only to daughters, so he cannot pass his X to sons or protect them. Daughters get one X from each parent and are at most carriers (X^H X^h) in this cross.

  Q24. Sickle cell anemia is caused by a single point mutation (GAG→GTG) in the hemoglobin gene, changing glutamic acid to valine. The sickle allele frequency is ~15% in parts of sub-Saharan Africa despite being lethal when homozygous (HbS HbS). A student asks why natural selection hasn't eliminated this harmful allele. The answer is:
    [ ] Natural selection doesn't operate on human populations
    [*] Heterozygote advantage — HbA HbS carriers resist malaria, so natural selection maintains both alleles in malaria-endemic regions
    [ ] The sickle allele is always beneficial regardless of zygosity
    [ ] The mutation is too recent for natural selection to act
    Explanation: This is the classic example of balanced polymorphism. In malaria-endemic regions: HbA HbA individuals are fully susceptible to malaria (disadvantage). HbS HbS individuals have severe sickle cell disease (disadvantage). HbA HbS carriers have mild sickling that disrupts the malaria parasite's lifecycle inside red blood cells (advantage). Since the heterozygote has the highest fitness, natural selection maintains both alleles — a situation called heterozygote advantage or overdominance.

  Q25. A geneticist analyzes a pedigree and observes: the trait appears in every generation, males and females are equally affected, and every affected individual has at least one affected parent. However, two unaffected parents in generation III have all unaffected children. This pattern is MOST consistent with:
    [ ] Mitochondrial — passed only from mothers
    [ ] Autosomal recessive — requires two alleles and can skip generations
    [ ] X-linked recessive — affects mostly males and skips generations
    [*] Autosomal dominant — only one allele needed, never skips generations, and unaffected parents cannot pass the trait
    Explanation: Key autosomal dominant signatures: (1) appears in every generation — doesn't skip; (2) affects males and females equally (autosomal); (3) affected individuals always have at least one affected parent; (4) two unaffected parents cannot produce affected offspring (unlike autosomal recessive, where two carrier parents can). Autosomal recessive CAN skip generations because carriers are unaffected. X-linked recessive disproportionately affects males. Mitochondrial always comes from the mother.

  Q26. A hospital performs karyotyping on two patients. Patient A has 47,XXY. Patient B has 45,X. The doctor explains the clinical significance of each. Which diagnosis is correct?
    [ ] Patient A has Turner syndrome; Patient B has Klinefelter syndrome
    [ ] Patient A has Edwards syndrome; Patient B has Patau syndrome
    [ ] Both patients have Down syndrome
    [*] Patient A has Klinefelter syndrome (male with extra X); Patient B has Turner syndrome (female missing one sex chromosome)
    Explanation: Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY): males with an extra X chromosome. Features include tall stature, reduced testosterone, possible learning differences, and infertility. Caused by nondisjunction. Turner syndrome (45,X): females with only one X chromosome. Features include short stature, webbed neck, and infertility. It's the only survivable human monosomy. Down syndrome is trisomy 21; Edwards is trisomy 18; Patau is trisomy 13 — these involve autosomes, not sex chromosomes.

  Q27. Both parents are carriers for PKU (Pp × Pp). They want to know: what is the probability that their next THREE children will ALL be unaffected? The genetic counselor calculates:
    [ ] 3/4 + 3/4 + 3/4 = 9/4 (use addition rule)
    [ ] 1/4 × 1/4 × 1/4 = 1/64
    [*] 3/4 × 3/4 × 3/4 = 27/64 — each child independently has a 3/4 chance of being unaffected
    [ ] 3/4 (same probability regardless of number of children)
    Explanation: From Pp × Pp: each child has a 3/4 chance of being unaffected (1/4 PP + 2/4 Pp) and a 1/4 chance of being affected (pp). For independent events occurring together, use the multiplication rule: P(all three unaffected) = 3/4 × 3/4 × 3/4 = 27/64 ≈ 42%. The addition rule applies to 'or' scenarios. Each pregnancy is independent — previous outcomes don't affect future ones.

  Q28. A family's pedigree shows: grandfather is color-blind, grandmother has normal vision, their daughter has normal vision, and their daughter's son is color-blind. The daughter's husband has normal vision. A student is confused about how the grandson inherited color blindness when neither parent is affected. The explanation is:
    [ ] Color blindness mutated spontaneously in the grandson
    [ ] Color blindness skipped two generations, not just one
    [ ] The grandson inherited color blindness from his father
    [*] The daughter is a carrier (X^N X^n) who inherited X^n from her color-blind father — she passed X^n to her son
    Explanation: This is the classic X-linked recessive 'skip a generation' pattern: color-blind grandfather (X^n Y) → all daughters are obligate carriers (X^N X^n, getting X^n from dad and X^N from mom) → 50% of carrier daughter's sons are color-blind (getting X^n from mom). Sons CANNOT inherit X-linked traits from their fathers because fathers pass Y (not X) to sons. The trait 'skipped' the daughter because she was a heterozygous carrier shielded by her normal X allele.

  Q29. In a population of 10,000, 16 individuals have a rare autosomal recessive disorder. Using Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (p² + 2pq + q² = 1), approximately how many individuals in this population are carriers?
    [*] Approximately 768 carriers (q² = 16/10,000, q = 0.04, 2pq ≈ 0.0768, × 10,000 ≈ 768)
    [ ] Approximately 16 carriers (same number as affected)
    [ ] Cannot be determined from this information
    [ ] Approximately 5,000 carriers (half the population)
    Explanation: Calculation: q² = 16/10,000 = 0.0016, so q = √0.0016 = 0.04. Since p + q = 1, p = 0.96. Carrier frequency: 2pq = 2(0.96)(0.04) = 0.0768 or 7.68%. In 10,000 people: 0.0768 × 10,000 ≈ 768 carriers. Key insight: carriers are ~48× more common than affected individuals! This is why recessive alleles persist in populations — the vast majority are 'hidden' in phenotypically normal carriers.

  Q30. A patient undergoes direct-to-consumer genetic testing and learns she carries one BRCA1 mutation, significantly increasing her breast and ovarian cancer risk. She is worried about discrimination. Her genetic counselor explains that GINA protects her from discrimination in health insurance and employment, but warns about a major gap. What is the gap?
    [ ] GINA only applies to government employees, not private citizens
    [*] GINA does NOT cover life insurance, disability insurance, or long-term care insurance — companies can use genetic information for these
    [ ] GINA provides no protections at all — it was repealed
    [ ] GINA covers all forms of insurance with no exceptions
    Explanation: GINA (2008) protects against genetic discrimination in health insurance and employment but has significant gaps. Life insurance, disability insurance, and long-term care insurance are NOT covered — companies can legally deny coverage or increase premiums based on genetic test results. This is a real concern in genetic counseling: patients must be informed before testing that results could affect their ability to obtain these types of insurance. Some states have passed additional protections beyond GINA.
