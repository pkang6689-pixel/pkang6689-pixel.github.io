--- Lesson 4.1: History of Evolutionary Thought (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: History of Evolutionary Thought
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is population density?
Population density is a fundamental measurement in ecology that describes how many individuals of a species occupy a given area (for terrestrial organisms) or volume (for aquatic organisms). It is calculated as D = N / A, where N is the number of individuals and A is the area. Population density can be expressed as crude density (total area) or ecological density (only habitable area). It helps ecologists assess habitat quality, resource competition, and disease transmission risk.
What is the difference between immigration and emigration in population dynamics?
Immigration and emigration are the two dispersal components of population change. Immigration adds individuals to a population (increasing its size and potentially its genetic diversity), while emigration removes individuals. The net migration rate (immigration − emigration) combines with the natural increase rate (births − deaths) to determine overall population change. Migration is particularly important for metapopulations — groups of spatially separated populations connected by dispersal.
What is the difference between population size and population density?
Population size (N) is simply the total count of individuals of a species in a defined area. Population density takes that count and divides it by the area or volume: D = N/A. Two populations can have the same size but very different densities if they occupy different-sized habitats. A population of 1,000 deer in a 10 km² park has a much higher density (100 deer/km²) than 1,000 deer spread across 1,000 km² (1 deer/km²), which affects competition, disease, and resource availability.
What is carrying capacity (K) in ecology?
Carrying capacity (K) is determined by limiting factors such as food availability, water, shelter, nesting sites, and space. K is not a fixed value — it can change over time due to seasonal variation, habitat degradation, climate change, or human activities. When a population exceeds K (overshoot), increased mortality and/or decreased reproduction typically bring it back down, sometimes causing boom-and-bust cycles. K is a central parameter in the logistic growth equation.
What is the complete equation for population change that includes all four factors?
The BIDE model (Births, Immigration, Deaths, Emigration) is the fundamental equation of population dynamics: ΔN = (B − D) + (I − E). Births and immigration are additions to the population (positive terms), while deaths and emigration are losses (negative terms). The population grows when additions exceed losses, declines when losses exceed additions, and remains stable when the two are equal. This equation applies to both open populations (with migration) and closed populations (where I and E are zero).
Ecologists studying a lake wish to estimate the population size of largemouth bass. They capture 100 fish, tag them with small fin clips, and release them. Two weeks later, they capture another sample of 120 fish and find that 15 of them bear tags from the first capture. Using the Lincoln-Petersen method, what is the estimated population size, and what assumption must hold for this estimate to be valid?
The mark-recapture (Lincoln-Petersen) method estimates population size using the formula: N = (M × C) / R, where M = number marked in first capture (100), C = total in second capture (120), and R = recaptured marked individuals (15). So N = (100 × 120) / 15 = 800. Key assumptions include: (1) marks are not lost or overlooked, (2) marked and unmarked animals have equal catchability, (3) the population is closed (no births, deaths, immigration, or emigration between samples), and (4) marked individuals mix randomly with the population.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which definition BEST describes an age structure diagram?
Age structure diagrams (population pyramids) divide a population into pre-reproductive, reproductive, and post-reproductive age classes. A broad base (many young) indicates rapid future growth (e.g., Nigeria, Uganda); a column shape indicates a stable population (e.g., United States, France); and an inverted pyramid or top-heavy shape indicates a declining population (e.g., Japan, Italy). These diagrams are essential tools for demographers, public health planners, and economists because they predict future workforce size, dependency ratios, and healthcare needs.
Example: In 1995, 14 gray wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park, where wolves had been absent for 70 years. By 2020, the population had grown to approximately 100 wolves within the park. Early growth was rapid, but the population stabilized between 80-120 wolves. The elk population, which had been overabundant, decreased by about 50%. Riverside vegetation recovered, and beaver populations returned. What population dynamics concepts does this example illustrate?
The Yellowstone wolf reintroduction is one of the best-documented examples of population dynamics and trophic cascades. Wolf population growth followed a logistic pattern: initially rapid when territories were unoccupied and elk were abundant, then leveling off as pack territories filled and prey populations adjusted. Carrying capacity for wolves is limited by territory size (packs defend territories of 200-500 km²), prey density, and intraspecific aggression. This case also illustrates how a top predator regulates prey populations, which cascades through the entire ecosystem.
Example: Easter Island (Rapa Nui) was settled by Polynesians around 1200 CE. The population grew rapidly to an estimated 10,000-15,000 people, supported by rich volcanic soil and abundant forests. By 1600 CE, the forests had been completely cleared (for agriculture, canoe building, and transporting moai statues), topsoil eroded, and the population crashed to an estimated 2,000-3,000. What population dynamics concept does this human example illustrate?
Easter Island is one of the most compelling case studies of human population dynamics in a closed system. The island's isolation (over 2,000 km from the nearest inhabited land) made it functionally a closed population where emigration was impossible. Deforestation eliminated the ability to build fishing canoes (losing a major protein source), caused erosion of agricultural soils, and eliminated wood for fuel and shelter. This case is often cited as a microcosm of global sustainability challenges — demonstrating what can happen when a population outstrips its resource base on a finite 'island.'
Example: In early 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated population dynamics principles in a human health context. Before interventions, case counts doubled approximately every 2-3 days in many countries, following an exponential growth pattern. Public health measures (social distancing, masking, vaccination) were designed to reduce the reproductive number (R₀) below 1. What population dynamics concept directly explains why 'flattening the curve' was the goal of these interventions?
COVID-19 spread provides a real-time demonstration of exponential and logistic growth applied to epidemiology. The basic reproductive number R₀ (the average number of secondary infections caused by one infected individual in a fully susceptible population) is analogous to the per capita growth rate r. When R₀ > 1, the infected population grows exponentially; when R₀ < 1, it declines. 'Flattening the curve' aimed to reduce R₀ through behavioral changes and vaccination, slowing the growth rate so that the peak number of simultaneous cases would not exceed hospital capacity (the healthcare system's K).

High birth rates tend to occur in species with short generation times, early maturity, and large clutch or litter sizes.

  Q3. What does the death rate (mortality) of a population represent?
    [ ] The number of individuals that migrate out of the population area
    [ ] The percentage of the population that is in the post-reproductive age class
    [*] The number of individuals dying per unit of population per unit of time
    [ ] The proportion of individuals that fail to reproduce during a breeding season
    Explanation: Death rate (mortality) is expressed as the number of deaths per 1,000 individuals per year. Like natality, mortality can be minimum (lowest possible death rate under ideal conditions) or realized (actual rate given environmental stresses). Ecologists also analyze age-specific mortality rates to construct life tables and survivorship curves (Type I, II, and III), which reveal whether most deaths occur early in life (Type III, e.g., sea turtles), are spread evenly across age groups (Type II, e.g., songbirds), or occur mainly in old age (Type I, e.g., humans).

  Q4. How is the growth rate (r) of a population calculated?
    [ ] r = carrying capacity minus the current population size
    [ ] r = the number of species in the community multiplied by the area
    [ ] r = total population size divided by the area of the habitat
    [*] r = birth rate − death rate (in a closed population without migration)
    Explanation: The intrinsic rate of natural increase (r) is the difference between the per capita birth rate (b) and per capita death rate (d): r = b − d. When immigration and emigration are included, the full population change equation becomes: ΔN/Δt = (B − D) + (I − E), where B = births, D = deaths, I = immigrants, and E = emigrants. A positive r means the population is growing, r = 0 means it is stable, and a negative r means it is declining.

  Q7. What characterizes exponential population growth?
    [ ] The population remains constant at zero growth indefinitely
    [ ] The population grows rapidly at first but then slows and levels off at carrying capacity
    [ ] The population decreases steadily due to increasing competition for resources
    [*] The population grows at a constant per capita rate without limits, producing a J-shaped curve where growth accelerates over time
    Explanation: Exponential growth is modeled by the equation dN/dt = rN, where N is population size and r is the per capita rate of increase. Because the rate of increase is proportional to the current population size, larger populations add more individuals per time period, creating the characteristic J-shaped curve. Exponential growth occurs when resources are unlimited and there is no competition, predation, or disease — conditions that are rare in nature but can occur temporarily when a species colonizes a new habitat.

  Q8. What characterizes logistic population growth?
    [ ] The population immediately reaches carrying capacity and stays constant from the start
    [*] Growth starts exponentially but slows as the population approaches carrying capacity (K), producing an S-shaped (sigmoid) curve
    [ ] Growth continues to accelerate indefinitely without any resource limitations
    [ ] The population oscillates randomly without any predictable pattern
    Explanation: Logistic growth is modeled by dN/dt = rN(K − N)/K, where the term (K − N)/K acts as an 'environmental brake' that progressively slows growth as N approaches K. When N is small relative to K, the population grows nearly exponentially; when N = K/2, growth rate is at its maximum; when N = K, growth stops (dN/dt = 0). The S-curve is more realistic than exponential growth because it accounts for density-dependent factors like resource depletion, increased competition, and waste accumulation.

K is not a fixed value — it can change over time due to seasonal variation, habitat degradation, climate change, or human activities. When a population exceeds K (overshoot), increased mortality and/or decreased reproduction typically bring it back down, sometimes causing boom-and-bust cycles. K is a central parameter in the logistic growth equation.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes population density?
    [ ] The genetic variability within a population that determines its adaptive potential
    [ ] The speed at which a population reaches its maximum size in a new habitat
    [ ] The total count of all species present within an ecosystem regardless of their spatial distribution
    [*] A measurement of the number of individuals of a species occupying a defined unit of area or volume, used to assess crowding, resource competition, and habitat quality
    Explanation: Population density (D = N/A) is one of the most important metrics in ecology because it directly influences the intensity of intraspecific interactions. High-density populations experience greater competition for food, water, and territory; higher disease transmission rates; and increased stress-related physiological responses. Ecologists distinguish between crude density (total individuals / total area) and ecological density (individuals / habitable area), with the latter being more ecologically meaningful.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes birth rate (natality)?
    [ ] The proportion of a population that survives from one year to the next
    [*] The per capita rate of reproduction in a population, typically expressed as the number of new offspring produced per 1,000 individuals per year
    [ ] The maximum number of individuals an environment can support before resources are depleted
    [ ] The movement of new individuals into an established population from other areas
    Explanation: Natality (birth rate) is one of the two intrinsic factors (along with mortality) that determine a population's natural rate of increase. Ecologists distinguish between absolute natality (total births per time period), specific natality (births per individual per time period), and age-specific natality (births per female of a specific age per time period). Fecundity schedules — tables listing age-specific reproductive rates — are essential tools for predicting population growth and are used in conservation to model endangered species recovery.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes death rate (mortality)?
    [ ] The decline in genetic diversity within a population over successive generations
    [*] The per capita rate of death in a population, typically expressed as the number of individuals dying per 1,000 individuals per year, reflecting survival conditions within the population
    [ ] The rate at which individuals leave a population by moving to adjacent habitats
    [ ] The seasonal reduction in food resources available to a population
    Explanation: Mortality rate is analyzed through life tables (originally developed for actuarial science) that track age-specific death rates (qx), survivorship (lx), and life expectancy (ex). Three general patterns emerge: Type I survivorship (most mortality in old age — humans, elephants), Type II (constant mortality across all ages — many songbirds, some lizards), and Type III (extremely high juvenile mortality with few survivors reaching adulthood — sea turtles, oysters, many fish). Understanding mortality patterns is critical for wildlife management and conservation.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes growth rate in population ecology?
    [ ] The rate at which an ecosystem accumulates new species through speciation events
    [ ] The physical growth rate of individual organisms measured in centimeters per year
    [ ] The speed at which habitat destruction reduces the total area available to a population
    [*] The per capita rate of change in population size over time, calculated as r = b − d (birth rate minus death rate) in a closed population, indicating whether the population is growing, stable, or declining
    Explanation: The intrinsic rate of natural increase (r or rmax) is the maximum per capita growth rate possible for a species under ideal conditions. It varies enormously across species: bacteria can have r values of 60+ per day, while elephants have r values near 0.02 per year. The parameter r drives both exponential growth (dN/dt = rN) and logistic growth (dN/dt = rN[(K−N)/K]). When r > 0, the population grows; when r = 0, it is at equilibrium; when r < 0, it is declining toward potential extinction.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes immigration and emigration?
    [ ] Immigration is the seasonal migration of entire populations, while emigration is permanent habitat abandonment
    [ ] Immigration refers to interbreeding between populations, while emigration refers to genetic isolation
    [ ] Immigration and emigration only apply to human populations and have no relevance to wildlife ecology
    [*] Immigration is the one-way movement of individuals into a population from elsewhere, while emigration is the one-way movement of individuals out of a population to another area — together they constitute the dispersal component of population change
    Explanation: Immigration and emigration are crucial for metapopulation dynamics — the study of multiple spatially separated populations connected by dispersal. Immigration can rescue declining populations from extinction (the 'rescue effect'), introduce new genetic variation that prevents inbreeding depression, and allow recolonization of habitats where local populations have gone extinct. Emigration relieves density-dependent pressures in source populations. Together, immigration and emigration maintain gene flow between populations, which is critical for long-term species persistence.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes exponential growth?
    [ ] Growth that slows progressively and eventually stops when the population reaches carrying capacity
    [*] A pattern of population growth where the population increases at a constant per capita rate (r) without environmental constraints, producing a J-shaped curve that accelerates without limit
    [ ] The gradual decline of a population due to accumulated environmental degradation
    [ ] A linear increase in population size over time at a fixed number of individuals per year
    Explanation: Exponential growth (dN/dt = rN) produces the characteristic J-curve because the absolute number of individuals added per time period increases as the population grows — the larger the population, the more offspring it produces. This type of growth is unsustainable long-term because no environment has infinite resources. However, exponential growth can occur temporarily when species colonize new habitats, when competitors or predators are removed, or during population recovery after a catastrophic decline.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes logistic growth?
    [ ] Unlimited population growth that continues to accelerate without any environmental constraints
    [*] A model of population growth that incorporates environmental resistance, where growth rate decreases as the population approaches carrying capacity (K), producing an S-shaped (sigmoid) curve
    [ ] A pattern where population size declines steadily due to habitat loss
    [ ] Random fluctuations in population size caused by genetic drift
    Explanation: The logistic growth model (dN/dt = rN[(K−N)/K]) is more realistic than exponential growth because it includes the feedback effect of increasing population density. The term (K−N)/K approaches 1 when N is small (near-exponential growth) and approaches 0 when N nears K (growth stops). Maximum population growth rate occurs at N = K/2, which has important implications for sustainable harvesting — fisheries management aims to keep populations near K/2 for maximum sustainable yield.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes carrying capacity (K)?
    [ ] The minimum viable population size required to prevent inbreeding and genetic drift
    [*] The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely, determined by limiting factors such as food, water, space, shelter, and waste accumulation capacity
    [ ] The physical weight or biomass that a habitat floor can structurally support
    [ ] The birth rate at which a population achieves zero population growth
    Explanation: Carrying capacity is a dynamic value, not a fixed constant — it changes with seasons, weather, resource availability, habitat modification, and the activities of other species. A drought reduces K for herbivores; invasive species can reduce K for native species by appropriating resources; and human land-use change alters K for wildlife. Populations can temporarily exceed K (overshoot), but this typically leads to resource depletion, increased mortality, and population crash (dieback), sometimes to levels well below the original K.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes demographic transition?
    [ ] The genetic adaptation of a population to a new environment over evolutionary time
    [ ] The seasonal movement of animal populations between breeding and feeding grounds
    [ ] The replacement of one community by another during ecological succession
    [*] The historical shift within developing societies from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates, typically occurring in four stages as industrialization, healthcare, and education improve
    Explanation: The demographic transition model has four stages: Stage 1 (pre-industrial) — high birth and death rates, slow population growth; Stage 2 (transitional) — death rates decline due to improved sanitation and medicine while birth rates remain high, causing rapid growth; Stage 3 (industrial) — birth rates decline as urbanization, education, and contraception spread; Stage 4 (post-industrial) — both rates are low, population growth stabilizes or even declines. Some demographers add Stage 5, where birth rates fall below death rates (as in Japan and parts of Europe).

  Q21. A single E. coli bacterium is placed in a flask of nutrient broth at time zero. E. coli divides by binary fission every 20 minutes under optimal conditions. After 10 hours (30 generations), the theoretical population would be over 1 billion cells. However, actual bacterial cultures in closed systems show growth slowing after a few hours and eventually stopping entirely. What explains the difference between the theoretical and actual growth?

    [ ] The bacteria evolve into a different species that does not divide as quickly

    [ ] Binary fission can only occur for a maximum of 30 generations before the cells become too small

This classic laboratory experiment perfectly illustrates why exponential growth is unsustainable and logistic growth is a better model for real populations.

  Q23. On a small island, a population of 25 white-tailed deer was introduced in 1920 with no natural predators. By 1935, the population had grown to over 2,000 deer.   Q24. The Kaibab Plateau in Arizona provides one of ecology's most cited case studies. In the early 1900s, the U.S. government removed wolves, mountain lions, and coyotes to protect the Kaibab deer herd. The mule deer population exploded from about 4,000 in 1906 to an estimated 100,000 by 1924. Severe overgrazing followed, and by 1939 the population had crashed to about 10,000.     Explanation: The Kaibab deer case is a dramatic illustration of the importance of density-dependent regulating factors in population dynamics. Predators had been keeping the deer population below K; when predators were removed, the population grew exponentially until food became limiting. Starvation (a density-dependent factor) then caused mass mortality. The case also illustrates that K can be permanently lowered — overgrazing destroyed root systems and topsoil, reducing the range's long-term capacity to support deer. This led to fundamental changes in wildlife management philosophy.

  Q26. Ecologists studying a lake wish to estimate the population size of largemouth bass. They capture 100 fish, tag them with small fin clips, and release them. Two weeks later, they capture another sample of 120 fish and find that 15 of them bear tags from the first capture. Using the Lincoln-Petersen method, what is the estimated population size, and what assumption must hold for this estimate to be valid?
    [*] Estimated N = 800 bass (100 × 120 / 15); the key assumption is that marked and unmarked fish mix randomly and have equal probability of capture in the second sample
    [ ] Estimated N = 235 bass (100 + 120 + 15); the key assumption is that all fish stay in one school
    [ ] Estimated N = 15 bass (only the recaptured tagged fish count); the key assumption is that fish cannot be tagged twice
    [ ] Estimated N = 12,000 bass (100 × 120); the key assumption is that marking kills some fish
    Explanation: The mark-recapture (Lincoln-Petersen) method estimates population size using the formula: N = (M × C) / R, where M = number marked in first capture (100), C = total in second capture (120), and R = recaptured marked individuals (15). So N = (100 × 120) / 15 = 800. Key assumptions include: (1) marks are not lost or overlooked, (2) marked and unmarked animals have equal catchability, (3) the population is closed (no births, deaths, immigration, or emigration between samples), and (4) marked individuals mix randomly with the population.

  Q27. Isle Royale National Park on Lake Superior has hosted a famous long-term study of wolves and moose since 1958 — the longest predator-prey study in the world.     [*] Lotka-Volterra predator-prey dynamics — the populations show density-dependent oscillations where predator abundance tracks prey abundance with a characteristic time lag
    [ ] Independent exponential growth — both species grow without any interaction between them
    [ ] Mutualism — the wolves and moose benefit each other through cooperative interactions
    [ ] Competitive exclusion — the wolves and moose are competing for the same food resources
    Explanation: The Isle Royale wolf-moose system closely mirrors the Lotka-Volterra predator-prey model, which predicts coupled oscillations: prey increase → predators increase (time lag) → prey decrease → predators decrease (time lag) → prey recover → cycle repeats. The study has also revealed complexities not captured by simple models: severe winters increase moose vulnerability, tick infestations weaken moose, genetic inbreeding reduced wolf fitness (the pack descended from a single colonizing pair), and canine parvovirus caused a wolf population crash in the 1980s.

  Q28. African elephant populations declined from an estimated 1.3 million in 1979 to about 400,000 by 2016, primarily due to ivory poaching.     Explanation: Elephant population dynamics illustrate the distinction between density-dependent and density-independent factors. Where poaching is controlled, density-dependent factors (food availability, water, habitat space) become the primary regulators, and populations grow toward K. Elephants' slow life history (15-month gestation, 10-15 year inter-birth intervals, sexual maturity at 10-15 years) means recovery is slow even under ideal conditions, making them particularly vulnerable to excessive mortality.

  Q29. Desert locusts (Schistocerca gregaria) normally live as solitary individuals across arid regions of Africa and Asia.   Q30. In early 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated population dynamics principles in a human health context. --- Lesson 4.2: Evidence of Evolution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Evidence of Evolution
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is exponential growth in the context of human population?
The human population exhibited near-exponential growth for much of the 20th century. At a growth rate of 2.1% per year (the peak in the late 1960s), the world population was doubling approximately every 33 years. The population grew from 1 billion in 1804 to 8 billion in 2022, with the most rapid absolute growth occurring between 1960-2000 when over 3 billion people were added in just 40 years.
What is the demographic transition model?
The demographic transition model has four stages: Stage 1 (pre-industrial) — high birth and death rates, slow growth; Stage 2 (early transition) — death rates drop due to improved sanitation, medicine, and agriculture while birth rates remain high, causing rapid growth; Stage 3 (late transition) — birth rates decline due to urbanization, education, and contraception access; Stage 4 (post-industrial) — both rates are low, near-zero or negative growth. Most developed nations are in Stage 4, while many developing nations are in Stages 2-3.
What is an age structure diagram (population pyramid)?
Age structure diagrams (population pyramids) divide a population into pre-reproductive (0-14 years), reproductive (15-44 years), and post-reproductive (45+ years) age classes, with males on one side and females on the other. The shape reveals the population's growth trajectory: a wide base (triangle) indicates rapid growth (many young people who will soon reproduce), a column shape indicates stability, and an inverted triangle indicates decline (fewer young than old).
What is doubling time and how is it estimated?
The Rule of 70 provides a quick estimate: doubling time ≈ 70 ÷ growth rate (%). For example, a population growing at 2% per year would double in approximately 70/2 = 35 years. At the current global growth rate of about 0.9%, the world population's doubling time is approximately 78 years. Doubling time has decreased dramatically through human history: it took 1,500+ years for the population to double from 250 million to 500 million, but only 12 years (1975-1987) to double from about 4 to 5 billion.
What is zero population growth (ZPG)?
Zero population growth (ZPG) occurs when the rate of population increase is zero — births + immigration exactly balance deaths + emigration. To achieve ZPG without migration, the total fertility rate must be at replacement level (approximately 2.1 children per woman in developed countries). Several countries, including Japan, Germany, and Italy, have already reached or fallen below ZPG, with declining populations that are aging rapidly.
What is the approximate current world population as of the 2020s?
The world population reached 8 billion on November 15, 2022, according to the United Nations. For perspective: it took all of human history until 1804 to reach 1 billion, then only 123 years to reach 2 billion (1927), 33 years for 3 billion (1960), 14 years for 4 billion (1974), 13 years for 5 billion (1987), 12 years for 6 billion (1999), 12 years for 7 billion (2011), and 11 years for 8 billion (2022). The rate of addition has slowed, and 9 billion is projected around 2037.
What is replacement-level fertility, and why is it approximately 2.1 in developed countries?
Replacement-level fertility is approximately 2.1 children per woman in developed countries because, on average, each woman needs to produce 2 children to replace herself and her partner, with the additional 0.1 compensating for children who die before reaching reproductive age. In developing countries with higher child mortality, replacement level can be 2.3 or higher. Even after reaching replacement-level fertility, a population can continue to grow for decades due to population momentum — the large number of people already in or entering their reproductive years.
The Green Revolution (1950s-1970s) introduced high-yield wheat and rice varieties, synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation to developing countries. Global grain production more than doubled between 1960 and 2000, preventing predicted mass famines. India went from famine conditions in 1966 to becoming a net food exporter. However, the Green Revolution also increased water consumption by 70% and made agriculture dependent on fossil fuel-based inputs. What is the demographic significance?
The Green Revolution is the most dramatic example of technology increasing human carrying capacity. Norman Borlaug's high-yield dwarf wheat and IR8 'miracle rice' saved an estimated 1+ billion people from famine. However, critics point out that the revolution created dependency on non-renewable inputs: synthetic fertilizers require natural gas, pesticides are petroleum-derived, and irrigation depletes aquifers (e.g., India's Punjab region shows declining water tables). This raises the critical question: has the Green Revolution truly raised K, or has it enabled a temporary overshoot that will eventually require correction?
Real-World Applications
Example: What is an age structure diagram (population pyramid)?
Age structure diagrams (population pyramids) divide a population into pre-reproductive (0-14 years), reproductive (15-44 years), and post-reproductive (45+ years) age classes, with males on one side and females on the other. The shape reveals the population's growth trajectory: a wide base (triangle) indicates rapid growth (many young people who will soon reproduce), a column shape indicates stability, and an inverted triangle indicates decline (fewer young than old).
Example: Which definition BEST describes an age structure diagram?
Age structure diagrams are powerful predictive tools because a population's current age distribution constrains its future growth. A country with 40% of its population under age 15 (like Uganda) will continue growing for decades even if fertility drops to replacement level, because those young people have not yet begun reproducing. This 'built-in' growth potential is called population momentum and explains why global population will continue growing for decades even as fertility rates decline worldwide.
Example: In 1798, Thomas Malthus published 'An Essay on the Principle of Population,' warning that human population growth (which he described as geometric/exponential) would inevitably outstrip food production (which he described as growing only arithmetically/linearly), leading to famine, disease, and war. Over 200 years later, the global population has grown from 1 billion to 8 billion, yet average caloric intake has increased. What happened that Malthus did not predict?
Malthus's key error was assuming food production could only grow linearly. In reality, the Green Revolution (1950s-1970s) introduced high-yield crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation that more than doubled global grain production. Norman Borlaug, the 'father of the Green Revolution,' is credited with saving over a billion people from starvation. However, neo-Malthusians argue that these gains are unsustainable — they depend on finite fossil fuels, depleting aquifers, and soil degradation, raising questions about whether technology can continue to expand Earth's carrying capacity for humans.
Example: China implemented its one-child policy in 1979 to control rapid population growth. The policy reduced China's TFR from approximately 6.0 in the 1960s to about 1.5 by the 2000s. However, it also created a severely distorted age structure: by 2030, China will have over 400 million people over age 60, a shrinking workforce, and a sex ratio skewed by cultural preference for male children. China reversed the policy, allowing two children in 2015 and three in 2021, but birth rates have not recovered. What demographic concept explains why?
China's experience illustrates the 'fertility trap' hypothesis: once TFR falls below a certain threshold (~1.5), it becomes self-reinforcing because small families become the cultural norm, childcare costs rise in economies designed for small families, and the reduced number of women of childbearing age (from previous low fertility) means fewer births even if individual fertility increases slightly. China's shift from population control to pro-natalist policies (including subsidies and tax breaks) has had minimal effect, mirroring similar failures in Japan, South Korea, and Singapore.
Example: After World War II (1945-1964), many developed countries experienced a dramatic increase in birth rates known as the 'Baby Boom.' In the United States, approximately 76 million babies were born during this period. This cohort is now entering retirement age (2010-2030). What demographic challenge does the aging of the Baby Boom generation create?
The Baby Boom illustrates how a temporary spike in fertility creates a 'population bulge' that moves through the age structure like a wave, creating different challenges at each stage: overcrowded schools (1950s-1960s), housing and job competition (1970s-1990s), and now pension/healthcare strain (2010s-2030s). In the US, the ratio of workers to Social Security beneficiaries is declining from 5.1:1 in 1960 to about 2.8:1 today and a projected 2.3:1 by 2035, threatening the financial sustainability of the system.
Example: Nigeria's population was approximately 220 million in 2023, making it Africa's most populous country. With a TFR of ~5.3 and 43% of the population under age 15, the UN projects Nigeria's population could reach 375 million by 2050 and potentially surpass 500 million by 2100, making it the world's third most populous country (after India and China). Only about 12% of married women use modern contraception. What combination of factors drives this projection?
Nigeria exemplifies the demographic challenge facing sub-Saharan Africa. With a TFR of 5.3 (each woman having, on average, over 5 children), the annual growth rate is about 2.5%, giving a doubling time of ~28 years. The massive under-15 cohort (43% of 220 million = ~95 million children) ensures decades of population momentum. Barriers to demographic transition include limited female education in northern regions, low contraceptive usage, early marriage (median age at first marriage is 15 in some northern states), and cultural valuation of large families. Accelerating the demographic transition through education and family planning is critical for Nigeria's sustainable development.

The 'demographic dividend' occurs when a country has a low dependency ratio (many working-age adults, few dependents), creating favorable conditions for economic growth.

--- Lesson 4.3: Sources of Genetic Variation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sources of Genetic Variation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is carrying capacity (K)?
Carrying capacity (K) is the equilibrium population size at which the population growth rate equals zero — births plus immigration exactly balance deaths plus emigration. K is determined by whichever resource is most limiting (Liebig's Law of the Minimum). Importantly, K is not a fixed value — it fluctuates with seasons, climate, resource renewal rates, and disturbances. When N > K, mortality exceeds reproduction and the population declines; when N < K, reproduction exceeds mortality and the population grows.
What is a limiting factor in ecology?
Limiting factors are the ecological 'bottlenecks' that constrain population growth. According to Liebig's Law of the Minimum, the factor in shortest supply relative to demand determines the maximum population size, regardless of how abundant other resources are. Common limiting factors include food, water, nesting sites, space, light (for plants), and nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus for aquatic systems). Identifying the primary limiting factor is crucial for wildlife management and conservation.
What is a density-dependent limiting factor?
Density-dependent factors are the primary mechanism of population regulation that produces logistic growth and maintains populations near carrying capacity. As population density increases: competition for food, water, and territory intensifies; disease transmission rates increase due to closer contact; predators concentrate on abundant prey; parasites spread more easily; and stress-related physiological effects (reduced reproduction, weakened immune systems) become more pronounced. These negative feedback mechanisms are what create the S-curve of logistic growth.
What is a density-independent limiting factor?
Density-independent factors kill a fixed percentage of the population regardless of whether the population is small or large, sparse or crowded. Examples include hurricanes, floods, volcanic eruptions, wildfires, extreme temperature events, droughts, and human activities like pesticide application or habitat destruction. These factors cannot regulate populations around K because they don't intensify as populations grow — they can cause dramatic crashes, but the population can subsequently rebound through exponential growth if the habitat recovers.
What is resource depletion in the context of population ecology?
Resource depletion is a key mechanism linking population overshoot to population crash. When consumption exceeds regeneration (for renewable resources) or when non-renewable resources are consumed, carrying capacity declines. This creates a destructive feedback loop: a growing population depletes resources → K decreases → N exceeds the new K → increased mortality and reduced reproduction → population crashes below K. In severe cases, the degraded environment supports a much smaller population than it originally could, making recovery slow or impossible.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a density-independent limiting factor?
Drought is density-independent because it destroys the same proportion of resources regardless of population size. A drought kills the same percentage of grass whether there are 50 or 5,000 herbivores grazing. Disease, competition, and increased predation are all density-dependent — their severity intensifies as population density rises. Other density-independent factors include floods, hurricanes, volcanic eruptions, wildfires, and extreme cold snaps.
Example: A researcher studying Yellowstone's wolf-elk dynamics observes that after wolf reintroduction in 1995, elk populations declined and shifted their foraging behavior. Willows and aspens along riverbanks regenerated, stabilizing stream banks and improving habitat for beavers, fish, and songbirds. This phenomenon, where a top predator's effects cascade through multiple trophic levels, illustrates what concept related to limiting factors?
This is Yellowstone's famous trophic cascade. Wolves are a density-dependent limiting factor for elk — as elk crowd river valleys, they become easier targets for wolf packs. The 'ecology of fear' also matters: elk avoid lingering in vulnerable riparian areas, reducing overgrazing even beyond what direct predation achieves. This restores vegetation, which increases K for beavers, fish, birds, and insects. The removal of a single limiting factor (wolf predation on elk) had allowed elk to overgraze and reduce K for the entire riparian community.
Example: A tropical island has a population of 500 iguanas. A severe hurricane kills 200 iguanas (40% mortality) regardless of age, sex, or health. The following year, a disease outbreak kills 150 of the remaining 300 iguanas, primarily affecting individuals in the most densely populated coastal areas. How should an ecologist classify these two mortality events?
This scenario tests the critical distinction between the two types of limiting factors. The hurricane killed 40% of iguanas regardless of local density — a hallmark of density-independent factors. Whether iguanas were crowded on the coast or spread inland, the same proportion died. The disease, however, preferentially affected the densely populated coastal zone because pathogen transmission depends on contact rates, which increase with density — classic density-dependent regulation. In nature, populations often experience both factor types simultaneously, and understanding which is operating is essential for conservation management.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes 'environmental resistance'?
    [ ] The physical ability of organisms to withstand harsh environmental conditions like extreme heat or cold
    [*] The combined effect of all environmental limiting factors (both density-dependent and density-independent) that collectively prevent a population from growing exponentially indefinitely
    [ ] The genetic resistance that evolves in populations exposed to pesticides or antibiotics
    [ ] The tendency of an ecosystem to resist invasion by non-native species
    Explanation: Environmental resistance is the sum of all forces that oppose biotic potential (the maximum reproductive rate under ideal conditions). It includes density-dependent factors (competition, predation, disease, parasitism) and density-independent factors (weather extremes, natural disasters). The difference between biotic potential and environmental resistance determines the actual growth rate: actual growth = biotic potential − environmental resistance. When these forces exactly balance, the population is at carrying capacity.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes 'overshoot' in population ecology?
    [ ] When a population consistently stays well below carrying capacity due to predation
    [*] When a population temporarily exceeds carrying capacity because growth momentum continues even as resources become limiting, often leading to a subsequent population crash
    [ ] When a population evolves adaptations that permanently increase carrying capacity
    [ ] When organisms migrate beyond their typical geographic range during favorable years
    Explanation: Overshoot occurs because there is a time lag between when the population exceeds K and when the negative effects of resource scarcity actually reduce population growth. During overshoot, consumption exceeds resource regeneration, potentially degrading the environment and lowering the subsequent K. The population then crashes, possibly overshooting K on the downside in a boom-and-bust cycle. Species with high reproductive rates (r-strategists) are especially prone to overshoot-and-crash dynamics.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes 'boom-and-bust cycle'?
    [ ] A pattern where population size increases gradually and then levels off at carrying capacity permanently
    [*] A pattern of dramatic population increase (boom) followed by a sharp decline (bust), typically caused by overshoot of carrying capacity and subsequent resource depletion
    [ ] The explosive growth of a new species that permanently replaces a native population
    [ ] The steady, predictable seasonal migration of a population between two habitats
    Explanation: Boom-and-bust cycles are characteristic of r-selected species in unstable or newly available environments and of populations that lack effective density-dependent regulation. The 'boom' phase shows exponential or near-exponential growth as resources are abundant. The 'bust' phase occurs when the population overshoots K, depletes resources, and crashes. Examples include locust swarms, algal blooms, lemming population cycles, and invasive species outbreaks. Some populations undergo repeated cycles while others may stabilize or go locally extinct after a bust.

  Q21. In 1944, the U.S. Coast Guard introduced 29 reindeer to St. Matthew Island in the Bering Sea. By 1963, the herd had grown to approximately 6,000 animals. During the harsh winter of 1963-64, the population crashed to just 42 individuals. What type of population growth pattern does this case study BEST illustrate?
    [ ] Classic logistic growth reaching a stable equilibrium at carrying capacity in accordance with classical theory
    [ ] A stable predator-prey cycle that naturally regulated the population under the given assumptions
    [ ] Exponential growth that was terminated by a volcanic eruption, particularly regarding best under normal circumstances
    [*] An overshoot-and-crash (boom-and-bust) cycle, where the reindeer exceeded the island's carrying capacity by depleting lichen reserves, and a density-independent factor (severe winter) triggered the final catastrophic crash
    Explanation: The St. Matthew Island reindeer case is one of ecology's most cited examples of overshoot-and-crash dynamics. Without predators or competitors, the reindeer population grew exponentially and vastly exceeded the island's lichen carrying capacity. The slow-growing lichen (taking decades to regenerate) was stripped bare. When the severe winter hit, the already resource-depleted population had no food reserves. This illustrates how density-dependent (resource depletion) and density-independent (extreme weather) factors can interact to cause catastrophic population collapse.

  Q22. In Australia, European rabbits introduced in 1859 (just 24 individuals) grew to an estimated 600 million by 1950. The government released the Myxoma virus to control them — initially killing 99.8% of infected rabbits. Over the following decades, however, the virus became less lethal (from 99.8% to ~25% mortality) and rabbit populations partially recovered. What population ecology concepts does this case study BEST illustrate?
    [ ] Normal logistic growth of rabbits regulated by native Australian predators, particularly regarding better following the established framework
    [ ] A density-dependent decline caused by intraspecific competition among rabbits under normal circumstances
    [ ] Successful permanent eradication of an invasive species through biological control as typically measured in practice
    [*] Explosive growth of an introduced species without natural limiting factors, followed by a density-independent control measure (biological control with Myxoma virus), and subsequent coevolution that reduced the virus's effectiveness as a limiting factor
    Explanation: This case demonstrates several key concepts: (1) Without natural predators, parasites, or competitors (the normal density-dependent limiting factors), the rabbits grew exponentially far beyond what the fragile Australian landscape could sustain. (2) The Myxoma virus acted as a dramatic density-independent-like control, crashing the population. (3) Coevolution then occurred — rabbits evolved resistance while the virus evolved reduced virulence (less lethal strains spread better), weakening the virus as a limiting factor. This is why single-strategy biological control often fails long-term.

  Q23. A marine biologist studying a coral reef notices that as sea otter populations increase in the surrounding kelp forest, sea urchin populations decrease. With fewer urchins, kelp forests grow denser, providing more habitat for fish and invertebrates. If otters were removed, what would MOST LIKELY happen to the carrying capacity of the kelp forest ecosystem?
    [ ] Carrying capacity would increase because fewer otters means less competition for fish in the context of this analysis
    [ ] The kelp would grow faster without otters disturbing the water column, particularly regarding urchins as observed in standard experiments
    [ ] Carrying capacity would remain unchanged because urchins are density-independent factors as predicted by standard models
    [*] Carrying capacity for kelp-dependent species would decrease because urchin populations would explode without predation (a density-dependent control), overgraze the kelp, and create barren 'urchin barrens' — a trophic cascade reducing habitat for hundreds of species
    Explanation: This describes a trophic cascade driven by a keystone predator. Sea otters are the primary density-dependent limiting factor for sea urchins. Without otters, urchin populations explode and overgraze kelp forests, creating 'urchin barrens.' This reduces K for the hundreds of species (fish, invertebrates, marine mammals) that depend on kelp for food and shelter. Historically, the Pacific coast fur trade nearly drove otters to extinction, and kelp forests collapsed. Otter reintroduction programs have restored kelp ecosystems, demonstrating how one species can control carrying capacity for entire communities.

  Q24. A wildlife ecologist is studying a lake where bass and bluegill populations coexist. She notices that in years with abundant aquatic insects (the primary food), both populations grow. In years with low insect numbers, bass population declines first because bass need more food per individual. Which ecological principle BEST explains why bass are the first to decline?
    [ ] Bass decline first because they are a density-independent factor for bluegill
    [ ] Bass are r-strategists that naturally have boom-and-bust population cycles
    [ ] Gause's Competitive Exclusion Principle — two species cannot coexist on the same resource
    [*] Liebig's Law of the Minimum — aquatic insects are the most limiting resource, and bass have higher per capita food requirements, so they reach their resource limit first
    Explanation: Liebig's Law states that the resource in shortest supply limits growth. When insects are abundant, they are not limiting for either species. When insects decline, they become the minimum resource. Because bass require more food per individual (higher metabolic demand), insects become limiting for bass before bluegill. This illustrates that different species in the same ecosystem can have different effective carrying capacities based on their per capita resource requirements for the most limiting factor.

  Q25. During the Irish Potato Famine (1845-1852), Ireland's population dropped from approximately 8.2 million to 6.5 million through death and emigration. The population had become almost entirely dependent on a single crop variety (the Irish Lumper potato), which was devastated by the pathogen Phytophthora infestans. What concept about carrying capacity does this historical event BEST illustrate?
    [*] When carrying capacity depends critically on a single resource with no redundancy, a sudden loss of that resource (by the density-independent pathogen) can cause catastrophic population decline — demonstrating the danger of resource homogeneity in determining K
    [ ] Human populations are immune to ecological carrying capacity because of technology when considering all relevant factors
    [ ] Density-dependent competition for food naturally regulates human populations to K based on the prevailing evidence
    [ ] Immigration always increases when populations are below carrying capacity, particularly regarding approximately when evaluated systematically
    Explanation: Ireland's population had grown rapidly because the potato allowed far more calories per acre than grain, effectively raising K. But this K was fragile — entirely dependent on one crop variety with no genetic diversity. When P. infestans destroyed the potato crop, K collapsed almost overnight. The population response (starvation deaths + massive emigration to America) illustrates overshoot followed by a crash when the resource base suddenly fails. This tragic example shows that carrying capacity based on a single vulnerable resource creates extreme fragility.

  Q26. A researcher studying Yellowstone's wolf-elk dynamics observes that after wolf reintroduction in 1995, elk populations declined and shifted their foraging behavior. Willows and aspens along riverbanks regenerated, stabilizing stream banks and improving habitat for beavers, fish, and songbirds. This phenomenon, where a top predator's effects cascade through multiple trophic levels, illustrates what concept related to limiting factors?
    [ ] Density-independent limitation because wolves kill elk regardless of elk population size
    [ ] Bottom-up regulation where increased plant growth caused wolves to be reintroduced
    [ ] Resource depletion by wolves that reduced the carrying capacity for all Yellowstone species
    [*] Top-down regulation through a trophic cascade — wolves act as a density-dependent limiting factor on elk, which changes the effective carrying capacity for plant communities and, consequently, for all species that depend on those plants
    Explanation: This is Yellowstone's famous trophic cascade. Wolves are a density-dependent limiting factor for elk — as elk crowd river valleys, they become easier targets for wolf packs. The 'ecology of fear' also matters: elk avoid lingering in vulnerable riparian areas, reducing overgrazing even beyond what direct predation achieves. This restores vegetation, which increases K for beavers, fish, birds, and insects. The removal of a single limiting factor (wolf predation on elk) had allowed elk to overgraze and reduce K for the entire riparian community.

  Q27. A conservation biologist is managing an endangered Florida panther population of approximately 120-230 individuals. Despite adequate prey and habitat, the population suffers from high rates of heart defects, kinked tails, and low sperm quality due to inbreeding. Introducing Texas pumas as genetic rescue in 1995 significantly improved fitness. What type of limiting factor is genetic diversity in this case?
    [ ] Not a limiting factor because genetics cannot affect carrying capacity
    [ ] A density-independent factor because genetic mutations occur randomly regardless of population size
    [ ] An example of Liebig's Law where genes are the minimum resource
    [*] A density-dependent limiting factor operating at small population sizes — as the population decreased and became more inbred, genetic problems intensified, further reducing fitness and reproductive success (an extinction vortex)
    Explanation: This illustrates the extinction vortex and Allee effect — at small population sizes, harmful effects intensify rather than diminish. Inbreeding depression is density-dependent in reverse: as the population gets smaller, genetic diversity decreases, deleterious alleles become homozygous more frequently, and fitness declines — which further reduces the population. The Texas puma introduction (genetic rescue) broke this cycle by introducing new alleles, increasing heterozygosity, and improving kitten survival from ~23% to ~67%. This shows that at very low densities, genetic factors can become the primary limiting factor.

  Q28. An ecologist comparing two lakes finds that Lake A (receiving agricultural runoff rich in phosphorus) has massive algal blooms every summer, depleting oxygen and killing fish. Lake B (with forested watershed) has clear water and diverse fish populations. Both lakes receive similar sunlight and temperature. What role does phosphorus play in Lake A?
    [ ] Phosphorus reduces the carrying capacity for algae, allowing fish populations to thrive in most standard conditions
    [*] Phosphorus was formerly the limiting factor for algae (Liebig's Law), and the agricultural runoff removed this limit, raising K for algae far above what the lake's oxygen budget can sustain — the resulting eutrophication then reduces K for fish and other aerobic organisms
    [ ] Phosphorus is a density-independent factor that kills fish directly through chemical toxicity following the established framework
    [ ] Phosphorus has no effect on carrying capacity because it is not a biological resource when evaluated systematically
    Explanation: This is a classic application of Liebig's Law and carrying capacity. In freshwater systems, phosphorus is typically the most limiting nutrient for algae. When agricultural runoff adds excess phosphorus, K for algae skyrockets, causing blooms. When the blooms die and decompose, bacteria consume dissolved oxygen (biological oxygen demand), creating hypoxic 'dead zones.' This cascading effect lowers K for aerobic organisms (fish, invertebrates). Eutrophication of the Chesapeake Bay, Lake Erie, and Gulf of Mexico dead zone all follow this pattern.

  Q29. In the Kaibab Plateau of Arizona (1906-1939), government hunters removed nearly all wolves, coyotes, and mountain lions to protect the mule deer herd. The deer population surged from ~4,000 to an estimated 100,000 by 1924, severely overgrazing the range. By 1939, the population had crashed to ~10,000 — less than what the plateau could have sustainably supported at the beginning. What does the final population falling BELOW the original K demonstrate?
    [ ] Carrying capacity always returns to its original value after a population crash as predicted by standard models
    [*] Resource depletion during overshoot permanently lowered carrying capacity — the deer destroyed vegetation faster than it could regenerate, causing soil erosion and habitat degradation that reduced K below its original level
    [ ] Predator removal had no effect on the deer population dynamics, particularly regarding during as determined by standard methodology
    [ ] The deer went extinct because density-independent factors destroyed all vegetation following the established framework
    Explanation: The Kaibab deer case is the classic example of how overshoot can permanently reduce carrying capacity. When predators (the primary density-dependent limiting factor) were removed, deer grew unchecked past K, overgrazing vegetation, compacting soil, and causing erosion. The range was so degraded that it could no longer support even the original 4,000 deer. This demonstrates that K is not just a number — it depends on habitat quality, and a population can destroy its own habitat during overshoot. The term 'irruptive growth' describes this pattern of rapid increase followed by environmental destruction and crash.

  Q30. A tropical island has a population of 500 iguanas. A severe hurricane kills 200 iguanas (40% mortality) regardless of age, sex, or health. The following year, a disease outbreak kills 150 of the remaining 300 iguanas, primarily affecting individuals in the most densely populated coastal areas. How should an ecologist classify these two mortality events?
    [ ] Both are density-independent because they both reduced the population significantly in most standard conditions
    [ ] The hurricane is density-dependent and the disease is density-independent under normal circumstances
    [*] The hurricane is a density-independent factor (kills a fixed proportion regardless of population density), while the disease is a density-dependent factor (its transmission and mortality increase with crowding in high-density coastal areas)
    [ ] Both are density-dependent because they both caused deaths in a dense population based on the prevailing evidence
    Explanation: This scenario tests the critical distinction between the two types of limiting factors. The hurricane killed 40% of iguanas regardless of local density — a hallmark of density-independent factors. Whether iguanas were crowded on the coast or spread inland, the same proportion died. The disease, however, preferentially affected the densely populated coastal zone because pathogen transmission depends on contact rates, which increase with density — classic density-dependent regulation. In nature, populations often experience both factor types simultaneously, and understanding which is operating is essential for conservation management.

--- Lesson 4.4: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an r-selected (r-strategist) species?
The 'r' in r-selection refers to the intrinsic rate of natural increase (r in the equation dN/dt = rN). r-strategists maximize their reproductive rate: short generation times, early maturity, high fecundity, small body size, and minimal parental investment. This strategy is favored in unpredictable or disturbed environments where rapid colonization matters more than competitive ability. r-strategists often display boom-and-bust population dynamics and typically exhibit Type III survivorship curves (high juvenile mortality).
What is a K-selected (K-strategist) species?
The 'K' in K-selection refers to carrying capacity (K in the logistic growth equation). K-strategists are adapted to live near carrying capacity in stable, competitive environments. Traits include large body size, late maturity, long lifespan, few offspring per reproductive event, high parental investment, and high juvenile survival rates. They typically show Type I survivorship curves. K-strategists are more vulnerable to population declines caused by human activities because their slow reproduction makes recovery difficult.
What is a population crash?
A population crash (die-off) is the bust phase of a boom-and-bust cycle. It occurs when a population that has exceeded K experiences severe resource scarcity and environmental degradation. The crash is often dramatic — the St. Matthew Island reindeer went from 6,000 to 42 in a single harsh winter. Crashes can overshoot on the downside, dropping the population well below K, especially if the overshoot phase damaged the resource base and lowered K itself. r-strategists experience crashes more frequently, while K-strategist crashes are rarer but often more devastating to the species.
What is a predator-prey cycle?
Predator-prey cycles are among the most important density-dependent population dynamics in ecology. The classic pattern: (1) prey increases → (2) predators increase (with a time lag, due to reproduction delays) → (3) heavy predation reduces prey → (4) food shortage reduces predators → cycle repeats. The Lotka-Volterra equations model this mathematically. The best-documented example is the Canadian lynx and snowshoe hare cycle (~9-11 year period), recorded in nearly 200 years of fur-trapping data from the Hudson's Bay Company.
Real-World Applications
Example: In Yellowstone National Park, elk populations grew dramatically after wolf extirpation in the 1920s. Elk overgrazed riparian vegetation, which increased stream erosion, reduced beaver habitat, and decreased biodiversity. After wolf reintroduction in 1995, elk numbers declined and shifted their behavior (avoiding valleys), allowing vegetation recovery. A wildlife manager argues this shows wolves are the SINGLE factor regulating elk. Is this claim fully supported?
Population regulation is almost always multi-factorial. Wolves provide important top-down density-dependent regulation (both through direct predation and the 'ecology of fear' that changes elk behavior). However, Yellowstone elk are also regulated by: bottom-up food limitation (winter range quality), other predators (bears, cougars), disease (chronic wasting disease, brucellosis), density-independent winter severity, and human hunting outside park boundaries. Research suggests wolf reintroduction was one of several concurrent factors in the elk decline. Understanding that regulation involves multiple interacting mechanisms — not a single silver bullet — is essential for ecosystem management.
Example: A pest management specialist notices that a ladybug (Coccinella septempunctata) population in an agricultural field surges after aphid numbers increase each spring, then declines in late summer when aphid populations crash. The next year, an early frost kills 70% of overwintering ladybugs before aphids emerge. How would this affect the predator-prey cycle?
This illustrates how density-independent events can disrupt density-dependent regulatory cycles. The normal cycle: aphids increase → ladybugs increase (lagged) → ladybug predation controls aphids → aphid crash → ladybug decline → cycle resets. The frost (density-independent) crashes ladybug numbers before the cycle begins, removing the density-dependent control on aphids. Without sufficient predation, aphids can explode, potentially causing crop damage. Integrated Pest Management (IPM) must account for these disruptions — farmers may need to release commercial ladybugs or apply targeted controls when winter conditions weaken natural predator-prey regulation.
Example: A conservation team is developing a recovery plan for the California condor, which was reduced to just 22 individuals in 1987. Condors are classic K-strategists: they don't breed until age 6-8, produce only one egg every other year, and have a 60-year lifespan. Despite a captive breeding program that has increased the population to ~500, the species remains critically dependent on human management. Why is the K-strategy life history a particular challenge for recovery?
K-strategist traits make conservation recovery extremely challenging. At one egg every two years and 6-8 years to maturity, even a perfect breeding program produces population growth far slower than r-strategists could achieve. Each individual lost represents a proportionally greater setback. The small population is vulnerable to the extinction vortex: inbreeding depression, demographic stochasticity (random variation in births/deaths), and Allee effects. Contrast with Kemp's ridley sea turtles (r-strategist): protected nesting beaches and headstarting programs produced rapid population increases because each female produces hundreds of eggs per season. K-strategists require sustained, multi-decade commitment and zero tolerance for ongoing mortality threats.

  Q5. What are survivorship curves?
    [ ] Graphs showing the total population size of a species over many centuries
    [ ] Charts ranking species from most to least likely to survive climate change
    [ ] Diagrams showing the geographic range where a species can survive
    [*] Graphs showing the proportion of individuals in a cohort that survive to each age, classified as Type I (late loss), Type II (constant loss), or Type III (early loss)
    Explanation: Survivorship curves plot the number of survivors (log scale on y-axis) against age (x-axis) for a cohort. Type I (convex): low early mortality, most deaths in old age — typical of K-strategists (humans, elephants, whales). Type II (linear/diagonal): roughly constant mortality at all ages — typical of some birds, rodents, and some reptiles. Type III (concave): extremely high juvenile mortality but high survival for the few that reach adulthood — typical of r-strategists (oysters, sea turtles, most fish, many plants). Most species fall somewhere between these idealized types.

  Q6. Which of the following traits is characteristic of an r-selected species?
    [ ] Constant population size with no fluctuations in any environment
    [*] Short lifespan, early sexual maturity, high fecundity, small body size, and minimal parental investment
    [ ] Large body size and exclusive reliance on K-strategy reproduction
    [ ] Long lifespan, late sexual maturity, few offspring, and extensive parental care
    Explanation: r-selected species invest in quantity over quality. Their traits — early maturity, small body, short generation time, many offspring, and no parental care — allow rapid population growth when conditions improve. This strategy is advantageous in unstable environments (disturbed habitats, seasonal ponds, early successional stages) where being first to exploit new resources outweighs competitive ability. Examples: bacteria (divide every 20 minutes), dandelions (thousands of wind-dispersed seeds), mosquitoes (hundreds of eggs per batch), and codfish (millions of eggs per female).

  Q7. How does territoriality function as a population regulation mechanism?
    [*] Territorial individuals defend exclusive areas, limiting access to resources like food, nesting sites, and mates — thereby capping the number of breeding individuals the habitat can support
    [ ] Territoriality only occurs in mammalian species and has no effect on population size
    [ ] Territorial species have no carrying capacity because they can always expand their territory
    [ ] Territorial behavior increases population density by forcing all individuals into smaller areas
    Explanation: Territoriality is a behavioral form of density-dependent population regulation. As density increases, more energy is spent defending territories and more individuals fail to obtain one. Non-territorial individuals ('floaters') typically cannot breed, effectively reducing the population's reproductive output. Examples include wolves defending pack territories, songbirds defending nesting areas with song, and cichlid fish defending spawning sites. Territory size often adjusts with resource availability — richer habitats support smaller territories and thus more individuals.

  Q8. In a predator-prey cycle, why does the predator population peak AFTER the prey population peaks?
    [ ] Because predators deliberately wait for prey populations to decline before reproducing
    [ ] Because predators always have longer lifespans than their prey
    [*] Because there is a time lag — predators need time to reproduce in response to abundant prey, so their population continues growing even after prey numbers begin declining
    [ ] Because predator populations are density-independent and unrelated to prey numbers
    Explanation: The time lag is the key to understanding predator-prey oscillations. When prey is abundant, predators have ample food → increased reproduction → predator population grows. But reproduction takes time (gestation, maturation), so predator numbers continue increasing even after prey has started declining. By the time predators peak, prey is already scarce, causing predator starvation and population decline. This lagged response creates the characteristic out-of-phase oscillations seen in the lynx-hare cycle (~9-11 year period) from Hudson's Bay Company records.

  Q9. What type of survivorship curve do most K-selected species exhibit?
    [*] Type I (convex curve) — low juvenile mortality, with most individuals surviving to old age before mortality increases sharply
    [ ] K-selected species do not follow any survivorship curve pattern
    [ ] Type II (linear curve) — equal probability of death at every age throughout the lifespan
    [ ] Type III (concave curve) — extremely high juvenile mortality with few survivors reaching adulthood
    Explanation: K-selected species invest heavily in parental care, protecting offspring through vulnerable early life stages. This produces a Type I survivorship curve: most individuals survive through youth and adulthood, with mortality concentrated among the elderly. Humans in developed nations, elephants, and whales show classic Type I patterns. On a semi-log plot, Type I appears as a convex curve that drops steeply near the maximum lifespan. This contrasts sharply with r-strategists that typically show Type III curves with massive early die-offs.

  Q10. How does negative feedback regulate populations near carrying capacity?
    [ ] Negative feedback only operates through predation and has no other mechanisms
    [ ] Negative feedback means that populations always shrink regardless of resource availability
    [*] As population size approaches or exceeds K, density-dependent factors intensify (competition, disease, predation), which reduces the growth rate and pushes the population back toward K
    [ ] Negative feedback always prevents populations from ever reaching carrying capacity
    Explanation: Negative feedback is the self-correcting mechanism in population regulation. When N < K, resources are abundant, growth rate is positive, and the population increases toward K. When N > K (overshoot), resources are scarce, growth rate becomes negative, and the population decreases toward K. This works like a thermostat: deviations from K trigger corrective responses. The logistic equation models this as dN/dt = rN((K−N)/K) — the term (K−N)/K decreases as N approaches K, slowing growth. At N = K, growth stops. Above K, growth is negative.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes 'r-strategist'?
    [ ] A species that produces few, large offspring with extensive parental care to maximize individual survival
    [ ] A species that relies on mutualistic relationships to reproduce successfully
    [ ] A species that always maintains its population exactly at carrying capacity
    [*] A species with a life history strategy that maximizes reproductive rate (r), producing many offspring with little parental investment — adapted for rapid colonization of unstable or newly available habitats
    Explanation: r-strategists maximize the 'r' in the population growth equation (dN/dt = rN). Key traits: small body size, short lifespan, early maturity, large numbers of small offspring, no parental care, Type III survivorship curve, and rapid population growth. They dominate in disturbed, unpredictable, or newly created habitats (early succession). The tradeoff: high reproduction comes at the cost of individual offspring survival. Examples: bacteria, annual plants (dandelions, ragweed), insects (mosquitoes, aphids), and many marine invertebrates (oysters producing millions of eggs).

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes 'K-strategist'?
    [*] A species with a life history strategy that maximizes competitive ability near carrying capacity (K), producing few offspring with high parental investment — adapted for stable, competitive environments
    [ ] A species that can only survive in environments with high levels of potassium
    [ ] A species that reproduces as quickly as possible with many small offspring and no parental care
    [ ] A species whose population size is always decreasing toward extinction
    Explanation: K-strategists are adapted to live at or near carrying capacity in mature, stable ecosystems (late succession). Key traits: large body size, long lifespan, late maturity, few offspring per event, extensive parental care, Type I survivorship, and strong competitive ability. The tradeoff: high individual survival comes at the cost of slow population growth, making these species vulnerable to population declines from human activities. Recovery from low numbers takes many generations. Examples: elephants (22-month gestation), blue whales (one calf every 2-3 years), great apes, and albatrosses.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes 'population crash'?
    [*] A rapid, dramatic decline in population size, typically caused by exceeding carrying capacity (overshoot), severe resource depletion, or catastrophic events that push mortality far above reproduction
    [ ] The merger of two populations into a single, larger population
    [ ] A gradual decrease in population size over many centuries as part of normal succession
    [ ] A temporary dip in population size that always returns to the previous level
    Explanation: Population crashes can result from density-dependent factors (starvation after overshoot, disease epidemics), density-independent factors (catastrophic weather, volcanic eruptions), or their combination. Key characteristics: speed (occurs over a short time frame relative to the species' generation time), severity (large percentage mortality), and potential irreversibility (if the resource base was damaged during overshoot, K may be permanently lowered). The crash phase is part of the boom-and-bust cycle common in r-strategist populations and in populations freed from normal density-dependent controls.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes 'predator-prey cycle'?
    [ ] A single event where predators completely eliminate a prey population from an ecosystem
    [*] A recurring oscillation in population sizes of predator and prey species, where increases in prey drive increases in predators (with a time lag), which then reduce prey, causing predator decline — and the cycle repeats
    [ ] A stable equilibrium where predator and prey populations remain at constant, equal sizes
    [ ] The evolutionary process by which prey develop defenses against predators over millions of years
    Explanation: Predator-prey cycles are a fundamental emergent property of density-dependent population regulation. The Lotka-Volterra equations model them mathematically: prey growth depends on prey density and predation rate; predator growth depends on prey availability. The cycle persists because of the time lag between prey abundance changes and predator reproductive responses. Cycle periods vary: lynx-hare (~9-11 years), lemming-arctic fox (~3-4 years), wolf-moose on Isle Royale (~30-40 years). Real cycles are often more complex than models predict, influenced by weather, alternative prey, and multiple predator species.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes 'survivorship curves'?
    [ ] Maps showing the geographic distribution of surviving populations of a species
    [*] Graphical representations of age-specific survival patterns in a population, classified into Type I (most mortality in old age), Type II (constant mortality at all ages), and Type III (most mortality in early life)
    [ ] Charts showing how quickly different species evolve over geological time
    [ ] Graphs showing the number of offspring produced by a species at each age
    Explanation: Survivorship curves are plotted with age on the x-axis and the log of the number of survivors on the y-axis, starting with a cohort of 1,000 (or 100%). Type I (convex): K-strategists like humans and elephants — most survive to old age. Type II (approximately linear on log scale): some birds, lizards, and small mammals — equal death probability at all ages. Type III (concave): r-strategists like oysters, sea turtles, and oak trees — massive early mortality with few survivors, but those few live long. Understanding survivorship curves is essential for conservation (predicting population viability) and demographics (life insurance actuarial tables).

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes 'negative feedback' in population regulation?
    [ ] The decline in genetic fitness caused by inbreeding in small populations
    [ ] A process where population growth continues to accelerate without any regulatory mechanism
    [*] A self-correcting mechanism where deviations from carrying capacity trigger density-dependent responses that push the population back toward equilibrium — growth slows as N approaches K and becomes negative if N exceeds K
    [ ] The harmful psychological effects of overcrowding on individual organisms
    Explanation: Negative feedback is the thermostat of population dynamics. In the logistic model, dN/dt = rN((K−N)/K), the term (K−N)/K provides negative feedback: as N rises toward K, this fraction shrinks, reducing the growth rate. At N = K, growth rate = 0. If N > K, growth becomes negative (population declines). The key density-dependent factors providing this feedback include intraspecific competition, disease transmission, predator functional/numerical responses, and stress-induced reproductive suppression. Without negative feedback, populations would grow exponentially until a catastrophic crash.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes 'territoriality' as a population regulation mechanism?
    [ ] A density-independent factor that affects populations through natural disasters
    [ ] The tendency of all organisms in a population to cluster together in the same location
    [*] A behavioral mechanism where individuals defend exclusive areas, limiting the number of breeding individuals that a habitat can support and thereby regulating population density through competition for space
    [ ] The geographic range of a species across continents and biomes
    Explanation: Territoriality is a density-dependent behavioral regulation mechanism. As population density increases, territory defense becomes more costly (more intrusions, more fights), and more individuals fail to secure territories. Non-territorial 'floaters' typically cannot breed, creating a ceiling on reproductive output. Species defend territories using songs (songbirds), scent marking (wolves, cats), physical displays (anole lizards), or direct combat (elephant seals). Territory size often decreases as population increases, partially compensating for density until a minimum viable territory size is reached.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes 'Type I survivorship curve'?
    [ ] A linear curve showing equal probability of death at every age throughout life
    [*] A convex survivorship pattern where most individuals survive through young and middle age, with the majority of mortality concentrated in the elderly — characteristic of K-strategists like humans, elephants, and whales
    [ ] A flat line indicating that no individuals in the population ever die
    [ ] A concave curve showing massive mortality in early life with few survivors reaching adulthood
    Explanation: Type I species invest heavily in few offspring, providing parental care, protection, and nutrition that ensure high juvenile survival. On a semi-log plot, the curve stays high (flat) for most of the lifespan, then drops sharply near the maximum age as physiological senescence causes rapid mortality. This pattern evolved in stable environments where competitive ability (not reproductive rate) determines fitness. In modern humans, medical advances have made our Type I curve even more extreme, with >95% of individuals surviving past age 50 in developed countries.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes 'Type II survivorship curve'?
    [ ] A convex curve where nearly all individuals survive until old age as predicted by standard models
    [*] A roughly linear survivorship pattern (on a log scale) where individuals have approximately the same probability of dying at any age throughout their lifespan — seen in some birds, small mammals, and certain reptiles
    [ ] A concave curve where most individuals die in early life stages, particularly regarding many according to conventional analysis
    [ ] A curve that applies exclusively to plant species and not to animals when measured under controlled conditions
    Explanation: Type II survivorship reflects a constant mortality rate that doesn't change with age — a one-year-old has the same probability of dying this year as a ten-year-old. On a semi-log plot, this appears as a straight diagonal line. This pattern occurs when mortality is primarily caused by random, unpredictable factors (predation, accidents) that affect all age classes equally. Examples include American robins, gray squirrels, some coral reef fish, and hydra. Many real species show patterns intermediate between Types I, II, and III.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes 'Type III survivorship curve'?
    [*] A concave survivorship pattern where the vast majority of individuals die in early life stages, but the few survivors that reach adulthood enjoy relatively high survival rates — characteristic of r-strategists like oysters, sea turtles, and most fish
    [ ] A convex curve showing high survival rates throughout most of the lifespan, particularly regarding nest within the typical range of values
    [ ] A linear curve with equal mortality at all ages, particularly regarding early in most standard conditions
    [ ] A curve showing that all individuals die at exactly the same age, particularly regarding survivors under normal circumstances
    Explanation: Type III species produce enormous numbers of offspring with minimal investment per individual. The vast majority perish as eggs, larvae, or juveniles due to predation, starvation, disease, or environmental exposure. On a semi-log plot, the curve drops steeply early then levels off as survivors become large enough to escape most mortality sources. An oyster may release millions of eggs; a sea turtle lays hundreds per nest; an oak tree drops thousands of acorns — but only a tiny fraction survive to reproduce. This strategy works because the few survivors have high reproductive output, completing the cycle.

  Q21. The Canadian lynx and snowshoe hare populations have been tracked through Hudson's Bay Company fur-trapping records for nearly 200 years. The data show a remarkably consistent ~9-11 year oscillation: hare numbers peak first, followed by lynx numbers peaking 1-2 years later. When hare populations crash, lynx populations crash shortly after. What population ecology concepts does this BEST illustrate?
    [ ] A density-independent cycle driven by climate patterns that affect both species equally
    [ ] A mutualistic relationship where both populations benefit from each other's presence
    [*] A classic predator-prey cycle with time-lagged density-dependent feedback — lynx reproduction responds to hare abundance with a delay, creating out-of-phase oscillations described by the Lotka-Volterra model
    [ ] Competition between lynx and hares for the same food resources
    Explanation: The lynx-hare system is ecology's most famous predator-prey cycle. The mechanism: abundant hares support lynx reproduction → lynx population grows (lag) → heavy predation crashes hare population → lynx starve and decline → reduced predation allows hare recovery → cycle repeats. The 1-2 year lag corresponds to lynx gestation and maturation time. Modern research shows the cycle is partly intrinsic to hare population dynamics (hare-plant interactions also oscillate), but lynx predation amplifies and synchronizes the cycles across vast geographic areas. This system beautifully illustrates density-dependent regulation through predation.

  Q22. Sea turtles are classic Type III survivorship organisms. A single female loggerhead sea turtle may lay approximately 100 eggs per nest and nest 3-5 times per season. However, only about 1 in 1,000 hatchlings survives to adulthood (taking 20-30 years to mature). Meanwhile, elephants are Type I organisms — a female produces one calf every 4-5 years with a 22-month gestation. Which statement BEST compares these species' population regulation strategies?
    [*] Sea turtles are r-strategists that compensate for massive juvenile mortality with high fecundity, while elephants are K-strategists that maximize individual offspring survival through parental investment — both strategies are evolutionarily stable under different selective pressures
    [ ] Sea turtles have a higher carrying capacity than elephants because they produce more offspring as typically measured in practice
    [ ] Elephants are more successful than sea turtles because K-strategy is always superior, particularly regarding strategies under the given assumptions
    [ ] Both species use the same survivorship strategy but at different speeds, particularly regarding rely when measured under controlled conditions
    Explanation: This comparison illustrates the r/K selection continuum and its connection to survivorship curves. Sea turtles (r/Type III): produce hundreds of eggs, provide no parental care, and rely on sheer numbers — if even 2-3 per female reach adulthood, the population is maintained. Elephants (K/Type I): slow reproduction but intense investment per offspring (2-year nursing, herd protection, decades of learning). The key conservation implication: elephant populations recover slowly from declines (decades), while sea turtle populations can theoretically recover faster IF the bottleneck (juvenile mortality from human causes like fishing bycatch) is addressed.

  Q23. In John B. Calhoun's famous 1962 'Universe 25' experiment, mice were given unlimited food, water, and nesting material in an enclosed space. The population grew rapidly at first, then stalled far below the theoretical resource-based carrying capacity. Males became aggressive or withdrawn, females abandoned pups, and the colony eventually went extinct despite abundant resources. What does this experiment suggest about population regulation?
    [ ] Unlimited resources always lead to unlimited population growth without any social effects
    [ ] The mice died because of a density-independent disease introduced by researchers
    [*] Behavioral and social stress from overcrowding can act as a density-dependent limiting factor that regulates populations below the resource-based carrying capacity — social breakdown can occur even when physical resources are abundant
    [ ] Carrying capacity is always determined exclusively by food and water availability
    Explanation: Calhoun's 'behavioral sink' experiments demonstrated that social and behavioral factors can be powerful density-dependent regulators independent of resource availability. At high densities, chronic social stress elevated cortisol levels, suppressed reproduction, disrupted maternal behavior, and increased aggression. The population regulated itself through behavioral dysfunction rather than starvation. While direct application to human urban populations is debated, the experiments expanded our understanding of population regulation beyond simple resource limitation to include psychological and social carrying capacity.

  Q24. On Isle Royale in Lake Superior, wolves and moose have been studied since 1958 — the longest continuous predator-prey study in the world. In the 1980s, a canine parvovirus epidemic reduced the wolf population from 50 to 14. Moose subsequently surged to over 2,400 individuals and heavily overbrowsed the island's vegetation. What does this event demonstrate about population regulation?
    [ ] The virus improved the ecosystem by permanently removing wolves as predators, particularly regarding vegetation according to conventional analysis
    [*] A density-independent factor (disease) disrupted the density-dependent predator-prey regulation, releasing the prey population from top-down control and leading to overshoot and habitat degradation — demonstrating that multiple regulatory mechanisms interact
    [ ] Moose populations are always regulated by density-independent factors, not predation in the context of this analysis
    [ ] Wolf-moose interactions on Isle Royale show perfect logistic growth with no oscillations following conventional understanding
    Explanation: This case shows the interplay between density-dependent and density-independent regulation. Wolves normally provided top-down density-dependent control on moose, keeping them below K and preventing overgrazing. The parvovirus (density-independent for this context — it arrived from outside the system) removed this control. Without wolf predation, moose overshot carrying capacity, degrading vegetation and triggering a subsequent crash. The study demonstrates that ecosystems rely on multiple regulatory mechanisms, and disrupting one (predation) can cascade through the entire food web. Recent genetic rescue of the wolf population (2018-2019) is attempting to restore this balance.

  Q25. African wild dogs (Lycaon pictus) live in packs of 6-20 adults. In each pack, typically only the dominant alpha pair breeds, while subordinate adults help raise pups. If pack size drops below about 5 adults, hunting success declines dramatically and pup survival approaches zero — the pack cannot sustain itself. What population ecology concept does this minimum threshold illustrate?
    [ ] Pack size is a density-independent factor unrelated to population regulation
    [ ] The carrying capacity of the environment has increased to favor smaller packs
    [ ] Density-dependent competition — smaller packs have less competition for food and should thrive
    [*] The Allee effect — a phenomenon where individual fitness or population growth rate decreases at low population densities, because cooperative behaviors essential for survival (pack hunting, group defense) require a minimum group size
    Explanation: The Allee effect is the opposite of typical density-dependent regulation. Normally, lower density means less competition and higher per capita growth. But below a critical threshold, fitness actually decreases with decreasing density. For African wild dogs: smaller packs can't take down large prey, can't defend kills from hyenas, can't protect pups from predators, and can't leave enough adults at the den while others hunt. This creates an extinction vortex — once a pack drops below ~5 adults, it spirals toward local extinction. The Allee effect is critical for conservation of social species and for understanding minimum viable population sizes.

  Q26. An aquaculture scientist is comparing survival curves for two fish species raised in identical conditions. Species A shows 90% survival past month 1, then rapid mortality after month 18 (maximum lifespan ~24 months). Species B shows 99% mortality by month 1 among eggs and fry, but 95% annual survival for individuals that reach month 3 (some living 15+ years). Which survivorship types do Species A and B represent, and what reproductive strategies would you predict?
    [ ] Species A is Type III and Species B is Type I based on total lifespan length
    [ ] Survivorship type cannot be determined from mortality patterns alone
    [ ] Both species show Type II survivorship with constant mortality rates at all ages
    [*] Species A shows Type I survivorship (K-strategist: few offspring, parental care, late-life mortality), while Species B shows Type III survivorship (r-strategist: many offspring, no parental care, massive early mortality)
    Explanation: Species A (high early survival, late-life mortality) matches Type I — it likely produces few eggs, may guard nests or brood young, and reaches reproductive age quickly relative to its short lifespan. Species B (massive early mortality, high adult survival) matches Type III — it likely produces thousands of eggs with no care, relying on numbers. The aquaculture implication: Species B needs protection during early life stages (hatchery rearing) but is robust as adults; Species A needs less early intervention but has lower reproductive output, making it harder to scale up production.

  Q27. In Yellowstone National Park, elk populations grew dramatically after wolf extirpation in the 1920s. Elk overgrazed riparian vegetation, which increased stream erosion, reduced beaver habitat, and decreased biodiversity. After wolf reintroduction in 1995, elk numbers declined and shifted their behavior (avoiding valleys), allowing vegetation recovery. A wildlife manager argues this shows wolves are the SINGLE factor regulating elk. Is this claim fully supported?
    [*] Not fully — while wolves are a major density-dependent regulatory factor, elk populations are also regulated by other density-dependent factors (winter food scarcity, disease like brucellosis) and density-independent factors (severe winters, drought) acting simultaneously
    [ ] Not fully — elk are actually regulated only by density-independent factors like fire and drought in accordance with classical theory
    [ ] No — wolves have no effect on elk populations; winter weather alone controls elk numbers when applying fundamental principles
    [ ] Yes — wolves are the only factor that regulates elk populations in Yellowstone, particularly regarding declined when considering all relevant factors
    Explanation: Population regulation is almost always multi-factorial. Wolves provide important top-down density-dependent regulation (both through direct predation and the 'ecology of fear' that changes elk behavior). However, Yellowstone elk are also regulated by: bottom-up food limitation (winter range quality), other predators (bears, cougars), disease (chronic wasting disease, brucellosis), density-independent winter severity, and human hunting outside park boundaries. Research suggests wolf reintroduction was one of several concurrent factors in the elk decline. Understanding that regulation involves multiple interacting mechanisms — not a single silver bullet — is essential for ecosystem management.

  Q28. A pest management specialist notices that a ladybug (Coccinella septempunctata) population in an agricultural field surges after aphid numbers increase each spring, then declines in late summer when aphid populations crash. The next year, an early frost kills 70% of overwintering ladybugs before aphids emerge. How would this affect the predator-prey cycle?
    [ ] Aphid populations would also decline by 70% because predator and prey always decline equally
    [*] The density-independent frost disrupts the predator-prey cycle by reducing the predator population at a critical time, potentially allowing an aphid outbreak in spring due to reduced predation — the cycle would be delayed and phase-shifted until ladybug numbers recover
    [ ] The frost would have no effect because ladybugs and aphids operate on density-dependent cycles only
    [ ] The ladybug population would immediately recover because r-strategists are resistant to cold
    Explanation: This illustrates how density-independent events can disrupt density-dependent regulatory cycles. The normal cycle: aphids increase → ladybugs increase (lagged) → ladybug predation controls aphids → aphid crash → ladybug decline → cycle resets. The frost (density-independent) crashes ladybug numbers before the cycle begins, removing the density-dependent control on aphids. Without sufficient predation, aphids can explode, potentially causing crop damage. Integrated Pest Management (IPM) must account for these disruptions — farmers may need to release commercial ladybugs or apply targeted controls when winter conditions weaken natural predator-prey regulation.

  Q29. A conservation team is developing a recovery plan for the California condor, which was reduced to just 22 individuals in 1987. Condors are classic K-strategists: they don't breed until age 6-8, produce only one egg every other year, and have a 60-year lifespan. Despite a captive breeding program that has increased the population to ~500, the species remains critically dependent on human management. Why is the K-strategy life history a particular challenge for recovery?
    [ ] The K-strategy provides no disadvantage because captive breeding eliminates all natural limiting factors under equilibrium conditions
    [ ] K-strategists recover faster than r-strategists because they have higher individual survival rates based on established principles
    [*] K-strategists' low fecundity, late maturity, and long generation time mean that even with captive breeding, population growth is inherently slow — it takes decades to build a self-sustaining population, and any setback (lead poisoning, power line collisions) disproportionately impacts the small population
    [ ] K-strategists cannot be managed through captive breeding programs under any circumstances, particularly regarding setback as predicted by standard models
    Explanation: K-strategist traits make conservation recovery extremely challenging. At one egg every two years and 6-8 years to maturity, even a perfect breeding program produces population growth far slower than r-strategists could achieve. Each individual lost represents a proportionally greater setback. The small population is vulnerable to the extinction vortex: inbreeding depression, demographic stochasticity (random variation in births/deaths), and Allee effects. Contrast with Kemp's ridley sea turtles (r-strategist): protected nesting beaches and headstarting programs produced rapid population increases because each female produces hundreds of eggs per season. K-strategists require sustained, multi-decade commitment and zero tolerance for ongoing mortality threats.

  Q30. A rural deer population has no natural predators (wolves were extirpated a century ago). The population cycles through a pattern: (1) population grows for 3-4 years, (2) deer exceed the range's winter food capacity, (3) many deer starve during a harsh winter, (4) the weakened survivors are hit by an epizootic of hemorrhagic disease, (5) the population crashes to ~30% of its peak, (6) with fewer deer, vegetation recovers and growth resumes. This pattern repeats every 5-7 years. What types of population regulation are at work?
    [ ] Only density-dependent factors are at work because the cycle is predictable and repeating, particularly regarding interact following the established framework
    [*] Multiple types interact: the starvation phase is density-dependent (food competition intensifies at high density), the harsh winter is density-independent (weather affects all regardless of density), and the disease outbreak is density-dependent (transmission increases with crowding and as starvation weakens immune systems) — creating a compound crash more severe than any single factor alone
    [ ] No regulation is occurring — the population changes are entirely random, particularly regarding kills under the given assumptions
    [ ] Only density-independent factors are at work because the winter weather triggers the crash, particularly regarding extirpated when considering all relevant factors
    Explanation: This scenario illustrates how density-dependent and density-independent factors interact synergistically to produce population regulation. Without wolves, the primary density-dependent predator is absent, so the population grows past K until food limitation (density-dependent) weakens individuals. A severe winter (density-independent) then kills many, and hemorrhagic disease (density-dependent — transmission increases in crowded, immunocompromised herds) delivers the final blow. The compound crash is worse than any factor alone because they're additive. Wildlife managers often implement regulated hunting as a substitute for the missing predator-prey regulation, aiming to keep deer below K and prevent the destructive overshoot-crash cycle.

--- Lesson 4.5: Natural Selection and Adaptation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Natural Selection and Adaptation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is conservation biology?
Conservation biology emerged as a 'crisis discipline' in the 1980s (coined by Michael Soulé) in response to the accelerating biodiversity crisis. It is inherently interdisciplinary, combining population genetics (minimum viable population, inbreeding depression), ecology (habitat requirements, trophic cascades), biogeography (island biogeography theory, habitat fragmentation), and social sciences (environmental policy, community engagement). Unlike pure ecology, conservation biology is explicitly mission-driven — it aims to preserve biodiversity and ecosystem function.
What is sustainable development?
The concept was defined by the Brundtland Commission (1987) in 'Our Common Future.' Sustainable development rests on three pillars: environmental protection (maintaining ecosystem services and biodiversity), economic viability (ensuring development is economically feasible), and social equity (ensuring benefits are shared fairly). The 17 UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs, 2015) operationalize this concept, addressing poverty, clean water, climate action, and life on land and below water. The key challenge is decoupling economic growth from environmental degradation.
What is a protected area in conservation?
Protected areas are the cornerstone of in situ conservation. The IUCN recognizes categories from strict nature reserves (Category I, minimal human use) to protected landscapes with sustainable use (Category VI). As of 2023, approximately 17% of land and 8% of oceans are in some form of protected status. The '30x30' initiative (Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, 2022) aims to protect 30% of land and sea by 2030. Protected areas are most effective when they are large, connected by wildlife corridors, and managed with local community involvement.
What is restoration ecology?
Restoration ecology applies ecological principles to reverse environmental degradation. Techniques include removing invasive species, replanting native vegetation, reintroducing keystone species, restoring hydrology (dam removal, wetland reconstruction), and remediating contaminated soils. The UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration (2021-2030) highlights the urgency — restoring 350 million hectares of degraded land could generate $9 trillion in ecosystem services and sequester significant carbon. Successful examples include Yellowstone wolf reintroduction, Florida Everglades restoration, and prairie reconstruction in the American Midwest.
What is the leading cause of species extinction worldwide?
Habitat destruction is responsible for approximately 85% of species listed as threatened or endangered on the IUCN Red List. The major drivers include tropical deforestation (losing ~10 million hectares/year), conversion of grasslands and wetlands to agriculture, urbanization, and infrastructure development. Habitat fragmentation is particularly damaging because it creates isolated populations too small to be genetically viable, disrupts migration routes, and increases edge effects. The other major threats — in order — are overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, and climate change.
What is the difference between in situ and ex situ conservation?
In situ (Latin: 'in place') conservation is generally preferred because it preserves entire ecosystems, maintains natural selection, and protects ecological relationships. Examples: national parks, biosphere reserves, marine protected areas. Ex situ (Latin: 'out of place') conservation is a critical backup for species on the brink of extinction. Examples: San Diego Zoo's captive breeding of California condors, Svalbard Global Seed Vault (1 million+ seed samples), and frozen tissue repositories. The best strategies integrate both: breed in captivity (ex situ), then reintroduce to restored habitat (in situ).
What is a wildlife corridor and why is it important?
Wildlife corridors address one of the most harmful effects of habitat fragmentation: population isolation. When habitats become islands surrounded by development, populations become too small and inbred to be viable. Corridors allow genetic exchange, seasonal migration, range shifts in response to climate change, and recolonization after local extinctions. Examples include highway wildlife overpasses and underpasses (Banff, Canada), the Yellowstone-to-Yukon corridor (Y2Y), and riparian buffer strips along rivers. Island biogeography theory predicts that connected habitats support more species than isolated ones of equal area.
What is the significance of the U.S. Endangered Species Act (ESA) of 1973?
The ESA is one of the world's strongest environmental laws. It has prevented the extinction of 99% of listed species, with notable successes including the bald eagle (delisted 2007), American alligator (recovered), gray wolf (controversial recovery), and peregrine falcon (delisted 1999). The ESA has two main protections: Section 7 requires federal agencies to consult with USFWS/NMFS on projects that may affect listed species; Section 9 prohibits 'take' of listed species by any person. Critical habitat designation protects the physical/biological features essential for a species' conservation.
Real-World Applications
Example: The Florida Everglades — originally 4.3 million acres of 'River of Grass' — was reduced by 50% through drainage, agriculture, and urban development. The Comprehensive Everglades Restoration Plan (CERP, 2000) is the largest ecosystem restoration project in history, costing an estimated $23.2 billion over 35 years. The plan aims to restore natural water flow, remove invasive species, and rebuild wetland habitat. What branch of conservation science guides this project?
CERP applies restoration ecology principles at an unprecedented scale. Key interventions include: removing 240 miles of levees and canals to restore natural sheet flow; treating agricultural runoff to reduce phosphorus pollution; controlling invasive Burmese pythons and Brazilian pepper trees; reconstructing wetland and mangrove habitats; and monitoring indicator species (wading birds, Florida panther, American crocodile). The project uses adaptive management — continuously adjusting plans based on monitoring data. Challenges include competing water demands (agriculture, urban supply), climate change (sea level rise), and political obstacles. Success would restore ecosystem services worth billions: water purification, flood protection, fisheries, and tourism.
Example: The Atlantic cod fishery off Newfoundland, Canada, was one of the world's most productive fisheries for 500 years. By 1992, overfishing had collapsed the cod population to 1% of its historical size. Despite a total fishing moratorium declared that year, 30 years later the cod population has still not recovered. What does this case study teach about sustainable resource management?
The Newfoundland cod collapse is the textbook example of un-sustainable resource exploitation. Despite warnings from scientists, political and economic pressure kept harvests above maximum sustainable yield (MSY). When the cod collapsed, a 'regime shift' occurred — the ecosystem reorganized with shrimp and crab replacing cod, and cod's prey competitors expanded to fill the niche. Young cod now face predation by species that cod once controlled. This competitive and predatory reorganization creates hysteresis — the ecosystem doesn't simply reverse when the original pressure is removed. The collapse cost 40,000 jobs and destroyed Newfoundland's economy, demonstrating that unsustainable harvesting has severe social consequences.
Example: The Svalbard Global Seed Vault, located deep inside a mountain on a Norwegian Arctic island, stores over 1.2 million seed samples from gene banks around the world. It functions as a backup in case regional seed collections are lost to natural disasters, war, equipment failure, or funding cuts. In 2015, seeds were withdrawn for the first time when Syria's gene bank was damaged by civil war. What conservation concept does this facility represent?
The Svalbard Seed Vault is the ultimate insurance policy for crop biodiversity. It stores seeds at -18°C in a naturally cold, dry, geologically stable location 130 meters inside a mountain. The vault's design ensures preservation even if power fails. Genetic diversity in crops is critical because: modern agriculture relies on a dangerously narrow genetic base (75% of crop diversity was lost in the 20th century); climate change requires new drought/heat-resistant varieties; and new diseases demand resistant genes that may only exist in wild relatives or traditional cultivars. The Syria withdrawal proved the vault's concept — without it, decades of agricultural research would have been irreversibly lost.

    Explanation: This scenario embodies the central challenge of sustainable development: how to lift people out of poverty without destroying the ecosystems they depend on. REDD+ (part of the UNFCCC framework) creates a financial mechanism that makes standing forest economically competitive with plantation agriculture. The forest provides: carbon storage (worth the REDD+ payments), biodiversity preservation (irreplaceable endemic species), ecosystem services (water regulation, soil protection), and potential for sustainable forest products and ecotourism. Challenges include ensuring payments reach local communities (not just governments), preventing 'leakage' (deforestation shifting elsewhere), and maintaining funding long-term. The Brundtland principle guides the solution: development that meets current needs while maintaining options for future generations.
