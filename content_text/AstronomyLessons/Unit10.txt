--- Lesson 10.1: Review of Key Concepts (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Kepler's third law states that P² ∝ a³, meaning:
    [ ] Orbital period is constant
    [*] The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis
    [ ] Planets orbit in circles
    [ ] Speed is constant
    Explanation: Fundamental orbital relationship.

  Q2. The four terrestrial planets are:
    [ ] Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune
    [*] Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars
    [ ] Only Earth and Mars
    [ ] Mercury, Venus, Jupiter, Mars
    Explanation: Rocky, inner planets.

  Q3. Nuclear fusion in the Sun converts:
    [ ] Iron to gold
    [*] Hydrogen to helium (releasing energy via E = mc²)
    [ ] Helium to carbon
    [ ] Oxygen to nitrogen
    Explanation: Powers the Sun.

  Q4. The main sequence on the HR diagram represents stars that:
    [ ] Are dying
    [*] Are fusing hydrogen to helium in their cores (most of a star's lifetime)
    [ ] Have no fuel
    [ ] Are forming
    Explanation: Stable phase.

  Q5. Seasons on Earth are caused by:
    [ ] Distance from the Sun
    [*] Earth's 23.5° axial tilt causing varying angles of sunlight during the year
    [ ] Moon phases
    [ ] Solar activity
    Explanation: Tilt, not distance.

  Q6. Lunar eclipses occur when:
    [ ] The Moon is between Earth and Sun
    [*] Earth is between the Sun and Moon (Earth's shadow on the Moon)
    [ ] The Sun is between Earth and Moon
    [ ] Stars align
    Explanation: Full Moon alignment.

  Q7. Dark matter is evidenced by:
    [ ] Photographs
    [*] Galaxy rotation curves showing more gravitational pull than visible matter can account for
    [ ] Radio signals
    [ ] Temperature readings
    Explanation: Invisible mass.

  Q8. The habitable zone is the region where:
    [ ] All stars exist
    [*] Liquid water could exist on a planet's surface
    [ ] Gravity is zero
    [ ] There is no radiation
    Explanation: Goldilocks zone.

  Q9. Transit spectroscopy reveals:
    [ ] The number of galaxies
    [*] The chemical composition of an exoplanet's atmosphere during transit
    [ ] Star size
    [ ] Moon phases
    Explanation: JWST application.

  Q10. Hubble's Law (v = H₀d) is evidence for:
    [ ] Static universe
    [*] An expanding universe (more distant galaxies recede faster)
    [ ] Contracting universe
    [ ] Rotating universe
    Explanation: Cosmic expansion.

  Q11. The ISS orbits in:
    [ ] GEO
    [*] LEO (~400 km altitude)
    [ ] MEO
    [ ] Deep space
    Explanation: Low Earth Orbit.

  Q12. Adaptive optics corrects:
    [ ] Mirror size
    [*] Atmospheric turbulence in real time using deformable mirrors
    [ ] Star color
    [ ] Telescope weight
    Explanation: Sharper ground images.

  Q13. Extremophiles demonstrate that life can thrive:
    [ ] Only in normal conditions
    [*] In extreme heat, cold, radiation, acidity, salinity, and pressure
    [ ] Only on Earth's surface
    [ ] Only in water
    Explanation: Expanded habitability.

  Q14. The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation relates Δv to:
    [ ] Gravity only
    [*] Exhaust velocity and mass ratio (Δv = v_e × ln(m₀/m_f))
    [ ] Distance only
    [ ] Time only
    Explanation: Fundamental rocket science.

  Q15. Radio astronomy can observe through:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Dust and gas clouds that block visible light
    [ ] Only clear skies
    [ ] Only at night
    Explanation: Different wavelength advantage.

  Q16. The Event Horizon Telescope imaged:
    [ ] A star
    [*] The shadow of supermassive black holes (M87* and Sgr A*)
    [ ] A planet
    [ ] A nebula
    Explanation: VLBI achievement.

  Q17. LIGO detected gravitational waves from:
    [ ] Planets
    [*] Merging black holes (first detection September 14, 2015)
    [ ] Stars
    [ ] Galaxies colliding
    Explanation: Spacetime ripples.

  Q18. The Fermi Paradox asks:
    [ ] How old is Earth?
    [*] If intelligent life should be common, why don't we see evidence of it?
    [ ] How far is Mars?
    [ ] What causes tides?
    Explanation: Where is everybody?

  Q19. Wien's law relates a blackbody's peak wavelength to:
    [ ] Mass
    [*] Temperature (λ_max = b/T; hotter → shorter peak wavelength)
    [ ] Distance
    [ ] Size
    Explanation: Color-temperature link.

  Q20. This review connects themes across all units by emphasizing:
    [ ] Only memorization
    [*] How physics principles (gravity, EM radiation, thermodynamics) unify our understanding from planets to galaxies
    [ ] Only one topic
    [ ] Only math
    Explanation: Unified framework.

  Key Concepts: AP Practice: Multiple Choice
AP Practice: Multiple Choice
This lesson presents AP-style multiple-choice questions covering all major topics across the course. Focus on applying physics principles, interpreting data, and making quantitative calculations.

--- Lesson 10.2: AP Practice: Multiple Choice (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A planet at 4 AU from a star takes 8 years to orbit. What is the orbital period of a planet at 1 AU? (Kepler's 3rd law: P²∝a³)
    [ ] 8 years
    [ ] 4 years
    [ ] 2 years
    [*] 1 year (P²=a³ → P²=1³=1 → P=1)
    Explanation: P²/a³ is constant.

  Q2. A star's spectrum peaks at 500 nm. Using Wien's law (b = 2.9×10⁶ nm·K), its surface temperature is:
    [ ] 3,000 K
    [*] ~5,800 K (T = 2.9×10⁶/500 = 5,800 K)
    [ ] 10,000 K
    [ ] 50,000 K
    Explanation: Apply Wien's law.

  Q3. An object has a recession velocity of 14,000 km/s. Using H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc, its distance is:
    [ ] 100 Mpc
    [*] 200 Mpc (d = v/H₀ = 14,000/70 = 200 Mpc)
    [ ] 70 Mpc
    [ ] 1,000 Mpc
    Explanation: Hubble's Law.

  Q4. A star has a parallax of 0.05 arcseconds. Its distance is:
    [ ] 5 pc
    [*] 20 pc (d = 1/p = 1/0.05 = 20 parsecs)
    [ ] 50 pc
    [ ] 0.05 pc
    Explanation: d = 1/p.

  Q5. During a planetary transit, the star's brightness decreases by 0.01%. The ratio (R_planet/R_star)² equals:
    [ ] 0.01
    [*] 0.0001 (0.01% = 0.0001)
    [ ] 0.1
    [ ] 0.001
    Explanation: R_p/R_* = √0.0001 = 0.01 = 1/100.

  Q6. Which is NOT a terrestrial planet?
    [ ] Mercury
    [ ] Venus
    [ ] Earth
    [*] Neptune (it's a gas/ice giant)
    Explanation: Inner rocky vs. outer giant.

  Q7. The Sun generates energy primarily through:
    [ ] Fission
    [ ] Chemical burning
    [*] Proton-proton chain fusion (H → He)
    [ ] Gravitational contraction
    Explanation: Nuclear fusion.

  Q8. A star cooler than the Sun would appear on the HR diagram:
    [ ] Upper left
    [*] To the right of the Sun (cooler = redder = right)
    [ ] Below and left
    [ ] Same spot
    Explanation: Temperature axis.

  Q9. Which observation supports the Big Bang theory?
    [ ] Static galaxies
    [*] Cosmic Microwave Background radiation — remnant heat from the early universe
    [ ] Flat Earth
    [ ] No redshift
    Explanation: Predicted and observed.

  Q10. If the mass ratio m₀/m_f = e² (≈7.39) and v_e = 3 km/s, Δv =:
    [ ] 3 km/s
    [*] 6 km/s (Δv = 3 × ln(e²) = 3 × 2 = 6 km/s)
    [ ] 9 km/s
    [ ] 12 km/s
    Explanation: Rocket equation.

  Q11. The habitable zone around a cooler (M-dwarf) star is _____ than around a Sun-like star.
    [ ] Farther
    [*] Closer (less luminosity → need to be nearer for liquid water temperatures)
    [ ] The same
    [ ] Nonexistent
    Explanation: Luminosity-dependent.

  Q12. Spectral lines shifted toward red indicate:
    [ ] The object is approaching
    [*] The object is receding (redshift = moving away)
    [ ] The object is stationary
    [ ] The object is hot
    Explanation: Doppler effect.

  Q13. Gravitational waves are produced by:
    [ ] Light
    [*] Accelerating massive objects (e.g., merging black holes or neutron stars)
    [ ] Sound
    [ ] Heat
    Explanation: Einstein predicted them.

  Q14. The Drake Equation variable with the MOST uncertainty is:
    [ ] R* (star formation rate)
    [ ] f_p (fraction with planets)
    [*] L (civilization lifetime)
    [ ] n_e (habitable planets)
    Explanation: Sociological, not astronomical.

  Q15. An astronaut on the ISS experiences microgravity because:
    [ ] There's no gravity
    [*] They're in free fall (continuously falling around Earth)
    [ ] The ISS blocks gravity
    [ ] They're too far from Earth
    Explanation: Orbit = free fall.

  Q16. JWST observes primarily in _____ to see through dust and detect high-redshift objects.
    [ ] UV
    [ ] Visible
    [*] Infrared
    [ ] X-ray
    Explanation: IR penetrates dust.

  Q17. The Cepheid period-luminosity relation is used to measure:
    [ ] Temperature
    [*] Distance (longer period = more luminous → compare to apparent brightness)
    [ ] Mass
    [ ] Age
    Explanation: Cosmic distance ladder.

  Q18. Which detection method is most productive for finding exoplanets?
    [ ] Direct imaging
    [ ] Radial velocity
    [*] Transit method (Kepler/TESS discovered thousands)
    [ ] Microlensing
    Explanation: Most detections.

  Q19. Dark energy is invoked to explain:
    [ ] Galaxy rotation
    [*] The accelerating expansion of the universe
    [ ] Star formation
    [ ] Planet orbits
    Explanation: ~68% of the universe's energy.

  Q20. Effective AP preparation requires:
    [ ] Only memorization
    [*] Understanding underlying physics principles and their application to diverse astronomical contexts
    [ ] Only reading
    [ ] Only calculation
    Explanation: Conceptual and quantitative.

--- Lesson 10.3: AP Practice: Case Studies (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Practice: Case Studies
AP Practice: Case Studies
AP-style questions based on real scenarios requiring multi-step reasoning and data interpretation.
Approach  • Identify relevant physics principles for each scenario.  • Extract data from the problem description.  • Show clear reasoning and justify conclusions.

--- Lesson 10.3: AP Practice: Case Studies (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A transit light curve shows a dip every 3.5 days with depth 0.02. The planet's radius relative to the star is:
    [ ] 0.02
    [*] ~0.14 (√0.02 ≈ 0.141)
    [ ] 0.2
    [ ] 0.5
    Explanation: R_p/R_* = √(depth).

  Q2. The same planet's star has a measured radial velocity wobble of 50 m/s. This data constrains the planet's:
    [ ] Color
    [*] Minimum mass (radial velocity amplitude depends on planet mass and orbital inclination)
    [ ] Temperature
    [ ] Age
    Explanation: Ma with sin(i) uncertainty.

  Q3. A galaxy's spectrum shows Hα at 672 nm instead of 656.3 nm. Its recession velocity is approximately:
    [ ] 100 km/s
    [*] ~7,170 km/s (Δλ/λ = v/c → v = c × 15.7/656.3 ≈ 7,170 km/s)
    [ ] 50,000 km/s
    [ ] 300,000 km/s
    Explanation: Doppler formula.

  Q4. Using H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc, this galaxy's distance is:
    [ ] 50 Mpc
    [*] ~102 Mpc (d = 7,170/70 ≈ 102 Mpc)
    [ ] 500 Mpc
    [ ] 10 Mpc
    Explanation: Hubble's Law application.

  Q5. A Cepheid variable in a distant galaxy has a period of 30 days. Using the period-luminosity relation, this constrains:
    [ ] Its temperature
    [*] Its absolute luminosity (and therefore distance when compared to apparent brightness)
    [ ] Its color
    [ ] Its mass
    Explanation: Standard candle.

  Q6. Mars's thin atmosphere (1% of Earth's) makes landed missions challenging because:
    [ ] Landing is easier
    [*] Less atmospheric braking available — spacecraft must use heat shields, parachutes, AND retrorockets
    [ ] It's too thick
    [ ] Wind is strong
    Explanation: Mars EDL problem.

  Q7. An astronomer finds methane AND oxygen in an exoplanet's atmosphere. This is notable because:
    [ ] Both are common gases
    [*] They should react and destroy each other — their coexistence suggests a replenishing source (possibly biological)
    [ ] Neither is important
    [ ] Only O₂ matters
    Explanation: Chemical disequilibrium.

  Q8. A binary system shows both spectral lines shifting periodically. Measuring both shifts allows determination of:
    [ ] Color only
    [*] The mass ratio of the two stars (and total mass with Kepler's third law)
    [ ] Distance only
    [ ] Age only
    Explanation: Double-lined spectroscopic binary.

  Q9. Europa's induced magnetic field suggests:
    [ ] A rocky surface
    [*] A subsurface layer of conducting fluid (salty ocean) beneath the ice shell
    [ ] No ocean
    [ ] Strong surface magnets
    Explanation: Galileo mission evidence.

  Q10. Enceladus ejects material at its south pole. A flythrough mission could directly sample for:
    [ ] Rock types
    [*] Organic molecules, salts, and potential biosignatures from the subsurface ocean
    [ ] Magnetic fields only
    [ ] Temperature only
    Explanation: Direct ocean sampling.

  Q11. A star on the HR diagram at high luminosity and low temperature is classified as:
    [ ] Main sequence
    [ ] White dwarf
    [*] Red giant or supergiant
    [ ] Neutron star
    Explanation: Upper-right region.

  Q12. If a spacecraft needs 9.4 km/s to reach LEO and v_e = 3 km/s, the mass ratio m₀/m_f must be at least:
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 10
    [*] ~23 (e^(9.4/3) = e^3.13 ≈ 22.9)
    [ ] 100
    Explanation: Rocket equation.

  Q13. Adaptive optics on ground telescopes achieve near-Hubble image quality by:
    [ ] Using bigger mirrors only
    [*] Rapidly deforming a mirror to counteract atmospheric turbulence measured 1000× per second
    [ ] Pointing differently
    [ ] Using radio waves
    Explanation: Real-time correction.

  Q14. The TRAPPIST-1 system is ideal for atmospheric study because:
    [ ] The star is hot
    [*] The star is small and cool — planet transits cause relatively large, detectable dips
    [ ] The planets are far apart
    [ ] It's near the Sun
    Explanation: Small star amplifies signal.

  Q15. A neutron star merger (GW170817) was detected in both gravitational waves and light. This 'multi-messenger' approach revealed:
    [ ] Nothing new
    [*] The creation of heavy elements, the nature of the merger, and confirmed that gravitational waves travel at light speed
    [ ] Only waves
    [ ] Only light
    Explanation: Multiple information channels.

  Q16. The CMB's tiny temperature fluctuations (~0.00001 K) map:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Density variations in the early universe that seeded the formation of galaxies and large-scale structure
    [ ] Only noise
    [ ] Only errors
    Explanation: Seeds of structure.

  Q17. Dark energy comprises ~68% of the universe's energy. Its primary evidence comes from:
    [ ] Galaxy rotation
    [*] Type Ia supernovae appearing dimmer than expected — the expansion is accelerating
    [ ] Star formation rates
    [ ] Planet orbits
    Explanation: Accelerating expansion.

  Q18. A comet's two tails (ion + dust) always point:
    [ ] Toward the Sun
    [*] Generally away from the Sun (ion tail: solar wind; dust tail: slightly curved by gravity)
    [ ] Toward Jupiter
    [ ] Randomly
    Explanation: Solar wind and radiation pressure.

  Q19. The Great Red Spot on Jupiter is:
    [ ] A mountain
    [*] A massive, persistent anticyclonic storm (larger than Earth)
    [ ] A crater
    [ ] An ocean
    Explanation: Atmospheric feature.

  Q20. Analyzing case studies demonstrates that astronomy requires:
    [ ] Only one skill
    [*] Integration of physics, chemistry, data analysis, and critical thinking across topics
    [ ] Only memorization
    [ ] Only observation
    Explanation: Interdisciplinary science.

--- Lesson 10.4: Real-World Applications of Astronomy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Real-World Applications of Astronomy
Real-World Applications of AstronomyTechnology Spin-offs
GPS (relativity corrections), satellite communications, weather forecasting, medical imaging (from X-ray telescope detectors), CCDs (digital cameras), WiFi (radio astronomy signal processing).
Planetary Defense
Near-Earth object (NEO) detection and deflection: DART mission successfully altered asteroid Dimorphos's orbit (2022).
Climate Science
Understanding solar variability, Earth's energy balance, and planetary atmospheres informs climate modeling.

--- Lesson 10.5: Capstone: Connecting Astronomy to Other Sciences (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Capstone: Connecting Astronomy to Other Sciences
Capstone: Connecting Astronomy to Other SciencesPhysics
Gravity (Newton/Einstein), electromagnetic radiation, nuclear physics, thermodynamics, relativity, quantum mechanics (spectral lines).
Chemistry
Stellar nucleosynthesis (origin of elements), spectroscopy, molecular clouds, astrochemistry, planetary atmospheres.
Biology
Astrobiology, extremophiles, evolution of life on Earth (astronomy-influenced events like mass extinctions from impacts).
Earth Science
Plate tectonics (radioactive decay), climate (solar variability), magnetic field (solar wind protection), tides.

--- Lesson 10.5: Capstone: Connecting Astronomy to Other Sciences (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Every element heavier than hydrogen and helium was created in:
    [ ] The Big Bang
    [ ] A laboratory
    [*] Stars (fusion) and stellar explosions/mergers (nucleosynthesis)
    [ ] Earth's core

  Q2. Astronomy relies on physics principles including:
    [ ] Only gravity
    [*] Gravity, electromagnetism, nuclear physics, thermodynamics, and relativity

    [ ] Only mechanics
    Explanation: Foundation.

  Q3. Spectral lines are explained by:
