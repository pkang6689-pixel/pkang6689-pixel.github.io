--- Lesson 1.1: History of Astronomy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: History of Astronomy
History of AstronomyAncient Astronomy  • Babylonians: Recorded star positions, predicted eclipses.  • Greeks: Aristotle (geocentric model, Earth is a sphere), Aristarchus (heliocentric idea), Eratosthenes (measured Earth's circumference).  • Ptolemy: Geocentric model with epicycles — dominant for 1,400 years.The Scientific Revolution  • Copernicus: Heliocentric model (1543). Galileo: Telescopic observations (moons of Jupiter, Venus phases).  • Tycho Brahe: Precise observations. Kepler: Laws of planetary motion (elliptical orbits).  • Newton: Universal gravitation — unified celestial and terrestrial mechanics.

--- Lesson 1.2: Tools of Astronomy (Telescopes, Satellites) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Tools of Astronomy (Telescopes, Satellites)
Tools of AstronomyTelescopes  • Refracting: Uses lenses; limited by chromatic aberration and lens size.  • Reflecting: Uses mirrors (parabolic); larger apertures possible. Newton invented the first.  • Radio telescopes: Detect radio waves; large dish antennas; can operate day/night, through clouds.Space-Based Instruments  • Hubble Space Telescope: Optical/UV/IR, above atmosphere → sharp images.  • James Webb Space Telescope (JWST): Infrared, 6.5 m mirror, L2 orbit → early universe studies.  • Satellites & probes: Chandra (X-ray), Spitzer (IR), Voyager (interstellar), New Horizons (Pluto).

--- Lesson 1.3: Scales of the Universe (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scales of the Universe
Scales of the UniverseDistance Measures  • AU: Astronomical Unit = average Earth-Sun distance ≈ 150 million km.  • Light-year: Distance light travels in one year ≈ 9.46 × 10¹² km.  • Parsec: Based on parallax; 1 pc ≈ 3.26 light-years.Cosmic Scale  • Solar system: ~0.001 ly across (to Oort cloud ~1 ly).  • Milky Way: ~100,000 ly diameter, ~200-400 billion stars.  • Observable universe: ~93 billion ly diameter, ~2 trillion galaxies.

--- Lesson 1.4: Scientific Method in Astronomy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scientific Method in Astronomy
Scientific Method in Astronomy
Astronomy uses the same scientific method as other sciences but adapts to unique constraints: objects can't be brought to the lab, experiments are largely observational, and timescales span billions of years.
Steps Applied to Astronomy  • Observation: Telescopes, satellites, ground stations gather data.  • Hypothesis: Testable explanations (e.g., 'dark matter causes rotation curves').  • Prediction & Testing: Models predict observations (e.g., CMB); computer simulations.  • Peer review & publication: Community verifies results.

--- Lesson 1.5: Observational Techniques (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Observational Techniques
Observational TechniquesPhotometry
Measuring the brightness (flux) of celestial objects. Apparent magnitude vs. absolute magnitude.
Spectroscopy
Splitting light into a spectrum to identify composition (absorption/emission lines), temperature, and radial velocity (Doppler shift).
Astrometry
Precise measurement of positions and motions of stars. Used for parallax distances and detecting exoplanets.
Imaging
CCD cameras, filters (UBVRI), and image processing. Multi-wavelength imaging reveals different structures.

--- Lesson 1.6: Case Studies in Ancient Astronomy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Ancient Astronomy
Case Studies in Ancient AstronomyStonehenge
Neolithic monument in England aligned with summer/winter solstice sunrise/sunset. Likely used as a solar/lunar calendar.
Ancient Egypt
Aligned pyramids with cardinal directions; used the heliacal rising of Sirius to predict Nile floods and define a 365-day calendar.
Maya Civilization
Sophisticated calendar systems; accurately predicted eclipses and tracked Venus. The Dresden Codex contains Venus tables.
Ancient China & India
Detailed eclipse records, star catalogs, and early concepts of cosmic cycles.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Measurement & Units (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Measurement & Units
AP Prep: Measurement & UnitsKey Units  • Distance: AU, light-year, parsec, km, m.  • Mass: kg, solar masses (M☉ = 1.989 × 10³⁰ kg).  • Temperature: Kelvin (absolute); K = °C + 273.15.  • Luminosity: Watts; solar luminosity L☉ = 3.828 × 10²⁶ W.Key Conversions & Formulas  • d = 1/p (parsec, where p is parallax in arcseconds).  • m − M = 5 log₁₀(d/10) (distance modulus).  • Wien's law: λ_max = 2.898 × 10⁶ / T (nm, K).  • Stefan-Boltzmann: L = 4πR²σT⁴.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Measurement & Units (Quiz) ---

  Q1. 1 AU is approximately:
    [ ] 1 light-year
    [*] 150 million km
    [ ] 1 parsec
    [ ] 10 km
    Explanation: Earth-Sun distance.

  Q2. 1 parsec is approximately:
    [ ] 1 light-year
    [*] 3.26 light-years
