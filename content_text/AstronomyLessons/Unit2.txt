--- Lesson 2.1: Earth's Rotation & Revolution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Earth's Rotation & Revolution
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the approximate tilt of Earth's axis?
Earth's axis is tilted about 23.5° relative to its orbital plane, which causes the seasons.
What is the shape of Earth's orbit around the Sun?
Earth's orbit is nearly circular with an eccentricity of about 0.017, making it only slightly elliptical.
What is 'rotation' in the context of Earth's motion?
Rotation refers to Earth spinning on its own axis, which takes about 24 hours and produces day and night.
What is 'revolution' in astronomy?
Revolution is the movement of one celestial body in an orbit around another, such as Earth orbiting the Sun.
What is a 'sidereal day'?
A sidereal day is measured by Earth's rotation relative to distant stars, about 23 hours 56 minutes.
What is the 'ecliptic'?
The ecliptic is the apparent path the Sun traces across the sky throughout the year, corresponding to Earth's orbital plane.
What is 'precession'?
Precession is the slow, conical wobble of Earth's axis over a ~26,000-year cycle, changing which star is the 'North Star.'
What is the 'Coriolis effect'?
The Coriolis effect is an apparent deflection of moving objects caused by Earth's rotation, influencing weather patterns and ocean currents.
What is the 'celestial equator'?
The celestial equator is the imaginary great circle on the celestial sphere directly above Earth's equator.
What is an 'analemma'?
An analemma is the figure-8 shape traced by the Sun's position in the sky when observed at the same time and place throughout the year.
A photographer takes a picture of the Sun at the same time every day for a year and notices a figure-8 pattern. What is this called?
The figure-8 pattern formed by the Sun's position at the same time each day over a year is called an analemma.

--- Lesson 2.2: Seasons & Tilt of Axis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Seasons & Tilt of Axis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the term for the longest day of the year in a given hemisphere?
The summer solstice marks the longest day and shortest night of the year for that hemisphere.
What is a 'solstice'?
A solstice occurs when Earth's axial tilt is most inclined toward or away from the Sun, producing the longest or shortest day.
What is an 'equinox'?
An equinox occurs when the Sun is directly above the equator, and day and night are roughly equal worldwide.
What is the 'Tropic of Cancer'?
The Tropic of Cancer at 23.5°N marks the northernmost latitude where the Sun can be directly overhead.
What is the 'Tropic of Capricorn'?
The Tropic of Capricorn at 23.5°S marks the southernmost latitude where the Sun can be directly overhead.
What is the 'Arctic Circle'?
The Arctic Circle (~66.5°N) marks the latitude above which 24-hour daylight (midnight sun) occurs at the June solstice.
What is 'obliquity'?
Obliquity is the technical term for the tilt of a planet's rotational axis relative to its orbital plane.
What is 'photoperiod'?
Photoperiod is the length of daylight during a day, which changes with seasons and affects plant and animal behavior.
What is the 'midnight sun'?
The midnight sun occurs at high latitudes when the Sun does not set for extended periods around the summer solstice.
A researcher at the North Pole experiences 24 hours of darkness on December 21. What is this phenomenon called?
Polar night occurs when the Sun stays below the horizon for 24 hours, happening at polar latitudes during their winter solstice.
A student argues that Earth is closer to the Sun in summer, causing warmer weather. What is wrong with this reasoning?
Earth's perihelion occurs in January. Seasons are caused by axial tilt, not distance from the Sun.

--- Lesson 2.3: Lunar Phases (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Lunar Phases
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a waxing crescent?
Waxing crescent is the phase just after new moon when a small, growing sliver of the Moon is visible.
What is a 'synodic month'?
A synodic month measures the interval between the same phases (e.g., new moon to new moon), about 29.5 days.
What is a 'gibbous' phase?
Gibbous (from Latin for 'hump') describes the Moon when more than half its face is illuminated.
What is 'earthshine'?
Earthshine is the faint glow on the Moon's unlit side, caused by sunlight bouncing off Earth and then reflecting back.
What is a 'lunar cycle'?
The lunar cycle (or lunation) is the full sequence of phases, taking about 29.5 days.
What is 'libration'?
Libration is the apparent rocking of the Moon that lets us see about 59% of its surface over time.
What is a 'sidereal month'?
A sidereal month measures the Moon's orbit against the background stars, about 27.3 days.
What is the 'terminator' on the Moon?
The lunar terminator is the dividing line between the sunlit and shadowed parts of the Moon's surface.
What is 'tidal locking'?
Tidal locking means the Moon rotates once per orbit, so the same face always points toward Earth.

--- Lesson 2.4: Solar & Lunar Eclipses (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solar & Lunar Eclipses
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'umbra'?
The umbra is the innermost, darkest region of a shadow where the light source is fully obscured.
What is the 'penumbra'?
The penumbra is the lighter, outer region of a shadow where the light source is only partially obscured.
What is an 'annular eclipse'?
An annular eclipse occurs when the Moon is too far to completely cover the Sun, leaving a bright ring (annulus) visible.
What is the 'corona' in the context of eclipses?
The corona is the Sun's hot, tenuous outer atmosphere, normally invisible but spectacularly visible during total solar eclipses.
What is a 'Saros cycle'?
The Saros cycle (~18 years 11 days) predicts when nearly identical eclipses will repeat.
What is 'totality'?
Totality is the brief period during an eclipse when the Sun (solar) or Moon (lunar) is fully within the shadow.
What is the 'path of totality'?
The path of totality is the narrow band (typically 100-160 km wide) across Earth where the Moon's umbra sweeps during a total solar eclipse.
What is the 'diamond ring effect'?
The diamond ring effect occurs when a single bead of sunlight shines through a lunar valley while the corona forms a bright ring.

--- Lesson 2.5: Tides & Gravitational Effects (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Tides & Gravitational Effects
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'spring tide'?
Spring tides (from 'springen,' to leap) produce the greatest tidal range, occurring at new and full moons.
What is a 'neap tide'?
Neap tides have the smallest difference between high and low water, occurring when Sun and Moon are at right angles.
What is 'tidal range'?
Tidal range is the vertical difference between the high water mark and the low water mark.
What is a 'tidal bulge'?
Tidal bulges are the raised areas of ocean water on the near and far sides of Earth relative to the Moon.
What is 'tidal friction'?
Tidal friction converts Earth's rotational energy into heat, gradually slowing Earth's spin.
What is a 'tidal bore'?
A tidal bore is a wave front that rushes up certain rivers and estuaries with the incoming tide.
What is the 'Roche limit'?
The Roche limit is the distance within which a celestial body held together only by gravity would be pulled apart by tidal forces.
What is 'tidal locking' in this context?
Tidal locking occurs when tidal forces synchronize a body's rotation with its orbital period, as with the Moon always showing one face to Earth.
What is 'tidal resonance'?
Tidal resonance occurs in bays like the Bay of Fundy where the basin's natural oscillation period enhances tidal amplitude.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Tidal force varies inversely with the cube of distance (1/r³), so doubling distance would reduce tidal force by a factor of 8.
Jupiter's massive gravity, combined with orbital resonances with other moons, flexes Io's interior, generating extreme heat and volcanism.
Real-World Applications
Example: Two fishing villages are on the same coastline but 200 km apart. One has a 10-meter tidal range while the other has only 2 meters. What explains this?
Coastal features like narrow bays, underwater topography, and resonance effects dramatically influence local tidal range.

--- Lesson 2.7: AP Prep: Orbital Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Orbital Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If a planet is 4 AU from its star, and we use Kepler's Third Law (T² = a³ in appropriate units), what is T²?
T² = a³ = 4³ = 64, so the orbital period squared is 64 (in years² for AU).
What is the gravitational force equation?
Newton's law of gravitation: F = GMm/r², where G is the gravitational constant, M and m are masses, and r is the separation distance.
What is the 'semi-major axis'?
The semi-major axis (a) is half the longest dimension of an ellipse, used to characterize orbital size.
What is 'orbital period'?
The orbital period (T) is the time it takes for a body to complete one full revolution around another body.
What is 'orbital velocity'?
Orbital velocity is the speed needed to maintain a stable orbit at a given distance from the central body.
What is 'escape velocity'?
Escape velocity is the minimum speed at which an object must travel to overcome a body's gravity permanently.
What is 'gravitational potential energy'?
Gravitational potential energy U = -GMm/r; it is negative because work must be done against gravity to separate the objects.
What is a 'geostationary orbit'?
A geostationary orbit at ~35,786 km altitude has a 24-hour period, keeping the satellite stationary relative to the ground.
What is 'centripetal force' in orbital mechanics?
Centripetal force (F = mv²/r) keeps orbiting objects curved inward; gravity provides this force in orbits.
What is an 'astronomical unit' (AU)?
1 AU ≈ 149.6 million km, defined as the mean Earth-Sun distance and used to measure distances within the solar system.
What is 'orbital eccentricity'?
Eccentricity (e) ranges from 0 (circle) to just under 1 (highly elongated ellipse); Earth's is ~0.017.
What is 'vis-viva equation'?
The vis-viva equation v² = GM(2/r - 1/a) gives the speed of an object at any point in its orbit.
A planet orbits a star at 2 AU. Using Kepler's Third Law (T² = a³ in solar units), what is the orbital period?
T² = a³ = 2³ = 8, so T = √8 ≈ 2.83 years.
If a new planet is discovered with an orbital period of 8 years around a Sun-like star, what is its distance from the star?
T² = a³ → 8² = 64 = a³ → a = ∛64 = 4 AU.
A comet has a very elongated orbit with a semi-major axis of 100 AU. What is its orbital period?
T² = a³ = 100³ = 1,000,000, so T = √1,000,000 = 1000 years.
