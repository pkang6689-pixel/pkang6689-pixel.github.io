--- Lesson 6.1: Types of Galaxies (Spiral, Elliptical, Irregular) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Galaxies (Spiral, Elliptical, Irregular)
Types of GalaxiesHubble's Classification (Tuning Fork)  • Spiral (S, SB): Flat disk with spiral arms, central bulge, active star formation. Barred spirals (SB) have a central bar. Milky Way is SBbc.  • Elliptical (E0–E7): Smooth, featureless, ellipsoidal. Older (red) stars; little gas/dust; minimal star formation. Range from nearly spherical (E0) to highly elongated (E7).  • Irregular (Irr): No defined shape. Often rich in gas and active star formation. Examples: Large & Small Magellanic Clouds.

--- Lesson 6.2: Milky Way Structure (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Milky Way Structure
Milky Way Structure
Our galaxy is a barred spiral ~100,000 light-years in diameter with ~200–400 billion stars.
Components  • Disk: Spiral arms with young stars, gas, dust. Sun is ~26,000 ly from center in the Orion Arm.  • Bulge: Central region with older yellow/red stars and Sgr A* (supermassive black hole, ~4 million M☉).  • Halo: Spherical region with old stars, globular clusters, and dark matter.  • Dark matter halo: Extends far beyond visible stars; ~85% of total mass.

--- Lesson 6.3: Galaxy Clusters (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Galaxy Clusters
Galaxy Clusters & Large-Scale Structure
Galaxy groups: ~50 galaxies (Local Group). Galaxy clusters: 100s–1,000s of galaxies bound by gravity (e.g., Virgo Cluster, Coma Cluster). Superclusters: clusters of clusters.
Large-Scale Structure
The universe has a cosmic web of filaments, walls, and voids. Galaxy clusters sit at intersections of filaments. Voids are vast, nearly empty regions.

--- Lesson 6.4: Dark Matter & Dark Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Dark Matter & Dark Energy
Dark Matter & Dark EnergyDark Matter (~27% of universe)
Does not emit/absorb light. Evidence: galaxy rotation curves (flat instead of Keplerian decline), gravitational lensing, galaxy cluster dynamics, CMB anisotropies. Candidates: WIMPs, axions.
Dark Energy (~68% of universe)
Mysterious force accelerating the universe's expansion. Discovered 1998 via Type Ia supernovae (dimmer than expected at high redshift). Possible explanation: cosmological constant (Λ).

--- Lesson 6.5: Expansion of the Universe (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Expansion of the Universe
Expansion of the UniverseHubble's Discovery (1929)
Edwin Hubble showed that galaxies are receding from us, with velocity proportional to distance: v = H₀ × d (Hubble's law). Current H₀ ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc (debated 'Hubble tension').
Big Bang
Extrapolating expansion backward → the universe began ~13.8 billion years ago from a hot, dense state. Key evidence: CMB (Cosmic Microwave Background, ~2.7 K), Big Bang nucleosynthesis (predicted H/He ratios), and Hubble expansion.

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Cosmology
Case Studies in CosmologyThe Hubble Deep Fields

--- Lesson 6.7: AP Prep: Hubble's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Hubble's Law
AP Prep: Hubble's LawCalculations  • v = H₀ × d: v in km/s, d in Mpc, H₀ ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc.  • Distance: d = v/H₀. If v = 2,100 km/s → d = 2100/70 = 30 Mpc.  • Redshift: z = Δλ/λ₀ = v/c (for v ≪ c). Higher z = greater distance = looking farther back in time.  • Age estimate: t ≈ 1/H₀ ≈ 14 billion years (rough upper bound).

--- Lesson 6.7: AP Prep: Hubble's Law (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Using Hubble's law, a galaxy receding at 7,000 km/s is at a distance of:

    [ ] 70 Mpc
    [*] 100 Mpc (d = 7000/70)

    Explanation: d = v/H₀.
