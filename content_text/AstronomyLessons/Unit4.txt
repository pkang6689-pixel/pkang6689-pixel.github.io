--- Lesson 4.1: Structure of the Sun (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Structure of the Sun
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the innermost layer of the Sun called?
The core is the Sun's central region where nuclear fusion occurs.
What is the Sun primarily composed of?
The Sun is about 73% hydrogen and 25% helium by mass.
What is the photosphere?
The photosphere is the layer of the Sun that emits visible light and appears as the solar 'surface.'
What is the radiative zone?
In the radiative zone, energy slowly moves outward as photons are repeatedly absorbed and re-emitted.
What is the convective zone?
In the convective zone, hot plasma rises to the surface and cooler plasma sinks, carrying energy outward.
What is the corona?
The corona is the Sun's outermost atmosphere with temperatures exceeding 1 million K.
What is the chromosphere?
The chromosphere is a thin, reddish layer above the photosphere, visible during eclipses.
What is granulation?
Granulation refers to the cell-like pattern on the photosphere caused by convection below the surface.
What is the tachocline?
The tachocline is the transition region where the Sun's rotation changes from uniform to differential.
What is differential rotation?
Because the Sun is gaseous, its equator rotates faster (~25 days) than its poles (~35 days).
A scientist measures the Sun's surface temperature at 5,800 K but finds the corona exceeds 1 million K. What is this mystery called?
The coronal heating problem is one of solar physics' biggest puzzles — why the corona is hundreds of times hotter than the surface.

--- Lesson 4.2: Nuclear Fusion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nuclear Fusion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary nuclear reaction occurring in the Sun's core?
The Sun primarily fuses hydrogen into helium via the proton-proton (pp) chain.
What is the net product of the proton-proton chain?
Four hydrogen nuclei fuse to produce one helium-4 nucleus, plus energy and neutrinos.
What is nuclear fusion?
Nuclear fusion joins lighter nuclei (like hydrogen) into heavier nuclei (like helium), releasing enormous energy.
What is the proton-proton chain?
The proton-proton chain is the dominant fusion process in the Sun, converting four protons into one helium-4 nucleus.
What is hydrostatic equilibrium?
Hydrostatic equilibrium keeps the Sun stable: gravity pulls inward while radiation and gas pressure push outward.
What is a positron?
A positron (e⁺) is produced during fusion; it immediately annihilates with an electron, releasing energy.
What is the mass defect?
The mass defect is the 'missing' mass converted into energy during fusion, as described by E=mc².
What is the CNO cycle?
The CNO cycle uses C, N, and O as catalysts; they are not consumed but facilitate hydrogen-to-helium fusion.
What is luminosity?
Luminosity is the intrinsic total power output of a star, measured in watts or solar luminosities.
What is quantum tunneling?
Quantum tunneling allows protons to fuse at temperatures lower than classical physics would predict by 'tunneling' through the Coulomb barrier.
What is a neutrino?
Neutrinos are elusive particles produced in fusion reactions that rarely interact with matter.
What is binding energy?
Binding energy per nucleon determines whether fusion or fission releases energy; iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon.
A star 10 times the Sun's mass fuses hydrogen much faster. What is the main consequence?
More massive stars burn through their fuel much faster due to higher core temperatures and pressures, living millions (not billions) of years.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Even at 4 million tonnes/second over billions of years, the total mass lost is only a tiny fraction of the Sun's enormous mass.

--- Lesson 4.3: Solar Activity (Sunspots, Flares, Prominences) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solar Activity (Sunspots, Flares, Prominences)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a solar flare?
Solar flares are sudden releases of magnetic energy that produce intense radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum.
What is a solar prominence?
Prominences are large, arching structures of plasma held above the Sun's surface by magnetic fields.
What is a coronal mass ejection (CME)?
CMEs are large expulsions of plasma and magnetic field from the Sun's corona into interplanetary space.
What is the solar cycle?
The solar cycle is the periodic variation in the Sun's activity (sunspots, flares, CMEs) with an ~11 year period.
What is solar maximum?
Solar maximum features peak sunspot numbers, frequent flares, and elevated CME activity.
What is solar minimum?
Solar minimum is the quiet phase with few sunspots and reduced solar activity.
What is a coronal hole?
Coronal holes have open magnetic field lines allowing fast solar wind to escape, appearing dark in X-ray images.
What is the Carrington Event?
The Carrington Event was one of the most powerful geomagnetic storms ever recorded, caused by a massive CME.
What is space weather?
Space weather encompasses solar wind, CMEs, flares, and their effects on Earth's magnetosphere, satellites, and communications.
Real-World Applications
Example: A satellite's electronics suddenly malfunction during a solar flare. What type of radiation likely caused the damage?
Solar flares emit X-rays and energetic particles that can damage satellite electronics and disrupt operations.

--- Lesson 4.4: Solar Wind & Magnetic Fields (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solar Wind & Magnetic Fields
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the solar wind?
The solar wind is a continuous flow of charged particles (mostly protons and electrons) from the Sun's corona.
What is the typical speed of the slow solar wind?
The slow solar wind travels at roughly 400 km/s, while the fast solar wind can reach 600-800 km/s.
What is the boundary of the heliosphere called?
The heliopause is where the solar wind's outward pressure is balanced by interstellar medium pressure.
What is the magnetosphere?
Earth's magnetosphere is the magnetic 'shield' that deflects solar wind and protects the planet.
What is the heliosphere?
The heliosphere extends beyond the orbits of all planets, enclosing the entire solar system in solar wind.
What is the heliopause?
The heliopause marks where the Sun's influence ends and interstellar space begins.
What is the Parker spiral?
Named after Eugene Parker, this spiral pattern results from the Sun's rotation winding the magnetic field lines.
What is a geomagnetic storm?
Geomagnetic storms occur when CMEs or fast solar wind streams disturb Earth's magnetosphere.
What is the bow shock?
The bow shock is analogous to the wave in front of a boat — it's where supersonic solar wind abruptly slows.
What is the magnetopause?
The magnetopause is the outer edge of the magnetosphere where the magnetic field balances solar wind pressure.
What is the magnetotail?
Solar wind pressure compresses the magnetosphere on the dayside and stretches it into a long tail on the nightside.
What is the solar dynamo?
The solar dynamo converts kinetic energy of plasma motion into magnetic field energy through electromagnetic induction.
During a solar particle event, astronauts on a Moon mission need to protect themselves. What is the best approach?
Without Earth's magnetosphere, lunar astronauts must rely on physical shielding to protect against energetic solar particles.
Real-World Applications
Example: Voyager 1 detected a sharp increase in cosmic rays and a decrease in solar particles. What boundary did it cross?
Increased cosmic rays and decreased solar particles indicated Voyager 1 had left the heliosphere and entered interstellar space.

What is Wien's displacement law?

What is flux?
Flux (F = L/4πr²) measures how much energy from a star reaches each square meter at a given distance.
What is the mass defect?

What is the Kelvin temperature scale?
Kelvin is the SI unit of temperature, essential in astrophysics because many laws use absolute temperature.
What is energy conservation?
In stellar physics, energy conservation means fusion energy equals the sum of radiation, neutrino energy, and kinetic energy.
What is a joule?
