--- Lesson 4.1: Structure of the Sun (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Structure of the Sun
Structure of the SunInterior  • Core: ~15 million K; nuclear fusion occurs here. ~25% of solar radius.  • Radiative zone: Energy moves outward via photon absorption/re-emission; takes ~170,000 years.  • Convective zone: Energy carried by rising hot plasma and sinking cool plasma (convection cells).Atmosphere  • Photosphere: Visible surface, ~5,800 K. Granulation visible.  • Chromosphere: Pinkish layer above photosphere; visible during eclipses.  • Corona: Outermost atmosphere; extremely hot (~1–3 million K); visible during total solar eclipses.

--- Lesson 4.2: Nuclear Fusion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nuclear Fusion
Nuclear Fusion in the SunThe Proton-Proton Chain
Dominant fusion process in the Sun. Net reaction: 4 ¹H → ¹ ⁴He + 2 positrons + 2 neutrinos + energy (26.7 MeV).
Key Physics  • Mass deficit (Δm) converted to energy via E = mc².  • ~0.7% of hydrogen mass converted to energy per reaction.  • Sun converts ~600 million tons of H into He every second.  • Solar neutrinos pass through matter nearly unimpeded → neutrino detectors confirm fusion.

--- Lesson 4.3: Solar Activity (Sunspots, Flares, Prominences) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solar Activity (Sunspots, Flares, Prominences)
Solar ActivitySunspots
Dark regions on the photosphere (~3,500–4,500 K vs ~5,800 K); caused by concentrated magnetic fields inhibiting convection. Follow an ~11-year solar cycle.
Solar Flares
Sudden eruptions of energy from the Sun's surface; release radiation across the EM spectrum. Can disrupt communications and GPS.
Prominences & CMEs
Prominences: arcs of plasma along magnetic field lines. Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs): massive expulsions of plasma and magnetic field; can trigger geomagnetic storms on Earth.

--- Lesson 4.4: Solar Wind & Magnetic Fields (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solar Wind & Magnetic Fields
Solar Wind & Magnetic FieldsSolar Wind
Continuous stream of charged particles (protons, electrons) flowing from the corona at ~400–800 km/s. Carves out the heliosphere — the Sun's region of influence — extending to ~120 AU.
Solar Magnetic Field
Generated by the solar dynamo (differential rotation + convection). Creates complex field lines that trap plasma, drive sunspots, flares, and CMEs. The interplanetary magnetic field (IMF) spirals outward (Parker spiral).

--- Lesson 4.5: Impact on Earth's Climate & Technology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Impact on Earth's Climate & Technology
Impact on Earth's Climate & TechnologyClimate Connections
Solar irradiance varies ~0.1% over the solar cycle — small but measurable. Longer-term variations (e.g., Maunder Minimum) may influence climate trends. However, current climate change is primarily driven by human greenhouse gas emissions.
Technology Impacts  • Geomagnetic storms → power grid surges (Québec 1989 blackout).  • Satellite drag in expanded upper atmosphere; GPS/communication disruptions.  • Radiation exposure for astronauts and high-altitude pilots.

Case Studies in Solar ObservationSDO (Solar Dynamics Observatory)

--- Lesson 4.7: AP Prep: Energy Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Energy Calculations
AP Prep: Energy CalculationsKey Equations  • E = mc²: Energy equivalent of mass. For solar fusion: Δm × c² = energy released per reaction.  • Luminosity: L = 4πR²σT⁴ (Stefan-Boltzmann law for stellar luminosity).  • Flux: F = L/(4πd²) — brightness decreases with distance squared.  • Wien's law: λ_max = 2.898 × 10⁶ / T (nm); peak wavelength of emission for a blackbody.

--- Lesson 4.7: AP Prep: Energy Calculations (Quiz) ---

  Q1. E = mc² means that 1 kg of mass is equivalent to:
    [ ] 9 × 10⁸ J
    [*] 9 × 10¹⁶ J
    [ ] 1 J
    [ ] 9 × 10²⁴ J
    Explanation: E = 1 × (3×10⁸)² = 9×10¹⁶ J.

  Q2. In the Sun's p-p chain, the mass deficit per reaction is ~0.048 × 10⁻²⁷ kg. The energy released is approximately:

    [ ] 1 kJ

  Q3. The Stefan-Boltzmann law states L = 4πR²σT⁴. If temperature doubles, luminosity changes by a factor of:

    [ ] 8
    [*] 16 (2⁴)

  Q4. If a star's radius doubles (same T), its luminosity changes by a factor of:

    [*] 4 (area quadruples)
    [ ] 16
    [ ] 8

  Q5. The inverse-square law for flux means that at twice the distance, you observe:
