--- Lesson 3.1: Formation of the Solar System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Formation of the Solar System
Formation of the Solar System
Nebular Hypothesis: ~4.6 billion years ago, a giant molecular cloud (solar nebula) collapsed — possibly triggered by a nearby supernova. Conservation of angular momentum caused the collapsing cloud to spin and flatten into a protoplanetary disk.
Steps  • Core heats → proto-Sun ignites hydrogen fusion.  • Dust grains collide and stick → planetesimals → protoplanets (accretion).  • Inner disk: too hot for volatiles → rocky terrestrial planets.  • Outer disk: frost line → ices + gas accumulate → gas/ice giants.  • Solar wind clears remaining gas; late heavy bombardment shapes surfaces.

--- Lesson 3.2: Terrestrial Planets (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Terrestrial Planets
Terrestrial Planets
Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars — the four inner, rocky planets.
Characteristics  • Mercury: Smallest; no atmosphere; extreme temperature swings (−180°C to 430°C); heavily cratered.  • Venus: Dense CO₂ atmosphere; runaway greenhouse → ~465°C surface; retrograde rotation; similar size to Earth.  • Earth: Liquid water; magnetic field; plate tectonics; supports life.  • Mars: Thin CO₂ atmosphere; Olympus Mons (tallest volcano); Valles Marineris; evidence of past water.

--- Lesson 3.3: Gas Giants (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gas Giants
Gas Giants (Jovian Planets)Jupiter
Largest planet; mostly H/He; Great Red Spot (persistent storm); strong magnetosphere; 95+ known moons (Io, Europa, Ganymede, Callisto are Galilean moons).
Saturn
Famous ring system (mostly ice particles); density Uranus & Neptune (Ice Giants)  • Uranus: Extreme axial tilt (~98°, rolls on its side); blue-green from methane; 27 known moons.  • Neptune: Strong winds (2,100 km/h); deep blue; Triton (large retrograde moon). Both have interiors rich in water, ammonia, methane ices.

--- Lesson 3.4: Dwarf Planets & Asteroids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Dwarf Planets & Asteroids
Dwarf Planets & AsteroidsDwarf Planets
Orbit the Sun, have enough mass for hydrostatic equilibrium (round shape), but have NOT cleared their orbital neighborhood. Examples: Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, Ceres.
Asteroids
Rocky/metallic remnants from the early solar system. Most are in the asteroid belt (between Mars and Jupiter). Types: C-type (carbonaceous), S-type (silicaceous), M-type (metallic).

Near-Earth Asteroids (NEAs) are monitored for potential impact hazards. Missions: NEAR Shoemaker, Hayabusa2, OSIRIS-REx, DART.

--- Lesson 3.5: Comets & Meteoroids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Comets & Meteoroids
Comets & MeteoroidsComets
Icy bodies ('dirty snowballs') from the Kuiper Belt or Oort Cloud. As they approach the Sun, ices sublimate → coma and tails (ion tail points away from Sun; dust tail curves).
Meteors & Meteorites  • Meteoroid: Small rocky/metallic debris in space.  • Meteor: Streak of light when a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere ('shooting star').  • Meteorite: Remnant that reaches the ground.  • Meteor showers occur when Earth passes through a comet's debris trail (e.g., Perseids from Comet Swift-Tuttle).

--- Lesson 3.6: Planetary Atmospheres (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Planetary Atmospheres
Planetary AtmospheresKey Factors  • Gravity: Determines escape velocity; larger planets retain lighter gases (H, He).  • Distance from Sun: Affects temperature → molecular speeds → ability to retain gases.  • Composition: Earth: N₂ 78%, O₂ 21%. Venus: CO₂ 96.5%. Mars: CO₂ 95% but very thin. Jupiter: H₂/He.Atmospheric Effects
Greenhouse effect, weather systems, protection from radiation, spectroscopic detection of exoplanet atmospheres (transit spectroscopy).

--- Lesson 3.8: AP Prep: Kepler's Laws (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Kepler's Laws
AP Prep: Kepler's LawsKepler's Three Laws of Planetary Motion  • 1st Law (Ellipses): Planets orbit the Sun in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.  • 2nd Law (Equal Areas): A line from Sun to planet sweeps equal areas in equal times → planets move faster at perihelion.  • 3rd Law (Harmonics): T² = a³ (years, AU). Newton's form: T² = (4π²/GM)a³.Problem-Solving
Use Kepler's 3rd to find period from semi-major axis and vice versa. Derive central mass from orbital data.

--- Lesson 3.8: AP Prep: Kepler's Laws (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Kepler's first law states that planetary orbits are:
    [ ] Circular
    [ ] Parabolic
    [*] Elliptical with the Sun at one focus
    [ ] Hyperbolic
    Explanation: Ellipses, not circles.

  Q2. Kepler's second law (equal areas) implies that a planet moves:
    [ ] At constant speed
    [ ] Faster at aphelion
    [*] Faster at perihelion (closest to Sun) and slower at aphelion
    [ ] Only in straight lines
    Explanation: Conservation of angular momentum.

  Q3. Using Kepler's 3rd law (T² = a³), a planet at 9 AU has a period of:
    [ ] 9 years
    [ ] 81 years
    [*] 27 years (T² = 729, T = 27)
    [ ] 3 years
    Explanation: T² = 9³ = 729; T = √729 = 27.

  Q4. If T = 8 years, what is the semi-major axis?
    [ ] 8 AU
    [ ] 2 AU
    [*] 4 AU (a³ = T² = 64; a = 4)
    [ ] 64 AU
    Explanation: a³ = 64; a = ∛64 = 4.

  Q5. The eccentricity of a perfect circle is:
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 0.5
    [*] 0
    [ ] Infinity
    Explanation: No elongation.

  Q6. Earth's orbital eccentricity (~0.017) means its orbit is:
    [ ] Highly elliptical
    [*] Nearly circular
    [ ] A parabola
    [ ] A hyperbola
    Explanation: Almost circular.

  Q7. A comet with a very elongated orbit has an eccentricity close to:
    [ ] 0
    [*] 1 (but less than 1 for a bound orbit)
    [ ] 0.5
    [ ] −1
    Explanation: Highly eccentric.
