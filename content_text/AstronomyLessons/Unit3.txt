--- Lesson 3.1: Formation of the Solar System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Formation of the Solar System
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'solar nebula'?
The solar nebula was the primordial cloud that collapsed under gravity to form our solar system ~4.6 billion years ago.
What is 'accretion'?
Accretion is how dust grains → pebbles → boulders → planetesimals → planets gradually built up through collisions.
What is a 'protoplanetary disk'?
A protoplanetary disk is the flattened disk of material orbiting a newborn star, where planets eventually coalesce.
What is a 'planetesimal'?
Planetesimals are intermediate-sized objects that formed early and merged to create planets.
What is the 'frost line'?
The frost line (~3 AU from the Sun) marks where temperatures were cool enough for ices to form in the solar nebula.
What is 'differentiation' in planetary science?
Differentiation occurs when a planet is hot enough for dense materials to sink to the center and lighter materials to rise, forming layers.
What is a 'protoplanet'?
Protoplanets are large embryonic planets — bigger than planetesimals but not yet full-sized planets.
What is 'angular momentum'?
Angular momentum (L = mvr for orbits) is conserved, which is why the solar nebula spun faster as it contracted.
All planets orbit the Sun in the same direction and roughly the same plane. A student asks why. What is the explanation?
The uniform direction and near-planar orbits reflect the original rotation and flattened shape of the protoplanetary disk.

--- Lesson 3.1: Formation of the Solar System (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The solar system formed from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust called a:
    [*] Solar nebula
    [ ] Red giant
    [ ] Black hole
    [ ] White dwarf
    Explanation: The solar nebula hypothesis states our solar system formed from a rotating cloud of gas and dust about 4.6 billion years ago.

  Q2. Approximately how old is our solar system?
    [ ] 100 million years
    [ ] 1 billion years
    [*] 4.6 billion years
    [ ] 13.8 billion years
    Explanation: Radiometric dating of meteorites gives a solar system age of approximately 4.6 billion years.

  Q3. As the solar nebula collapsed, it began to spin faster due to:
    [ ] Magnetic fields
    [ ] Solar wind
    [*] Conservation of angular momentum
    [ ] Dark energy
    Explanation: As the cloud contracted, conservation of angular momentum caused it to spin faster, like a figure skater pulling in her arms.

  Q4. The flat disk shape of the solar nebula explains why:
    [ ] Comets have tails
    [ ] The Sun is round
    [ ] Stars twinkle
    [*] Planets orbit roughly in the same plane
    Explanation: The collapsing nebula flattened into a protoplanetary disk, and planets formed within this disk, so they orbit in nearly the same plane.

  Q5. Which process built up planets from small particles colliding and sticking together?
    [*] Accretion
    [ ] Evaporation
    [ ] Fission
    [ ] Radiation
    Explanation: Accretion is the gradual growth of bodies as particles collide and stick, eventually forming planetesimals and planets.

  Q6. The 'frost line' in the solar nebula is the distance beyond which:
    [ ] The Sun's light could not reach
    [ ] Metals could melt
    [ ] Planets could not form
    [*] Water and other volatiles could condense into ice
    Explanation: Beyond the frost line (~3 AU), temperatures were low enough for water, ammonia, and methane to freeze, enabling gas giant formation.

  Q7. Rocky (terrestrial) planets formed closer to the Sun because:
    [ ] The Sun attracted rocks more than gas
    [ ] They were pushed inward by gravity
    [*] Only metals and rocks could condense at the high temperatures near the Sun
    [ ] Gas giants formed first and pushed them in
    Explanation: Near the Sun, temperatures were too high for volatile ices to condense, so only refractory materials (rock, metal) could accumulate.

  Q8. Gas giants formed farther from the Sun because:
    [*] Icy materials could condense beyond the frost line, creating massive cores that captured gas
    [ ] The Sun pushed gas outward
    [ ] They formed from the Sun's atmosphere
    [ ] They migrated from near the Sun outward
    Explanation: Beyond the frost line, large icy/rocky cores formed quickly and were massive enough to gravitationally capture hydrogen and helium gas.

  Q9. The early Sun went through a phase of strong solar wind called the:
    [ ] Main sequence phase
    [ ] Red giant phase
    [ ] Supernova phase
    [*] T Tauri phase
    Explanation: The T Tauri phase was a period of intense solar wind that swept away remaining gas from the inner solar system.

  Q10. Planetesimals are best described as:
    [ ] Large gaseous objects that collapsed under gravity but never reached fusion temperatures
    [ ] Fragments of solar material ejected outward during the earliest phase of stellar contraction
    [ ] Mature differentiated worlds with established cores, mantles, and stable atmospheric envelopes
    [*] Small rocky or icy bodies (1-10 km) that were building blocks of planets
    Explanation: Planetesimals were kilometer-sized objects that accumulated through accretion to form protoplanets and eventually planets.

  Q11. What is a 'solar nebula'?
    [*] The rotating cloud of gas and dust from which the Sun and solar system formed
    [ ] A luminous ball of plasma undergoing hydrogen fusion in its core, emitting light and heat
    [ ] An interstellar cloud of ionized gas currently drifting through the Sun's neighborhood
    [ ] The outermost gaseous layers surrounding the Sun, including its chromosphere and corona
    Explanation: The solar nebula was the primordial cloud that collapsed under gravity to form our solar system ~4.6 billion years ago.

  Q12. What is 'accretion'?
    [ ] The catastrophic disruption of a massive star at the end of its life, dispersing heavy elements
    [ ] The phase transition of liquid water into vapor, driven by solar radiation heating a surface
    [ ] The gradual solidification of molten rock as it loses thermal energy to its surroundings
    [*] The process of growth by gravitational attraction and collision of smaller bodies
    Explanation: Accretion is how dust grains → pebbles → boulders → planetesimals → planets gradually built up through collisions.

  Q13. What is a 'protoplanetary disk'?
    [ ] A flattened planetary body that formed under unusual rotational forces in the early nebula
    [*] A rotating disk of gas and dust around a young star from which planets form
    [ ] A band of icy particles and rocky debris orbiting within Saturn's gravitational Roche limit
    [ ] A massive collection of gas and dark matter collapsing to form billions of individual stars
    Explanation: A protoplanetary disk is the flattened disk of material orbiting a newborn star, where planets eventually coalesce.

  Q14. What is a 'planetesimal'?
    [ ] A low-mass stellar object that sustains hydrogen fusion at extremely low luminosity for trillions of years
    [ ] A nearly spherical body orbiting the Sun that has not gravitationally cleared its orbital neighborhood
    [ ] A rocky fragment orbiting in the inner solar system whose path occasionally crosses Earth's orbital path
    [*] A small body (1-10 km) formed by accretion in the early solar system that is a building block of planets
    Explanation: Planetesimals are intermediate-sized objects that formed early and merged to create planets.

  Q15. What is the 'frost line'?
    [ ] The outermost boundary of the classical Kuiper Belt where Pluto completes its eccentric orbit
    [ ] The altitude or latitude on Earth's surface at which water vapor condenses and freezes overnight
    [ ] The location in the outer solar system where background radiation produces the lowest measured temperature
    [*] The distance from the Sun beyond which volatile compounds like water can condense into ice
    Explanation: The frost line (~3 AU from the Sun) marks where temperatures were cool enough for ices to form in the solar nebula.

  Q16. What are 'refractory materials'?
    [ ] Frozen and liquid forms of volatile compounds such as water, methane, and ammonia in the nebula
    [ ] Substances with low boiling points that readily transition from solid or liquid into gaseous form
    [*] Materials like rock and metal that can withstand high temperatures without vaporizing
    [ ] The lightest elements in the periodic table, making up the bulk of stellar and nebular composition
    Explanation: Refractory materials (silicates, iron) have high melting points and could condense close to the Sun.

  Q17. What are 'volatiles'?
    [ ] Unstable isotopes that undergo nuclear decay over time, releasing particles and energy into surroundings
    [ ] Dense mineral aggregates formed from silicates and metals that remain solid at high nebular temperatures
    [ ] Dense metallic elements like iron and nickel that condensed early in the inner regions of the solar nebula
    [*] Substances like water, methane, and ammonia that easily vaporize at low temperatures
    Explanation: Volatiles are compounds with low boiling points that could only condense as solids far from the Sun in the cool outer nebula.

  Q18. What is 'differentiation' in planetary science?
    [ ] The difference between planets and stars
    [*] The separation of a planet's interior into layers (core, mantle, crust) based on density
    [ ] Erosion of a planet's surface
    [ ] The splitting of a planet into two
    Explanation: Differentiation occurs when a planet is hot enough for dense materials to sink to the center and lighter materials to rise, forming layers.

  Q19. What is a 'protoplanet'?
    [ ] A natural satellite coalescing from debris in orbit around a newly forming planet in the disk
    [*] A large body formed from planetesimals that is on its way to becoming a full planet
    [ ] A rocky or metallic fragment left over from the early solar system, typically found in the main belt
    [ ] A substellar object that accumulated gas but never reached the mass needed to ignite hydrogen fusion
    Explanation: Protoplanets are large embryonic planets — bigger than planetesimals but not yet full-sized planets.

  Q20. What is 'angular momentum'?
    [ ] The total kinetic and potential energy contained within an atom's electron cloud and nuclear binding forces
    [ ] The rate at which electromagnetic radiation changes direction when passing through a curved gravitational field
    [ ] The inclination of a planet's orbital plane measured relative to the reference plane of the solar system
    [*] The rotational equivalent of linear momentum — the tendency of a spinning object to keep spinning
    Explanation: Angular momentum (L = mvr for orbits) is conserved, which is why the solar nebula spun faster as it contracted.

  Q21. A young star system is observed with a protoplanetary disk. Inside the frost line, mainly rocky chunks are accreting. Beyond it, icy bodies are growing rapidly. What does this match?
    [ ] A protoplanetary environment where volatile-rich material dominates at all distances from the central star
    [*] Our solar system's formation model — terrestrial planets inside the frost line, gas giants outside
    [ ] A debris disk around a young star where gravitational instabilities failed to produce any planetary bodies
    [ ] A collapsing molecular cloud core that fragments into two separate protostars orbiting a common center
    Explanation: This mirrors the solar nebula model: rocky accretion inside the frost line, icy+gas accretion outside.

  Q22. A meteorite is radiometrically dated to 4.56 billion years. What does this tell us?
    [ ] It's younger than Earth
    [ ] Radiometric dating doesn't work on meteorites
    [ ] It came from another star system
    [*] It formed at the very beginning of the solar system and helps date its age
    Explanation: The oldest meteorites (chondrites) date to ~4.56 billion years, establishing the solar system's age.

  Q23. All planets orbit the Sun in the same direction and roughly the same plane. A student asks why. What is the explanation?
    [ ] Gravity pulls everything into a plane over time
    [ ] It's a coincidence
    [ ] The Sun's magnetic field aligns them
    [*] They all formed from the same rotating protoplanetary disk
    Explanation: The uniform direction and near-planar orbits reflect the original rotation and flattened shape of the protoplanetary disk.

  Q24. Jupiter is mostly hydrogen and helium. Why couldn't Earth accumulate these gases?
    [*] Earth's core was too small and too close to the Sun — it couldn't attract and retain light gases in the hot inner nebula
    [ ] Earth's global magnetic field generates a magnetosphere that deflects incoming charged particles and prevents gas accumulation
    [ ] Primordial hydrogen was absent from the inner solar system because it was consumed entirely during solar ignition
    [ ] Earth's accretion began only after the solar wind and radiation had completely dispersed all nebular hydrogen and helium
    Explanation: In the hot inner nebula, light gases didn't condense, and small rocky protoplanets lacked the gravity to capture them.

  Q25. A scientist finds that a meteorite contains tiny mineral grains older than the solar system (pre-solar grains). What does this suggest?
    [*] Some material in our solar system predates it, originating from earlier generations of stars
    [ ] The solar system is older than we think
    [ ] Minerals cannot survive that long
    [ ] The meteorite is fake
    Explanation: Pre-solar grains from older stars were incorporated into the solar nebula, surviving the formation process.

  Q26. In the solar nebula, most of the mass concentrated at the center to form the Sun. How much of the total mass is in the Sun?
    [ ] About 75%
    [ ] About 50%
    [ ] About 90%
    [*] About 99.8%
    Explanation: The Sun contains approximately 99.8% of the solar system's total mass.

  Q27. A young protoplanet experiences heavy bombardment from remaining planetesimals. What effects would this produce?
    [*] Heating of the surface, delivery of volatiles, and craters — contributing to differentiation
    [ ] Complete stripping of the protoplanet's gaseous envelope by intense solar radiation and stellar wind
    [ ] Rapid loss of internal heat through radiation into space, solidifying the surface and halting volcanism
    [ ] Instantaneous condensation of water vapor into stable liquid oceans covering the entire planetary surface
    Explanation: Impacts released kinetic energy as heat, helping melt the interior (enabling differentiation) and delivering water and other volatiles.

  Q28. Why does the solar system have an asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter instead of a planet?
    [ ] That area had no material
    [*] Jupiter's strong gravity prevented material from accreting into a single planet
    [ ] A planet exploded there
    [ ] The Sun's light prevented formation there
    Explanation: Jupiter's gravitational influence stirred up planetesimals in that region, increasing collision velocities and preventing accretion into a planet.

  Q29. The Nice model proposes that the gas giants migrated from their original positions. What evidence supports this?
    [ ] Radar observations confirmed permanently shadowed craters at Mercury's poles contain deposits of water ice
    [ ] Helioseismology measurements show the Sun's differential rotation rate varies significantly with latitude
    [ ] Observations confirm that Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune share nearly identical radii and masses
    [*] The Late Heavy Bombardment and the current distribution of small bodies in the outer solar system
    Explanation: The Nice model explains the Late Heavy Bombardment (~3.9 Ga) and the orbital structure of Kuiper Belt objects through giant planet migration.

  Q30. If the solar nebula had been much colder throughout, how might the solar system be different?
    [ ] Refractory silicates and metals would dominate accretion at every distance, producing exclusively terrestrial worlds
    [ ] A cooler nebula would collapse more slowly under gravity, allowing the central protostar to accumulate more mass
    [ ] Lower temperatures would reduce particle collision energies below the threshold needed for accretion to begin
    [*] Ices could condense closer to the Sun, potentially allowing gas giants to form nearer in
    Explanation: A cooler nebula would move the frost line inward, allowing icy materials to accumulate closer to the Sun, favoring gas giant formation there.

--- Lesson 3.2: Terrestrial Planets (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Terrestrial Planets
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'magnetosphere'?
A magnetosphere is the magnetic 'bubble' around a planet that shields it from charged particles in the solar wind.
What is the 'greenhouse effect'?
The greenhouse effect occurs when CO₂, water vapor, and other gases absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, warming the surface.
What is 'volcanism'?
Volcanism releases internal heat and materials, shaping planetary surfaces with lava flows, mountains, and craters.
What is 'plate tectonics'?
Plate tectonics is Earth's unique (among planets) process of crustal plates moving, colliding, and separating.
What is a 'runaway greenhouse effect'?
Venus likely experienced a runaway greenhouse effect where evaporating water increased heating, which evaporated more water, in a vicious cycle.
What is 'regolith'?
Regolith covers the Moon, Mars, and other bodies — it's fragmented surface material created by impacts and weathering.
What is 'albedo'?
Albedo ranges from 0 (absorbs all light) to 1 (reflects all light). Venus has a high albedo (~0.7) due to its thick clouds.
What is 'outgassing'?
Outgassing from volcanic activity built up the early atmospheres of terrestrial planets.
What is a 'shield volcano'?
Shield volcanoes have gentle slopes formed by fluid lava spreading widely — Olympus Mons is the largest example.
Venus and Earth are similar in size and mass, yet Venus's surface is ~460°C while Earth's averages ~15°C. What is the primary reason?
Venus's thick CO₂ atmosphere traps enormous amounts of heat. While Venus is closer to the Sun, the greenhouse effect is the dominant factor.
Real-World Applications
Example: Venus and Earth are similar in size and mass, yet Venus's surface is ~460°C while Earth's averages ~15°C. What is the primary reason?
Venus's thick CO₂ atmosphere traps enormous amounts of heat. While Venus is closer to the Sun, the greenhouse effect is the dominant factor.

--- Lesson 3.2: Terrestrial Planets (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which planets are classified as terrestrial?
    [ ] Mercury, Pluto, Earth, Venus
    [ ] Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune
    [ ] Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn
    [*] Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars
    Explanation: The four terrestrial (rocky) planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — small, dense worlds with solid surfaces.

  Q2. Terrestrial planets are primarily composed of:
    [ ] Nitrogen and methane
    [ ] Hydrogen and helium
    [*] Rock and metal
    [ ] Water and ice
    Explanation: Terrestrial planets are made mainly of silicate rocks and metals like iron, giving them high densities.

  Q3. Which terrestrial planet is the largest?
    [ ] Mercury
    [ ] Mars
    [*] Earth
    [ ] Venus
    Explanation: Earth is the largest terrestrial planet, with a diameter of about 12,742 km.

  Q4. Which terrestrial planet has the thickest atmosphere?
    [ ] Earth
    [ ] Mercury
    [*] Venus
    [ ] Mars
    Explanation: Venus has an extremely thick CO₂ atmosphere with surface pressure about 92 times Earth's.

  Q5. Mercury is notable for having:
    [ ] Liquid water on its surface
    [ ] The thickest atmosphere of all terrestrial planets
    [*] Virtually no atmosphere and extreme temperature variations
    [ ] Active volcanoes today
    Explanation: Mercury has almost no atmosphere, so temperatures swing from ~430°C (day) to -180°C (night).

  Q6. Mars appears red because:
    [ ] Its atmosphere filters out blue light
    [ ] Volcanic lava covers it
    [*] Iron oxide (rust) on its surface
    [ ] It reflects the Sun's red light
    Explanation: Mars's reddish color comes from iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) in its soil and dust.

  Q7. Which terrestrial planet has a strong magnetic field?
    [ ] Mars
    [*] Earth
    [ ] Mercury
    [ ] Venus
    Explanation: Earth's rotating liquid iron outer core generates a strong magnetic field that protects the atmosphere from solar wind.

  Q8. Venus rotates in the opposite direction from most planets. This is called:
    [ ] Synchronous rotation
    [*] Retrograde rotation
    [ ] Prograde rotation
    [ ] Precession
    Explanation: Venus has retrograde rotation — it spins clockwise when viewed from above the north pole, opposite to most planets.

  Q9. The largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, is on:
    [*] Mars
    [ ] Venus
    [ ] Earth
    [ ] Mercury
    Explanation: Olympus Mons on Mars is ~21 km high and ~600 km across, the largest known volcano in the solar system.

  Q10. Which terrestrial planet has evidence of past liquid water on its surface?
    [ ] All of them equally
    [ ] Venus
    [*] Mars
    [ ] Mercury
    Explanation: Mars shows extensive evidence of ancient riverbeds, lake basins, and mineral deposits that formed in water.

  Q11. What does 'terrestrial planet' mean?
    [ ] A massive world composed primarily of hydrogen and helium with deep atmospheric layers and no solid surface
    [ ] A large planet encircled by bands of icy particles and rocky debris held in place by gravitational forces
    [*] A rocky planet with a solid surface, similar in composition to Earth
    [ ] A world that has developed the right chemical conditions and liquid water to sustain biological organisms
    Explanation: Terrestrial planets (from Latin 'terra' = earth) are small, dense, rocky worlds with solid surfaces.

  Q12. What is a 'magnetosphere'?
    [ ] A naturally magnetized iron-bearing mineral found in a planet's crust that aligns with the local magnetic field
    [ ] The gaseous envelope surrounding a planet, held in place by gravity and composed of various molecular species
    [*] The region around a planet controlled by its magnetic field that deflects solar wind
    [ ] The dense innermost layer of a planet, typically composed of iron and nickel under extreme pressure and temperature
    Explanation: A magnetosphere is the magnetic 'bubble' around a planet that shields it from charged particles in the solar wind.

  Q13. What is the 'greenhouse effect'?
    [ ] When frozen surfaces bounce incoming solar radiation back into space, increasing a planet's overall albedo value
    [ ] When photosynthetic organisms thrive inside an enclosed glass structure that retains warmth from sunlight
    [*] When atmospheric gases trap heat, warming a planet's surface beyond what sunlight alone would produce
    [ ] When widespread biological pigments or mineral deposits give a planet's surface a distinctly green appearance
    Explanation: The greenhouse effect occurs when CO₂, water vapor, and other gases absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, warming the surface.

  Q14. What is 'volcanism'?
    [ ] The gradual wearing away of surface rock and soil by persistent atmospheric wind currents over geological timescales
    [*] The eruption of molten rock (lava) and gases from a planet's interior to its surface
    [ ] Intense electrical discharge events in a planet's atmosphere caused by charge separation within convective cloud systems
    [ ] The slow horizontal displacement of rigid crustal plates driven by convection currents circulating in the mantle below
    Explanation: Volcanism releases internal heat and materials, shaping planetary surfaces with lava flows, mountains, and craters.

  Q15. What is 'plate tectonics'?
    [ ] The breaking of plates in an earthquake
    [*] The movement of large rigid pieces of a planet's crust driven by convection in the mantle
    [ ] Mountain formation only
    [ ] A process unique to the Moon
    Explanation: Plate tectonics is Earth's unique (among planets) process of crustal plates moving, colliding, and separating.

  Q16. What is a 'runaway greenhouse effect'?
    [ ] A negative feedback mechanism where excessive cloud formation reflects sunlight and gradually lowers surface temperatures
    [*] An extreme greenhouse effect where rising temperatures cause more greenhouse gas release in a feedback loop
    [ ] A colloquial misunderstanding referring to a physical greenhouse structure that has been displaced by strong winds
    [ ] The predictable cyclical increase in surface temperatures caused by axial tilt during a planet's orbital progression
    Explanation: Venus likely experienced a runaway greenhouse effect where evaporating water increased heating, which evaporated more water, in a vicious cycle.

  Q17. What is 'regolith'?
    [*] The layer of loose, broken rock and dust covering a solid rocky surface
    [ ] An ignite rock formed from cooled magma or lava, typically composed of silicate minerals with crystalline structures
    [ ] Subsurface liquid water stored in porous rock formations and aquifers beneath a planet's solid crustal layer
    [ ] A naturally occurring inorganic crystalline solid with a defined chemical composition found in planetary crusts
    Explanation: Regolith covers the Moon, Mars, and other bodies — it's fragmented surface material created by impacts and weathering.

  Q18. What is 'albedo'?
    [*] The fraction of incoming light that a surface reflects
    [ ] The color of a planet
    [ ] The density of an atmosphere
    [ ] The temperature of a planet
    Explanation: Albedo ranges from 0 (absorbs all light) to 1 (reflects all light). Venus has a high albedo (~0.7) due to its thick clouds.

  Q19. What is 'outgassing'?
    [ ] The phase transition in which a gaseous substance loses thermal energy and condenses into a liquid state at lower temperatures
    [ ] The process by which interstellar gas enters a planet's gravitational influence and becomes trapped in its atmosphere
    [ ] The gradual removal of surface material by atmospheric wind currents carrying abrasive dust and sand particles across terrain
    [*] The release of gases from a planet's interior through volcanic activity
    Explanation: Outgassing from volcanic activity built up the early atmospheres of terrestrial planets.

  Q20. What is a 'shield volcano'?
    [ ] A large volcanic structure whose lava flow patterns and topography create a natural barrier shielding nearby settlements
    [ ] A steep-sided stratovolcano built from alternating layers of hardened lava, tephra, and volcanic ash from eruptions
    [*] A broad, gently sloping volcano built by successive lava flows, like Olympus Mons
    [ ] A volcanic vent located on the ocean floor that erupts magma into seawater, sometimes forming new islands over time
    Explanation: Shield volcanoes have gentle slopes formed by fluid lava spreading widely — Olympus Mons is the largest example.

  Q21. Venus and Earth are similar in size and mass, yet Venus's surface is ~460°C while Earth's averages ~15°C. What is the primary reason?
    [ ] Venus is much closer to the Sun
    [ ] Venus has more volcanoes
    [*] Venus has a massive CO₂ atmosphere causing an extreme greenhouse effect
    [ ] Earth's Moon cools it down
    Explanation: Venus's thick CO₂ atmosphere traps enormous amounts of heat. While Venus is closer to the Sun, the greenhouse effect is the dominant factor.

  Q22. Mars once had a thicker atmosphere and liquid water. What likely caused it to lose its atmosphere?
    [ ] Photosynthetic organisms absorbed atmospheric carbon dioxide through metabolic processes, converting it into organic compounds
    [ ] Gravitational interactions with other planets gradually shifted Mars into a more distant orbit, reducing its solar heating
    [*] Without a strong magnetic field, solar wind stripped away atmospheric particles over time
    [ ] A catastrophic impact from a large asteroid blasted the atmospheric gases into space through sheer kinetic energy transfer
    Explanation: Mars lost its global magnetic field early in its history, leaving the atmosphere unprotected against erosion by solar wind.

  Q23. Mercury has a thin exosphere but no proper atmosphere. A student asks why. What do you explain?
    [ ] Mercury's weak but present magnetic field creates a small magnetosphere that deflects incoming solar wind charged particles
    [*] Mercury's weak gravity and proximity to the Sun's heat and solar wind prevent it from holding gases
    [ ] Mercury's interior cooled too rapidly after formation to sustain any volcanic eruptions or outgassing of interior volatiles
    [ ] Mercury formed recently enough that it has not yet had sufficient time to accumulate atmospheric gases from outgassing
    Explanation: Mercury's low mass (weak gravity), high temperatures, and intense solar wind prevent it from retaining a significant atmosphere.

  Q24. Scientists discover clay minerals on Mars that only form in the presence of water. What does this imply?
    [ ] Water was delivered recently by comets
    [*] Mars had liquid water on its surface long enough for these minerals to form
    [ ] The minerals formed from dry chemical reactions
    [ ] Humans previously visited Mars
    Explanation: Clay (phyllosilicate) minerals require sustained liquid water contact, indicating Mars had a wet environment in its past.

  Q25. Earth is the only terrestrial planet with active plate tectonics. How does this benefit life?
    [ ] It produces gold and diamonds
    [ ] It has no benefit — plate tectonics is dangerous
    [ ] It creates beautiful mountains for tourists
    [*] It recycles carbon, regulates atmospheric CO₂, and maintains a habitable climate over geological time
    Explanation: Plate tectonics drives the carbon-silicate cycle, regulating CO₂ and climate — a key factor in maintaining habitable conditions.

  Q26. Venus rotates extremely slowly and in the opposite direction. If you lived on Venus, how would the Sun appear to move?
    [ ] It would rise in the east like on Earth
    [*] It would rise in the west and set in the east, and a solar day would last about 117 Earth days
    [ ] The Sun would never move
    [ ] The Sun would be invisible due to clouds
    Explanation: Venus's retrograde rotation means the Sun would appear to move opposite to Earth, and extreme slowness means very long solar days.

  Q27. A probe measures Mercury's density at 5.43 g/cm³ — almost as high as Earth's despite being much smaller. What does this suggest?
    [*] Mercury has an unusually large iron core relative to its size
    [ ] Mercury is made of gold
    [ ] The measurement is wrong
    [ ] Mercury has a thick atmosphere compressing it
    Explanation: Mercury's high density indicates a proportionally enormous iron core — about 60% of the planet by mass.

  Q28. Mars has two small, irregular moons (Phobos and Deimos). Their shapes suggest they are:
    [*] Captured asteroids
    [ ] Perfectly spherical like Earth's Moon
    [ ] Former comets
    [ ] Pieces of Mars broken off by an impact
    Explanation: Phobos and Deimos are small, irregularly shaped, and resemble asteroids — likely captured from the nearby asteroid belt.

  Q29. A researcher proposes terraforming Mars by increasing its greenhouse effect. What challenge would they face?
    [ ] Mars's regolith contains abundant nutrients and organic compounds that would support immediate large-scale plant growth
    [*] Mars has low gravity and no magnetic field, so added gases would gradually be stripped away by solar wind
    [ ] Mars possesses extensive subsurface water reserves and polar ice caps that would flood any terraformed lowland regions
    [ ] Mars orbits at a distance where solar radiation is intense enough to immediately dissociate any introduced atmospheric gases
    Explanation: Without a magnetic field, any new atmosphere would be slowly eroded. Mars's weaker gravity also makes gas retention harder.

  Q30. Earth, Venus, and Mars all had early volcanism releasing CO₂. Only Earth maintained a moderate CO₂ level. Why?
    [ ] Earth has more volcanoes
    [ ] Earth is farther from the Sun so CO₂ freezes
    [ ] Plants removed all CO₂ immediately
    [*] Earth has plate tectonics and liquid water that cycle CO₂ through rock weathering and subduction
    Explanation: Earth's unique combination of liquid water and plate tectonics enables the carbon-silicate cycle, which stabilizes CO₂ levels.

--- Lesson 3.3: Gas Giants (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gas Giants
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is metallic hydrogen?

--- Lesson 3.4: Dwarf Planets & Asteroids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Dwarf Planets & Asteroids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'dwarf planet'?
The IAU defined dwarf planets in 2006 as bodies that meet two of three planet criteria — the third (clearing the orbit) is not met.
What is an 'asteroid'?
Asteroids are rocky/metallic remnants from the solar system's formation that never accreted into a planet.
What is the 'Kuiper Belt'?
The Kuiper Belt is a vast ring of icy objects beyond Neptune, home to Pluto, Eris, Makemake, and Haumea.
What is a 'near-Earth object' (NEO)?
NEOs are asteroids or comets whose orbits bring them within 1.3 AU of the Sun, posing potential impact risks.
What is a 'Lagrange point'?
Lagrange points (L1-L5) are equilibrium positions where a small body can maintain a stable position relative to two larger bodies.
What is the 'scattered disc'?
The scattered disc contains icy objects (like Eris) with tilted, elongated orbits, scattered by Neptune's gravity.
What is 'albedo' in the context of asteroids?
Asteroid albedo helps determine composition: metallic asteroids are more reflective than carbon-rich ones.
What is 'orbital clearing'?
Orbital clearing is the third criterion for planet classification — planets must dominate their orbit gravitationally.
What is a 'carbonaceous asteroid' (C-type)?
C-type asteroids are dark, carbon-rich objects making up about 75% of known asteroids, common in the outer belt.
What is a 'binary asteroid'?
Binary asteroids are pairs of asteroids gravitationally bound and orbiting each other — about 15% of NEOs are binaries.

--- Lesson 3.5: Comets & Meteoroids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Comets & Meteoroids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'comet nucleus'?
The nucleus is the solid body of a comet, typically a few km across, composed of frozen gases, dust, and rock.
What is a 'coma'?
As a comet approaches the Sun, heat vaporizes surface ices, creating the coma — a diffuse cloud around the nucleus.
What is a 'meteoroid'?
A meteoroid is defined as a small body (from dust-grain to ~1 m) traveling through space.
What is the 'Oort Cloud'?
The Oort Cloud is theorized to contain trillions of icy objects at the outermost boundary of the Sun's gravitational influence.
What is 'sublimation' in comet science?
In the vacuum of space, comet ices sublimate (go directly from solid to gas) when heated by the Sun, forming the coma and tails.
What is a 'bolide'?
A bolide is a very bright fireball meteor, sometimes producing sonic booms and fragmenting explosively.
What is a 'meteor shower radiant'?
Due to perspective, parallel meteor trails seem to radiate from one point — the radiant. Showers are named for the constellation containing this point.
What is 'outgassing' in comets?
Outgassing produces jets of gas from a comet's nucleus, driving the formation of the coma and tails.
What is 'perihelion' for a comet?
At perihelion, a comet receives maximum solar heating, producing the most spectacular coma and tail.
What is a 'sungrazing comet'?
Sungrazing comets pass within a few solar radii of the Sun; many disintegrate due to extreme heat and tidal forces.

--- Lesson 3.6: Planetary Atmospheres (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Planetary Atmospheres
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'troposphere'?
The troposphere extends from the surface up to about 12 km on Earth and is where nearly all weather phenomena occur.
What is the 'stratosphere'?
The stratosphere extends from ~12-50 km and contains the ozone layer; temperature increases with altitude due to UV absorption.
What is 'atmospheric escape'?
Atmospheric escape occurs through thermal escape, solar wind stripping, and other processes — Mars lost much of its atmosphere this way.
What is 'escape velocity' in the context of atmospheres?
If gas molecules gain enough thermal speed to exceed escape velocity, they leave the planet permanently — lighter molecules escape more easily.
What is 'atmospheric pressure'?
Atmospheric pressure at Earth's surface averages ~101.3 kPa (1 atm), created by the weight of the atmosphere above.
What is an 'exosphere'?
The exosphere is where the atmosphere is so thin that molecules can travel without collisions and potentially escape.
What is 'adiabatic lapse rate'?
The adiabatic lapse rate is typically ~6.5°C/km on Earth — rising air expands and cools at this predictable rate.
What is 'thermal inversion'?
Temperature inversions occur when warm air sits above cooler air, preventing vertical mixing — common in the stratosphere.
What is 'sputtering' in atmospheric science?
Sputtering occurs when high-energy solar wind particles impact atmospheric atoms, giving them enough energy to escape.
What is 'Rayleigh scattering'?
Rayleigh scattering preferentially scatters shorter (blue) wavelengths of light, making Earth's sky appear blue.

--- Lesson 3.8: AP Prep: Kepler's Laws (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Kepler's Laws
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For a planet with a semi-major axis of 9 AU, what is its orbital period (using T² = a³)?
T² = 9³ = 729, so T = √729 = 27 years.
What is an 'ellipse'?
An ellipse is the geometric shape of all planetary orbits — a stretched circle with two focal points.
What is a 'focus' of an ellipse?
The two foci define the ellipse's shape. In a planetary orbit, the star occupies one focus; the other is empty.
What is 'eccentricity'?
Eccentricity e = c/a, where c is the distance from center to focus and a is the semi-major axis.
What is 'aphelion'?
At aphelion, a planet is at its maximum distance from the Sun and moves at its minimum orbital speed.
What is the 'areal velocity'?
Kepler's Second Law states that areal velocity (dA/dt) is constant throughout the orbit.
What is the 'harmonic law'?
The harmonic law relates orbital period (T) to semi-major axis (a), providing a universal relationship.
What is 'angular momentum' in orbit?
Conservation of angular momentum in a central force field (gravity) directly produces Kepler's Second Law.
What is a 'conic section'?
Orbits under gravity are always conic sections. Bound orbits are ellipses (or circles); unbound paths are parabolas or hyperbolas.
What is 'orbital mechanics'?
Orbital mechanics (celestial mechanics) uses Kepler's and Newton's laws to predict and plan spacecraft trajectories.
What is a 'Hohmann transfer orbit'?
A Hohmann transfer uses two engine burns to move between orbits along an ellipse tangent to both — the most fuel-efficient two-impulse method.
A student measures that a planet's period is 8 years. Using Kepler's Third Law, what is its semi-major axis?
T² = a³ → 8² = 64 → a = ∛64 = 4 AU.
Two planets orbit the same star. Planet A is at 1 AU with period 1 year. Planet B is at 8 AU. What is Planet B's period?
T² = a³ = 8³ = 512, so T = √512 ≈ 22.6 years.
