--- Lesson 7.1: History of Space Missions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: History of Space Missions
History of Space MissionsMilestones  • 1957: Sputnik (first satellite, USSR).  • 1961: Yuri Gagarin (first human in space); Alan Shepard (first American).  • 1969: Apollo 11 — Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walk on the Moon.  • 1971: Salyut 1 (first space station, USSR).  • 1981–2011: Space Shuttle program (135 missions).  • 1998–present: International Space Station (ISS).  • 2020s: Artemis program (return to the Moon); commercial spaceflight (SpaceX, Blue Origin).

--- Lesson 7.2: Satellites & Space Probes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Satellites & Space Probes
Satellites & Space ProbesSatellites
LEO (~200–2,000 km): Hubble, ISS, Earth observation. MEO (~2,000–35,786 km): GPS, navigation. GEO (~35,786 km): Weather satellites, communications (stationary over one point).
Space Probes
Robotic explorers sent to other worlds: Voyager (outer solar system), Cassini (Saturn), New Horizons (Pluto), Mars rovers, Juno (Jupiter). Use gravity assists to change trajectory.

--- Lesson 7.3: Human Spaceflight (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Human Spaceflight
Human SpaceflightChallenges  • Microgravity effects: Bone loss (~1–2%/month), muscle atrophy, fluid shift, vision changes.  • Radiation: Cosmic rays and solar particle events; increased cancer risk.  • Psychological: Isolation, confinement, communication delay (Mars: 4–24 min one-way).  • Life support: Oxygen, water recycling, food, waste management.
Exercise (2+ hours/day on ISS), pharmacological countermeasures, and research into artificial gravity are ongoing.

--- Lesson 7.4: International Space Station (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: International Space Station
International Space Station (ISS)
Orbits at ~400 km (LEO); ~109 m wide (solar arrays); ~420,000 kg. Collaborative effort: NASA, Roscosmos, ESA, JAXA, CSA. Continuously occupied since November 2000.
Research
Microgravity research in biology, physics, materials science, Earth observation, and technology. Over 3,000 experiments conducted. Plans for deorbit by ~2031; commercial stations to follow.

--- Lesson 7.5: Future of Space Travel (Mars & Beyond) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Future of Space Travel (Mars & Beyond)
Future of Space TravelMoon (Artemis)
Artemis program: return astronauts to the Moon, establish Gateway (lunar orbital station), and build a sustainable presence using lunar resources (ice at poles).
Mars
SpaceX Starship designed for Mars transit. Challenges: ~6–9 month journey, radiation, landing, ISRU, communication delay. Target: 2030s–2040s.
Beyond  • Asteroid mining, Jupiter's moons (Europa Clipper), Titan exploration, interstellar missions (Breakthrough Starshot concept).

--- Lesson 7.6: Case Studies in Space Technology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Space Technology
Case Studies in Space TechnologyReusable Rockets (SpaceX)
Falcon 9 first-stage landing (2015) revolutionized launch economics. Over 200 booster landings. Starship aims for full reusability.
James Webb Space Telescope (2021)
Largest space telescope; 6.5 m primary mirror; infrared; deployed at L2. Studying exoplanet atmospheres, early galaxies, star formation. Cost ~$10 billion over 25 years.
Mars Helicopter Ingenuity

--- Lesson 7.7: AP Prep: Rocket Science Basics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Rocket Science Basics
AP Prep: Rocket Science BasicsKey Principles  • Newton's 3rd Law: Exhaust gas exits backward → rocket moves forward.  • Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation: Δv = v_e × ln(m₀/m_f). Δv = change in velocity; v_e = exhaust velocity; m₀/m_f = initial/final mass ratio.  • Specific Impulse (I_sp): Measure of engine efficiency; I_sp = v_e/g₀ (seconds). Higher I_sp = more efficient.  • Orbital Mechanics: Δv required for orbit changes determined by vis-viva equation and Hohmann transfer calculations.

--- Lesson 7.7: AP Prep: Rocket Science Basics (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Rockets work based on Newton's _____ law of motion.
    [ ] 1st
    [ ] 2nd
    [*] 3rd (action-reaction: exhaust goes one way, rocket goes the other)
    [ ] None
    Explanation: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

  Q2. The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation is Δv = v_e × ln(m₀/m_f). If v_e = 3 km/s and mass ratio = e (≈2.718), then Δv =:
    [ ] 3 km/s as well
    [*] ~3 km/s (ln(e) = 1, so Δv = 3×1 = 3 km/s)
    [ ] 6 km/s
    [ ] 9 km/s
    Explanation: ln(e) = 1.

  Q3. Specific impulse (I_sp) measures:
    [ ] Thrust only
    [*] Engine efficiency: how much Δv per unit of propellant consumed (I_sp = v_e/g₀)
    [ ] Speed only
    [ ] Mass only
    Explanation: Higher I_sp = less fuel needed.

  Q4. Chemical rockets have I_sp of approximately:
    [ ] 10 s
    [*] ~250–450 s
    [ ] 5,000 s
    [ ] 100,000 s
    Explanation: Relatively low efficiency but high thrust.

  Q5. Ion engines have I_sp of approximately:
    [ ] 100 s
    [*] ~1,500–10,000 s (very efficient but very low thrust)
    [ ] 50 s
    [ ] 10 s
    Explanation: High efficiency, low thrust.

  Q6. The 'tyranny of the rocket equation' means:
    [ ] Rockets are always fast
    [*] Exponentially more fuel is needed for proportionally more Δv (mass ratio grows exponentially)
    [ ] Rockets are cheap
    [ ] Fuel is free
    Explanation: Most of a rocket's mass is fuel.

  Q7. Staging (multi-stage rockets) helps because:
    [ ] It looks cool
    [*] Discarding empty stages reduces mass, allowing remaining fuel to accelerate a lighter vehicle
    [ ] It's tradition
    [ ] No benefit
    Explanation: Shed dead weight.

  Q8. To reach LEO from Earth requires approximately:
    [ ] 1 km/s
    [*] ~9.4 km/s of Δv (including gravity and drag losses)

  Q9. Escape velocity from Earth's surface is:
    [ ] 1 km/s
    [*] ~11.2 km/s
    [ ] 100 km/s
    [ ] 30 km/s
    Explanation: Δv needed to leave Earth entirely.

  Q10. A Hohmann transfer orbit is the most _____ way to transfer between two circular orbits.
    [ ] Fastest
    [*] Fuel-efficient (minimum Δv, but slowest)

    [ ] Random

  Q11. To transfer from LEO to GEO requires a Δv of approximately:
    [ ] 1 km/s
    [*] ~3.9 km/s
    [ ] 10 km/s

  Q12. A Mars mission from LEO requires a Δv of approximately _____ for trans-Mars injection.

    [*] ~3.6 km/s
