--- Lesson 3.1: Weathering (Mechanical & Chemical) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Weathering (Mechanical & Chemical)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the main difference between mechanical and chemical weathering?
Mechanical weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces without altering its chemical composition, while chemical weathering transforms minerals through chemical reactions, creating entirely new substances.
What is the first step in soil formation?
Weathering breaks bedrock into smaller fragments that form the mineral component of soil, making it the essential first step in soil formation before organic matter accumulates.
What is frost wedging?
Frost wedging is a mechanical weathering process in which water enters cracks in rock, freezes and expands by approximately 9%, and gradually widens the crack with each freeze-thaw cycle until the rock splits apart.
What is exfoliation in the context of weathering?
Exfoliation, also called unloading, occurs when overlying rock is eroded away, reducing confining pressure and causing the exposed rock below to expand and crack in concentric sheets, much like layers peeling off an onion.
What is oxidation in the context of rock weathering?
Oxidation is a chemical weathering process in which iron in minerals reacts with oxygen and water to produce iron oxide (rust), weakening the rock structure and giving it a characteristic reddish-brown color.
What is carbonic acid and how does it form?
Carbonic acid (H2CO3) forms naturally when carbon dioxide in the atmosphere dissolves in rainwater, creating a weak acid that is particularly effective at dissolving carbonate rocks like limestone.
What is karst topography?
Karst topography develops when carbonic acid in groundwater dissolves soluble bedrock such as limestone over long periods, creating distinctive features including caves, sinkholes, disappearing streams, and underground rivers.
What is the 'angle of repose' as it relates to weathered material?
The angle of repose is the maximum slope angle at which loose granular material such as sand, gravel, or weathered rock fragments can rest without sliding, and it varies depending on particle size, shape, and moisture content.
What is salt crystal growth (haloclasty) in weathering?
Salt crystal growth is a mechanical weathering process common in arid and coastal environments where saltwater seeps into rock pores, evaporates, and leaves behind growing salt crystals that exert enough pressure to fracture the rock.
An archaeologist discovers that ancient marble statues displayed outdoors in an industrial city have lost their facial features and fine details, while identical statues stored indoors remain perfectly preserved. What is the most likely cause?
Marble is composed of calcium carbonate, which readily dissolves when exposed to acid rain containing sulfuric and nitric acids from industrial emissions, gradually eroding fine details from outdoor statues while indoor statues remain protected.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of mechanical weathering?
Root wedging is a form of mechanical (physical) weathering because the growing roots physically pry the rock apart without changing its chemical composition.
Example: A park ranger in Yosemite observes large, curved sheets of granite peeling away from the surface of Half Dome. What type of weathering is this an example of?
Half Dome's iconic shape is partly due to exfoliation, where the removal of overlying rock reduces pressure on the granite below, causing it to expand and crack in curved, concentric sheets that peel away from the surface.

  Q2. Frost wedging is most effective in climates where:
    [ ] It is always below freezing year-round
    [ ] It never freezes
    [ ] Rainfall is extremely heavy but temperatures stay warm
    [*] Temperatures frequently cycle above and below freezing

  Q3. Chemical weathering occurs fastest in which type of climate?
    [ ] Cold and dry
    [ ] Hot and dry
    [*] Warm and wet
    [ ] Cold and wet
    Explanation: Heat speeds up chemical reactions and water serves as both a reactant and a medium for dissolved chemicals, so warm, wet climates produce the fastest rates of chemical weathering.

    [*] Tree roots growing into rock cracks and splitting the rock apart
    [ ] Iron minerals rusting on a rock surface
    [ ] Limestone dissolving in acidic water
    [ ] Feldspar converting to clay minerals
    Explanation: Root wedging is a form of mechanical (physical) weathering because the growing roots physically pry the rock apart without changing its chemical composition.

  Q5. Oxidation in weathering is most similar to which everyday process?
    [ ] Blowing sand off a table
    [*] Rusting of iron
    [ ] Freezing water in a tray
    [ ] Melting an ice cube
    Explanation: Oxidation occurs when iron-bearing minerals in rock react with oxygen and water to produce iron oxide (rust), which is the same chemical process that causes metal tools and structures to rust.

  Q6. How does weathering differ from erosion?
    [ ] Erosion only occurs in deserts while weathering occurs everywhere
    [*] Weathering breaks down rock in place without moving it, while erosion transports the material
    [ ] Weathering involves transport of material while erosion does not
    [ ] Weathering requires glaciers while erosion requires wind
    Explanation: Weathering is the in-place breakdown of rock by physical or chemical means, while erosion is the transport of those weathered fragments to a new location by agents such as water, wind, ice, or gravity.

  Q7. Carbonation is responsible for creating which type of landscape?
    [ ] Volcanic plateaus
    [ ] Sand dune fields
    [ ] Glacial moraines
    [*] Karst landscapes with caves and sinkholes

  Q9. What is the first step in soil formation?
    [*] Weathering of bedrock into sediment

  Q13. What is oxidation in the context of rock weathering?

    [ ] The accumulation of salt crystals in rock pores
    [ ] The freezing of water inside rock fractures

    Explanation: Oxidation is a chemical weathering process in which iron in minerals reacts with oxygen and water to produce iron oxide (rust), weakening the rock structure and giving it a characteristic reddish-brown color.

  Q14. What is carbonic acid and how does it form?

    [ ] A strong industrial acid produced in factories

--- Lesson 3.2: Erosion & Deposition (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Erosion & Deposition
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the most powerful agent of erosion worldwide?
Running water, including rivers, streams, and surface runoff, erodes and transports more sediment than any other agent globally, shaping landscapes from mountains to coastlines.
What is erosion?
Erosion is the process by which weathered rock and soil particles are picked up and moved from one location to another by natural agents including flowing water, wind, glacial ice, and gravity.
What is deposition?
Deposition is the laying down of sediment that was being carried by an erosional agent, occurring when the agent loses energy and can no longer transport its load, building up new landforms over time.
What is loess?
Loess is wind-deposited silt, often originating from glacial outwash plains or desert surfaces, that accumulates in thick layers to form some of the most fertile agricultural soils in the world.
What is an alluvial fan?
Alluvial fans form at the base of mountains where fast-flowing streams suddenly lose gradient and spread out across a flat plain, depositing sediment in a broad fan shape as the water slows.
What is a barrier island?
Barrier islands are elongated sand deposits created by wave action and longshore drift that run parallel to the mainland coast, protecting the shore and creating sheltered lagoons behind them.
What is glacial till?
Glacial till is a mixture of unsorted sediment ranging from fine clay to large boulders that is deposited directly by glacial ice as it melts, unlike sorted sediment deposited by meltwater streams.
What is a sediment budget?
A sediment budget accounts for the input, transport, and output of sediment in a system such as a beach or river, determining whether the area is gaining sediment (building up) or losing it (eroding).
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the most powerful agent of erosion worldwide?
Running water, including rivers, streams, and surface runoff, erodes and transports more sediment than any other agent globally, shaping landscapes from mountains to coastlines.
Example: Deposition occurs when a transporting agent:
When a transporting agent such as a river, wind, or glacier slows down or loses energy, it can no longer carry its sediment load, and particles are deposited in order from largest to smallest.
Example: Glaciers are unique erosion agents because they can:
Glaciers have immense mass and can transport boulders weighing thousands of tons, carving wide U-shaped valleys through abrasion and plucking as they flow slowly downhill.
Example: A moraine is a feature created by which agent of erosion and deposition?
Moraines are ridges or mounds of unsorted glacial debris (till) deposited along the edges or at the terminus of a glacier, marking the former extent of ice advance.
Example: After a massive hurricane, a barrier island off the coast of North Carolina is completely breached, with ocean water flowing through a new inlet where houses once stood. However, satellite images show that new sand has been deposited further down the coast. What does this demonstrate about coastal erosion and deposition?
This scenario demonstrates the fundamental link between erosion and deposition: sediment removed by storm waves from one part of the coast is transported by currents and deposited elsewhere, constantly reshaping coastal landforms.
Example: A civil engineer is designing a new housing development on a hillside above a river valley. During site inspection, she notices that the hillside soil is thick clay on a steep slope, and the area receives heavy seasonal rainfall. She recommends extensive drainage systems, retaining walls, and preserving natural vegetation on the slope. What erosion and deposition risks is she trying to prevent?
Clay soils on steep slopes are highly susceptible to erosion and slope failure when saturated by heavy rainfall, so the engineer's measures aim to manage water drainage, stabilize the slope, and prevent sediment from eroding downhill.

    [ ] Clay particles
    [*] Large particles such as gravel and sand
    [ ] Silt particles

    [ ] Where a glacier retreats from a valley
    [ ] Where two rivers merge into one larger stream
    [*] Where a river enters a lake or ocean and deposits its sediment

    [ ] Only work on flat, level surfaces

  Q10. A moraine is a feature created by which agent of erosion and deposition?
    [ ] Wind
    [ ] Rivers
    [*] Glaciers

    [ ] The compaction and cementation of sediment into rock
    Explanation: Erosion is the process by which weathered rock and soil particles are picked up and moved from one location to another by natural agents including flowing water, wind, glacial ice, and gravity.

  Q12. What is deposition?

    Explanation: Loess is wind-deposited silt, often originating from glacial outwash plains or desert surfaces, that accumulates in thick layers to form some of the most fertile agricultural soils in the world.

--- Lesson 3.3: River Systems & Landforms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: River Systems & Landforms
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a watershed?
A watershed, also called a drainage basin, includes all the land where precipitation flows downhill into a particular river and its tributaries, bounded by ridges and divides that separate it from adjacent watersheds.
What is a meander?
A meander is a sinuous bend in a river channel that forms as water erodes the outer bank of a curve and deposits sediment on the inner bank, causing the river to migrate laterally across its floodplain.
What is a floodplain?
A floodplain is the relatively flat land bordering a river that is subject to periodic flooding, receiving deposits of fine, nutrient-rich alluvial sediment each time the river overtops its banks.
What is a point bar?
A point bar is a depositional feature that forms on the inner bank of a meander where water velocity is lowest, causing sediment to accumulate in a gently sloping deposit of sand and gravel.
What is a tributary?
A tributary is any stream or river that feeds into a larger river rather than flowing directly into the ocean, contributing water and sediment to the main channel and increasing its discharge downstream.
What is a river's base level?
Base level is the lowest elevation to which a river can erode its channel, with ultimate base level being sea level for rivers flowing to the ocean, though lakes and resistant rock can create local base levels.
What is a drainage divide?
A drainage divide is an elevated boundary, typically a ridge or mountain crest, that separates adjacent drainage basins so that precipitation falling on one side flows into one river system and precipitation on the other side flows into a different system.
A park ranger notices that a river in Yellowstone has undercut a tall bank of volcanic ash, creating an overhang. Heavy rain is forecast for the weekend. What is the most likely near-term consequence for this riverbank?
The undercut bank creates an overhang that becomes increasingly unstable as the unsupported weight of material grows, and heavy rain will saturate the ash, adding weight and reducing cohesion, making collapse into the river very likely.
A family living beside a river notices that every spring, their backyard seems to get a little smaller as the river edge creeps closer to their house. They live on the outside of a river bend. What is happening and what should they expect in the future?
Properties on the outer bank of a river meander are subject to ongoing lateral erosion because the fastest-flowing water concentrates against the outer bank, progressively undercutting and removing material year after year.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is a drainage divide?
A drainage divide is an elevated boundary, typically a ridge or mountain crest, that separates adjacent drainage basins so that precipitation falling on one side flows into one river system and precipitation on the other side flows into a different system.

--- Lesson 3.4: Glaciers & Ice Ages (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Glaciers & Ice Ages
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a glacial erratic?
A glacial erratic is a boulder transported by glacial ice far from its original location and deposited when the ice melts, often appearing completely different from the local bedrock and looking conspicuously out of place.
What is a moraine?
A moraine is an accumulation of unsorted glacial debris (till) deposited in ridges along the sides, center, or terminus of a glacier, serving as evidence of the glacier's former size and extent.
What is firn?
Firn is partially compacted granular snow that has survived at least one summer melt season, representing an intermediate stage between freshly fallen snow and the dense, crystal-clear glacial ice that forms under continued compression.
What is a continental glacier?
Continental glaciers, or ice sheets, are vast masses of ice that spread over large portions of a continent, reaching thicknesses of several kilometers, with the Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets being the two that exist today.
What is an alpine glacier?
Alpine glaciers, also called valley glaciers, form in mountain valleys above the snowline and flow downhill like slow rivers of ice, carving distinctive features like cirques, aretes, and U-shaped valleys.
What is a cirque?
A cirque is an amphitheater-shaped hollow carved into a mountainside by the erosive action of a glacier at its source, and after the glacier melts, the cirque may contain a small lake called a tarn.
A coastal city in Bangladesh is already experiencing frequent flooding at high tide, and scientists project that melting of the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets will raise sea levels by 1 to 2 meters by 2100. What is the primary concern for this city?
When ice sheets on land melt, the meltwater flows into the oceans and raises sea level, threatening low-lying coastal cities like those in Bangladesh with permanent flooding, saltwater intrusion, and displacement of populations.
A farmer in Wisconsin finds that his soil is a mixture of clay, sand, gravel, and boulders all jumbled together with no sorting by size. A geologist tells him this soil composition is typical of the area. What is the origin of this mixed-up soil?
Much of Wisconsin's soil is derived from glacial till, the unsorted mixture of all sediment sizes deposited directly by the melting ice sheet, which unlike water or wind deposits does not sort particles by size.
A climate scientist presents data showing that global glacial ice volume has been decreasing at an accelerating rate since the 1990s. Satellite measurements show that the Greenland ice sheet alone lost approximately 280 billion tons of ice per year from 2002 to 2020. A student asks what the practical consequence of this ice loss is. What is the most accurate answer?
Melting glacial ice on land directly raises sea levels as meltwater enters the oceans, and the loss of glaciers also threatens freshwater supplies for billions of people who depend on glacial meltwater for drinking water and agriculture.

--- Lesson 3.5: Mass Wasting & Landslides (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mass Wasting & Landslides
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the fastest type of mass wasting?
Rockfalls and debris avalanches are the fastest types of mass wasting, with material free-falling or tumbling at speeds that can exceed 200 km/h, making them among the most dangerous geological hazards.
What is the angle of repose?
The angle of repose is the maximum slope angle at which unconsolidated granular material can rest without failing, and it varies by material type, with angular gravel having a steeper angle of repose than smooth, rounded sand.
What is a slump?
A slump is a type of rotational mass movement where a block of material slides along a curved failure surface, causing the upper part of the mass to tilt backward while the lower portion pushes outward.
What is a debris flow?
A debris flow is a fast-moving, water-saturated mass of soil, rock, and organic material that behaves like a viscous fluid, flowing rapidly down channels and across surfaces, often triggered by intense rainfall or rapid snowmelt.
What is a lahar?
A lahar is a destructive mudflow composed of volcanic material and water that rushes down volcano slopes, often triggered by eruptions melting glacial ice or heavy rain on loose volcanic ash, moving like wet concrete at high speeds.
What is a talus slope?
A talus slope, also called a scree slope, is a cone-shaped accumulation of angular rock fragments at the base of a cliff or steep rock face, built up over time by repeated rockfalls from weathering of the cliff above.
What is a mudflow?
A mudflow is a fast-moving stream of water-saturated fine-grained material (clay and silt) that flows rapidly downslope with the consistency of wet concrete, often following established channels and capable of destroying structures in its path.
What is a 'slip surface' in mass wasting?
A slip surface is the zone of weakness along which the sliding mass separates from the stable slope material during a landslide or slump, often formed along a clay layer, bedding plane, or boundary between soil and bedrock.
A civil engineer is evaluating a site for a new road cut through a hillside. She discovers that the slope consists of clay-rich soil overlying smooth, tilted bedrock surfaces. Recent weather has been rainy. She recommends against cutting into the slope without extensive stabilization measures. What is she most concerned about?
Clay layers become extremely slippery when wet, and when they sit on inclined bedrock, they create ideal slip surfaces; cutting into the toe of the slope removes the support holding everything in place, dramatically increasing landslide risk.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Landslide dams are inherently unstable because they are made of loose, unconsolidated debris, and water pressure builds as lakes form behind them, creating the risk of sudden catastrophic breaching that can release devastating floods downstream.
Real-World Applications
Example: What are retaining walls and drainage systems designed to prevent?
Retaining walls provide physical support to hold slope material in place, while drainage systems remove excess water that would otherwise saturate the soil and reduce friction, together reducing the risk of mass wasting.

Emergency managers are most concerned about what secondary hazard?

    [ ] The formation of mud cracks during a drought
    Explanation: A mudflow is a fast-moving stream of water-saturated fine-grained material (clay and silt) that flows rapidly downslope with the consistency of wet concrete, often following established channels and capable of destroying structures in its path.

  Q19. What does 'soil liquefaction' mean?
    [ ] The process of soil being washed away by heavy rain
    [ ] The formation of underground water channels through soil
    [ ] The chemical breakdown of soil minerals by acid
    [*] When saturated soil loses its strength during earthquake shaking and behaves like a liquid
    Explanation: Soil liquefaction occurs when water-saturated, loosely packed soil is subjected to intense shaking during an earthquake, causing the soil particles to lose contact with each other and the ground to behave like a thick liquid.

  Q20. What is a 'slip surface' in mass wasting?
    [ ] The top surface of the soil exposed to weathering
    [ ] An icy road surface that causes vehicles to slide
    [ ] The surface of a river where water flows fastest
    [*] The boundary or plane along which a mass of material slides during a landslide or slump
    Explanation: A slip surface is the zone of weakness along which the sliding mass separates from the stable slope material during a landslide or slump, often formed along a clay layer, bedding plane, or boundary between soil and bedrock.

  Q21. After three days of continuous heavy rain, a hillside above a small town suddenly gives way, sending a wall of mud, rocks, and debris through several homes. Investigators find that the hillside had been recently cleared of trees for a housing development. What combination of factors most likely caused this deadly landslide?
    [ ] Underground volcanic activity weakened the hillside from below
    [ ] Wind erosion removed the top layer of soil, exposing unstable rock beneath
    [*] Prolonged rainfall saturated the deforested slope, adding weight and reducing friction, while the absence of stabilizing tree roots left the soil unanchored
    [ ] An earthquake shook the hillside during the rainstorm
    Explanation: The combination of deforestation (removing root reinforcement) and prolonged heavy rainfall (saturating soil, adding weight, and reducing friction) created ideal conditions for catastrophic slope failure.

  Q22. A homeowner notices that the fence posts in their backyard on a gentle hillside are slowly tilting downhill, and the retaining wall at the bottom of the yard has a slight bulge. These changes have occurred gradually over several years. What type of mass wasting is occurring?
    [*] Soil creep, the extremely slow downslope movement of soil driven by repeated freeze-thaw cycles and gravity
    [ ] A rapid landslide that the homeowner failed to notice
    [ ] Chemical weathering dissolving the fence post foundations
    [ ] A debris flow moving through the yard
    Explanation: Tilted fence posts and retaining wall bulges developing gradually over years are classic indicators of soil creep, the imperceptibly slow downslope movement of surface material caused by gravity, freeze-thaw action, and wetting-drying cycles.

  Q23. In 1985, the eruption of Nevado del Ruiz volcano in Colombia melted summit glacial ice, which mixed with volcanic ash and raced down river valleys at over 60 km/h, burying the town of Armero and killing over 23,000 people. What type of mass wasting event was this?
    [*] A lahar (volcanic mudflow) triggered by glacial meltwater mixing with volcanic debris
    [ ] A glacial advance that crushed the town
    [ ] A rockfall from the volcano's crater rim
    [ ] A slow slump that gradually buried the town over several days
    Explanation: The Armero tragedy was caused by a lahar, a volcanic mudflow created when eruption heat melted summit glaciers, producing a torrent of water that mixed with volcanic ash and debris and flowed rapidly down valleys to devastate the town.

  Q24. A civil engineer is evaluating a site for a new road cut through a hillside. She discovers that the slope consists of clay-rich soil overlying smooth, tilted bedrock surfaces. Recent weather has been rainy. She recommends against cutting into the slope without extensive stabilization measures. What is she most concerned about?
    [ ] Wind will blow the clay across the road and obstruct visibility
    [ ] The road will be too steep for vehicles to climb
    [*] Cutting the slope will remove support from the toe, and the clay layer will act as a lubricated slip surface on the tilted bedrock, triggering a landslide
    [ ] The clay will stain the road surface and make it slippery for cars
    Explanation: Clay layers become extremely slippery when wet, and when they sit on inclined bedrock, they create ideal slip surfaces; cutting into the toe of the slope removes the support holding everything in place, dramatically increasing landslide risk.

  Q25. Residents in a coastal California town notice that after heavy winter rains, a section of the bluff behind their homes has broken away in a crescent-shaped pattern. The top of the failed area has dropped down and tilted backward. What type of mass wasting has occurred?
    [ ] A rockfall from the cliff face
    [ ] Soil creep that accumulated over many decades
    [ ] A debris flow running down from the hills above
    [*] A rotational slump where a block of material slid along a curved failure surface
    Explanation: The crescent-shaped scarp and backward-tilted block are hallmarks of a rotational slump, where a mass of material slides along a curved failure surface, with the upper portion dropping down and rotating backward.

  Q26. A construction company is building homes at the base of a steep slope made of loose gravel and sand. The slope angle is very close to the material's natural angle of repose. A geologist warns the developer that any disturbance could trigger a mass wasting event. What would most likely cause the slope to fail?
    [*] Vibrations from construction equipment, heavy rain, or adding material to the top of the slope could exceed the angle of repose and trigger a slide
    [ ] Planting grass on the slope surface, particularly regarding loading according to conventional analysis
    [ ] Painting the houses at the base of the slope under equilibrium conditions
    [ ] Normal foot traffic along the base of the slope as predicted by standard models
    Explanation: When a slope is already near its angle of repose, even small disturbances like vibrations, added weight from rain, or loading material on top can exceed the critical stability threshold and cause the loose material to collapse.

  Q27. In Alaska, residents notice that buildings are sinking and roads are buckling in areas that were previously stable. Scientists attribute these problems to permafrost thawing due to rising temperatures. What mass wasting process is contributing to these structural failures?
    [ ] Rapid landslides triggered by earthquakes
    [ ] Wind erosion removing soil from beneath buildings
    [ ] River flooding washing away building foundations
    [*] Solifluction and ground subsidence as thawing permafrost loses structural integrity and the saturated surface layer flows
    Explanation: As permafrost thaws, the ice within it melts, causing the ground to lose its structural support and subside, while the saturated surface soil flows slowly downslope through solifluction, destabilizing roads and buildings built on formerly frozen ground.

  Q28. After a magnitude 7.0 earthquake strikes a mountainous region, satellite imagery reveals that hundreds of landslides have occurred across the affected area, blocking rivers and creating dangerous temporary lakes behind debris dams. Emergency managers are most concerned about what secondary hazard?
    [ ] The blocked rivers will permanently change course to flow in the opposite direction
    [ ] The landslide debris will undergo chemical weathering and become toxic
    [*] The temporary debris dams could suddenly breach, releasing catastrophic floods downstream
    [ ] The landslides will trigger volcanic eruptions in the mountains
    Explanation: Landslide dams are inherently unstable because they are made of loose, unconsolidated debris, and water pressure builds as lakes form behind them, creating the risk of sudden catastrophic breaching that can release devastating floods downstream.

  Q29. A mountain road in India passes through an area where the slope above has been undercut by road construction, and the slope below has been steepened by dumping excavated material. During the monsoon season, a section of the road collapses. An engineer explains that both the cutting above and filling below contributed to the failure. Why?
    [*] Cutting removed support from the upper slope while adding material below overloaded and steepened the lower slope, both increasing instability
    [ ] The excavated material chemically weakened the slope below
    [ ] The monsoon rains only affected the road surface, not the slopes
    [ ] The road surface was too smooth and allowed water to flow too quickly
    Explanation: Road construction creates a double instability: cutting into the uphill slope removes the toe support and oversteepens it, while dumping fill on the downhill side adds weight and increases the slope angle, both actions promoting mass wasting, especially when combined with monsoon saturation.

  Q30. A town in the Pacific Northwest is located at the base of Mount Rainier, an active volcano with 26 glaciers. Geologists have mapped ancient lahar deposits beneath the town, showing that massive volcanic mudflows have reached this area multiple times in the past 10,000 years. The town installs a lahar warning system with sirens. Why is this precaution necessary even when the volcano appears quiet?
    [*] Heavy rainfall or minor volcanic heat can melt glacial ice and trigger lahars even without a major eruption, and the geological record proves this area is in the direct path of past flows
    [ ] The town only needs to worry about lava flows, not mudflows
    [ ] The warning system is only needed for earthquake preparedness, not volcanic hazards
    [ ] Lahars can only occur during major eruptions, so the system warns of eruptions
    Explanation: Lahars can be triggered by events other than major eruptions, including heavy rainfall, minor volcanic heating, or glacial outburst floods, and the geological record of repeated lahar deposits beneath the town proves it lies in a high-risk zone regardless of current volcanic activity.

--- Lesson 3.5: Mass Wasting & Landslides (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Mass Wasting
     A: The downslope movement of rock and soil primarily driven by gravity

  2. Q: Landslide
     A: A rapid downslope movement of rock and/or soil along a failure surface

  3. Q: Creep
     A: Very slow, continuous downslope movement of soil, often indicated by tilted fences or trees

  4. Q: Mudflow
     A: A rapid flow of water-saturated soil and debris, often triggered by heavy rain

  5. Q: Angle of Repose
     A: The steepest angle at which loose, unconsolidated material remains stable
