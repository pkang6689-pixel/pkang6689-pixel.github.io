--- Lesson 3.1: Weathering (Mechanical & Chemical) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Weathering (Mechanical & Chemical)
Weathering is the breakdown of rocks at or near Earth's surface. It does not involve transport (that's erosion).
  • Mechanical (physical) weathering: rocks break into smaller pieces without changing composition.  • Frost wedging: water freezes in cracks, expanding and splitting rock  • Root pry: plant roots grow into cracks  • Abrasion: rock-on-rock grinding by wind, water, or ice  • Thermal expansion: repeated heating and cooling causes stress fractures  • Chemical weathering: minerals in rock are chemically altered or dissolved.  • Oxidation: iron-bearing minerals react with oxygen (rusting)  • Hydrolysis: minerals react with water to form clay  • Carbonation: CO₂ + water forms carbonic acid, dissolving limestone (karst landscapes)  • Acid rain accelerates chemical weathering  • Chemical weathering is fastest in warm, wet climates.

--- Lesson 3.2: Erosion & Deposition (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Erosion & Deposition
Erosion is the transport of weathered material by natural agents; deposition is the settling of that material in a new location.
  • Agents of erosion:  • Water: rivers, streams, runoff—the most powerful agent of erosion worldwide  • Wind: moves fine particles (sand, silt); dominant in deserts  • Ice: glaciers carve valleys and transport massive boulders  • Gravity: mass movements (landslides, mudflows, creep)  • Deposition occurs when the transporting agent loses energy:  • Rivers slow → deposit sediment in floodplains, deltas  • Wind slows → sand dunes, loess deposits  • Glaciers melt → moraines, glacial till  • The largest particles are deposited first; the finest (clay) travel farthest.

--- Lesson 3.3: River Systems & Landforms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: River Systems & Landforms
Rivers shape landscapes through erosion, transport, and deposition, creating distinctive landforms:
  • Upper course: steep gradient, fast flow, V-shaped valleys, waterfalls, rapids  • Middle course: moderate gradient, wider valley, meanders begin to form  • Lower course: gentle gradient, wide floodplain, meandering river, oxbow lakes, deltas  • Key features:  • Meanders: S-shaped curves; erosion on the outer bank, deposition on the inner bank  • Oxbow lakes: formed when a meander loop is cut off from the main river  • Floodplain: flat area adjacent to a river that floods periodically, receiving fertile sediment  • Drainage basin (watershed): the total area drained by a river and its tributaries

--- Lesson 3.4: Glaciers & Ice Ages (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Glaciers & Ice Ages
Glaciers are large, persistent bodies of ice that form from compacted snow and move under their own weight.
  • Types: alpine (valley) glaciers and continental ice sheets  • How glaciers shape the land:  • Erosion: plucking (pulling rock from bedrock) and abrasion (grinding with embedded rock)  • Creates U-shaped valleys, cirques, arêtes, horns, fjords  • Deposition: moraines, drumlins, glacial till, erratics, outwash plains  • Ice ages: periods when glaciers covered much of Earth's surface. The most recent ended ~11,700 years ago.  • Milankovitch cycles: variations in Earth's orbit (eccentricity, axial tilt, precession) that trigger glacial/interglacial periods.

--- Lesson 3.5: Mass Wasting & Landslides (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mass Wasting & Landslides
Mass wasting (mass movement) is the downslope movement of rock, soil, and debris under the influence of gravity.
  • Types (by speed and material):  • Rockfall: sudden free-fall of rock from a cliff  • Landslide: rapid sliding of a mass of rock/soil along a slope  • Mudflow/debris flow: rapid flow of water-saturated material  • Creep: very slow, gradual downslope movement (tilts fences, trees, headstones)  • Factors that increase risk: steep slopes, heavy rainfall (saturates soil), removal of vegetation, earthquakes, undercutting of slopes by erosion or construction  • Angle of repose: the steepest angle at which loose material remains stable without sliding

    [ ] The depth of a mudflow
    [*] The steepest angle at which loose material can remain stable
    [ ] The height of a cliff
    Explanation: Each material type has a characteristic angle of repose—exceed it and the material slides downslope.

  Q5. Removing vegetation from a hillside increases landslide risk because:
    [ ] Trees block sunlight
    [*] Plant roots stabilize soil and absorb water; without them, soil is loose and saturated
    [ ] Plants cause earthquakes
    [ ] Vegetation heats the ground
    Explanation: Root networks bind soil particles together and absorb water; removing them leaves soil loose, weak, and prone to saturation and sliding.

  Q6. A debris flow differs from a landslide primarily because debris flows:
    [ ] Move more slowly
    [ ] Only contain rock
    [*] Are saturated with water and flow like a thick liquid
    [ ] Occur only on flat ground
    Explanation: Debris flows are water-saturated masses that behave like viscous fluids, moving rapidly down channels—unlike the sliding motion of landslides.

  Q7. Earthquakes trigger mass wasting by:
    [ ] Drying out soil
    [*] Shaking slopes and reducing friction, causing unstable material to fail
    [ ] Heating rock
    [ ] Creating rivers
    Explanation: Seismic shaking reduces the cohesion and friction holding material in place, triggering landslides and rockfalls on susceptible slopes.

  Q8. Mass wasting is the downslope movement of material driven primarily by:
    [ ] Wind
    [ ] Water flow
    [*] Gravity
    [ ] Erosion alone
    Explanation: Gravity-driven.

  Q9. The fastest type of mass wasting is a:
    [ ] Creep
    [ ] Slump
    [*] Rockfall or debris avalanche (can exceed 200 km/h)
    [ ] Solifluction
    Explanation: Rapid movement.

  Q10. The slowest type of mass wasting is:
    [ ] Landslide
    [ ] Mudflow
    [*] Creep (imperceptibly slow — millimeters to centimeters per year, seen in tilted fences/trees)
    [ ] Rockfall
    Explanation: Very slow.

  Q11. A slump involves:
    [ ] Material flying through the air
    [*] A mass of material sliding along a curved surface — the block rotates backward as it moves
    [ ] Slow creep
    [ ] Only water
    Explanation: Rotational slide.

  Q12. Mudflows (lahars from volcanoes) are triggered by:
    [ ] Dry conditions
    [*] Saturated soil or volcanic material mixed with water, flowing rapidly downhill like wet concrete
    [ ] Freezing
    [ ] Only earthquakes
    Explanation: Water-saturated flow.

  Q13. Water contributes to mass wasting by:
    [ ] Drying slopes
    [*] Adding weight AND lubricating slip surfaces (water fills pore spaces, reducing friction between particles)
    [ ] Freezing the ground
    [ ] Only evaporating
    Explanation: Water's dual role.

  Q14. Factors that increase the risk of mass wasting include:
    [ ] Flat terrain and dense vegetation
    [*] Steep slopes, deforestation, heavy rainfall, earthquakes, and undercutting of slopes

  Q15. Deforestation increases mass wasting risk because:
    [ ] Trees are heavy

    [ ] It doesn't

  Q16. Retaining walls and drainage systems are used to:

  Q17. Solifluction occurs in _____ regions where the surface layer thaws and flows over frozen ground beneath.

  Q18. A talus slope is:

  Q19. The angle of repose is:

--- Lesson 3.5: Mass Wasting & Landslides (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Mass Wasting
     A: The downslope movement of rock and soil primarily driven by gravity

  2. Q: Landslide
     A: A rapid downslope movement of rock and/or soil along a failure surface

  3. Q: Creep
     A: Very slow, continuous downslope movement of soil, often indicated by tilted fences or trees

  4. Q: Mudflow
     A: A rapid flow of water-saturated soil and debris, often triggered by heavy rain

  5. Q: Angle of Repose
     A: The steepest angle at which loose, unconsolidated material remains stable
