--- Lesson 4.1: Relative Dating (Superposition, Cross-Cutting) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Relative Dating (Superposition, Cross-Cutting)
Relative dating determines the order of geological events without assigning specific ages.
  • Law of Superposition: in undisturbed sedimentary layers, the oldest rocks are at the bottom, youngest at the top.  • Principle of Original Horizontality: sedimentary layers are originally deposited horizontally; tilted layers indicate later deformation.  • Principle of Cross-Cutting Relationships: a fault or intrusion is younger than the rock it cuts through.  • Principle of Inclusion: fragments of rock included within another rock are older than the surrounding rock.  • Unconformities: gaps in the rock record showing missing time (angular unconformity, disconformity, nonconformity).

--- Lesson 4.2: Absolute Dating & Radiometric Methods (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Absolute Dating & Radiometric Methods
Absolute (numerical) dating assigns specific ages in years using radioactive decay.
  • Radioactive decay: unstable parent isotopes decay into stable daughter isotopes at a constant rate.  • Half-life: the time for half of a radioactive sample to decay. Each isotope has a unique half-life.  • Carbon-14: ~5,730 years (organic material up to ~50,000 years)  • Potassium-40 → Argon-40: 1.25 billion years (igneous rocks)  • Uranium-238 → Lead-206: 4.5 billion years (oldest rocks)  • Scientists measure the parent-to-daughter ratio to calculate age.  • After 1 half-life: 50% parent remains; after 2: 25%; after 3: 12.5%, etc.

--- Lesson 4.3: The Geologic Time Scale (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Geologic Time Scale
The geologic time scale divides Earth's 4.6-billion-year history into hierarchical units:
  • Eons → Eras → Periods → Epochs  • Major divisions:  • Precambrian (4.6 Ga–541 Ma): ~88% of Earth's history; simple life (bacteria, algae); formation of atmosphere and oceans  • Paleozoic Era (541–252 Ma): 'ancient life'; Cambrian explosion, first fish, amphibians, reptiles, forests; ends with largest mass extinction  • Mesozoic Era (252–66 Ma): 'age of reptiles/dinosaurs'; first mammals, birds, flowering plants; ends with asteroid impact extinction  • Cenozoic Era (66 Ma–present): 'age of mammals'; rise of primates, grasslands, ice ages  • Boundaries between eras are defined by mass extinction events.

--- Lesson 4.4: Fossils & the Fossil Record (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fossils & the Fossil Record
Fossils are preserved remains, traces, or impressions of organisms from past geological ages.
  • Types of fossils:  • Body fossils: actual preserved remains (bones, shells, teeth, whole organisms in amber or ice)  • Trace fossils: evidence of activity (footprints, burrows, coprolites)  • Molds and casts: impressions or filled-in impressions of organisms in rock  • Petrification: minerals replace original organic material (petrified wood)  • Index fossils: fossils of organisms that lived during a short, well-defined time period over a wide geographic area—used to date rock layers.  • Conditions for fossilization: rapid burial, hard body parts, low-oxygen environments (prevent decomposition).

--- Lesson 4.5: Mass Extinctions & Evolution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mass Extinctions & Evolution
Mass extinctions are catastrophic events where a significant percentage of all species go extinct in a geologically short time.
  • Big Five mass extinctions:  • End-Ordovician (~444 Ma): 85% of species lost; glaciation  • Late Devonian (~372 Ma): 75%; ocean anoxia  • End-Permian (~252 Ma): ~96% of species; 'The Great Dying'; volcanic activity  • End-Triassic (~201 Ma): ~80%; volcanic CO₂ and warming  • End-Cretaceous (~66 Ma): ~76%; asteroid impact + volcanism  • After each mass extinction, surviving species diversify to fill empty niches (adaptive radiation).  • Example: mammals diversified rapidly after the end-Cretaceous extinction that eliminated non-avian dinosaurs.  • Some scientists argue we are in a sixth mass extinction caused by human activities.

  1. Q: Mass Extinction
     A: A rapid, widespread event in which a large percentage of all species go extinct

  2. Q: The Great Dying
     A: The end-Permian mass extinction (~252 Ma) that killed ~96% of all species—the worst in Earth's history

  3. Q: Adaptive Radiation
     A: Rapid diversification of species to fill ecological niches left empty after an extinction

  4. Q: End-Cretaceous Extinction
     A: The mass extinction 66 Ma ago caused by an asteroid impact that killed non-avian dinosaurs

  5. Q: Sixth Mass Extinction
     A: The proposed current mass extinction driven by human activities like habitat destruction and climate change
