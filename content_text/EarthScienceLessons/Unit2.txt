--- Lesson 2.1: Continental Drift & Evidence (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Continental Drift & Evidence
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is continental drift?
Continental drift is the hypothesis, first proposed by Alfred Wegener, that Earth's continents are not fixed but have moved slowly across the surface over hundreds of millions of years.
What is Gondwana?
Gondwana (or Gondwanaland) was the southern supercontinent formed when Pangaea split, comprising South America, Africa, India, Australia, and Antarctica, which later separated into individual continents.
What is Laurasia?
Laurasia was the northern supercontinent that formed when Pangaea split into two halves, comprising what would become North America, Europe, and Asia.
A museum exhibit shows the distribution of four fossil organisms across the southern continents: Mesosaurus, Glossopteris, Lystrosaurus, and Cynognathus. A visitor asks why finding these on multiple continents is better evidence for drift than finding modern species shared across continents. What is the best response?
Ancient freshwater and land-dwelling organisms could not have crossed vast oceans, so their distribution across now-separated continents strongly indicates past land connections, unlike modern species that can be dispersed by various means.
A documentary filmmaker wants to illustrate what Earth looked like when Pangaea existed. She asks a geologist what the climate and environment would have been like in the interior of such a massive supercontinent. What is the most accurate description?
The vast interior of Pangaea would have been extremely arid because ocean moisture could not penetrate far inland across such an enormous landmass, creating extensive desert conditions in the continental interior.
A geology professor presents a map showing that the same distinctive sequence of rock layers, including glacial tillite, coal beds, and sandstone, appears in South America, Africa, India, Australia, and Antarctica. A student asks how this pattern specifically supports continental drift rather than coincidence. What is the strongest argument?
Finding the identical sequence of distinctive rock layers in the same chronological order on five now-separated continents is extraordinarily strong evidence that they were once a single landmass with a shared geological history.
Real-World Applications
Example: A geology student mapping rock formations in Scotland discovers that the Caledonian mountain rocks have the same age, composition, and structure as Appalachian mountain rocks in eastern North America. She hypothesizes these were once the same mountain chain. What evidence supports this claim?
The Appalachian and Caledonian mountains share matching rock types, ages, and geological structures, strongly supporting that they were once a single mountain chain before the Atlantic Ocean opened between them.

A visitor asks why finding these on multiple continents is better evidence for drift than finding modern species shared across continents. What is the best response?
Ancient freshwater and land-dwelling organisms could not have crossed vast oceans, so their distribution across now-separated continents strongly indicates past land connections, unlike modern species that can be dispersed by various means.

--- Lesson 2.2: Seafloor Spreading (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Seafloor Spreading
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is seafloor spreading?
Seafloor spreading is the process at mid-ocean ridges where tectonic plates diverge, allowing magma from the mantle to rise, cool, and form new oceanic crust that pushes older crust outward.
What is a mid-ocean ridge?
A mid-ocean ridge is a continuous underwater mountain system formed at divergent plate boundaries where magma rises from the mantle to create new oceanic crust, such as the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.
What is magnetic striping on the ocean floor?
Magnetic striping consists of symmetric bands of normal and reversed magnetic polarity on either side of mid-ocean ridges, formed as iron minerals in cooling lava recorded Earth's periodically reversing magnetic field.
What is paleomagnetism?
Paleomagnetism is the study of the record of Earth's past magnetic field preserved in rocks, which provided crucial evidence for seafloor spreading through the symmetric magnetic stripe patterns at ridges.
What is an ocean trench?
An ocean trench is the deepest type of ocean floor feature, formed at convergent boundaries where one plate subducts beneath another, such as the Mariana Trench which reaches nearly 11,000 meters deep.
A geophysicist towing a magnetometer behind a ship across the Atlantic Ocean records alternating bands of strong and weak magnetic signal. She notices the pattern is symmetrical on both sides of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. What is the best explanation for this symmetry?
As magma cools symmetrically on both sides of a mid-ocean ridge, iron minerals align with Earth's current magnetic field; when the field reverses, new crust records the opposite polarity, creating mirror-image stripes.
A research team comparing the age of continental rock (up to 4 billion years old) to oceanic crust (no older than about 200 million years) asks why there is such a dramatic age difference. What is the correct explanation based on seafloor spreading?
Oceanic crust is constantly recycled by subduction, preventing it from surviving more than about 200 million years, while continental crust is too buoyant and thick to subduct, allowing it to persist for billions of years.
A documentary crew filming in Iceland stands on a bridge that spans a visible rift valley between the North American and Eurasian plates. They can see the two plates pulling apart. A producer asks what will happen to Iceland over millions of years. What is the most scientifically accurate prediction?
As the North American and Eurasian plates continue to spread apart, volcanic activity along the rift in Iceland will produce new crust that fills the growing gap, causing the island to gradually widen over geological time.
A student examining a map of the ocean floor notices that heat flow measurements are highest at mid-ocean ridges and lowest far from ridges near continents. She asks her teacher to explain this pattern. What is the correct answer?
High heat flow at mid-ocean ridges is caused by hot mantle material rising to the surface, and as the newly formed crust spreads away from the ridge, it gradually cools, reducing heat flow with distance.
A geochemist analyzes the age of basalt samples collected from various distances along the ocean floor east of the East Pacific Rise. She plots the data and finds a nearly linear relationship between distance from the ridge and rock age. From this, she calculates a spreading rate. If basalt 200 km from the ridge is 4 million years old, what is the spreading rate?
Dividing 200 km by 4 million years gives a half-spreading rate of 50 km per million years, which equals about 5 cm per year, consistent with measured spreading rates at the East Pacific Rise.
A deep-sea exploration team using a submersible observes hydrothermal vents (black smokers) along a mid-ocean ridge. They see superheated water shooting out of chimney-like structures, surrounded by unique organisms. A biologist on the team asks what energy source supports life here. What is the correct answer?
Hydrothermal vent ecosystems are powered by chemosynthetic bacteria that use hydrogen sulfide and other chemicals from the superheated vent water to produce energy, supporting a unique food web independent of sunlight.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
During periods of fast spreading, mid-ocean ridges are larger and hotter, taking up more volume in ocean basins, which displaces seawater and causes global sea levels to rise and flood continental margins.
Real-World Applications
Example: The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is an example of a:
The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is a divergent plate boundary running down the center of the Atlantic Ocean, where the North American and Eurasian plates spread apart and new crust forms.
Example: A marine geologist examining deep-sea drill cores notices that sediment directly above the basalt ocean floor is 50 million years old at a location 500 km from a mid-ocean ridge, but only 10 million years old at a location 100 km from the ridge. What does this pattern demonstrate?
The progressive increase in sediment age with distance from the ridge is direct evidence of seafloor spreading: crust closest to the ridge is youngest, and the first sediment to land on it marks its age.

--- Lesson 2.3: Plate Boundaries (Convergent, Divergent, Transform) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Plate Boundaries (Convergent, Divergent, Transform)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a convergent boundary?
A convergent boundary is where two tectonic plates move toward each other, which can result in subduction (one plate diving under the other), mountain building, or both, depending on the types of crust involved.
What is a transform boundary?
A transform boundary is where two plates grind horizontally past each other along a fault, generating earthquakes from friction but no volcanic activity since no crust is created or destroyed.
What is the Ring of Fire?
The Ring of Fire is a 40,000 km horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean where about 75% of the world's active volcanoes are found and about 90% of earthquakes occur, associated with subduction zones.
What is an island arc?
An island arc is a curved chain of volcanic islands that forms parallel to an oceanic trench at an oceanic-oceanic convergent boundary, where melted material from the subducting plate rises to form volcanoes.
What is a rift valley?
A rift valley is a linear lowland between highlands or mountain ranges created by the action of a geologic rift, where continental crust is being pulled apart at a divergent boundary, such as the East African Rift.
A volcanologist studying the Cascades in Washington state explains that Mount Rainier is a dangerous composite volcano. A journalist asks what tectonic process creates these explosive volcanoes. What is the correct answer?
The Cascade volcanoes form at an oceanic-continental convergent boundary where the Juan de Fuca plate subducts beneath North America; water released from the sinking plate lowers the mantle's melting point, generating magma.
A geography teacher asks her class to explain why Japan experiences frequent earthquakes and has many active volcanoes, while central Africa has relatively few of either. What is the best explanation based on plate tectonics?
Japan sits at the junction of several tectonic plates with active subduction zones, producing intense seismic and volcanic activity, while central Africa lies within the stable interior of the African Plate far from plate boundaries.
A mountaineer climbing in the Himalayas finds marine fossils at 8,000 meters elevation. She asks her guide how ocean creatures ended up at the top of the world's highest mountains. What is the explanation based on plate tectonics?
Before the collision, the Tethys Sea existed between India and Asia, depositing marine sediments on its floor; when the plates collided, these sea-floor sediments were compressed and uplifted thousands of meters.
A documentary crew visits the Mariana Trench, the deepest point in the ocean at nearly 11,000 meters. They ask a geologist to explain what created such an extreme depth. What is the correct explanation?
The Mariana Trench formed at a convergent boundary where the old, dense Pacific Plate subducts steeply beneath the Philippine Plate, bending downward into the mantle and creating the deepest ocean floor on Earth.
A geologist comparing the Andes and Himalayas notes both are massive mountain ranges formed at convergent boundaries but have different characteristics. The Andes have active volcanoes while the Himalayas do not. She asks her students to explain this difference. What is the correct answer?
The Andes have active volcanoes because the oceanic Nazca Plate subducts and melts, generating magma, while at the Himalayas, two buoyant continental plates collide without subduction, so there is no mechanism to produce volcanic melting.

--- Lesson 2.5: Volcanoes & Hot Spots (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Volcanoes & Hot Spots
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is viscosity in the context of volcanic eruptions?
Viscosity measures a fluid's resistance to flow; high-viscosity magma (thick, like honey) traps gas and erupts explosively, while low-viscosity magma (thin, like water) allows gas to escape and flows gently.
What is a hot spot?
A hot spot is a volcanic region fed by a mantle plume, a column of abnormally hot rock rising from deep in the mantle, that remains relatively stationary while tectonic plates move over it.
What is a pyroclastic flow?
A pyroclastic flow is one of the most lethal volcanic hazards, consisting of a fast-moving current of superheated gas and volcanic debris that can travel at over 100 km/h with temperatures exceeding 700 degrees Celsius.
What is a lahar?
A lahar is a volcanic mudflow or debris flow made of a slurry of volcanic material and water that can travel rapidly down valleys, burying communities with a concrete-like mixture of ash, rock, and water.
What is a caldera?
A caldera is a large depression formed when the roof of a magma chamber collapses inward after a large volume of magma has been evacuated during an eruption, such as the Yellowstone Caldera.
A volcanologist monitoring a volcano in Indonesia notices that the magma has high silica content. She immediately raises the alert level. A colleague from Hawaii, where volcanoes typically have low-silica magma, asks why high silica is so dangerous. What is the correct explanation?
High silica content makes magma very viscous (thick), preventing dissolved gases from escaping; as the magma rises and pressure decreases, the trapped gas expands violently, causing explosive eruptions.
A tourist visiting Hawaii watches a slow lava flow from Kilauea enter the ocean, creating new land. She compares this to news footage of Mount Pinatubo's explosive eruption. A park ranger explains why the two volcanoes behave so differently. What is the key factor?
Magma composition determines eruption style: Kilauea's basaltic magma is low in silica and viscosity, allowing gas to escape easily for gentle eruptions, while Pinatubo's silica-rich magma traps gas for explosive eruptions.
A geology professor asks students to explain why Yellowstone's hot spot has created a chain of progressively older volcanic features stretching across Idaho to the northeast. A student suggests the hot spot has been moving southwest. The professor corrects her. What is actually happening?
The mantle plume beneath Yellowstone is stationary, but the North American Plate moves southwest over it, so older volcanic calderas and features are found to the northeast along the Snake River Plain in Idaho.
A volcanologist monitoring seismic activity beneath a dormant volcano detects increasing numbers of small earthquakes and ground uplift measured by GPS sensors. She warns that an eruption may be approaching. A local official asks how earthquakes beneath a volcano can predict an eruption. What is the scientific basis for this warning?
As magma rises through the crust, it forces rock apart and fractures surrounding material, generating swarms of small earthquakes and causing the ground surface to inflate, both of which are reliable precursors to volcanic eruptions.
Real-World Applications
Example: Mount St. Helens is an example of a:
Mount St. Helens is a composite volcano (stratovolcano) in the Cascade Range, known for its catastrophic 1980 explosive eruption that blew away the mountain's north face.
Example: Yellowstone is an example of a _____ hot spot.
Yellowstone is a continental hot spot where a mantle plume beneath the North American continental crust produces geysers, hot springs, and a supervolcano caldera with potential for massive eruptions.
Example: A geologist studying the Hawaiian island chain notices that the islands get progressively older from southeast to northwest. The Big Island (youngest) is currently volcanically active, while the northwestern islands are extinct and eroded. What mechanism explains this age progression?
The Pacific Plate moves northwest over the stationary Hawaiian hot spot, so each island was built while over the plume and then carried away, with the youngest island currently above the active magma source.
Example: A city emergency manager near Mount Rainier in Washington state is most concerned about lahars, not lava flows. She explains to the city council that lahars pose a greater threat to surrounding communities. Why are lahars especially dangerous for cities near snow-capped stratovolcanoes?
Mount Rainier's massive glaciers can melt during volcanic activity, mixing with ash and rock to form lahars that can travel down river valleys at high speed for tens of kilometers, reaching populated lowland areas.
Example: A tourist visiting Hawaii watches a slow lava flow from Kilauea enter the ocean, creating new land. She compares this to news footage of Mount Pinatubo's explosive eruption. A park ranger explains why the two volcanoes behave so differently. What is the key factor?
Magma composition determines eruption style: Kilauea's basaltic magma is low in silica and viscosity, allowing gas to escape easily for gentle eruptions, while Pinatubo's silica-rich magma traps gas for explosive eruptions.

    [ ] A flow of water mixed with volcanic mud
    [ ] A gentle emission of steam from a volcanic vent
    Explanation: A pyroclastic flow is one of the most lethal volcanic hazards, consisting of a fast-moving current of superheated gas and volcanic debris that can travel at over 100 km/h with temperatures exceeding 700 degrees Celsius.

  Q14. What is a lahar?
    [*] A destructive mudflow composed of volcanic debris and water that flows rapidly down volcanic slopes
    [ ] A type of dense volcanic rock formed when lava cools rapidly under high-pressure conditions deep underground
    [ ] A seismic event triggered by magma movement beneath a volcano that causes the surrounding ground to shake
    [ ] A cloud of toxic volcanic gases including sulfur dioxide and carbon dioxide released from an active vent
    Explanation: A lahar is a volcanic mudflow or debris flow made of a slurry of volcanic material and water that can travel rapidly down valleys, burying communities with a concrete-like mixture of ash, rock, and water.

  Q15. What does 'magma chamber' mean?
    [*] A large underground pool of molten rock beneath a volcano that feeds eruptions when pressure builds sufficiently
    [ ] A hollow cavity deep underground that is filled with pressurized volcanic gases which may vent through fumaroles at the surface
    [ ] The large bowl-shaped depression at the summit of a volcano formed by the collapse of rock after previous major eruptions
    [ ] A specially equipped research facility where volcanologists monitor seismic activity and carefully analyze volcanic rock samples
    Explanation: A magma chamber is a large reservoir of molten rock beneath a volcano, typically located several kilometers below the surface, that stores magma until enough pressure builds to force it upward in an eruption.

  Q16. What is a caldera?
    [ ] A broad, slow-moving type of lava flow that spreads across wide areas of flat terrain before cooling into a thin layer of basalt
    [ ] A volcanic island that forms gradually as eruptions from an oceanic hot spot build layers of basaltic lava above the sea surface
    [ ] A small secondary volcanic vent on the flanks of a larger volcano that intermittently releases steam, gases, and minor lava flows
    [*] A large, basin-shaped volcanic depression formed when a volcano collapses into its emptied magma chamber after a massive eruption
    Explanation: A caldera is a large depression formed when the roof of a magma chamber collapses inward after a large volume of magma has been evacuated during an eruption, such as the Yellowstone Caldera.

  Q17. What does 'mantle plume' mean?
    [ ] A hollow underground tunnel formed by flowing lava beneath the surface of a shield volcano that channels molten rock to distant vents
    [ ] A deep fracture in the ocean floor along a divergent plate boundary where tectonic plates are slowly spreading apart from one another
    [*] A column of abnormally hot rock rising from deep within Earth's mantle that can create volcanic hot spots at the surface
    [ ] A towering column of volcanic ash and fine particles ejected high into the atmosphere during an explosive stratovolcano eruption event
    Explanation: A mantle plume is a column of exceptionally hot rock rising from the deep mantle, possibly from near the core-mantle boundary, that creates localized volcanic activity at the surface independent of plate boundaries.

  Q18. What does 'effusive eruption' mean?
    [*] A relatively gentle eruption where low-viscosity lava flows steadily from a vent rather than exploding
    [ ] A violently explosive eruption with ash clouds
    [ ] An eruption that occurs underwater only
    [ ] A volcanic eruption that produces only gas
    Explanation: An effusive eruption is characterized by the relatively gentle outpouring of low-viscosity lava that flows freely from a vent, typical of shield volcanoes like those in Hawaii.

  Q19. What does 'geothermal energy' mean?
    [ ] Kinetic energy released during earthquakes as seismic waves travel through Earth's crust and cause widespread ground shaking
    [ ] Chemical potential energy stored within volcanic gases such as sulfur dioxide and hydrogen sulfide at active volcanic vents
    [*] Heat energy derived from Earth's interior that can be harnessed for electricity generation and direct heating
    [ ] Solar radiation absorbed and stored in exposed surface rocks, then gradually released as radiant heat during the nighttime hours
    Explanation: Geothermal energy is heat from Earth's interior, particularly abundant near volcanic areas and hot spots, that can be captured through wells and used to generate electricity or directly heat buildings.

  Q20. What does 'volcanic explosivity index (VEI)' mean?
    [*] A scale from 0 to 8 that measures the relative explosiveness of volcanic eruptions based on volume of material ejected and eruption column height
    [ ] A measurement of the velocity at which lava flows downslope from a volcanic vent, calculated using the lava's viscosity and terrain steepness
    [ ] A quantitative measure of erupted lava temperature in degrees Celsius, used to classify volcanic eruptions by their total thermal output
    [ ] A regional count of all currently active volcanic vents within a defined geographic area, updated annually by geological survey agencies
    Explanation: The VEI is a scale from 0 (gentle effusive) to 8 (mega-colossal) that classifies eruptions based on the volume of erupted material, eruption cloud height, and duration, similar to the Richter scale for earthquakes.

  Q21. A volcanologist monitoring a volcano in Indonesia notices that the magma has high silica content. She immediately raises the alert level. A colleague from Hawaii, where volcanoes typically have low-silica magma, asks why high silica is so dangerous. What is the correct explanation?
    [*] High silica content increases magma viscosity, trapping gases that build pressure and lead to violent explosive eruptions
    [ ] High silica only affects the color of the lava, not its behavior
    [ ] High silica makes the lava flow faster
    [ ] High silica makes the volcano taller
    Explanation: High silica content makes magma very viscous (thick), preventing dissolved gases from escaping; as the magma rises and pressure decreases, the trapped gas expands violently, causing explosive eruptions.

  Q22. A geologist studying the Hawaiian island chain notices that the islands get progressively older from southeast to northwest. The Big Island (youngest) is currently volcanically active, while the northwestern islands are extinct and eroded. What mechanism explains this age progression?
    [ ] Each island was formed by a different volcanic eruption at the same time
    [ ] The hot spot moves southeast, creating new islands in front of it
    [ ] Ocean currents erode the western islands faster
    [*] The Pacific Plate has been moving northwest over a stationary hot spot, so older islands have been carried away from the magma source
    Explanation: The Pacific Plate moves northwest over the stationary Hawaiian hot spot, so each island was built while over the plume and then carried away, with the youngest island currently above the active magma source.

  Q23. A city emergency manager near Mount Rainier in Washington state is most concerned about lahars, not lava flows. She explains to the city council that lahars pose a greater threat to surrounding communities. Why are lahars especially dangerous for cities near snow-capped stratovolcanoes?
    [ ] Lahars create poisonous gas that spreads over wide areas
    [ ] Lahars are hotter than lava and burn everything in their path
    [ ] Lahars only form in tropical volcanic regions
    [*] Volcanic heat melts glaciers and snow, mixing with volcanic debris to create fast-moving mudflows that can travel far down river valleys to populated areas
    Explanation: Mount Rainier's massive glaciers can melt during volcanic activity, mixing with ash and rock to form lahars that can travel down river valleys at high speed for tens of kilometers, reaching populated lowland areas.

  Q24. A historian studying the eruption of Mount Tambora in 1815 explains that the following year (1816) was called 'The Year Without a Summer' because global temperatures dropped and crops failed across Europe and North America. What volcanic process caused this worldwide cooling?
    [ ] The eruption caused an ice age by altering ocean currents
    [ ] Volcanic ash fell directly on crops worldwide
    [ ] The eruption blocked river water from reaching farmland
    [*] Massive amounts of sulfur dioxide and ash injected into the stratosphere formed an aerosol layer that reflected sunlight and reduced global temperatures
    Explanation: Tambora's massive eruption (VEI 7) injected enormous quantities of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere, forming sulfate aerosols that reflected sunlight globally, reducing temperatures enough to cause crop failures.

  Q25. A renewable energy company is evaluating Iceland as a location for a geothermal power plant. They note that Iceland already generates about 25% of its electricity from geothermal sources. Why is Iceland so well-suited for geothermal energy production?
    [ ] Iceland's cold climate concentrates geothermal energy
    [*] Iceland sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge where hot mantle material rises close to the surface, providing abundant accessible underground heat
    [ ] Iceland is located near the equator where it is warmest
    [ ] Iceland has more underground coal deposits than other countries
    Explanation: Iceland straddles the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, a divergent boundary where hot mantle material rises close to the surface, making geothermal reservoirs shallow and easily accessible for power generation and heating.

  Q26. A tourist visiting Hawaii watches a slow lava flow from Kilauea enter the ocean, creating new land. She compares this to news footage of Mount Pinatubo's explosive eruption. A park ranger explains why the two volcanoes behave so differently. What is the key factor?
    [ ] Kilauea has a much smaller magma chamber than Pinatubo, so it cannot build enough gas pressure for the violent explosive eruptions typical of Philippine volcanoes
    [ ] Kilauea formed more recently than Pinatubo and has not yet developed the deep magma reservoir needed to produce the violent explosive eruptions of older volcanoes
    [*] Kilauea erupts low-viscosity basaltic lava that flows easily, while Pinatubo erupts high-viscosity, silica-rich magma that traps gas and explodes violently
    [ ] Pacific Ocean water surrounding Hawaii constantly cools Kilauea's erupting lava and absorbs the thermal energy that would otherwise fuel explosive volcanic activity
    Explanation: Magma composition determines eruption style: Kilauea's basaltic magma is low in silica and viscosity, allowing gas to escape easily for gentle eruptions, while Pinatubo's silica-rich magma traps gas for explosive eruptions.

  Q27. A farmer living near an active volcano in Java, Indonesia, refuses to relocate despite eruption risks. She explains that her volcanic soil produces exceptional rice crops. A geologist confirms her reasoning. Why is soil near active volcanoes often among the most productive agricultural land?
    [*] Fresh volcanic ash weathers rapidly into mineral-rich soil packed with nutrients like potassium, phosphorus, and calcium that promote plant growth
    [ ] Volcanic regions receive more rainfall than other areas
    [ ] Volcanic heat warms the soil and helps plants grow faster
    [ ] Volcanic gases fertilize the soil directly
    Explanation: Volcanic ash is rich in essential plant nutrients and weathers much faster than most rocks, creating fertile soils in decades rather than centuries, which is why some of the world's most productive farmland surrounds active volcanoes.

  Q28. A geology professor asks students to explain why Yellowstone's hot spot has created a chain of progressively older volcanic features stretching across Idaho to the northeast. A student suggests the hot spot has been moving southwest. The professor corrects her. What is actually happening?
    [ ] Multiple separate hot spots have formed in a line
    [*] The North American Plate is moving southwest over the stationary hot spot, so older volcanic features are found to the northeast where the plate used to be
    [ ] Earthquakes have shifted the volcanic features to the northeast
    [ ] The hot spot moves southwest while the plate stays still
    Explanation: The mantle plume beneath Yellowstone is stationary, but the North American Plate moves southwest over it, so older volcanic calderas and features are found to the northeast along the Snake River Plain in Idaho.

  Q29. A disaster planner is creating an eruption response plan for a community near a composite volcano. She reviews the most common volcanic hazards: lava flows, pyroclastic flows, lahars, ashfall, and volcanic gases. Which hazard can travel the farthest from the volcano and affect the largest area?
    [ ] Lava flows, because their extreme temperatures melt and consume everything in their path for hundreds of kilometers before the molten rock cools enough to solidify
    [*] Ashfall, because fine volcanic ash can be carried hundreds to thousands of kilometers by wind, affecting air quality, agriculture, and transportation over vast areas
    [ ] Volcanic gases, because their lightweight molecular composition enables them to spread in all directions from the vent and travel great distances across entire continents
    [ ] Pyroclastic flows, because their extreme speed of over 700 km/h allows them to travel enormous distances from the volcano, devastating everything along their path
    Explanation: While pyroclastic flows and lahars are deadlier locally, volcanic ashfall can be carried by prevailing winds for hundreds to thousands of kilometers, disrupting aviation, damaging crops, and causing respiratory problems over enormous areas.

  Q30. A volcanologist monitoring seismic activity beneath a dormant volcano detects increasing numbers of small earthquakes and ground uplift measured by GPS sensors. She warns that an eruption may be approaching. A local official asks how earthquakes beneath a volcano can predict an eruption. What is the scientific basis for this warning?
    [ ] Earthquakes always cause volcanic eruptions
    [*] Rising magma fractures surrounding rock as it pushes toward the surface, causing increasing numbers of small earthquakes and measurable ground deformation
    [ ] Seismic activity has no relationship to volcanic eruptions
    [ ] The earthquakes are caused by tourists walking near the volcano
    Explanation: As magma rises through the crust, it forces rock apart and fractures surrounding material, generating swarms of small earthquakes and causing the ground surface to inflate, both of which are reliable precursors to volcanic eruptions.

--- Lesson 2.5: Volcanoes & Hot Spots (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Shield Volcano
     A: A broad, gently sloping volcano built by fluid basaltic lava flows (e.g., Mauna Loa)

  2. Q: Composite Volcano
     A: A tall, steep volcano with alternating lava and ash layers; capable of explosive eruptions

  3. Q: Hot Spot
     A: A stationary zone of intense heat in the mantle that creates volcanoes independent of plate boundaries

  4. Q: Magma
     A: Molten rock beneath Earth's surface; called lava when it reaches the surface

  5. Q: Viscosity
     A: A measure of a fluid's resistance to flow; high-viscosity magma produces more explosive eruptions
