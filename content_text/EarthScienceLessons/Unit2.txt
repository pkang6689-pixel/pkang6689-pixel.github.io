--- Lesson 2.1: Continental Drift & Evidence (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Continental Drift & Evidence
Continental drift is the theory proposed by Alfred Wegener in 1912 that continents were once joined in a supercontinent called Pangaea and have since drifted apart.
  • Evidence:  • Jigsaw fit of coastlines (especially South America and Africa)  • Matching fossil distributions across now-separated continents (e.g., Glossopteris, Mesosaurus)  • Similar rock sequences and mountain chains on different continents  • Paleoclimate evidence: glacial deposits in tropical regions, coal in polar regions  • Wegener's theory was initially rejected because he couldn't explain the mechanism for how continents moved.

--- Lesson 2.2: Seafloor Spreading (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Seafloor Spreading
Seafloor spreading is the process by which new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges and moves outward.
  • Proposed by Harry Hess in 1962, it provided the mechanism Wegener's theory lacked.  • At mid-ocean ridges, magma rises from the mantle, solidifies, and pushes older crust outward on both sides.  • Evidence:  • Magnetic striping: alternating patterns of normal and reversed magnetic polarity in ocean floor rocks, symmetric about ridges  • Age of oceanic crust increases with distance from the ridge  • Sediment thickness increases with distance from the ridge  • Seafloor spreading explains why oceanic crust is young (

--- Lesson 2.3: Plate Boundaries (Convergent, Divergent, Transform) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Plate Boundaries (Convergent, Divergent, Transform)
Earth's lithosphere is divided into tectonic plates that interact at three types of plate boundaries:
  • Divergent: plates move apart. Magma rises to fill the gap, creating new crust. Examples: Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East African Rift.  • Convergent: plates collide.  • Oceanic–oceanic: denser plate subducts → deep-ocean trenches, volcanic island arcs  • Oceanic–continental: oceanic plate subducts → mountain ranges, volcanoes (Andes)  • Continental–continental: neither subducts → folded mountain ranges (Himalayas)  • Transform: plates slide horizontally past each other. Produces earthquakes but no volcanism. Example: San Andreas Fault.

--- Lesson 2.4: Earthquakes & Seismic Waves (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Earthquakes & Seismic Waves
An earthquake is a sudden release of energy in Earth's lithosphere caused by movement along faults.
  • Focus (hypocenter): the point underground where the earthquake originates  • Epicenter: the point on the surface directly above the focus  • Seismic waves:  • P-waves (primary): fastest, compressional, travel through solids and liquids  • S-waves (secondary): slower, shear motion, travel through solids only  • Surface waves: slowest but most destructive, travel along Earth's surface  • Richter scale (magnitude): measures energy released; each whole number = 10× amplitude, ~32× energy  • Most earthquakes occur along plate boundaries, especially the Ring of Fire around the Pacific.

--- Lesson 2.5: Volcanoes & Hot Spots (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Volcanoes & Hot Spots
Volcanoes form where magma reaches Earth's surface. They are associated with convergent boundaries, divergent boundaries, and hot spots.
  • Types of volcanoes:  • Shield volcanoes: broad, gently sloping; built by low-viscosity basaltic lava flows (e.g., Mauna Loa)  • Cinder cone: small, steep; built from ejected lava fragments  • Composite (stratovolcano): tall, steep; alternating layers of lava and ash; explosive eruptions (e.g., Mount St. Helens)  • Hot spots: volcanic areas over unusually hot zones in the mantle, not at plate boundaries. Plates move over the stationary hot spot, creating chains of volcanoes (e.g., Hawaiian Islands).  • Volcanic eruptions release gases (CO₂, SO₂, H₂O), ash, and lava—they can affect global climate.

    [ ] Produces no lava
    Explanation: Hot spots are fixed points of intense mantle heating not associated with plate boundaries; the plate moves over them, creating volcano chains.

  Q5. Mount St. Helens is an example of a:
    [ ] Shield volcano
    [ ] Cinder cone
    [*] Composite (stratovolcano)
    [ ] Hot spot
    Explanation: Mount St. Helens is a composite volcano in the Cascades, known for its catastrophic 1980 explosive eruption.

  Q6. High-viscosity magma tends to produce _____ eruptions.
    [ ] Gentle effusive
    [ ] No eruptions
    [*] Explosive
    [ ] Underwater only
    Explanation: High-viscosity magma traps dissolved gases; when pressure is released, the trapped gas expands violently, causing explosive eruptions.

  Q7. Volcanic eruptions can affect global climate by:
    [ ] Absorbing all sunlight permanently
    [ ] Creating new oceans
    [*] Releasing SO₂ and ash into the atmosphere, reflecting sunlight and cooling temperatures
    [ ] Having no atmospheric effect
    Explanation: Volcanic sulfur dioxide forms sulfate aerosols in the stratosphere that reflect sunlight, causing temporary global cooling.

  Q8. Shield volcanoes have _____ slopes and are formed by _____ lava.

    [*] Gentle, low-viscosity (runny basaltic lava that flows far before solidifying)

  Q9. Composite (stratovolcanoes) are formed by alternating layers of:

  Q11. The viscosity of lava determines:
    [ ] Its color

    [*] How explosive an eruption will be (high viscosity = trapped gas = more explosive)

  Q12. Silica content affects lava viscosity: high silica means:
    [ ] Runny lava

  Q13. Hot spots are volcanic areas caused by:
    [ ] Plate boundaries

  Q14. The Hawaiian Islands formed from:

--- Lesson 2.5: Volcanoes & Hot Spots (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Shield Volcano
     A: A broad, gently sloping volcano built by fluid basaltic lava flows (e.g., Mauna Loa)

  2. Q: Composite Volcano
     A: A tall, steep volcano with alternating lava and ash layers; capable of explosive eruptions

  3. Q: Hot Spot
     A: A stationary zone of intense heat in the mantle that creates volcanoes independent of plate boundaries

  4. Q: Magma
     A: Molten rock beneath Earth's surface; called lava when it reaches the surface

  5. Q: Viscosity
     A: A measure of a fluid's resistance to flow; high-viscosity magma produces more explosive eruptions
