--- Lesson 6.1: Climate vs. Weather (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Climate vs. Weather
Weather is the short-term state of the atmosphere at a specific place and time (temperature, precipitation, wind, humidity).

Climate is the average of weather conditions over a long period (usually 30+ years) for a region.
  • Weather changes day to day; climate represents long-term patterns and expectations.  • Climate is described by average temperature, precipitation, seasonal patterns, and extreme events.  • 'Climate is what you expect; weather is what you get.'

--- Lesson 6.2: Factors Affecting Climate (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Factors Affecting Climate
Several factors determine the climate of a region:
  • Latitude: the most important factor; higher latitudes receive less direct solar energy → cooler climates  • Altitude: temperature decreases ~6.5°C per 1,000 m elevation gain  • Ocean currents: warm currents (Gulf Stream) moderate coastal climates; cold currents cool and dry adjacent coasts  • Distance from the ocean (continentality): oceans moderate temperature; interiors have more extreme seasonal swings  • Topography: mountain ranges create rain shadow effects—moist air rises on the windward side (rain), descends as dry air on the leeward side (desert)  • Prevailing winds: determine moisture transport and precipitation patterns

--- Lesson 6.3: Climate Zones & Biomes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Climate Zones & Biomes
Earth's climates are classified into zones that correspond to distinct biomes (large ecological regions):
  • Tropical (0°–23.5°): warm year-round, high rainfall → tropical rainforests, savannas  • Dry/Arid: low precipitation → deserts, steppes  • Temperate (~23.5°–66.5°): moderate temperatures, distinct seasons → deciduous forests, Mediterranean  • Continental (interior, mid-latitudes): large seasonal temperature swings → prairies, boreal forests  • Polar (>66.5°): very cold, low precipitation → tundra, ice caps
The Köppen climate classification system uses temperature and precipitation data to define climate types (A=tropical, B=arid, C=temperate, D=continental, E=polar).

--- Lesson 6.4: The Greenhouse Effect (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Greenhouse Effect
The greenhouse effect is a natural process that warms Earth's surface:
  • Sunlight (short-wave radiation) passes through the atmosphere and warms Earth's surface.  • Earth re-emits energy as infrared (long-wave) radiation.  • Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, H₂O vapor, O₃) absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, trapping heat in the atmosphere.  • Without the greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be about -18°C instead of +15°C.  • The enhanced greenhouse effect: human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation, agriculture) increase greenhouse gas concentrations, trapping more heat and causing global warming.

--- Lesson 6.5: Evidence of Climate Change (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Evidence of Climate Change
Multiple independent lines of evidence confirm that global climate is changing:
  • Temperature records: global average has risen ~1.1°C since the late 1800s; 20 of the 21 warmest years on record have occurred since 2000  • Ice cores: air bubbles trapped in ancient ice reveal past CO₂ levels and temperatures over 800,000 years  • Sea-level rise: ~20 cm since 1900 due to thermal expansion and melting ice  • Glacial retreat: glaciers worldwide are shrinking  • Arctic sea ice decline: summer minimum extent has decreased ~40% since 1979  • Ocean acidification: CO₂ dissolving in seawater lowers pH  • Ecosystem changes: shifting species ranges, earlier spring events, coral bleaching

--- Lesson 6.5: Evidence of Climate Change (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Ice Core
     A: A cylinder of ice drilled from glaciers or ice sheets that contains trapped air bubbles recording past atmospheric conditions

  2. Q: Thermal Expansion
     A: The increase in water volume as temperature rises, contributing to sea-level rise

  3. Q: Ocean Acidification
     A: The decrease in ocean pH caused by absorption of atmospheric CO₂

  4. Q: Glacial Retreat
     A: The shrinking of glaciers due to more ice melting than accumulating each year

  5. Q: Proxy Data
     A: Indirect records of past climate (ice cores, tree rings, ocean sediments, pollen) used to reconstruct climate history

--- Lesson 6.6: Human Impact on Climate (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Human Impact on Climate
Human activities are the primary driver of current climate change:
  • Burning fossil fuels: coal, oil, natural gas combustion releases CO₂—the largest source of greenhouse gas emissions (~75%)  • Deforestation: removes carbon sinks (trees absorb CO₂); burning/decomposing trees release stored carbon  • Agriculture: livestock produce CH₄; rice paddies release CH₄; fertilizers release N₂O  • Industry: cement production, manufacturing processes release CO₂ and other gases
Mitigation strategies: renewable energy, energy efficiency, reforestation, carbon capture, international agreements (Paris Agreement), reducing consumption

Adaptation strategies: building sea walls, developing drought-resistant crops, improving warning systems, urban planning
