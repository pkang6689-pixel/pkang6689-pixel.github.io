--- Lesson 7.1: Ocean Floor Features (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ocean Floor Features
The ocean floor has distinct topographic features shaped by plate tectonics:
  • Continental shelf: shallow, gently sloping extension of the continent underwater (avg. depth ~130 m)  • Continental slope: steep drop-off from the shelf to the deep ocean  • Abyssal plain: vast, flat, deep ocean floor (3,000–6,000 m depth) covered in fine sediment  • Mid-ocean ridge: underwater mountain chain formed at divergent plate boundaries (longest mountain range on Earth)  • Ocean trench: deep, narrow depression at convergent boundaries (e.g., Mariana Trench ~11,000 m deep)  • Seamounts: underwater volcanic mountains; flat-topped ones called guyots

--- Lesson 7.2: Ocean Water Properties (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ocean Water Properties
Seawater's physical and chemical properties affect all marine processes:
  • Salinity: average ~35 parts per thousand (ppt); mainly NaCl. Varies with evaporation, precipitation, river input, and ice formation.  • Temperature: ranges from -2°C (polar) to 30°C (tropical surface). Decreases with depth; the thermocline is the zone of rapid temperature change.  • Density: depends on temperature (cold = denser) and salinity (saltier = denser). Drives deep ocean circulation.  • Dissolved gases: O₂ (essential for marine life) and CO₂ (affects pH). Colder water holds more dissolved gas.  • pH: normally ~8.1 (slightly alkaline); decreasing due to ocean acidification from absorbed CO₂.

--- Lesson 7.3: Ocean Currents & Circulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ocean Currents & Circulation
Ocean currents are large-scale movements of seawater driven by wind, density differences, and the Coriolis effect:
  • Surface currents (upper ~400 m): driven by prevailing winds. Form circular patterns called gyres—clockwise in Northern Hemisphere, counterclockwise in Southern.  • Deep currents (thermohaline circulation): driven by density differences from temperature and salinity. Cold, salty water sinks at polar regions and flows along the ocean floor toward the equator.  • Upwelling: cold, nutrient-rich deep water rises to the surface (often near coasts). Supports highly productive fisheries.  • The global conveyor belt: a connected system of surface and deep currents that circulates water, heat, and nutrients around the globe.

--- Lesson 7.4: Tides & Their Causes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Tides & Their Causes
Tides are the regular rise and fall of ocean water levels caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun:
  • The Moon has the greatest tidal influence (closer to Earth than the Sun).  • Tides produce two bulges: one facing the Moon (gravitational pull) and one on the opposite side (centrifugal effect).  • Spring tides: Sun and Moon aligned (new moon, full moon) → largest tidal range (highest high tides, lowest low tides)  • Neap tides: Sun and Moon at right angles (first/third quarter) → smallest tidal range  • Most coasts experience two high tides and two low tides per day (semidiurnal), though some have diurnal (one cycle) or mixed patterns.  • Tidal range varies with coastal geography—the Bay of Fundy has the world's largest tides (~16 m).

--- Lesson 7.5: Marine Ecosystems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Marine Ecosystems
Earth's oceans contain diverse ecosystems, each with unique conditions and organisms:
  • Coral reefs: warm, shallow tropical waters; built by coral polyps with symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae); high biodiversity ('rainforests of the sea')  • Estuaries: where rivers meet the ocean; brackish water; highly productive nursery habitats  • Kelp forests: temperate coastal waters; giant kelp provides habitat for hundreds of species  • Open ocean (pelagic zone): vast but relatively low productivity; phytoplankton are the primary producers  • Deep-sea ecosystems: dark, cold, high pressure; organisms rely on marine snow or chemosynthesis at hydrothermal vents  • Intertidal zone: coastline exposed between tides; organisms must tolerate air exposure, wave action, and salinity changes

    [ ] Solar energy
    [ ] Wind power
    [*] Chemosynthesis
    [ ] Bioluminescence for food
    Explanation: Chemosynthetic bacteria at vents convert hydrogen sulfide and other chemicals into energy, forming the base of these sunless ecosystems.

  Q4. The primary producers in the open ocean are:
    [ ] Fish
    [ ] Seaweed
    [*] Phytoplankton
    [ ] Jellyfish
    Explanation: Phytoplankton are microscopic photosynthesizers that produce roughly half of Earth's oxygen and form the base of ocean food webs.

  Q5. Intertidal organisms must adapt to:
    [ ] Constant deep water
    [*] Changing conditions: exposure to air, waves, temperature shifts, and salinity changes
    [ ] Extreme darkness
    [ ] Very high pressure
    Explanation: The intertidal zone alternates between being submerged and exposed, requiring organisms to tolerate dramatic environmental changes.

  Q6. Kelp forests are primarily found in:
    [ ] Tropical waters
    [ ] Deep ocean
    [*] Cool, nutrient-rich temperate coastal waters
    [ ] Freshwater lakes
    Explanation: Giant kelp thrives in cold, nutrient-rich waters along temperate coasts, forming underwater forests that shelter diverse marine life.

  Q7. Zooxanthellae are important to coral reefs because:
    [ ] They prey on coral
    [ ] They build the reef structure
    [*] They are symbiotic algae that provide corals with nutrients through photosynthesis
    [ ] They filter ocean water
    Explanation: Zooxanthellae live inside coral tissues, photosynthesizing and providing up to 90% of the coral's energy needs.

  Q8. The photic zone extends from the surface to about _____ meters.
    [ ] 10
    [ ] 50
    [*] 200 (where enough sunlight penetrates for photosynthesis)
    [ ] 1,000
    Explanation: Light zone depth.

  Q9. The aphotic zone is:
    [ ] Well-lit
    [*] The dark zone below ~200 m where no significant sunlight penetrates — organisms rely on other energy sources
    [ ] The surface
    [ ] The intertidal zone
    Explanation: Dark zone.

  Q10. Phytoplankton are important because they:
    [ ] Are large fish
    [*] Produce approximately 50% of Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis (base of ocean food web)
    [ ] Only feed whales
    [ ] Live on land
    Explanation: Oxygen producers.

  Q11. Coral reefs are sometimes called the 'rainforests of the sea' because of their:
    [ ] Location
    [ ] Size
    [*] Extremely high biodiversity (supporting ~25% of all marine species despite covering <1% of ocean floor)
    [ ] Rainfall
    Explanation: Biodiversity hotspots.

  Q12. Coral reefs require _____ water temperatures and _____ water.
    [ ] Cold, deep
    [*] Warm (20-30°C), clear/shallow (sunlight for zooxanthellae algae photosynthesis)
    [ ] Any temperature, murky
    [ ] Hot, acidic
    Explanation: Coral requirements.

  Q13. Kelp forests are found in:
    [ ] Tropical waters
    [*] Cool, nutrient-rich coastal waters (providing habitat comparable to terrestrial forests)
    [ ] The deep sea
    [ ] Freshwater lakes
    Explanation: Temperate marine.

  Q14. Hydrothermal vent communities get energy from:
    [ ] Sunlight
    [*] Chemosynthesis (bacteria convert chemicals like hydrogen sulfide into energy — no sunlight needed)
    [ ] Wind
    [ ] Ocean currents
    Explanation: Chemical energy.

  Q15. The deep sea covers _____ of Earth's surface, making it the largest habitat.
    [ ] 10%
    [ ] 30%
    [*] ~65% (vast, cold, dark, high-pressure environment that remains largely unexplored)
    [ ] 90%
    Explanation: Largest habitat.

  Q16. An estuary is where:
    [ ] Two oceans meet
    [*] A river meets the sea (mixing fresh and salt water — extremely productive ecosystems, nurseries for many species)
    [ ] Deep currents form
    [ ] Volcanic vents exist
    Explanation: Freshwater-saltwater mixing.

  Q17. Mangrove forests protect coastlines by:
    [ ] Blocking sunlight
    [*] Reducing wave energy, trapping sediment, preventing erosion, and serving as nursery habitat for fish and shellfish
    [ ] Increasing waves
    [ ] Cooling water
    Explanation: Coastal protection.

  Q18. Overfishing threatens marine ecosystems by:
    [ ] Adding more fish
    [*] Removing species faster than they can reproduce, disrupting food webs and potentially causing ecosystem collapse
    [ ] Having no effect
    [ ] Only reducing diversity slightly
    Explanation: Fishery collapse risk.

  Q19. Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) help ecosystems by:
    [ ] Allowing unlimited fishing
    [*] Restricting human activity to allow populations and habitats to recover
    [ ] Only helping tourism
    [ ] Only limiting pollution
    Explanation: Conservation tool.

  Q20. Ocean plastic pollution affects marine life through:
    [ ] No significant impact
    [*] Ingestion (mistaken for food), entanglement, habitat damage, and microplastics entering the food chain
    [ ] Only visual pollution
    [ ] Only beach litter
    Explanation: Plastic threat.

--- Lesson 7.5: Marine Ecosystems (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Coral Reef
     A: A marine ecosystem built by coral polyps in warm, shallow tropical water; extremely high biodiversity

  2. Q: Estuary
     A: A partially enclosed coastal area where fresh river water mixes with saltwater; highly productive

  3. Q: Kelp Forest
     A: A temperate marine ecosystem dominated by large brown algae (kelp) that provides habitat for many species

  4. Q: Phytoplankton
     A: Microscopic photosynthetic organisms that are the base of most marine food webs

  5. Q: Chemosynthesis
     A: The production of energy by organisms using chemical reactions rather than sunlight, found at hydrothermal vents
