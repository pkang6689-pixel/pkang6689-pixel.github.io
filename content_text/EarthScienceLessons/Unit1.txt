--- Lesson 1.1: Earth's Layers (Crust, Mantle, Core) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Earth's Layers (Crust, Mantle, Core)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the physical state of Earth's outer core?
The outer core is liquid, composed mainly of molten iron and nickel, and its convection currents generate Earth's magnetic field.
What is the asthenosphere?
The asthenosphere is a weak, partially molten layer in the upper mantle whose ductile nature allows the rigid lithospheric plates above it to slide and move.
What is the Mohorovicic discontinuity (Moho)?
The Moho is the boundary between Earth's crust and mantle, identified by a sharp increase in seismic wave velocity due to the denser mantle rock below.
What is the geothermal gradient?
The geothermal gradient is the rate of temperature increase with depth, averaging about 25 to 30 degrees Celsius per kilometer in the crust.
What is the shadow zone in seismology?
The shadow zone is a region on Earth's surface (between about 104 and 140 degrees from an earthquake) where seismic waves are absent because the liquid outer core refracts P-waves and blocks S-waves.
What is the mesosphere in the context of Earth's interior?
In Earth's interior, the mesosphere (or lower mantle) is the strong, rigid layer of the mantle that extends from below the asthenosphere to the core-mantle boundary.
A mining company encounters increasingly hot conditions as they dig deeper into the Earth. At 3 km depth the rock temperature is 90 degrees Celsius above the surface temperature. A worker asks why. What is the best explanation?
The geothermal gradient, driven by residual heat from Earth's formation and radioactive decay, causes temperatures to rise about 25 to 30 degrees Celsius per kilometer of depth in the crust.
Real-World Applications
Example: The mantle makes up approximately what percentage of Earth's volume?
The mantle accounts for approximately 84% of Earth's total volume, extending from about 30 km below the surface to a depth of roughly 2,900 km.

--- Lesson 1.2: Mineral Identification (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mineral Identification
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the hardest mineral on the Mohs scale?
Diamond is rated 10 on the Mohs Hardness Scale, making it the hardest naturally occurring mineral known, capable of scratching all other minerals.
What is the most unreliable physical property for identifying minerals?
Color is the most unreliable property because many different minerals share the same color, and impurities can cause the same mineral to appear in a wide range of colors.
What is luster?
Luster describes the quality and intensity of light reflected from a mineral's surface, with major categories including metallic (like a metal) and non-metallic (glassy, waxy, earthy, silky, etc.).
What is cleavage in mineral identification?
Cleavage is the tendency of a mineral to break along flat, even surfaces that correspond to planes of weaker bonding in the crystal structure, producing smooth faces at consistent angles.
What is fracture?
Fracture describes how a mineral breaks when it does not follow cleavage planes, producing irregular surfaces such as conchoidal (curved, shell-like), splintery, or uneven breaks.
What is specific gravity?
Specific gravity is a dimensionless ratio comparing a mineral's density to that of water, helping to identify minerals by how heavy they feel relative to their size.
What is streak in mineral identification?
Streak is the color of the fine powder left behind when a mineral is rubbed across an unglazed porcelain streak plate, and it is more consistent than the mineral's external color.
What is a polymorph?
Polymorphs are minerals that share the same chemical composition but have different crystal structures, resulting in very different physical properties, as seen with diamond and graphite, both pure carbon.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is cleavage in mineral identification?
Cleavage is the tendency of a mineral to break along flat, even surfaces that correspond to planes of weaker bonding in the crystal structure, producing smooth faces at consistent angles.
Example: A geology student finds a mineral specimen that breaks into thin, flat sheets. Her instructor tells her this indicates the mineral has excellent cleavage in one direction. Which mineral is she most likely holding?
Mica minerals like muscovite have perfect basal cleavage in one direction, causing them to peel apart into thin, flat, flexible sheets, a property called perfect one-directional cleavage.

--- Lesson 1.4: The Rock Cycle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Rock Cycle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lithification?
Lithification is the process by which loose, unconsolidated sediments are transformed into solid sedimentary rock, primarily through the combined effects of compaction and cementation.
What is crystallization in the rock cycle?
Crystallization occurs when magma or lava cools and the atoms organize into orderly mineral crystal structures, forming the interlocking grain texture characteristic of igneous rocks.
What is 'uplift' in the context of the rock cycle?
Uplift is the tectonic process that raises rocks from depth to the surface through forces like mountain building, fault movement, or isostatic rebound, exposing them to surface weathering.
A mining company extracts marble from a quarry. A geologist explains that the marble was once a coral reef limestone that was buried and transformed. A new worker asks if the marble could ever return to being limestone. What is the correct response?
In the rock cycle, marble can be weathered and dissolved, and the calcium carbonate can be redeposited in water, eventually lithifying into new limestone, demonstrating the cycle's interconnected nature.
A coastal town notices that cliff faces are rapidly eroding after a series of storms. A geologist explains that this erosion is part of the rock cycle. A town council member asks where the eroded material goes. What is the most accurate answer?
Eroded cliff material is transported as sediment by waves and currents to the ocean floor, where it accumulates in layers and may eventually be compacted and cemented into new sedimentary rock.
Real-World Applications
Example: A farmer notices that the soil on her hillside field washes away during heavy rains, exposing bare rock. A conservation agent explains that vegetation removal has accelerated erosion. In the rock cycle, what happens to the washed-away sediment next?
Eroded sediment is transported by water to lower elevations (rivers, lakes, oceans) where it is deposited and may eventually undergo compaction and cementation to become new sedimentary rock.
Example: A student argues that the rock cycle takes millions of years, so it is not relevant to human timescales. Her teacher disagrees. Which example best shows the rock cycle operating on human-observable timescales?
Volcanic eruptions produce new igneous rock in hours to days, and that rock immediately begins weathering, demonstrating that parts of the rock cycle are observable on human timescales.

--- Lesson 1.5: Soil Formation & Composition (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Soil Formation & Composition
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is humus?
Humus is the dark, stable organic material formed from the decomposition of plant and animal matter, and it is essential for soil fertility, water retention, and maintaining good soil structure.
What is the O horizon?
The O horizon is the uppermost soil layer, consisting primarily of fresh and decomposing organic litter (leaves, twigs, dead organisms) that has not yet been fully incorporated into the mineral soil.
What is the C horizon?
The C horizon consists of partially weathered parent material, with broken bedrock fragments that are beginning to weather but have not yet been transformed into true soil.
What is soil texture?
Soil texture refers to the relative proportions of sand, silt, and clay-sized particles in a soil, which determines its drainage, nutrient-holding capacity, and workability for agriculture.
What is permafrost?
Permafrost is ground that stays at or below 0 degrees Celsius for at least two consecutive years, found primarily in polar and high-altitude regions, and it plays a major role in Arctic ecosystems and climate.
A soil scientist studying a cross-section of soil in a temperate forest identifies a dark layer at the top, a lighter layer beneath it, and a reddish layer below that. She labels these as O, A, and B horizons respectively. A student asks why the B horizon is reddish. What is the best explanation?
The reddish color of the B horizon is caused by iron oxides (rust) that were dissolved in water, leached from the upper A horizon, and deposited in the B horizon through the process of illuviation.
A student comparing two soil samples notices that Sample A from a tropical rainforest is thin and nutrient-poor despite heavy vegetation, while Sample B from a temperate grassland is thick and nutrient-rich. She asks why the rainforest soil is so poor. What is the best explanation?
In tropical rainforests, intense heat and heavy rainfall cause rapid decomposition and aggressive leaching, washing nutrients deep below root zones, so most nutrients are cycled through the living biomass rather than stored in the soil.
A team of archaeologists excavating a site finds a thick, dark A horizon with abundant artifacts. They are able to date different layers within the soil profile. A student asks how soil horizons can be used as a timeline. What is the correct explanation?
Soil horizons develop over time with newer material accumulating on top, so deeper layers generally represent older periods, allowing archaeologists to use soil stratigraphy as a relative dating tool.
A geologist studying soil development on a recently deposited volcanic lava flow finds only a thin layer of moss and lichen on bare rock after 50 years. She explains that soil formation from scratch is extremely slow. What is this initial stage of soil development on bare rock called, and why is it so slow?
Primary succession on bare rock begins with pioneer organisms like lichens that slowly break down the rock surface through chemical and physical weathering, creating the first thin layer of soil over decades to centuries.
Real-World Applications
Example: A landscape architect designing a rain garden to manage stormwater runoff needs soil that drains quickly but still supports plant growth. She has access to pure sand, pure clay, and loam. Which soil should she select for the rain garden?
Loam is the best choice because its balanced mix of sand, silt, and clay provides adequate drainage to handle stormwater while retaining enough moisture and nutrients to sustain the rain garden plants.
