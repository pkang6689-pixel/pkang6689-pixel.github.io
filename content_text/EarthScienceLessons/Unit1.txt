--- Lesson 1.1: Earth's Layers (Crust, Mantle, Core) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Earth's Layers (Crust, Mantle, Core)
Earth has four main layers, each with distinct properties:
  • Crust: the thin, rocky outer layer (5–70 km thick). Oceanic crust is denser (basalt); continental crust is thicker and less dense (granite).  • Mantle: the thickest layer (~2,900 km). The upper mantle contains the partially molten asthenosphere, which allows tectonic plates to move.  • Outer Core: liquid iron and nickel (~2,200 km thick). Convection here generates Earth's magnetic field.  • Inner Core: solid iron and nickel (~1,200 km radius), kept solid by immense pressure despite extreme temperatures (~5,400 °C).
Scientists study Earth's interior using seismic waves—P-waves travel through solids and liquids; S-waves travel only through solids.

--- Lesson 1.2: Mineral Identification (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mineral Identification
Minerals are naturally occurring, inorganic solids with a definite chemical composition and crystalline structure.
  • Hardness: resistance to scratching, measured by Mohs Hardness Scale (1 = talc, 10 = diamond)  • Luster: how a mineral reflects light (metallic vs. non-metallic: glassy, waxy, pearly, earthy)  • Streak: the color of a mineral's powder when rubbed on an unglazed porcelain plate; more reliable than surface color  • Cleavage: tendency to break along flat planes of weakness  • Fracture: irregular breaking pattern (conchoidal, fibrous, etc.)  • Specific gravity: ratio of a mineral's density to water's density

--- Lesson 1.3: Types of Rocks (Igneous, Sedimentary, Metamorphic) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Rocks (Igneous, Sedimentary, Metamorphic)
All rocks belong to one of three major categories based on how they form:
  • Igneous rocks form from the cooling and solidification of magma or lava.  • Intrusive (plutonic): cool slowly underground → large crystals (e.g., granite)  • Extrusive (volcanic): cool quickly at the surface → small/no crystals (e.g., basalt, obsidian)  • Sedimentary rocks form from compaction and cementation of sediments, or from chemical/biological processes.  • Clastic: made of rock fragments (sandstone, shale)  • Chemical: precipitated from solution (limestone, rock salt)  • Organic: from remains of organisms (coal, some limestone)  • Metamorphic rocks form when existing rocks are transformed by heat and/or pressure without melting.  • Foliated: layered appearance (slate, gneiss)  • Non-foliated: no layers (marble, quartzite)

--- Lesson 1.4: The Rock Cycle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Rock Cycle
The rock cycle describes how rocks continuously transform from one type to another through geological processes:
  • Igneous → Sedimentary: weathering and erosion break igneous rock into sediments, which are deposited, compacted, and cemented.  • Sedimentary → Metamorphic: burial increases heat and pressure, transforming sedimentary rock.  • Metamorphic → Igneous: further heat causes melting to magma, which cools into igneous rock.  • Any rock type can become any other type—the cycle has no fixed starting point.  • Uplift exposes buried rocks to weathering; subduction pulls surface rocks deep into the mantle.
The rock cycle is driven by Earth's internal heat (plate tectonics, volcanism) and external energy from the Sun (weathering, erosion).

--- Lesson 1.5: Soil Formation & Composition (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Soil Formation & Composition
Soil is a mixture of weathered rock (minerals), organic matter (humus), water, and air.
  • Soil formation (pedogenesis) depends on five factors: parent material, climate, topography, biological activity, and time.  • Soil horizons (layers from surface to bedrock):  • O horizon: organic matter (leaf litter, humus)  • A horizon (topsoil): rich in organic matter and minerals; most fertile  • B horizon (subsoil): accumulates minerals leached from above (clay, iron oxides)  • C horizon: partially weathered parent material  • R horizon: unweathered bedrock  • Soil texture depends on proportions of sand (largest), silt, and clay (smallest) particles. Loam—a balanced mix—is ideal for agriculture.

--- Lesson 1.5: Soil Formation & Composition (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Humus
     A: Dark, nutrient-rich organic material in soil formed from decomposed plant and animal matter

  2. Q: Soil Horizon
     A: A distinct horizontal layer of soil with characteristic color, texture, and composition

  3. Q: Topsoil (A Horizon)
     A: The uppermost mineral soil layer, rich in organic matter and most important for plant growth

  4. Q: Pedogenesis
     A: The process of soil formation from the weathering of rock and accumulation of organic material

  5. Q: Loam
     A: A soil texture with a balanced mixture of sand, silt, and clay; ideal for agriculture
