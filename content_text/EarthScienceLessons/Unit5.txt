--- Lesson 5.1: Composition & Layers of the Atmosphere (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Composition & Layers of the Atmosphere
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the troposphere?
The troposphere is the lowest atmospheric layer, extending from Earth's surface to roughly 12 km altitude, and it contains about 75% of the atmosphere's mass and all weather.
What is the stratosphere?
The stratosphere extends from about 12 to 50 km altitude and contains the ozone layer, which absorbs UV radiation and causes temperature to increase with height.
What is the tropopause?
The tropopause is the boundary layer between the troposphere and stratosphere, marking the altitude where the temperature trend reverses from decreasing to increasing.
What is the exosphere?
The exosphere is the outermost layer of Earth's atmosphere where gas molecules are so spread out they can escape Earth's gravity, gradually merging into space.
What is the mesosphere?
The mesosphere extends from about 50 to 85 km altitude and is where most meteors burn up due to friction with gas molecules, reaching the coldest temperatures in the atmosphere.
What is the thermosphere?
The thermosphere extends from about 85 to 600 km altitude with temperatures exceeding 1,000 degrees Celsius, but the air is so thin it would not feel warm to a person.
A shooting star streaks across the night sky. A student asks which atmospheric layer the meteor is burning up in and why it burns there. What is the correct explanation?
Meteors burn up in the mesosphere (50-85 km altitude) because the air, though thin, is dense enough to create intense frictional heating as the meteoroid enters at high speed.
Real-World Applications
Example: Carbon dioxide makes up approximately what percentage of the atmosphere?
CO₂ is about 0.04% (over 400 ppm) of the atmosphere, which is a small percentage but is significant because of its role as a greenhouse gas.
Example: A hiker notices that the temperature drops noticeably as she climbs a 3,000-meter mountain. She started at 20 degrees C at the base. Using the average lapse rate, what temperature should she expect at the summit?
At the average lapse rate of 6.5 degrees C per 1,000 m, a 3,000-meter climb would result in a temperature drop of about 19.5 degrees C, giving roughly 0.5 degrees C at the summit.
Example: A researcher at a weather station on a high mountain finds it difficult to breathe and must use supplemental oxygen. Based on what you know about the atmosphere, why does this happen even though the percentage of oxygen in the air is still about 21%?
At high altitudes, atmospheric pressure is much lower, so while oxygen is still 21% of the air, there are far fewer total molecules per breath, resulting in less oxygen intake.
Example: A space agency is designing a satellite to orbit Earth. Engineers choose an altitude of about 400 km. In which atmospheric layer would this satellite orbit, and why would it still experience a tiny amount of drag despite being so high?
At 400 km, the satellite orbits in the thermosphere where gas molecules are extremely spread out, but the few molecules present still create enough drag to gradually slow the satellite over months.

--- Lesson 5.2: Energy Transfer (Radiation, Conduction, Convection) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy Transfer (Radiation, Conduction, Convection)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is radiation in the context of heat transfer?
Radiation is the transfer of energy via electromagnetic waves, such as visible light and infrared radiation, and is unique because it does not require a medium to travel through.
What is conduction?
Conduction transfers heat through direct contact between molecules, such as when your hand touches a hot pan handle or when air molecules contact the warm ground.
What is convection?
Convection is the transfer of heat through the bulk movement of fluids (liquids or gases), driven by density differences caused by temperature variations.
What is albedo?
Albedo measures a surface's reflectivity on a scale from 0 to 1, where fresh snow has a high albedo (about 0.8) and dark ocean water has a low albedo (about 0.06).
What is infrared radiation?
Infrared radiation is electromagnetic energy with wavelengths longer than visible light, emitted by all warm objects including Earth's surface, and is the type of radiation trapped by greenhouse gases.
What is a convection cell?
A convection cell is a circular flow pattern in a fluid where heating causes rising motion, horizontal flow at the top, cooling and sinking, and return flow at the bottom.

Rising greenhouse gas levels absorb more of the outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit it back toward the surface, creating an energy imbalance that causes global warming.

--- Lesson 5.3: Air Pressure, Wind & Coriolis Effect (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Air Pressure, Wind & Coriolis Effect
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Coriolis effect?
The Coriolis effect is the apparent deflection of moving objects, including air and water, caused by Earth's rotation, deflecting them right in the Northern Hemisphere and left in the Southern.
What is the pressure gradient force?
The pressure gradient force is the initial force that causes air to move from high to low pressure areas, and greater pressure differences produce stronger winds.
What is an isobar?
An isobar is a contour line drawn on a weather map connecting locations that have the same atmospheric pressure, and their spacing indicates wind speed.
What is a jet stream?
Jet streams are fast-flowing, narrow air currents at high altitudes, typically near the tropopause at about 10 km, with speeds reaching 100-400 km/h, that guide weather patterns.
What is a sea breeze?
A sea breeze develops during the day when land heats faster than water, causing warm air to rise over land and drawing cooler air in from the sea to replace it.
What is a land breeze?
At night, land cools faster than water, so air rises over the relatively warmer sea and cooler air flows from the land to replace it, creating a land breeze.
What is a global wind belt?
Global wind belts are large-scale atmospheric circulation patterns divided into three zones per hemisphere: trade winds (0-30 degrees), westerlies (30-60 degrees), and polar easterlies (60-90 degrees).
A hurricane in the Northern Hemisphere spirals counterclockwise when viewed from above. A student asks why hurricanes spin in that direction. What is the correct explanation?
As air rushes toward the low-pressure center of a hurricane, the Coriolis effect deflects it to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, resulting in counterclockwise rotation.
A meteorologist tracks a low-pressure system approaching a city and predicts worsening weather. A friend asks why low pressure means bad weather while high pressure means fair weather. What is the best explanation?
Low pressure draws air inward and upward, where it cools and water vapor condenses into clouds and precipitation; high pressure forces air downward, warming it and preventing cloud formation.

--- Lesson 5.4: Cloud Types & Precipitation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cloud Types & Precipitation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the dew point?
The dew point is the temperature at which air reaches 100% relative humidity and water vapor begins to condense into liquid droplets or ice crystals.
What is a condensation nucleus?
Condensation nuclei are microscopic particles suspended in the atmosphere that provide surfaces for water vapor to condense upon, which is essential for cloud formation.
What is relative humidity?
Relative humidity is the ratio of the actual water vapor content in air to the maximum it could hold at that temperature, expressed as a percentage; 100% means the air is saturated.
What is precipitation?
Precipitation encompasses all forms of water that fall from the atmosphere to the surface, driven by gravity once cloud droplets or ice crystals become large enough.
What is a nimbostratus cloud?
Nimbostratus clouds are dense, gray cloud layers that block sunlight and produce continuous, moderate-to-heavy precipitation over large areas, often lasting several hours.
What is freezing rain?
Freezing rain occurs when supercooled liquid raindrops fall through a thin cold layer and freeze on contact with cold surfaces, creating a dangerous glaze of ice.
What is the rain shadow effect?
As moist air rises over a mountain, it cools and drops its moisture on the windward side; the air descending the leeward side is dry, creating an arid rain shadow zone.
What is a cirrostratus cloud?
Cirrostratus clouds are thin, transparent ice-crystal sheets at high altitudes that often produce a halo around the Sun or Moon and may signal an approaching warm front.
Real-World Applications
Example: A car insurance company reports that ice storm damage claims spike when the surface temperature is just below freezing and a warm layer exists above. What type of precipitation creates the most dangerous driving conditions and why?
Freezing rain is especially hazardous because it falls as liquid but instantly freezes on contact with cold surfaces, creating a transparent, slippery ice glaze that is difficult to see.

--- Lesson 5.5: Weather Fronts & Storms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Weather Fronts & Storms
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a weather front?
A weather front is the boundary zone where two air masses with different properties (temperature, humidity, density) meet, often producing clouds and precipitation.
What is a tornado?
A tornado is a violently rotating funnel of air that extends from a thunderstorm cloud to the ground, capable of producing winds exceeding 480 km/h in extreme cases.
What is storm surge?
Storm surge is a dome of water pushed ashore by hurricane winds and low pressure, often the most deadly hazard of hurricanes, capable of flooding coastal areas with several meters of water.
What is an air mass?
An air mass is a large volume of air that acquires consistent temperature and moisture properties from the surface it forms over, such as tropical ocean (warm, moist) or polar land (cold, dry).
What is a mid-latitude cyclone?
A mid-latitude cyclone is a large-scale low-pressure weather system in the 30-60 degree latitude belt where contrasting air masses interact along fronts, producing much of the changeable weather.
What is a blizzard?
A blizzard is officially defined by sustained or frequent winds of 56 km/h or more with considerable blowing snow that reduces visibility to less than 400 meters for at least three hours.
What is a mesocyclone?
A mesocyclone is a region of rotation within a supercell thunderstorm, typically 3-10 km in diameter, that can tighten and descend to produce a tornado.
A weather station reports that a hurricane was Category 4 over open ocean but weakened to a tropical storm within hours of making landfall. What is the primary reason hurricanes weaken after moving over land?
Hurricanes are heat engines fueled by warm ocean water; when they move over land, they lose their energy source and the increased surface friction from terrain disrupts the circulation.
A meteorologist explains that a large mid-latitude cyclone forming over the Midwest involves a cold front, a warm front, and an occluded front. A student asks what happens when the occluded front forms. What is the best answer?
An occluded front forms when the cold front overtakes the warm front, lifting all warm air aloft, which typically marks the mature-to-dissipating stage of the mid-latitude cyclone's life cycle.
Real-World Applications
Example: A family vacationing on a Gulf Coast beach is told to evacuate as a Category 3 hurricane approaches. The main danger the emergency manager warns about is not the wind, but flooding from storm surge. Why is storm surge often deadlier than wind?
Storm surge is historically the deadliest hurricane hazard because it can push several meters of ocean water far inland in minutes, flooding everything in low-lying coastal areas.
Example: A community along a river valley experiences a flash flood after a cluster of slow-moving thunderstorms dumped 15 cm of rain in two hours. Emergency managers note that the storms kept forming over the same area. What atmospheric process causes thunderstorms to repeatedly form over the same location?
Training thunderstorms occur when storm cells repeatedly form and move over the same area like train cars on a track, causing extreme rainfall totals and dangerous flash flooding.
