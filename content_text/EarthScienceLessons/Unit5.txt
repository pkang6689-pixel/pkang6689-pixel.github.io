--- Lesson 5.1: Composition & Layers of the Atmosphere (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Composition & Layers of the Atmosphere
Earth's atmosphere is a thin layer of gases essential for life:
  • Composition: 78% N₂, 21% O₂, ~1% Ar, ~0.04% CO₂, plus water vapor and trace gases  • Layers (from surface upward):  • Troposphere (0–12 km): weather occurs here; temperature decreases with altitude  • Stratosphere (12–50 km): contains the ozone layer; temperature increases  • Mesosphere (50–80 km): coldest layer; meteors burn up here  • Thermosphere (80–700 km): very hot but thin; auroras occur here; includes the ionosphere  • Exosphere (>700 km): outermost layer; transitions into space

--- Lesson 5.2: Energy Transfer (Radiation, Conduction, Convection) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy Transfer (Radiation, Conduction, Convection)
Energy from the Sun drives Earth's weather and climate through three heat transfer mechanisms:
  • Radiation: transfer of energy through electromagnetic waves; how solar energy reaches Earth through the vacuum of space  • Conduction: transfer of heat through direct contact between molecules; heats the air directly touching warm surfaces  • Convection: transfer of heat through the movement of fluids (liquids and gases); warm air rises, cool air sinks, creating convection cells that drive wind and weather
Earth's energy budget: ~30% of incoming solar radiation is reflected (albedo), ~70% is absorbed by the surface and atmosphere. Earth re-radiates energy as infrared radiation, some of which is trapped by greenhouse gases.

--- Lesson 5.3: Air Pressure, Wind & Coriolis Effect (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Air Pressure, Wind & Coriolis Effect
Air pressure is the weight of the atmosphere pressing down on a surface. It decreases with altitude.
  • Wind blows from high pressure to low pressure areas.  • The pressure gradient force drives wind speed—the steeper the gradient, the faster the wind.  • The Coriolis effect: Earth's rotation deflects moving air to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.  • Global wind patterns:  • Trade winds: 0°–30° latitude (easterlies)  • Westerlies: 30°–60° latitude  • Polar easterlies: 60°–90° latitude  • Jet streams: narrow bands of fast-moving air in the upper troposphere that influence weather patterns.

--- Lesson 5.4: Cloud Types & Precipitation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cloud Types & Precipitation
Clouds form when air rises, cools to its dew point, and water vapor condenses on tiny particles (condensation nuclei).
  • Cloud types by altitude:  • High clouds (>6 km): cirrus, cirrostratus, cirrocumulus — thin, wispy, made of ice crystals  • Middle clouds (2–6 km): altostratus, altocumulus — gray or white sheets or layers  • Low clouds (  • Vertical development: cumulus (fair weather) → cumulonimbus (thunderstorms; extend through all levels)  • Precipitation forms: rain, snow, sleet, freezing rain, hail  • Precipitation occurs when cloud droplets grow large enough to fall — through collision-coalescence (warm clouds) or the Bergeron process (cold clouds with ice + supercooled water).

--- Lesson 5.5: Weather Fronts & Storms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Weather Fronts & Storms
A front is the boundary between two air masses of different temperature and humidity.
  • Cold front: cold air pushes under warm air, forcing it up rapidly → towering clouds, heavy rain/thunderstorms, then clearing and cooler temperatures  • Warm front: warm air slides over retreating cold air → gradual cloud buildup, steady light precipitation  • Stationary front: neither air mass advances → prolonged cloudiness and precipitation  • Occluded front: a cold front overtakes a warm front → complex weather, precipitation
Storms:
  • Thunderstorms: caused by strong convection; produce lightning, heavy rain, wind, sometimes hail and tornadoes  • Hurricanes (tropical cyclones): form over warm ocean water (≥26.5°C); categories 1–5 on Saffir-Simpson scale  • Tornadoes: violently rotating columns of air; measured on Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale

    [ ] Severe thunderstorms and quick clearing
    [*] Prolonged cloudiness and precipitation in one area
    [ ] Tornadoes
    [ ] Clear skies
    Explanation: Since neither air mass is advancing, the front stalls, keeping clouds and precipitation over the same area for days.

  Q4. Tornadoes are measured on the:
    [ ] Richter scale
    [ ] Saffir-Simpson scale
    [ ] Mohs scale
    [*] Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale
    Explanation: The EF scale rates tornadoes 0–5 based on estimated wind speeds and observed damage.

  Q5. At a warm front, precipitation is generally:
    [ ] Heavy and brief
    [*] Light and steady over a wide area
    [ ] Absent
    [ ] Only snow
    Explanation: Warm air gently slides over cold air, producing a gradual buildup of clouds and widespread, light steady precipitation.

  Q6. Lightning forms in thunderstorms due to:
    [ ] Wind friction at the ground
    [*] Build-up and discharge of electrical charge within cumulonimbus clouds
    [ ] Solar radiation
    [ ] Cold fronts only
    Explanation: Charge separation occurs within towering cumulonimbus clouds; lightning is the discharge that equalizes the charge difference.

  Q7. An occluded front forms when:
    [ ] A front stops moving
    [ ] Two warm fronts meet
    [*] A cold front overtakes a warm front
    [ ] A hurricane makes landfall
    Explanation: In an occluded front, a faster-moving cold front catches up to a warm front, lifting the warm air mass off the ground entirely.

  Q8. A weather front is the boundary between:
    [ ] Land and water
    [*] Two different air masses (differing in temperature and/or humidity)
    [ ] Day and night
    [ ] Two cloud types
    Explanation: Air mass boundary.

  Q9. A cold front occurs when:
    [ ] Warm air pushes under cold air
    [*] A cold air mass advances and forces warm air upward rapidly — often causing thunderstorms
    [ ] Two air masses stop
    [ ] No air moves
    Explanation: Cold advances.

  Q10. A warm front occurs when:
    [ ] Cold air advances
    [*] A warm air mass advances and slides up over a retreating cold air mass — often causing steady rain
    [ ] Air masses stay still
    [ ] Wind stops
    Explanation: Warm advances.

  Q11. Cold fronts typically produce _____ weather than warm fronts.
    [ ] Calmer
    [*] More intense but shorter-duration (steep front → rapid lifting → thunderstorms, then clearing)
    [ ] Longer-lasting
    [ ] The same
    Explanation: Intensity difference.

  Q12. A stationary front occurs when:
    [ ] Fronts move rapidly
    [*] Neither air mass advances — the boundary stays in one place, often causing days of cloudy, rainy weather
    [ ] Only cold air moves
    [ ] Only warm air moves
    Explanation: Stalled boundary.

  Q13. An occluded front forms when:
    [ ] Two warm fronts merge
    [*] A faster-moving cold front overtakes a warm front, lifting the warm air completely off the ground
    [ ] Two cold fronts merge
    [ ] Stationary fronts move
    Explanation: Complex front.

  Q14. Thunderstorms require three conditions:
    [ ] Only heat
    [*] Moisture, instability (rapidly rising air), and a lifting mechanism (front, mountain, or heating)
    [ ] Only cold air
    [ ] Only fronts
    Explanation: Storm ingredients.

  Q15. Lightning is caused by:
    [ ] Wind
    [ ] Solar energy
    [*] Electrical discharge from charge separation within a cumulonimbus cloud (or between cloud and ground)
    [ ] Rain falling
    Explanation: Electrical discharge.

  Q16. Thunder is the sound produced by:
    [ ] Lightning striking
    [*] Rapid expansion of air heated by lightning (air superheats to ~30,000°C, expanding explosively)
    [ ] Wind
    [ ] Cloud collision
    Explanation: Heated air expansion.

  Q17. Tornadoes form from:
    [ ] Any cloud type
    [*] Severe thunderstorms (supercells) with strong wind shear that creates a rotating updraft (mesocyclone)
    [ ] Hurricanes only
    [ ] Clear skies
    Explanation: Supercell tornadoes.

  Q18. The Enhanced Fujita (EF) Scale rates tornadoes based on:
    [ ] Size
    [ ] Speed of rotation
    [*] Damage caused (EF0 weakest to EF5 strongest)
    [ ] Duration
    Explanation: Damage assessment.

  Q19. Hurricanes form over warm ocean water (≥26.5°C) and get energy from:
    [ ] Cold water
    [ ] Tides
    [*] Evaporation and condensation of warm ocean water (latent heat release fuels the storm)
    [ ] Ice
    Explanation: Tropical energy.

  Q20. Hurricanes weaken when they:
    [ ] Reach the equator
    [*] Move over land or cold water (losing their warm ocean water energy source)
    [ ] Gain speed
    [ ] Encounter more moisture
    Explanation: Energy source loss.

--- Lesson 5.5: Weather Fronts & Storms (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Cold Front
     A: Boundary where advancing cold air pushes beneath warmer air, causing rapid uplift and storms

  2. Q: Warm Front
     A: Boundary where advancing warm air slides over retreating cold air, causing gradual precipitation

  3. Q: Hurricane
     A: A large tropical cyclone with sustained winds ≥74 mph, fueled by warm ocean water

  4. Q: Tornado
     A: A violently rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground

  5. Q: Air Mass
     A: A large body of air with relatively uniform temperature and humidity characteristics
