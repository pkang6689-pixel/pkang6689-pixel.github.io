--- Lesson 9.1: Principles of Sustainability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Principles of Sustainability
Principles of Sustainability
Sustainability means meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Brundtland Commission, 1987).
Three Pillars  • Environmental: Maintaining ecosystem health and biodiversity.  • Economic: Supporting livelihoods and economic viability long-term.  • Social: Ensuring equity, well-being, and community resilience.Key Frameworks
UN Sustainable Development Goals (17 SDGs, 2015–2030). Ecological footprint analysis. Circular economy.

--- Lesson 9.2: Sustainable Agriculture (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sustainable Agriculture
Sustainable AgricultureProblems with Industrial Agriculture  • Soil degradation, erosion, and loss of topsoil.  • Water pollution from fertilizer runoff (dead zones).  • Pesticide impacts on pollinators and human health.  • ~25% of global greenhouse gas emissions from food systems.Sustainable Practices  • Crop rotation and cover crops: Maintain soil health, reduce erosion.  • Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Minimize pesticide use through biological and cultural controls.  • No-till farming: Reduces soil disturbance, maintains carbon in soil.  • Agroforestry: Integrating trees with crops and livestock.

--- Lesson 9.3: Sustainable Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sustainable Energy
Sustainable EnergyTransition to Renewables  • Solar: Fastest-growing energy source; costs dropped ~90% since 2010.  • Wind: Competitive with fossil fuels; onshore and expanding offshore.  • Hydropower: Largest renewable source but limited growth potential and ecological impacts.  • Energy storage: Battery technology (lithium-ion) crucial for variable renewables.Energy Efficiency
The cheapest and cleanest energy is the energy we don't use. Building standards, LED lighting, efficient appliances, and industrial optimization can reduce demand 30-50%.

--- Lesson 9.4: Green Building & Urban Planning (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Green Building & Urban Planning
Green Building & Urban PlanningSustainable Buildings  • Energy-efficient design: passive solar, insulation, LED lighting, smart systems.  • Green materials: recycled content, sustainably sourced wood, low-VOC paints.  • LEED certification: Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design.Sustainable Urban Planning  • Smart growth: Compact, mixed-use development reducing sprawl.  • Public transit: Reducing car dependence and emissions.  • Green infrastructure: Parks, green roofs, bioswales, urban forests for cooling and stormwater.

--- Lesson 9.5: Case Studies: Sustainable Practices (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies: Sustainable Practices
Case Studies: Sustainable PracticesFreiburg, Germany — Green City
Solar capital of Europe. 40% of trips by bike or transit. Vauban district: car-free, passive solar housing, district heating.
Costa Rica — Conservation Leader
~99% electricity from renewables. Reversed deforestation (forest cover 25% → 52%). Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) program.
Bhutan — Gross National Happiness
Carbon-negative country. Measures progress by well-being, not GDP. Constitutional mandate: 60%+ forest cover forever.

--- Lesson 9.6: AP Prep: Sustainability Metrics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Sustainability Metrics
AP Prep: Sustainability MetricsKey Metrics  • Ecological footprint: Demand on ecosystems in global hectares (gha).  • Carbon footprint: Total GHG emissions from an activity, product, or entity.  • IPAT equation: Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology.  • HDI (Human Development Index): Life expectancy + education + income → well-being measure.  • Genuine Progress Indicator: GDP adjusted for environmental and social costs.

--- Lesson 9.6: AP Prep: Sustainability Metrics (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The IPAT equation states that environmental impact equals:
    [ ] Only population
    [*] Population × Affluence × Technology (all three factors interact to determine impact)
    [ ] Only technology
    [ ] Only affluence
    Explanation: Three-factor model.

  Q2. Ecological footprint is measured in:
    [ ] Square miles
    [ ] Tons of CO₂
    [*] Global hectares (gha) — area of biologically productive land and water needed
    [ ] Dollars
    Explanation: Land area equivalent.

  Q3. A country's ecological footprint exceeding its biocapacity means:
    [ ] It's self-sufficient
    [*] It is consuming more resources than its ecosystems can regenerate (ecological deficit, relying on imports or depleting stocks)
    [ ] Nothing significant
    [ ] It's perfectly balanced
    Explanation: Living beyond means.

  Q4. Carbon footprint is measured in:
    [ ] Global hectares
    [*] Tons (or kilograms) of CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e)
    [ ] Dollars
    [ ] Gallons
    Explanation: Emission equivalents.

  Q5. The IPAT equation helps explain why _____ sometimes have the highest impact.
    [ ] Developing nations
    [*] Wealthy nations with high consumption (high A and T) even with moderate population
    [ ] Only large countries
    [ ] Only small countries
    Explanation: Consumption matters.

  Q6. Technology (T) in IPAT can _____ or _____ environmental impact.
    [ ] Only increase
    [ ] Only decrease
    [*] Either increase (dirty technology) or decrease (clean technology) — it's a variable
    [ ] Have no effect on
    Explanation: Double-edged factor.

  Q7. GDP as a measure of well-being is criticized because it:
    [ ] Is always accurate
    [*] Doesn't account for environmental degradation, inequality, unpaid work, or factors that reduce quality of life
    [ ] Measures sustainability
    [ ] Is comprehensive
    Explanation: Incomplete measure.

  Q8. The Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) improves on GDP by:
    [ ] Being identical
    [*] Adding positive non-market activities (volunteering, household work) and subtracting costs (pollution, crime, resource depletion)
    [ ] Only measuring money
    [ ] Ignoring the economy
    Explanation: Better well-being measure.

  Q9. The Human Development Index (HDI) measures:
    [ ] Only income
    [*] Life expectancy (health), education (years of schooling), and income (GNI per capita) — a broader measure than GDP alone
    [ ] Only GDP
    [ ] Only employment
    Explanation: Three dimensions.

  Q10. Sustainability indicators should measure:
    [ ] Only one dimension
    [*] Environmental health, economic viability, and social well-being together (reflecting the three pillars)
    [ ] Only economic growth
    [ ] Only environmental quality
    Explanation: All three pillars.

  Q11. Earth Overshoot Day marks when:
    [ ] Nothing special
    [*] Humanity has used more from nature than the planet can renew in the entire year (moving earlier each decade — currently late July)
    [ ] The year ends
    [ ] Seasons change
    Explanation: Annual deficit date.

  Q12. Water footprint measures:
    [ ] Only drinking water
    [*] Total volume of freshwater used to produce goods and services consumed by an individual, business, or nation
    [ ] Only irrigation
    [ ] Only rainfall
    Explanation: Hidden water use.

  Q13. Per capita ecological footprint is highest in:
    [ ] Most populated countries
    [*] High-income countries (US, Australia, UAE) due to high consumption levels
    [ ] Only one country
    [ ] Equally distributed
    Explanation: Consumption inequality.

  Q14. The concept of 'decoupling' refers to:
    [ ] Linking growth and impact
    [*] Achieving economic growth while reducing environmental impact (absolute decoupling = growing GDP while total impact decreases)
    [ ] Only theoretical
    [ ] Impossible in practice
    Explanation: Growth without degradation.

  Q15. Material footprint tracks:
    [ ] Only weight
    [*] Total raw materials extracted to meet a country's consumption needs (including materials embedded in imports)
    [ ] Only local resources
    [ ] Only waste
    Explanation: Hidden material flow.

  Q16. Social sustainability metrics include:
    [ ] Only income
    [*] Gini coefficient (inequality), access to education and healthcare, gender equity, and community well-being
    [ ] Only employment
    [ ] Only one measure
    Explanation: Social dimension.

  Q17. For AP calculations, students may need to convert between:
    [ ] Only one metric
    [*] Per capita and total values (multiply per capita × population), and compare footprints to biocapacity
    [ ] Only per capita
    [ ] Only total values
    Explanation: Quantitative skills.

  Q18. The Environmental Performance Index (EPI) ranks countries on:
    [ ] Only one factor
    [*] Environmental health (air quality, water, sanitation) and ecosystem vitality (climate, biodiversity, fisheries) — over 40 indicators
    [ ] Only GDP
    [ ] Only emissions
    Explanation: National comparison.

  Q19. Sustainable development metrics should track progress toward:
    [ ] Only economic growth
    [*] Specific, measurable goals (like the 17 SDGs) across environmental, social, and economic dimensions
    [ ] Only one goal
    [ ] Only environmental restoration
    Explanation: Goal-oriented measurement.

  Q20. For the AP exam, sustainability metrics require:
    [ ] Only definitions
    [*] Ability to calculate, interpret, compare, and critically evaluate metrics — understanding what each measures and its limitations
    [ ] Only memorization
    [ ] Only one formula
    Explanation: Analytical application.
