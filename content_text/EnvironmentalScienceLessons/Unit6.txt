--- Lesson 6.1: Air Pollution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Air Pollution
Air PollutionPrimary Pollutants
Emitted directly: CO, SO₂, NOₓ, particulate matter (PM), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), lead.
Secondary Pollutants
Formed by chemical reactions in the atmosphere: ozone (O₃), smog, acid rain.
Key Issues  • Criteria pollutants: 6 regulated by the Clean Air Act (CO, Pb, NO₂, O₃, PM, SO₂).  • Indoor air pollution: Radon, VOCs, mold, CO — kills ~3.8 million/year globally (cooking with solid fuels).

--- Lesson 6.1: Air Pollution (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Primary pollutants are:
    [ ] Formed in the atmosphere
    [*] Emitted directly from a source (e.g., CO from cars, SO₂ from power plants)
    [ ] Always natural
    [ ] Only indoors
    Explanation: Direct emissions.

  Q2. Secondary pollutants are:
    [ ] Emitted directly
    [*] Formed by chemical reactions in the atmosphere (e.g., ground-level ozone from NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight)

    [ ] 3

    [ ] Helpful

  Q18. Air Quality Index (AQI) measures:
    [ ] Only temperature

  Q19. Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) come from:

  Q20. For the AP exam, air pollution questions involve:
    [ ] Only definitions

    [ ] Only listing pollutants

--- Lesson 6.2: Water Pollution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Water Pollution
Water PollutionTypes  • Point source: Single identifiable source (factory pipe, sewage outfall).  • Non-point source: Diffuse (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater).Major Pollutants  • Nutrients: N, P → eutrophication → algal blooms → dead zones.  • Pathogens: Bacteria, viruses from sewage → cholera, dysentery.  • Toxics: Heavy metals (Hg, Pb), pesticides, pharmaceuticals.  • Thermal: Heated discharge from power plants reduces dissolved O₂.

--- Lesson 6.3: Soil Pollution & Land Degradation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Soil Pollution & Land Degradation
Soil Pollution & Land Degradation
Soil contamination comes from industrial waste, pesticides, mining, landfills, and atmospheric deposition. Land degradation affects ~25% of Earth's ice-free land.
Key Issues  • Brownfields: Former industrial sites with contamination requiring cleanup.  • Superfund (CERCLA): US law funding cleanup of the worst hazardous waste sites.  • Bioremediation: Using organisms to break down contaminants (bacteria, fungi, plants).

--- Lesson 6.4: Solid Waste Management (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solid Waste Management
Solid Waste Management
The US generates ~292 million tons of municipal solid waste (MSW) annually (~4.9 lbs/person/day). Globally: ~2 billion tons/year.
Hierarchy (most to least preferred)  • Reduce → Reuse → Recycle/Compost → Energy recovery → Landfill
US recycles ~32% of MSW. Landfills remain the primary disposal method despite methane emissions.

--- Lesson 6.5: Hazardous Waste & Recycling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hazardous Waste & Recycling
Hazardous Waste & Recycling
Hazardous waste: Waste that is ignitable, corrosive, reactive, or toxic. Regulated by RCRA (Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, 1976).
Recycling
Reduces need for virgin resources, saves energy (recycling aluminum uses 95% less energy than new), and reduces landfill burden. Challenges: contamination, market economics, single-stream confusion.

--- Lesson 6.6: Case Studies: Pollution Control (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies: Pollution Control
Case Studies: Pollution ControlUS Acid Rain Program (1990 Clean Air Act Amendments)

London Smog (1952)
Great Smog killed ~12,000 people. Led to UK's Clean Air Act (1956). Spurred global air quality awareness.

Water source switch corroded lead pipes → lead contamination. Disproportionately affected low-income, majority-Black community. Environmental justice failure.

--- Lesson 6.7: AP Prep: Pollution Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Pollution Models
AP Prep: Pollution ModelsKey Concepts for Calculations  • LD50: Dose lethal to 50% of test organisms; measures acute toxicity.  • Dose-response curve: Plots health effect vs. exposure dose; determines threshold and no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL).  • Bioaccumulation factor: Concentration in organism / concentration in environment.  • Biomagnification factor: Concentration at higher trophic level / concentration at lower level.  • Half-life: Time for pollutant concentration to decrease by half.

--- Lesson 6.7: AP Prep: Pollution Models (Quiz) ---

  Q1. LD50 measures:
    [ ] The lethal dose for all organisms
    [*] The dose that kills 50% of a test population (lower LD50 = more toxic)
    [ ] Only non-lethal effects
    [ ] Only chronic effects
    Explanation: Acute toxicity measure.

  Q2. A substance with a lower LD50 is:
    [ ] Less toxic
    [*] More toxic (it takes less of the substance to kill 50% of organisms)
    [ ] Equally toxic
    [ ] Non-toxic
    Explanation: Inverse relationship.

  Q3. If substance A has LD50 = 5 mg/kg and substance B has LD50 = 500 mg/kg, then:
    [ ] B is more toxic
    [*] A is more toxic (100× more toxic; requires much less to kill 50%)
    [ ] They're equal
    [ ] Neither is toxic
    Explanation: Compare LD50.

  Q4. A dose-response curve shows:
    [ ] Only one data point
    [*] The relationship between increasing exposure (dose) and the severity of biological response
    [ ] Only LD50
    [ ] Only mortality
    Explanation: Graphical analysis.

  Q5. The threshold in a dose-response curve is:
    [ ] Always zero
    [*] The dose below which no measurable effect is observed (though some scientists argue no safe threshold exists for carcinogens)
    [ ] Always high
    [ ] The LD50
    Explanation: Effect onset.

  Q6. NOAEL stands for:
    [ ] No Obvious Action Expected Locally
    [*] No Observed Adverse Effect Level (highest dose with no measurable harmful effect)
    [ ] Normal Observation Above Expected Limit
    [ ] None Of the Above Effects Listed
    Explanation: Safety benchmark.

  Q7. Bioaccumulation factor is calculated as:
    [ ] Environment ÷ organism
    [*] Concentration in organism ÷ concentration in environment
    [ ] Weight of organism
    [ ] Volume of water
    Explanation: Ratio of concentrations.

  Q8. If mercury in water is 0.001 ppm and in a fish is 1 ppm, the bioaccumulation factor is:

    [*] 1,000 (1/0.001 = 1,000)

    Explanation: 1,000× concentration.

  Q9. Biomagnification factor is:

  Q10. If DDT in zooplankton is 0.04 ppm and in fish is 0.5 ppm, the biomagnification factor is:

    [ ] 50
    Explanation: Step-up factor.

  Q11. The half-life of a pollutant is:
    [ ] Time to completely degrade

    [ ] Only for radioactive materials

  Q12. If a pesticide has a half-life of 30 days and initial concentration is 100 ppm, after 90 days:

    [ ] Short-term, high-dose
