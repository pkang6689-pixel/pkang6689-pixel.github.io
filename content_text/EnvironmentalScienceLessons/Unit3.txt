--- Lesson 3.1: Human Population Growth (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Human Population Growth
Human Population Growth
World population ~8 billion (2024). Grew slowly for millennia then exponentially after the Industrial Revolution (better agriculture, sanitation, medicine). Current growth rate ~1.0%/yr.
Key Milestones  • 1 billion: ~1800  • 2 billion: 1927  • 4 billion: 1974  • 8 billion: 2022
Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average children per woman. Replacement level ≈ 2.1. TFR varies: Sub-Saharan Africa ~4.5; Europe ~1.5.

--- Lesson 3.2: Demographic Transition Model (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Demographic Transition Model
Demographic Transition Model (DTM)Four Stages  • Stage 1 – Pre-industrial: High birth and death rates → slow/no growth.  • Stage 2 – Transitional: Death rates drop (better health care); birth rates remain high → rapid growth.  • Stage 3 – Industrial: Birth rates decline (urbanization, education, family planning) → growth slows.  • Stage 4 – Post-industrial: Low birth and death rates → slow/zero growth.
Some add Stage 5: Birth rate falls below death rate → population decline (Japan, parts of Europe).

--- Lesson 3.3: Age Structure Diagrams (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Age Structure Diagrams
Age Structure Diagrams
Population pyramids show age and sex distribution. Shape predicts future growth:
  • Expansive (wide base): Rapid growth — many young people (Nigeria, Ethiopia).  • Stationary (column-shaped): Stable — roughly equal numbers at each age (US, Australia).  • Constrictive (narrow base): Declining — fewer young than middle-aged (Japan, Germany, Italy).
Diagrams reveal dependency ratio and demographic momentum — critical for policy planning.

--- Lesson 3.4: Carrying Capacity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Carrying Capacity
Carrying Capacity & Human Population
Earth's carrying capacity for humans is debated. Estimates range from 4 to 16 billion depending on consumption levels and technology.
Key Concepts  • Ecological footprint: Area of productive land/water needed to sustain a person's lifestyle.  • Overshoot: Humanity currently uses ~1.7 Earths' worth of resources per year.  • Earth Overshoot Day: Date humanity has used more resources than Earth can regenerate that year (moves earlier each decade).

--- Lesson 3.5: Urbanization & Land Use (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Urbanization & Land Use
Urbanization & Land Use
Over 56% of the world's population lives in cities (projected 68% by 2050). Urbanization concentrates consumption but can also create efficiencies.
Impacts  • Urban heat island: Cities are warmer than surroundings (concrete + less vegetation + waste heat).  • Sprawl: Low-density, car-dependent expansion converts farmland/habitat.  • Smart growth: Compact, mixed-use, transit-oriented development to reduce sprawl.

--- Lesson 3.6: Case Studies: Population Policy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies: Population Policy
Case Studies: Population PolicyChina's One-Child Policy (1980–2015)
Reduced TFR from ~6 to ~1.7. Now facing aging crisis; shifted to two-child (2016) then three-child (2021) policy.
India
Voluntary family planning, women's education. TFR dropped from ~6 (1960) to ~2.0 (2023). Now world's most populous country.
Kerala Model
Indian state achieved low TFR (~1.6) through high female literacy (97%), good healthcare, without coercion.

--- Lesson 3.7: AP Prep: Demographic Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Demographic Calculations
AP Prep: Demographic CalculationsKey Formulas  • Growth rate (r): r = (CBR − CDR) / 10 (gives %; CBR and CDR per 1,000).  • Doubling time: t = 70 / r (Rule of 70).  • Rate of natural increase: (births − deaths) / total population × 100.  • Net migration rate: (immigration − emigration) / population × 1,000.  • Crude birth rate / Crude death rate: births or deaths per 1,000 people per year.

  Q1. If CBR = 30 and CDR = 10 (both per 1,000), the rate of natural increase is:
    [ ] 40%
    [*] 2.0% ((30-10)/10 = 2.0%)

    [ ] 0.2
    Explanation: (CBR-CDR)/10.

  Q2. Using Rule of 70 with r = 2%, doubling time is:
    [ ] 140 years
    [*] 35 years (70/2)

    [ ] 20 years
    Explanation: Quick calculation.

  Q3. If CBR = 45/1000 and CDR = 15/1000, doubling time is:
    [ ] 70 years
    [ ] 35 years
    [*] ~23 years (r = 3%; 70/3 ≈ 23.3)

    Explanation: r = (45-15)/10 = 3%.

  Q4. Net migration rate is calculated as:

    [*] (Immigration − Emigration) / population × 1,000

    [ ] Death rate only

  Q5. A country with CBR=12, CDR=11, and net migration=+3/1000 has total growth rate of:
