--- Lesson 2.1: Energy Flow in Ecosystems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy Flow in Ecosystems
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a trophic level?
A trophic level is each step in a food chain or food web where energy is transferred. Producers are the first level, primary consumers second, secondary consumers third, and so on.
What is gross primary productivity (GPP)?
Gross primary productivity is the total amount of energy captured through photosynthesis per unit area per unit time. It represents all carbon fixed by producers before subtracting respiratory losses.
What is net primary productivity (NPP)?
Net primary productivity is the rate at which producers store energy as biomass after using some for their own metabolism. It represents the energy actually available to be consumed by herbivores.
What is the 10% rule in ecology?
The 10% rule is an ecological approximation stating that roughly 10% of energy at each trophic level is passed to the next level. The remaining 90% is used for metabolism and lost as heat.
What is an energy pyramid?
An energy pyramid (ecological pyramid of energy) shows the amount of energy at each trophic level. It is always upright because energy decreases at higher levels due to heat loss at each transfer.
What is entropy?
Entropy is a measure of disorder that always increases during energy transformations (second law of thermodynamics). In ecosystems, entropy explains why energy is progressively lost as unusable heat.
What is assimilation efficiency?
Assimilation efficiency is the fraction of ingested food that an organism actually absorbs across its gut wall for use in metabolism and growth, versus what passes through as feces.
What is ecological efficiency?
Ecological efficiency (also called food chain efficiency) is the percentage of energy at one trophic level that is incorporated into biomass at the next trophic level, typically averaging around 10%.
What is standing crop biomass?
Standing crop biomass is the total amount of living biological material at a given trophic level or in an ecosystem at a particular moment in time, usually measured as dry mass per unit area.
What is cellular respiration in the context of energy flow?
Cellular respiration is the process by which all living organisms convert organic molecules into ATP energy, releasing CO2, water, and heat. It accounts for the majority of energy loss at each trophic level.
Real-World Applications
Example: Approximately what percentage of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next?
The 10% rule states that approximately 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed to the next. The remaining 90% is lost primarily as heat through cellular respiration and metabolic processes.
Example: A conservation manager must choose between protecting a 1,000-acre tropical rainforest or a 1,000-acre tundra ecosystem. From an energy flow perspective, protecting the rainforest would preserve more biological productivity because:
Tropical rainforests produce roughly 2,200 g/m2/year of NPP compared to about 140 g/m2/year in tundra, making them far more productive per unit area and supporting far greater biodiversity.
Example: An AP exam question asks why there are far fewer lions than zebras on the African savanna. The best answer, based on energy flow principles, is:
The 10% rule limits how many predators an ecosystem can support. Since only ~10% of zebra energy transfers to lions, far fewer lions can be sustained, which is why predators are always rarer than their prey.

--- Lesson 2.1: Energy Flow in Ecosystems (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Energy flows through ecosystems in:
    [ ] Random directions with no pattern
    [ ] Cycles that repeat endlessly
    [*] A one-way direction, from the sun through producers to consumers, eventually lost as heat
    [ ] Reverse, from consumers to producers
    Explanation: Unlike matter which cycles, energy flows in one direction through ecosystems. Solar energy is captured by producers, transferred to consumers, and eventually lost as heat at each trophic level.

  Q2. Approximately what percentage of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next?
    [*] 10% (the 10% rule)
    [ ] 50% (the half-energy rule)
    [ ] 100% (complete transfer efficiency)
    [ ] 1% (the minimal transfer rule)
    Explanation: The 10% rule states that approximately 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed to the next. The remaining 90% is lost primarily as heat through cellular respiration and metabolic processes.

  Q3. Gross primary productivity (GPP) is:
    [*] The total amount of energy (biomass) produced by photosynthesis in producers per unit area per unit time
    [ ] Energy lost as heat by decomposers
    [ ] Only the energy stored in animal tissues
    [ ] The total amount of energy consumed by all organisms
    Explanation: Gross primary productivity is the total rate at which producers (plants, algae) convert solar energy into chemical energy through photosynthesis, before any energy is used for their own respiration.

  Q4. Net primary productivity (NPP) equals:
    [*] GPP minus the energy producers use for their own respiration
    [ ] GPP + respiration
    [ ] Total ecosystem energy
    [ ] Only decomposer energy
    Explanation: Net primary productivity (NPP = GPP - R) is the energy remaining after producers use some for their own cellular respiration. NPP represents the energy available to consumers in the ecosystem.

  Q5. The first law of thermodynamics states that energy:
    [ ] Always increases in amount
    [*] Cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another
    [ ] Can be created from nothing
    [ ] Can be destroyed completely
    Explanation: The first law of thermodynamics (conservation of energy) states that energy cannot be created or destroyed. In ecosystems, solar energy is transformed into chemical energy by producers, then into kinetic energy and heat.

  Q6. The second law of thermodynamics explains why:
    [ ] Energy increases as it moves through food chains
    [ ] Ecosystems do not need energy inputs
    [*] Every energy transfer results in some energy being lost as heat, making energy transfers less than 100% efficient
    [ ] Matter can be created from energy
    Explanation: The second law of thermodynamics states that every energy transformation increases entropy (disorder). In ecosystems, this means usable energy decreases at each trophic level as heat is lost.

  Q7. If producers in an ecosystem capture 10,000 kcal of energy, approximately how much would be available to secondary consumers?
    [ ] 10,000 kcal (all energy transfers perfectly)
    [ ] 10 kcal (only 0.1% of energy transfers each step)

    [ ] 1,000 kcal (10% of the original producer energy)
    Explanation: Applying the 10% rule: producers have 10,000 kcal, primary consumers get 1,000 kcal (10%), and secondary consumers get 100 kcal (10% of 1,000). This explains why food chains rarely exceed 4-5 levels.

  Q8. Ecological pyramids of energy are always upright because:
    [*] Energy is lost as heat at each trophic level, so less energy is available at higher levels
    [ ] There are always more predators than prey
    [ ] Producers are always the smallest organisms
    [ ] Scientists design them to look that way
    Explanation: Energy pyramids are always upright because the second law of thermodynamics guarantees that energy is lost as heat at each transfer, so each higher trophic level always has less total energy available.

  Q9. Which biome has the highest net primary productivity?
    [ ] Open ocean
    [ ] Desert
    [ ] Tundra
    [*] Tropical rainforest
    Explanation: Tropical rainforests have the highest NPP per unit area due to abundant sunlight, warmth, and rainfall year-round, supporting dense vegetation that captures large amounts of solar energy.

  Q10. Most of the energy in an ecosystem is lost as:
    [*] Heat through cellular respiration at each trophic level
    [ ] Light energy radiated from bioluminescent organisms within the ecosystem
    [ ] Sound energy generated through animal communication and ecological interactions
    [ ] Chemical energy stored in waste products that are never broken down or recycled
    Explanation: At each trophic level, organisms use most of the energy they consume for cellular respiration (metabolism, movement, maintaining body temperature), which releases energy as heat that dissipates into the environment.

    [ ] A pyramid-shaped industrial power plant designed to generate electricity from biomass fuels

    Explanation: Ecological efficiency (also called food chain efficiency) is the percentage of energy at one trophic level that is incorporated into biomass at the next trophic level, typically averaging around 10%.

--- Lesson 2.2: Food Chains & Food Webs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Food Chains & Food Webs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a food chain?
A food chain is a linear pathway showing the transfer of energy and nutrients from one organism to the next, starting with a producer and moving through successive consumers at higher trophic levels.
What is a food web?
A food web is a comprehensive diagram showing all the interconnected feeding relationships in an ecosystem, representing the complex reality that most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple species.
What is a keystone species?
A keystone species has an outsized impact on ecosystem structure and function relative to its numbers. Removing it causes dramatic changes, like sea stars controlling mussel populations on rocky shores.
What is a trophic cascade?
A trophic cascade occurs when a predator's presence or absence indirectly affects organisms two or more trophic levels away. For example, wolves suppress elk, allowing willow trees to recover along rivers.
What is a detrital food chain?
A detrital food chain begins with dead organic matter and passes energy through organisms that consume and decompose it. In many forest and marine ecosystems, the detrital chain processes most of the energy.
What is an invasive species?
An invasive species is a non-native organism that, when introduced to a new environment, spreads rapidly and causes harm because it lacks the natural predators, diseases, or competitors that control it in its native range.
What is a generalist species?
Generalist species like raccoons and crows can thrive in many different environments and eat a wide variety of foods. This flexibility makes them more resilient to environmental changes than specialists.
What is a specialist species?
Specialist species depend on specific food sources or habitats, like koalas (eucalyptus leaves only) or giant pandas (bamboo). This narrow niche makes them particularly vulnerable to habitat loss.
What is bioaccumulation?
Bioaccumulation is the progressive buildup of substances like pesticides or heavy metals in an individual organism's tissues because the rate of intake exceeds the rate of excretion or metabolism.
What is biomagnification?
Biomagnification amplifies toxin concentrations up the food chain. Since predators consume many contaminated prey, they accumulate much higher concentrations, which is why DDT devastated apex predators like eagles.
Real-World Applications
Example: An organism that feeds at multiple trophic levels is called:
Omnivores eat both plants and animals, functioning at multiple trophic levels. Bears, for example, eat berries (acting as primary consumers) and fish (acting as secondary or tertiary consumers).
Example: When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, elk populations decreased, which allowed willow and aspen trees to regrow along rivers, stabilizing riverbanks and improving habitat for beavers and fish. This is an example of:
The Yellowstone wolf reintroduction is one of the most famous trophic cascades: wolves reduced elk overgrazing, allowing vegetation recovery that improved habitats for numerous other species.
Example: A marine biologist notices that a once-diverse coral reef now has only algae after overfishing removed most herbivorous fish. Without herbivores to control algae, corals were smothered. This is an example of:
Overfishing removed a critical trophic group (herbivorous fish) from the food web. Without grazing pressure on algae, the ecosystem shifted from coral-dominated to algae-dominated, a regime shift.
Example: A student examines a food web diagram and notices that a mouse is eaten by hawks, snakes, and foxes. If hawks were removed from this food web, the student would predict:
In complex food webs, removing one predator releases prey from some predation pressure. The effect is buffered because other predators (snakes, foxes) still consume mice, demonstrating food web resilience.

  Q1. A food web differs from a food chain because it:

    [ ] Is always shorter in length because it simplifies the number of trophic levels represented
    [ ] Has fewer total organisms because it only tracks one linear pathway of energy transfer
    [*] Shows the interconnected network of multiple overlapping food chains in an ecosystem
    Explanation: A food web is more realistic than a food chain because it shows the complex network of interconnected feeding relationships in an ecosystem, where most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple species.

  Q2. A food chain always begins with:
    [ ] A secondary consumer that feeds on herbivores and occupies the third trophic level of the chain
    [*] A producer (autotroph) that captures energy from sunlight or chemical reactions
    [ ] A decomposer that breaks down dead organic matter and recycles nutrients back into the soil
    [ ] A top predator that sits at the highest trophic level and regulates populations below it
    Explanation: Food chains always begin with producers because they are the organisms that convert inorganic energy (sunlight or chemicals) into organic molecules that all other organisms in the chain depend on.

  Q3. Removing a keystone species from a food web would:
    [ ] Only directly affect the keystone species population itself without impacting other organisms
    [ ] Improve overall ecosystem health by reducing competition for resources among the remaining species
    [*] Cause dramatic changes in ecosystem structure and potentially lead to species loss throughout the food web
    [ ] Have no measurable effect on the ecosystem because other species can easily fill the vacant niche
    Explanation: Keystone species have a disproportionately large effect on their ecosystem relative to their abundance. Removing them causes cascading effects throughout the food web, potentially collapsing entire communities.

    [ ] Predators to prey
    [ ] Decomposers to consumers

  Key Concepts: Population Dynamics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is carrying capacity?
Carrying capacity represents the long-term equilibrium population size determined by resource availability. It can change over time as environmental conditions, technology, or resource availability change.
What is exponential growth?
Exponential growth occurs when a population increases by a constant proportion each generation, resulting in ever-accelerating growth that produces a characteristic J-shaped curve.
What is logistic growth?
Logistic growth models the reality that resources are limited. Growth starts fast but slows due to increasing competition and resource scarcity, leveling off at the environment's carrying capacity.
What is a density-dependent factor?
Density-dependent factors have impacts proportional to population density. Disease spreads faster in dense populations, competition intensifies, and predators focus on abundant prey.
What is a density-independent factor?
Density-independent factors impact populations regardless of their size or density. A severe drought, volcanic eruption, or flood can kill organisms whether the population is large or small.
What is a population crash (die-off)?
A population crash occurs when a population that has exceeded its carrying capacity (overshoot) rapidly declines due to resource depletion, starvation, disease, or other limiting factors.
What is biotic potential?
Biotic potential (rmax) is the maximum rate at which a population can grow when resources are unlimited and environmental conditions are ideal. It varies greatly among species.
What is environmental resistance?
Environmental resistance is the combination of all biotic and abiotic factors that prevent a population from reaching its biotic potential, including predation, disease, limited food, and habitat constraints.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Disease is a density-dependent factor because pathogens spread more easily when individuals are crowded together, increasing contact rates. Sparse populations have lower transmission rates.

--- Lesson 2.4: Community Interactions (Competition, Symbiosis) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Community Interactions (Competition, Symbiosis)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is symbiosis?
Symbiosis literally means 'living together' and describes any close, prolonged interaction between two different species. The three main types are mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.
What is an ecological niche?
An ecological niche includes everything about how a species lives: what it eats, when it is active, where it lives, how it reproduces, and all its interactions with other species and the environment.
What is the competitive exclusion principle?
The competitive exclusion principle states that complete competitors cannot coexist. If two species occupy exactly the same niche, one will eventually exclude the other through superior resource use.
What is resource partitioning?
Resource partitioning is the evolutionary process by which competing species evolve to use different portions of a resource to reduce competition. It allows multiple similar species to coexist.
What is mutualism?
Mutualism is a +/+ symbiotic relationship where both partners benefit. Classic examples include mycorrhizal fungi helping plant roots absorb nutrients in exchange for sugars from the plant.
What is parasitism?
Parasitism is a +/- relationship where the parasite gains nutrition or shelter from the host while causing harm. Unlike predators, parasites typically do not kill their hosts immediately.
What is commensalism?
Commensalism is a +/0 relationship where one species benefits while the other is unaffected. Examples include birds nesting in trees or remora fish attaching to sharks for transportation.
What is coevolution?
Coevolution is reciprocal evolutionary change where interacting species drive each other's adaptation. Predator-prey arms races and pollinator-plant specialization are classic examples.
What is character displacement?
Character displacement occurs when similar species in the same area evolve differences in morphology or behavior (such as beak size) to reduce competition for the same resources.
What is amensalism?
Amensalism is a -/0 interaction where one species is negatively affected while the other is not. For example, a large tree shading out small plants beneath it without being affected by them.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The oxpecker-rhino relationship is mutualism (both benefit), while the egret-herbivore relationship is commensalism (egrets benefit from insects stirred up, but herbivores are unaffected).
Real-World Applications
Example: Five species of warblers live in the same spruce trees in New England forests. Rather than competing directly, each species feeds at a different height and on different parts of the tree. This is an example of:
MacArthur's warblers are a classic example of resource partitioning. By feeding at different heights and on different tree parts, the five species avoid direct competition and coexist in the same habitat.
Example: In a forest, a large oak tree shades out small plants beneath it, reducing their growth and survival. However, the small plants have no measurable effect on the oak tree. This interaction is an example of:
This is amensalism (-/0): the oak tree negatively affects understory plants by blocking sunlight, but the small plants have no measurable impact on the large tree, making it a one-sided negative interaction.

  Q1. Interspecific competition occurs between:
    [ ] Only predators that compete for the same prey species within overlapping hunting territories
    [ ] Only plants that grow in the same soil type and require identical mineral concentrations
    [*] Members of different species competing for the same limited resources
    [ ] Members of the same species that fight over mates, territory, and access to food resources
    Explanation: Interspecific competition occurs when individuals of different species compete for the same limited resources, such as food, water, space, or sunlight, reducing the fitness of both species.

  Q2. Mutualism is a symbiotic relationship in which:
    [ ] One organism benefits and the other is harmed
    [ ] One organism kills the other
    [*] Both organisms benefit from the interaction
    [ ] Neither organism is affected
    Explanation: Mutualism is a symbiotic relationship where both species benefit. Examples include bees pollinating flowers (bees get nectar, plants get pollinated) and mycorrhizal fungi helping plant roots absorb nutrients.

  Q3. Parasitism is a relationship in which:
    [ ] Both organisms are equally harmed through mutual exploitation of shared energy resources
    [ ] Both organisms benefit from a close association that enhances their respective survival rates
    [ ] Neither organism is significantly affected because the interaction has negligible ecological impact
    [*] One organism (the parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (the host), which is harmed
    Explanation: In parasitism, the parasite lives on or in a host organism, deriving nutrients at the host's expense. Unlike predation, parasites typically weaken but do not immediately kill their hosts.

  Q4. Commensalism is a relationship in which:
    [ ] Both species benefit
    [ ] Both species are harmed
    [ ] One species kills the other
    [*] One species benefits while the other is neither helped nor harmed
    Explanation: In commensalism, one species benefits from the relationship while the other is unaffected. An example is barnacles attaching to whales for transportation without affecting the whale.

  Q5. The competitive exclusion principle states that:
    [ ] Competition always leads to extinction
    [*] Two species competing for the exact same niche cannot coexist indefinitely; one will outcompete the other
    [ ] All species can share the same niche indefinitely
    [ ] Competition never affects populations
    Explanation: The competitive exclusion principle (Gause's principle) states that two species occupying the exact same ecological niche cannot stably coexist. One will inevitably outcompete and exclude the other.

  Q6. Resource partitioning allows species to coexist by:
    [ ] Competing more aggressively for the same resources until one species completely dominates the habitat
    [ ] Eliminating all competitors through chemical warfare and territorial aggression until one species remains
    [ ] Feeding exclusively on dead organisms and detritus rather than competing for live food sources
    [*] Dividing resources by using different parts of the habitat, different food sources, or being active at different times
    Explanation: Resource partitioning reduces competition by allowing similar species to use different portions of available resources. For example, warblers may feed at different heights in the same tree.

  Q7. Predation benefits ecosystems by:
    [ ] Only harming prey populations through persistent hunting that reduces their numbers below recovery
    [ ] Having no significant ecological value since predators merely consume without contributing to stability
    [ ] Eliminating all prey species in the ecosystem through unsustainable hunting over extended time periods
    [*] Regulating prey populations, preventing overgrazing, and maintaining biodiversity by preventing competitive dominance
    Explanation: Predation is a key ecological process that regulates prey populations, prevents any one species from dominating, and drives evolutionary adaptations, ultimately maintaining ecosystem balance and biodiversity.

  Q8. Mimicry is an adaptation where an organism:
    [ ] Moves to an entirely new habitat to avoid predation by finding safer locations with fewer threats
    [ ] Hides underground permanently, remaining dormant to avoid all encounters with potential predators
    [ ] Changes its diet completely to exploit entirely new food sources unavailable to competing organisms
    [*] Resembles another species or object to gain a survival advantage, such as avoiding predation
    Explanation: Mimicry is an evolutionary adaptation where one species evolves to resemble another species or environmental feature. Batesian mimicry involves harmless species imitating harmful ones to deter predators.

  Q9. Coevolution occurs when:
    [ ] One species evolves independently in response to changing environmental conditions without affecting others
    [ ] Only predators evolve new hunting adaptations while prey species remain unchanged across generations
    [*] Two or more species evolve in response to each other over time through reciprocal adaptations
    [ ] All species gradually stop evolving once they reach optimal adaptation to their current environment
    Explanation: Coevolution is a process where two or more species exert selective pressures on each other, driving reciprocal evolutionary changes. Examples include flowers and their specific pollinators evolving together.

  Q10. Intraspecific competition occurs between:
    [ ] Parasites and hosts only
    [ ] Members of different species
    [*] Members of the same species competing for the same resources
    [ ] Predators and their prey
    Explanation: Intraspecific competition is competition between individuals of the same species for limited resources. It is often more intense than interspecific competition because members need identical resources.

  Q11. What is symbiosis?
    [ ] The study of symbolic logic
    [ ] Competition between members of the same species
    [*] A close, long-term biological interaction between two different species living in close association
    [ ] A type of predator-prey relationship
    Explanation: Symbiosis literally means 'living together' and describes any close, prolonged interaction between two different species. The three main types are mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.

  Q12. What is an ecological niche?
    [*] The total set of biotic and abiotic conditions and resources a species needs to survive, including its role in the ecosystem
    [ ] A physical shelter or den within the habitat where an animal lives, raises young, and stores food
    [ ] A geographic location on a map that defines the boundaries of a species' territorial range precisely
    [ ] Only the food an organism eats and the specific feeding behaviors it uses to obtain nutrition
    Explanation: An ecological niche includes everything about how a species lives: what it eats, when it is active, where it lives, how it reproduces, and all its interactions with other species and the environment.

  Q13. What is the competitive exclusion principle?
    [ ] A rule that larger species always win
    [ ] A law that prevents companies from competing
    [ ] The idea that all competition is harmful
    [*] The principle that two species with identical ecological niches cannot coexist in the same habitat indefinitely
    Explanation: The competitive exclusion principle states that complete competitors cannot coexist. If two species occupy exactly the same niche, one will eventually exclude the other through superior resource use.

  Q14. What is resource partitioning?
    [ ] Sharing food equally among all species
    [ ] A government policy for distributing natural resources
    [ ] Dividing a territory into equal squares
    [*] The division of resources among species to reduce competition, allowing similar species to coexist in the same habitat
    Explanation: Resource partitioning is the evolutionary process by which competing species evolve to use different portions of a resource to reduce competition. It allows multiple similar species to coexist.

  Q15. What is mutualism?
    [ ] A relationship between members of the same species
    [ ] A relationship where one species always harms the other
    [*] A symbiotic relationship in which both species benefit from the interaction
    [ ] A type of predation
    Explanation: Mutualism is a +/+ symbiotic relationship where both partners benefit. Classic examples include mycorrhizal fungi helping plant roots absorb nutrients in exchange for sugars from the plant.

  Q16. What is parasitism?
    [ ] A symbiotic relationship in which both organisms involved benefit equally from the close association
    [ ] A relationship where neither species experiences measurable effects on survival or reproductive output
    [ ] A type of mutualism in which both species exchange nutrients and protection for mutual benefit
    [*] A symbiotic relationship in which one organism (the parasite) benefits at the expense of another (the host), which is harmed
    Explanation: Parasitism is a +/- relationship where the parasite gains nutrition or shelter from the host while causing harm. Unlike predators, parasites typically do not kill their hosts immediately.

  Q17. What is commensalism?
    [*] A symbiotic relationship where one species benefits and the other is neither helped nor significantly harmed
    [ ] A relationship where both species are harmed
    [ ] A type of competition
    [ ] A relationship that always involves parasites
    Explanation: Commensalism is a +/0 relationship where one species benefits while the other is unaffected. Examples include birds nesting in trees or remora fish attaching to sharks for transportation.

  Q18. What is coevolution?
    [ ] The simultaneous extinction of two species
    [ ] Evolution that happens twice in the same species
    [ ] Evolution that occurs only in controlled laboratories
    [*] The process by which two or more species reciprocally influence each other's evolution through close ecological interactions
    Explanation: Coevolution is reciprocal evolutionary change where interacting species drive each other's adaptation. Predator-prey arms races and pollinator-plant specialization are classic examples.

  Q19. What is character displacement?
    [*] The evolutionary divergence of competing species' traits in areas where they overlap, reducing competition
    [ ] The displacement of one character in a story by another
    [ ] Moving organisms from one habitat to another
    [ ] A fictional concept not found in nature
    Explanation: Character displacement occurs when similar species in the same area evolve differences in morphology or behavior (such as beak size) to reduce competition for the same resources.

  Q20. What is amensalism?
    [ ] A friendly relationship between competing species
    [ ] A type of mutualism
    [ ] A relationship where both species benefit
    [*] A relationship where one species is harmed while the other is unaffected
    Explanation: Amensalism is a -/0 interaction where one species is negatively affected while the other is not. For example, a large tree shading out small plants beneath it without being affected by them.

  Q21. In the Galapagos Islands, two species of finches coexist on the same island. One species has evolved a larger beak for cracking large seeds, while the other has a smaller beak for eating small seeds. If both species had identical beak sizes and ate the same seeds, the likely outcome would be:
    [ ] Both species would become predators
    [ ] Both species would thrive equally
    [*] One species would outcompete and exclude the other, according to the competitive exclusion principle
    [ ] Both species would go extinct simultaneously
    Explanation: According to competitive exclusion, two species with identical niches cannot coexist. The finches' different beak sizes represent character displacement through resource partitioning, allowing coexistence.

  Q22. A farmer discovers that mycorrhizal fungi on crop roots help plants absorb phosphorus from soil, while the plants provide sugars to the fungi. When the farmer uses a fungicide that kills the fungi, plant growth decreases significantly. This demonstrates:
    [ ] That plants do not need nutrients
    [ ] Commensalism, since only the plant benefits
    [ ] That fungi are harmful parasites
    [*] Mutualism, where both the plant and fungus benefit, and removing one partner harms the other
    Explanation: Mycorrhizal relationships are mutualistic: fungi enhance nutrient uptake for plants while receiving sugars in return. Disrupting this relationship demonstrates how dependent both partners are on each other.

  Q23. A tick attaches to a deer and feeds on its blood, potentially transmitting Lyme disease. The tick benefits while the deer is harmed. If the deer population crashes from disease, the tick population would also decline because:
    [ ] Ticks would switch to photosynthesis
    [ ] Disease does not affect tick populations
    [ ] Ticks do not need hosts
    [*] Parasites depend on their host populations; if hosts decline, parasites lose their resource base
    Explanation: Parasites are dependent on their host populations. If the host population crashes, parasites lose their food source and habitat, demonstrating the interconnected nature of parasitic relationships.

  Q24. On African savannas, oxpecker birds sit on rhinoceroses and eat ticks and parasites from their skin. The birds get food, and the rhinos get pest removal. Nearby, cattle egrets follow large herbivores and eat insects stirred up by their movement, but the herbivores are unaffected. These two scenarios illustrate:
    [*] Oxpeckers and rhinos show mutualism (+/+), while egrets and herbivores show commensalism (+/0)
    [ ] Both are examples of parasitism, since the birds exploit the large mammals for their own benefit
    [ ] Both are examples of predation, since both bird species actively hunt and consume the larger animals
    [ ] Both are examples of competition, because the birds and mammals compete for the same food resources
    Explanation: The oxpecker-rhino relationship is mutualism (both benefit), while the egret-herbivore relationship is commensalism (egrets benefit from insects stirred up, but herbivores are unaffected).

  Q25. Five species of warblers live in the same spruce trees in New England forests. Rather than competing directly, each species feeds at a different height and on different parts of the tree. This is an example of:
    [ ] All five species being the same species
    [ ] Parasitism among the warbler species
    [ ] Competitive exclusion driving four species extinct
    [*] Resource partitioning, where species divide habitat resources to reduce competition and coexist
    Explanation: MacArthur's warblers are a classic example of resource partitioning. By feeding at different heights and on different tree parts, the five species avoid direct competition and coexist in the same habitat.

  Q26. Monarch butterflies are toxic to birds due to chemicals from the milkweed they eat as caterpillars. Viceroy butterflies look almost identical to monarchs but are not toxic. Birds avoid eating viceroys because they resemble monarchs. This protective resemblance is called:
    [ ] Camouflage, where both butterfly species blend into their background foliage to avoid being detected
    [ ] Mutualism between the two butterfly species, as both gain protection from looking similar to each other
    [ ] Competitive exclusion, where the monarch population is gradually replacing the viceroy through competition
    [*] Batesian mimicry, where a harmless species imitates a harmful one to gain protection from predators
    Explanation: Batesian mimicry is an evolutionary strategy where a harmless species (viceroy) evolves to resemble a toxic or dangerous species (monarch), gaining protection because predators learn to avoid the harmful model.

  Q27. Clownfish live among the tentacles of sea anemones. The anemone's stinging tentacles protect the clownfish from predators, and the clownfish brings food to the anemone and defends it from butterflyfish. If the anemone dies, the clownfish loses its protection. This relationship demonstrates:
    [ ] Competition between the two species
    [ ] Commensalism, since only the clownfish benefits
    [*] Mutualism, where both species benefit: clownfish gains protection while anemone gains food and defense
    [ ] Parasitism, since the clownfish exploits the anemone
    Explanation: The clownfish-anemone relationship is a well-documented mutualism. Both partners receive significant benefits, and the loss of either partner negatively impacts the other.

  Q28. When European rabbits were introduced to Australia, they had no natural predators and outcompeted native marsupials for food and burrow sites. Many native species declined. This case study illustrates:
    [*] How interspecific competition from invasive species can devastate native communities when natural population controls are absent
    [ ] That competition never affects populations
    [ ] That introduced species always improve ecosystems
    [ ] That marsupials are always inferior competitors
    Explanation: European rabbits in Australia demonstrate destructive interspecific competition. Without natural predators or diseases, they outcompeted native species that had evolved without such intense competition.

  Q29. Acacia trees in Central America house aggressive ants in their hollow thorns and provide food bodies for the ants. In return, the ants attack herbivores and clear competing vegetation around the tree. When scientists removed the ants experimentally, the trees grew poorly and suffered more herbivory. This demonstrates:
    [ ] That acacia trees do not need protection
    [ ] That ants are parasites of acacia trees
    [ ] Commensalism, since only the ants benefit
    [*] Obligate mutualism, where both species depend on each other for survival and fitness
    Explanation: The acacia-ant relationship is an obligate mutualism: acacia trees provide shelter and food, while ants provide aggressive defense. Neither thrives without the other, demonstrating deep coevolutionary dependence.

  Q30. In a forest, a large oak tree shades out small plants beneath it, reducing their growth and survival. However, the small plants have no measurable effect on the oak tree. This interaction is an example of:
    [ ] Parasitism, because the oak tree extracts nutrients from the smaller plants growing beneath its canopy
    [ ] Competition, because both the oak tree and small plants are equally harmed by fighting for sunlight
    [ ] Mutualism, because the oak tree provides shelter to small plants which stabilize its root system
    [*] Amensalism, where the oak harms the small plants but is itself unaffected
    Explanation: This is amensalism (-/0): the oak tree negatively affects understory plants by blocking sunlight, but the small plants have no measurable impact on the large tree, making it a one-sided negative interaction.

--- Lesson 2.5: Succession (Primary & Secondary) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Succession (Primary & Secondary)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is ecological succession?
Ecological succession is the orderly, directional process of community change over time, where species replace one another as environmental conditions change, eventually reaching a relatively stable climax community.
What is a pioneer species?
Pioneer species are hardy organisms that are the first to colonize disturbed or new environments. In primary succession, these are lichens and mosses; in secondary succession, these are often fast-growing weeds and grasses.
What is a climax community?
A climax community is the mature, relatively stable endpoint of succession determined by a region's climate and soil conditions. It remains relatively unchanged unless a major disturbance occurs.
What is primary succession?
Primary succession begins on completely new or bare surfaces like cooled lava, exposed rock, or new sand dunes where no soil exists. It takes much longer than secondary succession because soil must develop.
What is secondary succession?
Secondary succession occurs after a disturbance (fire, flood, logging) that removes the community but leaves soil intact. It proceeds much faster than primary succession because the soil ecosystem remains.
What is a disturbance in ecology?
Ecological disturbances are events that alter ecosystem structure, such as fires, storms, floods, volcanic eruptions, or human activities. They play a natural role in maintaining biodiversity and driving succession.
What is a sere?
A sere is the complete sequence of successional stages (seral stages) from initial colonization to the climax community. Each stage in the sere is called a seral stage.
What is facilitation in succession?
Facilitation occurs when pioneer species change the environment (adding soil, shade, nutrients) in ways that allow later successional species to establish. For example, nitrogen-fixing plants enrich soil for later species.
What is inhibition in succession?
Inhibition is a succession model where established species prevent or slow the colonization of new species through competition, allelopathy (chemical inhibition), or physical occupation of space.
What is the intermediate disturbance hypothesis?
The intermediate disturbance hypothesis proposes that biodiversity is highest at moderate disturbance levels because this creates a mix of early and late successional habitats, supporting diverse species.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of primary succession?
Plants colonizing a cooled lava flow is primary succession because the lava surface has no pre-existing soil. The process begins with lichens and slowly progresses over decades or centuries.
Example: After the eruption of Mount St. Helens in 1980, the surrounding landscape was covered in volcanic ash and rock with no soil. Over the following decades, scientists observed lichens colonizing rocks, followed by mosses, then grasses, then shrubs, and eventually trees. This recovery is an example of:
Mount St. Helens is a classic example of primary succession. The volcanic eruption destroyed all existing soil and organisms, requiring colonization to begin from scratch on bare rock and ash.
Example: A lake gradually fills in with sediment and organic matter over thousands of years, transitioning from open water to a marsh, then a meadow, and finally a forest. This long-term process is an example of:
Lake succession (hydrosere) is primary succession where sediment accumulation and plant growth gradually fill in a lake, transitioning it through marsh and meadow stages to eventually become a terrestrial community.
Example: An ecologist studying a forest notices that after a moderate windstorm created several gaps in the canopy, new tree seedlings of different species are growing in the light-filled gaps. The result is a forest with trees of different ages and species. This supports:
Canopy gaps from moderate disturbance create patches of early successional habitat within a mature forest, resulting in a mosaic of succession stages that supports more species than an undisturbed uniform canopy.

--- Lesson 2.5: Succession (Primary & Secondary) (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Primary succession begins on:
    [ ] Only in tropical forest environments where warm temperatures and high rainfall enable rapid colonization
    [ ] Land that previously had established soil and vegetation but was cleared by human agricultural activity
    [*] Bare rock or surfaces with no pre-existing soil, such as after a volcanic eruption or glacier retreat
    [ ] Agricultural fields and farmland that have been abandoned and are returning to their natural wild state
    Explanation: Primary succession begins on newly exposed surfaces devoid of soil, such as cooled lava flows, exposed rock after glacier retreat, or newly formed islands. It is a slow process because soil must first develop.

  Q2. Secondary succession occurs after:
    [ ] A volcanic eruption that creates entirely new land surfaces where no soil has ever existed before
    [*] A disturbance that destroys the community but leaves the soil intact, such as a fire or abandoned farmland
    [ ] A glacier that completely scrapes away all soil and rock down to the bedrock underneath it
    [ ] Disturbances that only occur in ocean environments through underwater volcanic or seismic activity
    Explanation: Secondary succession occurs when a disturbance like fire, flood, or farming destroys vegetation but the soil remains. It is faster than primary succession because soil, seeds, and root systems are already present.

  Q3. Pioneer species in primary succession are typically:
    [*] Lichens and mosses that can colonize bare rock and begin soil formation
    [ ] Large mammals that migrate to newly exposed areas and establish feeding territories for herds
    [ ] Fish species that colonize newly formed freshwater pools and create the basis for aquatic food webs
    [ ] Large trees that rapidly establish deep root systems and create forest canopy in barren areas
    Explanation: Pioneer species like lichens and mosses are the first organisms to colonize bare rock. Lichens secrete acids that break down rock, and their decomposition begins creating a thin layer of soil.

  Q4. A climax community is:
    [ ] The first pioneer community to colonize a newly exposed area at the very beginning of succession
    [ ] A desert ecosystem that always forms as the permanent end stage of succession in any environment
    [*] A relatively stable, mature community that represents the endpoint of ecological succession for a given area
    [ ] A community that changes rapidly in species composition and never reaches ecological stability
    Explanation: A climax community is the final, relatively stable stage of succession for a particular climate and location. It persists until a major disturbance resets the succession process.

  Q5. Secondary succession is faster than primary succession because:
    [ ] It skips all intermediate stages
    [*] Soil, seeds, and root systems already exist, providing a foundation for regrowth
    [ ] Humans always assist in the process
    [ ] It occurs in warmer climates
    Explanation: Secondary succession is faster because the soil with its seed bank, nutrients, and microorganisms remains intact. Plants can regrow from surviving roots and seeds rather than starting from bare rock.

  Q6. Which of the following is an example of primary succession?

  Q7. As succession progresses, biodiversity generally:
    [ ] Decreases continuously
    [ ] Stays the same
    [ ] Only changes in aquatic ecosystems
    [*] Increases as more species colonize and ecological niches diversify
    Explanation: Biodiversity typically increases during succession as pioneer species modify the environment, creating new habitats and niches that allow additional species to establish and coexist.

  Q8. Fire can be a natural and beneficial part of some ecosystems because it:
    [ ] Always reduces biodiversity permanently by eliminating all native species from the affected fire zone

    [ ] Maximum disturbance produces maximum biodiversity
    [ ] No disturbance produces maximum biodiversity
    [*] Moderate levels of disturbance maintain the highest biodiversity
    Explanation: The intermediate disturbance hypothesis proposes that moderate disturbance creates a mosaic of habitats at different succession stages, supporting the greatest variety of species from both early and late successional stages.

--- Lesson 2.6: Biodiversity & Conservation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Biodiversity & Conservation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary cause of biodiversity loss worldwide?
Habitat destruction and fragmentation is the leading driver of biodiversity loss globally. When natural habitats are cleared for agriculture, development, or resource extraction, species lose the space and resources they need to survive and reproduce.
What is species richness?
Species richness is simply the count of different species present in a defined area, without considering how many individuals of each species exist. It is one of the simplest measures of biodiversity and is often used alongside species evenness to characterize community diversity.
What is an endemic species?
An endemic species is one that is native to and found only in a particular geographic region. Endemic species are especially vulnerable to extinction because their entire population is confined to a limited area, making them susceptible to localized threats.
What is a keystone species?
A keystone species has an outsized impact on community structure and function relative to its population size. Removal of a keystone species, such as sea otters in kelp forest ecosystems, can trigger cascading changes that fundamentally alter the entire community.
What is habitat fragmentation?
Habitat fragmentation occurs when large, continuous habitats are divided into smaller, disconnected patches by roads, agriculture, or development. Fragmentation reduces habitat area, increases edge effects, isolates populations, and limits gene flow between them.
What is ex situ conservation?
Ex situ conservation involves maintaining and breeding species outside their natural habitat in facilities like zoos, aquariums, botanical gardens, and seed banks. This approach serves as an insurance policy against extinction and can support reintroduction programs when wild habitats are restored.
What is genetic diversity?
Genetic diversity refers to the total variety of genes, alleles, and genotypes within a species or population. High genetic diversity enhances a population's ability to adapt to changing environmental conditions, resist diseases, and maintain long-term reproductive fitness.
What is an indicator species?
An indicator species is an organism whose presence, absence, or condition reflects the overall health of an ecosystem. For example, amphibians are often used as indicator species because their permeable skin makes them highly sensitive to water and air quality changes.
What is the edge effect in ecology?
The edge effect describes the altered environmental conditions and species composition found at the transition zone between two different habitats. Habitat edges often experience increased light, wind, temperature fluctuations, and invasion by generalist or invasive species.
What is a wildlife corridor?
A wildlife corridor is a strip of natural habitat that connects two or more larger habitat patches, allowing animals to move safely between them. Corridors help maintain genetic diversity, support migration patterns, and enable species to access resources across fragmented landscapes.
Real-World Applications
Example: What percentage of Earth's species are scientists estimated to have formally identified and described?
Scientists estimate that only about 10-20% of all species on Earth have been formally described. Millions of species, particularly insects, fungi, and deep-sea organisms, remain undiscovered, making conservation prioritization challenging.
Example: A marine biologist discovers that removing sea otters from a coastal kelp forest leads to a population explosion of sea urchins, which then overgraze and destroy the kelp beds. What does this example best illustrate about biodiversity conservation?
This is a classic example of a trophic cascade triggered by removing a keystone species. Sea otters control sea urchin populations, and without this predation, urchins overgraze kelp, transforming diverse kelp forests into barren urchin barrens.
Example: An island nation reports that an introduced rat species has driven three endemic bird species to extinction. Wildlife managers are now considering eradicating the rats. Which conservation challenge does this scenario primarily address?
Invasive species are one of the leading causes of extinction, particularly on islands where endemic species evolved without exposure to mainland predators. Invasive rat eradication programs on islands have successfully allowed native species to recover in many documented cases.
Example: A tropical country faces pressure to clear rainforest for palm oil plantations. An economist argues that the rainforest is worth more standing because it provides carbon storage, water filtration, and ecotourism revenue. This argument is based on:
This argument relies on assigning monetary value to the ecosystem services that biodiversity provides, such as carbon sequestration, water purification, and tourism. Economic valuation of ecosystem services has become an influential tool for making conservation arguments to policymakers and businesses.

--- Lesson 2.7: Case Studies in Ecology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Ecology
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a trophic cascade?
A trophic cascade occurs when changes at one trophic level cause cascading effects through the food web. For example, removing top predators allows herbivore populations to explode, which can overgraze vegetation and alter habitat structure for many other species.
What is eutrophication?
Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients, primarily nitrogen and phosphorus, enter a water body and stimulate excessive algal growth. When these algae die and decompose, the process depletes dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic or anoxic dead zones that cannot support most aquatic life.
What is bioaccumulation?
Bioaccumulation is the process by which chemicals such as pesticides, heavy metals, or other toxins accumulate in an organism's tissues faster than they can be metabolized or excreted. Over time, even low environmental concentrations can reach harmful levels within individual organisms.
What is biomagnification?
Biomagnification occurs when toxin concentrations increase at each successive level of a food chain. Top predators accumulate the highest concentrations because they consume large numbers of contaminated prey. DDT biomagnification in bald eagles causing eggshell thinning is a classic example.
What is an ecological dead zone?
An ecological dead zone is a hypoxic area in a body of water where dissolved oxygen levels are too low to support most marine life. Dead zones are typically caused by eutrophication, where excess nutrients stimulate algal blooms whose decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen.
What is coral bleaching?
Coral bleaching occurs when environmental stressors, most commonly elevated water temperatures, cause corals to expel the symbiotic zooxanthellae algae that provide them with food and color. Without these algae, the coral appears white and may die if conditions do not improve quickly.
What is the term 'overfishing' defined as in fisheries ecology?
Overfishing occurs when fish are caught faster than populations can reproduce, leading to declining stocks. When harvest rates consistently exceed the maximum sustainable yield, populations can collapse, as demonstrated by the Atlantic cod fishery off Newfoundland.
What is maximum sustainable yield (MSY)?
Maximum sustainable yield is the largest number of individuals that can be harvested from a renewable resource population without reducing the population's ability to sustain itself. Exceeding MSY over time leads to population decline and potential collapse.
What is a keystone predator?
A keystone predator is a predator whose ecological influence on a community is disproportionately large relative to its abundance. The sea star Pisaster ochraceus in Pacific intertidal zones is a classic example, as its removal allows mussels to dominate and exclude other species.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The Exxon Valdez spill demonstrated that ecological recovery from pollution events can take decades. Oil persisted in beach sediments for years, continuing to harm organisms, and the loss of breeding adults created a demographic bottleneck that slowed population recovery long after the initial spill.
Real-World Applications
Example: The reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone National Park in 1995 led to significant changes throughout the ecosystem. This is an example of:
The reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone triggered a trophic cascade, where the effects of a top predator rippled down through multiple trophic levels. Wolves reduced elk overgrazing, allowing vegetation to recover, which stabilized riverbanks and benefited many other species.
Example: The Great Barrier Reef has experienced multiple mass coral bleaching events primarily due to:
Mass coral bleaching occurs when elevated water temperatures stress corals, causing them to expel their symbiotic zooxanthellae algae. Without these algae, corals turn white and lose their primary energy source, and prolonged bleaching leads to coral death and reef ecosystem collapse.
Example: A city restores a former industrial site by removing contaminated soil, planting native species, and reintroducing a natural stream channel. Five years later, surveys show the site supports 40 native plant species, nesting birds, and a reproducing fish population. This is an example of:
This scenario describes ecological restoration of a brownfield site, where contaminated or degraded land is actively rehabilitated to support native ecosystems. Successful restoration requires addressing contamination, restoring physical habitat structure, and reestablishing native plant and animal communities.
Example: A conservation group manages to restore wetlands along a river that feeds into a lake suffering from harmful algal blooms. Over the following years, algal blooms decrease significantly. Local officials ask whether the wetlands or new wastewater treatment plants deserve the credit. How would an ecologist evaluate this?
Environmental problems typically have multiple causes and benefit from multiple solutions. Wetlands naturally filter nutrients from agricultural runoff while wastewater treatment reduces point-source nutrient inputs. Evaluating the relative contribution of each requires monitoring nutrient loads from different sources.

These nutrients fuel algal blooms that create oxygen-depleted dead zones, harming fish, shellfish, and submerged aquatic vegetation.

    [ ] Improved pest control for agriculture
    [*] The extinction or severe decline of most native bird species
    Explanation: The brown tree snake, accidentally introduced to Guam, decimated nearly all of the island's native forest bird species because they had no evolutionary defenses against this predator.     Explanation: Biomagnification occurs when toxin concentrations increase at each successive level of a food chain.     Explanation: An ecological dead zone is a hypoxic area in a body of water where dissolved oxygen levels are too low to support most marine life. Dead zones are typically caused by eutrophication, where excess nutrients stimulate algal blooms whose decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen.

  Q16. What is coral bleaching?

    Explanation: Coral bleaching occurs when environmental stressors, most commonly elevated water temperatures, cause corals to expel the symbiotic zooxanthellae algae that provide them with food and color. --- Lesson 2.8: AP Prep: Population Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Population Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is carrying capacity (K)?
Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely given available resources such as food, water, shelter, and space. It is a dynamic value that can change as environmental conditions change.
What is the Rule of 70 used to calculate?
The Rule of 70 states that the doubling time of an exponentially growing population equals 70 divided by the growth rate expressed as a percentage. For example, a population growing at 2% per year will double in approximately 70/2 = 35 years.
What is a density-independent factor?
Density-independent factors affect populations regardless of their size or density. Natural disasters, extreme weather events, and seasonal changes are examples that kill or displace a proportion of the population without regard to how many individuals are present.
What is a survivorship curve?
A survivorship curve plots the proportion of a cohort still alive at each successive age. The three standard types (I, II, III) describe different patterns of mortality across a lifespan and reflect different life history strategies among species.
What is environmental resistance?
Environmental resistance is the sum of all biotic and abiotic factors that prevent a population from growing exponentially. These include predation, disease, competition, limited resources, and adverse weather, which collectively determine the carrying capacity.
What is a K-selected species?
K-selected species are adapted to stable environments and tend to have populations near carrying capacity. They invest heavily in fewer offspring through long gestation periods, extensive parental care, and late maturity. Examples include elephants, whales, and humans.
What is a population bottleneck?
A population bottleneck occurs when a population undergoes a dramatic size reduction due to a catastrophic event, reducing its genetic diversity. Even if the population recovers in numbers, the lost genetic variation can reduce the population's ability to adapt to future environmental changes.
What is the difference between immigration and emigration in population ecology?
Immigration adds individuals to a population when they move in from elsewhere, while emigration removes individuals when they move out. Together with births and deaths, immigration and emigration determine the overall change in population size over time.
What is the minimum viable population (MVP)?
The minimum viable population is the smallest number of individuals needed for a population to have a high probability of surviving over a specified time period, typically 100 or 1,000 years. Below this threshold, populations face increased risks from genetic drift, inbreeding, and random events.
A country's population is 10 million, with a birth rate of 30 per 1,000 and a death rate of 10 per 1,000 per year. Ignoring migration, what is the population growth rate and approximate doubling time?
The growth rate r = (birth rate - death rate)/1000 = (30-10)/1000 = 0.02 or 2%. Using the Rule of 70: doubling time = 70/2 = 35 years. This means the population would grow from 10 million to 20 million in approximately 35 years without changes in birth or death rates.
A population of bacteria in a lab culture shows the following pattern: slow initial growth, rapid exponential increase, then leveling off. When graphed, this produces an S-shaped curve. A student asks why the growth slowed. What is the best explanation?
The S-shaped logistic growth curve results from density-dependent factors. As bacteria multiplied, nutrients became depleted and metabolic waste products accumulated, slowing reproduction and increasing death rates. This environmental resistance brought the population toward carrying capacity.
Real-World Applications
Example: A wildlife manager wants to maximize the sustainable harvest from a deer population with K = 2,000. According to the logistic growth model, at what population size should the herd be maintained to maximize annual harvest?
Maximum sustainable yield occurs at N = K/2. With K = 2,000, the population should be maintained at 1,000 deer. At this size, the population produces the greatest surplus of individuals per year that can be harvested without reducing the population below this optimal level.
Example: A fisheries biologist monitors a lake where bass have been overfished. She implements catch limits, and after five years the population has recovered to K/2. She recommends maintaining the population at this level. Why does K/2 represent the optimal management target?
At N = K/2, the logistic growth model predicts maximum population growth rate (dN/dt). This means the population produces the largest surplus of individuals per year at this point. Harvesting this surplus allows the greatest sustained catch while keeping the population stable at K/2.

  Q1. In the exponential growth model, the equation dN/dt = rN assumes that:
    [ ] The population is at carrying capacity
    [ ] The death rate exceeds the birth rate
    [ ] Resources are limited and competition occurs
    [*] The population has unlimited resources and no environmental resistance
    Explanation: The exponential growth model assumes ideal conditions with unlimited resources, no predation, no disease, and no competition. Under these assumptions, the population grows at a constant per capita rate (r), producing a J-shaped growth curve.

  Q2. In the logistic growth model, what happens to population growth rate as N approaches K?
    [ ] Growth rate increases exponentially
    [ ] Growth rate becomes negative permanently
    [*] Growth rate approaches zero
    [ ] Growth rate remains constant
    Explanation: In the logistic growth model (dN/dt = rN(K-N)/K), as the population size N approaches the carrying capacity K, the term (K-N)/K approaches zero, which reduces the growth rate to near zero. This creates the characteristic S-shaped sigmoid curve.

  Q3. The intrinsic rate of natural increase (r) in a population is calculated as:
    [ ] Death rate minus birth rate
    [*] Birth rate minus death rate
    [ ] Birth rate divided by death rate
    [ ] Birth rate multiplied by death rate
    Explanation: The intrinsic rate of natural increase (r) equals the per capita birth rate (b) minus the per capita death rate (d). When r is positive, the population grows; when r is negative, the population declines; and when r equals zero, the population is stable.

    [ ] S-shaped (sigmoid) curve

    [ ] 300 deer
    [*] 72 deer
    Explanation: Using the logistic equation: dN/dt = rN(K-N)/K = 0.3 x 600 x (1000-600)/1000 = 0.3 x 600 x 0.4 = 72 deer per year. The growth is significantly slowed compared to exponential growth (which would add 180) because the population has already used 60% of the carrying capacity.

  Q23. A wildlife manager wants to maximize the sustainable harvest from a deer population with K = 2,000. According to the logistic growth model, at what population size should the herd be maintained to maximize annual harvest?
    [ ] 1,500 deer
    [*] 1,000 deer
    [ ] 2,000 deer
    [ ] 500 deer
    Explanation: Maximum sustainable yield occurs at N = K/2. With K = 2,000, the population should be maintained at 1,000 deer. At this size, the population produces the greatest surplus of individuals per year that can be harvested without reducing the population below this optimal level.

  Q24. A country's population is 10 million, with a birth rate of 30 per 1,000 and a death rate of 10 per 1,000 per year. Ignoring migration, what is the population growth rate and approximate doubling time?
    [ ] r = 1%, doubling time approximately 70 years
    [*] r = 2%, doubling time approximately 35 years
    [ ] r = 3%, doubling time approximately 23 years
    [ ] r = 4%, doubling time approximately 18 years
    Explanation: The growth rate r = (birth rate - death rate)/1000 = (30-10)/1000 = 0.02 or 2%. Using the Rule of 70: doubling time = 70/2 = 35 years. This means the population would grow from 10 million to 20 million in approximately 35 years without changes in birth or death rates.

  Q25. Two species of Paramecium are grown separately and each thrives in the lab. When placed together in the same culture with limited resources, one species declines to extinction while the other persists. This experiment demonstrates:
    [ ] Predator-prey dynamics, particularly regarding will based on the prevailing evidence
    [ ] Mutualism between closely related species in most standard conditions
    [ ] Density-independent mortality, particularly regarding typically as determined by
    [*] The competitive exclusion principle, where two species cannot coexist on the same limiting resource
    Explanation: This is Gause's classic competitive exclusion experiment. When two species compete for identical resources in a uniform environment, the species with even a slight competitive advantage will eventually exclude the other. This principle explains why coexisting species typically partition resources.

  Q26. A population of bacteria in a lab culture shows the following pattern: slow initial growth, rapid exponential increase, then leveling off. When graphed, this produces an S-shaped curve. A student asks why the growth slowed. What is the best explanation?
    [ ] The temperature in the lab decreased over time
    [ ] The bacteria reached their maximum possible lifespan
    [ ] The bacteria mutated to reproduce more slowly
    [*] As the population grew, resources became scarce and waste products accumulated, increasing environmental resistance
    Explanation: The S-shaped logistic growth curve results from density-dependent factors. As bacteria multiplied, nutrients became depleted and metabolic waste products accumulated, slowing reproduction and increasing death rates. This environmental resistance brought the population toward carrying capacity.

  Q27. A population of elk has K = 800 and currently has 800 individuals. A harsh winter kills 200 elk, reducing the population to 600. According to the logistic model, what will happen next?
    [ ] The population will remain stable at 600
    [ ] The carrying capacity will permanently drop to 600
    [ ] The population will continue to decline
    [*] The population will grow back toward K = 800 because more resources are now available per individual
    Explanation: When a population drops below carrying capacity, reduced competition for resources leads to increased survival and reproduction, driving population growth back toward K. At N = 600 with K = 800, the logistic term (K-N)/K = 0.25, allowing positive growth to occur.

  Q28. An ecologist compares two island bird populations of the same species. Island A has 50 birds and Island B has 500 birds. Both islands are hit by a severe storm that randomly kills 40% of birds on each island. Which population faces a greater risk of extinction and why?
    [*] Island A, because the remaining 30 individuals may fall below the minimum viable population and are more vulnerable to random events
    [ ] Both face equal risk because the same percentage was killed
    [ ] Neither faces extinction risk from a single storm event
    [ ] Island B, because it loses more total individuals
    Explanation: Small populations are more vulnerable to extinction from random events because they have fewer individuals to buffer losses. Island A's 30 survivors may fall below the minimum viable population, face genetic problems from inbreeding, and have a higher probability of extinction from subsequent random events.

  Q29. A fisheries biologist monitors a lake where bass have been overfished. She implements catch limits, and after five years the population has recovered to K/2. She recommends maintaining the population at this level. Why does K/2 represent the optimal management target?
    [ ] Because at K/2 there are no density-dependent effects
    [ ] Because K/2 is the smallest stable population size
    [*] Because at K/2 the population produces its maximum surplus, allowing the greatest sustainable harvest
    [ ] Because at K/2 individual fish are largest and most valuable
    Explanation: At N = K/2, the logistic growth model predicts maximum population growth rate (dN/dt). This means the population produces the largest surplus of individuals per year at this point. Harvesting this surplus allows the greatest sustained catch while keeping the population stable at K/2.

  Q30. A conservation biologist models two endangered species. Species A has r = 0.5 and K = 100. Species B has r = 0.05 and K = 10,000. Both populations are currently at 10% of their carrying capacity. If both face the same proportional habitat loss that reduces K by 50%, which species is likely more vulnerable to extinction?
    [ ] Species B, because it has a lower growth rate
    [ ] Both species are equally vulnerable because they lose the same proportion
    [*] Species A, because it has a smaller carrying capacity and the 50% K reduction leaves fewer individuals to maintain genetic viability
    [ ] Neither species is at risk because they are both below their original K
    Explanation: When K is reduced by 50%, Species A's new K becomes 50, leaving only about 10 individuals at 10% capacity. This is dangerously close to or below the minimum viable population, where genetic drift, inbreeding, and random events can drive extinction. Species B, with a new K of 5,000 and about 1,000 individuals, has a much larger buffer against extinction.
