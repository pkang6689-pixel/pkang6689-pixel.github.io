--- Lesson 5.1: Fossil Fuels (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fossil Fuels
Fossil Fuels
Coal, oil (petroleum), and natural gas supply ~80% of global energy. Formed from ancient organic matter over millions of years.
Impacts  • CO₂ emissions: Leading cause of climate change.  • Air pollution: SO₂, NOₓ, particulates → smog, acid rain, respiratory illness.  • Extraction damage: Mountaintop removal, oil spills, fracking (hydrofracturing) → water contamination.
Coal is dirtiest per unit energy; natural gas cleanest fossil fuel (~50% less CO₂ than coal). Oil used mostly for transportation.

--- Lesson 5.2: Nuclear Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nuclear Energy
Nuclear Energy
Fission: Splitting heavy atoms (uranium-235, plutonium-239) releases enormous energy. Provides ~10% of global electricity with near-zero CO₂ during operation.
Pros & Cons  • Pros: High energy density, low carbon, reliable baseload power.  • Cons: Radioactive waste (remains hazardous for thousands of years), meltdown risk (Chernobyl, Fukushima), high construction costs, uranium mining impacts.

--- Lesson 5.3: Renewable Energy (Solar, Wind, Hydro, Geothermal) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Renewable Energy (Solar, Wind, Hydro, Geothermal)
Renewable Energy SourcesSolar
Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight directly to electricity. Fastest-growing energy source; costs dropped ~90% since 2010.
Wind
Turbines convert kinetic energy of wind. 2nd-fastest growing; concerns include bird/bat mortality and visual impact.
Hydroelectric
Largest renewable source (~16% of global electricity). Dams displace communities and alter ecosystems.
Geothermal
Heat from Earth's interior. Limited to tectonically active areas but very reliable.

--- Lesson 5.4: Energy Efficiency & Conservation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy Efficiency & Conservation
Energy Efficiency & Conservation
Efficiency: Getting more useful work from less energy input. Conservation: Using less energy through behavioral changes.
Key Strategies  • LED lighting uses ~75% less energy than incandescent.  • Building insulation, efficient appliances, ENERGY STAR standards.  • Cogeneration (CHP): Uses waste heat from electricity generation for heating (~80% efficiency vs. ~33% for conventional plants).  • Transportation: EVs, public transit, fuel economy standards.

--- Lesson 5.5: Case Studies: Energy Policy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies: Energy Policy
Case Studies: Energy PolicyGermany's Energiewende
Ambitious transition to renewables. ~50% of electricity from renewables (2023). Also phased out nuclear. Higher costs; criticized for coal persistence during transition.
Iceland
~100% of electricity from renewables (geothermal + hydro), thanks to abundant volcanic activity. Model for renewable-powered society.
US Energy Policy
Mix of federal/state policies: clean energy tax credits, CAFE standards, state renewable portfolio standards. Inflation Reduction Act (2022) largest US climate investment.

--- Lesson 5.6: AP Prep: Energy Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Energy Calculations
AP Prep: Energy CalculationsKey Formulas  • Efficiency: η = (useful energy output / total energy input) × 100%  • Power: P = Energy / Time (watts = joules/seconds)  • Electrical energy: E = P × t (kWh = kW × hours)  • Cost: Cost = kWh × price per kWh  • 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
