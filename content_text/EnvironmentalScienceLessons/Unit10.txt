--- Lesson 10.1: Review: Key Concepts (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Review: Key Concepts
Review: Key Concepts Across Environmental ScienceCore Themes  • Ecosystems & Biodiversity: Energy flow, nutrient cycling, species interactions, biomes, biodiversity value.  • Population & Resources: Carrying capacity, demographic transition, renewable vs. nonrenewable resources.  • Pollution & Health: Air, water, soil pollution sources and effects; bioaccumulation; environmental health.  • Climate Change: Greenhouse effect, evidence, impacts, mitigation, adaptation, international agreements.  • Sustainability: Three pillars, IPAT, ecological footprint, sustainable practices.  • Policy & Law: Major US laws, international treaties, environmental justice.

--- Lesson 10.1: Review: Key Concepts (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The 10% rule states that approximately _____ of energy transfers between trophic levels.
    [ ] 100%
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 25%
    [*] ~10% (the rest is lost as heat through cellular respiration)
    Explanation: Energy loss at each level.

  Q2. Primary productivity is highest in:
    [ ] Deserts
    [*] Tropical rainforests and estuaries (high sunlight, water, and nutrients)
    [ ] Tundra
    [ ] Open ocean per area
    Explanation: Most productive ecosystems.

  Q3. The water cycle is driven primarily by:
    [ ] Gravity alone
    [*] Solar energy (evaporation) and gravity (precipitation, runoff)
    [ ] Only human activity
    [ ] Only wind
    Explanation: Solar-powered cycle.

  Q4. The nitrogen cycle requires _____ to convert N₂ gas into usable forms.
    [ ] Photosynthesis
    [*] Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (and human-made processes like Haber-Bosch)
    [ ] Only lightning
    [ ] Only decomposition
    Explanation: Biological fixation.

  Q5. Phosphorus differs from other nutrient cycles because it:
    [ ] Has a gas phase
    [*] Has no significant atmospheric component — cycles through rocks, water, soil, and organisms
    [ ] Is not essential
    [ ] Cycles fastest
    Explanation: No gas phase.

  Q6. Demographic transition describes how:
    [ ] Population always grows
    [*] Countries move from high birth/death rates to low birth/death rates as they develop economically
    [ ] Population always declines
    [ ] Nothing changes
    Explanation: Population pattern with development.

  Q7. The greatest threat to biodiversity globally is:
    [ ] Pollution alone
    [*] Habitat loss and degradation (followed by overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, and climate change)
    [ ] Only climate change
    [ ] Only hunting
    Explanation: #1 threat: habitat loss.

  Q8. Eutrophication is caused by:
    [ ] Clean water
    [*] Excess nutrients (N and P) in water bodies → algal blooms → decomposition → oxygen depletion → aquatic dead zones
    [ ] Only temperature
    [ ] Only sediment
    Explanation: Nutrient overload.

  Q9. The ozone layer protects life by:
    [ ] Trapping heat
    [*] Absorbing harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation from the sun (preventing skin cancer, cataracts, and ecosystem damage)
    [ ] Reflecting visible light
    [ ] Producing oxygen
    Explanation: UV shield.

  Q10. Acid rain is caused by emissions of:
    [ ] Only CO₂
    [*] SO₂ and NOₓ (from fossil fuel combustion) reacting with water vapor to form sulfuric and nitric acid
    [ ] Only natural gases
    [ ] Only methane
    Explanation: Acidic deposition.

  Q11. The tragedy of the commons occurs when:
    [ ] Resources are privately owned
    [*] Individuals overuse shared resources because each person's benefit outweighs their share of the cost
    [ ] Resources are unlimited
    [ ] Everyone cooperates
    Explanation: Overuse of shared resources.

  Q12. Keystone species are important because they:
    [ ] Are the most numerous
    [*] Have a disproportionately large impact on ecosystem structure and function relative to their abundance
    [ ] Only affect one species
    [ ] Are always predators
    Explanation: Outsized importance.

  Q13. R-selected species vs. K-selected species differ in:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Reproductive strategy: r-selected = many offspring, little care, rapid growth; K-selected = few offspring, much care, slow growth
    [ ] Only size
    [ ] Only habitat
    Explanation: Life history strategies.

  Q14. Human ecological footprint exceeds Earth's biocapacity, meaning:
    [ ] We're fine
    [*] We are using resources faster than Earth can regenerate them (ecological deficit since the 1970s)
    [ ] We're under-consuming
    [ ] Balance is maintained
    Explanation: Overshoot.

  Q15. Environmental Impact Assessments are required by:
    [ ] No law
    [*] NEPA (National Environmental Policy Act) — for major federal actions significantly affecting the environment
    [ ] Only state law
    [ ] Only for businesses
    Explanation: Federal requirement.

  Q16. The Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and ESA are all enforced primarily by:
    [ ] States only
    [*] The EPA (Environmental Protection Agency) and relevant agencies (USFWS for ESA)
    [ ] Only courts
    [ ] Only local government
    Explanation: Federal enforcement.

  Q17. Environmental justice means that:
    [ ] Only wealthy areas are protected
    [*] All communities receive fair treatment and meaningful involvement in environmental decisions, regardless of race or income
    [ ] Only environmental issues matter
    [ ] Justice and environment are separate
    Explanation: Equity in environment.

  Q18. The Paris Agreement asks each nation to set _____ for emission reductions.
    [ ] Binding targets
    [*] Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) — voluntary, updated every 5 years with increasing ambition
    [ ] No targets
    [ ] Only one-time goals
    Explanation: Bottom-up pledges.

  Q19. For AP review, the most important skill is:
    [ ] Only memorizing facts
    [*] Connecting concepts across topics — e.g., linking agriculture to water pollution to dead zones to policy solutions
    [ ] Only calculations
    [ ] Only definitions
    Explanation: Systems thinking.

  Q20. The central message of environmental science is:
    [ ] Nature is separate from humans
    [*] Human well-being depends on the health of Earth's systems — understanding these connections enables better decisions
    [ ] Nothing can be done
    [ ] Only technology matters
    Explanation: Interdependence.

--- Lesson 10.2: AP-Style Practice (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP-Style Practice
AP-Style Practice Questions
This lesson focuses on practice with the types of questions found on the AP Environmental Science (APES) exam: multiple choice and free-response, covering all major topics.
Exam Format  • 80 multiple-choice questions (90 minutes) — 60% of score.  • 3 free-response questions (70 minutes) — 40% of score.  • FRQ types: Design an Investigation, Analyze an Environmental Problem, Propose a Solution.

--- Lesson 10.2: AP-Style Practice (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The APES exam has _____ multiple-choice questions.
    [ ] 40
    [ ] 60
    [*] 80
    [ ] 100
    Explanation: Section I format.

  Q2. The three FRQ types on APES are:
    [ ] Only essays
    [*] Design an Investigation, Analyze an Environmental Problem, and Propose a Solution
    [ ] Only calculations
    [ ] Only short answers
    Explanation: FRQ categories.

  Q3. A well-constructed FRQ response uses:
    [ ] Only opinions
    [*] Claim-Evidence-Reasoning: clear claims backed by specific scientific evidence with logical reasoning
    [ ] Only vocabulary
    [ ] Only diagrams
    Explanation: Response structure.

  Q4. If a city of 500,000 produces 2 kg of waste per person per day, total daily waste is:
    [ ] 500,000 kg
    [*] 1,000,000 kg (1,000 metric tons)
    [ ] 100,000 kg
    [ ] 10,000,000 kg
    Explanation: Per capita calculation.

  Q5. A population of 10 million with birth rate 15/1000 and death rate 8/1000 has a growth rate of:
    [ ] 15‰
    [ ] 8‰
    [*] 7‰ (0.7% per year)
    [ ] 23‰
    Explanation: Birth rate − death rate.

  Q6. Using the Rule of 70, a population growing at 2% will double in:
    [ ] 20 years
    [*] 35 years (70 ÷ 2)
    [ ] 70 years
    [ ] 140 years
    Explanation: Doubling time calculation.

  Q7. If a coal plant emits 0.9 kg CO₂ per kWh and a solar farm emits 0.05 kg, switching 1,000,000 kWh saves:
    [ ] 50,000 kg
    [*] 850,000 kg of CO₂ (0.9 − 0.05 = 0.85 per kWh × 1,000,000)
    [ ] 900,000 kg
    [ ] 100,000 kg
    Explanation: Emission reduction calculation.

  Q8. When designing an investigation for AP, include:
    [ ] Only a hypothesis
    [*] Hypothesis, independent/dependent variables, control, procedure, and how you'd analyze data
    [ ] Only the question
    [ ] Only the conclusion
    Explanation: Complete experimental design.

  Q9. 'Analyze an Environmental Problem' FRQs require:
    [ ] Only describing the problem
    [*] Identifying causes, describing effects on ecosystems AND humans, and explaining the science behind the connections
    [ ] Only solutions
    [ ] Only one paragraph
    Explanation: Multi-part analysis.

  Q10. 'Propose a Solution' FRQs require:
    [ ] Only naming one solution
    [*] Describing the solution, explaining how it addresses the problem, identifying trade-offs, and justifying why it's effective
    [ ] Only technology
    [ ] Only government action
    Explanation: Complete solution proposal.

  Q11. AP scoring rubrics award points for:
    [ ] Length of response
    [*] Specific, correct, relevant information — each point has clear earning criteria
    [ ] Good handwriting
    [ ] Vocabulary use only
    Explanation: Precision matters.

  Q12. To maximize AP FRQ scores, students should:
    [ ] Write everything they know
    [*] Answer exactly what is asked, be specific, provide evidence, and address all parts of the question
    [ ] Write short answers
    [ ] Only use bullet points
    Explanation: Targeted responses.

  Q13. Energy efficiency calculations may ask: if a car gets 25 mpg and drives 12,000 mi/year, annual fuel use is:
    [ ] 250 gallons
    [*] 480 gallons (12,000 ÷ 25)
    [ ] 1,200 gallons
    [ ] 120 gallons
    Explanation: Division.

  Q14. If electricity costs $0.12/kWh and a household uses 900 kWh/month, monthly cost is:
    [ ] $90
    [*] $108 (900 × $0.12)
    [ ] $120
    [ ] $900
    Explanation: Multiplication.

  Q15. Percent change formula is:
    [ ] New − old
    [*] (New − Old) ÷ Old × 100%
    [ ] Old − new
    [ ] New ÷ old
    Explanation: Standard formula.

  Q16. If species count drops from 50 to 35 in a habitat, percent decline is:
    [ ] 15%
    [ ] 25%
    [*] 30% ((50−35) ÷ 50 × 100)
    [ ] 35%
    Explanation: Percent change.

  Q17. AP questions about LD50 (lethal dose 50%) test understanding of:
    [ ] Only chemistry
    [*] Toxicology: the dose at which 50% of test organisms die — key for evaluating chemical hazards
    [ ] Only ecology
    [ ] Only one concept
    Explanation: Dose-response.

  Q18. When time is limited on FRQs, prioritize:
    [ ] Writing introductions
    [*] Answering the easiest parts first to secure points; returning to harder parts with remaining time
    [ ] Perfect grammar
    [ ] Long conclusions
    Explanation: Strategic time use.

  Q19. Common AP mistakes include:
    [ ] Being too specific
    [*] Not answering all parts of the question, being too vague, and not connecting evidence to claims
    [ ] Only calculation errors
    [ ] Only vocabulary errors
    Explanation: Avoid vagueness.

  Q20. The best AP preparation strategy is:
    [ ] Memorize everything
    [*] Practice with past exam questions, understand concepts deeply, connect topics across units, and practice calculations
    [ ] Only read the textbook
    [ ] Only make flashcards
    Explanation: Active practice.

--- Lesson 10.3: Case Studies: AP APES (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies: AP APES
Case Studies for AP APES
The AP exam frequently uses case studies to test application of environmental concepts to real-world situations.
Classic APES Case Studies  • Chesapeake Bay: Nutrient pollution, dead zones, multi-state management.  • Aral Sea: Water diversion for irrigation → ecological disaster.  • Easter Island: Resource depletion and societal collapse.  • Three Gorges Dam: Hydropower benefits vs. displacement and ecological costs.  • Ogallala Aquifer: Groundwater depletion from agricultural irrigation.

--- Lesson 10.3: Case Studies: AP APES (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The Chesapeake Bay has dead zones caused by:
    [ ] Only industrial discharge
    [*] Agricultural runoff (nitrogen and phosphorus) from farms across 6 states + DC, causing algal blooms and oxygen depletion
    [ ] Only one source
    [ ] Only natural processes
    Explanation: Multi-source nutrient pollution.

  Q2. The Chesapeake Bay cleanup illustrates the difficulty of:
    [ ] Point-source pollution
    [*] Managing non-point source pollution across multiple jurisdictions (6 states, complex watershed)
    [ ] Single-source issues
    [ ] Only local problems
    Explanation: Governance challenge.

  Q3. The Aral Sea disaster was caused by:
    [ ] Climate change
    [*] Soviet-era diversion of rivers (Amu Darya and Syr Darya) for cotton irrigation in Central Asia
    [ ] Natural evaporation
    [ ] Tectonic activity
    Explanation: Human-caused disaster.

  Q4. The Aral Sea has shrunk by approximately:
    [ ] 10%
    [ ] 50%
    [*] ~90% of its original volume (one of the planet's worst environmental disasters)
    [ ] 25%
    Explanation: Catastrophic shrinkage.

  Q5. Consequences of the Aral Sea disaster include:
    [ ] Only less water
    [*] Fisheries collapse, dust storms from exposed toxic seabed, climate changes, health crises, and economic devastation
    [ ] Only one effect
    [ ] Nothing significant
    Explanation: Multi-dimensional impacts.

  Q6. Easter Island illustrates:
    [ ] Sustainable living
    [*] Resource depletion and societal collapse — deforestation led to soil erosion, food decline, and population crash
    [ ] Only isolation effects
    [ ] Only cultural change
    Explanation: Cautionary tale.

  Q7. Easter Island is often used as a metaphor for:
    [ ] Successful management
    [*] Earth as a whole — an isolated system with finite resources that can be depleted if mismanaged
    [ ] Only Polynesian culture
    [ ] Only archaeology
    Explanation: Earth as an island.

  Q8. The Three Gorges Dam produces _____ GW of hydroelectric capacity.
    [ ] 5 GW
    [ ] 10 GW
    [*] ~22.5 GW (world's largest hydroelectric facility)
    [ ] 50 GW
    Explanation: Massive energy output.

  Q9. Costs of the Three Gorges Dam include:
    [ ] Only financial costs
    [*] Displacement of 1.3 million people, submerged archaeological sites, increased earthquake risk, disrupted river ecology, and sediment trapping
    [ ] Only one cost
    [ ] No significant costs
    Explanation: High social and environmental costs.

  Q10. The Ogallala Aquifer is being depleted because:
    [ ] It recharges quickly
    [*] Agricultural irrigation withdraws water far faster than the ~2,500-year natural recharge rate
    [ ] It's not used much
    [ ] Only industry uses it
    Explanation: Unsustainable withdrawal.

  Q11. The Ogallala underlies _____ Great Plains states.
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 4
    [*] 8 (supporting ~30% of US irrigated agriculture)
    [ ] All 50
    Explanation: Regional importance.

  Q12. The Deepwater Horizon spill (2010) demonstrated:
    [ ] Perfect safety
    [*] Risks of deep-sea drilling: 4.9 million barrels of oil released, devastating Gulf ecosystems and fishing communities
    [ ] Only minor damage
    [ ] Only financial costs
    Explanation: Catastrophic spill.

  Q13. Bhopal (1984) is relevant to APES because it illustrates:
    [ ] Safe industry
    [*] The intersection of industrial pollution, environmental justice, and the disproportionate impact on vulnerable populations in developing countries
    [ ] Only one issue
    [ ] Only Indian problems
    Explanation: Industrial + equity.

  Q14. Chernobyl (1986) and Fukushima (2011) demonstrate:
    [ ] Nuclear is perfectly safe
    [*] Risks of nuclear energy: potential for catastrophic accidents with long-lasting environmental and health consequences
    [ ] Only one incident
    [ ] Only historical interest
    Explanation: Nuclear risk.

  Q15. The Dust Bowl (1930s) was caused by:
    [ ] Only drought
    [*] Over-plowing of Great Plains grasslands combined with severe drought → massive soil erosion and ecological disaster
    [ ] Only farming
    [ ] Only wind
    Explanation: Agricultural + climate.

  Q16. For AP case study questions, students should:
    [ ] Only describe what happened
    [*] Identify the environmental issue, explain causes, describe impacts on ecosystems AND humans, and evaluate solutions
    [ ] Only list facts
    [ ] Only give opinions
    Explanation: Full analysis required.

  Q17. Case studies test ability to:
    [ ] Memorize events
    [*] Apply environmental science concepts to real-world situations and analyze complex, multi-factor problems
    [ ] Only name locations
    [ ] Only cite dates
    Explanation: Applied knowledge.

  Q18. The common thread in environmental case studies is:
    [ ] Problems are simple
    [*] Environmental problems involve complex interactions between human activities and natural systems, often with unintended consequences
    [ ] All problems are the same
    [ ] Only technology fails
    Explanation: Complexity and interconnection.

  Q19. Successful environmental case studies (e.g., ozone recovery) show that:
    [ ] Nothing works
    [*] With scientific understanding, political will, and international cooperation, environmental problems CAN be solved
    [ ] Only technology works
    [ ] Only economics drives change
    Explanation: Hope for action.

  Q20. For APES, be prepared to discuss _____ case studies in detail.
    [ ] Only one
    [*] Multiple case studies across different topics (water, energy, pollution, biodiversity, climate, policy)
    [ ] Only US examples
    [ ] Only recent ones
    Explanation: Broad preparation.

--- Lesson 10.5: Capstone: Environmental Impact Study (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Capstone: Environmental Impact Study
Capstone: Environmental Impact Study
This lesson guides students through conducting their own environmental impact analysis, integrating concepts from all units.
Steps for an Environmental Impact Study  • Step 1: Define the project or action being assessed.  • Step 2: Identify affected environmental components (air, water, soil, biodiversity, communities).  • Step 3: Predict impacts (positive and negative, short-term and long-term).  • Step 4: Evaluate significance and propose mitigation measures.  • Step 5: Consider alternatives, including the 'no-action' alternative.  • Step 6: Communicate findings with data, evidence, and recommendations.

--- Lesson 10.5: Capstone: Environmental Impact Study (Quiz) ---

  Q1. An Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) is required for:
    [ ] All projects
    [*] Major federal actions significantly affecting the quality of the human environment (NEPA requirement)
    [ ] Only private projects
    [ ] Only state projects
    Explanation: Federal trigger.

  Q2. A baseline assessment establishes:
    [ ] Future conditions
    [*] Current environmental conditions before the project — the 'before' comparison for measuring impacts
    [ ] Only one variable
    [ ] Only human impacts
    Explanation: Starting point.

  Q3. Scoping in an EIS process determines:
    [ ] Only cost
    [*] Which environmental issues and alternatives should be studied in detail — narrowing focus to the most significant concerns
    [ ] Only the timeline
    [ ] Only public participation
    Explanation: Focus the analysis.

  Q4. Public participation in the EIS process is important because:
    [ ] It's not important
    [*] Affected communities have knowledge of local conditions and concerns that improve the analysis and legitimacy of decisions
    [ ] Only a legal requirement
    [ ] Only for activists
    Explanation: Community knowledge.

  Q5. The 'no-action alternative' is required because it:
    [ ] Wastes time
    [*] Provides a baseline comparison — what happens if we don't proceed? Sometimes the best option is no action
    [ ] Only for formality
    [ ] Is always the worst option
    Explanation: Comparison baseline.

  Q6. Mitigation measures in an EIS follow a hierarchy:
    [ ] Only compensation
    [*] Avoid → minimize → restore → compensate (avoid the impact first; compensation is the last resort)
    [ ] Only avoidance
    [ ] Only restoration
    Explanation: Mitigation hierarchy.

  Q7. Cumulative impact assessment considers:
    [ ] Only the project alone
    [*] The combined effects of the project PLUS other past, present, and foreseeable future actions in the area
    [ ] Only one project
    [ ] Only present conditions
    Explanation: Broader context.

  Q8. Secondary (indirect) impacts include:
    [ ] Only direct construction damage
    [*] Effects that are caused by the project but occur later or farther away (e.g., new road → development → habitat fragmentation)
    [ ] Only on-site effects
    [ ] Only immediate effects
    Explanation: Ripple effects.

  Q9. Air quality impacts might be assessed through:
    [ ] Only observation
    [*] Dispersion modeling, emission inventories, monitoring data, and comparison to NAAQS standards
    [ ] Only one method
    [ ] Only lab tests
    Explanation: Multi-method assessment.

  Q10. Water impact assessment includes:
    [ ] Only one factor
    [*] Quantity (flow, drainage), quality (pollutants, sediment, temperature), habitat (aquatic species, wetlands), and groundwater
    [ ] Only surface water
    [ ] Only drinking water
    Explanation: Comprehensive water analysis.

  Q11. Biodiversity impact assessment examines:
    [ ] Only one species
    [*] Habitat loss, fragmentation, species displacement, impacts on threatened/endangered species, and connectivity
    [ ] Only mammals
    [ ] Only rare species
    Explanation: Ecosystem-level review.

  Q12. Social impact assessment evaluates:
    [ ] Only jobs
    [*] Effects on communities: displacement, environmental justice, traffic, noise, visual impact, cultural resources, and economic changes
    [ ] Only one factor
    [ ] Only property values
    Explanation: Human community effects.

  Q13. When conducting a student environmental impact study, the project could be:
    [ ] Only a real construction project
    [*] Any proposed action: school renovation, local development proposal, park expansion, energy project, or even a hypothetical scenario
    [ ] Only federal projects
    [ ] Only large projects
    Explanation: Flexible scope.

  Q14. Data collection for an impact study might include:
    [ ] Only library research
    [*] Field observations, water/soil/air sampling, species surveys, community interviews, GIS analysis, and existing data review
    [ ] Only one source
    [ ] Only internet research
    Explanation: Multiple data sources.

--- Lesson 10.6: Comprehensive Review & AP Prep (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The most important strategy for the AP exam is:
    [ ] Memorizing everything
    [*] Understanding concepts AND being able to apply them to novel situations with specific evidence
    [ ] Only practicing FRQs
    [ ] Only making flashcards
    Explanation: Applied understanding.

  Q2. The Rule of 70 is used for:
    [ ] Only money
    [*] Estimating doubling time: 70 ÷ growth rate (%) = years to double (works for population, investments, etc.)
    [ ] Only population
    [ ] Only energy
    Explanation: Quick estimation.

  Q3. If world population is 8 billion and growing at 1%, doubling time is:
    [ ] 10 years
    [ ] 35 years
    [*] 70 years (70 ÷ 1)
    [ ] 700 years
    Explanation: Rule of 70.

  Q4. Per capita CO₂ calculations require:
    [ ] Only total emissions
    [*] Dividing total national CO₂ emissions by population (important for comparing countries of different sizes)
    [ ] Only population
    [ ] Only one country's data
    Explanation: Total ÷ population.

  Q5. Connecting agriculture to dead zones requires understanding:
    [ ] Only farming
    [*] Fertilizer application → nutrient runoff → eutrophication → algal blooms → oxygen depletion → dead zone (cause-effect chain)
    [ ] Only water pollution
    [ ] Only one link
    Explanation: Full cause-effect chain.

  Q6. For FRQs about proposing solutions, always include:
    [ ] Only one idea
    [*] The solution, how it works, evidence it's effective, potential trade-offs, and why it's the best option
    [ ] Only a description
    [ ] Only costs
    Explanation: Complete analysis.

  Q7. The difference between primary succession and secondary succession is:
    [ ] No difference
    [*] Primary: colonization of bare substrate (no prior soil); Secondary: recovery after disturbance where soil remains
    [ ] Only time
    [ ] Only location
    Explanation: Starting conditions differ.

  Q8. Competitive exclusion principle states:
    [ ] All species coexist
    [*] Two species competing for the same limited resource cannot coexist — one will outcompete the other
    [ ] Species never compete
    [ ] Only predators compete
    Explanation: Niche separation.

  Q9. The carbon cycle connects to climate change through:
    [ ] No connection
    [*] Fossil fuel burning releases stored carbon as CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming → climate impacts
    [ ] Only photosynthesis
    [ ] Only decomposition
    Explanation: Carbon → climate.

  Q10. Indicator species are useful because they:
    [ ] Are the most common
    [*] Signal environmental conditions — their presence, absence, or health reflects ecosystem quality (e.g., amphibians for water quality)
    [ ] Only indicate one thing
    [ ] Are the most visible
    Explanation: Environmental signals.

  Q11. Point-source pollution differs from non-point in that it:
    [ ] Is worse
    [*] Comes from a single identifiable source (pipe, smokestack) vs. diffuse sources (runoff from land, atmospheric deposition)
    [ ] Is always industrial
    [ ] Cannot be regulated
    Explanation: Identifiable vs. diffuse.

  Q12. For AP, always identify trade-offs when discussing solutions because:
    [ ] There are no trade-offs
    [*] Every environmental solution involves trade-offs (economic costs, other environmental impacts, social effects) — acknowledging them shows sophistication
    [ ] Only science matters
    [ ] Only one perspective matters
    Explanation: Balanced analysis.

  Q13. The relationship between biodiversity and ecosystem stability is:
    [ ] No relationship
    [*] Greater biodiversity generally increases ecosystem resilience (more species = more functional redundancy and adaptive capacity)
    [ ] Inverse
    [ ] Only for forests
    Explanation: Diversity promotes stability.

  Q14. Genetic diversity within species is important because:
    [ ] Only for breeding
    [*] It provides raw material for adaptation to changing conditions — low genetic diversity = vulnerability to disease, environmental change
    [ ] Only matters for rare species
    [ ] Only for agriculture
    Explanation: Adaptive capacity.

  Q15. When comparing developed and developing nations, remember:
    [ ] They're the same
    [*] Different per capita impacts, historical responsibility, vulnerability to climate change, and capacity to adapt — equity matters
    [ ] Only GDP differences
    [ ] Only population differences
    Explanation: Equity in analysis.

  Q16. Feedback loops in environmental systems:
    [ ] Don't exist
    [*] Can amplify change (positive feedback: ice melt → less reflection → more warming) or stabilize (negative feedback: predator-prey balance)
    [ ] Only amplify
    [ ] Only stabilize
    Explanation: System dynamics.

    [ ] Partial credit doesn't exist

    [ ] Only final answers matter

  Q18. The AP exam tests environmental science as:
    [ ] Isolated facts
    [*] An integrated, interdisciplinary science where concepts connect across topics — the exam rewards systems thinking
    [ ] Only biology
    [ ] Only chemistry
    Explanation: Integrated science.
