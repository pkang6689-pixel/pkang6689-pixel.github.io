--- Lesson 1.1: What is Environmental Science? (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: What is Environmental Science?
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an ecosystem?
An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (biotic factors) interacting with their physical environment (abiotic factors) as an integrated system through nutrient cycling and energy flow.
What is biodiversity?
Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth at three levels: genetic diversity within species, species diversity within communities, and ecosystem diversity across landscapes.
What is sustainability?
Sustainability, as defined by the Brundtland Commission in 1987, means meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
What is a natural resource?
Natural resources include any materials or energy sources found in nature that are useful to humans, such as water, minerals, forests, soil, fossil fuels, and solar energy.
What is a nonrenewable resource?
Nonrenewable resources, such as fossil fuels and minerals, form over millions of years through geological processes and exist in finite quantities that can be exhausted by human consumption.
What is pollution?
Pollution is the introduction of contaminants or harmful levels of energy (noise, heat, light) into the environment, causing adverse effects on ecosystems and human health.
What is the ecological footprint?
The ecological footprint measures the total amount of biologically productive land and water required to produce the resources consumed and absorb the waste generated by a person or population.
What is an environmental indicator?
Environmental indicators are species, substances, or measurements that scientists use to gauge the condition and health of ecosystems, such as amphibian populations or water quality parameters.

--- Lesson 1.2: Interdisciplinary Nature of the Field (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interdisciplinary Nature of the Field
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is ecology?
Ecology is the scientific study of the interactions between organisms and their environment, including relationships between species and the flow of energy and nutrients through ecosystems.
What is environmental ethics?
Environmental ethics is a branch of philosophy that considers the moral obligations humans have toward nature, including questions about the intrinsic value of species and ecosystems.
What is toxicology?
Toxicology is the scientific study of the harmful effects of chemical, physical, or biological agents on living organisms, which is critical for understanding pollution and setting safety standards.
What is environmental policy?
Environmental policy encompasses the laws, regulations, and guidelines created by governments and organizations to address environmental issues, protect ecosystems, and manage natural resource use.
What is epidemiology in the context of environmental science?
Environmental epidemiology studies how environmental exposures such as air pollution, contaminated water, or chemical spills affect patterns of disease and health outcomes in human populations.
What is a cost-benefit analysis in environmental decision-making?
Cost-benefit analysis is a systematic approach to evaluating the pros and cons of a decision by comparing all expected costs against all expected benefits, including environmental and social factors.
What is the anthropocene?
The Anthropocene is a proposed geological epoch beginning when human activities started having a significant global impact on Earth's geology and ecosystems, such as through climate change and mass extinction.
What is environmental modeling?
Environmental modeling uses mathematical equations and computer simulations to represent complex environmental systems, allowing scientists to test hypotheses and predict future changes like climate scenarios.
Real-World Applications
Example: A geologist studying groundwater contamination near an old landfill works with a toxicologist to determine health risks and an engineer to design a remediation system. This real-world example shows that:
Groundwater remediation requires geological knowledge of water flow, toxicological assessment of health risks, and engineering expertise to design cleanup systems, illustrating interdisciplinary problem-solving.
Example: A research team uses satellite imagery (physics), water sampling (chemistry), fish surveys (biology), and community interviews (sociology) to assess the health of a major lake system. This comprehensive approach reflects:
Assessing lake health requires physical, chemical, biological, and social data because each discipline reveals different aspects of environmental condition that together provide a complete assessment.

--- Lesson 1.3: Ecosystem Basics (Producers, Consumers, Decomposers) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ecosystem Basics (Producers, Consumers, Decomposers)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is photosynthesis?
Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria use sunlight energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose (food energy) and oxygen as a byproduct.
What is a trophic level?
A trophic level represents each step in a food chain where energy is transferred, from producers (level 1) through primary consumers (level 2), secondary consumers (level 3), and so on.
What is cellular respiration?
Cellular respiration is the metabolic process in which cells break down glucose using oxygen to produce ATP (usable energy), carbon dioxide, and water, occurring in all living organisms.
What is a detritivore?
Detritivores are organisms that consume dead organic matter (detritus), physically breaking it down into smaller pieces. Examples include earthworms, millipedes, and dung beetles.
What is chemosynthesis?
Chemosynthesis is an alternative to photosynthesis used by certain bacteria to produce food energy from inorganic chemicals like hydrogen sulfide, supporting ecosystems in deep-sea hydrothermal vents.
What is a herbivore?
A herbivore is an animal that feeds exclusively on plants or other producers, serving as a primary consumer and forming a critical link between producers and higher-level consumers in food webs.
What is a heterotroph?
Heterotrophs are organisms that cannot produce their own food and must consume other organisms for energy. All consumers and decomposers are heterotrophs.
What is a scavenger?
Scavengers are animals that consume dead organisms they did not kill, such as vultures and hyenas. They play an important role in ecosystems by helping recycle nutrients from dead animals.
What is an apex predator?
An apex predator sits at the top of a food chain with no natural predators, such as wolves, sharks, or eagles. They help regulate populations of prey species and maintain ecosystem balance.
What is biomass?
Biomass is the total mass of living organic material at a given trophic level or in an ecosystem, typically measured in grams per square meter. It decreases at each higher trophic level.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a producer?
Green plants are producers because they use photosynthesis to convert sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water into glucose and oxygen, creating the food and energy base for ecosystems.

--- Lesson 1.4: Biogeochemical Cycles (Carbon, Nitrogen, Water) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Biogeochemical Cycles (Carbon, Nitrogen, Water)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a biogeochemical cycle?
A biogeochemical cycle is the pathway by which a chemical element or compound moves through the biosphere (living systems), lithosphere (rocks), hydrosphere (water), and atmosphere.
What is a carbon sink?
A carbon sink is any system that absorbs more carbon than it releases, such as forests, oceans, and soil. Carbon sinks help regulate atmospheric CO2 and mitigate climate change.
What is nitrification?
Nitrification is a two-step process carried out by nitrifying bacteria that convert ammonia first to nitrite (NO2-) and then to nitrate (NO3-), which is the form of nitrogen most easily absorbed by plants.
What is the water table?
The water table is the boundary below which all spaces in soil and rock are completely saturated with groundwater. Its depth varies with precipitation, geology, and water usage.
What is ammonification?
Ammonification is the process by which bacteria and fungi decompose organic nitrogen from dead organisms and waste products into ammonia (NH3) or ammonium (NH4+), returning nitrogen to the soil.
What is runoff in the water cycle?
Runoff is the flow of water over land that occurs when the soil is saturated or the surface is impervious. It carries sediment, nutrients, and pollutants into waterways and is a major source of water pollution.
What is condensation?
Condensation occurs when water vapor in the atmosphere cools and changes from a gaseous state to liquid droplets, forming clouds and fog. It is the reverse of evaporation and leads to precipitation.
What is nitrogen assimilation?
Nitrogen assimilation is the process by which plants absorb ammonium (NH4+) or nitrate (NO3-) from soil through their roots and use it to build proteins, nucleic acids, and other organic molecules.
What is infiltration in the water cycle?
Infiltration is the process by which precipitation or surface water seeps into the soil and percolates downward to recharge groundwater aquifers. It is reduced by impervious surfaces like pavement.
Real-World Applications
Example: Nitrogen gas (N2) makes up approximately what percentage of Earth's atmosphere?
Nitrogen gas (N2) makes up approximately 78% of Earth's atmosphere, but most organisms cannot use it in this gaseous form. It must first be converted (fixed) into usable forms like ammonia or nitrate.

--- Lesson 1.5: Sustainability Concepts (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sustainability Concepts
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the ecological footprint?
The ecological footprint measures how much biologically productive land and water is needed to produce the resources a person or population consumes and to absorb the waste they generate.
What is the tragedy of the commons?
The tragedy of the commons is an economic concept describing how shared resources are overexploited when each user acts in self-interest, ultimately degrading the resource for everyone.
What is an ecosystem service?
Ecosystem services are the benefits humans derive from natural ecosystems, including provisioning (food, water), regulating (climate, flood control), supporting (nutrient cycling), and cultural (recreation) services.
What is sustainable development?
Sustainable development integrates economic growth, environmental protection, and social equity so that present needs are met while preserving resources and opportunities for future generations.
What is a carbon footprint?
A carbon footprint quantifies the total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by an individual, organization, event, or product, usually expressed as tonnes of CO2 equivalent.
What is a circular economy?
A circular economy is an alternative to the linear take-make-dispose model, designed so that products, materials, and resources are maintained at their highest value for as long as possible.
What is greenwashing?
Greenwashing is a deceptive marketing practice where companies make unsubstantiated or misleading claims about their environmental practices to appeal to eco-conscious consumers without making real changes.
What is the precautionary principle?
The precautionary principle states that when an activity raises threats to the environment or human health, preventive action should be taken even if some cause-and-effect relationships are not fully established.
What is biocapacity?
Biocapacity measures the ability of ecosystems to produce useful biological materials and absorb waste, expressed in global hectares. When ecological footprint exceeds biocapacity, resources are being depleted.

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Environmental Issues
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is bioaccumulation?
Bioaccumulation occurs when a substance (like mercury or DDT) is absorbed by an organism faster than it can be metabolized or excreted, causing concentrations to build up in tissues over time.
What is biomagnification?
Biomagnification is the process by which the concentration of a persistent toxin increases at each trophic level of a food chain, meaning top predators accumulate the highest concentrations.
What is a Superfund site?
Superfund sites are contaminated locations designated by the EPA for long-term cleanup under CERCLA, enacted in 1980 in response to Love Canal and similar toxic waste disasters.
What is an environmental impact assessment (EIA)?
An EIA is a systematic process required by law (under NEPA in the US) that evaluates the likely environmental consequences of a proposed project and explores alternatives and mitigation measures.
What is the ozone layer?
The ozone layer is a region in the stratosphere (15-35 km altitude) containing relatively high concentrations of ozone (O3) that absorbs 97-99% of the sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation.
What is a point source of pollution?
Point source pollution originates from a single, identifiable location such as a discharge pipe, smokestack, or oil well. It is generally easier to monitor and regulate than nonpoint source pollution.
What is a nonpoint source of pollution?
Nonpoint source pollution comes from many scattered or diffuse sources, such as fertilizer runoff from farms, urban stormwater, and atmospheric deposition. It is harder to control than point source pollution.
What is remediation in environmental science?
Environmental remediation is the process of removing pollutants or contaminants from soil, groundwater, or surface water to protect human health and restore ecosystem function at contaminated sites.
What is an indicator species?
Indicator species are organisms sensitive to environmental changes whose population status reflects ecosystem health. For example, amphibians indicate water quality because their permeable skin absorbs pollutants.
What is the Endangered Species Act (ESA)?
The Endangered Species Act of 1973 is one of the most powerful US conservation laws, providing legal protection for species at risk of extinction and the habitats they need to survive.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Systems Thinking (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Systems Thinking
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a feedback loop?
A feedback loop is a mechanism where the output of a process cycles back to affect the input, creating either a self-reinforcing (positive) or self-correcting (negative) cycle within the system.
What is homeostasis?
Homeostasis is the ability of a system to maintain internal stability through negative feedback mechanisms that counteract disturbances, keeping conditions within a viable range for the system to function.
What is a tipping point in environmental science?
An environmental tipping point is a critical threshold where a relatively small change causes a disproportionately large and often irreversible shift in system behavior, like the collapse of a fishery.
What is resilience in the context of environmental systems?
Resilience is the capacity of an ecosystem or environmental system to withstand disturbances (like fires, floods, or pollution) and return to its previous functional state rather than shifting to a new one.
What is albedo?
Albedo measures the reflectivity of a surface, expressed as a fraction from 0 (absorbs all light) to 1 (reflects all light). Snow and ice have high albedo, while dark surfaces like asphalt have low albedo.
What is an emergent property?
Emergent properties arise from interactions between system components and cannot be predicted by studying parts in isolation. For example, the ability of a forest to regulate climate emerges from the collective action of trees, soil, and water.
What is a system in environmental science?
In environmental science, a system is a set of interconnected components that work together as a unified whole, with defined boundaries, inputs (energy, matter), internal processes, and outputs.
What is a steady state?
A steady state occurs when system inputs balance outputs, maintaining overall stability even though processes continue within the system. It does not mean the system is static or unchanging internally.
What is systems thinking?
Systems thinking is an analytical approach that considers how individual components interact within a larger system, recognizing that the behavior of the whole emerges from these interconnections.

  Q25. Scientists studying climate change note that increased CO2 may initially boost plant growth (which absorbs CO2, a negative feedback), but extreme warming can cause droughts and wildfires that kill forests (releasing CO2, a positive feedback). This shows that:
    [ ] Feedback loops never interact with each other
    [ ] Only one type of feedback exists in climate systems
    [*] Complex environmental systems contain both positive and negative feedback loops that may interact and shift in dominance
    [ ] Climate change will automatically be corrected by plants
    Explanation: Real environmental systems contain multiple interacting feedback loops. Whether a system stabilizes or destabilizes depends on the relative strength of competing positive and negative feedbacks.

  Q26. A coral reef has survived minor temperature increases for decades through its resilience. However, a prolonged heat wave bleaches 80% of the coral, and the reef shifts to an algae-dominated ecosystem that does not support the same biodiversity. An AP exam question might ask you to identify this as:
    [ ] A negative feedback restoring the reef
    [*] A system crossing a tipping point and shifting to a new, lower-functioning regime
    [ ] Normal coral behavior
    [ ] A steady state being maintained
    Explanation: This scenario describes a regime shift where the coral reef ecosystem crosses a tipping point and transitions to a fundamentally different state (algae-dominated) that is resistant to reversal.

  Q27. In an AP free-response question, you are asked to explain how the water cycle is an example of a system with inputs, outputs, and feedback. A strong answer would include:
    [ ] Drawing a simple picture of rain and clouds without providing any written explanation or analysis
    [ ] Only naming the three physical phases of water: solid ice, liquid water, and gaseous water vapor
    [*] Identifying inputs (solar energy, precipitation), outputs (evaporation, runoff), and feedback mechanisms (e.g., more evaporation leads to more clouds, which can increase precipitation or reflect sunlight)
    [ ] Only providing a textbook definition of the water cycle without discussing specific system components
    Explanation: AP free-response questions require identifying system components (inputs, outputs, processes) and explaining feedback mechanisms. The water cycle contains both positive and negative feedbacks involving clouds and temperature.

  Q28. A city builds more roads to reduce traffic congestion. However, the new roads attract more drivers (induced demand), creating even worse congestion, leading to demands for yet more roads. This urban planning problem is an example of:
    [ ] A system at perfect equilibrium
    [ ] A negative feedback loop solving the problem
    [*] A positive feedback loop where the attempted solution amplifies the original problem
    [ ] A closed system with no inputs
    Explanation: Induced demand is a positive feedback loop in transportation systems: building more roads encourages more driving, which increases congestion, prompting more road-building. Systems thinking reveals why this approach fails.

  Q29. An island ecosystem has maintained stable populations of plants, herbivores, and predators for centuries. When humans introduce a non-native predator, the native herbivores are quickly eliminated because they have no defenses. Plants overgrow without herbivores, and the native predator starves. This demonstrates:
    [ ] That ecosystems are immune to disruption
    [ ] That island ecosystems have no food webs
    [ ] That introduced species always improve ecosystems
    [*] How disrupting one component of a system can cascade through interconnected feedback loops, destabilizing the entire system
    Explanation: This demonstrates how interconnected system components depend on feedback loops for stability. Disrupting one link (introducing a new predator) cascades through the system, illustrating why systems thinking is essential.

  Q30. For the AP Environmental Science exam, a question presents a diagram showing rising global temperatures leading to increased water vapor in the atmosphere (a greenhouse gas), which causes further warming. You are asked to classify and explain this. The correct response identifies this as:
    [*] A positive feedback loop because warming increases water vapor, which is a greenhouse gas that amplifies the original warming
    [ ] A tipping point that has already been crossed
    [ ] A negative feedback loop because water vapor cools the atmosphere
    [ ] An isolated system with no feedback
    Explanation: The water vapor feedback is a positive feedback loop: warming increases evaporation, adding more water vapor (a greenhouse gas) to the atmosphere, which traps more heat and amplifies the original warming.
