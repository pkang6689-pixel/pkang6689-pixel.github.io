--- Lesson 1.1: What is Environmental Science? (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: What is Environmental Science?
What is Environmental Science?
Environmental science is the interdisciplinary study of how humans interact with the environment. It integrates biology, chemistry, physics, geology, economics, and social sciences to understand and solve environmental problems.
Key Themes  • Science and the environment: Using the scientific method to study natural systems.  • Human impact: Agriculture, industry, urbanization, and resource consumption alter ecosystems.  • Sustainability: Meeting present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs.

--- Lesson 1.2: Interdisciplinary Nature of the Field (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interdisciplinary Nature of the Field
Interdisciplinary Nature of the Field
Environmental science draws on multiple disciplines:
  • Biology/Ecology: Species interactions, ecosystems, biodiversity.  • Chemistry: Pollutant behavior, biogeochemical cycles, water chemistry.  • Physics: Energy flow, thermodynamics, climate dynamics.  • Geology: Soil science, mineral resources, natural hazards.  • Social Sciences: Economics (cost-benefit), policy, ethics, demographics.  • Engineering: Pollution control, renewable energy technology, sustainable design.

--- Lesson 1.3: Ecosystem Basics (Producers, Consumers, Decomposers) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ecosystem Basics (Producers, Consumers, Decomposers)
Ecosystem BasicsTrophic Levels  • Producers (autotrophs): Photosynthesizers (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) and chemosynthesizers.  • Primary consumers (herbivores): Eat producers (deer, grasshoppers, zooplankton).  • Secondary consumers: Eat primary consumers (frogs, small fish).  • Tertiary consumers: Top predators (hawks, sharks, lions).  • Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi break down dead matter, recycling nutrients.
10% Rule: Approximately 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels; ~90% lost as heat.

--- Lesson 1.4: Biogeochemical Cycles (Carbon, Nitrogen, Water) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Biogeochemical Cycles (Carbon, Nitrogen, Water)
Biogeochemical CyclesCarbon Cycle
Photosynthesis (removes CO₂), respiration/decomposition (releases CO₂), fossil fuel combustion, ocean absorption. Human burning of fossil fuels accelerates atmospheric CO₂ (pre-industrial ~280 ppm → 2024 ~425 ppm).
Nitrogen Cycle
N₂ (78% of atmosphere) → fixed by bacteria/lightning → NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (usable by plants) → consumed by animals → decomposition/denitrification returns N₂. Haber-Bosch process produces synthetic fertilizer.
Water Cycle
Evaporation → condensation → precipitation → runoff/infiltration → groundwater → transpiration. Human impacts: damming, irrigation, groundwater depletion, pollution.

--- Lesson 1.5: Sustainability Concepts (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sustainability Concepts
Sustainability ConceptsThree Pillars  • Environmental: Maintaining healthy ecosystems, biodiversity, and natural resources.  • Economic: Long-term economic viability without depleting natural capital.  • Social: Equity, justice, health, education, and community well-being.Key Metrics  • Ecological footprint, carbon footprint, IPAT equation (Impact = Population × Affluence × Technology).  • Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): 17 UN goals for 2030.

--- Lesson 1.6: Case Studies in Environmental Issues (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Environmental Issues
Case Studies in Environmental IssuesDust Bowl (1930s)
Overplowing Great Plains → topsoil loss → massive dust storms. Led to soil conservation practices and the Soil Conservation Service.
Aral Sea Shrinkage
Soviet-era irrigation diverted rivers → Aral Sea shrank ~90%. Fisheries collapsed, salt/dust storms, health crises.
Ozone Hole
CFCs destroyed stratospheric ozone → UV radiation risk. Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs; ozone recovering.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Systems Thinking (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Systems Thinking
AP Prep: Systems ThinkingKey Concepts  • Open vs. closed systems: Open exchange matter and energy (most ecosystems); closed exchange only energy (Earth for matter, approximately).  • Feedback loops: Positive (amplifying: ice melt → less reflection → more warming) and negative (stabilizing: predator-prey cycles).  • Inputs, outputs, throughputs: Energy and matter flow through systems.  • Emergent properties: Properties arising from system interactions that individual components don't possess.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Systems Thinking (Quiz) ---

  Q1. An open system exchanges:
    [ ] Only energy
    [ ] Only matter
    [*] Both matter and energy with its surroundings
    [ ] Neither
    Explanation: Most ecosystems are open.

  Q2. Earth is approximately a closed system for:
    [ ] Energy
    [*] Matter (very little matter enters or leaves; energy from Sun enters, heat radiates out)
    [ ] Both
    [ ] Neither
    Explanation: Sunlight in, heat out; matter stays.

  Q3. A positive feedback loop:
    [ ] Stabilizes a system
    [*] Amplifies a change — the output reinforces the initial change (e.g., more warming → more ice melt → more warming)
    [ ] Has no effect
    [ ] Is always beneficial
    Explanation: Runaway effect.

  Q4. A negative feedback loop:
    [ ] Amplifies change
    [*] Counteracts a change — the output opposes the initial change, stabilizing the system
    [ ] Destroys the system
    [ ] Is always harmful
    Explanation: Self-regulating.

  Q5. The ice-albedo feedback is a _____ feedback loop.
    [ ] Negative
    [*] Positive (ice melts → darker surface → absorbs more heat → more ice melts)
    [ ] Neutral
    [ ] Unrelated
    Explanation: Amplifying effect.

  Q6. Predator-prey dynamics are an example of _____ feedback.
    [ ] Positive
    [*] Negative (more prey → more predators → fewer prey → fewer predators → more prey)
    [ ] No feedback
    [ ] Random
    Explanation: Stabilizing oscillation.

  Q7. If CO₂ increases temperature, which causes more CO₂ release from oceans, this is:
    [ ] Negative feedback
    [*] Positive feedback (warming → more CO₂ → more warming)
    [ ] No feedback
    [ ] A closed system
    Explanation: Amplifying.

  Q8. Thermoregulation in the human body (sweating when hot) is:
    [ ] Positive feedback
    [*] Negative feedback (body temperature rises → sweating cools body → temperature decreases)
    [ ] No regulation
    [ ] Random
    Explanation: Homeostasis.

  Q9. Emergent properties means:
    [ ] Parts have all properties
    [*] The whole system has properties that individual components lack (e.g., a forest is more than individual trees)
    [ ] Properties disappear
    [ ] Nothing special
    Explanation: Systems-level characteristics.

  Q10. In the AP exam, systems thinking questions may involve:
    [ ] Only memorization
    [*] Identifying feedback loops, predicting system behavior, and analyzing inputs/outputs
    [ ] Only calculations
    [ ] Only vocabulary
    Explanation: Applied reasoning.

  Q11. A tipping point in an environmental system is:
    [ ] Gradual change
    [*] A threshold beyond which a system shifts to a new state and may not easily return
    [ ] Normal variation
    [ ] Predictable
    Explanation: Irreversible change.

  Q12. The Earth system includes interconnected _____ (spheres).
    [ ] 2
    [*] 4 main spheres: atmosphere, hydrosphere, geosphere, and biosphere
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 10
    Explanation: Interacting subsystems.

  Q13. Steady state in a system means:
    [ ] No energy flows
    [*] Inputs balance outputs — the system appears stable even though matter/energy flow continuously
    [ ] Nothing moves
    [ ] It's dead
    Explanation: Dynamic equilibrium.

  Q14. Lag time in environmental systems means:
    [ ] Immediate response
    [*] There is a delay between a cause and its full effect (e.g., CO₂ emissions today → warming for decades)
    [ ] No delay
    [ ] Only in seconds
    Explanation: Delayed consequences.

  Q15. Resilience is a system's ability to:
    [ ] Never change
    [*] Absorb disturbance and return to its original state
    [ ] Always collapse
    [ ] Resist all change
    Explanation: Bounce back.

  Q16. Resistance is a system's ability to:
    [ ] Change easily
    [*] Withstand disturbance without changing
    [ ] Never recover
    [ ] Always change
    Explanation: Resist change.

  Q17. A systems diagram typically shows:
    [ ] Only text
    [*] Boxes (components), arrows (flows), and feedback loops connecting parts of the system
    [ ] Only numbers
    [ ] Only photographs
    Explanation: Visual representation.

  Q18. For AP quantitative problems, students may need to:
    [ ] Only describe
    [*] Calculate changes in inputs/outputs, trace matter/energy through systems, and predict feedback effects
    [ ] Only memorize
    [ ] Only observe
    Explanation: Quantitative reasoning.

  Q19. The concept of 'thresholds' is important because:
    [ ] Changes are always gradual
    [*] Small changes can trigger abrupt, often irreversible shifts when a threshold is crossed
    [ ] Thresholds don't exist
    [ ] Only theory
    Explanation: Non-linear dynamics.

  Q20. Systems thinking is the foundation of APES because:
    [ ] It's simple
    [*] Every environmental topic involves interconnected systems, feedback loops, and complex cause-effect relationships
    [ ] Only one topic uses it
    [ ] It's optional
    Explanation: Unifying framework.
