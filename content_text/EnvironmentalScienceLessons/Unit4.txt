--- Lesson 4.1: Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources
Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources
Renewable: Replenished on a human timescale — solar, wind, water, biomass, geothermal. Sustainable if used at or below replacement rate.

Nonrenewable: Formed over millions of years — fossil fuels, minerals, metals. Finite; extraction degrades the environment.
Key Distinction
Even renewables can become depleted if overexploited (e.g., overfishing, deforestation faster than regrowth).

--- Lesson 4.2: Water Resources & Management (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Water Resources & Management
Water Resources & Management
Only ~2.5% of Earth's water is fresh; ~69% of that is in glaciers/ice caps. Available fresh water (Key Issues  • Aquifer depletion: Ogallala Aquifer depleting faster than recharge (irrigation in Great Plains).  • Water pollution: Point source (factory pipe) vs. Non-point source (agricultural runoff).  • Water stress: ~4 billion people face severe water scarcity at least one month per year.

--- Lesson 4.3: Soil & Agriculture (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Soil & Agriculture
Soil & Agriculture
Soil forms from weathered rock + organic matter over centuries. Soil horizons: O (organic litter), A (topsoil), B (subsoil), C (weathered parent material), R (bedrock).
Agricultural Impacts  • Erosion: Wind and water remove topsoil; ~24 billion tons lost annually.  • Salinization: Salt buildup from irrigation in arid regions.  • Green Revolution: High-yield crops + fertilizers + irrigation; fed billions but increased environmental costs.

--- Lesson 4.4: Forests & Deforestation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Forests & Deforestation
Forests & Deforestation
Forests cover ~31% of Earth's land. They provide ecosystem services: carbon storage, biodiversity habitat, water cycling, soil protection, timber, and recreation.
Deforestation
~10 million hectares/year lost (2015–2020). Main drivers: cattle ranching, soy/palm oil farming, logging, and urbanization. Tropical forests are most threatened.

--- Lesson 4.5: Fisheries & Ocean Resources (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fisheries & Ocean Resources
Fisheries & Ocean Resources
Fish provide ~17% of animal protein consumed globally. ~34% of fish stocks are overfished; ~60% are fished at maximum sustained yield.
Key Concepts  • Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY): Largest catch that can be taken indefinitely.  • Bycatch: Unintended species caught and often discarded (dolphins, turtles, sharks).  • Aquaculture: Farmed fish; now supplies >50% of fish consumed but has environmental impacts.

Case Studies: Resource UseAral Sea Disaster

--- Lesson 4.7: AP Prep: Resource Management Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Resource Management Models
AP Prep: Resource Management ModelsKey Models & Concepts  • Tragedy of the commons: Shared resources depleted by self-interest (Hardin, 1968). Solutions: regulation, privatization, community management.  • Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY): Largest harvest sustainable indefinitely. Applied to fisheries, forestry.  • Cost-benefit analysis: Weighing economic costs vs. benefits of a resource decision; externalities often excluded.  • Precautionary principle: When uncertain, err on the side of protection.

--- Lesson 4.7: AP Prep: Resource Management Models (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The tragedy of the commons describes:
    [ ] Private property problems
    [*] Overuse of shared resources when individuals maximize personal gain at collective expense
    [ ] Government failure only
    [ ] Only historical events
    Explanation: Shared resource dilemma.

  Q2. Garrett Hardin proposed solutions including:
    [ ] More exploitation
    [*] Privatization (assigning ownership) or regulation (government-enforced limits)
    [ ] Ignoring the problem
    [ ] Only education
    Explanation: Institutional solutions.

  Q3. Elinor Ostrom's research showed that:
    [ ] Only government can manage commons
    [*] Communities can successfully self-manage common resources through local rules and cooperation (Nobel Prize 2009)
    [ ] Commons always fail
    [ ] Privatization always works
    Explanation: Community governance.

  Q4. MSY assumes population growth follows:
    [ ] Exponential growth only
    [*] Logistic growth — maximum reproduction occurs at N = K/2
    [ ] No growth
    [ ] Linear growth
    Explanation: Population ecology basis.

  Q5. Cost-benefit analysis often underestimates environmental damage because:
    [ ] It's perfect
    [*] Externalities (pollution costs, ecosystem service losses) are difficult to monetize and often excluded
    [ ] Everything is counted
    [ ] Damage is minor
    Explanation: Incomplete accounting.

  Q6. An externality is:
    [ ] An internal cost
    [*] A cost or benefit not reflected in market prices (e.g., factory pollution harming neighbors)
    [ ] A profit
    [ ] A subsidy
    Explanation: Uncaptured impact.

  Q7. The precautionary principle states:
    [ ] Wait for certainty
    [*] Take protective action when there's potential for serious harm, even without full scientific certainty
    [ ] Ignore risk
    [ ] Only act after damage
    Explanation: Better safe than sorry.

  Q8. Adaptive management involves:
    [ ] Setting fixed rules forever
    [*] Monitoring outcomes and adjusting management strategies based on new data and results
    [ ] Never changing plans
    [ ] Only one approach
    Explanation: Learn and adjust.

  Q9. A discount rate in resource economics:
    [ ] Increases future value
    [*] Reduces the present value of future costs and benefits — high discount rates favor short-term exploitation
    [ ] Has no effect
    [ ] Only applies to money

  Q10. High discount rates lead to:
    [ ] More conservation
    [*] More resource exploitation now (future resources are valued less than current ones)
    [ ] Balanced management
    [ ] No economic change
    Explanation: Short-term bias.

  Q11. Natural capital refers to:
    [ ] Only money
    [*] The stock of natural resources and ecosystem services that provide value to humans
    [ ] Man-made capital
    [ ] Only living things
    Explanation: Nature as asset.

  Q12. Ecosystem services include:
    [ ] Only timber

    [ ] Nothing measurable
    Explanation: Four categories (MEA).

  Q13. Payments for Ecosystem Services (PES) programs:
    [ ] Charge nature
    [*] Compensate landowners for maintaining ecosystem services (e.g., paying farmers to protect watersheds)
    [ ] Only tax polluters

  Q16. The safe minimum standard approach says we should:

    [ ] Only profit
