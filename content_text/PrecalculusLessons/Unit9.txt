--- Lesson 9.1: Concept of Limits (graphical, numerical approaches) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Concept of Limits (graphical, numerical approaches)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If f(x) = 5 for all x, what is lim(x→a) f(x)?
The limit of a constant function is the constant itself: lim(x→a) 5 = 5.
What is lim(x→0) (x + 3)?
For a polynomial, simply substitute: lim(x→0)(x + 3) = 0 + 3 = 3.
From a graph, if f(x) approaches 2 from both sides as x → 1, but f(1) = 5, what is lim(x→1) f(x)?
The limit depends on what f(x) approaches, not the actual value f(1). Since f(x) → 2 from both sides, the limit is 2.
What is lim(x→4) √x?
lim(x→4) √x = √4 = 2 by direct substitution.
What is lim(x→∞) 1/x?
As x grows without bound, 1/x approaches 0.
What is lim(x→0) |x|/x?
From the right, |x|/x = 1; from the left, |x|/x = −1. Since the one-sided limits differ, the limit does not exist.
What is a 'limit' in mathematics?
A limit is the value that f(x) approaches as x gets closer and closer to a specific point, regardless of whether f(x) actually equals that value at the point.
What is 'direct substitution' in limit evaluation?
Direct substitution means evaluating f(a) directly. If the result is a real number (no 0/0), the limit equals f(a).
What is a 'graphical approach' to finding limits?
The graphical approach examines the graph to see what y-value the curve approaches from both sides as x approaches the target value.
What is a 'numerical approach' to finding limits?
The numerical approach creates a table of values with x getting closer to the target (from both sides) and observes what f(x) is approaching.
A biologist models a population as P(t) = 1000t/(t + 5). What is lim(t→∞) P(t), the carrying capacity?
Divide by t: P(t) = 1000/(1 + 5/t). As t → ∞, 5/t → 0, so P → 1000.
A falling object has velocity v(t) = (4.9t² − 4.9)/(t − 1) m/s near t = 1. Using a table approach, what is lim(t→1) v(t)?
4.9(t² − 1)/(t − 1) = 4.9(t + 1). As t → 1: 4.9(2) = 9.8 m/s.
An economics model gives revenue R(x) = (3x² − 12)/(x − 2) thousand dollars for x units (in thousands). What is lim(x→2) R(x)?
Factor: 3(x² − 4)/(x − 2) = 3(x+2). As x → 2: 3(4) = 12 thousand dollars.
A traffic flow model gives vehicles per minute as f(t) = (t² − 9)/(t − 3) near rush hour at t = 3. What is lim(t→3) f(t)?
(t² − 9)/(t − 3) = (t+3) for t ≠ 3. As t → 3: t + 3 = 6.
An engineer studies the stress σ(x) = (x³ − 8)/(x − 2) kPa at critical point x = 2. What is lim(x→2) σ(x)?
(x³ − 8)/(x − 2) = x² + 2x + 4 by factoring the difference of cubes. At x = 2: 4 + 4 + 4 = 12 kPa.

--- Lesson 9.2: Evaluating Limits Algebraically (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Evaluating Limits Algebraically
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'conjugate method' for evaluating limits?
Rationalizing by multiplying by the conjugate (e.g., √a + √b) eliminates radicals and resolves 0/0 forms involving square roots.
What is 'algebraic simplification' in the context of limits?
Algebraic simplification in limits refers to techniques like factoring, expanding, or rationalizing to rewrite 0/0 forms into expressions that can be evaluated.
What is a 'common factor' in the context of limit evaluation?
The common factor (e.g., x − a) in both numerator and denominator causes both to be zero at x = a, creating the 0/0 form.
What is the 'special trigonometric limit' lim(x→0) sin x / x?
lim(x→0) sin x / x = 1 is a fundamental trigonometric limit, proven using the Squeeze Theorem.
An engineer computes lim(t→3) (t² − 6t + 9)/(t² − 9) for a stress analysis. What is the result?
(t−3)²/[(t−3)(t+3)] = (t−3)/(t+3). At t = 3: 0/6 = 0.
A financial analyst evaluates lim(n→∞) (1 + 1/n)ⁿ to determine continuously compounded growth. What is this famous limit?
lim(n→∞)(1 + 1/n)ⁿ = e ≈ 2.71828..., the base of natural logarithms and foundation of continuous compounding.
A biologist models enzyme reaction rate as R(x) = (5x − 5√x)/(x − 1) near x = 1. What is lim(x→1) R(x)?
Factor: 5(x − √x)/(x − 1) = 5√x(√x − 1)/[(√x−1)(√x+1)] = 5√x/(√x+1). At x=1: 5(1)/(1+1) = 5/2.
A student encounters lim(x→0) (1 − cos x)/x² on a practice exam. What is the answer?
Multiply by (1+cos x)/(1+cos x): sin²x/[x²(1+cos x)]. As x→0: [sin x/x]² · 1/(1+cos x) = 1 · 1/2 = 1/2.
A civil engineer needs lim(h→0) [√(16 + h) − 4]/h to find the rate of change of a bridge cable's length. What is this limit?
Rationalize: h/[h(√(16+h)+4)] = 1/(√(16+h)+4). At h=0: 1/(4+4) = 1/8.
A data scientist evaluates lim(x→∞) (5x³ − 2x)/(x³ + 100) to determine the asymptotic behavior of an algorithm. What is the limit?
Divide by x³: (5 − 2/x²)/(1 + 100/x³) → 5/1 = 5 as x → ∞.
An AP student encounters lim(x→0) tan(2x)/x. What is the value?
tan(2x)/x = [sin(2x)/cos(2x)]/x = [sin(2x)/x] · [1/cos(2x)] = 2 · 1 = 2.
A chemistry student needs lim(x→1) (x¹⁰ − 1)/(x − 1) to model a reaction rate. What is the answer?
(xⁿ − 1)/(x − 1) = xⁿ⁻¹ + xⁿ⁻² + ... + x + 1. At x = 1 with n = 10: sum of 10 ones = 10.
A pharmaceutical researcher evaluates lim(x→0) [e^x − 1]/x to study drug absorption kinetics. What is this fundamental limit?
lim(x→0)(eˣ − 1)/x = 1. This is the definition of the derivative of eˣ at x = 0, since d/dx(eˣ)|₀ = e⁰ = 1.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student encounters lim(x→0) (1 − cos x)/x² on a practice exam. What is the answer?
Multiply by (1+cos x)/(1+cos x): sin²x/[x²(1+cos x)]. As x→0: [sin x/x]² · 1/(1+cos x) = 1 · 1/2 = 1/2.

  Q1. Evaluate: lim(x→3) (x² − 9)/(x − 3).
    [ ] 9
    [ ] 0
    [*] 6
    [ ] 3
    Explanation: Factor: (x−3)(x+3)/(x−3) = x+3. At x = 3: 3 + 3 = 6.

  Q2. Evaluate: lim(x→5) (2x − 10)/(x² − 25).
    [ ] 2/5
    [ ] 0
    [*] 1/5
    [ ] 2/10
    Explanation: 2(x−5)/[(x−5)(x+5)] = 2/(x+5). At x = 5: 2/10 = 1/5.

  Q3. Evaluate: lim(x→0) (√(x+4) − 2)/x.
    [*] 1/4
    [ ] 0
    [ ] 1/2
    [ ] 2
    Explanation: Multiply by conjugate: (√(x+4)−2)(√(x+4)+2)/[x(√(x+4)+2)] = x/[x(√(x+4)+2)] = 1/(√(x+4)+2). At x=0: 1/(2+2) = 1/4.

  Q4. Evaluate: lim(x→2) (x³ − 8)/(x − 2).
    [ ] 4

    [ ] 6
    [*] 12
    Explanation: x³ − 8 = (x−2)(x²+2x+4). Cancel (x−2): x²+2x+4. At x=2: 4+4+4 = 12.

  Key Concepts: One-Sided Limits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lim(x→0⁺) 1/x?
As x approaches 0 from the right (positive values), 1/x grows without bound toward +∞.
What is lim(x→0⁻) 1/x?
As x approaches 0 from the left (negative values), 1/x decreases without bound toward −∞.
If f(x) = {x+1, x<2; 5, x≥2}, what is lim(x→2⁻) f(x)?
For x < 2, f(x) = x + 1. As x → 2⁻: 2 + 1 = 3.
Using the same function from Q4, what is lim(x→2⁺) f(x)?
For x ≥ 2, f(x) = 5. As x → 2⁺: f(x) = 5.
What is lim(x→3⁺) √(x − 3)?
As x → 3⁺, x − 3 → 0⁺, so √(x − 3) → 0.
What is lim(x→3⁻) √(x − 3)?
For x < 3, x − 3 is negative, so √(x − 3) is not real. The left-hand limit does not exist in the real numbers.
If f(x) = |x − 1|/(x − 1), what is lim(x→1⁺) f(x)?
For x > 1, |x−1| = x−1, so f(x) = (x−1)/(x−1) = 1.
What is lim(x→1⁻) |x − 1|/(x − 1)?
For x < 1, |x−1| = −(x−1), so f(x) = −(x−1)/(x−1) = −1.
What is a 'one-sided limit'?
A one-sided limit examines the behavior of f(x) as x approaches a from one side only: left (x → a⁻) or right (x → a⁺).
What is a 'jump discontinuity'?
A jump discontinuity occurs when lim(x→a⁻) f(x) and lim(x→a⁺) f(x) both exist but are different values.
What is a 'left-hand limit'?
The left-hand limit, written lim(x→a⁻) f(x), is the value f(x) approaches when x comes from the left (x < a).
What is a 'right-hand limit'?
The right-hand limit, written lim(x→a⁺) f(x), is the value f(x) approaches when x comes from the right (x > a).
What is the 'greatest integer function' (floor function)?
The floor function ⌊x⌋ rounds down to the nearest integer. For example, ⌊3.7⌋ = 3 and ⌊−1.2⌋ = −2.

For t just under 2 hours, the charge is $5 + $3 = $8 (first hour plus one additional hour).

A dose of medication is modeled by D(t) = {100 if t < 6; 200 if t ≥ 6} mg, where a second dose is given at t = 6 hours. What is lim(t→6⁻) D(t)?
Just before the second dose at t = 6, D(t) = 100 mg.

For w just above 1 lb, the cost switches to $8.
An electrical circuit switches on at t = 0. The current is I(t) = {0, t < 0; 5, t ≥ 0} amps. What is lim(t→0⁻) I(t)?
Before the switch at t = 0, I(t) = 0. So lim(t→0⁻) I(t) = 0.

For usage just above 500 kWh, the marginal rate is $0.15/kWh.

--- Lesson 9.4: Continuity & Types of Discontinuities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Continuity & Types of Discontinuities
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'removable discontinuity'?
A removable discontinuity is a 'hole' where the limit exists but f(a) either is undefined or does not match the limit. It can be removed by redefining f(a).
What is a 'jump discontinuity'?
A jump discontinuity occurs when lim(x→a⁻) and lim(x→a⁺) both exist but are different, causing a 'jump' in the graph.
What is an 'infinite discontinuity'?
An infinite discontinuity occurs when f(x) → ±∞ as x → a from one or both sides, typically at a vertical asymptote.
What is an 'oscillating discontinuity'?
An oscillating discontinuity occurs when f(x) oscillates infinitely near x = a (like sin(1/x) as x → 0), preventing the limit from existing.
How to Calculate
Example: A velocity function is v(t) = {3t², t<1; at+b, t≥1}. Find a and b so that v is continuous and has a continuous first derivative at t = 1.
Solution: Continuity: 3(1)² = a(1)+b → 3 = a+b. Smooth: v'(1⁻) = 6t|₁ = 6 = a. So a = 6, b = 3 − 6 = −3.

--- Lesson 9.5: Infinite Limits & Asymptotic Behavior (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Infinite Limits & Asymptotic Behavior
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lim(x→0⁺) 1/x²?
As x → 0⁺, x² → 0⁺, so 1/x² → +∞.
What is the horizontal asymptote of f(x) = (2x + 1)/(x − 3)?
The degrees are equal, so the horizontal asymptote is y = leading coefficient ratio = 2/1 = 2.
What is the vertical asymptote of f(x) = 1/(x − 5)?
The denominator is zero at x = 5, so there is a vertical asymptote at x = 5.
What is lim(x→∞) (3x² + x)/(x² + 1)?
Divide by x²: (3 + 1/x)/(1 + 1/x²) → 3/1 = 3.
What is lim(x→∞) (x + 2)/(x³ − 1)?
Degree of numerator (1) < degree of denominator (3), so the limit is 0.
What is lim(x→∞) (x³ − 2x)/(x² + 4)?
Degree of numerator (3) > degree of denominator (2), so the limit is ∞.
What is lim(x→2⁺) 1/(x − 2)?
As x → 2⁺, (x − 2) → 0⁺, so 1/(x − 2) → +∞.
What is the horizontal asymptote of f(x) = 5/(x² + 1)?
Degree of numerator (0) < degree of denominator (2), so lim(x→±∞) = 0. The horizontal asymptote is y = 0.
What is an 'infinite limit'?
An infinite limit means f(x) → +∞ or −∞ as x → a, indicating the function grows without bound near that point.
What is a 'vertical asymptote'?
A vertical asymptote is a vertical line x = a where the function approaches ±∞ from one or both sides.
What is a 'horizontal asymptote'?
A horizontal asymptote y = L is a horizontal line that the graph approaches as x goes to ±∞: lim(x→±∞) f(x) = L.
What is a 'limit at infinity'?
A limit at infinity examines the end behavior of f(x) as x grows positively or negatively without bound.
What is an 'oblique (slant) asymptote'?
An oblique asymptote occurs when the numerator's degree is exactly one more than the denominator's. Polynomial long division reveals y = mx + b.
A population model is P(t) = 5000t/(t + 50). What is the long-term population (carrying capacity)?
lim(t→∞) 5000t/(t+50) = lim 5000/(1+50/t) = 5000.
A chemical reaction follows C(t) = 200/(1 + 4e^(−0.5t)). What is the equilibrium concentration as t → ∞?
As t → ∞, e^(−0.5t) → 0, so C → 200/(1+0) = 200.
An internet bandwidth model gives speed as S(n) = 100n/(n + 10) Mbps where n is the number of servers. What is the maximum theoretical speed?
lim(n→∞) 100n/(n+10) = 100. Even with unlimited servers, the speed approaches but never exceeds 100 Mbps.
An engineer graphs f(x) = (x² − 1)/(x² + 1). What is the horizontal asymptote, and does the function ever cross it?
lim(x→±∞) (x²−1)/(x²+1) = 1. Setting f(x) = 1 gives x²−1 = x²+1, or −1 = 1, which is impossible. It never reaches y = 1.
A logistic growth model for a fish population is P(t) = 10000/(1 + 99e^(−0.3t)). What is P(0) and what does P approach as t → ∞?
P(0) = 10000/(1+99) = 10000/100 = 100. As t → ∞, e^(−0.3t) → 0, so P → 10000/1 = 10,000.
Real-World Applications
Example: An economist models cost per unit as C(x) = (500 + 3x)/x. What does the average cost approach as production becomes very large?
C(x) = 500/x + 3. As x → ∞, 500/x → 0, so C(x) → $3.
Example: A drug dosage model gives concentration C(t) = 50t·e^(−t). As t → ∞, what concentration does the drug approach?
e^(−t) decays faster than t grows, so 50t·e^(−t) → 0 as t → ∞.

--- Lesson 9.6: Applications: Instantaneous Rate of Change (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications: Instantaneous Rate of Change
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the instantaneous rate of change of f(x) = x² at x = 3?
f'(x) = 2x. At x = 3: f'(3) = 6.
A ball's height is h(t) = −16t² + 64t. What is the velocity at t = 1?
h'(t) = −32t + 64. At t = 1: h'(1) = −32 + 64 = 32 ft/s.
What is the slope of the tangent line to f(x) = √x at x = 9?
f'(x) = 1/(2√x). At x = 9: 1/(2·3) = 1/6.
Using the limit definition, what is f'(x) for f(x) = 1/x?
lim(h→0)[1/(x+h) − 1/x]/h = lim(h→0)[−h/(x(x+h)h)] = lim −1/(x(x+h)) = −1/x².
A particle's position is s(t) = t³ − 3t. What is the instantaneous velocity at t = 2?
s'(t) = 3t² − 3. At t = 2: 3(4) − 3 = 9.
What is the equation of the tangent line to y = x² at (2, 4)?
Slope = f'(2) = 4. Tangent line: y − 4 = 4(x − 2) → y = 4x − 4.
A car's position is s(t) = 5t² + 10t. What is the average velocity from t = 0 to t = 3?
[s(3)−s(0)]/3 = [5(9)+30−0]/3 = 75/3 = 25 m/s.
What is a 'tangent line'?
The tangent line at x = a touches the curve and has slope f'(a), representing the instantaneous direction of the curve.
What is a 'secant line'?
A secant line connects two points on a curve. Its slope [f(b)−f(a)]/(b−a) represents the average rate of change.
What is 'slope of a curve at a point'?
The slope of a curve at x = a is f'(a), the instantaneous rate of change, which equals the slope of the tangent line.
A drone's altitude is h(t) = −4.9t² + 20t + 5 meters. What is the instantaneous velocity at t = 2 seconds?
h'(t) = −9.8t + 20. At t = 2: h'(2) = −19.6 + 20 = 0.4 m/s.
A company's revenue is R(x) = 200x − 0.5x² (dollars) for x units sold. What is the marginal revenue at x = 100?
R'(x) = 200 − x. At x = 100: R'(100) = 200 − 100 = $100.
Water fills a tank at a rate given by V(t) = 3t² liters. What is the rate of water flow at t = 4 minutes?
V'(t) = 6t. At t = 4: V'(4) = 24 L/min.
The temperature of a cooling coffee is T(t) = 20 + 60e^(−0.1t). What is the rate of cooling at t = 10 minutes?
T'(t) = −6e^(−0.1t). At t = 10: T'(10) = −6e⁻¹ ≈ −6(0.3679) ≈ −2.21°C/min.
A roller coaster's height is h(t) = 50 sin(πt/10) meters. What is the vertical speed at t = 5?
h'(t) = 50(π/10)cos(πt/10) = 5π cos(πt/10). At t = 5: 5π cos(π/2) = 5π(0) = 0. The coaster is at its peak.
A spherical balloon is being inflated. Its volume is V(r) = (4/3)πr³. What is the rate of change of volume with respect to radius when r = 5 cm?
V'(r) = 4πr². At r = 5: V'(5) = 4π(25) = 100π cm³/cm.
A spring's displacement is x(t) = 4 cos(3t) cm. What is the velocity at t = π/6?
x'(t) = −12 sin(3t). At t = π/6: x'(π/6) = −12 sin(π/2) = −12(1) = −12 cm/s.
A savings account balance is B(t) = 5000e^(0.04t). What is the rate of growth at t = 5 years?
B'(t) = 200e^(0.04t). At t = 5: B'(5) = 200e^0.2 = 200(1.2214) ≈ $244/year.
Real-World Applications
Example: The average rate of change of f(x) = x² from x = 1 to x = 4 is:
[f(4) − f(1)]/(4 − 1) = (16 − 1)/3 = 15/3 = 5.
Example: A car's position is s(t) = 5t² + 10t. What is the average velocity from t = 0 to t = 3?
[s(3)−s(0)]/3 = [5(9)+30−0]/3 = 75/3 = 25 m/s.

    [ ] 12
    [ ] 3
    Explanation: f'(x) = 2x. At x = 3: f'(3) = 6.

  Q2. Using the limit definition, compute lim(h→0) [(2+h)³ − 8]/h.
    [*] 12
    [ ] 6
    [ ] 24
    [ ] 8
    Explanation: (2+h)³ = 8 + 12h + 6h² + h³. Subtract 8: 12h + 6h² + h³. Divide by h: 12 + 6h + h² → 12.

  Q3. The average rate of change of f(x) = x² from x = 1 to x = 4 is:
    [*] 5
    [ ] 8
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 15
    Explanation: [f(4) − f(1)]/(4 − 1) = (16 − 1)/3 = 15/3 = 5.

  Q4. A ball's height is h(t) = −16t² + 64t. What is the velocity at t = 1?
    [ ] 16 ft/s
    [ ] 48 ft/s
    [*] 32 ft/s
    [ ] 64 ft/s
    Explanation: h'(t) = −32t + 64. At t = 1: h'(1) = −32 + 64 = 32 ft/s.

  Q5. What is the slope of the tangent line to f(x) = √x at x = 9?
    [*] 1/6
    [ ] 6
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 1/3
    Explanation: f'(x) = 1/(2√x). At x = 9: 1/(2·3) = 1/6.

  Q6. Compute the derivative of f(x) = 3x² + 2x − 1 using the power rule.
    [ ] 3x + 2
    [*] 6x + 2
    [ ] 6x − 1
    [ ] 6x² + 2
    Explanation: f'(x) = 6x + 2 (power rule: bring down exponent and reduce by 1).

  Q7. Using the limit definition, what is f'(x) for f(x) = 1/x?
    [ ] 1/x²
    [ ] −1/x
    [ ] −2/x
    [*] −1/x²
    Explanation: lim(h→0)[1/(x+h) − 1/x]/h = lim(h→0)[−h/(x(x+h)h)] = lim −1/(x(x+h)) = −1/x².

  Q8. A particle's position is s(t) = t³ − 3t. What is the instantaneous velocity at t = 2?
    [*] 9
    [ ] 6
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 12
    Explanation: s'(t) = 3t² − 3. At t = 2: 3(4) − 3 = 9.

  Q9. What is the equation of the tangent line to y = x² at (2, 4)?
    [ ] y = 2x
    [ ] y = 4x + 4
    [*] y = 4x − 4
    [ ] y = 2x + 4
    Explanation: Slope = f'(2) = 4. Tangent line: y − 4 = 4(x − 2) → y = 4x − 4.

  Q10. A car's position is s(t) = 5t² + 10t. What is the average velocity from t = 0 to t = 3?
    [ ] 30 m/s
    [ ] 20 m/s
    [ ] 15 m/s
    [*] 25 m/s
    Explanation: [s(3)−s(0)]/3 = [5(9)+30−0]/3 = 75/3 = 25 m/s.

  Q11. What does 'instantaneous rate of change' mean?
    [ ] The average rate of change over a time interval
    [ ] The fastest rate the function achieves
    [ ] The second derivative of the function
    [*] The rate of change at a single specific moment, equal to the derivative at that point
    Explanation: The instantaneous rate of change at x = a is f'(a) = lim(h→0)[f(a+h)−f(a)]/h, the derivative at that point.

  Q12. What is a 'tangent line'?
    [ ] The x-axis of the graph
    [ ] A line perpendicular to the curve
    [*] A line that touches a curve at one point and has the same slope as the curve at that point
    [ ] A line connecting two points on a curve
    Explanation: The tangent line at x = a touches the curve and has slope f'(a), representing the instantaneous direction of the curve.

  Q13. What is a 'secant line'?
    [ ] A vertical line through the domain
    [*] A line passing through two points on a curve
    [ ] A horizontal line through a maximum
    [ ] A line tangent to the curve at one point
    Explanation: A secant line connects two points on a curve. Its slope [f(b)−f(a)]/(b−a) represents the average rate of change.

  Q14. What does the 'difference quotient' represent?
    [*] [f(x+h) − f(x)]/h, the slope of the secant line that approaches the derivative as h → 0
    [ ] f(x+h) − f(x), the change in the function
    [ ] The area under the curve
    [ ] The second derivative of the function
    Explanation: The difference quotient [f(x+h)−f(x)]/h gives the slope of the secant line and approaches f'(x) as h → 0.

  Q15. What does 'velocity' mean in physics?
    [ ] The total distance traveled
    [ ] The rate of change of velocity
    [ ] The absolute value of speed
    [*] The rate of change of position with respect to time (the derivative of position)

  Q16. What does 'acceleration' mean in terms of derivatives?
    [ ] The area under the velocity curve
    [*] The rate of change of velocity: a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)
    [ ] The speed of an object
    [ ] The first derivative of position
    Explanation: Acceleration is the derivative of velocity (or second derivative of position): a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t).

  Q17. What does 'marginal cost' mean in economics?

    [ ] The total cost of production

    [ ] The profit margin per unit
    Explanation: Marginal cost is C'(x), the instantaneous rate of change of cost — approximately the cost to produce one more unit.

  Q18. What is 'slope of a curve at a point'?
    [ ] The rise over run between two endpoints
    [*] The derivative f'(a), which equals the slope of the tangent line at that point
    [ ] The curvature of the graph

  Q19. What does 'rate of change' mean generally?

    [ ] The constant in a linear function
    [*] How quickly one quantity changes relative to another
    [ ] The maximum value of a function

--- Lesson 9.7: Piecewise Functions & Limits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Piecewise Functions & Limits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For f(x) = {2x, x<1; x+3, x≥1}, what is f(1)?
Since 1 ≥ 1, use the second piece: f(1) = 1 + 3 = 4.
For the same function, what is lim(x→1⁻) f(x)?
From the left (x < 1): f(x) = 2x → 2(1) = 2.
For g(x) = {x², x≤0; sin x, x>0}, what is lim(x→0) g(x)?
lim(x→0⁻) x² = 0 and lim(x→0⁺) sin x = 0. Both equal 0, so the limit is 0.
For f(x) = {3, x<−1; x², −1≤x≤2; 5, x>2}, what is f(−1)?
Since −1 is in [−1, 2], use f(x) = x²: f(−1) = (−1)² = 1.
For h(x) = {(x²−4)/(x−2), x≠2; 5, x=2}, what is lim(x→2) h(x)?
For x ≠ 2: (x²−4)/(x−2) = x+2. lim(x→2) = 4. The limit is 4, though h(2) = 5.
What is a 'piecewise function'?
A piecewise function uses different formulas on different sub-intervals, joined at boundary points. It may or may not be continuous.
What is a 'Heaviside step function'?
The Heaviside step function jumps from 0 to 1 at x = 0. It models on/off switches in engineering.
What is a 'spline'?
A spline is a piecewise polynomial where not only the function but also its derivatives match at boundary points, ensuring smooth connections.
What is a 'removable discontinuity' in a piecewise function?
A removable discontinuity can be 'repaired' by redefining the function value to equal the limit at that point.
A tax system uses T(x) = {0.10x, x≤20000; 2000+0.20(x−20000), x>20000}. What is the tax on $25,000?
T(25000) = 2000 + 0.20(25000 − 20000) = 2000 + 0.20(5000) = 2000 + 1000 = $3,000.
A medication dosage model is D(t) = {100(1−e^(−t)), 0≤t<8; D₀·e^(−0.5(t−8)), t≥8} where D₀ makes D continuous at t = 8. What is D₀?
For continuity at t = 8: D₀·e⁰ = 100(1−e⁻⁸). So D₀ = 100(1−e⁻⁸) ≈ 99.97.
A robot's velocity changes according to v(t) = {2t, 0≤t<5; 10, 5≤t<10; 10−(t−10), t≥10}. What is the velocity at t = 12?
For t ≥ 10: v(12) = 10 − (12 − 10) = 10 − 2 = 8 m/s.
A temperature controller uses T(t) = {20+5t, 0≤t<4; 40, 4≤t<8; 40−3(t−8), t≥8}. What is the temperature at t = 10?
For t ≥ 8: T(10) = 40 − 3(10 − 8) = 40 − 6 = 34°C.
How to Calculate
Example: Find a so that f(x) = {ax+1, x<3; 2x−2, x≥3} is continuous at x = 3.
Solution: Continuity: a(3) + 1 = 2(3) − 2 → 3a + 1 = 4 → 3a = 3 → a = 1.
Example: For f(x) = {x²−1, x<1; k, x=1; 2x−1, x>1}, find k so that f is continuous at x = 1.
Solution: lim(x→1⁻) = 1−1 = 0, lim(x→1⁺) = 2−1 = 1. Since 0 ≠ 1, no value of k makes f continuous.
Real-World Applications
Example: A medication dosage model is D(t) = {100(1−e^(−t)), 0≤t<8; D₀·e^(−0.5(t−8)), t≥8} where D₀ makes D continuous at t = 8. What is D₀?
For continuity at t = 8: D₀·e⁰ = 100(1−e⁻⁸). So D₀ = 100(1−e⁻⁸) ≈ 99.97.

--- Lesson 9.8: AP Calculus Prep (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Calculus Prep
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the horizontal asymptote of f(x) = (5x − 1)/(2x + 3)?
Same degree → ratio of leading coefficients: 5/2.
Using the limit definition, what is the derivative of f(x) = x³ at x = 1?
f'(x) = 3x². At x = 1: 3(1)² = 3.
What is the average rate of change of f(x) = x³ from x = 1 to x = 3?
[f(3) − f(1)]/(3 − 1) = (27 − 1)/2 = 26/2 = 13.
What is the 'Intermediate Value Theorem'?
The IVT guarantees that a continuous function on [a,b] attains every value between f(a) and f(b).
What is a 'limit at infinity'?
A limit at infinity describes the end behavior of f(x) as x grows without bound in the positive or negative direction.
What is the 'Squeeze Theorem'?
The Squeeze Theorem traps f(x) between two functions with the same limit, forcing f to have that limit as well.
What is the 'limit definition of the derivative'?
The derivative is defined as f'(x) = lim(h→0)[f(x+h)−f(x)]/h, representing the instantaneous rate of change.
What is 'end behavior'?
End behavior describes how a function behaves as x goes to positive or negative infinity.
What is a 'tangent line approximation'?
The tangent line approximation (linearization) uses L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x−a) to estimate f(x) near x = a.
On an AP exam, a student must evaluate lim(x→0) [1/x − 1/(x+x²)]. What is the result?
1/x − 1/(x(1+x)) = [1+x−1]/(x(1+x)) = x/(x(1+x)) = 1/(1+x). As x→0: 1/1 = 1.

Solution: k(1)² = 3(1) + 1 → k = 4.

    Explanation: Continuity requires: (1) f(a) is defined, (2) lim(x→a) f(x) exists, (3) the limit equals f(a).

    [*] Infinite (vertical asymptote)

  Q11. What is the 'Intermediate Value Theorem'?
    [*] If f is continuous on [a,b], then for every value N between f(a) and f(b), there exists c in (a,b) with f(c) = N
    [ ] The derivative of a continuous function is also continuous
    [ ] A continuous function must be differentiable
    [ ] Every function has a maximum and minimum on a closed interval
    Explanation: The IVT guarantees that a continuous function on [a,b] attains every value between f(a) and f(b).

  Q12. What does 'differentiable' mean?
    [ ] The function has no vertical asymptotes
    [ ] The function can be graphed
    [ ] The function is continuous
    [*] The derivative f'(a) exists at a point, meaning the limit of the difference quotient exists
    Explanation: A function is differentiable at a if f'(a) = lim(h→0)[f(a+h)−f(a)]/h exists as a finite number.

  Q13. What is a 'limit at infinity'?
    [ ] A limit that equals infinity
    [*] The value f(x) approaches as x → +∞ or x → −∞
    [ ] The maximum value of f(x)
    [ ] A limit computed at x = 10⁶
    Explanation: A limit at infinity describes the end behavior of f(x) as x grows without bound in the positive or negative direction.

  Q14. What is the 'Squeeze Theorem'?
    [*] If g(x) ≤ f(x) ≤ h(x) near a and lim g(x) = lim h(x) = L, then lim f(x) = L
    [ ] If f is between two constants, f is continuous
    [ ] A function squeezed between two curves has no derivative
    [ ] The limit of f equals the average of the bounding limits
    Explanation: The Squeeze Theorem traps f(x) between two functions with the same limit, forcing f to have that limit as well.

  Q15. What does 'indeterminate form' mean?
    [*] An expression like 0/0 or ∞/∞ that requires further analysis to determine the limit
    [ ] A limit that does not exist
    [ ] An expression that always equals zero
    [ ] A function with no closed form
    Explanation: Indeterminate forms (0/0, ∞/∞, 0·∞, etc.) mean direct substitution fails and algebraic techniques are needed.

  Q16. What does 'average rate of change' mean?

    [ ] The sum of all rates divided by the number of intervals
    Explanation: The average rate of change over [a,b] is Δy/Δx = [f(b)−f(a)]/(b−a), the slope of the secant line.

  Q17. What is the 'limit definition of the derivative'?
    [*] f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) − f(x)]/h

    [ ] f'(x) = [f(b) − f(a)]/(b − a)
    [ ] f'(x) = f(x+1) − f(x)
    Explanation: The derivative is defined as f'(x) = lim(h→0)[f(x+h)−f(x)]/h, representing the instantaneous rate of change.

  Q18. What does 'one-sided limit' mean?

  Q19. What is 'end behavior'?

  Q20. What is a 'tangent line approximation'?

  Q21. An AP exam question asks: if f is continuous on [0, 5], f(0) = −3, and f(5) = 7, must there be a c in (0, 5) where f(c) = 0?
