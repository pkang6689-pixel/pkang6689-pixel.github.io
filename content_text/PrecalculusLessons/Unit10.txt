--- Lesson 10.1: Derivative as Slope of Tangent Line (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Derivative as Slope of Tangent Line
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the slope of the tangent line to y = x² at x = 3?
f'(x) = 2x. At x = 3: slope = 2(3) = 6.
What is the equation of the tangent line to y = x³ at (1, 1)?
f'(x) = 3x², f'(1) = 3. Tangent: y − 1 = 3(x − 1) → y = 3x − 2.
What is the slope of the secant line through (1, 1) and (3, 9) on y = x²?
Slope = (9 − 1)/(3 − 1) = 8/2 = 4.
Using the limit definition, what is the derivative of f(x) = x² + 3x?
f'(x) = lim(h→0)[(x+h)²+3(x+h)−x²−3x]/h = lim(2xh+h²+3h)/h = 2x+3.
If the tangent line to y = f(x) at x = 2 has equation y = 5x − 3, what is f'(2)?
The slope of the tangent line is 5, so f'(2) = 5.
What is the derivative of a constant function f(x) = 7?
The derivative of any constant is 0, since the graph is a horizontal line with slope 0.
What is a 'derivative'?
The derivative f'(x) = lim(h→0)[f(x+h)−f(x)]/h is the instantaneous rate of change and slope of the tangent line.
What is a 'tangent line' to a curve?
The tangent line at (a, f(a)) has slope f'(a) and represents the best linear approximation to the curve at that point.
What is the 'difference quotient'?
The difference quotient [f(x+h)−f(x)]/h measures the average rate of change over an interval of width h.
What is the notation f'(x) called?
f'(x) is Lagrange (prime) notation. Leibniz notation writes df/dx. Both represent the derivative.
A car's position is s(t) = t² + 4t meters. Using the derivative, what is the car's speed at t = 3 seconds?
s'(t) = 2t + 4. At t = 3: s'(3) = 6 + 4 = 10 m/s.
An economist models profit as P(x) = −x² + 100x − 500. What is the slope of the tangent line at x = 30 units?
P'(x) = −2x + 100. At x = 30: P'(30) = −60 + 100 = 40.
A biologist models bacterial growth as N(t) = 100t². What is the growth rate at t = 5 hours?
N'(t) = 200t. At t = 5: N'(5) = 1000 bacteria/hour.
A student uses the tangent line to y = x³ at x = 2 to approximate 2.1³. What is the estimate?
f(2) = 8, f'(2) = 12. L(2.1) = 8 + 12(0.1) = 8 + 1.2 = 9.2.
A company's revenue is R(x) = 50√x thousand dollars. What is the marginal revenue at x = 100?
R'(x) = 25/√x. At x = 100: R'(100) = 25/10 = 2.5 thousand = $2,500.
A skier's altitude is h(x) = 2000 − 0.1x² meters after traveling x meters horizontally. What is the slope of the hill at x = 50?
h'(x) = −0.2x. At x = 50: h'(50) = −10 (descending steeply).
An engineer computes the tangent to the stress-strain curve σ(ε) = 200ε − 50ε² at ε = 1. What is the tangent slope (Young's modulus at that point)?
σ'(ε) = 200 − 100ε. At ε = 1: σ'(1) = 200 − 100 = 100.

--- Lesson 10.2: Derivative Rules (power rule basics) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Derivative Rules (power rule basics)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Using the power rule, what is the derivative of f(x) = x⁵?
Power rule: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹. So d/dx(x⁵) = 5x⁴.
What is the derivative of f(x) = 3x⁴ − 2x² + 7?
f'(x) = 3(4x³) − 2(2x) + 0 = 12x³ − 4x.
What is the derivative of f(x) = √x = x^(1/2)?
d/dx(x^(1/2)) = (1/2)x^(−1/2) = 1/(2√x).
What is the derivative of f(x) = 1/x = x⁻¹?
d/dx(x⁻¹) = −1 · x⁻² = −1/x².
If f(x) = 4x³ and g(x) = 2x², what is (f + g)'(x)?
f'(x) = 12x², g'(x) = 4x. (f+g)' = 12x² + 4x.
What is the derivative of f(x) = 5?
The derivative of any constant is 0.
What is d/dx(x)?
d/dx(x) = d/dx(x¹) = 1 · x⁰ = 1.
What is the derivative of f(x) = x³/3?
f'(x) = (1/3)(3x²) = x².
What is the second derivative of f(x) = x⁴?
f'(x) = 4x³, f''(x) = 12x².
What is the derivative of f(x) = 2x⁻³?
f'(x) = 2(−3)x⁻⁴ = −6x⁻⁴ = −6/x⁴.
What is the 'power rule'?
The power rule states d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹. Multiply by the exponent, then decrease the exponent by 1.
What is the 'constant multiple rule'?
The constant multiple rule says constants can be pulled out of the derivative: d/dx[cf(x)] = c·f'(x).
What is the 'sum rule' for derivatives?
The sum rule: the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives.
What is the 'difference rule' for derivatives?
The difference rule: the derivative of a difference is the difference of the derivatives.
What is the 'constant rule'?
The derivative of any constant is zero because a constant function has zero rate of change.
A physics student finds the acceleration of a particle with position s(t) = 2t⁵ − 3t³ + t. What is a(t) = s''(t)?
s'(t) = 10t⁴ − 9t² + 1. s''(t) = 40t³ − 18t.
A manufacturer's cost function is C(x) = 0.001x³ − 0.5x² + 100x + 5000. What is the marginal cost at x = 200?
C'(x) = 0.003x² − x + 100. At x = 200: 0.003(40000) − 200 + 100 = 120 − 200 + 100 = $20.
A civil engineer models a bridge cable's shape as y = 0.01x⁴ − 0.5x² + 10. What is the slope at x = 5?
y' = 0.04x³ − x. At x = 5: 0.04(125) − 5 = 5 − 5 = 0.
An electrical engineer models current as I(t) = 3t^(2/3) amps. What is the rate of change at t = 8?
I'(t) = 3(2/3)t^(−1/3) = 2t^(−1/3). At t = 8: 2(8)^(−1/3) = 2/2 = 1 amp/s.
A biologist models a population as P(t) = 500t^(1/2) + 200. What is the growth rate at t = 25 years?
P'(t) = 250t^(−1/2) = 250/√t. At t = 25: 250/5 = 50 per year.
A student finds that f(x) = x⁶ − 3x⁴ + 2x. What is f'''(x)?
f'=6x⁵−12x³+2, f''=30x⁴−36x², f'''=120x³−72x.
An economist models GDP as G(t) = 0.5t³ + 2t² + 100t billion dollars (t in decades from 2000). What is the GDP growth rate at t = 2 (year 2020)?
G'(t) = 1.5t² + 4t + 100. At t = 2: 1.5(4) + 8 + 100 = 6 + 8 + 100 = 114.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the 'constant multiple rule'?
The constant multiple rule says constants can be pulled out of the derivative: d/dx[cf(x)] = c·f'(x).

  Q7. What is d/dx(x)?
    [ ] 0
    [*] 1

    Explanation: d/dx(x) = d/dx(x¹) = 1 · x⁰ = 1.

  Q11. What is the 'power rule'?
    [ ] The derivative of a power function is another power function with the same exponent
    [ ] If f(x) = nˣ, then f'(x) = nˣ ln n
    [*] If f(x) = xⁿ, then f'(x) = nxⁿ⁻¹ for any real number n
    [ ] If f(x) = xⁿ, then f'(x) = xⁿ⁻¹
    Explanation: The power rule states d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹. Multiply by the exponent, then decrease the exponent by 1.

  Q12. What is the 'constant multiple rule'?
    [ ] d/dx[c · f(x)] = c · f(x)
    [ ] The derivative of a constant is 1
    [ ] Constants cannot be differentiated
    [*] d/dx[c · f(x)] = c · f'(x), where c is a constant
    Explanation: The constant multiple rule says constants can be pulled out of the derivative: d/dx[cf(x)] = c·f'(x).

  Q13. What is the 'sum rule' for derivatives?
    [ ] d/dx[f(x) + g(x)] = f(x) + g'(x)
    [ ] The sum of derivatives equals zero
    [*] d/dx[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)
    [ ] d/dx[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) · g'(x)
    Explanation: The sum rule: the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives.

  Q14. What is the 'difference rule' for derivatives?
    [ ] d/dx[f(x) − g(x)] = 0
    [*] d/dx[f(x) − g(x)] = f'(x) − g'(x)
    [ ] d/dx[f(x) − g(x)] = f'(x)/g'(x)
    [ ] d/dx[f(x) − g(x)] = f'(x) · g'(x)
    Explanation: The difference rule: the derivative of a difference is the difference of the derivatives.

  Q15. What does 'second derivative' mean?
    [ ] The derivative evaluated at x = 2
    [ ] Twice the first derivative
    [ ] The derivative of x²
    [*] The derivative of the derivative: f''(x) = d/dx[f'(x)]
    Explanation: The second derivative f''(x) is the derivative of f'(x). It measures the rate of change of the rate of change (e.g., acceleration).

  Q16. What does 'higher-order derivative' mean?

    [ ] Derivatives that are larger than the original function
    [*] Derivatives taken multiple times: f'', f''', f⁽⁴⁾, etc.
    [ ] Derivatives in higher dimensions
    Explanation: Higher-order derivatives are obtained by differentiating repeatedly. The nth derivative is denoted f⁽ⁿ⁾(x).

  Q17. What is the 'constant rule'?
    [*] d/dx(c) = 0 for any constant c
    [ ] Constants have no derivatives
    [ ] d/dx(c) = 1
    [ ] d/dx(c) = c
    Explanation: The derivative of any constant is zero because a constant function has zero rate of change.

  Q18. What does 'polynomial derivative' mean?

    [ ] A polynomial of degree n has derivative of degree n
    [ ] A derivative that is itself a polynomial
    Explanation: To differentiate a polynomial, apply the power rule to each term. The result is a polynomial of degree one less.

  Q19. What does 'negative exponent' mean in the power rule?

    [*] The power rule works for negative exponents: d/dx(x⁻ⁿ) = −nx⁻ⁿ⁻¹
    [ ] The derivative of a negative exponent is always positive
    [ ] Negative exponents cannot be differentiated
    Explanation: The power rule extends to negative exponents. For example, d/dx(x⁻²) = −2x⁻³.

  Q20. What does 'fractional exponent' mean in the power rule?

    [ ] Fractional exponents only apply to perfect squares
    [ ] Fractional exponents cannot be differentiated
    [ ] The derivative of a fractional power is always a fraction
    Explanation: The power rule applies to any real exponent, including fractions: d/dx(x^(1/3)) = (1/3)x^(−2/3).

--- Lesson 10.3: Applications in Optimization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Optimization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the maximum value of f(x) = -2x^2 + 8x + 1?
Vertex at x = -8/(2(-2)) = 2. f(2) = -2(4) + 8(2) + 1 = -8 + 16 + 1 = 9. Since a < 0, the parabola opens downward and 9 is the maximum.
What is the minimum value of f(x) = x^2 + 4x + 7?
Vertex at x = -4/2 = -2. f(-2) = 4 - 8 + 7 = 3. Since a = 1 > 0, the parabola opens upward and the minimum value is 3.
What is the maximum product of two numbers whose sum is 30?
P = x(30 - x) = 30x - x^2. Maximum at x = 15, so P = 15 * 15 = 225.
What is a 'critical point' of a function?
A critical point is a value in the domain of f where f'(x) = 0 or f'(x) does not exist. These are the candidates for local extrema.
What is a 'constraint' in an optimization problem?
A constraint is a condition (such as a fixed perimeter, budget, or material amount) that restricts which values the variables can take in an optimization problem.
What is the 'objective function' in an optimization problem?
The objective function is the quantity you seek to optimize (maximize or minimize). For example, if maximizing the area of a garden, the area formula is the objective function.
What is an 'inflection point'?
An inflection point is where a function changes concavity, switching from concave up to concave down or vice versa. At such points, f''(x) = 0 or is undefined.
A company finds its profit function is P(x) = -x^2 + 120x - 2000 dollars, where x is units produced. How many units maximize profit, and what is the maximum profit?
P'(x) = -2x + 120 = 0 gives x = 60. P(60) = -3600 + 7200 - 2000 = 1600. Since the leading coefficient is negative, x = 60 gives the maximum profit of $1600.
A ball is thrown upward from ground level with an initial velocity of 48 ft/s. Its height is h(t) = 48t - 16t^2. What is the maximum height reached?
h'(t) = 48 - 32t = 0 gives t = 1.5 s. h(1.5) = 48(1.5) - 16(2.25) = 72 - 36 = 36 ft.
Real-World Applications
Example: A store sells 200 shirts per month at $25 each. For every $1 price increase, sales drop by 5 shirts. What price maximizes monthly revenue?
Let x = number of $1 increases. Revenue R = (25 + x)(200 - 5x) = 5000 + 75x - 5x^2. R'(x) = 75 - 10x = 0 gives x = 7.5. Optimal price = 25 + 7.5 = $32.50.

--- Lesson 10.4: Area Under Curves (intro to integration) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Area Under Curves (intro to integration)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the area under f(x) = 4 from x = 0 to x = 5?
The area under a constant function f(x) = 4 from x = 0 to x = 5 is a rectangle with width 5 and height 4, so A = 4 * 5 = 20.
What is the area under f(x) = x from x = 0 to x = 6?
The region is a triangle with base 6 and height 6. Area = (1/2)(6)(6) = 18.
What is the area under f(x) = 2x from x = 0 to x = 3?
The region is a triangle with base 3 and height f(3) = 6. Area = (1/2)(3)(6) = 9.
What is the exact area under f(x) = x^2 from x = 0 to x = 3?
The antiderivative of x^2 is x^3/3. Evaluating from 0 to 3: (3^3)/3 - 0 = 27/3 = 9.
Using the trapezoidal rule with n = 2 on f(x) = x^2 from x = 0 to x = 2, what is the estimate?
h = (2-0)/2 = 1. Trap = (h/2)[f(0) + 2f(1) + f(2)] = (1/2)[0 + 2(1) + 4] = (1/2)(6) = 3.
What is the area of the region bounded by f(x) = 3 and the x-axis from x = 2 to x = 7?
The region is a rectangle with width (7 - 2) = 5 and height 3. Area = 3 * 5 = 15.
What is a 'Riemann sum'?
A Riemann sum approximates the area under a curve by dividing the region into rectangles, computing each rectangle's area, and summing them all.
What is the 'definite integral'?
The definite integral of f(x) from a to b is defined as the limit of Riemann sums as the number of subintervals n approaches infinity and the width of each subinterval approaches zero.
What is an 'antiderivative' of a function f(x)?
An antiderivative (or indefinite integral) of f(x) is a function F(x) such that F'(x) = f(x). For example, x^3/3 is an antiderivative of x^2.
What is the 'trapezoidal rule'?
The trapezoidal rule approximates the area under a curve by connecting consecutive points with straight lines, forming trapezoids. It typically gives a better estimate than left or right Riemann sums.
What is the meaning of the integral symbol with limits: the integral from a to b of f(x) dx?
The notation represents the definite integral, which computes the net signed area between the curve y = f(x) and the x-axis over the interval [a, b].
A company's marginal revenue function is MR(x) = 50 - 0.4x dollars per unit. What is the total revenue earned from selling the first 100 units?
TR = integral from 0 to 100 of (50 - 0.4x) dx = [50x - 0.2x^2] from 0 to 100 = 5000 - 2000 = $3000.
A meteorologist models rainfall rate as r(t) = 0.5t inches per hour for t in [0, 4]. Using left-endpoint Riemann sum with 4 rectangles of width 1, what is the estimated total rainfall?
Left endpoints: t = 0, 1, 2, 3. Heights: r(0) = 0, r(1) = 0.5, r(2) = 1.0, r(3) = 1.5. Sum = 1(0 + 0.5 + 1.0 + 1.5) = 3 inches.

--- Lesson 10.5: Connecting Precalculus to Calculus (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Connecting Precalculus to Calculus
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between the slope of a secant line and the slope of a tangent line?
As the two points on a secant line get closer together, the secant line approaches the tangent line. The tangent slope is the limit of the secant slopes, which is the derivative.
What is the 'difference quotient' and how does it connect to calculus?
The difference quotient [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h measures the average rate of change over an interval of width h. Taking the limit as h approaches 0 gives the derivative, the instantaneous rate of change.
What is meant by 'local linearity' of a function?
Local linearity means that a differentiable function, when viewed at a very small scale near a point, appears nearly straight. The tangent line (derivative) provides this local linear approximation.
What is a 'transcendental function' and why is it important in calculus?
Transcendental functions like e^x, sin x, cos x, and ln x go beyond algebraic operations. They are studied extensively in precalculus and have elegant derivative and integral formulas in calculus.
What is the 'Mean Value Theorem' and what precalculus idea does it generalize?
The MVT states that for a continuous function on [a,b] that is differentiable on (a,b), there exists c in (a,b) where f'(c) = [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a). This connects the instantaneous rate of change to the average rate of change.
What is 'linearization' of a function at a point?
Linearization approximates a differentiable function near a point using its tangent line: L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a). This is the simplest Taylor approximation and builds on the precalculus idea of tangent lines.
A biologist models a population with P(t) = 500e^(0.03t). Using the precalculus concept of exponential growth and the calculus derivative, what is the population's instantaneous growth rate at t = 10 years?
P'(t) = 500(0.03)e^(0.03t) = 15e^(0.03t). At t = 10: P'(10) = 15e^(0.3) = 15(1.3499) = approximately 20.2 organisms per year.
An engineer uses the function h(t) = -16t^2 + 80t + 5 to model the height of a projectile. Combining precalculus vertex analysis with calculus, when does the projectile reach its maximum height and what is that height?
h'(t) = -32t + 80 = 0 gives t = 2.5 s. h(2.5) = -16(6.25) + 80(2.5) + 5 = -100 + 200 + 5 = 105 ft. This combines the vertex formula from precalculus with the derivative from calculus.
A student uses the precalculus identity sin^2(x) + cos^2(x) = 1 and the calculus derivative d/dx[sin x] = cos x. What is d/dx[sin^2(x)]?
Using the chain rule: d/dx[sin^2(x)] = 2sin(x) * cos(x) = 2sin(x)cos(x), which by the double angle identity equals sin(2x). This shows how trig identities and calculus work together.
An architect designs a parabolic arch described by y = 20 - 0.2x^2 (in feet). Using precalculus to find the x-intercepts and calculus to find the area, what is the area under the arch?
x-intercepts: 20 - 0.2x^2 = 0 gives x^2 = 100, so x = -10 and x = 10. Area = integral from -10 to 10 of (20 - 0.2x^2) dx = [20x - 0.2x^3/3] from -10 to 10 = (200 - 200/3) - (-200 + 200/3) = 400 - 400/3 = 800/3 which is approximately 266.7 ft^2.
A financial analyst models investment value as V(t) = 10000(1.06)^t. Using the connection between exponential functions and calculus, what is the instantaneous rate of growth at t = 5 years?
V'(t) = 10000(1.06)^t * ln(1.06). At t = 5: V'(5) = 10000(1.06)^5 * ln(1.06) = 10000(1.3382)(0.05827) = approximately $779.69 per year.
A student is asked to find the area between y = x^2 and y = x from x = 0 to x = 1. Using precalculus graphing and calculus integration, what is this area?
On [0,1], x >= x^2. Area = integral from 0 to 1 of (x - x^2) dx = [x^2/2 - x^3/3] from 0 to 1 = 1/2 - 1/3 = 1/6.
Real-World Applications
Example: The average rate of change of f(x) from x = a to x = b is given by:
The average rate of change is the slope of the secant line connecting (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)), calculated as [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a). This leads directly to the derivative when b approaches a.

  Key Concepts: AP-Style Practice Problems
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the limit as x approaches 2 of (x^2 - 4)/(x - 2)?
Factor: (x^2 - 4)/(x - 2) = (x+2)(x-2)/(x-2) = x+2 for x != 2. As x approaches 2: limit = 2 + 2 = 4.
If f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2, what is f'(1)?
f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x. f'(1) = 3(1) - 6(1) = 3 - 6 = -3.
What is the equation of the tangent line to y = x^2 at the point (3, 9)?
y' = 2x, so slope at x = 3 is 6. Tangent line: y - 9 = 6(x - 3), giving y = 6x - 9.
What is the limit as x approaches infinity of (3x^2 + 5)/(x^2 - 1)?
When degrees are equal, the limit is the ratio of leading coefficients: 3/1 = 3.

f'(x) = 2cos(2x). f'(pi/4) = 2cos(pi/2) = 2(0) = 0.

L'Hopital's Rule states that if lim f(x)/g(x) gives 0/0 or infinity/infinity, then lim f(x)/g(x) = lim f'(x)/g'(x), provided the latter limit exists.
What is the 'chain rule' in differentiation?
The chain rule states that the derivative of a composite function f(g(x)) is f'(g(x)) * g'(x). You differentiate the outer function evaluated at the inner, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.
What is a 'removable discontinuity'?
A removable discontinuity (hole) occurs when the limit of f(x) as x approaches c exists, but either f(c) is undefined or f(c) does not equal the limit. It can be 'removed' by redefining f(c).
What is the 'product rule' for derivatives?
The product rule states that the derivative of f(x)g(x) is f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x). You differentiate each factor while keeping the other unchanged, then add the results.
What is 'related rates' in calculus?
Related rates problems involve two or more quantities that change with time, connected by an equation. By implicitly differentiating with respect to time, you can find how one rate of change relates to another.
The temperature of a cup of coffee is modeled by T(t) = 70 + 130e^(-0.05t) degrees F, where t is minutes. What is the rate of cooling at t = 10 minutes?

A particle has acceleration a(t) = 6t - 2 and initial velocity v(0) = 5. An AP exam question asks: what is the displacement of the particle from t = 0 to t = 3?
v(t) = integral of a(t) dt = 3t^2 - 2t + C. v(0) = 5 gives C = 5, so v(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 5. Displacement = integral from 0 to 3 of (3t^2 - 2t + 5) dt = [t^3 - t^2 + 5t] from 0 to 3 = 27 - 9 + 15 = 33.

The average value of f on [a, b] is defined as (1/(b-a)) * integral from a to b of f(x) dx. It represents the constant height of a rectangle with the same area as the region under f.

--- Lesson 10.7: Capstone Project: Modeling with Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Capstone Project: Modeling with Functions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'mathematical model'?
A mathematical model uses functions, equations, or systems to represent and predict real-world phenomena. Models simplify reality to capture the most important relationships.
What is a 'residual' in regression analysis?
A residual is the difference between an observed data value and the value predicted by the model: residual = observed - predicted. Small residuals indicate a good fit.
What is the meaning of 'parameter' in a mathematical model?
A parameter is a constant in a model (like a, b in y = ab^x) whose specific value is determined by fitting the model to observed data. Changing parameters adjusts the model's shape.
What is 'regression' in the context of data analysis?
Regression is a statistical technique that determines the parameters of a function that best fits a set of data, typically by minimizing the sum of squared residuals.
What is the meaning of 'domain restriction' when applying a model to real-world data?
When modeling real situations, the mathematical domain must be restricted to values that are physically meaningful. For example, time and quantity cannot be negative in most models.
A town's population was 5000 in 2010 and 8000 in 2020. Assuming exponential growth P(t) = 5000*b^t where t is years since 2010, what is the predicted population in 2030?
At t = 10: 8000 = 5000*b^10, so b^10 = 1.6. At t = 20: P = 5000*b^20 = 5000*(b^10)^2 = 5000*(1.6)^2 = 5000*2.56 = 12800.
A scientist collects the following data for a decaying substance: (0, 100), (2, 82), (4, 67), (6, 55), (8, 45). She fits an exponential model y = 100*e^(-kt). Using the point (2, 82), what is the approximate value of k?
82 = 100*e^(-2k), so e^(-2k) = 0.82. Taking ln: -2k = ln(0.82) = -0.1985. Thus k = 0.0993, approximately 0.099.
A car's value depreciates: Year 0: $30,000; Year 1: $25,500; Year 2: $21,675; Year 3: $18,424. What type of model best fits this data, and what is the decay factor?
Checking ratios: 25500/30000 = 0.85, 21675/25500 = 0.85, 18424/21675 = 0.85. Constant ratio confirms exponential decay V(t) = 30000(0.85)^t with decay factor 0.85 (15% loss per year).
A biologist tracks a bacterial colony that grows logistically. The data shows: initial population 100, rapid growth phase reaching 500 after 6 hours, and leveling off near 1000. Using P(t) = 1000/(1 + 9e^(-0.5t)), what is the predicted population at t = 4 hours?
P(4) = 1000/(1 + 9e^(-2)) = 1000/(1 + 9(0.1353)) = 1000/(1 + 1.218) = 1000/2.218 = approximately 451 bacteria.
An economist models housing prices over 10 years. The data shows: prices rose linearly from $200K to $350K in years 0-5, then dropped linearly from $350K to $280K in years 5-10. What is the appropriate piecewise model?
First segment: slope = (350-200)/5 = 30, so P = 30t + 200. Second segment: slope = (280-350)/5 = -14, and at t=5, P=350, so 350 = -14(5) + b gives b = 420. Thus P = -14t + 420 for 5 < t <= 10.
A pharmaceutical researcher tests drug absorption: data shows rapid initial increase fitting a square root model y = 50*sqrt(t) for the first 4 hours. What is the predicted absorption at t = 9 hours if the model continues to hold?
y(9) = 50*sqrt(9) = 50 * 3 = 150 units. Note: this is extrapolation beyond the observed data range (0-4 hours), so the prediction should be used cautiously.
An environmental agency monitors carbon dioxide levels (ppm) over 12 months: the data appears sinusoidal with a mean of 415 ppm, amplitude of 5 ppm, and a period of 12 months (peaking in May, month 5). Using T(m) = 5*cos((2*pi/12)(m - 5)) + 415, what is the predicted CO2 level in September (month 9)?
T(9) = 5*cos((2*pi/12)(9 - 5)) + 415 = 5*cos(2*pi/3) + 415 = 5(-0.5) + 415 = -2.5 + 415 = 412.5 ppm.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which function type is best for modeling data that grows by a constant percentage each period?
Constant percentage growth (constant ratio between successive values) is modeled by an exponential function f(x) = ab^x, where b is the growth factor.
Example: Monthly average temperatures in a city vary between 30 F (January) and 80 F (July). Using a sinusoidal model T(m) = A*sin(B(m - C)) + D where m is the month number, what are A and D?
Amplitude A = (max - min)/2 = (80 - 30)/2 = 25. Vertical shift D = (max + min)/2 = (80 + 30)/2 = 55. So the temperature oscillates 25 degrees above and below 55 F.

--- Lesson 10.8: Comprehensive Review & AP Exam Prep (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Comprehensive Review & AP Exam Prep
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the exact value of sin(pi/3)?
From the unit circle, sin(pi/3) = sin(60 degrees) = sqrt(3)/2. This is one of the key values from the 30-60-90 triangle.
What is the sum of the geometric series 3 + 3/2 + 3/4 + 3/8 + ... ?
First term a = 3, common ratio r = 1/2. Since |r| < 1, the sum = a/(1-r) = 3/(1 - 1/2) = 3/(1/2) = 6.
What is the limit as x approaches 3 of (x^2 - 9)/(x - 3)?
Factor: (x^2 - 9)/(x - 3) = (x + 3)(x - 3)/(x - 3) = x + 3. Limit as x approaches 3 = 3 + 3 = 6.
If f(x) = 3x^4 - 2x^2 + 1, what is f'(x)?
Using the power rule: f'(x) = 3(4)x^3 - 2(2)x + 0 = 12x^3 - 4x.
What is the horizontal asymptote of f(x) = (5x^2 + 3)/(2x^2 - 1)?
When the degrees of the numerator and denominator are equal, the horizontal asymptote is the ratio of leading coefficients: y = 5/2.
Using the double angle formula, what is sin(2*pi/3)?
sin(2pi/3) = sin(120 degrees). Since 120 degrees is in QII, sin is positive. sin(120) = sin(180-60) = sin(60) = sqrt(3)/2.
What is the integral from 1 to 4 of 2x dx?
Integral of 2x dx = x^2. From 1 to 4: 4^2 - 1^2 = 16 - 1 = 15.
If log_2(x) = 5, what is x?
log_2(x) = 5 means 2^5 = x, so x = 32.
What is the period of y = 3sin(4x)?
For y = A*sin(Bx), the period is 2*pi/|B|. Here B = 4, so period = 2*pi/4 = pi/2.
What is an 'asymptote'?
An asymptote is a line that a function's graph approaches as x or y goes to infinity (or at a point of discontinuity). Types include horizontal, vertical, and oblique asymptotes.
What is 'mathematical induction' used for?
Mathematical induction proves statements for all positive integers by: (1) proving it for n = 1 (base case), and (2) showing that if it holds for n = k, it also holds for n = k+1 (inductive step).
What is the 'unit circle' and why is it important?
The unit circle has radius 1 and center at the origin. Any point (cos theta, sin theta) on it gives the cosine and sine of angle theta, providing a geometric foundation for trigonometry.
What is a 'polar coordinate'?
Polar coordinates represent a point as (r, theta), where r is the distance from the origin and theta is the angle from the positive x-axis. They relate to Cartesian coordinates by x = r*cos(theta), y = r*sin(theta).
What is a 'vector' in precalculus?
A vector has both magnitude (length) and direction. In 2D, it can be written as  or ai + bj. Vectors are essential in physics and calculus for describing forces, velocity, and displacement.
What is 'sigma notation'?
Sigma notation uses the symbol (capital sigma) to express sums compactly. For example, the sum from i=1 to n of i^2 means 1^2 + 2^2 + ... + n^2.
A Ferris wheel has a radius of 40 ft, and its center is 50 ft above the ground. It completes one revolution every 60 seconds. A rider boards at the lowest point. What is the rider's height after 15 seconds?
h(t) = -40cos(2pi*t/60) + 50 (starts at bottom). At t = 15: h = -40cos(pi/2) + 50 = -40(0) + 50 = 50 ft. After a quarter revolution, the rider is at the center height.
Using the binomial theorem, what is the coefficient of x^3 in the expansion of (2x + 1)^5?
The x^3 term uses C(5,3)(2x)^3(1)^2 = 10 * 8x^3 * 1 = 80x^3. The coefficient is 80.
A pendulum's angle from vertical is modeled by theta(t) = 0.3cos(5t) radians, where t is in seconds. What is the maximum angular velocity, and when does it first occur?
theta'(t) = -1.5sin(5t). Maximum angular speed |theta'(t)| = 1.5 occurs when |sin(5t)| = 1. The first time is when 5t = pi/2, so t = pi/10 seconds.
A student must evaluate the limit as x approaches 0 of (e^x - 1)/x on an AP exam. What is the answer?
This is the definition of the derivative of e^x at x = 0. Since d/dx[e^x] = e^x and e^0 = 1, the limit equals 1. Alternatively, by L'Hopital's Rule: lim e^x/1 = 1.
An AP problem asks: given that the integral from 0 to 6 of f(x) dx = 10, and f(x) >= 0 on [0, 6], what is the average value of f on [0, 6]?
Average value = (1/(b-a)) * integral from a to b of f(x) dx = (1/6)(10) = 10/6 = 5/3.
A comprehensive AP problem: a rock is thrown upward from an 80-ft cliff with initial velocity 64 ft/s. Height is h(t) = -16t^2 + 64t + 80. (a) When does it reach maximum height, (b) what is the maximum height, and (c) when does it hit the ground?
(a) h'(t) = -32t + 64 = 0 gives t = 2 s. (b) h(2) = -64 + 128 + 80 = 144 ft. (c) -16t^2 + 64t + 80 = 0. Dividing by -16: t^2 - 4t - 5 = 0 = (t - 5)(t + 1), so t = 5 s (positive root).

    Explanation: v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t - 1)(t - 3). v(t) = 0 at t = 1 and t = 3. At t = 1, velocity changes from positive to negative (check: v(0) = 9 > 0, v(2) = -3 < 0), so the particle first changes direction at t = 1.

  Q24. Using the binomial theorem, what is the coefficient of x^3 in the expansion of (2x + 1)^5?
    [ ] 10
    [ ] 40
    [ ] 160
    [*] 80
    Explanation: The x^3 term uses C(5,3)(2x)^3(1)^2 = 10 * 8x^3 * 1 = 80x^3. The coefficient is 80.

  Q25. A manufacturer finds that the cost to produce x items is C(x) = 2000 + 15x + 0.01x^2 and the revenue is R(x) = 40x - 0.02x^2. How many items should be produced to maximize profit?
    [ ] 1000 items
    [ ] 250 items
    [*] Approximately 417 items
    [ ] 500 items
    Explanation: P(x) = R(x) - C(x) = 40x - 0.02x^2 - 2000 - 15x - 0.01x^2 = -0.03x^2 + 25x - 2000. P'(x) = -0.06x + 25 = 0 gives x = 25/0.06 = approximately 416.7, or about 417 items.

  Q26. A pendulum's angle from vertical is modeled by theta(t) = 0.3cos(5t) radians, where t is in seconds. What is the maximum angular velocity, and when does it first occur?
    [ ] 0.3 rad/s, first occurring at t = 0
    [ ] 0.15 rad/s, first occurring at t = pi/10 seconds
    [ ] 1.5 rad/s, first occurring at t = pi/5 seconds
    [*] 1.5 rad/s, first occurring at t = pi/10 seconds
    Explanation: theta'(t) = -1.5sin(5t). Maximum angular speed |theta'(t)| = 1.5 occurs when |sin(5t)| = 1. The first time is when 5t = pi/2, so t = pi/10 seconds.

  Q27. A student must evaluate the limit as x approaches 0 of (e^x - 1)/x on an AP exam. What is the answer?
    [ ] 0
    [ ] e
    [*] 1
    [ ] Does not exist
    Explanation: This is the definition of the derivative of e^x at x = 0. Since d/dx[e^x] = e^x and e^0 = 1, the limit equals 1. Alternatively, by L'Hopital's Rule: lim e^x/1 = 1.

  Q28. A city's population follows the logistic model P(t) = 50000/(1 + 24e^(-0.2t)) where t is years since 2000. In what year does the population reach 25000 (half the carrying capacity)?
    [ ] Approximately 2010
    [ ] Approximately 2020
    [*] Approximately 2016
    [ ] Approximately 2025
    Explanation: 25000 = 50000/(1 + 24e^(-0.2t)), so 1 + 24e^(-0.2t) = 2, giving 24e^(-0.2t) = 1, e^(-0.2t) = 1/24. Taking ln: -0.2t = ln(1/24) = -ln(24) = -3.178. So t = 15.89, approximately year 2016.

  Q29. An AP problem asks: given that the integral from 0 to 6 of f(x) dx = 10, and f(x) >= 0 on [0, 6], what is the average value of f on [0, 6]?
    [ ] 10/3
    [ ] 60
    [ ] 10
    [*] 5/3
    Explanation: Average value = (1/(b-a)) * integral from a to b of f(x) dx = (1/6)(10) = 10/6 = 5/3.

  Q30. A comprehensive AP problem: a rock is thrown upward from an 80-ft cliff with initial velocity 64 ft/s. Height is h(t) = -16t^2 + 64t + 80. (a) When does it reach maximum height, (b) what is the maximum height, and (c) when does it hit the ground?
    [*] (a) t = 2 s, (b) 144 ft, (c) t = 5 s
    [ ] (a) t = 2 s, (b) 144 ft, (c) t = 4 s
    [ ] (a) t = 3 s, (b) 180 ft, (c) t = 7 s
    [ ] (a) t = 4 s, (b) 200 ft, (c) t = 6 s
    Explanation: (a) h'(t) = -32t + 64 = 0 gives t = 2 s. (b) h(2) = -64 + 128 + 80 = 144 ft. (c) -16t^2 + 64t + 80 = 0. Dividing by -16: t^2 - 4t - 5 = 0 = (t - 5)(t + 1), so t = 5 s (positive root).
