--- Lesson 3.1: Characteristics of Polynomials (degree, leading coefficient) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Characteristics of Polynomials (degree, leading coefficient)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the degree of f(x) = 4x³ − 2x + 7?
The highest exponent in the polynomial is 3, so the degree is 3.
What is the leading coefficient of f(x) = −5x⁴ + 3x² − 1?
The term with the highest degree is −5x⁴, so the leading coefficient is −5.
What is the degree of the constant function f(x) = 8?
A nonzero constant is equivalent to 8x⁰, so the degree is 0.
f(x) = 5x³ − 2x² + x − 4. What is f(0)?
f(0) equals the constant term, which is −4.
What is the degree of a polynomial?
The degree of a polynomial is defined as the highest power (exponent) of the variable that appears with a nonzero coefficient.
What is a 'monomial'?
A monomial is a polynomial expression consisting of exactly one term, such as 5x³ or −7.
What is a 'quartic' polynomial?
A quartic polynomial has degree 4. The name comes from the Latin word for four.
What is a 'turning point' of a polynomial?
A turning point is a local maximum or minimum where the graph switches direction from rising to falling or from falling to rising.
A civil engineer models the deflection of a beam as d(x) = 0.002x⁴ − 0.08x³ + 0.5x² for 0 ≤ x ≤ 10 meters, where d is deflection in centimeters. What is the degree of this polynomial model?
The highest exponent on x is 4, so the polynomial model is degree 4.
A pharmaceutical company tracks the concentration of a drug in the bloodstream with C(t) = −0.3t³ + 4.5t² − 12t, where t is hours after administration. What is the leading coefficient, and what does its sign suggest about long-term behavior?
The leading coefficient is −0.3 (from the −0.3t³ term). Since it is negative with odd degree, the function falls to −∞ as t → ∞, meaning the model predicts the concentration eventually drops.
A city planner models daily traffic volume as V(t) = −2t⁴ + 48t³ − 288t² + 1200t for 0 ≤ t ≤ 12, where t is hours after 6 AM. What is the maximum number of turning points this model can have?
A degree-4 polynomial has at most n − 1 = 3 turning points, which could represent morning rush, midday lull, and evening rush.
An economist models a country's GDP (in billions) as G(t) = 0.05t³ − 0.8t² + 5t + 120, where t is years since 2010. What is the initial GDP at t = 0?
G(0) = 0 − 0 + 0 + 120 = 120, so the initial GDP is $120 billion.
A manufacturing process produces items whose cost is modeled by C(x) = 0.25x³ − 6x² + 50x + 200 dollars for x items. An engineer needs the leading term to predict long-range costs. What is it?
The leading term is 0.25x³ because it has the highest exponent (3). For large x, this term dominates the cost model.
A data scientist fits a polynomial to monthly sales data: S(m) = −3m⁵ + 40m⁴ − 150m³ + 200m² + 500m + 1000 for months 1 through 12. The company asks about the minimum number of real zeros this polynomial must have. What is it?
An odd-degree polynomial (degree 5) must cross the x-axis at least once, so it always has at least 1 real zero.
A landscape architect designs a garden border using the curve f(x) = x⁴ − πx² + 2. A colleague asks whether this expression qualifies as a polynomial. What is the correct answer?
Polynomials require non-negative integer exponents and real coefficients. Since π is a real number and all exponents are non-negative integers, x⁴ − πx² + 2 is a valid polynomial.
Real-World Applications
Example: How many complex zeros does x⁴ + 1 have (counting multiplicity)?
By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, a degree-4 polynomial has exactly 4 complex zeros.
Example: A data scientist fits a polynomial to monthly sales data: S(m) = −3m⁵ + 40m⁴ − 150m³ + 200m² + 500m + 1000 for months 1 through 12. The company asks about the minimum number of real zeros this polynomial must have. What is it?
An odd-degree polynomial (degree 5) must cross the x-axis at least once, so it always has at least 1 real zero.

--- Lesson 3.2: End Behavior & Graph Shapes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: End Behavior & Graph Shapes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the meaning of 'even-degree polynomial' with respect to end behavior?
An even-degree polynomial has matching end behavior on both sides: both ends rise if the leading coefficient is positive, or both fall if negative.
What is a 'quintic' polynomial?
A quintic polynomial is one of degree 5, meaning the highest power of the variable is 5.
A meteorologist models temperature over a 24-hour period as T(t) = −0.01t⁴ + 0.5t³ − 7t² + 30t + 50, where t is hours after midnight and T is in degrees Fahrenheit. Based on the leading term, what is the end behavior as t grows large?
The leading term is −0.01t⁴ (even degree, negative coefficient), so both ends fall to −∞.
A physics student launches a model rocket whose altitude profile is well-approximated by h(t) = −0.5t⁴ + 8t³ − 36t² + 48t for 0 ≤ t ≤ 6. Based on the degree, what is the maximum number of times the rocket can be at ground level (h = 0)?
A degree-4 polynomial can have at most 4 real zeros, meaning the rocket can be at ground level at most 4 times.
A marine biologist notices that a dolphin pod's population over 10 years follows a pattern: increases, decreases, then increases again. What is the minimum degree polynomial needed to model this behavior?
The pattern increase-decrease-increase requires 2 turning points. A polynomial needs degree at least n + 1 = 3 to have n = 2 turning points.
A civil engineer observes that a suspension bridge cable follows a curve with both ends rising and three turning points. What is the minimum degree polynomial that could model this cable?
Three turning points requires at least degree 4 (since at most n − 1 turning points). Both ends rising requires an even degree with positive leading coefficient, and degree 4 fits.
A theme park ride designer needs a polynomial track profile that falls on the left end, has 4 turning points, and rises on the right end. What is the minimum degree polynomial that could satisfy all three conditions?
Falls left and rises right means opposite end behaviors, requiring odd degree with positive leading coefficient. Four turning points requires at least degree 5 (since at most n − 1 turning points). Degree 5 satisfies all conditions.

--- Lesson 3.5: Intermediate Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Intermediate Value Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Intermediate Value Theorem?
The IVT states that if f is continuous on [a, b] and N is any value between f(a) and f(b), then there exists at least one c in (a, b) such that f(c) = N.
What is the 'bisection method'?
The bisection method uses IVT repeatedly: check the midpoint of an interval with a sign change, determine which half contains the zero, and repeat to get increasingly precise estimates.
What is a 'discontinuity'?
A discontinuity is a point where the function fails to be continuous -- the graph has a break, jump, hole, or vertical asymptote.
What is a 'closed interval' [a, b]?
A closed interval [a, b] includes all points from a to b, including both endpoints a and b.

--- Lesson 3.6: Graphing Polynomial Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphing Polynomial Functions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
f(x) = (x − 1)(x + 3). What is the y-intercept?
f(0) = (0 − 1)(0 + 3) = (−1)(3) = −3.
What is a 'y-intercept' of a polynomial graph?
The y-intercept is the point (0, f(0)) where the graph meets the y-axis. It equals the constant term of the polynomial.
What is a 'sign chart' for a polynomial?
A sign chart (or sign analysis) identifies the intervals between consecutive zeros and indicates whether f(x) is positive or negative on each interval, helping to sketch the graph.
What is the meaning of 'polynomial passes through a point'?
Saying a polynomial 'passes through' a point (a, b) means f(a) = b. The point lies on the graph of the function.
A landscape designer uses f(x) = −x(x − 4)(x − 8) to model the height profile of a garden hill for 0 ≤ x ≤ 8 meters. What is the y-intercept of this profile?
f(0) = −0(0 − 4)(0 − 8) = 0. The garden profile starts at ground level.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'multiplicity affects graph behavior' mean?
At a zero with even multiplicity, the graph touches and bounces off the x-axis. At a zero with odd multiplicity, the graph crosses through.
Example: An architect graphs f(x) = (x − 1)(x − 3)(x − 5)(x − 7) to design an archway with multiple curves. Between consecutive zeros, the graph stays entirely above or below the x-axis. The sign of f(x) on the interval (3, 5) is:
Test x = 4: f(4) = (3)(1)(−1)(−3) = 9 > 0. The function is positive on (3, 5).
Example: A data analyst plots quarterly sales data and fits it with f(x) = −2x³ + 12x² − 18x for 0 ≤ x ≤ 5. To sketch the graph, she first finds the zeros. What are they?
Factor: −2x(x² − 6x + 9) = −2x(x − 3)². The zeros are x = 0 (mult 1) and x = 3 (mult 2).

--- Lesson 3.7: Rational Root Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Rational Root Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between the Rational Root Theorem and irrational zeros?
The Rational Root Theorem only generates rational candidates. Irrational zeros (such as √2 or √3) will not appear on the list and must be found by other methods.
What is the 'upper bound' rule in the context of testing zeros?
The upper bound theorem states that if you perform synthetic division with a positive c and all resulting coefficients are non-negative, then no real zero is greater than c.
An engineer models the load capacity of a beam as L(x) = 2x³ − 3x² − 11x + 6. After testing candidates from the Rational Root Theorem, she finds L(3) = 0. What is the reduced polynomial after dividing by (x − 3)?
Synthetic division with c = 3: [2, −3, −11, 6] gives quotient 2x² + 3x − 2 with remainder 0.
An economist models GDP growth as G(x) = 4x³ + 8x² − x − 2, where x is in years. She applies the Rational Root Theorem with p dividing −2 and q dividing 4. After testing x = 1/2, she finds G(1/2) = 4(1/8) + 8(1/4) − 1/2 − 2 = 0.5 + 2 − 0.5 − 2 = 0. What is the complete factorization?
After dividing by (x − 1/2), or equivalently factoring out (2x − 1), we get 4x³ + 8x² − x − 2 = (2x − 1)(2x² + 5x + 2) = (2x − 1)(2x + 1)(x + 2).
Real-World Applications
Example: A pharmaceutical company models drug effectiveness as E(d) = 6d³ − 11d² − 3d + 2, where d is dosage in mg. Using the Rational Root Theorem, the possible rational dosages where effectiveness is zero include p/q where p divides 2 and q divides 6. Which is an actual zero?
E(2) = 6(8) − 11(4) − 3(2) + 2 = 48 − 44 − 6 + 2 = 0. So d = 2 mg is a zero.

--- Lesson 3.8: Applications of Polynomial Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications of Polynomial Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A ball follows h(t) = −4.9t² + 20t + 1.5. What is the initial height?
The initial height is h(0) = 1.5 m (the constant term).
What is 'marginal cost'?
Marginal cost is the derivative C'(x), representing the additional cost to produce one more unit at production level x.
A civil engineer designs a road curve following y(x) = 0.001x³ − 0.06x² + x for 0 ≤ x ≤ 40 meters. What is the road height at x = 0?
y(0) = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0 meters.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'average cost' mean?
Average cost per unit is the total cost divided by the number of units: C̄(x) = C(x)/x.
Example: A pharmaceutical company finds that the effectiveness of a drug follows E(d) = −d³ + 12d² − 36d for dosage d mg. To find the dosage that produces zero effectiveness, they need to solve E(d) = 0. Factoring gives:
E(d) = −d(d² − 12d + 36) = −d(d − 6)². Zero effectiveness at d = 0 and d = 6.
Example: A package delivery company's daily cost is C(x) = 0.01x³ − 0.6x² + 15x + 200 for x packages. The cost of delivering 0 packages is $200. What does this $200 represent?
C(0) = $200 is the fixed cost, representing expenses the company incurs regardless of how many packages are delivered.

  Q8. The leading term of V(x) = x(20 − 2x)(16 − 2x) is:
    [ ] −4x³
    [ ] x³
    [*] 4x³
    [ ] 20x
    Explanation: Multiply leading terms: x · (−2x) · (−2x) = 4x³.

  Q9. Maximum revenue from R(p) = 100p − 2p² is:
    [ ] $2500
    [*] $1250
    [ ] $1000
    [ ] $625
    Explanation: At p = 25: R(25) = 100(25) − 2(625) = 2500 − 1250 = $1250.

  Q10. A population model P(t) = −t³ + 12t² + 100t + 500 for 0 ≤ t ≤ 10. At t = 0, P =
    [ ] 100
    [*] 500
    [ ] 0
    [ ] 612
    Explanation: P(0) = 500 (the initial population).

  Q11. What does 'practical domain' mean for a polynomial model?
    [ ] The set of all real numbers
    [*] The set of input values that make physical sense in the real-world context
    [ ] The interval where the polynomial is positive
    [ ] The range of the polynomial
    Explanation: The practical domain restricts inputs to values that are meaningful in context (e.g., non-negative lengths, non-negative time, production quantities).

  Q12. What does 'fixed cost' mean in a cost polynomial C(x)?
    [ ] The maximum cost, particularly regarding rent under equilibrium conditions
    [ ] The cost per unit produced in accordance with classical theory
    [ ] The leading coefficient as typically measured in practice
    [*] The cost that stays the same regardless of production quantity, equal to C(0)
    Explanation: Fixed cost is the constant term C(0), representing costs incurred even when no units are produced (rent, insurance, etc.).

  Q13. What does 'revenue function' mean?
    [ ] R(x) = the number of units produced
    [ ] R(x) = the profit per unit
    [*] R(x) = price per unit times number of units sold
    [ ] R(x) = total cost minus profit
    Explanation: Revenue is the total income from sales: R = (price per unit) × (number of units sold).

  Q14. What does 'break-even point' mean?
    [ ] The point where the function is undefined
    [ ] The production level where revenue is maximum
    [*] The production level where revenue equals cost (profit = 0)
    [ ] The production level where cost is zero
    Explanation: The break-even point is where P(x) = R(x) − C(x) = 0, meaning revenue exactly equals cost with zero profit.

  Q15. What does 'optimization' mean in polynomial modeling?
    [ ] Making the polynomial simpler
    [ ] Increasing the degree of the polynomial
    [ ] Finding all zeros of the polynomial
    [*] Finding the input value that maximizes or minimizes the output of a model
    Explanation: Optimization involves finding the input that produces the maximum or minimum output, such as maximizing profit or minimizing cost.

  Q16. What does 'overfitting' mean in polynomial regression?
    [*] A high-degree polynomial fits the data perfectly but captures noise rather than the true trend
    [ ] The polynomial has no real zeros
    [ ] The polynomial crosses the x-axis too many times
    [ ] The polynomial has too few terms to model the data
    Explanation: Overfitting occurs when a polynomial model has too high a degree, capturing random noise in data rather than the underlying pattern, leading to poor predictions.

  Q17. What is 'marginal cost'?
    [ ] The cost at zero production
    [ ] The fixed cost of production
    [*] The approximate cost of producing one additional unit, represented by C'(x)
    [ ] The total cost divided by the number of units
    Explanation: Marginal cost is the derivative C'(x), representing the additional cost to produce one more unit at production level x.

  Q18. What does 'average cost' mean?
    [ ] The median value of the cost function
    [*] C̄(x) = C(x)/x, the total cost divided by the number of units produced
    [ ] C(x) evaluated at x = 0
    [ ] The cost at the average production level
    Explanation: Average cost per unit is the total cost divided by the number of units: C̄(x) = C(x)/x.

  Q19. What does 'profit function' represent?
    [ ] P(x) = R(x) + C(x), the sum of revenue and cost
    [ ] P(x) = C(x)/R(x), the cost-to-revenue ratio
    [*] P(x) = R(x) − C(x), the difference between revenue and cost
    [ ] P(x) = R(x) · C(x), the product of revenue and cost
    Explanation: Profit equals revenue minus cost: P(x) = R(x) − C(x). Positive profit means the company is making money.

  Q20. What does 'polynomial regression' mean?
    [ ] Reducing the degree of a polynomial
    [ ] Dividing one polynomial by another
    [*] Fitting a polynomial function to a set of data points to model the relationship
    [ ] Finding the zeros of a polynomial
    Explanation: Polynomial regression uses data to find the best-fitting polynomial of a given degree, minimizing the difference between predicted and actual values.

  Q21. A manufacturer makes cylindrical cans with volume 500 cm³. If the radius is r cm, the surface area is S(r) = 2πr² + 1000/r. This can be rewritten as S(r) = (2πr³ + 1000)/r. What degree polynomial is in the numerator?
    [ ] 1
    [*] 3
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 4
    Explanation: The numerator is 2πr³ + 1000, which is a degree-3 polynomial in r.

  Q22. A farmer has 120 meters of fencing to enclose a rectangular area along a river (no fencing needed on the river side). If the width is w meters, the area A(w) = w(120 − 2w). The maximum area is:
    [ ] 1200 m²
    [ ] 3600 m²
    [ ] 900 m²
    [*] 1800 m²
    Explanation: A(w) = 120w − 2w². Vertex: w = −120/(2·(−2)) = 30. A(30) = 120(30) − 2(900) = 3600 − 1800 = 1800 m².

  Q23. A tech company models revenue as R(x) = −0.5x³ + 15x² + 200x (in thousands), where x is months after launch. At what month does the revenue equal the initial rate? R(0) = 0 and R(1) = −0.5 + 15 + 200 = 214.5. R(10) = −500 + 1500 + 2000 = 3000. What degree polynomial is R(x)?
    [*] 3
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 4
    Explanation: The highest power is x³, so R(x) is a degree-3 (cubic) polynomial.

  Q24. A civil engineer designs a road curve following y(x) = 0.001x³ − 0.06x² + x for 0 ≤ x ≤ 40 meters. What is the road height at x = 0?
    [ ] 0.06 meters
    [ ] 1 meter
    [*] 0 meters
    [ ] 0.001 meters
    Explanation: y(0) = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0 meters.

  Q25. A pharmaceutical company finds that the effectiveness of a drug follows E(d) = −d³ + 12d² − 36d for dosage d mg. To find the dosage that produces zero effectiveness, they need to solve E(d) = 0. Factoring gives:
    [*] −d(d − 6)²
    [ ] −d(d² − 36)
    [ ] −d(d − 12)(d − 3)
    [ ] −d(d + 6)²
    Explanation: E(d) = −d(d² − 12d + 36) = −d(d − 6)². Zero effectiveness at d = 0 and d = 6.

  Q26. A package delivery company's daily cost is C(x) = 0.01x³ − 0.6x² + 15x + 200 for x packages. The cost of delivering 0 packages is $200. What does this $200 represent?
    [ ] Variable cost in most
    [ ] Profit, particularly
    [*] Fixed cost (rent, insurance, etc.)
    [ ] Revenue, particularly
    Explanation: C(0) = $200 is the fixed cost, representing expenses the company incurs regardless of how many packages are delivered.

  Q27. A toy company's profit function is P(x) = −x² + 40x − 100. The break-even points (where profit = 0) are approximately x ≈ 2.68 and x ≈ 37.32. The company is profitable when:
    [*] 2.68 < x < 37.32
    [ ] x = 20 only
    [ ] x < 2.68 or x > 37.32
    [ ] For all x > 0
    Explanation: Since P(x) opens downward (negative leading coefficient), the profit is positive between the two break-even points: 2.68 < x < 37.32.

  Q28. A swimming pool is being filled with water. The volume of water at time t hours is V(t) = 2t³ − 15t² + 36t cubic meters for 0 ≤ t ≤ 4. The pool starts empty. When does V(t) first equal 23 cubic meters? V(1) = 23, V(2) = 16 − 60 + 72 = 28. So V(1) = 2 − 15 + 36 = 23. The answer is:
    [ ] t = 3 hours
    [*] t = 1 hour
    [ ] t = 2 hours
    [ ] t = 0.5 hours
    Explanation: V(1) = 2(1) − 15(1) + 36(1) = 2 − 15 + 36 = 23. The pool first has 23 m³ at t = 1 hour.

  Q29. A projectile is launched vertically with h(t) = −16t² + 80t feet. The maximum height and the time to reach it are:
    [ ] 64 ft at t = 4 s
    [ ] 160 ft at t = 2 s
    [ ] 80 ft at t = 5 s
    [*] 100 ft at t = 2.5 s
    Explanation: t = −80/(2·(−16)) = 2.5 s. h(2.5) = −16(6.25) + 80(2.5) = −100 + 200 = 100 ft.

  Q30. An environmental scientist uses a cubic model P(t) = 0.1t³ − 3t² + 25t + 100 to predict the population of an endangered species over 20 years. At t = 20, P(20) = 0.1(8000) − 3(400) + 25(20) + 100 = 800 − 1200 + 500 + 100 = 200. Compared to the initial population, the species count has:
    [ ] Decreased from 100 to 50
    [*] Doubled from 100 to 200
    [ ] Stayed the same at 100
    [ ] Tripled from 100 to 300
    Explanation: P(0) = 100 (initial). P(20) = 200. The population doubled from 100 to 200 over 20 years.

  Q1. A firm's cost is C(x) = 0.5x² + 10x + 100. The fixed cost is:
    [ ] 0.5
    [ ] 10
    [ ] 110.5
    [*] 100
    Explanation: C(0) = 100, which represents the fixed cost.

  Q2. Revenue R(x) = 50x − 0.5x². Profit P(x) = R(x) − C(x) where C(x) = 0.5x² + 10x + 100. Profit function is:
    [*] −x² + 40x − 100
    [ ] −x² + 60x − 100
    [ ] 50x − x² − 10x − 100
    [ ] 40x − 100
    Explanation: P = (50x − 0.5x²) − (0.5x² + 10x + 100) = −x² + 40x − 100.

  Q3. Maximum profit from P(x) = −x² + 40x − 100 occurs at x =
    [ ] 40
    [ ] 100
    [*] 20
    [ ] 10
    Explanation: x = −40/(2·(−1)) = 20 units.

  Q4. The maximum profit value P(20) =
    [ ] $200
    [ ] $500
    [ ] $400
    [*] $300
    Explanation: P(20) = −400 + 800 − 100 = $300.

  Q5. A car's position is s(t) = 4t² + 2t meters. Distance at t = 3 seconds is:
    [ ] 36 m
    [*] 42 m
    [ ] 12 m
    [ ] 18 m
    Explanation: s(3) = 4(9) + 2(3) = 36 + 6 = 42 m.

  Q6. Velocity from s(t) = 4t² + 2t is v(t) = s'(t) =
    [ ] 4t + 2
    [*] 8t + 2
    [ ] 8t² + 2
    [ ] 2t + 4
    Explanation: Derivative: v(t) = 8t + 2.

  Q7. Average cost C̄(x) = C(x)/x = (0.5x² + 10x + 100)/x =
    [*] 0.5x + 10 + 100/x
    [ ] 0.5x + 10x
    [ ] 0.5x² + 10 + 100/x
    [ ] 50/x + 10
    Explanation: Divide each term by x: 0.5x + 10 + 100/x.

  Q8. A projectile: h(t) = −16t² + 80t. Maximum height?
    [ ] 160 ft
    [ ] 80 ft
    [*] 100 ft
    [ ] 64 ft
    Explanation: t = 80/32 = 2.5 s. h(2.5) = −16(6.25) + 200 = −100 + 200 = 100 ft.

  Q9. Marginal cost from C(x) = 0.5x² + 10x + 100 is C'(x) =
    [*] x + 10
    [ ] 0.5x + 100
    [ ] 10
    [ ] 0.5x² + 10
    Explanation: C'(x) = 2(0.5)x + 10 = x + 10.

  Q10. In physics, polynomial models are exact for:
    [ ] Circular motion
    [ ] Wave motion
    [ ] All motion
    [*] Constant acceleration
    Explanation: The kinematic equation s(t) = s₀ + v₀t + ½at² is a quadratic polynomial that is exact when acceleration is constant.

  Q11. What does 'variable cost' mean?
    [ ] The constant term in the cost function
    [ ] The maximum cost possible
    [*] The portion of total cost that changes with the level of production
    [ ] A cost that varies randomly
    Explanation: Variable cost is C(x) − C(0), the additional cost beyond fixed costs that changes as production quantity x changes.

  Q12. What does 'demand function' mean in economics?
    [ ] A function measuring the supply of goods
    [ ] The cost of producing goods
    [*] A function relating the price of a good to the quantity consumers will buy
    [ ] The profit from selling goods
    Explanation: The demand function D(p) gives the quantity of a product consumers will purchase at a given price p.

  Q13. What does 'constant acceleration' mean in kinematics?
    [ ] The velocity stays constant
    [ ] The position doesn't change
    [*] The rate of change of velocity remains the same over time
    [ ] The object moves in a circle
    Explanation: Constant acceleration means the velocity changes at a uniform rate. Under constant acceleration, position is a quadratic polynomial in time.

  Q14. What does 'elastic potential energy' mean?
    [ ] Energy due to height above the ground
    [ ] Energy from motion
    [ ] Energy stored in chemical bonds
    [*] Energy stored in a compressed or stretched spring, given by ½kx²
    Explanation: Elastic potential energy is the energy stored in a spring when it is displaced by distance x from equilibrium: E = ½kx², a quadratic polynomial in x.

  Q15. What does 'equilibrium point' mean in an economic model?
    [ ] The y-intercept of the profit function
    [ ] The maximum profit point
    [ ] The point where cost equals zero
    [*] The point where supply equals demand
    Explanation: In economics, the equilibrium point is where the supply and demand curves intersect, determining the market price and quantity.

  Q16. What is 'kinematic equation' in physics?
    [ ] An equation that describes wave behavior
    [ ] An equation for circular motion only
    [ ] Any equation involving polynomials
    [*] An equation relating position, velocity, acceleration, and time under constant acceleration
    Explanation: Kinematic equations describe motion under constant acceleration. The position equation s(t) = s₀ + v₀t + ½at² is a key example.

  Q17. What does 'economies of scale' mean?
    [ ] Revenue always exceeds cost
    [*] As production increases, the average cost per unit decreases
    [ ] The fixed cost increases with production
    [ ] Larger companies always make more profit
    Explanation: Economies of scale occur when the average cost per unit C̄(x) = C(x)/x decreases as production x increases, because fixed costs are spread over more units.

  Q18. What does 'initial velocity' mean in projectile motion?
    [ ] The maximum velocity reached
    [*] The velocity of the object at time t = 0
    [ ] The acceleration due to gravity
    [ ] The velocity when the object hits the ground
    Explanation: Initial velocity v₀ is the speed and direction of the object at the moment of launch (t = 0). In h(t) = −16t² + v₀t + h₀, v₀ is the coefficient of t.

  Q19. What does 'diminishing returns' mean in a production model?
    [ ] The company loses money on every unit
    [ ] The polynomial model has a negative constant term
    [ ] Returns are always decreasing
    [*] Beyond a certain point, each additional unit of input produces less additional output
    Explanation: Diminishing returns means the marginal benefit of increasing input decreases over time. In polynomial models, this often shows as a concave-down region.

  Q20. What does 'Hooke's Law' state?
    [ ] Force equals mass times acceleration
    [ ] Every action has an equal and opposite reaction
    [*] The force exerted by a spring is proportional to its displacement: F = −kx
    [ ] Energy is always conserved
    Explanation: Hooke's Law states F = −kx, where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement from equilibrium. The force is a linear polynomial in x.

  Q21. A startup's revenue over its first 5 years is modeled by R(t) = −2t³ + 18t² + 48t thousand dollars. The maximum revenue and when it occurs can be found using the vertex concept for cubics. At t = 0, R = 0. At t = 2, R(2) = −16 + 72 + 96 = 152. At t = 4, R(4) = −128 + 288 + 192 = 352. At t = 6, R(6) = −432 + 648 + 288 = 504. At t = 8, R(8) = −1024 + 1152 + 384 = 512.     [ ] t = 4 years
    [ ] t = 6 years
    [ ] t = 9 years
    [*] t = 8 years
    Explanation: Comparing values: R(6) = 504, R(8) = 512, R(9) = 432. Revenue peaks near t = 8 with $512 thousand.

  Q22. A physics student drops a ball from 80 meters. The height is h(t) = 80 − 4.9t². When does the ball hit the ground?
    [ ] t ≈ 16.3 seconds
    [ ] t = 80 seconds
    [ ] t = 5 seconds
    [*] t ≈ 4.04 seconds
    Explanation: Set h(t) = 0: 80 − 4.9t² = 0 → t² = 80/4.9 ≈ 16.33 → t ≈ 4.04 seconds.

    [ ] p = 100
    [ ] p = 0
    [*] p = 50
    [ ] p = 25
    Explanation: D(p) = 0 → 100 − 2p = 0 → p = 50. No one buys when the price reaches $50.

    [ ] 30 J

    Explanation: E = ½(200)(0.3²) = ½(200)(0.09) = 100(0.09) = 9 joules.

    [ ] $8
    [*] $5

    [ ] v₀ = 10 m/s, h₀ = 50 m
