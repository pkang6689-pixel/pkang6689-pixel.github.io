--- Lesson 3.1: Characteristics of Polynomials (degree, leading coefficient) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Characteristics of Polynomials (degree, leading coefficient)
Characteristics of Polynomials
A polynomial function has the form f(x) = aₙxⁿ + aₙ₋₁xⁿ⁻¹ + … + a₁x + a₀, where the exponents are non-negative integers and the coefficients are real numbers.
Key Vocabulary  • Degree: The highest exponent. It determines the overall shape and maximum number of turning points (≤ n − 1).  • Leading coefficient (aₙ): The coefficient of the highest-degree term. Its sign determines end behavior.  • Constant term (a₀): The y-intercept when x = 0.Classification by Degree  • Degree 0 → constant, Degree 1 → linear, Degree 2 → quadratic, Degree 3 → cubic, Degree 4 → quartic, Degree 5 → quintic.Number of Zeros  • A polynomial of degree n has at most n real zeros (Fundamental Theorem of Algebra: exactly n zeros counting multiplicity over the complex numbers).

--- Lesson 3.2: End Behavior & Graph Shapes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: End Behavior & Graph Shapes
End Behavior & Graph Shapes
End behavior describes what happens to f(x) as x → +∞ and x → −∞. It depends on the degree and the sign of the leading coefficient.
Rules  • Even degree, positive leading coeff: ↑ left and ↑ right (both ends rise).  • Even degree, negative leading coeff: ↓ left and ↓ right (both ends fall).  • Odd degree, positive leading coeff: ↓ left and ↑ right.  • Odd degree, negative leading coeff: ↑ left and ↓ right.Graph Shape Clues  • Degree 2 (quadratic): parabola shape.  • Degree 3 (cubic): S-shaped curve.  • Degree 4 (quartic): W- or M-shaped (up to 3 turning points).  • The number of turning points is at most n − 1.

--- Lesson 3.3: Factoring Techniques (synthetic division, long division) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Factoring Techniques (synthetic division, long division)
Factoring Techniques
Dividing polynomials is essential for finding factors and zeros of higher-degree polynomials.
Polynomial Long Division  • Works exactly like numerical long division.  • Divide the leading term of the dividend by the leading term of the divisor, multiply, subtract, bring down, and repeat.  • Result: f(x) = d(x)·q(x) + r(x), where r has lower degree than d.Synthetic Division  • A shortcut when dividing by (x − c).  • Use only the coefficients; bring down, multiply by c, add, repeat.  • The last number is the remainder (also equals f(c) by the Remainder Theorem).Remainder & Factor Theorems  • Remainder Theorem: When f(x) is divided by (x − c), the remainder is f(c).  • Factor Theorem: (x − c) is a factor of f(x) if and only if f(c) = 0.

--- Lesson 3.4: Roots, Zeros, and Multiplicity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Roots, Zeros, and Multiplicity
Roots, Zeros, and Multiplicity
The zeros (roots) of a polynomial f(x) are the x-values where f(x) = 0. They correspond to x-intercepts of the graph.
Multiplicity  • If (x − c)ᵏ is a factor and k ≥ 1, then c is a zero of multiplicity k.  • Odd multiplicity: the graph crosses the x-axis at x = c.  • Even multiplicity: the graph touches (bounces off) the x-axis at x = c.Finding Zeros  • Factor the polynomial completely.  • Set each factor equal to zero.  • Use the quadratic formula for irreducible quadratic factors.Writing a Polynomial from Zeros  • If zeros are c₁, c₂, …, cₙ, then f(x) = a(x − c₁)(x − c₂)…(x − cₙ).  • Use a given point to find the leading coefficient a.

--- Lesson 3.5: Intermediate Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Intermediate Value Theorem
Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)
The Intermediate Value Theorem states: if f is continuous on [a, b] and N is any number between f(a) and f(b), then there exists at least one c in (a, b) such that f(c) = N.
Key Points  • All polynomials are continuous everywhere, so IVT always applies to polynomials.  • Most commonly used with N = 0 to guarantee the existence of a real zero between a and b when f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs.Applying the IVT  • Evaluate f(a) and f(b).  • If f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs, the IVT guarantees at least one zero in (a, b).  • The theorem does NOT tell you the exact value of the zero—only that it exists.Limitations  • IVT requires continuity; it does not apply to functions with discontinuities on [a, b].  • A sign change is sufficient but not necessary—a zero can exist even without a sign change (e.g., touching the axis).

  Key Concepts: Graphing Polynomial Functions
Graphing Polynomial Functions
To sketch the graph of a polynomial, combine what you know about zeros, multiplicity, end behavior, and turning points.
Step-by-Step Graphing  • Find the y-intercept: evaluate f(0).  • Find the zeros: factor and solve f(x) = 0. --- Lesson 3.7: Rational Root Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Rational Root Theorem
Rational Root Theorem
The Rational Root Theorem helps find possible rational zeros of a polynomial with integer coefficients.
Statement  • If f(x) = aₙxⁿ + … + a₀ has integer coefficients, then any rational zero p/q (in lowest terms) satisfies:  • p divides a₀ (the constant term) and q divides aₙ (the leading coefficient).Using the Theorem  • List all factors of the constant term (±).  • List all factors of the leading coefficient (±).  • Form all possible fractions p/q.  • Test each candidate using direct substitution or synthetic division.  • Once a zero is found, factor it out and repeat with the reduced polynomial.Limitations  • Only identifies possible rational zeros; irrational and complex zeros are not found this way.  • Not all candidates will be actual zeros—you must test them.

--- Lesson 3.9: Case Studies in Economics & Physics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Economics & Physics
Case Studies in Economics & Physics
Polynomials appear naturally in both economics and physics. This lesson explores real-world examples that connect polynomial theory to practice.
Economics Applications  • Cost functions: C(x) = aₙxⁿ + … + a₁x + a₀ models total cost of producing x units.  • Demand & Revenue: When price depends linearly on quantity, revenue is quadratic. Higher-order demand models yield cubic or quartic revenue.  • Marginal cost/revenue: The derivative of cost or revenue, foreshadowing calculus.Physics Applications  • Kinematics: Position as a polynomial in time: s(t) = s₀ + v₀t + ½at² (quadratic for constant acceleration).  • Work & Energy: Work done by a variable force can involve polynomial integration.  • Optics: Polynomial approximations (e.g., Snell's law corrections) appear in lens design.Strategy  • Identify the polynomial model, determine the domain, and use zeros, vertices, or technology to answer the question.

    Explanation: x = (40 ± √(1600−400))/2 = (40 ± √1200)/2 ≈ 2.68, 37.32.

  Q7. A car's position: s(t) = 4t² + 2t (meters). Distance at t = 3?
    [ ] 12
    [ ] 18
    [*] 42
    [ ] 36
    Explanation: s(3) = 4(9) + 2(3) = 36 + 6 = 42 m.

  Q8. Velocity from s(t) = 4t² + 2t is v(t) = s'(t) =
    [ ] 4t + 2
    [*] 8t + 2
    [ ] 8t² + 2
    [ ] 2t + 4
    Explanation: Derivative: 8t + 2.

  Q9. At t = 0, the initial velocity is:
    [ ] 0 m/s
    [ ] 8 m/s
    [*] 2 m/s
    [ ] 4 m/s
    Explanation: v(0) = 8(0) + 2 = 2 m/s.

  Q10. Average cost C̄(x) = C(x)/x = (0.5x² + 10x + 100)/x =
    [ ] 0.5x² + 10 + 100/x
    [*] 0.5x + 10 + 100/x
    [ ] 0.5x + 10x
    [ ] 50/x + 10
    Explanation: Divide each term by x.

  Q11. As x → ∞, C̄(x) ≈
    [ ] 100/x
    [ ] 10
    [*] 0.5x + 10 (grows)
    [ ] 0
    Explanation: The 100/x vanishes; the dominant terms give 0.5x + 10.

  Q12. A projectile: h(t) = −16t² + 80t. Max height?
    [ ] 80 ft
    [*] 100 ft
    [ ] 160 ft
    [ ] 64 ft
    Explanation: t = 80/32 = 2.5 s. h(2.5) = −16(6.25) + 200 = −100 + 200 = 100 ft.

  Q13. A demand model: D(x) = 200 − 0.1x². At x = 10:
    [ ] 100
    [ ] 190
    [*] 190
    [ ] 200
    Explanation: D(10) = 200 − 0.1(100) = 200 − 10 = 190.

  Q14. Revenue R = x·D(x) = 200x − 0.1x³. This is a __ degree polynomial.
    [ ] 2
    [*] 3
    [ ] 4
    [ ] 1
    Explanation: Highest power is x³ → degree 3.

  Q15. For R = 200x − 0.1x³, R'(x) = 200 − 0.3x². Set to 0: x ≈
    [ ] 10
    [*] 25.8
    [ ] 100
    [ ] 200
    Explanation: 0.3x² = 200 → x² = 666.7 → x ≈ 25.8.

  Q16. Marginal cost from C(x) = 0.5x² + 10x + 100 is C'(x) =
    [ ] 0.5x + 100
    [*] x + 10
    [ ] 0.5x² + 10
    [ ] 10
    Explanation: C'(x) = x + 10.

  Q17. At x = 30, marginal cost =
    [ ] 30
    [*] 40
    [ ] 50
    [ ] 10
    Explanation: C'(30) = 30 + 10 = 40.

  Q18. In physics, polynomial models are exact for:
    [ ] All motion
    [*] Constant acceleration
    [ ] Circular motion
    [ ] Wave motion
    Explanation: s(t) = s₀ + v₀t + ½at² is exact when acceleration is constant.

  Q19. A spring force F(x) = −kx. Work W = ∫F dx from 0 to d gives:
    [ ] −kd
    [ ] kd
    [*] −kd²/2 (or kd²/2 stored energy)
    [ ] kd²
    Explanation: W = ½kd² (stored elastic potential energy).

  Q20. A polynomial model for stock price over 4 quarters requires at most degree:
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 2
    [*] 3
    [ ] 4
    Explanation: 4 data points → at most degree 3 for exact fit.
