--- Lesson 3.1: Electrons, Protons, and Neutrons (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electrons, Protons, and Neutrons
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the charge of a neutron?
Neutrons carry zero electric charge. Their name literally comes from 'neutral.' They add mass to the nucleus without affecting charge.
What is the charge of an electron?
Each electron carries a charge of −1. This negative charge balances the positive charge of protons in a neutral atom.
If an atom has 11 protons and 12 neutrons, what is its mass number?
Mass number = protons + neutrons = 11 + 12 = 23. This atom is sodium-23.
What is the definition of atomic number?
Atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in an atom's nucleus. It uniquely identifies the element and determines its place on the periodic table.
What is a nucleon?
Nucleon is the collective term for particles residing in the nucleus — protons and neutrons. The mass number counts the total nucleons.
What is the correct definition of a proton?
A proton carries a +1 charge and resides in the nucleus. Its count (atomic number) determines which element the atom is.
What is the term for particles that orbit the nucleus and determine chemical bonding?
Electrons occupy energy levels around the nucleus and are responsible for chemical bonding and reactivity. Their arrangement dictates how atoms interact.
What is the definition of an isotope?
Isotopes share the same atomic number (same element) but differ in neutron count, giving them different mass numbers.
A sodium atom loses one electron. What is the charge of the resulting ion?
Sodium has 11 protons. Losing one electron leaves 10 electrons, so the net charge is 11(+) − 10(−) = +1. It forms the Na⁺ cation.
Two atoms both have 6 protons but one has 6 neutrons and the other has 8. What is the relationship between them?
Both have 6 protons, so both are carbon. Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons; carbon-14 has 8 neutrons. They are isotopes — same element, different mass numbers.
In a nuclear reactor, uranium-235 is used as fuel. If uranium has 92 protons, what is the neutron count of U-235?
Neutrons = 235 − 92 = 143. Heavy elements like uranium have many more neutrons than protons to help stabilize the large nucleus.

--- Lesson 3.2: Atomic Structure (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Atomic Structure
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the dense, central part of an atom called?
The nucleus is the tiny, dense core of an atom where protons and neutrons are packed together. It contains nearly all the atom's mass.
What is the definition of the nucleus?
The nucleus is the small, dense center of an atom that houses all the protons and neutrons (nucleons). It has a positive charge.
What is an energy level in atomic structure?
Energy levels (shells) are fixed regions numbered n = 1, 2, 3… where electrons with quantized energies are found.
What is atomic radius?
Atomic radius measures the size of an atom from the center of its nucleus to the boundary of the outermost electron cloud.
What is an ion?
An ion forms when an atom gains electrons (anion, negative charge) or loses electrons (cation, positive charge). The number of protons stays the same.
What is the correct definition of an atom?
An atom is the fundamental unit of an element. It consists of a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons and keeps the element's identity.
What is a cation?
A cation has fewer electrons than protons, resulting in a net positive charge. Metals typically form cations by losing valence electrons.
What is an anion?
An anion has more electrons than protons, giving a net negative charge. Nonmetals typically form anions by gaining electrons to fill their valence shell.

--- Lesson 3.3: Quantum Mechanical Model & Orbitals (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Quantum Mechanical Model & Orbitals
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an orbital?
An orbital is a mathematical probability map showing the region in space where there is about a 90% chance of finding an electron.
What is the shape of an s orbital?
The s orbital is spherical, centered on the nucleus. Each energy level contains exactly one s orbital that holds up to 2 electrons.
What is the shape of a p orbital?
P orbitals have a dumbbell shape with two lobes on opposite sides of the nucleus. There are three p orbitals per sublevel (px, py, pz).
What is the Aufbau principle?
The Aufbau principle (German for 'building up') states that electrons occupy the lowest available energy orbitals before filling higher ones.
What is the definition of a quantum number?
Four quantum numbers (n, l, mₗ, mₛ) completely describe the state of an electron: its energy level, orbital shape, spatial orientation, and spin direction.
What is a sublevel (subshell)?
Each energy level n contains sublevels: n = 1 has s; n = 2 has s, p; n = 3 has s, p, d; n = 4 has s, p, d, f.

--- Lesson 3.4: Electromagnetic Spectrum & Atomic Emission Spectra (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electromagnetic Spectrum & Atomic Emission Spectra
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is wavelength (λ)?
Wavelength is the peak-to-peak distance of a wave, measured in meters or nanometers. It determines the type and color of electromagnetic radiation.
What is frequency (ν)?
Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second. Higher frequency means more wave cycles pass each second.
What is the electromagnetic spectrum?
The EM spectrum spans from low-energy radio waves through microwaves, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, to high-energy gamma rays.
What is a photon?
A photon is the smallest discrete unit of light energy. It has no mass but carries energy proportional to its frequency: E = hν.
What is an emission spectrum?
Each element's electrons have unique energy level spacings, so they emit distinct sets of wavelengths — producing a line spectrum that can identify the element.
What is an absorption spectrum?
When white light passes through a gas, atoms absorb specific wavelengths (electrons jump up), leaving dark lines — the inverse of an emission spectrum.
What is the definition of wavelength?
Wavelength (λ) is the spatial period of a wave — the distance from one crest to the next. It is measured in meters (m) or nanometers (nm).
What is a continuous spectrum?
A continuous spectrum shows an unbroken band of all colors, like a rainbow. White light from an incandescent bulb produces a continuous spectrum.
What is a line spectrum?
A line spectrum (emission spectrum) shows discrete bright lines at specific wavelengths corresponding to electron transitions in an element.
A radio station broadcasts at 101.1 MHz. What is its wavelength?
λ = c/ν = (3.0 × 10⁸ m/s) / (101.1 × 10⁶ Hz) ≈ 2.97 m. Radio waves have long wavelengths measured in meters.
A lab uses UV light at 254 nm wavelength to sterilize equipment. What is the frequency of this light?
ν = c/λ = (3.0 × 10⁸ m/s) / (254 × 10⁻⁹ m) ≈ 1.18 × 10¹⁵ Hz. UV light has very high frequency.
A forensic chemist uses emission spectra to identify an unknown element found at a crime scene. The lab observes bright lines at wavelengths matching no known single element. What is the best explanation?
When multiple elements are present, their emission lines overlap. The spectrum can be decomposed to identify each contributing element — a powerful analytical technique.
Real-World Applications
Example: Airport security uses X-rays to scan luggage. Compared to visible light, X-rays have:
X-rays have wavelengths of about 0.01–10 nm, far shorter than visible light (400–700 nm). Shorter wavelength means higher frequency and energy.
Important Concepts
As wavelength increases, frequency:
Wavelength and frequency are inversely related: c = λν. Since the speed of light is constant, a longer wavelength means a lower frequency.
The relationship between photon energy and frequency is:
Planck's equation E = hν shows energy is directly proportional to frequency. Higher frequency photons carry more energy.
What is frequency (ν)
Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second. Higher frequency means more wave cycles pass each second.
What unit is used to measure frequency
Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), equal to cycles per second (s⁻¹).
A lab uses UV light at 254 nm wavelength to sterilize equipment. What is the frequency of this light
ν = c/λ = (3.0 × 10⁸ m/s) / (254 × 10⁻⁹ m) ≈ 1.18 × 10¹⁵ Hz. UV light has very high frequency.
Calculate the energy of a photon with frequency 5.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz.
E = hν = (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)(5.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz) = 3.31 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.

--- Lesson 3.5: Radioactivity & Stability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Radioactivity & Stability
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is radioactivity?
Radioactivity is the process by which unstable atomic nuclei release energy in the form of particles or electromagnetic radiation to reach a more stable state.
What is alpha decay?
Alpha decay emits an alpha particle (⁴₂He), reducing the mass number by 4 and the atomic number by 2, transforming the element into a new one.
What is the definition of radioactive decay?
Radioactive decay is a nuclear process (not chemical) where the nucleus emits particles or energy to move toward a more stable configuration.
What is the band of stability?
The band of stability plots neutrons vs. protons. Nuclei within this belt are stable; those outside decay to reach it via alpha, beta, or other decay modes.
What is nuclear stability?
Nuclear stability exists when the attractive strong nuclear force between nucleons balances or exceeds the electromagnetic repulsion between protons.
What is an alpha particle?
An alpha particle is identical to a helium-4 nucleus: 2 protons and 2 neutrons bound together, carrying a +2 charge.
What is a beta particle?
In beta-minus decay, a neutron converts to a proton inside the nucleus, ejecting an electron (beta particle) and an antineutrino.
What is half-life?
Half-life is a fixed time interval during which exactly half the radioactive atoms in a sample undergo decay. It is a characteristic constant for each isotope.
Uranium-238 undergoes alpha decay. What is the daughter nucleus?
Alpha decay: U-238 → Th-234 + He-4. Atomic number drops from 92 to 90 (thorium), mass drops from 238 to 234.
Radon-222 is a radioactive gas that seeps into homes from the ground. It undergoes alpha decay. What is the daughter product?
Rn-222 → Po-218 + He-4. Atomic number drops from 86 to 84 (polonium), mass drops from 222 to 218.

--- Lesson 3.6: Isotopes & Radioisotopes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Isotopes & Radioisotopes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the mass number of an isotope?
Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons. It is always a whole number, unlike atomic mass which is a weighted average.
What is the definition of an isotope?
Isotopes share the same atomic number (same element) but differ in neutron count and therefore mass number. Not all isotopes are radioactive — most are stable.
What is atomic mass (in the context of isotopes)?
Atomic mass is calculated as: Σ(isotope mass × fractional abundance). It is measured in atomic mass units (amu) and appears as a decimal on the periodic table.
What is the term for isotope notation written as Element-mass number (e.g., Carbon-14)?
Hyphen notation (e.g., Uranium-235) uses the element name followed by the mass number. Nuclear notation shows the mass number as a superscript and atomic number as a subscript.
What is the unit used for atomic mass?
One amu is defined as 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom. It is the standard unit for expressing masses on the atomic scale.
A chemist needs to calculate the atomic mass of an element with two isotopes: X-10 (20% abundance) and X-11 (80% abundance). What is the atomic mass?
Weighted average: (0.20 × 10) + (0.80 × 11) = 2.0 + 8.8 = 10.8 amu. The heavier isotope dominates because it is more abundant.
A mass spectrometer analysis shows two peaks for neon: one at mass 20 (90.5%) and one at mass 22 (9.5%). What is the approximate atomic mass of neon?
(0.905 × 20) + (0.095 × 22) = 18.10 + 2.09 = 20.19 amu. This is very close to the periodic table value of 20.18 for neon.
Scientists discover a new mineral containing element Z. Mass spectrometry shows Z-50 at 60% and Z-52 at 40%. What is the approximate atomic mass of element Z?
(0.60 × 50) + (0.40 × 52) = 30 + 20.8 = 50.8 amu. The result sits between the two isotope masses, weighted toward the more abundant Z-50.

Atomic mass is a weighted average: each isotope's mass is multiplied by its percent abundance, then summed. More abundant isotopes contribute more to the average.

In a weighted average, the most abundant isotope has the largest weight factor. A-24 at 78.99% dominates the calculation, pulling the average close to 24.

--- Lesson 3.7: Element Synthesis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Element Synthesis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of nuclear fusion?
Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei (e.g., H + H → He) and releases energy because the product has lower mass per nucleon. This powers all main-sequence stars.
What is a particle accelerator?
Particle accelerators (like cyclotrons and linear accelerators) use electric and magnetic fields to accelerate ions to enormous speeds before colliding them with target nuclei.
What is nucleosynthesis?
Nucleosynthesis encompasses Big Bang nucleosynthesis (light elements), stellar nucleosynthesis (up to iron), and supernova/merger nucleosynthesis (elements heavier than iron).
What is the definition of nuclear fission?
Fission splits heavy nuclei (like U-235) into smaller fragments plus neutrons and energy. It is the process used in nuclear power plants and atomic weapons.
What is the s-process in nucleosynthesis?
The s-process (slow process) occurs in AGB stars. Nuclei slowly capture neutrons one at a time, with time to beta-decay between captures, building elements up to bismuth.
What is the r-process in nucleosynthesis?
The r-process captures many neutrons in seconds during extreme events, forming highly neutron-rich nuclei that then beta-decay to create stable heavy elements like gold and platinum.
Real-World Applications
Example: Plutonium-239 is produced in nuclear reactors when U-238 captures a neutron and undergoes two beta decays. This is an example of:
U-238 + n → U-239 → Np-239 (beta) → Pu-239 (beta). This converts a natural element into a synthetic transuranium element through neutron capture and beta decays.

--- Lesson 3.8: Atomic Theory (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Atomic Theory
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Dalton's atomic theory?
Dalton's key postulates: (1) all matter is made of atoms, (2) atoms of the same element are identical, (3) atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios, (4) atoms are not created or destroyed in reactions.
What is the plum pudding model?
The plum pudding model envisioned the atom as a sphere of diffuse positive charge with negative electrons scattered throughout — like raisins (plums) in a pudding.
What is the nuclear model of the atom?
Rutherford (1911) proposed that nearly all atomic mass is concentrated in a tiny nucleus, with electrons orbiting at relatively large distances — mostly empty space.
What is the Bohr model?
Bohr's model successfully explained hydrogen's line spectrum by quantizing electron orbits. Electrons can only exist at certain distances from the nucleus, each with a fixed energy.
What is the quantum mechanical model?
Developed by Schrödinger and Heisenberg in the 1920s, this model uses wave functions to calculate the probability of finding an electron in a given region (orbital).
What is the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle?
Heisenberg showed that the more precisely you know an electron's position, the less precisely you can know its momentum, and vice versa. This is a fundamental limit, not a measurement error.

    Explanation: Developed by Schrödinger and Heisenberg in the 1920s, this model uses wave functions to calculate the probability of finding an electron in a given region (orbital).
