--- Lesson 12.1: Nuclear Fusion and Fission (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Nuclear fission is the process of:
    [*] Splitting a heavy nucleus into smaller nuclei
    [ ] Combining light nuclei into a heavier one
    [ ] Removing electrons
    [ ] Adding neutrons
    Explanation: Nuclear fission splits a heavy nucleus (like uranium-235) into lighter nuclei, releasing neutrons and energy. A chain reaction occurs when released neutrons trigger more fissions. The answer is 'Splitting a heavy nucleus into smaller nuclei'. The other options — 'Combining light nuclei into a heavier one', 'Removing electrons', and 'Adding neutrons' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q2. Nuclear fusion is the process of:
    [ ] Breaking chemical bonds
    [ ] Splitting heavy nuclei
    [ ] Ionizing atoms
    [*] Combining light nuclei into a heavier nucleus
    Explanation: Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei (like hydrogen) into heavier ones, releasing energy. It requires extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees) to overcome electrostatic repulsion. The answer is 'Combining light nuclei into a heavier nucleus'. The other options — 'Ionizing atoms', 'Breaking chemical bonds', and 'Splitting heavy nuclei' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. The sun produces energy through:
    [ ] Radioactive decay
    [ ] Chemical combustion
    [ ] Nuclear fission
    [*] Nuclear fusion
    Explanation: Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei into heavier ones, releasing enormous energy. It requires extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees). The answer is 'Nuclear fusion'. The other options — 'Nuclear fission', 'Radioactive decay', and 'Chemical combustion' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q4. Nuclear power plants use:
    [ ] Combustion of coal
    [ ] Fusion of hydrogen
    [ ] Solar energy
    [*] Fission of uranium-235
    Explanation: Nuclear power generates electricity via fission. Main concerns include radioactive waste disposal (remains hazardous for thousands of years), potential meltdowns, and weapons proliferation. The answer is 'Fission of uranium-235'. The other options — 'Fusion of hydrogen', 'Solar energy', and 'Combustion of coal' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q5. Fusion requires extremely high:
    [ ] Number of atoms
    [ ] Volumes
    [*] Temperatures (millions of degrees)
    [ ] Pressures only
    Explanation: Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei (like hydrogen) into heavier ones, releasing energy. It requires extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees) to overcome electrostatic repulsion. The answer is 'Temperatures (millions of degrees)'. The other options — 'Number of atoms', 'Pressures only', and 'Volumes' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q6. The mass defect in nuclear reactions represents:
    [ ] Lost neutrons
    [ ] Destroyed matter
    [ ] Gained protons
    [*] Mass converted to energy (E = mc²)
    Explanation: Mass Defect — The difference between the mass of the nucleus and the sum of its individual protons and neutrons — converted to energy via E = mc². The other options — 'Destroyed matter', 'Lost neutrons', and 'Gained protons' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q7. Which process releases more energy per unit mass?
    [ ] Fission
    [*] Fusion
    [ ] Chemical combustion
    [ ] They are equal
    Explanation: Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei into heavier ones, releasing enormous energy. It requires extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees). The answer is 'Fusion'. The other options — 'Fission', 'Chemical combustion', and 'They are equal' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q8. Nuclear fission releases energy because:
    [ ] Heat from chemical bonds is released under equilibrium conditions
    [*] Heavy nuclei split into lighter nuclei with combined lower binding energy (mass converted to energy)
    [ ] Neutrons gain kinetic energy only based on the prevailing evidence
    [ ] Electrons are released, particularly regarding than as determined by standard methodology
    Explanation: In fission, a heavy nucleus (U-235) splits; the products have less mass than the original — this mass deficit is converted to energy via E=mc².

  Q9. Nuclear fusion releases energy because:
    [ ] Atoms evaporate, particularly regarding less in accordance with classical theory
    [ ] Electrons are released, particularly regarding nuclear in most standard conditions
    [*] Light nuclei combine to form heavier nucleus with higher binding energy per nucleon (mass converted to energy)
    [ ] Chemical bonds form, particularly regarding nuclei based on established principles
    Explanation: Fusion of light nuclei (H isotopes → He) produces a product with less mass than reactants — mass defect converted to enormous energy.

  Q10. Which produces more energy per unit mass of fuel?
    [ ] Radioactive decay
    [*] Fusion
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Fission
    Explanation: Fusion (e.g., D+T → He) releases approximately 3–4× more energy per unit mass than fission and millions of times more than chemical combustion.

  Q11. Nuclear fission is used in:
    [ ] Cold fusion reactors
    [ ] The sun and stars
    [ ] Chemical batteries
    [*] Nuclear power plants and atomic bombs
    Explanation: Nuclear power plants use controlled fission (U-235 or Pu-239) to generate heat; atomic bombs use uncontrolled fission.

  Q12. Nuclear fusion is the energy source of:
    [*] The sun and all stars (hydrogen fusion into helium)
    [ ] Nuclear power plants
    [ ] Coal-burning plants
    [ ] Radioactive waste
    Explanation: Stars fuse hydrogen → helium in their cores, releasing the enormous energy that makes stars shine. This is the primary energy source of the universe.

  Q13. The equation E = mc² means:
    [ ] Temperature determines energy
    [ ] Energy and mass are never related
    [ ] Only chemical energy applies
    [*] A small amount of mass converts to an enormous amount of energy
    Explanation: E = mc² (Einstein): m = mass converted (kg), c = speed of light (3×10⁸ m/s). Even tiny masses produce enormous energy due to c².

  Q14. Critical mass refers to:
    [*] The minimum mass of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction
    [ ] The mass of the nucleus
    [ ] The total mass of reactants
    [ ] The mass number minus atomic number
    Explanation: Critical mass is the minimum amount of fissile material (e.g., U-235) needed so that enough neutrons hit other nuclei to sustain a chain reaction.

  Q15. A nuclear chain reaction is sustained when:
    [ ] The reaction is slowed by control rods
    [ ] Energy output equals energy input
    [ ] All neutrons escape the fuel
    [*] Each fission event produces enough neutrons to trigger at least one more fission
    Explanation: A self-sustaining chain reaction: each fission releases 2–3 neutrons; at least one of those must trigger another fission for the chain to continue.

  Q16. Control rods in a nuclear reactor:
    [ ] Generate neutrons
    [ ] Produce electricity directly
    [*] Absorb neutrons to slow/stop the chain reaction
    [ ] Cool the reactor
    Explanation: Control rods (e.g., boron or cadmium) absorb free neutrons. Inserting them deeper slows/stops the chain reaction; withdrawing allows it to speed up.

  Q17. The main challenge in achieving controlled fusion on Earth is:
    [*] Plasma must be heated to extreme temperatures (100 million °C) and confined
    [ ] Fusion produces too little energy
    [ ] The fuel is too expensive
    [ ] No helium is produced
    Explanation: Fusion requires extremely high temperatures (to overcome proton repulsion) and containment of plasma — currently achieved only in experimental reactors (tokamaks, inertial confinement).

  Q18. In fission of U-235 by a neutron: U-235 + n → Ba-141 + Kr-92 + 3n. The 3 released neutrons:
    [*] Can trigger 3 more fission events, creating a chain reaction
    [ ] Are electrons, particularly regarding released
    [ ] Are absorbed by the nucleus
    [ ] Stop the reaction, particularly regarding
    Explanation: The 3 released neutrons can each cause another U-235 to fission, creating an exponentially growing chain reaction if not controlled.

  Q19. Hydrogen bomb (thermonuclear bomb) uses:
    [*] Fusion triggered by a fission explosion
    [ ] Radioactive beta decay
    [ ] Only chemical explosives
    [ ] Only fission
    Explanation: A hydrogen bomb: a fission bomb creates the extreme heat/pressure needed to ignite fusion of hydrogen isotopes (D and T), releasing far more energy than fission alone.

  Q20. Binding energy is:
    [*] The energy that holds the nucleus together (equals the energy released when nucleons combine)
    [ ] The energy needed to break electron bonds based on established principles
    [ ] The kinetic energy of electrons based on the prevailing evidence
    [ ] Heat of fusion, particularly regarding individual when applying fundamental principles
    Explanation: Binding energy = energy released when a nucleus is assembled from individual protons and neutrons. More binding energy per nucleon = more stable nucleus.

  Q21. Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon. This means:
    [ ] Iron is the most abundant element, particularly regarding toward under the given assumptions
    [ ] Iron cannot undergo any nuclear reactions as typically measured in practice
    [ ] Iron-56 is radioactive, particularly regarding this as determined by standard methodology
    [*] Iron-56 is the most stable nucleus; elements lighter or heavier than Fe release energy when fusing or fissioning toward Fe
    Explanation: Iron-56 is at the energy minimum of the binding energy curve. Light nuclei release energy fusing toward Fe; heavy nuclei release energy fissioning toward Fe.

  Q22. The sun converts approximately how much mass to energy per second?
    [ ] ~4 kg per second
    [*] ~4 million metric tons per second via fusion
    [ ] ~4 billion metric tons of fuel burned chemically
    [ ] ~1 kg per second via fission
    Explanation: The sun converts ~4×10⁹ kg (4 million metric tons) of mass to energy every second via the proton-proton fusion chain, releasing ~3.8×10²⁶ watts.

  Q23. Cold fusion refers to:
    [*] A hypothetical form of nuclear fusion occurring at room temperature (not yet reproducibly confirmed)
    [ ] Slow fission at low temperature in the context of this analysis
    [ ] Fusion using ice as fuel, particularly regarding cold when applying fundamental principles
    [ ] Fusion at −273°C, particularly regarding temperature according to conventional analysis
    Explanation: Cold fusion (claimed 1989 by Pons/Fleischmann) proposed fusion at room temperature — it has not been reproducibly confirmed by the scientific community.

  Q24. Which isotopes of hydrogen are used in fusion reactions?
    [*] Deuterium (²H) and Tritium (³H) → Helium-4 + neutron
    [ ] Ordinary hydrogen (¹H) only
    [ ] Helium-3 and helium-4
    [ ] Carbon-12 and carbon-14
    Explanation: D-T fusion: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV. This is the most promising fusion reaction for power generation.

  Q25. Nuclear power produces electricity without:
    [ ] Using turbines
    [ ] Using heat to make steam
    [ ] Using water for cooling
    [*] Direct CO₂ emissions (unlike fossil fuels)
    Explanation: Nuclear fission generates heat → steam → turbine → generator. No CO₂ is produced, though there are other environmental considerations (waste, accident risk).

  Q26. In a chain reaction, if fewer than 1 neutron per fission triggers a new fission, the reaction is:
    [*] Subcritical (the reaction dies out)
    [ ] Supercritical as observed
    [ ] Critical, particularly
    [ ] Explosive, particularly
    Explanation: Subcritical: neutrons lost faster than produced → chain reaction dies. Critical: exactly 1 neutron per fission → stable chain. Supercritical: >1 → exponential growth → explosion.

  Q27. The mass number (A) in nuclear reactions:
    [ ] Equals the charge
    [*] Is conserved (sum of A values same on both sides)
    [ ] Increases on the product side
    [ ] Is not conserved
    Explanation: Conservation of mass number: Σ A_reactants = Σ A_products. Also, atomic number (Z) is conserved in nuclear equations.

  Q28. Plasma confinement in fusion reactors uses:
    [ ] Chemical reactions to contain plasma
    [*] Strong magnetic fields (tokamak) or high-powered lasers (inertial confinement)
    [ ] Solid containment vessels when evaluated systematically
    [ ] Gravity only, particularly regarding contain based on the
    Explanation: At 100 million °C, no material can contain plasma directly. Tokamaks use magnetic fields; inertial confinement fusion uses lasers to compress tiny pellets.

  Q29. Nuclear waste from fission reactors is challenging because:
    [ ] It can reignite the chain reaction indefinitely
    [ ] It contains fusion byproducts
    [*] It contains long-lived radioactive isotopes that remain hazardous for thousands of years
    [ ] It produces CO₂
    Explanation: Spent nuclear fuel contains fission products (e.g., Cs-137, Sr-90) and actinides with half-lives of hundreds to thousands of years, requiring long-term secure storage.

  Q30. ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) is:
    [ ] A fission power plant, particularly regarding thermonuclear in the context of this analysis
    [*] A large experimental fusion reactor being built internationally to demonstrate net energy gain from fusion
    [ ] A research lab for cold fusion, particularly regarding that within the typical range of values
    [ ] An existing commercial fusion power plant when evaluated systematically
    Explanation: ITER (in France) is a joint international project (35 countries) to demonstrate that fusion can produce more energy than is put in — a critical milestone for fusion energy.

--- Lesson 12.2: Alpha, Beta, & Gamma Decay (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Alpha, Beta, & Gamma Decay
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the key difference between ionizing and non-ionizing radiation?
Ionizing radiation: E > ~10 eV, can break chemical bonds. Non-ionizing (radio, microwave, IR, visible, UV-A): insufficient energy to ionize atoms directly.

--- Lesson 12.2: Alpha, Beta, & Gamma Decay (Quiz) ---

  Q1. An alpha particle consists of:
    [ ] 1 proton and 1 neutron
    [ ] An electron
    [ ] A photon
    [*] 2 protons and 2 neutrons (He-4 nucleus)
    Explanation: Alpha particles (⁴₂He) are heavy (+2 charge) and can be stopped by paper or skin. In alpha decay, the parent loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons (atomic number -2, mass number -4). The answer is '2 protons and 2 neutrons (He-4 nucleus)'. The other options — 'An electron', 'A photon', and '1 proton and 1 neutron' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q2. A beta particle is:
    [ ] A massless photon of electromagnetic energy (gamma ray)
    [*] A high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus
    [ ] A positively charged proton ejected from the nucleus
    [ ] A helium-4 nucleus with 2 protons and 2 neutrons
    Explanation: Beta particles are high-energy electrons (β⁻) or positrons (β⁺). In β⁻ decay, a neutron converts to a proton (atomic number +1). Beta particles are stopped by aluminum foil. The answer is 'A high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus'. The other options — 'A helium nucleus', 'A proton', and 'A photon' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. Gamma radiation is:
    [ ] A charged particle with significant mass and momentum
    [ ] A high-speed electron emitted during nuclear decay
    [*] High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons)
    [ ] A helium-4 nucleus released during alpha decay events
    Explanation: Gamma rays (γ) are high-energy electromagnetic radiation with no mass or charge. They are the most penetrating, requiring thick lead or concrete to block. The answer is 'High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons)'. The other options — 'A particle with mass', 'A helium nucleus', and 'An electron' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q4. Which type of radiation has the greatest penetrating power?
    [ ] All equal
    [*] Gamma
    [ ] Beta
    [ ] Alpha
    Explanation: Gamma Decay (γ) : Emission of high-energy electromagnetic radiation. Key Concepts — Alpha Beta & Gamma Decay Core Definitions Alpha Decay (α) : Emission of a helium-4 nucleus (²₄He). The other options — 'Alpha', 'All equal', and 'Beta' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q5. Alpha particles can be stopped by:
    [ ] Concrete walls
    [ ] Several centimeters of aluminum
    [ ] Lead shielding
    [*] A sheet of paper
    Explanation: Alpha particles (⁴₂He) are large and heavily charged (+2), so they lose energy quickly when colliding with matter. A single sheet of paper — or even a few centimeters of air — is enough to stop them. Lead shielding and concrete walls are overkill for alphas (those are needed for gamma rays and neutrons). Aluminum centimeters would stop beta particles but are also unnecessary for alphas.

  Q6. In alpha decay, the atomic number decreases by:
    [ ] 4
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 0
    [*] 2
    Explanation: The atomic number (Z) equals the number of protons in the nucleus. It defines the element's identity and determines its position on the periodic table. The answer is '2'. The other options — '1', '0', and '4' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q7. In beta decay, the atomic number:
    [ ] Decreases by 1 (proton converts to neutron)
    [ ] Increases by 2 (two neutrons convert to protons)
    [*] Increases by 1 (neutron converts to proton)
    [ ] Stays the same (no change in proton count)
    Explanation: The atomic number (Z) equals the number of protons in the nucleus. It defines the element's identity and determines its position on the periodic table. The answer is 'Increases by 1 (neutron converts to proton)'. The other options — 'Increases by 2', 'Stays the same', and 'Decreases by 1' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q8. Which type of radiation has the greatest penetrating power?
    [*] Gamma rays (γ)
    [ ] Alpha particles (α)
    [ ] Neutron radiation
    [ ] Beta particles (β)
    Explanation: Gamma rays are high-energy electromagnetic radiation with no mass or charge — they penetrate deeply through materials and require dense shielding (lead, thick concrete).

  Q9. Alpha particles can be stopped by:
    [ ] Thick lead
    [ ] Nothing — they penetrate everything
    [ ] Concrete walls
    [*] A sheet of paper or a few centimeters of air
    Explanation: Alpha particles (²He nuclei) are large and slow; they lose energy quickly. A sheet of paper or even skin stops them.

  Q10. Beta particles can be stopped by:
    [*] A few millimeters of aluminum or plastic
    [ ] Nothing practical
    [ ] Thick lead only
    [ ] A sheet of paper
    Explanation: Beta particles (electrons or positrons) are small and fast. A few mm of aluminum, plastic, or glass stops them.

  Q11. Gamma rays are best shielded by:
    [ ] Paper or thin cardboard sheets
    [ ] Human skin or clothing layers
    [ ] A thin sheet of aluminum foil
    [*] Thick lead or concrete
    Explanation: High-energy gamma photons penetrate deeply. Dense materials (lead, thick concrete) are needed for effective shielding.

  Q12. An alpha particle is identical to:
    [*] A helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons)
    [ ] A single neutron with no electric charge
    [ ] An electron from the inner atomic shell
    [ ] A hydrogen-1 atom (1 proton and 1 electron)
    Explanation: α particle = ²₄He nucleus = 2 protons + 2 neutrons. It carries a +2 charge.

  Q13. A beta particle (β⁻) is:
    [*] A high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus
    [ ] A gamma photon released from the excited nucleus
    [ ] A helium nucleus with 2 protons and 2 neutrons
    [ ] A positron (antielectron with positive charge)
    Explanation: β⁻ decay: a neutron converts to a proton + electron + antineutrino. The electron (beta particle) is emitted from the nucleus.

  Q14. A positron (β⁺) is:
    [ ] An alpha particle consisting of 2 protons and 2 neutrons from the nucleus
    [ ] A proton ejected from the nucleus during radioactive decay of the atom
    [ ] A positively charged neutron formed during nuclear fission of heavy atoms
    [*] An antielectron (same mass as electron, but positive charge)
    Explanation: A positron = antiparticle of electron: same mass (9.11×10⁻³¹ kg) but charge +1. Emitted in β⁺ decay when a proton converts to a neutron.

  Q15. Gamma rays are:
    [ ] Electrons emitted from the nucleus at high speed during beta decay events
    [*] High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons) with no mass or charge
    [ ] Protons ejected from the nucleus with high kinetic energy during nuclear decay
    [ ] Helium nuclei containing 2 protons and 2 neutrons emitted in alpha decay
    Explanation: Gamma rays: pure electromagnetic radiation, λ < 0.01 nm. They accompany alpha or beta decay but carry no charge or mass.

  Q16. What happens to atomic number (Z) during alpha decay?
    [ ] Decreases by 1 (only one proton is lost in the decay)
    [*] Decreases by 2 (2 protons lost in the α particle)
    [ ] Stays the same (only neutrons are lost, not protons)
    [ ] Increases by 2 (two neutrons convert into protons)
    Explanation: Alpha particle = 2 protons + 2 neutrons. When emitted, Z decreases by 2 and A decreases by 4.

  Q17. What happens to atomic number (Z) during beta-minus decay?
    [ ] Decreases by 1
    [ ] Stays the same
    [*] Increases by 1 (neutron → proton + e⁻)
    [ ] Increases by 2
    Explanation: β⁻ decay: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄. The nucleus gains a proton (Z +1) and loses a neutron (N −1); A stays the same.

  Q18. Neutron radiation is particularly dangerous because:
    [ ] Neutrons are the same as alpha particles in charge, mass, and penetrating ability through matter
    [ ] Neutrons have a +2 charge that causes them to ionize atoms strongly as they pass through tissue
    [ ] Neutrons are large and slow-moving particles that are easily stopped by a thin sheet of paper
    [*] Neutrons are uncharged and penetrate deeply, and can make materials radioactive (activation)
    Explanation: Neutrons: no charge → not deflected → penetrate deeply. They can also be absorbed by stable nuclei, making them radioactive (neutron activation).

  Q19. Which radiation is most dangerous when ingested or inhaled (internally)?
    [ ] Gamma radiation (penetrating electromagnetic photons)
    [ ] Beta radiation (high-energy electrons from nucleus)
    [*] Alpha radiation (high ionizing power inside the body)
    [ ] Neutron radiation (uncharged particles from fission)
    Explanation: Inside the body, alpha particles deposit all their energy in a tiny area — causing tremendous local damage to DNA/tissues. External alpha is harmless (stopped by skin).

  Q20. The becquerel (Bq) is a unit measuring:
    [ ] Penetrating power
    [ ] Radiation dose absorbed
    [ ] Radiation energy in tissue
    [*] Radioactivity (1 disintegration per second)
    Explanation: 1 Becquerel = 1 nuclear decay (disintegration) per second. It measures the activity (decay rate) of a radioactive source.

  Q21. The sievert (Sv) measures:
    [*] Biological dose equivalent (radiation effect on human body)
    [ ] Gamma ray energy
    [ ] Number of decays per second
    [ ] Mass of radioactive material
    Explanation: Sievert accounts for both the energy absorbed (Gray) and the biological effectiveness of different radiation types on human tissue.

  Q22. The rad/gray unit measures:
    [ ] Penetrating power of the radiation through materials
    [ ] Biological damage from different radiation types
    [ ] Number of nuclear decays per second in the source
    [*] Energy absorbed per unit mass of tissue (absorbed dose)
    Explanation: 1 Gray = 1 J of energy absorbed per kg of tissue. 1 rad = 0.01 Gy (older unit). These measure absorbed dose, not biological effect.

  Q23. Ionizing radiation is dangerous because:
    [ ] It produces bright visible light that causes retinal damage and permanent blindness in exposed workers
    [*] It has enough energy to remove electrons from atoms, breaking chemical bonds and damaging DNA
    [ ] It heats tissue only, causing thermal burns without any chemical or molecular damage to the cells
    [ ] It causes chemical burns on the surface of the skin through direct contact with radioactive material
    Explanation: Ionizing radiation strips electrons from atoms/molecules, breaking chemical bonds — including DNA. This can cause mutations, cancer, or cell death.

  Q24. PET (positron emission tomography) scans use:
    [*] β⁺ (positron) emitters that produce gamma rays when positrons annihilate with electrons
    [ ] Neutron activation to make stable atoms radioactive inside the patient’s body for imaging
    [ ] Gamma ray external beams directed at the patient from multiple angles outside the body
    [ ] Alpha emitters injected into the bloodstream that deposit energy in nearby body tissues
    Explanation: PET: a β⁺ emitter (e.g., F-18) is injected; emitted positrons annihilate with electrons → two 511 keV gamma rays detected by the scanner to create 3D images.

  Q25. Electron capture is a nuclear process where:
    [*] The nucleus captures an inner electron; a proton converts to a neutron (Z decreases by 1)
    [ ] The nucleus absorbs a gamma ray photon, converting energy into mass and increasing the atomic number
    [ ] A neutron is emitted from the nucleus, reducing the mass number by one while keeping Z the same
    [ ] An electron is emitted from the nucleus as a beta particle, increasing the atomic number by one
    Explanation: Electron capture: p + e⁻ → n + ν. The nucleus absorbs an inner-shell electron; Z decreases by 1 (like β⁺ decay). Often followed by X-ray emission.

  Q26. Which radiation type has the largest mass?
    [ ] Neutron (mass ≈ 1 u but not listed)
    [ ] Gamma ray (zero mass)
    [*] Alpha particle (mass ≈ 4 u; 2 protons + 2 neutrons)
    [ ] Beta particle (mass ≈ 0.00055 u)
    Explanation: Alpha particles (⁴He nuclei) have the largest mass of the common radiation types: ~4 atomic mass units vs. ~0.00055 u for a beta particle.

  Q27. Radiation workers wear dosimeter badges to:
    [ ] Attract radiation away from the body
    [ ] Reflect gamma rays
    [ ] Speed up radioactive decay
    [*] Monitor accumulated radiation dose
    Explanation: Dosimeters (film badges, TLDs, or electronic) track the total ionizing radiation dose received by the wearer, ensuring it stays below safe limits.

  Q28. Radon-222 is a health concern because:
    [*] It decays by alpha emission; radon is a gas that can be inhaled, depositing alpha radiation in the lungs
    [ ] It is only found outdoors in the open atmosphere where wind disperses it to safe concentration levels
    [ ] It emits only gamma rays that pass through the body without depositing significant energy in tissue
    [ ] It is a stable element with no radioactive decay, but its chemical toxicity damages lung tissue
    Explanation: Radon-222 (alpha emitter) is produced in soil from U-238 decay. The gas seeps into buildings; inhaled Rn-222 and its radioactive daughters deposit alpha radiation in lung tissue, increasing lung cancer risk.

  Q29. What is the key difference between ionizing and non-ionizing radiation?
    [ ] Non-ionizing radiation is always more energetic than ionizing radiation because it includes microwaves and infrared that carry thermal energy
    [ ] Ionizing radiation includes only gamma rays from nuclear decay; all other types of radiation are classified as non-ionizing by definition
    [ ] Non-ionizing radiation is always harmful to living tissue because radio waves and visible light disrupt cellular processes at any exposure level
    [*] Ionizing radiation has enough energy to remove electrons (e.g., gamma, X-ray, alpha, beta); non-ionizing does not (e.g., visible light, radio waves)
    Explanation: Ionizing radiation: E > ~10 eV, can break chemical bonds. Non-ionizing (radio, microwave, IR, visible, UV-A): insufficient energy to ionize atoms directly.

  Q30. Why do nuclear plants monitor radiation levels continuously?
    [ ] To measure electricity production
    [ ] To control the fusion reaction
    [*] To detect any unexpected releases and protect workers and the public
    [ ] To cool the reactor more efficiently
    Explanation: Continuous monitoring detects radiation leaks early, protecting workers (exposure limits) and public safety, and is required by nuclear regulatory authorities.

Real-World Applications
Example: A positron is the antiparticle of:
A positron (β⁺) is the antiparticle of an electron — same mass but positive charge. In positron emission, a proton converts to a neutron (atomic number -1). The answer is 'An electron'. The other options — 'An alpha particle', 'A neutron', and 'A proton' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q1. In nuclear equations, both mass number and atomic number must be:
    [ ] Halved
    [ ] Destroyed
    [*] Conserved (balanced)
    [ ] Created
    Explanation: The atomic number (Z) equals the number of protons in the nucleus. It defines the element's identity and determines its position on the periodic table. The answer is 'Conserved (balanced)'. The other options — 'Created', 'Destroyed', and 'Halved' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q2. Transmutation is the conversion of:
    [*] One element into another
    [ ] An acid to a base
    [ ] Energy to mass
    [ ] A solid to a liquid
    Explanation: Mutations are changes in DNA sequence that can be caused by errors in replication, environmental factors (mutagens), or radiation. They may be harmful, neutral, or beneficial, and are the ultimate source of genetic variation. The answer is 'One element into another'. The other options — 'An acid to a base', 'A solid to a liquid', and 'Energy to mass' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. Artificial transmutation can be achieved by:
    [ ] Applying pressure
    [ ] Heating a substance
    [ ] Dissolving in water

--- Lesson 12.4: Half-Life Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Half-Life Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A radioactive sample decreases from 600 to 75 atoms in 30 minutes. What is the half-life?
600 → 75 = divide by 8 = 2³, so 3 half-lives. 30 min / 3 = 10 min half-life.
Real-World Applications
Example: The age of rocks can be determined by measuring:
Radiometric dating: by measuring the parent/daughter ratio and knowing t₁/₂, scientists calculate how long decay has been occurring (= age of rock).
Example: After 1 half-life, what percentage of the original material has decayed?
After 1 half-life: half has decayed → 50% remains, 50% has transformed to daughter isotope.

--- Lesson 12.5: Applications of Nuclear Chemistry (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Radioactive tracers are used in medicine to:
    [ ] Build weapons using concentrated radioactive materials
    [ ] Clean water supplies by killing harmful bacteria
    [*] Track biological processes and diagnose diseases
    [ ] Generate electricity in portable medical devices
    Explanation: Radioactive tracers are isotopes used in medicine to track biological processes (e.g., PET scans use fluorine-18). They emit detectable radiation while posing minimal health risk due to short half-lives. The answer is 'Track biological processes and diagnose diseases'. The other options — 'Generate electricity', 'Clean water', and 'Build weapons' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q2. Carbon-14 dating is used to determine the age of:
    [ ] Metals
    [ ] Rocks billions of years old
    [ ] Living organisms only
    [*] Organic materials up to ~50,000 years old
    Explanation: Carbon-14 dating measures the ratio of C-14 to C-12 in organic materials. C-14 has a half-life of ~5,730 years, making it useful for dating materials up to ~50,000 years old. The answer is 'Organic materials up to ~50,000 years old'. The other options — 'Metals', 'Living organisms only', and 'Rocks billions of years old' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. PET scans use:
    [*] Positron-emitting isotopes
    [ ] Gamma-only radiation
    [ ] Alpha particles
    [ ] X-rays
    Explanation: Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons, giving them different mass numbers but identical chemical properties. The answer is 'Positron-emitting isotopes'. The other options — 'X-rays', 'Alpha particles', and 'Gamma-only radiation' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q4. Nuclear energy produces electricity by using heat from fission to:
    [ ] Directly convert radiation to electricity
    [*] Generate steam that turns turbines
    [ ] Burn radioactive fuel
    [ ] Create fusion reactions
    Explanation: Nuclear fission splits a heavy nucleus (like uranium-235) into lighter nuclei, releasing neutrons and energy. A chain reaction occurs when released neutrons trigger more fissions. The answer is 'Generate steam that turns turbines'. The other options — 'Directly convert radiation to electricity', 'Burn radioactive fuel', and 'Create fusion reactions' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q5. Radiation therapy treats cancer by:
    [ ] Injecting radioactive dye
    [ ] Removing all cells
    [*] Targeting and killing cancer cells with radiation
    [ ] Using only alpha particles
    Explanation: Medical Uses — Radioisotopes used in imaging (PET scans), cancer treatment (radiation therapy), and tracers. The other options — 'Using only alpha particles', 'Injecting radioactive dye', and 'Removing all cells' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q6. Food irradiation uses radiation to:
    [ ] Heat food for cooking
    [*] Kill bacteria and extend shelf life
    [ ] Change the taste of food
    [ ] Make food radioactive
    Explanation: Food Irradiation — Gamma rays kill bacteria in food to extend shelf life without making food radioactive. The other options — 'Change the taste of food', 'Make food radioactive', and 'Heat food for cooking' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q7. The main concern with nuclear power is:
    [ ] Air pollution from burning
    [ ] High fuel cost
    [ ] Low energy output
    [*] Radioactive waste disposal and potential accidents
    Explanation: Nuclear power generates electricity via fission. Main concerns include radioactive waste disposal (remains hazardous for thousands of years), potential meltdowns, and weapons proliferation. The answer is 'Radioactive waste disposal and potential accidents'. The other options — 'High fuel cost', 'Air pollution from burning', and 'Low energy output' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q8. Positron emission tomography (PET) scans work by:
    [ ] Using neutron beams directed through the patient to image internal organ density
    [ ] Measuring the heat generated from radioactive decay of tracers in the body
    [*] Detecting gamma rays produced when emitted positrons annihilate with electrons in tissue
    [ ] Reflecting X-rays off bones and dense structures to create contrast images
    Explanation: PET: a radiotracer (e.g., F-18 labeled glucose) is injected. Emitted positrons annihilate with electrons → 2 back-to-back 511 keV gamma rays detected by PET scanner → 3D metabolic images.

  Q9. Radiation therapy for cancer uses:
    [*] High-energy radiation to damage DNA of cancer cells and stop their reproduction
    [ ] Sound waves focused on tumor cells to mechanically disrupt their cellular membranes
    [ ] Laser light only, directed at cancer cells to heat and destroy the tumor tissue
    [ ] Radioactive isotopes ingested as oral medicine that selectively target cancer cells
    Explanation: Radiation therapy: ionizing radiation damages DNA in rapidly dividing cancer cells. External beam radiation (gamma, X-ray, proton) or brachytherapy (internal seeds).

  Q10. Nuclear power plants use nuclear fission to:
    [ ] Directly produce electricity from radiation
    [ ] Produce fusion reactions
    [*] Generate heat → steam → electricity (via turbines/generators)
    [ ] Generate fusion plasma
    Explanation: Nuclear fission (U-235) generates heat → boils water → steam drives turbines → generators produce electricity. About 20% of US electricity comes from nuclear power.

  Q11. Food irradiation uses:
    [ ] Neutron beams to irradiate food and make it safe for long-term storage and consumption
    [ ] Alpha particles directed at food surfaces to eliminate bacterial contamination entirely
    [*] Gamma rays to kill bacteria and extend shelf life without making food radioactive
    [ ] UV light only for surface decontamination of pre-packaged food products and beverages
    Explanation: Food irradiation: gamma rays (from Co-60 or Cs-137) or X-rays kill pathogens (Salmonella, E. coli), insects, and slow spoilage. The food does NOT become radioactive.

  Q12. Radioactive tracers in medicine are used to:
    [ ] Replace damaged tissues with regenerated cells activated by low-dose radiation treatment
    [*] Track physiological processes (blood flow, organ function) using the radiation emitted
    [ ] Provide energy to cells through radioactive decay powering cellular metabolism directly
    [ ] Kill bacteria inside the body using concentrated doses of targeted radiation beams
    Explanation: Medical tracers: low-dose radioisotopes injected into the body follow physiological processes. Their emitted radiation is detected externally to create images of organ function.

  Q13. The CANDU reactor uses heavy water (D₂O) instead of regular water. The advantage is:
    [ ] D₂O is less radioactive than regular water and reduces the radiation hazard in the reactor
    [ ] D₂O produces more energy per fission event than regular water due to its heavier mass
    [ ] D₂O is cheaper to produce and easier to purify in large industrial quantities for reactors
    [*] D₂O moderates neutrons without absorbing them — allows use of natural uranium fuel (no enrichment)
    Explanation: Heavy water moderates neutrons effectively but absorbs far fewer neutrons than H₂O. This allows CANDU reactors to run on unenriched natural uranium (0.7% U-235).

  Q14. Nuclear medicine uses gamma-emitting isotopes because:
    [ ] Alpha particles cannot leave the body
    [*] Gamma rays penetrate the body and can be detected externally without surgery
    [ ] Beta particles cause more harm
    [ ] No radiation is needed
    Explanation: Gamma emitters: radiation passes through body tissue and can be detected by external gamma cameras, enabling non-invasive imaging without the heavy tissue damage of alpha or beta particles.

  Q15. Radon mitigation in homes involves:
    [ ] Increasing air pressure inside
    [ ] Using lead paint
    [ ] Sealing all windows
    [*] Ventilating the basement to prevent radon gas accumulation
    Explanation: Radon enters buildings from soil/rock. Mitigation systems use sub-slab depressurization (suction pipes below foundation) and ventilation to keep radon levels below EPA action level (4 pCi/L).

  Q16. Nuclear reactors must enrich uranium because:
    [ ] Enrichment creates fusion fuel
    [*] Natural uranium is only 0.7% U-235 (fissile); reactors typically require 3–5% U-235
    [ ] Natural uranium is not radioactive
    [ ] All U-238 must be removed
    Explanation: Natural uranium: 99.3% U-238 (non-fissile), 0.7% U-235 (fissile). Light-water reactors need 3–5% U-235 enrichment to sustain a chain reaction.

  Q17. Smoke detectors often use:
    [ ] Iodine-131 (β emitter used in thyroid imaging and treatment)
    [ ] Cobalt-60 (γ emitter used in industrial radiography sources)
    [*] Americium-241 (α emitter that ionizes air in the detector chamber)
    [ ] Uranium-235 (fissile isotope used as nuclear reactor fuel)
    Explanation: Am-241 ionizes air between two charged plates; ions create a tiny current. Smoke particles disrupt the current → alarm triggers. Am-241 is safe (alpha stopped by detector casing).

  Q18. Nuclear waste is classified as high-level waste (HLW) when it:
    [*] Contains spent nuclear fuel and is highly radioactive with long-lived isotopes
    [ ] Contains medical waste from hospitals that used short-lived diagnostic radioisotopes
    [ ] Is only slightly radioactive and can be disposed of in standard municipal landfills
    [ ] Comes from fossil fuel combustion and contains naturally occurring radioactive materials
    Explanation: HLW: spent nuclear fuel rods and reprocessing waste. Intensely radioactive (requires heavy shielding), heat-generating, and contains long-lived radionuclides (e.g., Pu-239 t₁/₂=24,100 yr).

  Q19. Radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) power:
    [ ] Aircraft engines using small nuclear reactors mounted on the wings for long-range flight
    [ ] Nuclear power plants that generate electricity for cities through controlled fission reactions
    [*] Deep space probes (Voyager, Cassini, Curiosity rover) using heat from radioactive decay
    [ ] Medical devices only, such as pacemakers and insulin pumps, using small sealed sources
    Explanation: RTGs use the heat from decay of Pu-238 (t₁/₂=87.7 yr) to generate electricity via thermoelectrics — reliable power for decades in space where solar power isn't practical.

  Q20. Iodine-131 (t₁/₂ = 8 days) is used to treat hyperthyroidism because:
    [ ] It has a 100-year half-life, which provides continuous treatment over many decades for the patient
    [*] The thyroid selectively absorbs iodine; I-131 beta radiation destroys overactive thyroid cells
    [ ] It is a stable isotope that does not undergo radioactive decay, acting purely as a chemical agent
    [ ] It emits alpha particles that kill all cells in the body, requiring precise shielding for treatment
    Explanation: The thyroid absorbs iodine to make hormones. I-131 concentrates in the thyroid; local beta radiation selectively destroys overactive cells while sparing other tissues.

  Q21. Nuclear fusion is considered a 'clean' energy source because:
    [*] It produces helium (not CO₂ or long-lived radioactive waste) and uses seawater-derived fuel
    [ ] It produces no radiation at all during the fusion reaction or afterward from its products
    [ ] It uses uranium fuel enriched to high concentrations for the fusion process to operate
    [ ] It produces water as the only byproduct, with no other materials or energy released
    Explanation: D-T fusion produces helium-4 and a neutron. No CO₂, no long-lived waste (though reactor components become activated), and fuel (deuterium from seawater, tritium bred from lithium) is abundant.

  Q22. Radiation sterilization of medical devices uses:
    [ ] Autoclaves only, using high-pressure steam to sterilize heat-resistant metal instruments
    [ ] Chemical disinfectants only, applied to surfaces to eliminate bacteria and viral pathogens
    [ ] UV light directed at the surface of instruments for short-duration sterilization cycles
    [*] Gamma rays or electron beams to kill all microorganisms on surgical instruments and packaging
    Explanation: Gamma sterilization (often Co-60) penetrates packaged devices, killing all microorganisms without heat damage — ideal for plastics and pre-packaged single-use medical devices.

  Q23. Neutron activation analysis (NAA) identifies elements in a sample by:
    [ ] Measuring mass only using a precision analytical balance to determine elemental composition
    [ ] Measuring optical spectrum using visible light absorption patterns of each element present
    [*] Bombarding with neutrons, making elements radioactive; their specific gamma rays identify the elements
    [ ] Dissolving the sample in acid and then separating elements using chemical precipitation
    Explanation: NAA: expose sample to neutrons → stable isotopes absorb neutrons → become radioactive → emit characteristic gamma rays → identify and quantify elements by their unique gamma spectra.

  Q24. Why is the Chernobyl exclusion zone still restricted decades later?
    [ ] The reactor is still operating
    [*] Long-lived isotopes (Cs-137 t₁/₂=30yr, Sr-90 t₁/₂=29yr) still contaminate the area
    [ ] All radiation has decayed
    [ ] Only short-lived isotopes were released
    Explanation: The 1986 Chernobyl explosion released large quantities of Cs-137 and Sr-90 (among others). With 30-year half-lives, these isotopes still contaminate soil and vegetation in the exclusion zone.

  Q25. Carbon-14 dating is reliable for:
    [ ] Metal artifacts
    [ ] Inorganic compounds
    [ ] Rocks billions of years old
    [*] Organic materials up to ~50,000 years old
    Explanation: C-14 (t₁/₂=5730 yr) is practical for ~10 half-lives → up to ~57,300 years. Beyond that, the C-14 signal is too small to measure accurately.

  Q26. Cyclotrons in hospitals produce medical radioisotopes by:
    [*] Accelerating protons to bombard stable targets, creating short-lived isotopes like F-18 for PET
    [ ] Electrolysis of radioactive solutions to separate isotopes by their different ionic charges
    [ ] Natural radioactive decay only, collecting daughter isotopes produced over time in storage
    [ ] Fission of uranium fuel rods in a small reactor contained within the hospital facility
    Explanation: Hospital cyclotrons accelerate protons and bombard targets (e.g., O-18 water to make F-18). On-site production of short-lived isotopes is essential since they can't be shipped from far away.

  Q27. The main advantage of nuclear power over fossil fuels is:
    [ ] Nuclear uses renewable resources
    [ ] Nuclear is completely safe and risk-free
    [ ] Nuclear produces no radioactive materials
    [*] No direct CO₂ emissions; enormous energy density (1 kg U-235 ≈ 1500 tonnes of coal)
    Explanation: 1 kg of U-235 produces as much energy as ~1500 tonnes of coal (via E=mc²). No CO₂ is emitted during operation, though mining, construction, and waste management have environmental impacts.

  Q28. Brachytherapy involves:
    [*] Placing radioactive seeds directly inside or near a tumor (e.g., prostate cancer treatment)
    [ ] Radioactive injection into bloodstream
    [ ] External beam radiation
    [ ] Swallowed radioactive pills only
    Explanation: Brachytherapy: small radioactive seeds (e.g., I-125 or Pd-103) implanted directly in or near tumor tissue, delivering high local radiation dose while sparing surrounding healthy tissue.

  Q29. The half-life of the sun's proton-proton fusion fuel (hydrogen) is approximately:
    [ ] 100 billion years (the sun will burn hydrogen far longer than the current age of the universe)
    [*] ~5 billion years (half the sun's current hydrogen has been consumed in 4.6 billion years)
    [ ] 4.6 billion years total lifetime (equal to the current age of the sun and solar system)
    [ ] 1 billion years (the sun will exhaust its hydrogen supply within the next billion years)
    Explanation: The sun has been burning hydrogen for ~4.6 billion years and has enough H for another ~5 billion years. It will exhaust its core hydrogen in about 5 billion years.

  Q30. The primary goal of the ITER fusion project is to:
    [ ] Build the first commercial fusion power plant
    [ ] Produce medical radioisotopes
    [*] Demonstrate a fusion reaction that produces 10× more energy than is put in (Q≥10)
    [ ] Demonstrate cold fusion
    Explanation: ITER's goal: achieve plasma Q ≥ 10 (500 MW output from 50 MW input) — proving fusion can produce net energy. ITER is expected to operate in the 2030s.
