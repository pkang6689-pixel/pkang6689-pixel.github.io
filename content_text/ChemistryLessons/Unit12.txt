--- Lesson 12.1:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nuclear Fusion and Fission
Core Definitions

  • Nuclear Fission: The splitting of a heavy nucleus into two lighter nuclei, releasing large amounts of energy.
  • Nuclear Fusion: The combining of two light nuclei into one heavier nucleus, releasing even more energy.
  • Chain Reaction: In fission, released neutrons trigger further fission events, sustaining the reaction.
  • Mass Defect: The difference between the mass of the nucleus and the sum of its individual protons and neutrons — converted to energy via E = mc².

--- Lesson 12.2:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Alpha Beta & Gamma Decay

Core Definitions

  • Alpha Decay (α): Emission of a helium-4 nucleus (²₄He). Atomic number decreases by 2, mass number by 4.
  • Beta Decay (β): A neutron converts to a proton and emits an electron (⁰₋₁e). Atomic number increases by 1.
  • Gamma Decay (γ): Emission of high-energy electromagnetic radiation. No change in atomic or mass number.
  • Penetrating Power: α

--- Lesson 12.2:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. An alpha particle consists of:
    [ ] An electron
    [*] 2 protons and 2 neutrons (He-4 nucleus)
    [ ] A photon
    [ ] 1 proton and 1 neutron
    Explanation: Alpha particles (⁴₂He) are heavy (+2 charge) and can be stopped by paper or skin. In alpha decay, the parent loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons (atomic number -2, mass number -4). The answer is 'A high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus'. The other options — 'A helium nucleus', 'A proton', and 'A photon' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. Gamma radiation is:
    [ ] A particle with mass
    [*] High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons)
    [ ] A helium nucleus
    [ ] An electron
    Explanation: Gamma rays (γ) are high-energy electromagnetic radiation with no mass or charge. They are the most penetrating, requiring thick lead or concrete to block. The answer is 'High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons)'. The other options — 'A particle with mass', 'A helium nucleus', and 'An electron' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q5. Alpha particles can be stopped by:

--- Lesson 12.3:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nuclear Reactions
Core Definitions

  • Nuclear Equation: Shows the parent nucleus, emitted particle, and daughter nucleus. Mass and atomic numbers must balance.
  • Transmutation: The conversion of one element into another through nuclear reactions.
  • Balancing Rule: Sum of mass numbers on left = sum on right; sum of atomic numbers on left = sum on right.
  • Particle Symbols: α = ⁴₂He, β = ⁰₋₁e, γ = ⁰₀γ, neutron = ¹₀n, proton = ¹₁p.

--- Lesson 12.4:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Half-Life Calculations

Core Definitions

  • Half-Life (t½): The time it takes for half of a radioactive sample to decay.
  • Formula: Remaining = Original × (½)ⁿ, where n = number of half-lives elapsed.
  • Finding n: n = total time ÷ half-life.
  • Key Feature: Half-life is constant — it does not change regardless of the amount of sample.

    Explanation: Half-life is the time for half the substance to decay/react. After n half-lives, the remaining amount = initial × (1/2)ⁿ. The answer is Half of a radioactive sample to decay. The other options — 'A reaction to complete', 'A sample to double', and 'All of a sample to decay' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

--- Lesson 12.5:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Applications of Nuclear Chemistry

Core Definitions

  • Nuclear Power: Fission of uranium-235 heats water to produce steam that drives turbines for electricity.
  • Medical Uses: Radioisotopes used in imaging (PET scans), cancer treatment (radiation therapy), and tracers.
  • Carbon Dating: Uses the decay of carbon-14 to determine the age of organic materials up to ~50,000 years.
  • Food Irradiation: Gamma rays kill bacteria in food to extend shelf life without making food radioactive.

--- Lesson 12.5:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Radioactive tracers are used in medicine to:
    [ ] Generate electricity
    [ ] Clean water
    [ ] Build weapons
    [*] Track biological processes and diagnose diseases
    Explanation: Radioactive tracers are isotopes used in medicine to track biological processes (e.g., PET scans use fluorine-18). The other options — 'Metals', 'Living organisms only', and 'Rocks billions of years old' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
