--- Lesson 7.1: Writing Correct Formulas (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Writing Correct Formulas
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'polyatomic ion'?
Polyatomic ions like SO₄²⁻, NH₄⁺, and NO₃⁻ contain multiple atoms bonded together that behave as a single charged unit in ionic compounds.
What is the 'neutral compound rule'?
All ionic compounds are electrically neutral overall. The sum of all positive charges from cations must exactly balance the sum of all negative charges from anions.
What is a 'formula unit'?
Because ionic compounds form crystal lattices rather than individual molecules, we describe them as formula units — the simplest ratio of cations to anions (e.g., NaCl).
A student needs to write the formula for sodium phosphate. Na⁺ and PO₄³⁻ are the ions. What is the correct formula?
Criss-cross: Na's 1 becomes PO₄'s subscript (stays 1), PO₄'s 3 becomes Na's subscript → Na₃PO₄. Check: 3(+1) + 1(−3) = 0.
A student writes 'MgO₂' for magnesium oxide. What is the error?
Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ both have magnitude 2, so criss-cross gives Mg₂O₂, which reduces to MgO. One-to-one ratio already balances charges.
A compound contains Fe³⁺ and S²⁻. What is the correct formula?
Criss-cross: Fe₂S₃. Check: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = +6 − 6 = 0. The formula is electrically neutral.
Baking soda is sodium bicarbonate. Given Na⁺ and HCO₃⁻, what is the formula?
Both ions have magnitude 1 (Na⁺ and HCO₃⁻), so they combine 1:1. No parentheses are needed when the subscript would be 1.
Copper(II) nitrate contains Cu²⁺ and NO₃⁻. What is the formula?
Cu²⁺ needs 2 NO₃⁻ ions to balance. Parentheses are required around the polyatomic ion when the subscript is >1 → Cu(NO₃)₂.
Limestone is calcium carbonate. Given Ca²⁺ and CO₃²⁻, what is the formula?
Both ions have ±2 charges, so they combine in a 1:1 ratio → CaCO₃. No parentheses needed because the subscript for carbonate is 1.

--- Lesson 7.2: Molar Mass & Molecular Mass (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Molar Mass & Molecular Mass
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the molar mass of H₂SO₄?
H₂SO₄: 2(1.01) + 32.07 + 4(16.00) = 2.02 + 32.07 + 64.00 = 98.09 g/mol.
What is an 'atomic mass unit' (amu)?
The amu provides a convenient scale for expressing atomic and molecular masses. 1 amu ≈ 1.66 × 10⁻²⁴ grams.
What is the difference between molecular mass and molar mass?
Numerically they are the same value, but the units differ: molecular mass uses amu (one molecule scale), molar mass uses g/mol (mole scale).
What is a 'formula unit'?
Since ionic compounds form extended lattices, we describe them in terms of formula units (e.g., one NaCl unit = one Na⁺ paired with one Cl⁻).
A student says 'the molar mass of Fe is 55.85 amu.' What is wrong with this statement?
55.85 is correct numerically but the units are wrong for molar mass. Atomic mass = 55.85 amu (per atom). Molar mass = 55.85 g/mol (per mole).
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'average atomic mass' mean on the periodic table?
Elements have multiple isotopes with different masses. The periodic table value is a weighted average based on each isotope's natural abundance.

Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

The conversion map places moles in the center. From moles you can go to grams (× molar mass), particles (× Avogadro's number), or liters (× 22.4 at STP).
What is a 'conversion factor'?
A conversion factor expresses a relationship between two units. Multiplying by it changes units without changing the actual amount.

--- Lesson 7.5: Empirical vs. Molecular Formulas (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Empirical vs. Molecular Formulas
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'percent composition'?
Calculated as (mass of element in 1 mol ÷ molar mass of compound) × 100%. All percentages must add up to 100%.
What is the 'GCF method' for finding an empirical formula?
Example: C₆H₁₂O₆ — the GCF of 6, 12, 6 is 6. Dividing gives C₁H₂O₁ = CH₂O.
What is the difference between 'structural formula' and 'molecular formula'?
C₂H₆O is the molecular formula for both ethanol and dimethyl ether. Their structural formulas differ, showing different atom connections.
What is the purpose of 'combustion analysis'?
Burning a compound in excess O₂ produces CO₂ and H₂O. Measuring these products reveals the moles of C and H, from which the empirical formula can be derived.
A compound is 40% C, 6.7% H, and 53.3% O by mass. What is its empirical formula?
In 100 g: 40 g C ÷ 12 = 3.33 mol, 6.7 g H ÷ 1 = 6.7 mol, 53.3 g O ÷ 16 = 3.33 mol. Ratio: 3.33/3.33 : 6.7/3.33 : 3.33/3.33 = 1:2:1 → CH₂O.
A student finds the empirical formula of a vitamin is C₃H₄O₃ (88 g/mol empirical mass). The actual molar mass is 176 g/mol. What is the molecular formula?
n = 176 ÷ 88 = 2. Molecular formula = 2 × C₃H₄O₃ = C₆H₈O₆, which is ascorbic acid (vitamin C).
A forensic chemist analyzes a white powder and finds it is 52.2% C, 13.0% H, and 34.8% O. What is the empirical formula?
52.2÷12 = 4.35, 13.0÷1 = 13.0, 34.8÷16 = 2.175. Divide by 2.175: 2:6:1 → C₂H₆O.
Benzene has the empirical formula CH and a molar mass of 78 g/mol. What is its molecular formula?
Empirical formula mass of CH = 12 + 1 = 13 g/mol. n = 78 ÷ 13 = 6. Molecular formula = C₆H₆.
A pharmaceutical company finds a drug is 58.8% C, 9.8% H, and 31.4% O. If the molar mass is 102 g/mol, what is the molecular formula?
58.8÷12 = 4.9, 9.8÷1 = 9.8, 31.4÷16 = 1.96. Divide by 1.96: 2.5:5:1. Multiply by 2: 5:10:2 → C₅H₁₀O₂ (EFM = 102, n = 1).

--- Lesson 7.6: Limiting Reagents (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Limiting Reagents
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'stoichiometric ratio'?
Stoichiometric ratios come from the coefficients in the balanced equation. For 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, the H₂:O₂ ratio is 2:1.
What is the 'ICE table method' for limiting reagent problems?
ICE tables track Initial amounts, Change during reaction (negative for reactants, positive for products), and End amounts. They clearly show what's left over.
What is the difference between 'limiting' and 'excess' in lab terminology?
These are opposite roles: the limiting reagent is fully used up, while the excess reagent has some amount remaining unreacted.
For N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃, if you start with 2 mol N₂ and 3 mol H₂, what is the limiting reagent and how much NH₃ forms?
2 mol N₂ could produce 4 mol NH₃ (×2/1). 3 mol H₂ could produce 2 mol NH₃ (×2/3). H₂ gives less → H₂ is limiting → 2 mol NH₃.
In a student lab, 4.0 g of NaOH (40 g/mol) reacts with 7.3 g of HCl (36.5 g/mol) in: NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O. What is the limiting reagent and how much NaCl forms?
4.0÷40 = 0.1 mol NaOH. 7.3÷36.5 = 0.2 mol HCl. 1:1 ratio → NaOH (0.1) runs out first → 0.1 mol NaCl × 58.5 g/mol = 5.85 g.

--- Lesson 7.7: Stoichiometric Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Stoichiometric Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 4-step stoichiometry method?
Step 1: Balance. Step 2: Convert given quantity to moles. Step 3: Apply mole ratio. Step 4: Convert to requested unit.
What is a 'mass-to-mass problem'?
Mass-to-mass: given grams → ÷ molar mass → moles → mole ratio → moles of target → × molar mass → grams of target.
What is a 'mole-to-mole problem'?
Mole-to-mole is the simplest stoichiometry type: given moles of A × (coefficient B ÷ coefficient A) = moles of B.
A car engine burns octane: 2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O. What is the mole ratio of octane to CO₂?
From the coefficients: 2 mol C₈H₁₈ : 16 mol CO₂, which simplifies to 1:8. Each mole of octane produces 8 moles of CO₂.
