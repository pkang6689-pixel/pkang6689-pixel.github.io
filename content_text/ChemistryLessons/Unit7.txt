--- Lesson 7.1:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Writing Correct Formulas

Core Definitions

  • Chemical Formula: Uses element symbols and subscripts to show the composition of a compound (e.g., H₂O).
  • Cation: A positively charged ion formed when an atom loses electrons.
  • Anion: A negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains electrons.
  • Criss-Cross Method: Use the charge of each ion as the subscript of the other to write formulas.
  • Polyatomic Ion: A group of atoms bonded together that carry a charge (e.g., SO₄²⁻, NH₄⁺).
  • Neutral Compound Rule: The total positive charge must equal the total negative charge in a formula.

--- Lesson 7.1:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A chemical formula uses element symbols and subscripts to show:
    [*] The types and numbers of atoms in a compound
    [ ] The color of the compound
    [ ] Only the charge of the compound
    [ ] Only the mass of the compound
    Explanation: A chemical formula like H₂O tells us which elements are present (H and O) and how many atoms of each (2 H, 1 O) through subscripts.

  Q2. In H₂O, the subscript 2 indicates there are:
    [*] 2 hydrogen atoms
    [ ] 2 water molecules
    [ ] 2 oxygen atoms
    [ ] Hydrogen with charge 2
    Explanation: A subscript applies to the element directly before it. In H₂O, the 2 follows H, meaning 2 hydrogen atoms per molecule of water.

  Q3. The formula for calcium chloride (Ca²⁺ and Cl⁻) is:
    [*] CaCl₂
    [ ] CaCl
    [ ] Ca₂Cl
    [ ] Ca₃Cl₂
    Explanation: Ca²⁺ needs two Cl⁻ ions to balance charges. Using the criss-cross method: Ca's 2+ becomes Cl's subscript → CaCl₂.

  Q4. When writing formulas for ionic compounds, the total charges must:
    [*] Balance to zero
    [ ] Be all positive
    [ ] Be all negative
    [ ] Equal the number of atoms
    Explanation: Ionic compounds are electrically neutral. The total positive charge from cations must equal the total negative charge from anions.

  Q5. The formula for aluminum sulfate (Al³⁺ and SO₄²⁻) is:
    [*] Al₂(SO₄)₃
    [ ] AlSO₄
    [ ] Al₃(SO₄)₂
    [ ] Al(SO₄)₃
    Explanation: Criss-cross: Al's 3 becomes the subscript for SO₄, and SO₄'s 2 becomes the subscript for Al → Al₂(SO₄)₃. Total charges: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = 0.

  Q6. Parentheses in a formula like Ca(OH)₂ mean:
    [*] The subscript outside applies to the entire polyatomic ion
    [ ] The charge is negative
    [ ] The element is rare
    [ ] The compound is organic
    Explanation: In Ca(OH)₂, the subscript 2 applies to the entire hydroxide ion (OH), giving 2 oxygen and 2 hydrogen atoms per formula unit.

  Q7. The correct formula for iron(III) oxide is:
    [*] Fe₂O₃
    [ ] FeO₃
    [ ] FeO
    [ ] Fe₃O₂
    Explanation: Iron(III) means Fe³⁺. Oxide is O²⁻. Criss-cross: Fe₂O₃. Check: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = 0. Charges balance.

  Q8. A cation is an ion with a:
    [*] Positive charge, formed by losing electrons
    [ ] Negative charge
    [ ] No charge
    [ ] Positive charge, formed by gaining electrons
    Explanation: Cations form when atoms lose electrons. Metals typically form cations (Na → Na⁺, Ca → Ca²⁺). Losing negative electrons leaves a positive charge.

  Q9. The criss-cross method involves using each ion's charge as:
    [*] The other ion's subscript
    [ ] The coefficient
    [ ] The superscript
    [ ] A multiplier for mass
    Explanation: In the criss-cross method, the magnitude of each ion's charge becomes the subscript of the other ion. Then simplify to the lowest whole-number ratio.

  Q10. Which formula correctly represents magnesium nitrate?
    [*] Mg(NO₃)₂
    [ ] MgNO₃
    [ ] Mg₂NO₃
    [ ] Mg₂(NO₃)₃
    Explanation: Mg²⁺ and NO₃⁻: criss-cross gives Mg₁(NO₃)₂. Two nitrate ions balance the +2 charge of magnesium.

  Q11. Define 'cation.'
    [*] A positively charged ion formed when an atom loses electrons
    [ ] A negatively charged ion
    [ ] An uncharged atom
    [ ] A molecule with no charge
    Explanation: The prefix 'cat-' can be remembered as 'paws-itive.' Metals lose electrons to form cations: Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Al³⁺.

  Q12. Define 'anion.'
    [*] A negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains electrons
    [ ] A positively charged ion
    [ ] A neutral atom
    [ ] A noble gas
    Explanation: Nonmetals gain electrons to achieve a full outer shell, forming anions: Cl⁻, O²⁻, N³⁻. The added electrons create a net negative charge.

  Q13. What is a 'polyatomic ion'?
    [*] A group of covalently bonded atoms that carry a net charge
    [ ] A single atom with a charge
    [ ] A neutral molecule
    [ ] An element on the periodic table
    Explanation: Polyatomic ions like SO₄²⁻, NH₄⁺, and NO₃⁻ contain multiple atoms bonded together that behave as a single charged unit in ionic compounds.

  Q14. What does the 'criss-cross method' do?
    [*] Uses the charge of each ion as the subscript of the other to write correct formulas
    [ ] Swaps the two elements' positions
    [ ] Calculates the molar mass
    [ ] Determines the state of matter
    Explanation: For Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻: the 2 from Mg becomes Cl's subscript and the 1 from Cl becomes Mg's subscript → MgCl₂. Always reduce to simplest ratio.

  Q15. What is the 'neutral compound rule'?
    [*] The total positive charges must equal the total negative charges so the compound is electrically neutral
    [ ] Compounds are always neutral in pH
    [ ] Only noble gases form neutral compounds
    [ ] Mass must equal zero
    Explanation: All ionic compounds are electrically neutral overall. The sum of all positive charges from cations must exactly balance the sum of all negative charges from anions.

  Q16. What does 'chemical formula' mean?
    [*] A representation using element symbols and subscripts showing the composition of a compound
    [ ] A word equation for a reaction
    [ ] The name of a compound
    [ ] The physical properties of a substance
    Explanation: Chemical formulas like NaCl, H₂SO₄, and C₆H₁₂O₆ use element symbols from the periodic table and numerical subscripts to show composition.

  Q17. What does 'subscript' mean in a chemical formula?
    [*] A small number below and to the right of an element symbol indicating the number of atoms
    [ ] A number in front of the formula showing molecules
    [ ] The charge on an ion
    [ ] The atomic number
    Explanation: Subscripts tell how many atoms of each element are in one formula unit. In Al₂O₃, there are 2 aluminum atoms and 3 oxygen atoms.

  Q18. What is a 'formula unit'?
    [*] The lowest whole-number ratio of ions in an ionic compound
    [ ] A single molecule of a covalent compound
    [ ] The atomic mass of an element
    [ ] A unit of measurement for volume
    Explanation: Because ionic compounds form crystal lattices rather than individual molecules, we describe them as formula units — the simplest ratio of cations to anions (e.g., NaCl).

  Q19. What does 'transition metal' mean for writing formulas?
    [*] Metals that can form ions with multiple charges, so Roman numerals indicate which charge
    [ ] Metals that do not form ions
    [ ] Elements that are always gases
    [ ] Nonmetals with positive charges
    Explanation: Transition metals like iron can be Fe²⁺ or Fe³⁺. Roman numerals in the name (iron(II) vs iron(III)) specify which charge to use when writing formulas.

  Q20. What does 'reducing subscripts' mean when writing formulas?
    [*] Simplifying subscripts to the smallest whole-number ratio after criss-crossing
    [ ] Removing all subscripts from a formula
    [ ] Making subscripts larger
    [ ] Converting subscripts to coefficients
    Explanation: After criss-crossing Pb⁴⁺ and O²⁻, you get Pb₂O₄. Both divide by 2 → PbO₂. Always simplify to the lowest ratio.

  Q21. A student needs to write the formula for sodium phosphate. Na⁺ and PO₄³⁻ are the ions. What is the correct formula?
    [*] Na₃PO₄
    [ ] NaPO₄
    [ ] Na₃(PO₄)₃
    [ ] Na(PO₄)₃
    Explanation: Criss-cross: Na's 1 becomes PO₄'s subscript (stays 1), PO₄'s 3 becomes Na's subscript → Na₃PO₄. Check: 3(+1) + 1(−3) = 0.

  Q22. A lab report shows the formula 'AlCl₃' for a compound. How many total ions are in one formula unit?
    [*] 4 ions — 1 Al³⁺ and 3 Cl⁻
    [ ] 3 ions total
    [ ] 2 ions total
    [ ] 6 ions total
    Explanation: AlCl₃ contains 1 aluminum ion (Al³⁺) and 3 chloride ions (Cl⁻), making 4 ions total per formula unit.

  Q23. A student writes 'MgO₂' for magnesium oxide. What is the error?
    [*] Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ already balance 1:1, so the formula should be MgO
    [ ] Magnesium cannot bond with oxygen
    [ ] The formula should be Mg₂O
    [ ] Oxygen cannot form ions
    Explanation: Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ both have magnitude 2, so criss-cross gives Mg₂O₂, which reduces to MgO. One-to-one ratio already balances charges.

  Q24. Write the formula for ammonium sulfate given NH₄⁺ and SO₄²⁻.
    [*] (NH₄)₂SO₄
    [ ] NH₄SO₄
    [ ] NH₄(SO₄)₂
    [ ] (NH₄)₃SO₄
    Explanation: Criss-cross: NH₄'s 1+ charge → SO₄ subscript 1, SO₄'s 2− charge → NH₄ subscript 2. Two NH₄⁺ ions: 2(+1) = +2 balances one SO₄²⁻.

  Q25. A compound contains Fe³⁺ and S²⁻. What is the correct formula?
    [*] Fe₂S₃
    [ ] FeS
    [ ] Fe₃S₂
    [ ] FeS₃
    Explanation: Criss-cross: Fe₂S₃. Check: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = +6 − 6 = 0. The formula is electrically neutral.

  Q26. Baking soda is sodium bicarbonate. Given Na⁺ and HCO₃⁻, what is the formula?
    [*] NaHCO₃
    [ ] Na₂HCO₃
    [ ] Na(HCO₃)₂
    [ ] NaH₂CO₃
    Explanation: Both ions have magnitude 1 (Na⁺ and HCO₃⁻), so they combine 1:1. No parentheses are needed when the subscript would be 1.

  Q27. A student writes Ca₂SO₄ for calcium sulfate. What should be corrected?
    [*] Ca²⁺ and SO₄²⁻ balance 1:1, so the formula should be CaSO₄
    [ ] The formula is correct as written
    [ ] It should be Ca₃(SO₄)₂
    [ ] Calcium cannot bond with sulfate
    Explanation: Ca²⁺ and SO₄²⁻ each have charge ±2. Criss-cross gives Ca₂(SO₄)₂ which reduces to CaSO₄. The student forgot to simplify.

  Q28. Copper(II) nitrate contains Cu²⁺ and NO₃⁻. What is the formula?
    [*] Cu(NO₃)₂
    [ ] CuNO₃
    [ ] Cu₂NO₃
    [ ] Cu₂(NO₃)₃
    Explanation: Cu²⁺ needs 2 NO₃⁻ ions to balance. Parentheses are required around the polyatomic ion when the subscript is >1 → Cu(NO₃)₂.

  Q29. Rust is iron(III) oxide. Using Fe³⁺ and O²⁻, determine its formula.
    [*] Fe₂O₃
    [ ] FeO
    [ ] Fe₃O₂
    [ ] FeO₃
    Explanation: Criss-cross Fe³⁺ and O²⁻: Fe₂O₃. Check: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = 0. This reddish-brown compound is common rust.

  Q30. Limestone is calcium carbonate. Given Ca²⁺ and CO₃²⁻, what is the formula?
    [*] CaCO₃
    [ ] Ca₂CO₃
    [ ] Ca(CO₃)₂
    [ ] Ca₂(CO₃)₂
    Explanation: Both ions have ±2 charges, so they combine in a 1:1 ratio → CaCO₃. No parentheses needed because the subscript for carbonate is 1.

--- Lesson 7.2:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Molar Mass & Molecular Mass
Core Definitions

  • Molar Mass: The mass of one mole of a substance in grams per mole (g/mol); equals atomic mass from periodic table.
  • Molecular Mass: The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecular formula.
  • Formula Mass: The sum of atomic masses for ionic compounds.
  • Calculating Molar Mass: Add up the atomic masses of each element multiplied by their subscript.
  • Example: H₂O: 2(1.01) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol.
  • Units: Molar mass is expressed in g/mol; molecular mass in amu (atomic mass units).

--- Lesson 7.2:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Molar mass is measured in:
    [*] Grams per mole (g/mol)
    [ ] Moles per gram
    [ ] Kilograms
    [ ] Liters per mole
    Explanation: Molar mass tells us how many grams one mole of a substance weighs. For any element the molar mass in g/mol numerically equals its atomic mass in amu.

  Q2. The molar mass of water (H₂O) is approximately:
    [*] 18 g/mol
    [ ] 20 g/mol
    [ ] 16 g/mol
    [ ] 2 g/mol
    Explanation: H₂O: 2(1.01) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol. Sum each element's atomic mass multiplied by its subscript.

  Q3. To find the molar mass of a compound, you:
    [*] Add up the atomic masses of all atoms in the formula
    [ ] Divide by Avogadro's number
    [ ] Multiply atomic numbers
    [ ] Count only the heaviest atom
    Explanation: Look up each element's atomic mass on the periodic table, multiply by the subscript for that element, then add all contributions together.

  Q4. The molar mass of NaCl is approximately:
    [*] 58.44 g/mol
    [ ] 23 g/mol
    [ ] 35.45 g/mol
    [ ] 22.99 g/mol
    Explanation: NaCl: Na = 22.99 + Cl = 35.45 → 58.44 g/mol. 23 and 35.45 are only the individual atomic masses.

  Q5. Molecular mass applies specifically to:
    [*] Covalent (molecular) compounds
    [ ] Ionic compounds only
    [ ] Elements only
    [ ] Noble gases only
    Explanation: The term 'molecular mass' is reserved for covalent compounds that form true molecules (like H₂O, CO₂). For ionic compounds we say 'formula mass' instead.

  Q6. Formula mass is used for:
    [*] Ionic compounds
    [ ] Gases only
    [ ] Elements only
    [ ] Molecular compounds only
    Explanation: Ionic compounds like NaCl form lattice networks, not individual molecules. We use 'formula mass' to describe the mass of one formula unit.

  Q7. The molar mass of CO₂ is approximately:
    [*] 44 g/mol
    [ ] 28 g/mol
    [ ] 32 g/mol
    [ ] 16 g/mol
    Explanation: CO₂: C = 12.01 + 2(O = 16.00) = 12.01 + 32.00 = 44.01 g/mol.

  Q8. What is the molar mass of H₂SO₄?
    [*] 98.09 g/mol
    [ ] 49 g/mol
    [ ] 80 g/mol
    [ ] 34 g/mol
    Explanation: H₂SO₄: 2(1.01) + 32.07 + 4(16.00) = 2.02 + 32.07 + 64.00 = 98.09 g/mol.

  Q9. The atomic mass unit (amu) is defined as:
    [*] One-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom
    [ ] The mass of one hydrogen atom
    [ ] One gram
    [ ] The mass of one electron
    Explanation: By definition, 1 amu = 1/12 the mass of a ¹²C atom. This standard makes 1 amu ≈ 1.66 × 10⁻²⁴ g.

  Q10. The molar mass of O₂ is:
    [*] 32 g/mol
    [ ] 16 g/mol
    [ ] 48 g/mol
    [ ] 8 g/mol
    Explanation: O₂ contains 2 oxygen atoms: 2 × 16.00 = 32.00 g/mol. 16 g/mol is the atomic mass of a single oxygen atom.

  Q11. Define 'molar mass.'
    [*] The mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in grams per mole (g/mol)
    [ ] The number of atoms in a substance
    [ ] The volume of one mole of gas
    [ ] The density of a substance
    Explanation: Molar mass bridges the microscopic (atoms, molecules) and macroscopic (grams) worlds. Numerically equal to atomic/molecular mass but in g/mol.

  Q12. Define 'molecular mass.'
    [*] The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecular formula, in amu
    [ ] The number of molecules in a sample
    [ ] The mass in grams of one molecule
    [ ] The charge of a molecule
    Explanation: Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses (in amu) for all atoms in one molecule. For H₂O: 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.02 amu.

  Q13. Define 'formula mass.'
    [*] The sum of atomic masses for one formula unit of an ionic compound
    [ ] The mass of a single ion
    [ ] The number of ions in a crystal
    [ ] The volume of an ionic compound
    Explanation: Since ionic compounds don't form discrete molecules, we add up the atomic masses of one formula unit (e.g., NaCl = 22.99 + 35.45 = 58.44 amu).

  Q14. What is an 'atomic mass unit' (amu)?
    [*] A unit of mass equal to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom
    [ ] A unit of volume
    [ ] A unit of charge
    [ ] A unit of temperature
    Explanation: The amu provides a convenient scale for expressing atomic and molecular masses. 1 amu ≈ 1.66 × 10⁻²⁴ grams.

  Q15. What does 'g/mol' mean?
    [*] Grams per mole — the mass of one mole of a substance
    [ ] Grams per molecule
    [ ] Grams per liter
    [ ] Grams per atom
    Explanation: g/mol tells us how many grams are in exactly one mole (6.022 × 10²³ particles) of a substance.

  Q16. What does 'subscript' tell you when calculating molar mass?
    [*] How many atoms of that element to include — multiply the atomic mass by the subscript
    [ ] The charge of the ion
    [ ] The number of moles
    [ ] The temperature needed
    Explanation: In Ca(OH)₂, the subscript 2 tells you to count 2 O atoms and 2 H atoms. Multiply each element's atomic mass by its total count and add.

  Q17. What is the difference between molecular mass and molar mass?
    [*] Molecular mass is in amu (for one molecule); molar mass is in g/mol (for one mole)
    [ ] They are identical with the same units
    [ ] Molecular mass is always larger
    [ ] Molar mass applies only to gases
    Explanation: Numerically they are the same value, but the units differ: molecular mass uses amu (one molecule scale), molar mass uses g/mol (mole scale).

  Q18. What does 'average atomic mass' mean on the periodic table?
    [*] A weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes of that element
    [ ] The mass of the most common isotope only
    [ ] The total mass of all isotopes added together
    [ ] The number of protons
    Explanation: Elements have multiple isotopes with different masses. The periodic table value is a weighted average based on each isotope's natural abundance.

  Q19. Define 'mole' as a counting unit.
    [*] 6.022 × 10²³ particles — one mole of any substance contains this many atoms, molecules, or formula units
    [ ] 100 particles
    [ ] 1 gram of a substance
    [ ] The atomic number of an element
    Explanation: The mole is chemistry's counting unit, like a 'dozen' (12) but much larger. One mole of carbon = 6.022 × 10²³ carbon atoms = 12.01 g.

  Q20. What is a 'formula unit'?
    [*] The simplest ratio of ions in an ionic compound, analogous to a molecule for covalent compounds
    [ ] A single atom of an element
    [ ] One liter of a solution
    [ ] A measure of temperature
    Explanation: Since ionic compounds form extended lattices, we describe them in terms of formula units (e.g., one NaCl unit = one Na⁺ paired with one Cl⁻).

  Q21. A pharmacist needs to measure out 0.5 mol of aspirin (C₉H₈O₄, molar mass ≈ 180 g/mol). How many grams is that?
    [*] 90 g
    [ ] 180 g
    [ ] 360 g
    [ ] 45 g
    Explanation: Grams = moles × molar mass = 0.5 × 180 = 90 g. This conversion uses the definition of molar mass.

  Q22. A student calculates the molar mass of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆). What should they get?
    [*] 180.18 g/mol
    [ ] 30 g/mol
    [ ] 342 g/mol
    [ ] 90 g/mol
    Explanation: C₆H₁₂O₆: 6(12.01) + 12(1.01) + 6(16.00) = 72.06 + 12.12 + 96.00 = 180.18 g/mol. 30 g/mol is the empirical formula mass (CH₂O).

  Q23. A lab tech has 100 g of CaCO₃ (molar mass = 100.09 g/mol). Approximately how many moles is this?
    [*] ≈ 1 mol
    [ ] ≈ 10 mol
    [ ] ≈ 0.1 mol
    [ ] ≈ 100 mol
    Explanation: Moles = grams ÷ molar mass = 100 ÷ 100.09 ≈ 1.00 mol.

  Q24. Which compound has the greatest molar mass: H₂O (18), NaCl (58.44), C₆H₁₂O₆ (180), or CO₂ (44)?
    [*] C₆H₁₂O₆ (180 g/mol)
    [ ] NaCl (58.44 g/mol)
    [ ] CO₂ (44 g/mol)
    [ ] H₂O (18 g/mol)
    Explanation: Comparing: 18 < 44 < 58.44 < 180. Glucose has the largest and most complex formula, giving the highest molar mass.

  Q25. A student says 'the molar mass of Fe is 55.85 amu.' What is wrong with this statement?
    [*] Molar mass should be in g/mol, not amu — the atomic mass is 55.85 amu
    [ ] The number 55.85 is incorrect
    [ ] Fe does not have a molar mass
    [ ] Nothing is wrong
    Explanation: 55.85 is correct numerically but the units are wrong for molar mass. Atomic mass = 55.85 amu (per atom). Molar mass = 55.85 g/mol (per mole).

  Q26. Calculate the molar mass of calcium phosphate, Ca₃(PO₄)₂.
    [*] 310.18 g/mol
    [ ] 135 g/mol
    [ ] 215 g/mol
    [ ] 278 g/mol
    Explanation: 3(40.08) + 2(30.97) + 8(16.00) = 120.24 + 61.94 + 128.00 = 310.18 g/mol. Remember the subscript 2 applies to the entire PO₄.

  Q27. An experiment requires 2 moles of NaOH (molar mass = 40 g/mol). How many grams should be measured?
    [*] 80 g
    [ ] 40 g
    [ ] 20 g
    [ ] 160 g
    Explanation: Grams = moles × molar mass = 2 × 40 = 80 g.

  Q28. Why does one mole of lead (Pb) weigh more than one mole of carbon (C)?
    [*] Lead atoms are individually heavier — Pb has a higher atomic mass (207.2 vs 12.01)
    [ ] One mole of Pb has more atoms
    [ ] Lead is denser
    [ ] Carbon atoms are larger
    Explanation: Both contain 6.022 × 10²³ atoms, but each Pb atom is much heavier than each C atom. Molar mass reflects the mass of individual atoms scaled up.

  Q29. A container holds exactly 44 g of CO₂. How many moles of CO₂ is this?
    [*] 1 mol
    [ ] 2 mol
    [ ] 0.5 mol
    [ ] 44 mol
    Explanation: Moles = grams ÷ molar mass = 44 ÷ 44 = 1 mol.

  Q30. A student needs to determine if a sample is pure NaCl. The sample has a mass of 116.88 g and contains 2.00 mol. Does this match NaCl?
    [*] Yes — 116.88 ÷ 2.00 = 58.44 g/mol, which is exactly the molar mass of NaCl
    [ ] No — it should be 23 g/mol
    [ ] No — it should be 35.45 g/mol
    [ ] Cannot be determined from this data
    Explanation: Measured molar mass = 116.88 g ÷ 2.00 mol = 58.44 g/mol. This matches the calculated molar mass of NaCl (22.99 + 35.45), confirming identity.

--- Lesson 7.3:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Avogadro's Number
Core Definitions

  • Avogadro's Number: 6.022 × 10²³ — the number of particles (atoms, molecules, ions) in one mole.
  • Mole: The SI unit for amount of substance; 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles.
  • Mole-to-Particles: Multiply moles × 6.022 × 10²³ to find the number of particles.
  • Particles-to-Moles: Divide number of particles by 6.022 × 10²³ to find moles.
  • Amedeo Avogadro: Italian scientist who contributed to molecular theory; the number is named after him.
  • Universal Counting Unit: A mole relates microscopic particles to macroscopic measurable amounts.

--- Lesson 7.3:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Avogadro's number is approximately:
    [*] 6.022 × 10²³
    [ ] 3.01 × 10²³
    [ ] 6.02 × 10²²
    [ ] 6.02 × 10²⁴
    Explanation: Avogadro's number, 6.022 × 10²³, defines the number of particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) in exactly one mole of any substance.

  Q2. One mole of any substance contains:
    [*] 6.022 × 10²³ particles
    [ ] 1 gram of particles
    [ ] 100 atoms
    [ ] 1 million molecules
    Explanation: Regardless of the substance, one mole always contains 6.022 × 10²³ particles. The mass may differ, but the count is always the same.

  Q3. Avogadro's number defines the number of particles in:
    [*] One mole of a substance
    [ ] One gram of a substance
    [ ] One liter of a gas
    [ ] One kilogram of a substance
    Explanation: The mole is a counting unit defined by Avogadro's number. One mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles, regardless of the substance's identity.

  Q4. How many molecules are in 2 moles of H₂O?
    [*] 1.204 × 10²⁴
    [ ] 6.022 × 10²³
    [ ] 3.011 × 10²³
    [ ] 1.806 × 10²⁴
    Explanation: Particles = moles × Avogadro's number = 2 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules.

  Q5. The mole is used because atoms are:
    [*] Too small to count individually
    [ ] Always in groups of 100
    [ ] Too large to measure
    [ ] All identical in mass
    Explanation: Individual atoms are incredibly tiny. The mole bridges the gap between the atomic scale and the lab scale by grouping enormous numbers of particles.

  Q6. One mole of carbon-12 has a mass of:
    [*] 12 grams
    [ ] 24 grams
    [ ] 1 gram
    [ ] 6 grams
    Explanation: By definition, 1 mole of ¹²C atoms (6.022 × 10²³ atoms) has a mass of exactly 12 grams. This is the basis for the mole concept.

  Q7. To convert from particles to moles, you:
    [*] Divide by Avogadro's number
    [ ] Multiply by Avogadro's number
    [ ] Subtract Avogadro's number
    [ ] Add Avogadro's number
    Explanation: Moles = particles ÷ 6.022 × 10²³. Since multiplying moles × Avogadro's number gives particles, the reverse (particles → moles) requires division.

  Q8. How many atoms are in 0.5 moles of iron (Fe)?
    [*] 3.011 × 10²³ atoms
    [ ] 6.022 × 10²³ atoms
    [ ] 1.204 × 10²⁴ atoms

--- Lesson 7.4:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Molar Conversions

Core Definitions

  • Moles to Grams: Multiply moles × molar mass (g/mol).
  • Grams to Moles: Divide grams by molar mass (g/mol).
  • Moles to Particles: Multiply moles × 6.022 × 10²³.
  • Particles to Moles: Divide number of particles by 6.022 × 10²³.
  • Moles to Liters (Gas at STP): Multiply moles × 22.4 L/mol.
  • Conversion Map: Particles ↔ Moles ↔ Grams; Moles ↔ Liters (gas at STP).

--- Lesson 7.5:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Empirical vs Molecular Formulas

Core Definitions

  • Empirical Formula: The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound (e.g., CH₂O).
  • Molecular Formula: The actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule (e.g., C₆H₁₂O₆).
  • Finding Empirical Formula: Convert percentages to grams → to moles → divide by smallest mole value → round to whole numbers.
  • Relationship: Molecular formula = n × empirical formula, where n = molar mass / empirical mass.
  • Percent Composition: The percentage by mass of each element in a compound.
  • Example: Glucose: empirical = CH₂O (30 g/mol), molecular = C₆H₁₂O₆ (180 g/mol), n = 6.

--- Lesson 7.6:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Limiting Reagents
Core Definitions

  • Limiting Reagent: The reactant that is completely consumed first and determines the maximum amount of product formed.
  • Excess Reagent: The reactant that is left over after the reaction is complete.
  • Finding the Limiting Reagent: Convert both reactants to moles of product; the one that produces LESS is the limiting reagent.
  • Stoichiometry: Using mole ratios from balanced equations to calculate amounts of reactants/products.
  • Theoretical Yield: The maximum amount of product calculated from the limiting reagent.
  • Analogy: Like a sandwich recipe: if you run out of bread before cheese, bread is the limiting reagent.

--- Lesson 7.7:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Stoichiometric Calculations
Core Definitions

  • Stoichiometry: The calculation of quantities in chemical reactions using mole ratios from balanced equations.
  • Mole Ratio: The ratio of moles of one substance to another from the coefficients of a balanced equation.
  • Steps: 1) Balance equation. 2) Convert given to moles. 3) Use mole ratio. 4) Convert to desired unit.
  • Mass-to-Mass: Given grams → moles (÷ molar mass) → mole ratio → moles → grams (× molar mass).
  • Volume-to-Volume (Gas): At STP, mole ratios equal volume ratios for gases.
  • Balanced Equation is Key: All stoichiometric calculations begin with a correctly balanced equation.

--- Lesson 7.7:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Stoichiometry uses balanced equations to calculate:
    [*] Amounts of reactants and products
    [ ] Bond angles
    [ ] Atomic numbers

--- Lesson 7.8:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Percent Yield
Core Definitions

  • Actual Yield: The amount of product actually obtained from an experiment.
  • Theoretical Yield: The maximum amount of product calculated from stoichiometry (from limiting reagent).
  • Percent Yield: (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100%.
  • Why Less Than 100%: Side reactions, incomplete reactions, loss during transfer, or impurities.
  • 100% Yield: Means the actual yield equals the theoretical yield (rare in practice).
  • Greater Than 100%: Usually indicates experimental error (e.g., impurities adding mass).
