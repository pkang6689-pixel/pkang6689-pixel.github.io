--- Lesson 4.1: Chemical Symbols (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Chemical Symbols
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the chemical symbol for gold?
Gold's symbol Au comes from its Latin name 'aurum.' Many elements have Latin-derived symbols: Na (natrium/sodium), Fe (ferrum/iron), Au (aurum/gold).
What is the chemical symbol for sodium?
Sodium's symbol Na comes from 'natrium,' its Latin name. S is sulfur, not sodium.
What is the chemical symbol for potassium?
Potassium's symbol K comes from 'kalium,' its Latin/Arabic name. P is phosphorus, Po is polonium, and Pt is platinum.
What is the chemical symbol for mercury?
Mercury's symbol Hg comes from 'hydrargyrum,' meaning 'liquid silver' in Latin. Mercury is the only metal that is liquid at room temperature.
What is the chemical symbol for lead?
Lead's symbol Pb comes from 'plumbum' in Latin — which is also the origin of the word 'plumber,' since ancient pipes were made of lead.
What is the chemical symbol for silver?
Silver's symbol Ag comes from 'argentum' in Latin. Si is silicon, Sr is strontium.
What is a chemical symbol?
Chemical symbols are standardized abbreviations used worldwide, regardless of language, making scientific communication universal.
What is the periodic table?
The periodic table arranges elements in order of increasing atomic number. Elements in the same column (group) share similar properties due to having the same number of valence electrons.
What is a group (family) on the periodic table?
Groups (columns) contain elements with the same number of valence electrons, giving them similar reactivity and bonding behavior.
What is a period on the periodic table?
Each period corresponds to a principal energy level. Period 1 elements have 1 shell, period 2 has 2 shells, etc. Properties change systematically across a period.
What is atomic notation?
Atomic notation displays the mass number (superscript) and atomic number (subscript) alongside the element symbol, providing complete nuclear information at a glance.
A medical technician administers Tc-99m for an imaging scan. What is element Tc?
Technetium (Tc) was the first artificially produced element. Tc-99m (metastable) is the most widely used radioisotope in medical imaging due to its ideal half-life and gamma emission.
Real-World Applications
Example: A chemistry student sees NaCl on a reagent bottle. How many different elements are represented?
Na is sodium (one symbol, two letters) and Cl is chlorine (one symbol, two letters). NaCl is the compound sodium chloride — table salt.

--- Lesson 4.2: Periodic Table Arrangement (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Periodic Table Arrangement
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a metalloid?
Metalloids (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te) have intermediate properties. Silicon, for example, is a semiconductor — it conducts electricity under certain conditions but not others, making it essential for electronics.
What is a nonmetal?
Nonmetals include essential elements like carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and halogens. They tend to gain electrons in chemical reactions, can be gases, liquids (bromine), or solids, and have lower melting points than metals.
What is the s-block on the periodic table?
The periodic table is divided into blocks based on which orbital the last electron occupies: s-block (Groups 1-2), p-block (Groups 13-18), d-block (Groups 3-12), and f-block (lanthanides/actinides).

--- Lesson 4.3: Valence Electrons & Reactivity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Valence Electrons & Reactivity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the octet rule?
The octet rule explains why atoms bond. Having 8 valence electrons (like noble gases) is energetically favorable. Atoms achieve this by ionic bonding (transferring electrons) or covalent bonding (sharing electrons).
What is electronegativity?
Electronegativity measures how strongly an atom pulls bonding electrons toward itself. Fluorine has the highest electronegativity (3.98). It increases up and to the right on the periodic table (excluding noble gases).
What is ionization energy?
Ionization energy increases across a period (stronger nuclear pull) and decreases down a group (electrons farther from nucleus). High ionization energy means the atom holds its electrons tightly.
What is electron affinity?
Electron affinity is most negative (most energy released) for halogens, which strongly attract electrons. Noble gases have near-zero electron affinity because their shells are already full.
What is the duet rule?
The duet rule is the exception to the octet rule for very small atoms. Hydrogen needs only 2 electrons (filling its 1s orbital) to be stable, which is why H₂ forms a single covalent bond.
Real-World Applications
Example: A swimming pool uses chlorine (Cl₂) to kill bacteria. The pool manager switches to bromine (Br₂) for a hot tub. Why are both effective but used differently?

--- Lesson 4.4: Electron Suborbitals (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electron Suborbitals
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a sublevel (subshell)?
Energy levels (n=1, 2, 3...) are divided into sublevels. The number of sublevels equals the principal quantum number: n=1 has only s; n=2 has s and p; n=3 has s, p, and d; n=4 has s, p, d, and f.
What is an orbital?
Unlike Bohr's circular orbits, orbitals are three-dimensional probability clouds. An orbital describes where an electron is likely (about 90% probability) to be found, not a definite path.
What is the Aufbau principle?
'Aufbau' is German for 'building up.' Electrons fill in the order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d... The diagonal rule helps remember this sequence.
What is the Pauli exclusion principle?
Wolfgang Pauli's principle limits each orbital to 2 electrons with opposite spins (+½ and -½). This is why s holds 2, p holds 6 (3×2), d holds 10 (5×2), and f holds 14 (7×2).
What is Hund's rule?
Think of Hund's rule like bus seating: passengers sit alone before doubling up. In the 2p sublevel with 3 orbitals, the first 3 electrons each go into separate orbitals before any pairing occurs.
What is the principal quantum number (n)?
The principal quantum number n determines the energy level and approximate distance from the nucleus. As n increases, orbitals get larger, and the electron has more energy.
What is a degenerate orbital?
The three p orbitals (px, py, pz) in the same shell all have equal energy — they are degenerate. Hund's rule applies to degenerate orbitals: fill each singly before pairing.
What is the diagonal rule used for?
The diagonal rule (or Madelung's rule) gives the filling order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f... Notice that 4s fills before 3d because 4s is lower in energy.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student asks why transition metals like iron can form multiple ions (Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺). The answer relates to:
Iron's configuration is [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s². Since 3d and 4s are close in energy, iron can lose 2 electrons (Fe²⁺: lose 4s²) or 3 electrons (Fe³⁺: lose 4s² and one 3d). This versatility is characteristic of d-block elements.

--- Lesson 4.5: Electron Configuration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electron Configuration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the electron configuration of carbon (Z=6)?
Carbon has 6 electrons: 1s² fills first (2e), then 2s² (2e), then 2p² (2e). By Hund's rule, the 2 electrons in 2p occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins.
What is electron configuration?
Electron configuration tells us exactly how electrons are distributed among energy levels and sublevels. It predicts chemical properties, bonding behavior, and magnetic properties of elements.
What is noble gas shorthand notation?
Instead of writing 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹ for sodium, we write [Ne] 3s¹. The [Ne] represents the first 10 electrons, and we only write the remaining valence configuration.
What is the diagonal rule (Madelung's rule)?
The diagonal rule helps remember the Aufbau filling order. Write sublevel columns (1s, 2s 2p, 3s 3p 3d, 4s 4p 4d 4f...) and draw diagonal arrows to get the sequence.
What is an excited state electron configuration?
When energy is absorbed (heat, light), an electron can jump to a higher sublevel. For example, excited lithium might be 1s² 2p¹ instead of ground state 1s² 2s¹. It's unstable and will emit a photon to return.
What is a ground state electron configuration?
The ground state is the most stable configuration. All electrons are in the lowest available orbitals. This is what we normally write as the electron configuration of an element.
What is an isoelectronic species?
Na⁺ (11p, 10e), Ne (10p, 10e), and F⁻ (9p, 10e) are isoelectronic — all have the configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁶. They have identical electron arrangements despite different nuclear charges.
What is the electron configuration of a calcium ion (Ca²⁺)?
Calcium (Z=20) is [Ar] 4s². When it forms Ca²⁺, it loses both 4s electrons, leaving 18 electrons with the same configuration as argon: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶.
A student writes nitrogen's configuration as 1s² 2s² 2p³ with all three 2p electrons in one orbital. What is wrong?
The 2p sublevel has 3 orbitals. With 3 electrons, each one occupies a separate orbital (↑ ↑ ↑) per Hund's rule, minimizing repulsion. Pauli's principle also limits each orbital to 2 electrons maximum.
Real-World Applications
Example: A medical test uses paramagnetic contrast agents in MRI scans. Gadolinium (Gd³⁺) is used because it has 7 unpaired electrons. Its configuration is [Xe] 4f⁷. Why is this exceptionally stable?
Like the d⁵ exception in chromium, the half-filled f⁷ sublevel is extra stable. Gd³⁺'s 7 unpaired electrons create a strong magnetic moment, making it ideal for MRI contrast enhancement.

--- Lesson 4.6: Periodic Trends (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Periodic Trends
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is ionization energy?
Ionization energy (IE) measures how tightly an atom holds its electrons. First IE removes the most loosely held electron; second IE removes the next, and so on. Successive IEs always increase.
What is electronegativity?
Electronegativity (Pauling scale) measures electron-attracting ability in bonds. The difference in EN between two bonded atoms determines bond polarity: >1.7 = ionic, 0.4–1.7 = polar covalent, <0.4 = nonpolar.
What is atomic radius?
Since atoms don't have sharp boundaries, atomic radius is measured as half the internuclear distance (bond length) in a homonuclear diatomic molecule (e.g., Cl–Cl). Covalent and van der Waals radii are different measures.
What is electron affinity?
A negative electron affinity means energy is released when an electron is gained (exothermic). A positive EA means energy must be added. Noble gases have positive EA — their full shells resist gaining electrons.
What is metallic character?
Metallic character relates to how easily atoms lose electrons to form cations. Elements with low ionization energy and few valence electrons (like alkali metals) are most metallic.
What is a periodic trend?
Periodic trends emerge because electron configuration follows a repeating pattern. Elements in the same group have similar valence electron arrangements, giving them similar properties.
What is the second ionization energy (IE₂)?
IE₂ is always greater than IE₁ for the same element because the remaining electrons experience more nuclear charge. For Na, IE₂ is dramatically higher (4562 vs 496 kJ/mol) because it removes a core electron.
What is the ionic radius of Na⁺ compared to neutral Na?
Na (186 pm) → Na⁺ (95 pm). Removing the single 3s electron eliminates the third shell entirely. Also, 11 protons now pull on only 10 electrons, drawing them closer.

--- Lesson 4.7: Shielding Effect (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Shielding Effect
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the shielding effect (electron shielding)?
Shielding is an electrostatic effect: the negative charge of core electrons repels the valence electrons outward, partially counteracting the positive nuclear charge. It's a key factor in explaining periodic trends.
What is penetration in the context of shielding?
An s electron has a higher probability of being found near the nucleus than a p, d, or f electron at the same energy level. This is why 4s fills before 3d — the 4s electron penetrates more and feels a higher Zeff.
What is Coulomb's law in the context of atomic structure?
F = kq₁q₂/r². The nucleus (+) attracts valence electrons (-), but the force weakens with distance (r²). This is why distant valence electrons in large atoms are held weakly and are easily removed.
What is the lanthanide contraction?
The 14 lanthanide 4f electrons shield poorly. Each added proton increases Zeff significantly, shrinking the atoms. This makes Period 6 transition metals (Hf, Ta, W) nearly identical in size to their Period 5 counterparts (Zr, Nb, Mo).

  Q1. The shielding effect occurs when inner electrons:
    [ ] Merge with the nucleus
    [ ] Gain energy
    [ ] Attract other atoms
    [*] Block the nuclear pull on outer electrons
    Explanation: Inner (core) electrons create a 'shield' of negative charge between the nucleus and outer electrons. This repulsion partially cancels the nuclear attraction, so valence electrons feel less pull.

  Q2. As shielding increases, valence electrons are:
    [ ] Destroyed
    [ ] Harder to remove
    [ ] Unaffected
    [*] Easier to remove
    Explanation: More shielding reduces the effective nuclear charge (Zeff) felt by valence electrons. With weaker attraction to the nucleus, less energy is needed to remove them — hence lower ionization energy.

  Q3. Shielding effect increases as you go:
    [*] Down a group
    [ ] Up a group
    [ ] Across a period
    [ ] Diagonally
    Explanation: Going down a group adds entire electron shells (1s → 1s,2s,2p → etc.). Each added shell provides an additional layer of shielding for valence electrons. Li has 2 core e⁻; Cs has 54.

  Q4. Effective nuclear charge (Zeff) is the net charge felt by:
    [*] Valence electrons
    [ ] Neutrons
    [ ] Inner electrons
    [ ] Protons

    [ ] Less shielding effect
    [ ] No shielding
    [*] Greater shielding effect
    [ ] More nuclear charge

    [ ] Neither has shielding

    [ ] Neutrons increase

    [ ] Shielding increases faster than protons
    [ ] Electrons decrease

    [ ] When electrons physically block each other

--- Lesson 4.8: VSEPR Molecule Shapes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: VSEPR Molecule Shapes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the electron geometry of water?
Electron geometry counts ALL electron pairs (bonding + lone). Water has 4 pairs around O (2 bonding, 2 lone), so its electron geometry is tetrahedral. The molecular shape (bent) only describes the atom positions.
What is the bond angle in a linear molecule?
In a linear arrangement, atoms form a straight line with maximum separation. CO₂, HCN, and BeCl₂ all have 180° bond angles.
What is VSEPR theory?
VSEPR uses the simple idea that electron clouds repel each other to predict geometry. Count the electron groups (bonding + lone pairs) around the central atom, then determine the shape that maximizes their spacing.
What is a lone pair?
Lone pairs are non-bonding electron pairs on the central atom. They are invisible in the molecular structure but still repel bonding pairs, distorting the shape. Oxygen in water has 2 lone pairs.
What is the difference between electron geometry and molecular geometry?
For ammonia: electron geometry is tetrahedral (4 electron groups). But one position is a lone pair we can't 'see,' so molecular geometry is trigonal pyramidal. Electron geometry determines the molecular geometry.
What is a trigonal bipyramidal shape?
Trigonal bipyramidal occurs with 5 electron groups. Three equatorial positions form a triangle (120° apart), and two axial positions are above and below (90° from equatorial). PCl₅ and PF₅ are examples.
What is an octahedral shape?
Six electron pairs point to the vertices of an octahedron — imagine a square with one atom above and one below. All bond angles are 90°. SF₆ is the classic example.
What is a seesaw molecular shape?
In a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement, the lone pair occupies an equatorial position (less repulsion). The remaining 4 bonds form a seesaw shape with SF₄ as the classic example.
What is a T-shaped molecule?
With 2 equatorial lone pairs in a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement, the 3 remaining bonding pairs form a T shape with angles less than 90°. ClF₃ and BrF₃ are examples.
What is a square planar shape?
In octahedral geometry with 2 lone pairs, the lone pairs go in opposite positions (trans) to minimize repulsion, leaving 4 bonds in a flat square plane at 90° angles.

    [ ] Trigonal planar
    [ ] Linear
    Explanation: Four electron pairs maximize distance by pointing to the corners of a tetrahedron, giving bond angles of 109.5°. Methane (CH₄) is the classic example.

  Q3. A molecule with 2 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs has what shape?
    [ ] Trigonal planar
    [ ] Bent
    [*] Linear
    [ ] Tetrahedral
    Explanation: Two bonding pairs repel to opposite sides of the central atom, forming a straight line with 180° bond angle. Examples: CO₂ (O=C=O) and BeCl₂.

  Q4. A molecule with 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair has what shape?
    [ ] Linear
    [ ] Tetrahedral
    [ ] Trigonal planar
    [*] Trigonal pyramidal
    Explanation: The electron geometry is tetrahedral (4 pairs total), but one position is a lone pair. The visible molecular shape is trigonal pyramidal with ~107° bond angles. NH₃ is the classic example.

  Q5. The bond angle in a tetrahedral molecule is approximately:
    [ ] 120°
    [ ] 180°
    [*] 109.5°
    [ ] 90°
    Explanation: The tetrahedral angle of 109.5° is the angle that maximizes the distance between 4 points on a sphere. It appears throughout organic chemistry — every sp³ carbon has approximately this angle.

  Q6. Lone pairs of electrons affect molecular shape by:
    [ ] Adding to the bond angle by increasing electron density between atoms
    [*] Pushing bonding pairs closer together, compressing bond angles
    [ ] Making bonds longer by stretching the distance between bonded nuclei
    [ ] Having no effect on the molecular shape or any bond angles whatsoever
    Explanation: Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs (they spread out more). In water, 2 lone pairs push the H-O-H bond angle from tetrahedral 109.5° down to 104.5°.

  Q7. Water (H₂O) has what molecular shape?
    [*] Bent
    [ ] Trigonal planar
    [ ] Tetrahedral
    [ ] Linear
    Explanation: Water has 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs. The electron geometry is tetrahedral, but the molecular shape (what you can see) is bent at ~104.5°. The 2 lone pairs compress the bond angle below 109.5°.

  Q8. A molecule with 3 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs has what shape?
    [ ] Trigonal pyramidal
    [ ] Tetrahedral
    [ ] Bent
    [*] Trigonal planar
    Explanation: Three bonding pairs arrange in a flat triangle with 120° bond angles. BF₃ is a classic trigonal planar molecule with all atoms in the same plane.

  Q9. What is the electron geometry of water?
    [*] Tetrahedral — 4 total electron pairs surround oxygen
    [ ] Bent — 2 bonding pairs push to opposite sides of oxygen
    [ ] Trigonal planar — 3 electron pairs in a flat plane
    [ ] Linear — 2 bonds form a straight line through oxygen
    Explanation: Electron geometry counts ALL electron pairs (bonding + lone). Water has 4 pairs around O (2 bonding, 2 lone), so its electron geometry is tetrahedral. The molecular shape (bent) only describes the atom positions.

  Q10. What is the bond angle in a linear molecule?
    [ ] 109.5°
    [*] 180°
    [ ] 90°
    [ ] 120°
    Explanation: In a linear arrangement, atoms form a straight line with maximum separation. CO₂, HCN, and BeCl₂ all have 180° bond angles.

  Q11. What is VSEPR theory?
    [ ] A method for calculating atomic mass based on isotope abundance and nuclear binding energy
    [*] A model that predicts molecular shapes based on the principle that electron pairs repel each other and arrange to minimize repulsion
    [ ] A theory about electron speed that predicts how fast electrons orbit the nucleus of an atom
    [ ] A model for nuclear structure that describes the arrangement of protons and neutrons in shells
    Explanation: VSEPR uses the simple idea that electron clouds repel each other to predict geometry. Count the electron groups (bonding + lone pairs) around the central atom, then determine the shape that maximizes their spacing.

  Q12. What is a lone pair?
    [ ] Core electrons found in the inner shells that don’t participate in bonding or molecular geometry
    [ ] A single unpaired electron that occupies an orbital by itself and creates a radical species
    [ ] Electrons shared between atoms in a covalent bond that contribute to the bonding framework
    [*] A pair of valence electrons not shared in bonding that takes up space around the central atom
    Explanation: Lone pairs are non-bonding electron pairs on the central atom. They are invisible in the molecular structure but still repel bonding pairs, distorting the shape. Oxygen in water has 2 lone pairs.

  Q13. What is the difference between electron geometry and molecular geometry?
    [ ] Molecular geometry counts all electron pairs including both bonding and lone pairs around the atom
    [*] Electron geometry counts all electron pairs; molecular geometry only describes the positions of atoms (ignoring lone pairs)
    [ ] Electron geometry ignores lone pairs and only considers the positions of bonding electron groups
    [ ] They are the same thing — both terms describe the arrangement of atoms in a molecule identically
    Explanation: For ammonia: electron geometry is tetrahedral (4 electron groups). But one position is a lone pair we can't 'see,' so molecular geometry is trigonal pyramidal. Electron geometry determines the molecular geometry.

  Q14. What is a trigonal bipyramidal shape?
    [*] A 3D shape with 5 bonding pairs, having axial positions (180°) and equatorial positions (120°), like PCl₅
    [ ] A flat triangle with 3 bonding pairs in one plane at 120° bond angles, like BF₃
    [ ] An octahedral shape with 6 bonding pairs at 90° angles around a central atom, like SF₆
    [ ] A shape with 4 bonding pairs in a tetrahedron at 109.5° angles, like CH₄
    Explanation: Trigonal bipyramidal occurs with 5 electron groups. Three equatorial positions form a triangle (120° apart), and two axial positions are above and below (90° from equatorial). PCl₅ and PF₅ are examples.

  Q15. What is an octahedral shape?
    [ ] A tetrahedral with extra pairs distorting the 109.5° bond angles to produce a bent shape
    [ ] A flat hexagon with 6 bonding pairs arranged in a plane with 60° angles between each bond
    [*] A 3D shape with 6 bonding pairs arranged at 90° angles, like SF₆
    [ ] Any 8-sided shape with pairs at all vertices, regardless of whether lone pairs are present
    Explanation: Six electron pairs point to the vertices of an octahedron — imagine a square with one atom above and one below. All bond angles are 90°. SF₆ is the classic example.

  Q16. What is a seesaw molecular shape?
    [*] A shape from 4 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair on a trigonal bipyramidal electron geometry (e.g., SF₄)
    [ ] A shape with 6 bonding pairs in an octahedral arrangement at 90° bond angles around the center
    [ ] A synonym for linear geometry with 2 bonding pairs at 180° on opposite sides of the atom
    [ ] A perfectly flat molecule with all atoms in one plane and 120° bond angles between them
    Explanation: In a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement, the lone pair occupies an equatorial position (less repulsion). The remaining 4 bonds form a seesaw shape with SF₄ as the classic example.

  Q17. What is a T-shaped molecule?
    [*] A shape from 3 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs on a trigonal bipyramidal electron geometry (e.g., ClF₃)
    [ ] A molecule shaped like the letter H with 4 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair on the central atom
    [ ] A tetrahedral shape with 4 bonding pairs at 109.5° angles and no lone pairs on the center
    [ ] A planar square with 4 bonding pairs at 90° and 2 lone pairs above and below the plane
    Explanation: With 2 equatorial lone pairs in a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement, the 3 remaining bonding pairs form a T shape with angles less than 90°. ClF₃ and BrF₃ are examples.

  Q18. What is a square planar shape?
    [*] A flat shape with 4 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs from an octahedral electron geometry (e.g., XeF₄)
    [ ] A tetrahedron with 4 bonding pairs at 109.5° angles and no lone pairs on the central atom
    [ ] A molecule with 6 bonds all in the same plane forming a hexagonal arrangement at 60° angles
    [ ] A perfect cube shape with 8 bonding pairs at the corners and 90° angles between all bonds
    Explanation: In octahedral geometry with 2 lone pairs, the lone pairs go in opposite positions (trans) to minimize repulsion, leaving 4 bonds in a flat square plane at 90° angles.

  Q19. Why do lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs?
    [ ] Lone pairs have more electrons (3 per pair) compared to bonding pairs that only have 2 each
    [*] Lone pairs are held closer to the central atom and spread over a larger volume, creating more repulsion
    [ ] Bonding electrons have no charge so they cannot repel other electron pairs in the molecule
    [ ] Lone pairs don’t actually repel — they only affect the color of the molecule not its shape
    Explanation: Bonding pairs are stretched between two nuclei, concentrated in a narrow region. Lone pairs are attracted to only one nucleus and spread out more, occupying a larger angular space and pushing harder on neighboring pairs.

  Q20. Double and triple bonds are treated as how many electron groups in VSEPR?
    [ ] Two for double, three for triple
    [*] One — multiple bonds count as a single electron group
    [ ] They cannot be counted
    [ ] Zero
    Explanation: VSEPR counts electron 'groups' (regions of electron density), not individual pairs. A C=O double bond is one group, a C≡N triple bond is one group. This is why CO₂ has 2 groups and is linear.

  Q21. A student draws CO₂ and predicts it is bent because oxygen is electronegative. Using VSEPR, why is this wrong?
    [ ] CO₂ is actually bent because oxygen’s electronegativity pulls the bonds into a V-shape
    [ ] Carbon has lone pairs that push the O atoms below 120°, creating a trigonal pyramidal shape

    [ ] VSEPR doesn’t apply to CO₂ because it contains double bonds between the carbon and oxygens

    [ ] Both should be linear because they each have exactly 2 bonded atoms and belong to the same group
    [ ] CO₂ is heavier than water so gravity pulls its atoms into a straighter arrangement than H₂O
    [ ] Water has more atoms than CO₂ so the extra atoms force the molecule into a nonlinear geometry

    Explanation: H₂O: 4 electron groups (2 bonding + 2 lone) → tetrahedral electron geometry → bent molecular shape (104.5°). CO₂: 2 electron groups (2 double bonds, 0 lone) → linear (180°). The presence of lone pairs changes everything.

  Q23. A pharmaceutical chemist needs to determine the shape of ammonia (NH₃) for drug design. VSEPR predicts:
    [*] Trigonal pyramidal — 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair on nitrogen give this shape with ~107° bond angles
    [ ] Bent — 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs on nitrogen give this shape with ~104° bond angles

    [ ] Tetrahedral with approximately 109.5° bond angles between all atoms
    [ ] Linear with 180° bond angles between hydrogen and oxygen atoms

--- Lesson 4.9: Suborbital Shapes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Suborbital Shapes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a node in an orbital?
Nodes are surfaces where the wave function (ψ) passes through zero. A 2p orbital has a nodal plane through the nucleus. Radial nodes are spherical shells; angular nodes are planes or cones.
What is an orbital?
Unlike Bohr's orbits (fixed circular paths), quantum mechanical orbitals are probability clouds. The boundary surface shown in textbooks typically encloses 90% of the electron's probability density.
What is a radial node?
The number of radial nodes = n - l - 1 (where l is the angular momentum quantum number). A 2s orbital has 1 radial node (a spherical shell); the probability is zero at that specific radius.
What is an angular node?
Angular nodes = l. An s orbital (l=0) has 0 angular nodes. A p orbital (l=1) has 1 angular nodal plane. A d orbital (l=2) has 2 angular nodes. They determine the orbital's shape.
What is the principal quantum number (n)?
n determines energy and size: higher n = more energy = larger orbital. n=1 has only 1s. n=2 has 2s and 2p. n=3 has 3s, 3p, and 3d. The number of possible subshells equals n.
What is the angular momentum quantum number (l)?
l ranges from 0 to n-1. For n=3: l can be 0 (3s, spherical), 1 (3p, dumbbell), or 2 (3d, cloverleaf). The letter designations (s, p, d, f) come from spectroscopic terms.
What is the magnetic quantum number (ml)?
For l=1 (p): ml = -1, 0, +1. These three values correspond to px, py, pz orientations. For l=2 (d): ml = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2 = 5 orientations. Total orbitals = 2l + 1.
What is orbital degeneracy?
In an isolated atom, all three 2p orbitals have the same energy — they are degenerate. In a molecule or crystal field, this degeneracy can be broken, causing energy splitting (key in transition metal chemistry).
What is the relationship between orbital shape and bonding?
Head-on overlap (s-s, s-p, p-p) forms sigma (σ) bonds. Side-by-side p overlap forms pi (π) bonds. A double bond = 1σ + 1π; a triple bond = 1σ + 2π. Orbital geometry controls molecular geometry.

    [ ] Dumbbell
    [*] Cloverleaf
    [ ] Pyramid
    [ ] Spherical
    Explanation: Four of the five d orbitals have a four-lobed cloverleaf shape. The fifth (dz²) has a unique dumbbell-with-donut shape — a dumbbell along the z-axis surrounded by a torus in the xy-plane.

    [ ] 7

    [ ] 5
    [ ] 1

    [*] The x, y, and z axes
    [ ] The nucleus
    [ ] Random directions
    [ ] Only the x axis

    [ ] Decreases
    [ ] Stays the same
    [*] Increases
    [ ] Becomes zero

    [ ] More protons within the atomic nucleus
    [ ] Lower energy levels (smaller quantum number)

    [ ] 3
    [ ] 1

    [ ] 9
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 5

    [ ] The center of the orbital
    [ ] Where electrons are most likely to be found
    [*] A region where the probability of finding an electron is zero
    [ ] The edge of the atom
    Explanation: Nodes are surfaces where the wave function (ψ) passes through zero. A 2p orbital has a nodal plane through the nucleus. Radial nodes are spherical shells; angular nodes are planes or cones.

    [ ] A particle inside the nucleus that contributes to atomic mass and nuclear stability of the atom
    [ ] A circular path electrons follow around the nucleus at a fixed radius as described by the Bohr model
    [ ] A type of energy emitted when electrons transition between two different shells inside of the atom
    [*] A 3D region of space where there is a high probability (typically 90%) of finding an electron
    Explanation: Unlike Bohr's orbits (fixed circular paths), quantum mechanical orbitals are probability clouds. The boundary surface shown in textbooks typically encloses 90% of the electron's probability density.

    [ ] A point at the edge of the orbital boundary surface where probability is at its maximum value

    Explanation: n determines energy and size: higher n = more energy = larger orbital. n=1 has only 1s. n=2 has 2s and 2p. n=3 has 3s, 3p, and 3d. The number of possible subshells equals n.

    [ ] The number of electrons in an orbital, which is always limited to a maximum of 2 by the Pauli rule

    [*] The number that defines the shape of an orbital: l=0 (s), l=1 (p), l=2 (d), l=3 (f)

    [ ] Orbital shapes don’t affect bonding because bonds depend only on electronegativity differences

    Explanation: Both are dumbbell-shaped (l=1 for all p orbitals). The 3p has 1 radial node (n-l-1 = 3-1-1 = 1) while 2p has 0 radial nodes. Higher n means more energy, larger size, and more nodes.

  Q21. A chemistry student draws the 1s orbital as a flat circle. Why is this incorrect?
    [ ] A flat circle is correct because all s orbitals are two-dimensional rings around the nucleus
    [ ] The 1s orbital doesn’t have a shape because it is the lowest energy level with no defined geometry
    [*] The 1s orbital is a 3D sphere — representing it as a flat circle loses the spatial information needed to understand bonding
    [ ] The 1s orbital is a cube because quantum mechanics predicts right-angled probability distributions
    Explanation: Orbitals are 3D probability distributions. Textbook 2D representations are cross-sections. The actual 1s orbital is a sphere, and understanding this 3D nature is crucial for predicting molecular geometry and bond overlap.

  Q22. A student must determine the total number of nodes in a 3d orbital. The answer is:
    [ ] 0 — 3d orbitals have no nodes because the d sublevel is the lowest energy within the third shell
    [*] 2 — total nodes = n-1 = 3-1 = 2 (both are angular nodes since l=2)
    [ ] 5 — total nodes equal l squared, so for d orbitals with l=2 the total is 2 squared plus one
    [ ] 3 — total nodes equal n, so for the third energy level there must be exactly three nodal surfaces
    Explanation: Total nodes = n - 1 = 2. Angular nodes = l = 2. Radial nodes = n - l - 1 = 0. So a 3d orbital has 2 angular nodal surfaces and 0 radial nodes. The cloverleaf shape comes from these 2 angular nodes.

  Q23. A materials scientist uses d orbitals to explain why transition metal compounds are colored. The key orbital property is:
    [ ] Only f orbitals produce color because they have the most complex shapes and largest energy gaps
    [*] d orbital degeneracy can be split by ligands, and electrons jumping between split d levels absorb specific light wavelengths
    [ ] d orbitals emit light spontaneously without any external energy input from photons or heat
    [ ] d orbitals are always colored regardless of whether the sublevel is split by surrounding ligands
    Explanation: In crystal field theory, surrounding ligands split the 5 degenerate d orbitals into higher and lower energy groups. Electrons absorb photons matching this energy gap. The remaining wavelengths give the compound its color.

  Q24. A biochemistry student asks why carbon always forms 4 bonds. The orbital explanation involves:
    [*] Carbon's 2s and three 2p orbitals hybridize to form 4 equivalent sp³ orbitals, each pointing to a corner of a tetrahedron
    [ ] Carbon forms only 2 bonds because it has 2 unpaired electrons in its ground state 2p sublevel
    [ ] Carbon only has s orbitals available for bonding since its p orbitals are too high in energy
    [ ] Carbon has d orbitals available for bonding which expand its capacity beyond the octet rule
    Explanation: In methane (CH₄), one 2s and three 2p orbitals mix to create four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals at 109.5°. This explains carbon's tetrahedral geometry and why it consistently forms 4 bonds in organic molecules.

  Q25. A researcher explains that MRI machines detect hydrogen nuclei based on their electron orbital environment. This is possible because:
    [ ] Orbital shapes don’t affect magnetic properties because magnetism depends only on nuclear spin
    [ ] MRI detects orbital shapes directly by bouncing radio waves off the electron probability clouds
    [*] The shape and density of surrounding orbitals create slightly different magnetic environments for different hydrogen atoms
    [ ] All hydrogen atoms look identical in MRI scans because they all have the same 1s orbital type
    Explanation: Electrons in nearby orbitals shield hydrogen nuclei from the external magnetic field. The orbital type (s vs p) and proximity determine the degree of shielding, causing different resonance frequencies — this is the basis of NMR/MRI.

  Q26. An advanced student asks why helium (1s²) is unreactive while hydrogen (1s¹) is reactive, if both use the same orbital shape. The answer is:
    [*] Helium's 1s orbital is completely filled — a full orbital is low-energy and stable, with no tendency to form bonds
    [ ] Helium has a different orbital shape than hydrogen — a dumbbell instead of a sphere for its 1s
    [ ] Both are equally reactive because they occupy the same s orbital type in the first energy level
    [ ] Hydrogen’s s orbital is larger than helium’s, which is why hydrogen has a lower ionization energy
    Explanation: Hydrogen's half-filled 1s¹ gains stability by sharing electrons in bonds. Helium's full 1s² has no energetic benefit from bonding — adding to a bonding orbital is offset by populating the antibonding orbital.

  Q27. A photochemist studies why π bonds absorb UV light more easily than σ bonds. The orbital shapes involved are:
    [*] π bonds involve side-by-side p orbital overlap with a smaller energy gap to antibonding orbitals, requiring less energy (longer wavelength) to excite
    [ ] π bonds don’t absorb light at all because side-by-side overlap creates a node in the bonding region
    [ ] σ bonds involve p orbitals exclusively while π bonds involve only s orbitals in head-on overlap
    [ ] All bonds absorb the same wavelength because orbital overlap type has no effect on energy gaps
    Explanation: Side-by-side p overlap (π) is weaker than head-on overlap (σ), so the π→π* energy gap is smaller than σ→σ*. This means π electrons can be excited by lower-energy UV photons, which is why conjugated molecules absorb in the UV-visible range.

  Q28. A nanotechnology researcher uses quantum dots where electron confinement changes energy levels. As dot size decreases, the relationship between orbital size and energy means:
    [*] Smaller dots confine electrons to smaller orbitals with higher energy, shifting emitted light toward shorter (bluer) wavelengths
    [ ] Smaller dots emit red light because confining electrons lowers their energy and increases wavelength
    [ ] Larger dots emit blue light because having more atoms increases the energy of emitted photons
    [ ] Dot size doesn’t affect energy because quantum confinement only changes the shape not the levels
    Explanation: Quantum confinement forces electrons into smaller regions (smaller 'orbitals'), increasing the energy gap between levels. Larger gap = higher energy photons = shorter wavelength = bluer light. This is why QD size controls color.

  Q29. A student calculates that a 4f orbital has ___ total nodes:
    [ ] 4 — total nodes equal n, so for the fourth energy level there must be exactly four nodal surfaces
    [ ] 7 — total nodes equal the number of orbitals in the sublevel, and f has seven individual orbitals
    [*] 3 — total nodes = n-1 = 4-1 = 3 (all 3 are angular nodes, 0 radial)
    [ ] 0 — f orbitals have no nodes because their complex shapes fill all of the surrounding space fully
    Explanation: For 4f: n=4, l=3. Total nodes = n-1 = 3. Angular nodes = l = 3. Radial nodes = n-l-1 = 0. The 3 angular nodes create the complex multi-lobed shapes characteristic of f orbitals.

  Q30. A forensic chemist uses atomic emission spectroscopy to identify elements in evidence. Each element has unique emission lines because:
    [*] Each element has unique orbital sizes and energy spacings, so electrons emit photons at characteristic wavelengths when returning to lower orbitals
    [ ] All elements emit the same color because orbital energy spacings are identical across the table
    [ ] Emission has nothing to do with orbitals — it comes from nuclear vibrations releasing thermal energy
    [ ] Only f orbital transitions produce light because they have the largest energy gaps between levels
    Explanation: Each element has a unique nuclear charge, creating unique orbital energy levels. When excited electrons fall from higher to lower orbitals, they emit photons with element-specific wavelengths — an atomic fingerprint.
