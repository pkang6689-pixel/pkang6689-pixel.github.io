--- Lesson 11.1:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heat Conversion
Core Definitions

  • Heat (q): Energy transferred between objects due to a temperature difference, measured in joules (J) or calories (cal).
  • Joule (J): The SI unit of energy. 1 calorie = 4.184 joules.
  • Calorie (cal): The amount of energy needed to raise 1 g of water by 1°C.
  • Kilocalorie (kcal): 1 kcal = 1000 cal = 1 food Calorie (Cal).

--- Lesson 11.2:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Specific Heat
Core Definitions

  • Specific Heat (c): The amount of energy needed to raise 1 gram of a substance by 1°C.
  • Formula: q = mcΔT, where q = heat, m = mass, c = specific heat, ΔT = change in temperature.
  • Water's Specific Heat: 4.184 J/g°C — very high, which is why water heats and cools slowly.
  • Units: J/(g·°C) or cal/(g·°C).

--- Lesson 11.3:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heat Capacity
Core Definitions

  • Heat Capacity (C): The amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of an entire object by 1°C.
  • Difference from Specific Heat: Heat capacity depends on total mass; specific heat is per gram.
  • Formula: q = CΔT, where C = heat capacity of the object.
  • Relationship: C = m × c (heat capacity = mass × specific heat).

--- Lesson 11.4:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Calorimetry
Core Definitions

  • Calorimetry: The measurement of heat changes during chemical or physical processes.
  • Calorimeter: A device used to measure heat exchange. A simple version is a coffee-cup calorimeter.
  • Principle: q_lost = −q_gained. Heat lost by a hot object equals heat gained by a cold object.
  • Bomb Calorimeter: Used for combustion reactions; measures heat at constant volume.

--- Lesson 11.6:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hess's Law
Core Definitions

  • Hess's Law: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the pathway taken.
  • Application: Add ΔH values of individual steps to find the overall ΔH of a reaction.
  • Reversing a Reaction: Changes the sign of ΔH. If forward is −100 kJ, reverse is +100 kJ.
  • Multiplying a Reaction: Multiply ΔH by the same factor. If doubled, ΔH doubles.
