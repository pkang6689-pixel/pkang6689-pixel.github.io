--- Lesson 11.3: Heat Capacity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heat Capacity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the total heat capacity of a 1 kg block of aluminum (c = 0.900 J/g·°C)?
C = mc = 1000 g × 0.900 J/g·°C = 900 J/°C.
A 100 g sample of unknown metal absorbs 500 J and its temperature rises from 20°C to 45°C. What is specific heat?
ΔT = 45−20 = 25°C; c = q/(mΔT) = 500/(100 × 25) = 500/2500 = 0.2 J/g·°C.

--- Lesson 11.5: Enthalpy, Entropy, Free Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Enthalpy, Entropy, Free Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between ΔG° and equilibrium?
ΔG (under actual conditions) = 0 at equilibrium. ΔG° (standard) determines K via ΔG° = −RT ln K. A negative ΔG° means K > 1 (products favored at equilibrium).

    [*] ΔG = ΔH − TΔS
    Explanation: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Gibbs free energy combines enthalpy and entropy to predict spontaneity.

  Q16. At equilibrium, ΔG =
    [ ] Negative
    [ ] Positive
    [ ] 1
    [*] 0
    Explanation: At equilibrium, the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; the system is at minimum Gibbs energy — ΔG = 0.

  Q17. An endothermic reaction (ΔH > 0) becomes spontaneous at high temperature when:
    [ ] ΔS < 0 (entropy decreases, making the reaction less favorable)
    [ ] ΔH becomes negative at higher temperatures due to increased energy
    [*] ΔS > 0 and TΔS term overcomes ΔH (ΔG = ΔH − TΔS < 0 at high T)
    [ ] Temperature has no effect on spontaneity because ΔG depends only on ΔH
    Explanation: If ΔS > 0, then at high T, TΔS > ΔH, making ΔG = ΔH − TΔS negative → spontaneous.

  Q18. The standard free energy change (ΔG°) is measured at:
    [ ] 0°C and 1 atm (STP)
    [ ] Any temperature
    [*] 25°C (298 K) and 1 atm (standard conditions)
    [ ] 100°C and 1 atm
    Explanation: Standard conditions for thermodynamics: 25°C (298 K), 1 bar (≈1 atm), 1 M concentrations.

  Q19. ΔG° is related to the equilibrium constant K by:
    [*] ΔG° = −RT ln K
    [ ] ΔG° = K/RT
    [ ] ΔG° = ln K
    [ ] ΔG° = RT ln K
    Explanation: The relationship ΔG° = −RT ln K connects thermodynamics to equilibrium: negative ΔG° → K > 1 (products favored).

  Q20. For a reaction with K = 1, ΔG° =
    [*] 0
    [ ] Cannot be determined
    [ ] Negative
    [ ] Positive
    Explanation: ΔG° = −RT ln K. If K = 1, ln(1) = 0, so ΔG° = 0.

  Q21. When ice melts at 0°C under standard conditions, ΔG =
    [ ] Positive (melting is non-spontaneous at this temperature)
    [ ] Negative (melting is fully spontaneous at this temperature)
    [ ] Undefined (cannot be calculated at a phase transition)
    [*] 0 (at the melting point, ice and water are in equilibrium)
    Explanation: At exactly 0°C (melting point), ice and water are in equilibrium → ΔG = 0.

  Q22. Which change increases entropy the most?
    [ ] Gas → liquid
    [*] Solid → gas (sublimation)
    [ ] Liquid → solid
    [ ] Solid → liquid
    Explanation: The solid→gas transition has the largest entropy increase; gas has far more disorder than solid.

  Q23. Dissolving a salt in water generally:
    [ ] Has no effect on entropy
    [*] Increases entropy (ions become dispersed in solution)
    [ ] Decreases enthalpy only
    [ ] Decreases entropy
    Explanation: When a salt dissolves, ordered lattice → dispersed ions in solution → more microstates → entropy increases.

  Q24. The standard enthalpy of combustion of methane is −890 kJ/mol. This means:
    [ ] ΔH is positive
    [ ] 890 kJ is needed to burn methane
    [*] 890 kJ of heat is released per mole of CH₄ burned
    [ ] Methane absorbs 890 kJ from surroundings
    Explanation: Negative ΔH = heat released. 890 kJ released per mole of CH₄ combusted: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O.

  Q25. The relationship between ΔG, ΔH, ΔS: if ΔH and ΔS both are negative, the reaction is:
    [ ] Always spontaneous
    [*] Spontaneous at low T (when −TΔS penalty is small)
    [ ] Spontaneous at high T
    [ ] Never spontaneous
    Explanation: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Both ΔH and ΔS negative: ΔG = (negative) − T(negative) = (neg) + T(pos). At low T, the negative ΔH dominates → ΔG < 0 (spontaneous).

  Q26. Free energy is 'free' in the sense that it is:
    [ ] Heat energy only
    [*] Energy available to do useful work
    [ ] Energy that does not cost anything
    [ ] Energy at zero temperature
    Explanation: 'Free energy' is the maximum energy available to do useful work under constant T and P conditions.

  Q27. Combustion of glucose (exothermic, ΔH < 0; ΔS > 0 because gas CO₂ is produced) is:
    [ ] Only spontaneous below 0°C
    [*] Spontaneous at all temperatures
    [ ] Non-spontaneous
    [ ] Only spontaneous above 100°C
    Explanation: ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0 → ΔG = ΔH − TΔS is always negative regardless of T → always spontaneous.

  Q28. A reaction has ΔG = +50 kJ/mol. This means:
    [*] The reaction is non-spontaneous as written
    [ ] Equilibrium favors products
    [ ] The reaction occurs quickly
    [ ] Products are favored
    Explanation: ΔG > 0 means the reaction is not spontaneous in the forward direction; the reverse reaction is favored under these conditions.

  Q29. What is the relationship between ΔG° and equilibrium?
    [ ] ΔG° < 0 means equilibrium favors neither side and reactants and products are present in equal amounts
    [ ] K = ΔG° (the equilibrium constant is numerically equal to the standard free energy change)
    [*] ΔG° = 0 only at equilibrium doesn't hold; ΔG (not ΔG°) = 0 at equilibrium. ΔG° determines K.
    [ ] ΔG° = 0 at all equilibria regardless of the specific reaction or temperature conditions involved
    Explanation: ΔG (under actual conditions) = 0 at equilibrium. ΔG° (standard) determines K via ΔG° = −RT ln K. A negative ΔG° means K > 1 (products favored at equilibrium).

  Q30. The third law of thermodynamics states:
    [ ] Entropy of the universe always increases
    [*] The entropy of a perfect crystal at absolute zero is zero
    [ ] Energy is conserved
    [ ] Free energy is always negative
    Explanation: The third law: S = 0 at T = 0 K for a perfect crystal. This provides an absolute reference point for entropy values.

    Explanation: Core Definitions Hess's Law : The total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the pathway taken.     [ ] Random variable

Core Definitions Hess's Law : The total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the pathway taken. The other options — 'Subtract all values', 'Ignore intermediate steps', and 'Only use one reaction' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q4. If a reaction is reversed, the sign of ΔH:

The other options — 'At 0°C and 0 atm', 'At 100°C and 2 atm', and 'At any conditions' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q9. If you reverse a reaction, ΔH:
    [ ] Becomes zero

  Q14. Using Hess's Law with ΔH°f: ΔH°rxn =
    [ ] Σ ΔH°f(products) + Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
