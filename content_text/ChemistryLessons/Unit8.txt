--- Lesson 8.1:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Monatomic & Diatomic Gases
Core Definitions

  • Monatomic Gas: A gas made of single atoms (e.g., noble gases: He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn).
  • Diatomic Gas: A gas made of molecules with two atoms bonded together.
  • Seven Diatomic Elements: H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ — remember HONClBrIF.
  • Noble Gases: Monatomic; full outer shells make them stable and unreactive.
  • Diatomic in Equations: Always write diatomic elements as molecules (e.g., O₂, not O) in chemical equations.
  • Most Abundant Gas: Nitrogen (N₂) makes up about 78% of Earth's atmosphere.

--- Lesson 8.2:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Pressure & Standard Atmosphere

Core Definitions

  • Pressure: Force per unit area exerted by gas particles colliding with container walls.
  • Standard Atmosphere (atm): 1 atm = 101.325 kPa = 760 mmHg = 760 torr.
  • Pascal (Pa): The SI unit of pressure; 1 kPa = 1000 Pa.
  • Barometer: An instrument that measures atmospheric pressure.
  • Manometer: An instrument that measures the pressure of a gas in a container.
  • STP: Standard Temperature and Pressure: 0°C (273 K) and 1 atm.

--- Lesson 8.3:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Kinetic Molecular Theory
Core Definitions

  • KMT Assumption 1: Gas particles are in constant, random, straight-line motion.
  • KMT Assumption 2: Gas particles have negligible volume compared to the container.
  • KMT Assumption 3: There are no attractive or repulsive forces between gas particles.
  • KMT Assumption 4: Collisions between gas particles are perfectly elastic (no energy lost).
  • KMT Assumption 5: The average kinetic energy is proportional to absolute temperature (K).
  • Ideal Gas: A hypothetical gas that perfectly follows all KMT assumptions.

--- Lesson 8.4:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Boyle's Law
Core Definitions

  • Boyle's Law: At constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂.
  • Inverse Relationship: As pressure increases, volume decreases (and vice versa).
  • Constant Temperature: Boyle's Law only applies when temperature does not change.
  • Example: Squeezing a balloon: increased pressure → decreased volume.
  • Graph: P vs. V graph is a downward curve (hyperbola).
  • Units: Pressure and volume can use any units as long as they match on both sides.

--- Lesson 8.5:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Charles' Law
Core Definitions

  • Charles' Law: At constant pressure, volume and temperature are directly proportional: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂.
  • Direct Relationship: As temperature increases, volume increases (and vice versa).
  • Constant Pressure: Charles' Law only applies when pressure does not change.
  • Temperature in Kelvin: Always use Kelvin for gas law calculations (K = °C + 273).
  • Absolute Zero: 0 K (−273°C); theoretically, gas volume would be zero.
  • Example: A hot air balloon rises because heated air expands (greater volume, same pressure).

--- Lesson 8.6:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gay-Lussac's Law
Core Definitions

  • Gay-Lussac's Law: At constant volume, pressure and temperature are directly proportional: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂.
  • Direct Relationship: As temperature increases, pressure increases (and vice versa).
  • Constant Volume: Applies to rigid containers where volume cannot change.
  • Temperature in Kelvin: Always use Kelvin for gas law calculations.
  • Example: A tire's pressure increases on a hot day (same volume, higher temperature).
  • Pressure Cooker: Sealed container: increasing temperature raises pressure, cooking food faster.

--- Lesson 8.7:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Combined Gas Law
Core Definitions

  • Combined Gas Law: Combines Boyle's, Charles', and Gay-Lussac's: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂.
  • When to Use: When pressure, volume, AND temperature all change (nothing held constant except moles).
  • Special Cases: Hold T constant → Boyle's; Hold P constant → Charles'; Hold V constant → Gay-Lussac's.
  • Temperature: Must always be in Kelvin.
  • Solving: Identify known values, solve for the unknown variable algebraically.
  • Units: Pressure and volume units must be consistent on both sides.

--- Lesson 8.8:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ideal Gas Law
Core Definitions

  • Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT; relates pressure, volume, moles, and temperature.
  • R (Gas Constant): 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) or 8.314 J/(mol·K).
  • n: The number of moles of gas.
  • When to Use: When you need to find P, V, n, or T and have the other three values.
  • Ideal vs. Real: Ideal gases perfectly follow PV = nRT; real gases deviate at high P and low T.
  • Units Must Match R: If R = 0.0821, use atm, L, mol, and K.

--- Lesson 8.9:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Real Gases & Deviations

Core Definitions

  • Real Gases: Actual gases that deviate from ideal behavior, especially at high pressure and low temperature.
  • Intermolecular Forces: Real gas particles DO attract each other, unlike ideal gas assumptions.
  • Particle Volume: Real gas particles DO occupy space, unlike the ideal assumption of negligible volume.
  • High Pressure: Particles are forced close together; volume and intermolecular forces become significant.
  • Low Temperature: Particles move slowly; attractive forces have greater effect, causing deviation.
  • Most Ideal Behavior: Gases behave most ideally at HIGH temperature and LOW pressure.
