--- Lesson 10.1: Acid & Base Properties (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acid & Base Properties
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Real-World Applications
Example: Why do carbonated beverages taste slightly acidic?
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid), which partially ionizes to give H⁺, making carbonated beverages slightly acidic (pH ~4–5).

--- Lesson 10.1: Acid & Base Properties (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Acids taste:
    [ ] Sweet
    [*] Sour
    [ ] Salty
    [ ] Bitter
    Explanation: Core Definitions Acid — A substance that produces H⁺ (hydrogen) ions in solution; tastes sour; pH below 7. The other options — 'Salty', 'Bitter', and 'Sweet' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q2. Bases taste:
    [ ] No taste
    [ ] Sour
    [ ] Sweet
    [*] Bitter
    Explanation: Base — A substance that produces OH⁻ (hydroxide) ions in solution; tastes bitter, feels slippery; pH above 7. The other options — 'Sour', 'Sweet', and 'No taste' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. Acids turn blue litmus paper:
    [ ] No change
    [*] Red
    [ ] Blue
    [ ] Green
    Explanation: Litmus paper turns red in acidic solutions (pH < 7) and blue in basic solutions (pH > 7). Other indicators like phenolphthalein change color at specific pH ranges. The answer is 'Red'. The other options — 'No change', 'Blue', and 'Green' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q4. Bases feel:
    [ ] Dry
    [ ] Gritty
    [*] Slippery
    [ ] Rough
    Explanation: Base — A substance that produces OH⁻ (hydroxide) ions in solution; tastes bitter, feels slippery; pH above 7. The other options — 'Rough', 'Gritty', and 'Dry' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q5. Acids produce what ion in water?
    [ ] Na⁺, particularly
    [*] H⁺ (hydrogen ion / hydronium)
    [ ] OH⁻ (hydroxide)
    [ ] Cl⁻, particularly
    Explanation: Arrhenius Definition — Acids produce H⁺ in water; bases produce OH⁻ in water. The other options — 'Cl⁻', 'OH⁻ (hydroxide)', and 'Na⁺' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q6. Bases produce what ion in water?
    [*] OH⁻ (hydroxide ion)
    [ ] Na⁺
    [ ] SO₄²⁻
    [ ] H⁺ (hydrogen ion)
    Explanation: Arrhenius Definition — Acids produce H⁺ in water; bases produce OH⁻ in water. The other options — 'Na⁺', 'SO₄²⁻', and 'H⁺ (hydrogen ion)' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q7. Bases turn red litmus paper:
    [ ] Yellow
    [ ] Red
    [ ] Green
    [*] Blue
    Explanation: Litmus paper turns red in acidic solutions (pH < 7) and blue in basic solutions (pH > 7). Other indicators like phenolphthalein change color at specific pH ranges. The answer is 'Blue'. The other options — 'Green', 'Red', and 'Yellow' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q8. Which ion do acids produce in aqueous solution?
    [*] H⁺ (or H₃O⁺)
    [ ] Cl⁻, part
    [ ] Na⁺, part
    [ ] OH⁻, part
    Explanation: Acids produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) or hydronium ions (H₃O⁺) when dissolved in water — a key defining property.

  Q9. Which ion do bases produce in aqueous solution?
    [ ] Cl⁻, particular
    [*] OH⁻ (hydroxide ion)
    [ ] Na⁺, particular
    [ ] H⁺, particularl
    Explanation: Bases produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in aqueous solution — the defining property of bases according to Arrhenius.

  Q10. A base that makes your hands feel slippery is because:
    [ ] It contains sodium
    [*] It reacts with oils and proteins in skin (saponification)
    [ ] It is less dense than water
    [ ] It contains a lubricant
    Explanation: Bases react with skin oils/fats in a saponification-like reaction, producing soap-like glycerol and fatty acid salts — creating the slippery feeling.

  Q11. Acids are described as corrosive because:
    [ ] They produce light
    [ ] They contain oxygen
    [*] They react with metals and tissue, causing damage
    [ ] They are always hot
    Explanation: Acids can corrode metals (react to form salts + H₂), dissolve skin tissue, and damage materials — hence 'corrosive.'

  Q12. What color does red litmus paper turn in the presence of a base?
    [*] Blue
    [ ] Red (stays)
    [ ] Green
    [ ] Yellow
    Explanation: Bases turn red litmus paper blue. Acids turn blue litmus paper red.

  Q13. Common household acids include:
    [ ] Baking soda and soap
    [ ] Bleach and ammonia
    [ ] Salt and sugar water
    [*] Vinegar (acetic acid) and lemon juice (citric acid)
    Explanation: Vinegar contains acetic acid (CH₃COOH) and lemon juice contains citric acid — both are common household acids.

  Q14. Common household bases include:
    [ ] Salt and sugar
    [ ] Vinegar and orange juice
    [ ] Carbonated water and battery acid
    [*] Baking soda, bleach, and ammonia cleaner
    Explanation: Baking soda (NaHCO₃), bleach (NaOCl solution), and ammonia-based cleaners are all basic household chemicals.

  Q15. Acids react with metals to produce:
    [ ] Water and a salt
    [*] Hydrogen gas and a salt
    [ ] Metal oxide and water
    [ ] Carbon dioxide and water
    Explanation: Acids react with active metals: Acid + Metal → Salt + H₂(g). For example, 2HCl + Zn → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑.

  Q16. Acids react with carbonates to produce:
    [*] Carbon dioxide gas, water, and a salt
    [ ] A precipitate following
    [ ] Hydrogen gas in most standard
    [ ] Only salt, particularly
    Explanation: Acid + carbonate → salt + H₂O + CO₂. For example, 2HCl + CaCO₃ → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑.

  Q17. The term 'amphoteric' describes a substance that:
    [ ] Is always neutral
    [ ] Is an acid in all reactions
    [ ] Is insoluble in water
    [*] Can act as either an acid or a base depending on the reaction
    Explanation: Amphoteric substances (like water, Al₂O₃, amino acids) can donate or accept protons depending on the reaction partner.

  Q18. Water is amphoteric because:
    [ ] It is neutral
    [ ] It contains both H⁺ and OH⁻
    [ ] It has a pH of exactly 7 always
    [*] It can donate an H⁺ (acting as acid) or accept an H⁺ (acting as base)
    Explanation: In autoionization: H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻ (one water acts as acid, one as base).

  Q19. Concentrated HCl (hydrochloric acid) is a strong acid. 'Concentrated' means:
    [ ] The acid is highly reactive in all reactions
    [*] High molarity — large amount of HCl per liter
    [ ] The acid completely ionizes
    [ ] The solution is hot
    Explanation: 'Concentrated' refers to high molarity (amount of solute per volume). 'Strong' refers to degree of ionization. These are independent properties.

  Q20. Battery acid (car battery) is primarily:
    [ ] Acetic acid
    [ ] Hydrochloric acid (HCl)
    [ ] Citric acid
    [*] Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)
    Explanation: Car batteries use sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) as the electrolyte in lead-acid batteries.

  Q21. Stings from bee venom are acidic; stings from wasps are basic. A home remedy for bee stings would be:
    [ ] Applying an acid (like vinegar)
    [ ] Applying pure water
    [ ] Applying salt
    [*] Applying a base (like baking soda)
    Explanation: Bee stings are acidic; applying a basic substance (baking soda, NaHCO₃) partially neutralizes the acid, reducing the sting.

  Q22. The sour taste of lemons is due to:
    [ ] Sugar concentration
    [ ] Sodium chloride
    [*] Citric acid
    [ ] Carbon dioxide
    Explanation: The sourness of citric fruits is due to citric acid (C₆H₈O₇), which produces H⁺ ions that stimulate sour taste receptors.

  Q23. Magnesium hydroxide [Mg(OH)₂] used in antacids works by:
    [ ] Adding more HCl to the stomach
    [*] Neutralizing stomach acid (HCl)
    [ ] Absorbing water in the stomach
    [ ] Increasing stomach acid production
    Explanation: Mg(OH)₂ (milk of magnesia) is a base that reacts with stomach HCl: Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O, reducing acid indigestion.

  Q24. Acids conduct electricity in water because:
    [ ] They are denser than water
    [*] They produce ions (H⁺ and negative ions) that carry charge
    [ ] They are polar
    [ ] They react with the electrodes
    Explanation: The ions produced when acids dissolve (H⁺ and the associated anion) can carry electrical charge, making acid solutions electrically conductive.

  Q25. Which of the following produces OH⁻ in water?
    [ ] H₂SO₄, particularl
    [ ] HCl, particularly
    [*] NaOH (sodium hydroxide)
    [ ] Acetic acid
    Explanation: NaOH is a base that completely dissociates in water: NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻.

  Q26. The 'pH' scale was developed to express:
    [ ] The molarity of any solution
    [ ] The concentration of all ions
    [ ] Temperature effects on solutions
    [*] Hydrogen ion concentration conveniently on a compact scale
    Explanation: The pH scale was created to express [H⁺] (which can range over many orders of magnitude) as a simple number from 0–14.

  Q27. Acids and bases can be hazardous in the lab because:
    [*] They can corrode skin, metals, and equipment
    [ ] They are always explosive
    [ ] They produce toxic gases in all reactions
    [ ] They are always highly flammable
    Explanation: Strong acids and bases are corrosive and can cause chemical burns, making proper safety procedures and PPE essential.

  Q28. Why do carbonated beverages taste slightly acidic?
    [ ] Carbonation creates HCl
    [ ] Sugar becomes acidic when dissolved
    [*] CO₂ dissolves in water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)
    [ ] They contain citric acid only
    Explanation: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid), which partially ionizes to give H⁺, making carbonated beverages slightly acidic (pH ~4–5).

  Q29. Which household product is the most acidic?
    [*] Battery acid (sulfuric acid, pH ~0)
    [ ] Orange juice (pH ~3.5)
    [ ] Coffee (pH ~5)
    [ ] Vinegar (pH ~3)
    Explanation: Battery acid (concentrated H₂SO₄) has pH near 0 — the most acidic common household item.

  Q30. The neutralization of an acid by a base is described as:
    [ ] A combustion reaction
    [ ] A decomposition reaction
    [ ] An oxidation-reduction reaction
    [*] An acid-base reaction producing salt and water
    Explanation: Acid + Base → Salt + Water: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. This is a neutralization reaction — the H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to form water.

--- Lesson 10.2: Binary Acids vs. Oxyacids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Binary Acids vs. Oxyacids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the correct name for H₂S?
H₂S is a binary acid: hydro + sulfur → sulfide → hydrosulfuric acid.
What is the formula for sulfurous acid?
Sulfurous acid comes from sulfite (SO₃²⁻). Adding 2H⁺ to SO₃²⁻ gives H₂SO₃.
Real-World Applications
Example: H₂SO₄ is an example of:
Oxyacid — An acid containing hydrogen, oxygen, and another element (e.g., H₂SO₄, HNO₃). Oxyacid Naming — If the polyatomic ion ends in "-ate," the acid ends in "-ic acid"; if "-ite," then "-ous acid.". The other options — 'A binary acid', 'A base', and 'A salt' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

--- Lesson 10.3: Naming Acids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Acids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the name for H₂CrO₄?
H₂CrO₄ comes from chromate (CrO₄²⁻). Chromate (-ate) → chromic acid (-ic acid).
What is the formula for selenious acid (from selenite, SeO₃²⁻)?
Selenite (SeO₃²⁻) + 2H⁺ → H₂SeO₃ (selenious acid). -ite → -ous acid.
What is the anion in hydrochloric acid (HCl) solutions?
HCl is a binary acid. In solution: HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻. The anion is chloride, not a chlorine-oxygen polyatomic ion.

--- Lesson 10.4: Identifying Acids & Bases (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Identifying Acids & Bases
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
According to the Brønsted-Lowry theory, HCl donates a proton to water. What is water acting as?
When HCl donates H⁺ to H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻, water accepts the proton — it acts as a Brønsted-Lowry base.
In HCl(aq) + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻, what is the conjugate acid-base pair?
Two conjugate pairs: HCl (acid) / Cl⁻ (conjugate base) AND H₂O (base) / H₃O⁺ (conjugate acid).

Overview

--- Lesson 10.9: pH & pOH Scale (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: pH & pOH Scale
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the pH of a solution with [H⁺] = 1×10⁻⁴ M?
pH = −log[H⁺] = −log(1×10⁻⁴) = −(−4) = 4.
A solution has pH = 9. What is [OH⁻]?
pH = 9 → pOH = 14−9 = 5 → [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁵ M.
If [H⁺] = 0.001 M, what is the pH?
pH = −log(0.001) = −log(10⁻³) = 3.
What is the pH of a 0.01 M HCl solution?
HCl is a strong acid, completely ionized: [H⁺] = 0.01 M = 10⁻² M. pH = −log(10⁻²) = 2.
What is the pOH of a 0.001 M NaOH solution?
NaOH completely dissociates: [OH⁻] = 0.001 = 10⁻³ M. pOH = −log(10⁻³) = 3.
What is [H⁺] in a solution with pH = 5?
[H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ = 10⁻⁵ M.
What is [H⁺] in a solution with pOH = 11?
pOH = 11 → pH = 3 → [H⁺] = 10⁻³ M.
