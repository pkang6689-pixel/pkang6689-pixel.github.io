--- Lesson 5B.1: Covalent Bonds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Covalent Bonds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a covalent bond?
A bond formed by the sharing of electron pairs between atoms is correct. This is a key concept in Covalent Bonds.

--- Lesson 5B.1: Covalent Bonds (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is a covalent bond?
    [ ] An attraction between ions
    [ ] A bond formed by transferring electrons
    [ ] A metallic bond
    [*] A bond formed by the sharing of electron pairs between atoms
    Explanation: A bond formed by the sharing of electron pairs between atoms is correct. This is a key concept in Covalent Bonds.

  Q2. A single covalent bond consists of:
    [ ] Two shared pairs
    [*] One shared pair of electrons (2 electrons)
    [ ] Three electrons
    [ ] One electron
    Explanation: A single bond = 1 shared electron pair = 2 electrons shared between two atoms.

  Q3. A double covalent bond consists of:
    [ ] Three shared pairs
    [ ] Two single electrons
    [*] Two shared pairs of electrons (4 electrons)
    [ ] One shared pair
    Explanation: Double bond = 2 shared pairs = 4 electrons. E.g., O=O in O₂; C=O in CO₂.

  Q4. A triple covalent bond consists of:
    [*] Three shared pairs of electrons (6 electrons)
    [ ] One shared pair
    [ ] Two shared pairs
    [ ] Three single electrons
    Explanation: Triple bond = 3 shared pairs = 6 electrons. E.g., N≡N in N₂; C≡C in alkynes.

  Q5. Which molecule has a triple bond?
    [ ] H₂O (H-O-H single bonds)
    [ ] O₂ (O=O double bond)
    [ ] CO₂ (C=O double bonds)
    [*] N₂ (N≡N)
    Explanation: N₂ has a triple bond (N≡N). Bond order = 3, very high bond energy (945 kJ/mol), making N₂ very stable and unreactive.

  Q6. In a Lewis structure, a pair of dots represents:
    [ ] A single bond
    [ ] A positive charge
    [ ] A double bond
    [*] A lone pair (non-bonding electron pair)
    Explanation: Lone pairs (non-bonding pairs) are shown as two dots (::) on atoms in Lewis structures. They satisfy the octet but are not shared.

  Q7. The VSEPR model predicts:
    [ ] Bond lengths only between atoms in the molecule
    [ ] Reaction rates for chemical processes
    [ ] Electron density maps of atomic orbitals
    [*] Molecular geometry based on minimizing repulsion between electron pairs
    Explanation: VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion): electron pairs around a central atom arrange to minimize repulsion, determining molecular shape.

  Q8. A molecule with 2 bonding pairs and no lone pairs around the central atom is:
    [ ] Bent
    [ ] Tetrahedral
    [ ] Trigonal planar
    [*] Linear (180°)
    Explanation: 2 bonding pairs, no lone pairs → linear (180°). Example: CO₂ (O=C=O, all bonding pairs around C, which has none).

  Q9. A water molecule (H₂O) is bent because:
    [ ] The bonds are ionic in nature, not covalent
    [ ] Oxygen is heavier than hydrogen atoms
    [*] The 2 lone pairs on oxygen repel the bonding pairs, bending the H-O-H angle to ~104.5°
    [ ] Hydrogen atoms repel each other directly
    Explanation: Water: O has 2 bonding pairs (to H) and 2 lone pairs. Lone pair-lone pair and lone pair-bond pair repulsion compresses the H-O-H angle to ~104.5° (bent).

  Q10. Polar covalent bonds form when:
    [ ] The atoms have no electronegativity difference
    [ ] Two identical atoms share electrons
    [ ] Electrons are transferred completely
    [*] Two atoms share electrons unequally due to electronegativity difference (0.5–1.7)
    Explanation: Polar covalent: unequal electron sharing → δ+ on less electronegative atom, δ- on more electronegative atom. E.g., H-Cl, H-O.

  Q11. Nonpolar covalent bonds form when:
    [ ] Large electronegativity difference exists
    [ ] One atom is a metal
    [*] Two atoms share electrons equally (same or similar electronegativity, ΔEN < 0.5)
    [ ] Electrons are transferred
    Explanation: Nonpolar covalent: equal sharing. Example: H-H, Cl-Cl, C-C. No partial charges.

  Q12. The bond in Cl₂ is:
    [ ] Metallic (delocalized electron sharing)
    [ ] Ionic (electron transfer between atoms)
    [*] Nonpolar covalent (two identical atoms)
    [ ] Polar covalent (unequal electron sharing)
    Explanation: Cl-Cl: identical atoms → same electronegativity → equal electron sharing → nonpolar covalent bond.

  Q13. Which is the correct Lewis structure for HCl?
    [*] H-Cl: with 3 lone pairs on Cl
    [ ] H=Cl: double bond
    [ ] H:Cl: with Cl having 3 lone pairs and H having 1 lone pair
    [ ] H-Cl-H
    Explanation: HCl: H shares 1 electron with Cl (single bond). Cl has 3 lone pairs. H has no lone pairs. Structure: H-Cl: (with 3 lone pairs on Cl).

  Q14. In a Lewis structure for CH₄:
    [*] Carbon forms 4 single bonds to hydrogen (no lone pairs on C)
    [ ] Hydrogen forms 2 bonds
    [ ] Carbon has 2 lone pairs
    [ ] Carbon forms 2 double bonds
    Explanation: CH₄ (methane): C has 4 valence electrons, each H has 1. C forms 4 single bonds to H. C and each H achieve stable electron configuration. No lone pairs on C.

  Q15. Bond length:
    [ ] Depends only on atomic mass
    [*] Decreases as bond order increases (triple < double < single)
    [ ] Is the same for all bonds
    [ ] Increases with bond order
    Explanation: Triple bonds are shorter and stronger than double bonds, which are shorter and stronger than single bonds. More shared electrons pull nuclei closer together.

  Q16. Bond energy:
    [*] Increases as bond order increases (triple > double > single)
    [ ] Depends on color of compound
    [ ] Is unrelated to bond order
    [ ] Decreases with bond order
    Explanation: Higher bond order → stronger bond → more energy required to break. Bond energies: triple > double > single.

  Q17. A resonance structure occurs when:
    [ ] Ionic and covalent bonds coexist within the molecule simultaneously
    [*] A molecule can be drawn with multiple valid Lewis structures that differ in bond placement (electrons delocalized)
    [ ] The molecule vibrates between two different physical shapes rapidly
    [ ] A molecule has two different shapes depending on its temperature
    Explanation: Resonance: when a single Lewis structure can't accurately represent a molecule, multiple structures are drawn (e.g., ozone O₃). The real structure is a hybrid (average) of all resonance forms.

  Q18. Ozone (O₃) has resonance because:
    [ ] The molecule is symmetric with no bonds between any of its oxygen atoms
    [ ] One bond is ionic while the other is a standard covalent single bond
    [*] The central O-O bonds are equivalent (between single and double bond character), not fixed
    [ ] All bonds are triple bonds with six electrons shared between atoms
    Explanation: O₃ has 2 resonance structures where the double bond alternates. The actual structure has equal bond lengths (~128 pm, between O=O 121 pm and O-O 148 pm).

  Q19. An expanded octet occurs when:
    [ ] Any atom has exactly 8 electrons filling its complete outer shell
    [ ] Noble gases form compounds by sharing their full valence shell
    [ ] Only transition metals form bonds using their d orbital electrons
    [*] A central atom has more than 8 electrons due to d-orbital availability (e.g., PCl₅, SF₆)
    Explanation: Expanded octets: elements in period 3+ can accommodate >8 electrons using d orbitals. PCl₅ (10 e around P), SF₆ (12 e around S).

  Q20. The formal charge on an atom in a Lewis structure is:
    [ ] Mass number − atomic number
    [ ] Oxidation state
    [*] Valence electrons − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻)
    [ ] Number of bonds
    Explanation: Formal charge = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻). Used to determine best Lewis structure (minimize formal charges, negative on most electronegative atom).

  Q21. Which shape does a tetrahedral molecule (like CH₄) have?
    [*] 4 equivalent bonds at ~109.5° angles
    [ ] Linear
    [ ] Square
    [ ] Pyramidal with one extra side
    Explanation: CH₄: 4 bonding pairs, no lone pairs → tetrahedral geometry, bond angles ~109.5°.

  Q22. NH₃ (ammonia) has a trigonal pyramidal shape because:
    [ ] N is linear with bonds at 180° between hydrogens
    [ ] N has 4 bonds to H with no lone pairs on nitrogen
    [*] N has 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair; the lone pair pushes bonding pairs into a pyramid
    [ ] All electrons are bonding pairs around the nitrogen
    Explanation: NH₃: N has 3 bonds to H + 1 lone pair. VSEPR: 4 electron pairs → tetrahedral electron geometry, but trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry (lone pair is not an atom). Bond angles ≈107°.

  Q23. Electronegativity of fluorine compared to other elements:
    [*] F has the highest electronegativity (3.98) of all elements
    [ ] F has the lowest electronegativity (0.98) of all elements
    [ ] F is 2.5 on the Pauling electronegativity scale
    [ ] F is equal to oxygen at 3.44 on the Pauling scale
    Explanation: Fluorine is the most electronegative element (Pauling scale: F = 3.98, O = 3.44, N = 3.04). It attracts bonding electrons most strongly.

  Q24. A coordinate covalent bond (dative bond) is:
    [*] A covalent bond where both electrons come from one atom (the Lewis base)
    [ ] A double bond
    [ ] A triple bond
    [ ] An ionic bond where both electrons come from the metal
    Explanation: Coordinate/dative bond: one atom donates both electrons. Example: NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺ (N donates lone pair to H⁺); also O→metal bonds in coordination compounds.

  Q25. Sigma (σ) bonds are:
    [ ] Always ionic in character between metal atoms
    [ ] Only found in triple bonds between two atoms
    [*] End-to-end orbital overlap; present in all single bonds and as first bond in multiple bonds
    [ ] Side-to-side overlap of parallel p orbitals
    Explanation: σ bonds: direct (end-on) overlap of orbitals along the internuclear axis. All single bonds are σ bonds. Double bonds = 1σ + 1π; triple bonds = 1σ + 2π.

  Q26. Pi (π) bonds are:
    [ ] Only in ionic compounds between metals and nonmetals
    [*] Side-to-side (lateral) orbital overlap; present in double and triple bonds
    [ ] The same as sigma bonds in terms of overlap geometry
    [ ] End-to-end overlap along the internuclear axis
    Explanation: π bonds: lateral overlap of p orbitals above and below the internuclear axis. π bonds are weaker than σ bonds and are found in double (1π) and triple (2π) bonds.

  Q27. Hybridization of carbon in CH₄ is:
    [ ] sp² (3 bonds, trigonal planar, 120°)
    [ ] sp (2 bonds, linear geometry, 180°)
    [ ] dsp² (4 bonds, square planar, 90°)
    [*] sp³ (4 single bonds, tetrahedral, 109.5°)
    Explanation: CH₄: C has 4 equivalent single bonds → sp³ hybridization (1 s + 3 p orbitals mix to form 4 equivalent sp³ orbitals). Tetrahedral geometry, 109.5° angles.

  Q28. Hybridization of carbon in C₂H₄ (ethylene) is:
    [ ] dsp³ (5 bonds, trigonal bipyramidal, mixed angles)
    [*] sp² (double bond, trigonal planar, 120°)
    [ ] sp (2 bonds, linear geometry, 180°)
    [ ] sp³ (4 bonds, tetrahedral, 109.5°)
    Explanation: Each C in C₂H₄: 1 double bond to other C + 2 single bonds to H. sp² hybridization (1s+2p → 3 sp² orbitals). The double bond = 1σ (sp²-sp²) + 1π (p-p side overlap).

  Q29. Hybridization of carbon in C₂H₂ (acetylene) is:
    [ ] sp² (3 bonds, trigonal planar, 120°)
    [ ] sp³ (4 bonds, tetrahedral, 109.5°)
    [ ] dsp² (4 bonds, square planar, 90°)
    [*] sp (triple bond, linear, 180°)
    Explanation: Each C in C₂H₂: 1 triple bond + 1 single bond → sp hybridization. Linear geometry. Triple bond = 1σ + 2π.

  Q30. Molecular polarity depends on:
    [ ] Only molecular weight of the compound
    [*] Both the polarity of individual bonds AND the molecular geometry (symmetry may cancel dipoles)
    [ ] Only electronegativity of the atoms
    [ ] Only bond polarity without considering shape
    Explanation: A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds AND the dipoles don't cancel. E.g., CO₂ (nonpolar, symmetric) vs. H₂O (polar, bent). Shape determines if bond dipoles cancel.

--- Lesson 5B.2: Naming Covalent Compounds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Covalent Compounds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for sulfur hexafluoride?
Sulfur (S, 1 atom) + hexafluoride (6 F). Formula: SF₆. Used as an electrical insulator; also a potent greenhouse gas.
What is the name for XeF₄?
XeF₄: Xe (xenon, 1 atom, no prefix) + F₄ (tetra-). Name: xenon tetrafluoride. A noble gas compound — Xe can form compounds due to its large, polarizable electron cloud.
What is the formula for carbon monoxide?
Carbon (1 C, no prefix) + monoxide (1 O). Formula: CO. Carbon monoxide is a toxic gas that binds hemoglobin more tightly than O₂.
What is the formula for nitrogen trifluoride?
Nitrogen (N, 1 atom) + trifluoride (3 F). Formula: NF₃.
What is the formula for disulfur dichloride?
Disulfur (S₂) + dichloride (Cl₂). Formula: S₂Cl₂.

  Q1. What prefix is used for 3 atoms in covalent naming?
    [ ] Mono-
    [ ] Tetra-
    [ ] Di-
    [*] Tri-
    Explanation: Tri- is correct. This is a key concept in Naming Covalent Compounds.

  Q2. The prefix 'tetra-' means:
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 5
    [*] 4
    [ ] 3
    Explanation: Greek prefix tetra- = 4. Used in covalent compound names: e.g., CCl₄ = carbon tetrachloride.

  Q3. The systematic name for CO₂ is:
    [ ] Dicarbon oxide
    [ ] Monocarbon dioxide
    [ ] Carbon oxide
    [*] Carbon dioxide
    Explanation: CO₂: 1 C (mono- dropped for first element) + 2 O (di-). Name: carbon dioxide.

  Q4. The systematic name for N₂O is:
    [ ] Dinitrogen oxide
    [ ] Nitrogen monoxide
    [ ] Nitrogen oxide
    [*] Dinitrogen monoxide
    Explanation: N₂O: 2 N (di-) + 1 O (mono-). Name: dinitrogen monoxide. Common name: nitrous oxide or laughing gas.

  Q5. The formula for dinitrogen pentoxide is:
    [ ] N₅O₂
    [ ] N₂O
    [ ] NO₅
    [*] N₂O₅
    Explanation: Dinitrogen (N₂) + pentoxide (O₅) = N₂O₅. This is an acidic oxide; reacts with water to form nitric acid.

  Q6. The prefix 'mono-' is:
    [*] Dropped from the first element's name (but kept on the second)
    [ ] Always used on the first element
    [ ] Added to all elements
    [ ] Never used
    Explanation: IUPAC covalent naming: omit 'mono-' from the first element. E.g., CO = carbon monoxide (not 'monocarbon monoxide'). But keep it on the second: CO₂ = carbon dioxide (not 'dimonoxide').

  Q7. Name the compound PCl₃:

  Q8. What is the formula for sulfur hexafluoride?
    [ ] S₆F
    [*] SF₆
    [ ] S₆F₆
    [ ] SF₂
    Explanation: Sulfur (S, 1 atom) + hexafluoride (6 F). Formula: SF₆. Used as an electrical insulator; also a potent greenhouse gas.

  Q9. The prefix 'hexa-' means:
    [*] 6

  Q10. Name the compound NO:

  Q11. Name the compound Cl₂O₇:

  Q12. Why do covalent compounds use Greek prefixes but ionic compounds do not?

    [ ] Ionic compounds have no atoms

  Q13. The compound P₄O₁₀ is named:

--- Lesson 5B.3: Metallic Bonds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Metallic Bonds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a metallic bond?
A bond where electrons are shared in a 'sea' of delocalized electrons is correct. This is a key concept in Metallic Bonds.

--- Lesson 5B.3: Metallic Bonds (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is a metallic bond?
    [ ] An ionic bond between metals
    [ ] A covalent bond in metals
    [ ] A bond between a metal and nonmetal
    [*] A bond where electrons are shared in a 'sea' of delocalized electrons
    Explanation: A bond where electrons are shared in a 'sea' of delocalized electrons is correct. This is a key concept in Metallic Bonds.

  Q2. The 'sea of electrons' model describes:
    [*] Delocalized valence electrons in metals that are free to move throughout the structure
    [ ] Ionic bonds between metals with electron transfer
    [ ] Fixed electrons in metal bound to specific atoms
    [ ] Covalent bonds between metal atoms sharing pairs
    Explanation: In metallic bonding, valence electrons are delocalized — not associated with individual atoms but shared throughout the entire metal crystal lattice.

  Q3. Metallic bonds explain why metals are:
    [ ] Poor thermal conductors that insulate against heat flow
    [ ] Brittle solids that shatter under mechanical stress
    [*] Excellent electrical conductors (free electrons carry charge)
    [ ] Transparent materials that allow light to pass through
    Explanation: The delocalized electrons in metals are free to move in response to an electric field → metals are excellent electrical conductors.

  Q4. Metals are good thermal conductors because:
    [ ] Metal atoms vibrate slowly
    [ ] Heat can't pass through metals
    [ ] Metals have high density
    [*] Free electrons rapidly transfer kinetic energy through the metal
    Explanation: Delocalized electrons can rapidly transport thermal energy (kinetic energy) from hot regions to cool regions, making metals good thermal conductors.

  Q5. Malleability (ability to be hammered into sheets) in metals is due to:
    [ ] Strong directional bonds that resist any deformation
    [*] Metal layers can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond (unlike brittle ionic crystals)
    [ ] High density preventing atoms from moving at all
    [ ] Electrons being fixed to specific atoms in the lattice
    Explanation: In metals, the non-directional electron sea allows layers to slide. New metallic bonds reform in the new position. Ionic crystals fracture because like charges would align.

  Q6. Ductility (ability to be drawn into wires) in metals is due to:
    [*] Non-directional metallic bonds allow atoms to slide while maintaining bonding
    [ ] Fixed ionic positions that prevent any atomic movement
    [ ] Electrons repelling each other and causing brittleness
    [ ] Directed covalent bonds between neighboring metal atoms
    Explanation: Same reason as malleability: delocalized electrons allow plastic deformation. Metals can be stretched into wires without breaking.

  Q7. The strength of a metallic bond is related to:
    [ ] The color of the metal surface under visible light
    [*] The number of valence electrons available (more e⁻ → stronger bond) and the nuclear charge
    [ ] The density only of the metal crystal lattice
    [ ] Whether the metal is magnetic or diamagnetic
    Explanation: More valence electrons = more electron density in the 'sea' = stronger metallic bonding. Transition metals (with d electrons) are generally harder and have higher melting points than alkali metals.

  Q8. Tungsten (W) has the highest melting point of all metals (3422°C) because:
    [ ] It is the heaviest metal element on the periodic table
    [*] Strong metallic bonds due to its high number of valence electrons (d-orbital electrons)
    [ ] It has the largest atomic radius among all metal atoms
    [ ] It forms ionic bonds instead of metallic bonds in solid
    Explanation: W has 6 valence electrons (5d⁴6s²) contributing to metallic bonding. Very strong metallic bonds → very high melting point.

  Q9. Alkali metals (Li, Na, K) have low melting points compared to transition metals because:
    [ ] They have more electrons
    [ ] They are lighter
    [ ] They form covalent bonds
    [*] They have only 1 valence electron → weaker metallic bonds
    Explanation: Alkali metals: 1 valence electron → relatively few delocalized electrons per atom → weaker metallic bonding → lower melting points (Na mp: 98°C vs. W mp: 3422°C).

  Q10. Alloys are:
    [ ] Ionic compounds of metals dissolved in aqueous solution
    [*] Mixtures of metals (or metal + nonmetal) with enhanced properties compared to pure metals
    [ ] Covalent molecules of metals with directional bonding
    [ ] Pure single-element metals with no added components
    Explanation: Alloys: mixtures of metals (e.g., steel = Fe + C; brass = Cu + Zn; bronze = Cu + Sn). Alloying disrupts the regular lattice → often harder, stronger, or more corrosion-resistant than pure metals.

  Q11. Steel is an alloy of:
    [ ] Iron and gold (forming a dense precious metal alloy)
    [*] Iron and carbon (plus other elements for specialized properties)
    [ ] Aluminum and silicon (forming a lightweight composite)
    [ ] Copper and zinc (forming a corrosion-resistant alloy)
    Explanation: Steel: primarily iron with 0.2–2.1% carbon. Carbon atoms occupy interstitial spaces in the iron lattice, impeding dislocation movement → stronger and harder than pure iron.

  Q12. Brass is an alloy of:
    [ ] Copper and tin
    [*] Copper and zinc
    [ ] Gold and silver
    [ ] Iron and zinc
    Explanation: Brass: Cu + Zn (typically 60–80% Cu). Used for musical instruments, plumbing fittings — more workable and corrosion-resistant than pure copper.

  Q13. Bronze is an alloy of:
    [ ] Copper and zinc
    [ ] Iron and carbon
    [*] Copper and tin
    [ ] Nickel and chromium
    Explanation: Bronze: Cu + Sn (originally). Harder than copper; used historically for tools, weapons, and sculptures.

  Q14. The luster (shininess) of metals is due to:
    [ ] No absorption of light
    [*] Free electrons absorbing and re-emitting light of all wavelengths
    [ ] Transparent crystal structure
    [ ] The density of the metal
    Explanation: Free electrons in metals can absorb photons of many wavelengths and re-emit them as visible light → metals appear shiny/lustrous.

  Q15. Stainless steel is an alloy of iron with:
    [ ] Pure iron only without any additional alloying elements
    [*] Chromium (and nickel) — Cr forms a protective oxide layer preventing rust
    [ ] Iron and copper (Cu provides conductivity enhancement)
    [ ] Iron and lead (Pb provides density and corrosion protection)
    Explanation: Stainless steel: Fe + Cr (10–30%) + often Ni. Chromium forms a thin Cr₂O₃ passivation layer that protects the steel from oxidation/rusting.

  Q16. Gold is often alloyed with copper or silver because:
    [*] Pure gold (24K) is too soft for jewelry; alloys are harder and more durable
    [ ] Pure gold is brittle and fractures easily when stressed
    [ ] Gold doesn’t conduct electricity unless combined with copper
    [ ] Gold is too expensive to use in its pure elemental form
    Explanation: 24-karat gold is nearly pure gold but too soft to maintain shape in jewelry. 18K gold (75% Au, 25% Cu/Ag/other) is harder and more durable.

  Q17. The band theory of metals explains:
    [ ] Covalent single bonds between metal atoms
    [*] Atomic orbitals of metal atoms overlap to form continuous bands of energy levels
    [ ] Fixed energy levels in isolated atoms
    [ ] Ionic bonding only
    Explanation: Band theory: in a metal crystal, atomic orbitals combine to form bands (conduction band, valence band). Partially filled conduction band allows electron flow → conductivity.

  Q18. Semiconductors (like silicon) differ from metals in conductivity because:
    [*] Si has a full valence band and empty conduction band with a small gap that can be crossed with added energy
    [ ] Si has no free electrons at any temperature or condition
    [ ] Si is an insulator at all temperatures without exception
    [ ] Si has only ionic bonds between its atoms in the crystal
    Explanation: Semiconductors: bandgap between filled valence band and empty conduction band is small (~1 eV for Si). At room temp or with added energy (light, heat, doping), electrons jump to conduction band → conductivity.

  Q19. The work function of a metal is:
    [ ] The lattice energy needed to separate the metal into atoms
    [ ] The bonding energy between two metal atoms in the crystal
    [ ] The energy to melt the metal from solid to liquid phase
    [*] The minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal surface (used in photoelectric effect)
    Explanation: Work function φ: minimum photon energy needed to free an electron from a metal surface. The photoelectric effect (Einstein, 1905) uses this concept.

  Q20. Metallic bonds are:
    [ ] Highly directional
    [ ] Always between two specific atoms
    [ ] Equivalent to ionic bonds
    [*] Non-directional (unlike covalent bonds) — they have no preferred geometry
    Explanation: Metallic bonds have no preferred direction because the delocalized electrons form a uniform 'sea.' This non-directionality allows plastic deformation (malleability/ductility).

  Q21. Which metal is the best conductor of electricity?
    [*] Silver (Ag)
    [ ] Copper
    [ ] Gold
    [ ] Aluminum
    Explanation: Silver has the highest electrical conductivity of any element. However, copper is more commonly used in wiring due to its lower cost.

  Q22. Mercury (Hg) is unusual among metals because:
    [ ] It is a nonmetal that belongs with the halogens on the table
    [ ] It has no free electrons available for conduction at all
    [ ] It is a gas at room temperature like the other Group 12 elements
    [*] It is liquid at room temperature due to relativistic effects weakening its metallic bonds
    Explanation: Mercury: only metal liquid at room temperature (mp = −38.8°C). Relativistic effects cause Hg's 6s electrons to contract and not overlap as effectively with neighbors → weak metallic bonds.

  Q23. Superalloys used in jet engines contain:
    [ ] Only iron with carbon added for basic structural strength
    [*] Nickel, cobalt, chromium, and other elements that maintain strength at high temperatures
    [ ] Only aluminum with silicon for lightweight construction
    [ ] Carbon and silicon mixed without any metallic components
    Explanation: Superalloys (e.g., Inconel): Ni/Co/Cr-based alloys designed to maintain mechanical properties at temperatures above 1000°C for jet turbine blades.

  Q24. The hall-effect experiment shows:
    [ ] All metals have the same carrier density
    [ ] Charge carriers are positive holes
    [ ] Metals have no charge carriers
    [*] Charge carriers in metals are negative electrons
    Explanation: The Hall effect (1879): a magnetic field deflects current-carrying electrons in a conductor, generating a voltage perpendicular to both. Proved electrons are the charge carriers in metals.

  Q25. Superconductivity is:
    [ ] Very low resistance at room temperature without cooling needed
    [*] Zero electrical resistance below a critical temperature, as electron pairs (Cooper pairs) flow without scattering
    [ ] Complete absence of metallic bonding between metal atoms
    [ ] Perfect thermal conductivity only without electrical effects
    Explanation: Superconductors below Tc: electron pairs (Cooper pairs) condense into a quantum state and flow without resistance. Applications: MRI magnets, maglev trains.

  Q26. The interstitial alloy type (like steel) works by:
    [ ] Two metals of equal size mixing randomly in the lattice
    [*] Small atoms (C, N) fit into spaces between large metal atoms, impeding dislocation movement → harder
    [ ] Forming covalent bonds between the metal and nonmetal atoms
    [ ] Large atoms replacing metal atoms at lattice positions
    Explanation: Interstitial alloys: small atoms (C, N, B, H) occupy interstices between larger metal atoms. They pin dislocations, increasing hardness and strength.

  Q27. Substitutional alloys (like brass) work by:
    [ ] Small atoms fit in gaps
    [ ] Covalent bonds between metals
    [ ] Ion exchange
    [*] Atoms of similar size replace host metal atoms in the lattice
    Explanation: Substitutional alloys: solute atoms (similar size to solvent atoms) randomly substitute in the lattice positions. Lattice strain around substituted atoms impedes dislocation movement → harder.

  Q28. Why are transition metals generally harder and denser than main-group metals?
    [ ] They form covalent bonds instead of metallic bonds between atoms
    [*] Transition metals have partially filled d orbitals contributing additional metallic bonding (more valence electrons)
    [ ] They are heavier individually which increases their density alone
    [ ] They have ionic bonds between atoms rather than metallic bonding
    Explanation: d orbitals in transition metals provide additional valence electrons for metallic bonding → stronger bonds → higher melting points, hardness, and density compared to s-block metals.

  Q29. Shape memory alloys (like Nitinol) can:
    [*] Return to their original shape when heated due to a reversible crystal structure transformation
    [ ] Never be deformed because their crystal structure is too rigid
    [ ] Conduct electricity only when bent into a specific curved shape
    [ ] Only be used at low temperatures below their critical point
    Explanation: Nitinol (Ni-Ti): undergoes reversible martensitic transformation. Deformed at low T → heated → returns to original shape. Used in medical stents, dental wires, aerospace actuators.

  Q30. The relationship between metallic bond strength and periodic trends: as you go down a group of metals:
    [ ] Bond strength is constant regardless of position in the group
    [ ] Bond strength increases going down due to more electron shells
    [ ] Melting point increases going down as atomic mass gets larger
    [*] Bond strength generally decreases (more shielding, larger atomic radius, weaker orbital overlap)
    Explanation: Going down a group: atomic radius increases, more shielding → reduced effective nuclear charge on valence electrons → weaker metallic bonding → generally lower melting points (Na → Cs).

--- Lesson 5B.4: Organic Compounds (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What element is the basis of all organic compounds?
    [ ] Oxygen
    [*] Carbon
    [ ] Hydrogen
    [ ] Nitrogen
    Explanation: Carbon is correct. This is a key concept in Organic Compounds.

  Q2. Organic chemistry is the study of:
    [*] Carbon-containing compounds (mainly with C-H bonds)
    [ ] Inorganic salts and minerals
    [ ] All elements in the periodic table
    [ ] Compounds without carbon
    Explanation: Organic chemistry: the chemistry of carbon. Carbon's ability to form 4 bonds, long chains, and rings creates millions of organic compounds essential to life.

  Q3. Alkanes have the general formula:
    [ ] CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (one C≡C triple bond)
    [*] CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (only single bonds)
    [ ] CₙHₙ (equal carbons and hydrogens)
    [ ] CₙH₂ₙ (one C=C double bond)
    Explanation: Alkanes (saturated hydrocarbons): CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Examples: methane CH₄ (n=1), ethane C₂H₆ (n=2), propane C₃H₈ (n=3).

  Q4. Alkenes have the general formula:
    [ ] CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (only single C-C bonds)
    [*] CₙH₂ₙ (one C=C double bond)
    [ ] CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (one C≡C triple bond)
    [ ] CₙHₙ (equal carbons and hydrogens)
    Explanation: Alkenes (one C=C double bond): CₙH₂ₙ. Examples: ethene C₂H₄, propene C₃H₆.

  Q5. Alkynes have the general formula:
    [ ] CₙH₂ₙ (one C=C double bond only)
    [ ] CₙHₙ₊₁ (odd hydrogen ratio)
    [ ] CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (only single C-C bonds)
    [*] CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (one C≡C triple bond)
    Explanation: Alkynes (one C≡C triple bond): CₙH₂ₙ₋₂. Examples: ethyne (acetylene) C₂H₂, propyne C₃H₄.

  Q6. A functional group in organic chemistry is:
    [*] A specific atom or group of atoms that gives a molecule characteristic chemical properties
    [ ] The total number of carbons in the chain backbone
    [ ] Any carbon chain regardless of attached atoms
    [ ] The molecular weight of the entire compound
    Explanation: Functional groups (e.g., -OH, -COOH, -NH₂, C=O) determine the reactivity and properties of organic molecules regardless of carbon chain length.

  Q7. The hydroxyl group (-OH) is characteristic of:
    [*] Alcohols
    [ ] Amines
    [ ] Carboxylic acids
    [ ] Aldehydes
    Explanation: Alcohols: R-OH (where R = carbon chain). Examples: methanol CH₃OH, ethanol C₂H₅OH.

  Q8. The carboxyl group (-COOH) is characteristic of:
    [ ] Ketones
    [ ] Ethers
    [*] Carboxylic acids
    [ ] Alcohols
    Explanation: Carboxylic acids: R-COOH. Examples: acetic acid (CH₃COOH), formic acid (HCOOH). Weak acids (partially ionize in water).

  Q9. The carbonyl group (C=O) in the middle of a carbon chain is characteristic of:
    [*] Ketones
    [ ] Aldehydes (which have C=O at the end of chain)
    [ ] Carboxylic acids
    [ ] Alcohols
    Explanation: Ketones: R-CO-R (carbonyl not at chain end). Aldehydes: R-CHO (carbonyl at chain end). E.g., acetone (CH₃COCH₃) is a ketone.

  Q10. Isomers are compounds that:
    [*] Have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements
    [ ] Have different numbers of carbons
    [ ] Are identical in all ways
    [ ] Have the same structure but different formulas
    Explanation: Structural isomers: same molecular formula (e.g., C₄H₁₀) but different connectivity: n-butane (straight chain) vs. isobutane (branched). Different chemical/physical properties.

  Q11. The IUPAC name for CH₄ is:
    [ ] Carbane
    [*] Methane
    [ ] Monane
    [ ] Formane
    Explanation: IUPAC alkane names: 1C=methane, 2C=ethane, 3C=propane, 4C=butane, 5C=pentane, 6C=hexane, 7C=heptane, 8C=octane, 9C=nonane, 10C=decane.

  Q12. Which is the name for a 2-carbon alkane?
    [ ] Butane
    [*] Ethane
    [ ] Propane
    [ ] Methane
    Explanation: Ethane: C₂H₆. Eth- prefix = 2 carbons.

  Q13. Benzene (C₆H₆) is an example of:
    [ ] An alcohol
    [ ] An alkane
    [ ] An alkyne
    [*] An aromatic hydrocarbon
    Explanation: Benzene: C₆H₆ with delocalized π electrons in a ring. Aromatic compounds are cyclic, conjugated, and unusually stable due to aromaticity.

  Q14. The amine functional group (-NH₂) is found in:
    [*] Amines and amino acids
    [ ] Esters
    [ ] Ketones
    [ ] Carboxylic acids
    Explanation: Amines: R-NH₂ (primary amine). Amino acids contain both -NH₂ and -COOH groups. Amines are weak bases (lone pair on N accepts H⁺).

  Q15. Esters form from:
    [*] Carboxylic acids + alcohols (condensation reaction, releasing water)
    [ ] Two carboxylic acids (condensation reaction, releasing CO₂)
    [ ] Ketones + water (hydration reaction, adding hydroxyl groups)
    [ ] Two alcohols (dehydration reaction, releasing hydrogen gas)
    Explanation: Esterification: R-COOH + HO-R' → R-COO-R' + H₂O. Esters often have pleasant fruit fragrances (e.g., isoamyl acetate = banana smell).

  Q16. Polymers are:
    [ ] Ionic crystals held together by electrostatic forces
    [*] Long chain molecules made of repeating units (monomers) covalently bonded
    [ ] Short molecules with only two or three atoms bonded
    [ ] Inorganic salts formed from acid-base neutralization
    Explanation: Polymers: macromolecules built from monomers. Natural: proteins, DNA, cellulose. Synthetic: polyethylene, nylon, PVC, Teflon.

  Q17. Saturated hydrocarbons contain:
    [ ] At least one double bond
    [ ] At least one triple bond
    [*] Only single bonds (C-C and C-H)
    [ ] A benzene ring
    Explanation: 'Saturated' = hydrogen-saturated = only single bonds. Cannot add more H without breaking existing bonds. General formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ for acyclic alkanes.

  Q18. Unsaturated hydrocarbons contain:
    [ ] Only ionic bonds
    [ ] Only single bonds
    [*] At least one C=C double or C≡C triple bond
    [ ] No hydrogen
    Explanation: 'Unsaturated' = contain double or triple bonds. Can undergo addition reactions to add H₂ (hydrogenation) or other molecules across the double/triple bond.

  Q19. The process of adding hydrogen across a C=C double bond is called:
    [ ] Polymerization
    [ ] Dehydration
    [ ] Halogenation
    [*] Hydrogenation
    Explanation: Hydrogenation: C=C + H₂ → C-C (single bond). Used industrially to produce saturated fats from unsaturated vegetable oils (e.g., making margarine).

  Q20. Structural formula vs. molecular formula:
    [ ] Structural formula shows only element symbols
    [*] Structural formula shows atom connectivity; molecular formula shows only total atom count
    [ ] They are the same
    [ ] Molecular formula shows all bonds
    Explanation: Molecular formula: tells you which atoms and how many (e.g., C₂H₆). Structural formula: shows how atoms are bonded (H₃C-CH₃). More informative for identifying the compound.

  Q21. Which compound is a primary alcohol?
    [ ] CH₃CHO (acetaldehyde, not an alcohol)
    [ ] (CH₃)₂CHOH (2-propanol, secondary)
    [ ] (CH₃)₃COH (tert-butanol, tertiary)
    [*] CH₃CH₂OH (ethanol) — OH attached to a carbon with 1 other carbon
    Explanation: Primary alcohol: -OH attached to a carbon bonded to only 1 other carbon. Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) has the -OH on the terminal carbon → primary.

  Q22. Combustion of organic compounds produces:
    [ ] SO₂ only from the sulfur impurities in the fuel
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O (complete combustion) or CO and H₂O (incomplete combustion)
    [ ] No byproducts because combustion is perfectly efficient
    [ ] O₂ and N₂ as the main gaseous reaction products
    Explanation: Complete combustion: fuel + excess O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. Incomplete combustion (insufficient O₂): produces CO (toxic) and soot (C).

  Q23. Condensed structural formula for propane is:
    [*] CH₃CH₂CH₃
    [ ] C₃H₈ (this is molecular formula)
    [ ] C-C-C (this is too simplified)
    [ ] (CH₃)₂ (incomplete)
    Explanation: Propane condensed structural formula: CH₃CH₂CH₃ (3 carbons in a row, showing connectivity). Molecular formula: C₃H₈.

  Q24. The halogenation of alkanes involves:
    [ ] Addition of halogens to double bonds
    [ ] Ionic reactions
    [ ] Elimination of HX
    [*] Substitution of H atoms by halogen atoms (Cl, Br) under UV light
    Explanation: Alkane halogenation: R-H + X₂ → R-X + HX (free radical substitution, requires UV or heat). E.g., CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl.

  Q25. Addition polymers form when:
    [*] Monomers with double bonds add together to form long chains (e.g., polyethylene from ethylene)
    [ ] Monomers condense releasing water molecules as a byproduct
    [ ] Cyclic molecules open into linear chains through ring opening
    [ ] Ions attract each other forming an ionic polymer network
    Explanation: Addition polymerization: the C=C double bonds in monomers open and join. No atoms are lost. Example: n CH₂=CH₂ → (-CH₂-CH₂-)ₙ (polyethylene).

  Q26. Condensation polymers form when:
    [*] Monomers join by releasing a small molecule (usually water); e.g., nylon, polyester, proteins
    [ ] Ionic bonds form between monomers holding the chain together
    [ ] Monomers add without losing atoms in the reaction process
    [ ] Cyclic monomers open their rings to form a linear polymer
    Explanation: Condensation polymerization: each bond formed between monomers releases a small molecule (H₂O, HCl, etc.). Nylon (from diamine + diacid), polyester (from diol + diacid), proteins (from amino acids).

  Q27. The ester linkage (-COO-) is found in:
    [ ] Polyethylene (made from ethylene addition polymerization)
    [ ] Proteins (made from amino acids via peptide bonds)
    [*] Polyesters (like PET) and fats/oils (triglycerides)
    [ ] Nylon (made from diamines and diacids via amide bonds)
    Explanation: Polyesters contain -COO- ester linkages throughout the chain. PET (polyethylene terephthalate) = Dacron/Mylar. Triglycerides (fats) are esters of glycerol with fatty acids.

  Q28. Amino acids link together through:
    [ ] Ionic bonds formed by complete electron transfer between amino acids
    [*] Peptide bonds (-CO-NH-) forming proteins (condensation reaction, releasing H₂O)
    [ ] Hydrogen bonds only without any covalent bond formation between units
    [ ] Ester bonds (-COO-) formed by condensation of carboxyl and hydroxyl
    Explanation: Peptide bonds: the -COOH of one amino acid + the -NH₂ of another → -CO-NH- + H₂O. Chains of amino acids linked by peptide bonds = proteins (polypeptides).

  Q29. DNA and RNA are:
    [ ] Lipids (fatty acid esters of glycerol)
    [ ] Polysaccharides (sugar chain polymers)
    [ ] Proteins (amino acid chain polymers)
    [*] Biological polymers (polynucleotides) made of nucleotide monomers
    Explanation: DNA and RNA are nucleic acid polymers. Each nucleotide monomer = pentose sugar + phosphate group + nitrogenous base. The polymer backbone is held together by phosphodiester bonds.

  Q30. The carbon cycle involves:
    [ ] Nuclear reactions involving radioactive decay of carbon isotopes
    [ ] Only physical processes like evaporation and condensation of water
    [ ] Movement of carbon only as CO₂ between the atmosphere and oceans
    [*] Conversion of CO₂ (inorganic) to organic compounds (photosynthesis) and back to CO₂ (respiration/combustion)
    Explanation: Carbon cycle: CO₂ (atmosphere) → organic compounds (photosynthesis) → CO₂ (respiration/decomposition/combustion). Maintains balance of carbon between atmosphere, biosphere, and geosphere.

--- Lesson 5B.5: Mixed Nomenclature Practice (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mixed Nomenclature Practice
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for calcium nitrate?
Ca²⁺ (+2) + NO₃⁻ (−1): need 2 NO₃⁻ to balance Ca²⁺. Formula: Ca(NO₃)₂.
What is the formula for manganese(IV) oxide?
Mn(IV) = Mn⁴⁺. O²⁻: 1 Mn⁴⁺ + 2 O²⁻ = +4 + (−4) = 0. Formula: MnO₂ (manganese dioxide — also used in dry cell batteries).
What is the formula for chromium(III) sulfate?
Cr³⁺ and SO₄²⁻: crisscross → Cr₂(SO₄)₃. Verify: 2×(+3) = +6 and 3×(−2) = −6. ✓
What is the formula for tin(IV) chloride?
Sn(IV) = Sn⁴⁺. Cl⁻: need 4 Cl⁻ to balance Sn⁴⁺. Formula: SnCl₄. (Tin tetrachloride; used as a Lewis acid catalyst.)
What is the formula for ammonium dichromate?
Ammonium = NH₄⁺ (+1); dichromate = Cr₂O₇²⁻ (−2). Need 2 NH₄⁺: (NH₄)₂Cr₂O₇.
What is the formula for iron(III) carbonate?
Fe³⁺ and CO₃²⁻: crisscross → Fe₂(CO₃)₃. Verify: 2×(+3) = +6; 3×(−2) = −6. ✓
What is the formula for nitrogen monoxide?
Nitrogen (1 N, no prefix for first atom) + monoxide (1 O). Formula: NO.
What is the formula for potassium permanganate?
K⁺ (+1) + MnO₄⁻ (permanganate, −1). 1:1 ratio. Formula: KMnO₄. Intense purple color; strong oxidizing agent.
A student writes 'dicalcium dioxide' for a compound. What is wrong?
Ionic compounds (metal + nonmetal) are named using ion names, not Greek prefixes. 'Dicalcium dioxide' is incorrect; the formula CaO is named 'calcium oxide.'

    [ ] Copper(I) nitrate
    [ ] Copper nitrate
    [*] Copper(II) nitrate
    Explanation: Cu(NO₃)₂: 2 NO₃⁻ = −2 → Cu = +2 = Cu²⁺. Copper has variable charge → Roman numeral: copper(II) nitrate.

  Q12. Name the compound AgCl:
    [ ] Silver(II) chloride
    [ ] Silver(I) chloride (also acceptable)
    [*] Silver chloride
    [ ] Silver dichloride
    Explanation: AgCl: Ag⁺ (silver is typically +1, though Ag can be +2 rarely) + Cl⁻. Name: silver chloride. (Silver(I) chloride is also acceptable.)

  Q13. What is the formula for chromium(III) sulfate?
    [ ] Cr₃(SO₄)₂
    [ ] Cr(SO₄)₃
    [*] Cr₂(SO₄)₃
    [ ] CrSO₄
    Explanation: Cr³⁺ and SO₄²⁻: crisscross → Cr₂(SO₄)₃. Verify: 2×(+3) = +6 and 3×(−2) = −6. ✓

  Q14. Name the compound P₄O₆:
    [ ] Tetraphosphorus oxide
    [ ] Phosphorus trioxide
    [ ] Phosphorus(III) oxide
    [*] Tetraphosphorus hexoxide
    Explanation: P₄O₆: covalent. 4 P (tetra-) + 6 O (hex- → hexoxide). Name: tetraphosphorus hexoxide.

  Q15. What is the formula for tin(IV) chloride?
    [*] SnCl₄
    [ ] Sn₂Cl₄
    [ ] SnCl₂
    [ ] SnCl
    Explanation: Sn(IV) = Sn⁴⁺. Cl⁻: need 4 Cl⁻ to balance Sn⁴⁺. Formula: SnCl₄. (Tin tetrachloride; used as a Lewis acid catalyst.)

  Q16. Name the compound MnSO₄:
    [ ] Manganese sulfate
    [ ] Monomanganese sulfate
    [ ] Manganese(IV) sulfate
    [*] Manganese(II) sulfate
    Explanation: MnSO₄: SO₄²⁻ = −2 → Mn = +2 = Mn²⁺. Mn has variable charge → Roman numeral: manganese(II) sulfate.

  Q17. Name the compound Li₃PO₄:
    [ ] Lithium(I) phosphate
    [ ] Trilithium phosphate
    [ ] Lithium triphosphate
    [*] Lithium phosphate
    Explanation: Li₃PO₄: Li⁺ (fixed +1) × 3 = +3; PO₄³⁻ = −3. Balanced. Name: lithium phosphate.

  Q18. Name the covalent compound BrF₅:
    [*] Bromine pentafluoride
    [ ] Fluorine pentabromide
    [ ] Pentabromine fluoride
    [ ] Bromine(V) fluoride
    Explanation: BrF₅: covalent. Br (1 atom, mono- dropped) + F₅ (penta-). Name: bromine pentafluoride.

  Q19. Name the compound PbCO₃:
    [*] Lead(II) carbonate
    [ ] Monolead carbonate
    [ ] Lead carbonate
    [ ] Lead(IV) carbonate
    Explanation: PbCO₃: CO₃²⁻ = −2 → Pb = +2 = Pb²⁺. Lead has variable charge → Roman numeral: lead(II) carbonate.

  Q20. What is the formula for ammonium dichromate?
    [*] (NH₄)₂Cr₂O₇
    [ ] NH₄CrO₄
    [ ] NH₄Cr₂O₇
    [ ] (NH₄)₂CrO₄
    Explanation: Ammonium = NH₄⁺ (+1); dichromate = Cr₂O₇²⁻ (−2). Need 2 NH₄⁺: (NH₄)₂Cr₂O₇.

  Q21. Identify: Al₂(SO₄)₃ — name and compound type:
    [ ] Aluminum sulfite; ionic
    [*] Aluminum sulfate; ionic compound
    [ ] Dialuminum trisulfate; covalent
    [ ] Aluminum(II) sulfate; ionic
    Explanation: Al₂(SO₄)₃: ionic (metal + polyatomic anion). Al always Al³⁺ (fixed charge). Name: aluminum sulfate.

  Q22. Name the compound HgS:
    [ ] Mercury(I) sulfide
    [*] Mercury(II) sulfide
    [ ] Mercury sulfide
    [ ] Mercury disulfide
    Explanation: HgS: S²⁻ = −2 → Hg = +2 = Hg²⁺. Mercury has variable charge (Hg⁺ and Hg²⁺) → Roman numeral: mercury(II) sulfide.

  Q23. What is the formula for iron(III) carbonate?
    [ ] Fe₃CO₃
    [ ] FeCO₃
    [ ] Fe(CO₃)₃
    [*] Fe₂(CO₃)₃
    Explanation: Fe³⁺ and CO₃²⁻: crisscross → Fe₂(CO₃)₃. Verify: 2×(+3) = +6; 3×(−2) = −6. ✓

  Q24. Name the compound CS₂:
    [*] Carbon disulfide
    [ ] Monocarbon disulfide
    [ ] Carbon(II) sulfide
    [ ] Dicarbon sulfide
    Explanation: CS₂: covalent. C (1 atom, mono- dropped) + S₂ (di-). Name: carbon disulfide. Used as an industrial solvent.

  Q25. Name the compound Sr(OH)₂:
    [ ] Strontium(II) hydroxide
    [ ] Strontium dihydroxide
    [ ] Distrontium hydroxide
    [*] Strontium hydroxide
    Explanation: Sr(OH)₂: Sr²⁺ (strontium, fixed +2, alkaline earth metal) + 2 OH⁻. No Roman numeral needed. Name: strontium hydroxide.

  Q26. What is the formula for nitrogen monoxide?
    [ ] NO₂
    [*] NO
    [ ] N₂O₅
    [ ] N₂O
    Explanation: Nitrogen (1 N, no prefix for first atom) + monoxide (1 O). Formula: NO.

  Q27. Identify and name: CaCO₃
    [ ] Calcium bicarbonate; ionic
    [*] Calcium carbonate; ionic (main component of limestone and chalk)
    [ ] Calcium carbide; ionic
    [ ] Calcium carbite; covalent
    Explanation: CaCO₃: Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ (carbonate) → calcium carbonate. Main mineral in limestone, marble, chalk, and shells.

  Q28. What is the formula for potassium permanganate?
    [ ] K₂Mn₂O₇
    [ ] KMnO₂
    [ ] K₂MnO₄
    [*] KMnO₄
    Explanation: K⁺ (+1) + MnO₄⁻ (permanganate, −1). 1:1 ratio. Formula: KMnO₄. Intense purple color; strong oxidizing agent.

  Q29. Name the compound Mg₃N₂:
    [ ] Trimagnesium dinitride
    [ ] Magnesium nitrogen
    [*] Magnesium nitride
    [ ] Magnesium(II) nitride
    Explanation: Mg₃N₂: Mg is alkaline earth metal (fixed +2); no Roman numeral needed. N³⁻ = nitride. Name: magnesium nitride. (Formed when Mg burns in N₂.)

  Q30. A student writes 'dicalcium dioxide' for a compound. What is wrong?
    [ ] 'Di' is correct for polyatomic ions based on established principles
    [ ] Only 'dioxide' is wrong, particularly regarding oxide based on the prevailing evidence
    [ ] The name is correct for Ca₂O₂ following conventional understanding
    [*] Calcium is a metal — ionic compounds don't use Greek prefixes. Correct name: calcium oxide (CaO)
    Explanation: Ionic compounds (metal + nonmetal) are named using ion names, not Greek prefixes. 'Dicalcium dioxide' is incorrect; the formula CaO is named 'calcium oxide.'
