--- Lesson 6.1: Types of Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the term for a reaction in which two or more reactants form a single product?
A synthesis or combination reaction combines two or more substances into one product. It is distinct from decomposition, which does the opposite.
What is the 'activity series' used for?
The activity series ranks metals by reactivity. A metal higher on the series can replace a lower metal from its compound; if not, no reaction occurs.
What is the meaning of 'double replacement' in chemistry?
Double replacement (metathesis) reactions involve two ionic compounds in solution swapping their cation–anion partners: AB + CD → AD + CB.
What is a 'reactant' versus a 'product'?
Reactants appear on the left side of the arrow and are consumed. Products appear on the right and are formed during the reaction.
In a lab, mixing lead(II) nitrate and potassium iodide solutions produces a bright yellow solid. What is the yellow solid and what reaction type produced it?
Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂↓ + 2KNO₃. The ions swap partners (double replacement), and bright yellow lead(II) iodide is the insoluble precipitate.

--- Lesson 6.1: Types of Reactions (Quiz) ---

  Q1. In a synthesis reaction, two or more substances combine to form:
    [ ] Two products
    [ ] Only a gas
    [ ] No products
    [*] One product
    Explanation: Synthesis (combination) reactions follow the pattern A + B → AB, where multiple reactants combine to form a single, more complex product.

  Q2. AB → A + B represents which type of reaction?
    [ ] Synthesis
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Combustion
    Explanation: Decomposition is the reverse of synthesis — one compound breaks apart into two or more simpler substances: AB → A + B.

  Q3. In a single replacement reaction, what happens?
    [ ] A compound breaks apart
    [*] One element replaces another in a compound
    [ ] Two compounds swap ions
    [ ] Two elements combine
    Explanation: Single replacement follows A + BC → AC + B, where a lone element displaces another element from a compound based on activity.

  Q4. What are the products of complete combustion of a hydrocarbon?
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    [ ] CO and H₂
    [ ] Only CO₂
    [ ] C and H₂O
    Explanation: Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon in excess oxygen always produces carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O).

  Q5. In a double replacement reaction, AB + CD →
    [*] AD + CB
    [ ] A + B + C + D
    [ ] AC + BD
    [ ] ABCD
    Explanation: In double replacement, the cations of two ionic compounds trade anion partners: AB + CD → AD + CB.

  Q6. An insoluble solid formed in a double replacement reaction is called a:
    [ ] Solute
    [ ] Catalyst
    [*] Precipitate
    [ ] Electrolyte
    Explanation: When two aqueous solutions mix in a double replacement reaction and form an insoluble product, that solid is called a precipitate.

  Q7. Which reaction type ALWAYS requires oxygen as a reactant?
    [ ] Decomposition
    [*] Combustion
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: Combustion is defined as the rapid reaction of a substance with oxygen, producing heat and light. Oxygen is always a required reactant.

  Q8. A + B → AB is the general form of which reaction type?
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Double replacement
    [*] Synthesis
    [ ] Decomposition
    Explanation: Synthesis (combination) reactions have the general form A + B → AB. Two or more simpler substances combine to form one complex product.

  Q9. The activity series ranks elements by their ability to:
    [ ] Conduct heat, particularly regarding lower
    [ ] Resist decomposition under equilibrium conditions
    [*] Displace other elements in single replacement reactions
    [ ] Form precipitates in most standard conditions
    Explanation: The activity series is a ranking of metals (and hydrogen) by their reactivity. A higher-ranked element can displace a lower-ranked one from a compound in a single replacement reaction.

  Q10. Which reaction type has only ONE reactant?
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Double replacement
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Combustion
    Explanation: Decomposition reactions start with a single compound that breaks down into two or more simpler substances (AB → A + B), so there is only one reactant.

  Q11. What is the term for a reaction in which two or more reactants form a single product?
    [ ] Decomposition reaction
    [ ] Neutralization reaction
    [*] Synthesis (combination) reaction
    [ ] Endothermic reaction
    Explanation: A synthesis or combination reaction combines two or more substances into one product. It is distinct from decomposition, which does the opposite.

  Q12. Define 'precipitate' in the context of chemical reactions.
    [ ] A gaseous product that escapes
    [ ] A catalyst that speeds up the reaction
    [ ] A dissolved ionic compound
    [*] An insoluble solid product formed during a double replacement reaction
    Explanation: A precipitate is an insoluble solid that forms when ions in two aqueous reactants combine during a double replacement reaction.

  Q13. What does the term 'combustion' mean in chemistry?
    [*] A rapid exothermic reaction of a substance with oxygen
    [ ] Breaking a compound into elements
    [ ] The slow oxidation of iron
    [ ] Any reaction that produces heat
    Explanation: Combustion specifically refers to a substance reacting rapidly with oxygen, producing heat and light. While rusting involves oxygen, it is too slow to be called combustion.

  Q14. What is the 'activity series' used for?
    [*] Predicting whether a single replacement reaction will occur
    [ ] Determining molar mass
    [ ] Balancing chemical equations
    [ ] Classifying acids and bases
    Explanation: The activity series ranks metals by reactivity. A metal higher on the series can replace a lower metal from its compound; if not, no reaction occurs.

  Q15. What distinguishes a single replacement from a double replacement reaction?
    [*] Single replacement involves one element and one compound; double involves two compounds
    [ ] Single replacement produces gas; double produces solid
    [ ] There is no difference
    [ ] Single replacement uses oxygen; double does not
    Explanation: In single replacement (A + BC → AC + B), a free element displaces one in a compound. In double replacement (AB + CD → AD + CB), two compounds exchange ions.

  Q16. Define 'decomposition reaction.'
    [ ] A reaction that always produces a gas
    [ ] A reaction where two elements combine into a compound
    [*] A reaction where one compound breaks apart into two or more simpler substances
    [ ] A reaction that requires a catalyst
    Explanation: Decomposition reactions break a single compound into two or more simpler products. Energy (heat, light, or electricity) is often required to drive them.

  Q17. What is the meaning of 'double replacement' in chemistry?
    [*] Two ionic compounds exchange cations and anions to form new compounds
    [ ] Two reactions happen simultaneously
    [ ] A compound breaks into exactly two parts
    [ ] Two elements switch energy levels
    Explanation: Double replacement (metathesis) reactions involve two ionic compounds in solution swapping their cation–anion partners: AB + CD → AD + CB.

  Q18. What does the term 'hydrocarbon' refer to?
    [ ] Any compound containing oxygen
    [ ] An element found in Group 14
    [ ] A charged ion in solution
    [*] A compound containing only carbon and hydrogen
    Explanation: Hydrocarbons are organic molecules made entirely of carbon and hydrogen atoms (e.g., CH₄ methane, C₃H₈ propane). They are common fuels for combustion.

  Q19. What is a 'reactant' versus a 'product'?
    [ ] Reactants are on the right side of the arrow
    [ ] There is no difference
    [*] Reactants are starting substances; products are substances formed
    [ ] Products are always gases
    Explanation: Reactants appear on the left side of the arrow and are consumed. Products appear on the right and are formed during the reaction.

  Q20. What does the word 'exothermic' mean when describing combustion?
    [*] The reaction releases energy as heat and light
    [ ] The reaction does not involve energy changes
    [ ] The reaction occurs at extreme temperatures only
    [ ] The reaction absorbs energy from surroundings
    Explanation: Exothermic means 'heat out.' Combustion reactions are always exothermic — they release energy as heat and light, which is why fuels are useful energy sources.

  Q21. A student mixes baking soda (NaHCO₃) and vinegar (acetic acid). Bubbles form. What type of reaction is this?
    [ ] Combustion, particularly
    [ ] Synthesis, particularly
    [*] Double replacement (producing CO₂ gas)
    [ ] Decomposition based on the
    Explanation: An acid reacting with a carbonate is a double replacement reaction. The carbonate product immediately decomposes to CO₂ gas (the bubbles) and water.

  Q22. Rust forms when iron is exposed to oxygen and moisture: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃. This is which reaction type?
    [ ] Decomposition
    [*] Synthesis
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: Iron and oxygen combine to form a single product (iron(III) oxide), fitting the pattern A + B → AB. This is a synthesis reaction.

  Q23. Heating mercury(II) oxide produces liquid mercury and oxygen gas: 2HgO → 2Hg + O₂. What type of reaction is this?
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Double replacement
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Synthesis
    Explanation: One compound (HgO) breaks down into simpler substances (Hg and O₂). This is the hallmark of a decomposition reaction.

  Q24. A student drops a zinc strip into copper(II) sulfate solution and sees copper metal deposit. What reaction type occurred?
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Decomposition
    [ ] Double replacement
    [*] Single replacement
    Explanation: Zinc is higher than copper on the activity series, so it displaces copper: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. A free element replaced another in a compound — single replacement.

  Q25. A car engine burns gasoline (octane, C₈H₁₈) with air. What reaction type powers the engine?
    [*] Combustion
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    Explanation: Gasoline (a hydrocarbon) reacts with O₂ from air to produce CO₂ and H₂O, releasing enormous energy as heat. This is combustion.

  Q26. Silver nitrate is mixed with sodium chloride in solution. A white solid (AgCl) forms. What reaction type is this?
    [ ] Decomposition as typically
    [ ] Combustion as determined
    [*] Double replacement (precipitation)
    [ ] Synthesis, particularly
    Explanation: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃. Two compounds swap ions, and the insoluble AgCl precipitate forms. This is a double replacement reaction.

  Q27. Electrolysis of water produces hydrogen and oxygen gas: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. A student must classify this reaction. What type is it?
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Combustion
    Explanation: A single compound (water) is broken down into simpler substances (H₂ and O₂) using electrical energy. This fits the decomposition pattern AB → A + B.

  Q28. A student tries to react gold with hydrochloric acid, but nothing happens. Why?
    [*] Gold is below hydrogen on the activity series and cannot displace it
    [ ] Gold only reacts at high temperatures
    [ ] HCl is not an acid
    [ ] Single replacement reactions never happen with metals
    Explanation: Gold is extremely low on the activity series — it cannot displace hydrogen from HCl. The activity series predicts that no single replacement reaction will occur.

  Q29. Fireworks contain metal salts that burn in air. Why are fireworks classified as combustion reactions?
    [*] The metal compounds react rapidly with O₂, releasing heat and light
    [ ] They produce precipitates
    [ ] They require no oxygen
    [ ] They only involve decomposition of compounds
    Explanation: Fireworks involve combustion because substances react with oxygen at high speed, releasing energy as heat and brilliant light of various colors from excited metal ions.

  Q30. In a lab, mixing lead(II) nitrate and potassium iodide solutions produces a bright yellow solid. What is the yellow solid and what reaction type produced it?
    [*] Lead(II) iodide precipitate from a double replacement reaction
    [ ] Potassium nitrate from a synthesis reaction
    [ ] Elemental lead from a decomposition reaction
    [ ] Nitrogen gas from a single replacement reaction
    Explanation: Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂↓ + 2KNO₃. The ions swap partners (double replacement), and bright yellow lead(II) iodide is the insoluble precipitate.

--- Lesson 6.2: Combustion Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Combustion Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is meant by 'exothermic' in the context of combustion?
Exothermic ('exo' = out, 'thermic' = heat) means the reaction releases energy. Combustion is strongly exothermic, which is why burning fuels heats surroundings.
What is 'soot'?
Soot is finely divided carbon (C) that results when there is insufficient oxygen to convert all carbon to CO₂ during combustion.
What is 'activation energy' in the context of starting a fire?
A spark or match provides the activation energy to start combustion. Once the reaction begins, it releases enough heat to sustain itself.
What is the meaning of 'ignition temperature'?
Ignition temperature is the threshold temperature at which a substance begins sustained combustion without needing a continued external heat source.
A CO detector alarm goes off in a home with a gas furnace. What is likely happening?
CO detectors sense carbon monoxide. If CO is accumulating, the furnace's air intake is likely restricted, causing incomplete combustion and producing toxic CO instead of CO₂.

--- Lesson 6.2: Combustion Reactions (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A combustion reaction always requires which gas as a reactant?
    [ ] Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
    [ ] Hydrogen (H₂)
    [*] Oxygen (O₂)
    [ ] Nitrogen (N₂)
    Explanation: By definition, combustion is the rapid reaction of a substance with oxygen. Without O₂, combustion cannot occur.

  Q2. Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon produces:
    [ ] CO₂ and H₂
    [ ] CO and H₂O
    [ ] C and H₂O
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    Explanation: When a hydrocarbon burns in excess oxygen, all carbon is converted to CO₂ and all hydrogen to H₂O — these are the only products of complete combustion.

  Q3. Incomplete combustion occurs when there is:
    [*] Insufficient oxygen
    [ ] Too much fuel
    [ ] No fuel present
    [ ] Excess oxygen
    Explanation: When the oxygen supply is limited, the fuel cannot burn completely. This produces CO (carbon monoxide) or soot (C) instead of CO₂.

  Q4. Products of incomplete combustion include:
    [ ] Only CO₂ and H₂O
    [*] Carbon monoxide (CO) or soot (C)
    [ ] Nitrogen oxides only
    [ ] Noble gases
    Explanation: With insufficient O₂, not all carbon is fully oxidized to CO₂. Instead, toxic CO or solid carbon (soot) forms.

  Q5. Combustion reactions are always:
    [*] Exothermic (release energy)
    [ ] Neither exo- nor endothermic
    [ ] Endothermic (absorb energy)
    [ ] Always slow
    Explanation: Combustion releases energy as heat and light, making it exothermic. This energy release is why fossil fuels are used for power.

  Q6. Which equation represents a combustion reaction?
    [ ] AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
    [ ] NaCl → Na + Cl₂
    [ ] Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
    [*] CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
    Explanation: CH₄ (methane, a hydrocarbon) reacting with O₂ to produce CO₂ and H₂O is a classic combustion reaction. The other options are decomposition, double replacement, and single replacement.

  Q7. The primary fuel in a combustion reaction is usually:
    [*] A hydrocarbon or organic compound
    [ ] A metal oxide
    [ ] Pure oxygen
    [ ] A noble gas
    Explanation: Fuels like methane (CH₄), propane (C₃H₈), and octane (C₈H₁₈) are hydrocarbons. Organic compounds containing C and H readily undergo combustion.

  Q8. What phase is water in immediately after a high-temperature combustion?
    [ ] Solid (ice)
    [*] Gas (steam/vapor)
    [ ] Plasma
    [ ] Liquid
    Explanation: At the high temperatures produced by combustion, water exists as steam (gaseous H₂O). It may condense to liquid as it cools.

  Q9. Why is carbon monoxide (CO) from incomplete combustion dangerous?
    [*] It is a colorless, odorless toxic gas that binds to hemoglobin
    [ ] It causes explosions on contact with water
    [ ] It is a strong acid
    [ ] It is radioactive
    Explanation: CO binds to hemoglobin ~200× more tightly than O₂, blocking oxygen transport in the blood. Because CO is colorless and odorless, victims may not notice exposure.

  Q10. What distinguishes complete from incomplete combustion?
    [ ] Complete occurs at lower temperatures
    [*] Complete has sufficient O₂ giving CO₂ and H₂O; incomplete has limited O₂ giving CO or C
    [ ] Incomplete always produces NO₂
    [ ] There is no real difference
    Explanation: The key variable is oxygen supply. Excess O₂ leads to complete combustion (CO₂ + H₂O). Limited O₂ leads to incomplete combustion with CO, C, or both.

  Q11. What does the term 'hydrocarbon' mean?
    [ ] A compound containing carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen
    [*] A compound composed only of carbon and hydrogen atoms
    [ ] Any compound containing water
    [ ] A mixture of hydrogen gas and carbon dioxide
    Explanation: Hydrocarbons contain only H and C (e.g., CH₄, C₂H₆, C₈H₁₈). Compounds that also contain O, N, or other elements are not pure hydrocarbons.

  Q12. What is meant by 'exothermic' in the context of combustion?
    [ ] The products weigh more than the reactants
    [ ] The reaction absorbs heat from the surroundings
    [ ] The reaction occurs at constant temperature
    [*] The reaction releases energy to the surroundings as heat and light
    Explanation: Exothermic ('exo' = out, 'thermic' = heat) means the reaction releases energy. Combustion is strongly exothermic, which is why burning fuels heats surroundings.

  Q13. Define 'fuel' in chemistry.
    [ ] Any substance that is liquid at room temperature
    [ ] An element found in Group 1 of the periodic table
    [*] A substance that undergoes combustion to release usable energy
    [ ] A catalyst for industrial reactions
    Explanation: A fuel is a material that stores chemical energy and releases it via combustion. Common fuels include natural gas (CH₄), propane, gasoline, and wood.

  Q14. What is 'soot'?
    [ ] An allotrope of oxygen
    [ ] Water vapor from complete combustion
    [ ] A type of catalyst
    [*] Solid carbon particles produced by incomplete combustion
    Explanation: Soot is finely divided carbon (C) that results when there is insufficient oxygen to convert all carbon to CO₂ during combustion.

  Q15. What does 'complete combustion' specifically require?
    [ ] High pressure only
    [*] Sufficient (excess) oxygen to fully oxidize the fuel
    [ ] The absence of any nitrogen
    [ ] A metal catalyst
    Explanation: Complete combustion means every carbon becomes CO₂ and every hydrogen becomes H₂O. This only happens when enough O₂ is present for full oxidation.

  Q16. What does the general equation CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O represent?
    [ ] Single replacement in organic chemistry
    [ ] Decomposition of a hydrocarbon
    [*] Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon
    [ ] Synthesis of a hydrocarbon
    Explanation: This is the general template for complete combustion: a hydrocarbon plus excess oxygen yields carbon dioxide and water.

  Q17. What is 'activation energy' in the context of starting a fire?
    [*] The minimum energy needed to initiate the combustion reaction
    [ ] The temperature of the products
    [ ] The total energy released by the fire
    [ ] The mass of fuel required
    Explanation: A spark or match provides the activation energy to start combustion. Once the reaction begins, it releases enough heat to sustain itself.

  Q18. What is the meaning of 'ignition temperature'?
    [ ] The maximum temperature a flame can reach
    [*] The minimum temperature at which a fuel begins to burn in air
    [ ] The boiling point of the fuel
    [ ] The temperature at which oxygen liquefies

  Q19. Define 'incomplete combustion' in your own scientific terms.
    [*] Burning with insufficient O₂, yielding CO, C (soot), or both instead of only CO₂
    [ ] Burning that produces no heat
    [ ] A reaction that never finishes
    [ ] A reaction where the fuel is left over

    [ ] The furnace is producing too much CO₂
    [ ] The detector is sensing water vapor

    [ ] CO and soot
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    [ ] Nitrogen gas and water

    Explanation: Kerosene is a hydrocarbon. Complete combustion in a jet engine (with excess O₂) produces CO₂ and H₂O, same as any hydrocarbon combustion.

    [ ] Add more fuel to make the fire bigger

    [ ] Seal the cave entrance to contain heat

    Explanation: The orange flame indicates incomplete combustion (low O₂). Improving ventilation brings in fresh air, supplying more O₂ and shifting toward complete, efficient combustion.

  Q30. A chemistry teacher burns magnesium ribbon in air: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO. Is this a combustion reaction even though Mg is not a hydrocarbon?

    [ ] No — this is a synthesis reaction only

    [ ] Yes — but only because MgO is a gas
    Explanation: Combustion includes any vigorous reaction with O₂ that produces heat and light. Metals like Mg burn brightly in air, qualifying as combustion even though they are not hydrocarbons.

--- Lesson 6.3: Redox Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Redox Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is meant by 'electron transfer' in the context of redox?
Redox reactions fundamentally involve electrons physically moving from one species (reducing agent) to another (oxidizing agent), changing their oxidation states.
What is the meaning of 'disproportionation'?
Disproportionation is a special redox reaction where atoms of the same element in one substance are simultaneously oxidized and reduced (e.g., 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂).
Bleach whitens stains on clothing. In redox terms, what is bleach doing?
Bleach (typically sodium hypochlorite) is a strong oxidizing agent. It oxidizes chromophore molecules in the stain, breaking their color-producing bonds.
In cellular respiration, glucose is broken down: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O. What is oxidized and what is reduced?
Carbon in glucose goes from a low oxidation state to +4 in CO₂ (oxidized). Oxygen goes from 0 in O₂ to -2 in H₂O and CO₂ (reduced).
Real-World Applications
Example: Define 'oxidizing agent.'
The oxidizing agent accepts electrons (is itself reduced), which forces the other reactant to lose electrons (be oxidized). It 'causes' oxidation in another species.
Example: Define 'reducing agent.'
The reducing agent donates electrons (is itself oxidized), supplying the electrons needed for the other species to be reduced.

--- Lesson 6.3: Redox Reactions (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Oxidation is defined as the:
    [ ] Gain of neutrons
    [*] Loss of electrons
    [ ] Gain of electrons
    [ ] Loss of protons
    Explanation: Oxidation means losing electrons. The mnemonic OIL (Oxidation Is Loss) helps remember this. When a species loses electrons, its oxidation number increases.

  Q2. Reduction is defined as the:
    [ ] Gain of protons
    [ ] Loss of mass
    [*] Gain of electrons
    [ ] Loss of electrons
    Explanation: Reduction means gaining electrons. The mnemonic RIG (Reduction Is Gain) helps. When a species gains electrons, its oxidation number decreases (is 'reduced').

  Q3. In a redox reaction, oxidation and reduction occur:
    [ ] Only oxidation occurs
    [*] Simultaneously
    [ ] At different times
    [ ] In separate containers
    Explanation: In every redox reaction, one species is oxidized while another is simultaneously reduced. Electrons lost by one are gained by the other.

  Q4. The substance that is reduced in a redox reaction is called the:
    [*] Oxidizing agent
    [ ] Spectator ion
    [ ] Catalyst
    [ ] Reducing agent
    Explanation: The oxidizing agent gains electrons (is reduced) and thereby causes another species to be oxidized. It is called 'oxidizing' because it oxidizes the other substance.

  Q5. The substance that is oxidized in a redox reaction is called the:
    [ ] Product
    [ ] Inhibitor
    [*] Reducing agent
    [ ] Oxidizing agent
    Explanation: The reducing agent loses electrons (is oxidized) and donates them to the oxidizing agent, causing it to be reduced.

  Q6. In Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu, which species is oxidized?
    [ ] Cu (copper metal)
    [ ] Zn²⁺
    [ ] Cu²⁺ (copper ion)
    [*] Zn (zinc)
    Explanation: Zinc goes from 0 to +2 oxidation state, meaning it lost 2 electrons. Losing electrons = oxidation. Zn is the reducing agent.

  Q7. OIL RIG stands for:
    [ ] Oxidation Is Last, Reduction Is Great
    [ ] Oxygen Is Lost, Reduction Is Gained
    [*] Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain
    [ ] Only Ions Lose, Reactants In Gain
    Explanation: OIL RIG is a popular mnemonic: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). It helps students remember which process involves which electron change.

  Q8. When an atom's oxidation number increases, the atom has been:
    [*] Oxidized
    [ ] Reduced
    [ ] Catalyzed
    [ ] Neutralized
    Explanation: An increase in oxidation number means the atom lost electrons (became more positive). Losing electrons = oxidation.

  Q9. In the reaction 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl, sodium is:
    [*] Oxidized (loses an electron to become Na⁺)
    [ ] Reduced (gains an electron)
    [ ] Unchanged
    [ ] Acting as a catalyst
    Explanation: Sodium goes from 0 to +1 — it loses one electron per atom. Losing electrons is oxidation. Chlorine gains those electrons (reduction).

  Q10. The oxidation number of any pure, uncombined element is:
    [ ] +1
    [ ] -1
    [*] 0
    [ ] +2
    Explanation: By convention, any element in its free, uncombined state (e.g., Fe, O₂, N₂, Na) has an oxidation number of zero.

  Q11. What does 'oxidation number' (oxidation state) represent?
    [ ] The number of bonds an atom forms
    [*] The apparent charge on an atom in a compound, tracking electron distribution
    [ ] The mass of the atom
    [ ] The number of protons in an atom
    Explanation: Oxidation numbers are bookkeeping tools that assign an apparent charge to each atom in a compound, helping track which atoms lose or gain electrons in redox reactions.

  Q12. Define 'oxidizing agent.'
    [*] The substance that is reduced and causes another substance to be oxidized
    [ ] A substance unchanged by the reaction
    [ ] The substance that is oxidized and donates electrons
    [ ] A substance that prevents oxidation
    Explanation: The oxidizing agent accepts electrons (is itself reduced), which forces the other reactant to lose electrons (be oxidized). It 'causes' oxidation in another species.

  Q13. Define 'reducing agent.'
    [ ] The substance that gains electrons
    [*] The substance that is oxidized and donates electrons to reduce another substance
    [ ] A catalyst that speeds up reduction
    [ ] An inert gas
    Explanation: The reducing agent donates electrons (is itself oxidized), supplying the electrons needed for the other species to be reduced.

  Q14. What does the term 'half-reaction' mean in redox chemistry?
    [ ] The point where equilibrium is reached
    [ ] A reaction that is only half complete
    [*] An equation showing only the oxidation or only the reduction part of a redox reaction
    [ ] A reaction involving half the atoms
    Explanation: Half-reactions separate the electron loss (oxidation half) and electron gain (reduction half) so each process can be analyzed independently.

    [ ] Energy transfers but no particles move
    [*] Electrons move from the reducing agent to the oxidizing agent
    [ ] Protons move between atoms
    Explanation: Redox reactions fundamentally involve electrons physically moving from one species (reducing agent) to another (oxidizing agent), changing their oxidation states.

    [ ] The reduced species gains protons
    [*] The reduced species gains electrons, lowering its oxidation number
    [ ] The reduced species gains energy only
    [ ] The reduced species gains mass
    Explanation: Reduction IS Gain of electrons. By gaining negatively charged electrons, the atom's oxidation number decreases — it is 'reduced.'

  Q17. What does 'redox' stand for?
    [*] Reduction-oxidation
    [ ] Reduced oxygen
    [ ] Reverse oxidation
    [ ] Reactive-deoxidation
    Explanation: Redox is a portmanteau of 'reduction' and 'oxidation.' It describes any reaction in which electron transfer occurs between species.

    [*] It reveals which atoms lost or gained electrons, confirming a redox reaction
    Explanation: Tracking oxidation number changes across a reaction identifies which species were oxidized (number increased) and reduced (number decreased), confirming it is a redox process.

    [ ] A reaction with unequal masses of reactants
    [*] A reaction where the same element is both oxidized and reduced

--- Lesson 6.4: Activation Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Activation Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'activated complex' (transition state)?
The activated complex is a fleeting, high-energy intermediate where old bonds are partly broken and new bonds partly formed. It cannot be isolated.
What is a 'catalyst' in chemistry?
A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower Ea. It participates mechanistically but is regenerated, emerging unchanged after the reaction.
What is an 'energy diagram' (potential energy diagram)?
An energy diagram shows energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis. It visually displays reactant/product energy levels, Ea, and the transition state.
Hydrogen peroxide decomposes slowly on its own but rapidly with MnO₂ added. What is MnO₂ in this case?
MnO₂ lowers the activation energy for the decomposition of H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂. It can be recovered unchanged after the reaction, confirming it is a catalyst.

--- Lesson 6.4: Activation Energy (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Activation energy is the minimum energy needed to:
    [ ] End a chemical reaction
    [ ] Cool down products
    [ ] Create an element
    [*] Start a chemical reaction
    Explanation: Activation energy (Ea) is the energy barrier that reactant molecules must overcome for a reaction to begin. Without enough Ea, no reaction occurs.

  Q2. On an energy diagram, activation energy is represented by the:
    [ ] Total energy of the products
    [*] Height of the energy barrier between reactants and the peak
    [ ] Width of the graph
    [ ] Difference between products and reactants
    Explanation: On an energy diagram, Ea is measured from the energy level of the reactants up to the peak (transition state). It's the 'hill' the reactants must climb.

  Q3. A catalyst speeds up a reaction by:
    [*] Lowering the activation energy
    [ ] Adding more reactant
    [ ] Increasing the temperature
    [ ] Changing the products
    Explanation: A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, allowing more molecules to react at a given temperature without the catalyst being consumed.

  Q4. In an exothermic reaction, the products have:
    [*] Lower energy than the reactants
    [ ] No energy
    [ ] Higher energy than the reactants
    [ ] Equal energy to the reactants
    Explanation: Exothermic reactions release energy to the surroundings. The products sit at a lower energy level than the reactants on the energy diagram.

  Q5. In an endothermic reaction, the products have:
    [*] Higher energy than the reactants
    [ ] Lower energy than the reactants
    [ ] No activation energy
    [ ] The same energy as the reactants
    Explanation: Endothermic reactions absorb energy from the surroundings. Products are at a higher energy level than reactants, and ΔH is positive.

  Q6. The activated complex (transition state) exists at which point on the energy diagram?
    [ ] At the lowest energy point
    [ ] At the product energy level
    [*] At the highest energy point (the peak)
    [ ] At the reactant energy level

  Q7. Without sufficient activation energy, a reaction will:
    [*] Not occur
    [ ] Become endothermic
    [ ] Proceed faster
    [ ] Release more energy

    [ ] Is always destroyed
    [*] Can be recovered unchanged after the reaction
    [ ] Changes into a different element
    [ ] Becomes one of the products
    Explanation: A catalyst participates mechanistically in the reaction but is regenerated. It emerges unchanged, ready to catalyze additional reaction cycles.

  Q9. What does ΔH represent on an energy diagram?
    [*] The energy difference between products and reactants
    [ ] The activation energy

    Explanation: ΔH (enthalpy change) is the vertical distance between reactant and product energy levels. Negative ΔH = exothermic; positive ΔH = endothermic.

    [*] The overall energy change (ΔH) of the reaction

    [*] An unstable, high-energy arrangement of atoms at the top of the energy barrier

    Explanation: The activated complex is a fleeting, high-energy intermediate where old bonds are partly broken and new bonds partly formed. It cannot be isolated.

  Q13. What does 'exothermic' mean?

--- Lesson 6.5: Balancing Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Balancing Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the first step in balancing a chemical equation?
Step 1: Write the correct formulas for all reactants and products. Then count atoms, add coefficients, and verify the balance.
What is a 'coefficient' in a chemical equation?
A coefficient is the whole number placed before a chemical formula (e.g., the 3 in 3H₂O). It tells how many molecules or moles of that substance participate.
What is a 'subscript' in a chemical formula?
Subscripts are the small numbers after element symbols (e.g., the 2 in H₂O means 2 hydrogen atoms). Changing a subscript changes what compound it is.
What is a 'skeleton equation'?
A skeleton equation shows the correct chemical formulas for reactants and products but hasn't had coefficients adjusted yet — it's the starting point for balancing.
A lab report says: 'Mg + O₂ → MgO.' The student needs to balance it. What is the balanced equation?
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO: 2 Mg and 2 O on each side. Options B and D illegally change subscripts or element formulas.

  Key Concepts: Reaction Rates & Catalysts
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'activation energy' in the context of collision theory?
For a collision to be effective, the combined kinetic energy of the colliding molecules must equal or exceed the activation energy barrier.
What is an 'inhibitor'?

--- Lesson 6.7: Chemical Equilibrium & Le Chatelier’s Principle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Chemical Equilibrium & Le Chatelier’s Principle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'reversible reaction'?
Reversible reactions proceed in both directions: reactants → products AND products → reactants. The double arrow ⇌ distinguishes them from irreversible reactions (→).
What is the 'equilibrium constant' (Keq)?
Keq = [products]^coefficients / [reactants]^coefficients. It is a fixed value at a given temperature and tells how far the reaction proceeds toward products.
What is a 'stress' in the context of Le Chatelier's principle?
A stress is any change that disrupts equilibrium. Common stresses include adding/removing reactants or products, changing temperature, and changing pressure for gaseous systems.
What is the 'reaction quotient' (Q)?
Q has the same formula as K but is calculated at any moment, not just equilibrium. If Q < K the reaction shifts right; if Q > K it shifts left; if Q = K the system is at equilibrium.

    [ ] The reaction stops
    [ ] The reverse reaction speeds up
    [ ] All reactants turn into products permanently
    Explanation: Shifting right means the forward reaction temporarily becomes faster than the reverse, consuming reactants and forming more products until a new equilibrium is established.

  Q18. What does K << 1 indicate about a reaction?
    [*] Reactants are favored — the mixture is mostly reactants at equilibrium
    [ ] Products are heavily favored
    [ ] The reaction is very fast
    [ ] The reaction does not occur
    Explanation: When K is much less than 1, the denominator (reactants) is larger than the numerator (products). The reaction barely proceeds toward products before equilibrium is reached.

  Q19. What is the 'reaction quotient' (Q)?
    [*] The ratio of products to reactants at any point in the reaction, used to predict which way the reaction will shift
    [ ] The mass of products formed, particularly regarding just when measured under controlled conditions
    [ ] The same as the equilibrium constant at all times as observed in standard experiments
    [ ] The temperature of the reaction, particularly regarding moment in most standard conditions
    Explanation: Q has the same formula as K but is calculated at any moment, not just equilibrium. If Q < K the reaction shifts right; if Q > K it shifts left; if Q = K the system is at equilibrium.

  Q20. What does 'position of equilibrium' refer to?
    [ ] The color of the solution
    [*] The relative proportions of reactants and products at equilibrium
    [ ] The speed of the reaction
    [ ] The physical location of the reaction vessel
    Explanation: The position of equilibrium describes whether the mixture favors products (lies to the right) or reactants (lies to the left). It is reflected in the value of K.

  Q21. In the Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, exothermic), factories use high pressure. Why does this favor ammonia production?
    [ ] Pressure acts as a catalyst
    [ ] Pressure breaks the N≡N triple bond directly
    [*] High pressure shifts equilibrium toward fewer gas moles — 4 moles of gas become 2 moles of NH₃
    [ ] Pressure increases the temperature
    Explanation: The left side has 4 moles of gas (1 N₂ + 3 H₂) and the right side has 2 moles (2 NH₃). Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium toward fewer gas moles — right, favoring ammonia.

  Q22. Carbonated drinks go flat faster when opened and left at room temperature. Which equilibrium concept explains this?
    [ ] A catalyst is added when the bottle is opened
    [ ] Sugar decomposes at room temperature
    [*] Removing CO₂ gas (opening) and higher temperature both shift CO₂(aq) ⇌ CO₂(g) to the right
    [ ] The drink reaches a new equilibrium with more dissolved CO₂
    Explanation: When the cap is removed, CO₂ escapes, lowering its partial pressure above the liquid. By Le Chatelier's principle, more dissolved CO₂ comes out of solution. Warmth further decreases gas solubility.

  Q23. An industrial chemist wants to increase the yield of product C in the reaction: A + B ⇌ C + heat. Which action would help?
    [ ] Raise the temperature, particularly regarding replace in the
    [ ] Remove A from the mixture in the context of this analysis
    [ ] Add more product C, particularly regarding more as typically
    [*] Lower the temperature to shift equilibrium toward the exothermic (product) side
    Explanation: Since the forward reaction is exothermic (produces heat), lowering temperature is like removing heat — a product. The system shifts right to replace the lost heat, making more C.

  Q24. A sealed container holds the equilibrium N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2 NO₂(g). The colorless N₂O₄ is on the left and brown NO₂ on the right. If the container is placed in ice, the gas becomes lighter in color. What happened?
    [ ] The equilibrium shifted right
    [ ] The cold destroyed the NO₂ molecules
    [*] Cooling shifted equilibrium left toward colorless N₂O₄, decreasing the brown NO₂
    [ ] Ice added a catalyst
    Explanation: The forward reaction (N₂O₄ → 2 NO₂) is endothermic. Cooling removes heat, so the system shifts in the exothermic direction (left), forming more colorless N₂O₄ and less brown NO₂.

  Q25. In a closed limekiln, CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g). A worker opens the kiln to vent CO₂. What happens to the equilibrium?
    [ ] Equilibrium shifts left
    [ ] The reaction stops
    [ ] No change — solids don't affect equilibrium
    [*] Equilibrium shifts right — more CaCO₃ decomposes to replace the lost CO₂
    Explanation: Removing CO₂ decreases product concentration. By Le Chatelier's principle, the system shifts right to produce more CO₂ (and CaO), consuming more CaCO₃.

  Q26. A student adds a catalyst to an equilibrium mixture and observes that concentrations don't change. Why?
    [*] A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally — it does not change the position of equilibrium
    [ ] Catalysts only affect the forward reaction in most standard conditions
    [ ] The catalyst was defective, particularly regarding that when applying fundamental principles
    [ ] The reaction was already at maximum rate based on the prevailing evidence
    Explanation: Catalysts lower the activation energy for both directions equally. They help the system reach equilibrium faster but do not change the value of K or the equilibrium position.

  Q27. Blood carries O₂ via hemoglobin: Hb + O₂ ⇌ HbO₂. At high altitude where O₂ is low, what happens?
    [*] Equilibrium shifts left — less HbO₂ forms, causing altitude sickness
    [ ] The equilibrium is unaffected
    [ ] More HbO₂ is produced
    [ ] Hemoglobin breaks down permanently
    Explanation: Low O₂ concentration at altitude decreases the reactant level. By Le Chatelier's, equilibrium shifts left — less HbO₂ forms, meaning less oxygen delivery to tissues.

  Q28. In the Contact process for sulfuric acid, 2 SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2 SO₃ (exothermic). Why is a moderate temperature (~450°C) used instead of a very low one?
    [ ] Higher temperature increases the value of K
    [ ] Temperature has no effect on this reaction
    [ ] Low temperature shifts equilibrium right so there is no need for a catalyst
    [*] Low temperature favors products but makes the reaction too slow — moderate temperature balances yield and rate
    Explanation: Le Chatelier's says low T favors the exothermic direction (more SO₃), but at very low T the reaction is impractically slow. Industrial conditions balance thermodynamic yield with kinetic speed.

  Q29. A beaker contains CoCl₄²⁻ (blue) ⇌ Co(H₂O)₆²⁺ (pink). Adding HCl turns the solution blue. Adding water turns it pink. Explain.
    [ ] HCl is a catalyst for the blue form
    [ ] Water destroys the cobalt complex
    [ ] Temperature changes cause the color shift
    [*] HCl adds Cl⁻ (reactant), shifting left toward blue; water dilutes Cl⁻, shifting right toward pink
    Explanation: Adding Cl⁻ ions increases reactant concentration for the CoCl₄²⁻ side, shifting left (blue). Adding water dilutes Cl⁻, shifting right toward the aqua complex (pink). Classic Le Chatelier demonstration.

  Q30. A reaction at equilibrium has K = 1. A student doubles the concentration of one reactant. What happens next?
    [ ] The reaction shifts left
    [ ] Nothing — K = 1 means the reaction cannot shift
    [*] Q < K, so the reaction shifts right (toward products) until a new equilibrium is established
    [ ] The value of K changes to 2
    Explanation: Adding reactant increases the denominator in Q, making Q < K. The system shifts right to restore equilibrium. K itself does not change (it is constant at a given temperature).
