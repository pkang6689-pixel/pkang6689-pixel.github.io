--- Lesson 6.1:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Reactions
Core Definitions

  • Synthesis (Combination): Two or more reactants combine to form one product: A + B → AB.
  • Decomposition: One compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances: AB → A + B.
  • Single Replacement: One element replaces another in a compound: A + BC → AC + B.
  • Double Replacement: Ions in two compounds exchange partners: AB + CD → AD + CB.
  • Combustion: A substance reacts with oxygen, releasing energy as heat and light.
  • Precipitate: An insoluble solid formed in a double replacement reaction.

--- Lesson 6.1:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. In a synthesis reaction, two or more substances combine to form:
    [*] One product
    [ ] Two products
    [ ] No products
    [ ] Only a gas
    Explanation: Synthesis (combination) reactions follow the pattern A + B → AB, where multiple reactants combine to form a single, more complex product.

  Q2. AB → A + B represents which type of reaction?
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Combustion
    Explanation: Decomposition is the reverse of synthesis — one compound breaks apart into two or more simpler substances: AB → A + B.

  Q3. In a single replacement reaction, what happens?
    [*] One element replaces another in a compound
    [ ] Two compounds swap ions
    [ ] A compound breaks apart
    [ ] Two elements combine
    Explanation: Single replacement follows A + BC → AC + B, where a lone element displaces another element from a compound based on activity.

  Q4. What are the products of complete combustion of a hydrocarbon?
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    [ ] CO and H₂
    [ ] Only CO₂
    [ ] C and H₂O
    Explanation: Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon in excess oxygen always produces carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O).

  Q5. In a double replacement reaction, AB + CD →
    [*] AD + CB
    [ ] AC + BD
    [ ] ABCD
    [ ] A + B + C + D
    Explanation: In double replacement, the cations of two ionic compounds trade anion partners: AB + CD → AD + CB.

  Q6. An insoluble solid formed in a double replacement reaction is called a:
    [*] Precipitate
    [ ] Catalyst
    [ ] Solute
    [ ] Electrolyte
    Explanation: When two aqueous solutions mix in a double replacement reaction and form an insoluble product, that solid is called a precipitate.

  Q7. Which reaction type ALWAYS requires oxygen as a reactant?
    [*] Combustion
    [ ] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: Combustion is defined as the rapid reaction of a substance with oxygen, producing heat and light. Oxygen is always a required reactant.

  Q8. A + B → AB is the general form of which reaction type?
    [*] Synthesis
    [ ] Decomposition
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: Synthesis (combination) reactions have the general form A + B → AB. Two or more simpler substances combine to form one complex product.

  Q9. The activity series ranks elements by their ability to:
    [*] Displace other elements in single replacement reactions
    [ ] Conduct heat
    [ ] Form precipitates
    [ ] Resist decomposition
    Explanation: The activity series is a ranking of metals (and hydrogen) by their reactivity. A higher-ranked element can displace a lower-ranked one from a compound in a single replacement reaction.

  Q10. Which reaction type has only ONE reactant?
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: Decomposition reactions start with a single compound that breaks down into two or more simpler substances (AB → A + B), so there is only one reactant.

  Q11. What is the term for a reaction in which two or more reactants form a single product?
    [*] Synthesis (combination) reaction
    [ ] Decomposition reaction
    [ ] Neutralization reaction
    [ ] Endothermic reaction
    Explanation: A synthesis or combination reaction combines two or more substances into one product. It is distinct from decomposition, which does the opposite.

  Q12. Define 'precipitate' in the context of chemical reactions.
    [*] An insoluble solid product formed during a double replacement reaction
    [ ] A gaseous product that escapes
    [ ] A catalyst that speeds up the reaction
    [ ] A dissolved ionic compound
    Explanation: A precipitate is an insoluble solid that forms when ions in two aqueous reactants combine during a double replacement reaction.

  Q13. What does the term 'combustion' mean in chemistry?
    [*] A rapid exothermic reaction of a substance with oxygen
    [ ] The slow oxidation of iron
    [ ] Any reaction that produces heat
    [ ] Breaking a compound into elements
    Explanation: Combustion specifically refers to a substance reacting rapidly with oxygen, producing heat and light. While rusting involves oxygen, it is too slow to be called combustion.

  Q14. What is the 'activity series' used for?
    [*] Predicting whether a single replacement reaction will occur
    [ ] Balancing chemical equations
    [ ] Classifying acids and bases
    [ ] Determining molar mass
    Explanation: The activity series ranks metals by reactivity. A metal higher on the series can replace a lower metal from its compound; if not, no reaction occurs.

  Q15. What distinguishes a single replacement from a double replacement reaction?
    [*] Single replacement involves one element and one compound; double involves two compounds
    [ ] Single replacement uses oxygen; double does not
    [ ] Single replacement produces gas; double produces solid
    [ ] There is no difference
    Explanation: In single replacement (A + BC → AC + B), a free element displaces one in a compound. In double replacement (AB + CD → AD + CB), two compounds exchange ions.

  Q16. Define 'decomposition reaction.'
    [*] A reaction where one compound breaks apart into two or more simpler substances
    [ ] A reaction where two elements combine into a compound
    [ ] A reaction that always produces a gas
    [ ] A reaction that requires a catalyst
    Explanation: Decomposition reactions break a single compound into two or more simpler products. Energy (heat, light, or electricity) is often required to drive them.

  Q17. What is the meaning of 'double replacement' in chemistry?
    [*] Two ionic compounds exchange cations and anions to form new compounds
    [ ] Two elements switch energy levels
    [ ] A compound breaks into exactly two parts
    [ ] Two reactions happen simultaneously
    Explanation: Double replacement (metathesis) reactions involve two ionic compounds in solution swapping their cation–anion partners: AB + CD → AD + CB.

  Q18. What does the term 'hydrocarbon' refer to?
    [*] A compound containing only carbon and hydrogen
    [ ] Any compound containing oxygen
    [ ] A charged ion in solution
    [ ] An element found in Group 14
    Explanation: Hydrocarbons are organic molecules made entirely of carbon and hydrogen atoms (e.g., CH₄ methane, C₃H₈ propane). They are common fuels for combustion.

  Q19. What is a 'reactant' versus a 'product'?
    [*] Reactants are starting substances; products are substances formed
    [ ] Reactants are on the right side of the arrow
    [ ] Products are always gases
    [ ] There is no difference
    Explanation: Reactants appear on the left side of the arrow and are consumed. Products appear on the right and are formed during the reaction.

  Q20. What does the word 'exothermic' mean when describing combustion?
    [*] The reaction releases energy as heat and light
    [ ] The reaction absorbs energy from surroundings
    [ ] The reaction occurs at extreme temperatures only
    [ ] The reaction does not involve energy changes
    Explanation: Exothermic means 'heat out.' Combustion reactions are always exothermic — they release energy as heat and light, which is why fuels are useful energy sources.

  Q21. A student mixes baking soda (NaHCO₃) and vinegar (acetic acid). Bubbles form. What type of reaction is this?
    [*] Double replacement (producing CO₂ gas)
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Decomposition
    Explanation: An acid reacting with a carbonate is a double replacement reaction. The carbonate product immediately decomposes to CO₂ gas (the bubbles) and water.

  Q22. Rust forms when iron is exposed to oxygen and moisture: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃. This is which reaction type?
    [*] Synthesis
    [ ] Decomposition
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: Iron and oxygen combine to form a single product (iron(III) oxide), fitting the pattern A + B → AB. This is a synthesis reaction.

  Q23. Heating mercury(II) oxide produces liquid mercury and oxygen gas: 2HgO → 2Hg + O₂. What type of reaction is this?
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Double replacement
    Explanation: One compound (HgO) breaks down into simpler substances (Hg and O₂). This is the hallmark of a decomposition reaction.

  Q24. A student drops a zinc strip into copper(II) sulfate solution and sees copper metal deposit. What reaction type occurred?
    [*] Single replacement
    [ ] Double replacement
    [ ] Decomposition
    [ ] Combustion
    Explanation: Zinc is higher than copper on the activity series, so it displaces copper: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. A free element replaced another in a compound — single replacement.

  Q25. A car engine burns gasoline (octane, C₈H₁₈) with air. What reaction type powers the engine?
    [*] Combustion
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Decomposition
    Explanation: Gasoline (a hydrocarbon) reacts with O₂ from air to produce CO₂ and H₂O, releasing enormous energy as heat. This is combustion.

  Q26. Silver nitrate is mixed with sodium chloride in solution. A white solid (AgCl) forms. What reaction type is this?
    [*] Double replacement (precipitation)
    [ ] Combustion
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Decomposition
    Explanation: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃. Two compounds swap ions, and the insoluble AgCl precipitate forms. This is a double replacement reaction.

  Q27. Electrolysis of water produces hydrogen and oxygen gas: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. A student must classify this reaction. What type is it?
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Single replacement
    [ ] Combustion
    Explanation: A single compound (water) is broken down into simpler substances (H₂ and O₂) using electrical energy. This fits the decomposition pattern AB → A + B.

  Q28. A student tries to react gold with hydrochloric acid, but nothing happens. Why?
    [*] Gold is below hydrogen on the activity series and cannot displace it
    [ ] HCl is not an acid
    [ ] Gold only reacts at high temperatures
    [ ] Single replacement reactions never happen with metals
    Explanation: Gold is extremely low on the activity series — it cannot displace hydrogen from HCl. The activity series predicts that no single replacement reaction will occur.

  Q29. Fireworks contain metal salts that burn in air. Why are fireworks classified as combustion reactions?
    [*] The metal compounds react rapidly with O₂, releasing heat and light
    [ ] They only involve decomposition of compounds
    [ ] They produce precipitates
    [ ] They require no oxygen
    Explanation: Fireworks involve combustion because substances react with oxygen at high speed, releasing energy as heat and brilliant light of various colors from excited metal ions.

  Q30. In a lab, mixing lead(II) nitrate and potassium iodide solutions produces a bright yellow solid. What is the yellow solid and what reaction type produced it?
    [*] Lead(II) iodide precipitate from a double replacement reaction
    [ ] Potassium nitrate from a synthesis reaction
    [ ] Elemental lead from a decomposition reaction
    [ ] Nitrogen gas from a single replacement reaction
    Explanation: Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂↓ + 2KNO₃. The ions swap partners (double replacement), and bright yellow lead(II) iodide is the insoluble precipitate.

--- Lesson 6.2:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Combustion Reactions
Core Definitions

  • Combustion: A rapid reaction of a substance with oxygen that produces heat and light (fire).
  • Complete Combustion: Hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O; occurs with plenty of oxygen.
  • Incomplete Combustion: Produces CO (carbon monoxide) or C (soot) instead of CO₂ due to limited oxygen.
  • Hydrocarbon: A compound containing only carbon and hydrogen (e.g., CH₄, C₃H₈).
  • Fuel: A substance that undergoes combustion to release usable energy.
  • Products of Complete Combustion: Always carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O).

--- Lesson 6.2:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A combustion reaction always requires which gas as a reactant?
    [*] Oxygen (O₂)
    [ ] Nitrogen (N₂)
    [ ] Hydrogen (H₂)
    [ ] Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
    Explanation: By definition, combustion is the rapid reaction of a substance with oxygen. Without O₂, combustion cannot occur.

  Q2. Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon produces:
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    [ ] CO and H₂O
    [ ] CO₂ and H₂
    [ ] C and H₂O
    Explanation: When a hydrocarbon burns in excess oxygen, all carbon is converted to CO₂ and all hydrogen to H₂O — these are the only products of complete combustion.

  Q3. Incomplete combustion occurs when there is:
    [*] Insufficient oxygen
    [ ] Excess oxygen
    [ ] Too much fuel
    [ ] No fuel present
    Explanation: When the oxygen supply is limited, the fuel cannot burn completely. This produces CO (carbon monoxide) or soot (C) instead of CO₂.

  Q4. Products of incomplete combustion include:
    [*] Carbon monoxide (CO) or soot (C)
    [ ] Only CO₂ and H₂O
    [ ] Noble gases
    [ ] Nitrogen oxides only
    Explanation: With insufficient O₂, not all carbon is fully oxidized to CO₂. Instead, toxic CO or solid carbon (soot) forms.

  Q5. Combustion reactions are always:
    [*] Exothermic (release energy)
    [ ] Endothermic (absorb energy)
    [ ] Neither exo- nor endothermic
    [ ] Always slow
    Explanation: Combustion releases energy as heat and light, making it exothermic. This energy release is why fossil fuels are used for power.

  Q6. Which equation represents a combustion reaction?
    [*] CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
    [ ] NaCl → Na + Cl₂
    [ ] AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
    [ ] Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
    Explanation: CH₄ (methane, a hydrocarbon) reacting with O₂ to produce CO₂ and H₂O is a classic combustion reaction. The other options are decomposition, double replacement, and single replacement.

  Q7. The primary fuel in a combustion reaction is usually:
    [*] A hydrocarbon or organic compound
    [ ] A noble gas
    [ ] Pure oxygen
    [ ] A metal oxide
    Explanation: Fuels like methane (CH₄), propane (C₃H₈), and octane (C₈H₁₈) are hydrocarbons. Organic compounds containing C and H readily undergo combustion.

  Q8. What phase is water in immediately after a high-temperature combustion?
    [*] Gas (steam/vapor)
    [ ] Liquid
    [ ] Solid (ice)
    [ ] Plasma
    Explanation: At the high temperatures produced by combustion, water exists as steam (gaseous H₂O). It may condense to liquid as it cools.

  Q9. Why is carbon monoxide (CO) from incomplete combustion dangerous?
    [*] It is a colorless, odorless toxic gas that binds to hemoglobin
    [ ] It causes explosions on contact with water
    [ ] It is a strong acid
    [ ] It is radioactive
    Explanation: CO binds to hemoglobin ~200× more tightly than O₂, blocking oxygen transport in the blood. Because CO is colorless and odorless, victims may not notice exposure.

  Q10. What distinguishes complete from incomplete combustion?
    [*] Complete has sufficient O₂ giving CO₂ and H₂O; incomplete has limited O₂ giving CO or C
    [ ] Complete occurs at lower temperatures
    [ ] Incomplete always produces NO₂
    [ ] There is no real difference
    Explanation: The key variable is oxygen supply. Excess O₂ leads to complete combustion (CO₂ + H₂O). Limited O₂ leads to incomplete combustion with CO, C, or both.

  Q11. What does the term 'hydrocarbon' mean?
    [*] A compound composed only of carbon and hydrogen atoms
    [ ] Any compound containing water
    [ ] A mixture of hydrogen gas and carbon dioxide
    [ ] A compound containing carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen
    Explanation: Hydrocarbons contain only H and C (e.g., CH₄, C₂H₆, C₈H₁₈). Compounds that also contain O, N, or other elements are not pure hydrocarbons.

  Q12. What is meant by 'exothermic' in the context of combustion?
    [*] The reaction releases energy to the surroundings as heat and light
    [ ] The reaction absorbs heat from the surroundings
    [ ] The reaction occurs at constant temperature
    [ ] The products weigh more than the reactants
    Explanation: Exothermic ('exo' = out, 'thermic' = heat) means the reaction releases energy. Combustion is strongly exothermic, which is why burning fuels heats surroundings.

  Q13. Define 'fuel' in chemistry.
    [*] A substance that undergoes combustion to release usable energy
    [ ] Any substance that is liquid at room temperature
    [ ] A catalyst for industrial reactions
    [ ] An element found in Group 1 of the periodic table
    Explanation: A fuel is a material that stores chemical energy and releases it via combustion. Common fuels include natural gas (CH₄), propane, gasoline, and wood.

  Q14. What is 'soot'?
    [*] Solid carbon particles produced by incomplete combustion
    [ ] Water vapor from complete combustion
    [ ] A type of catalyst
    [ ] An allotrope of oxygen
    Explanation: Soot is finely divided carbon (C) that results when there is insufficient oxygen to convert all carbon to CO₂ during combustion.

  Q15. What does 'complete combustion' specifically require?
    [*] Sufficient (excess) oxygen to fully oxidize the fuel
    [ ] High pressure only
    [ ] A metal catalyst
    [ ] The absence of any nitrogen
    Explanation: Complete combustion means every carbon becomes CO₂ and every hydrogen becomes H₂O. This only happens when enough O₂ is present for full oxidation.

  Q16. What does the general equation CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O represent?
    [*] Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon
    [ ] Decomposition of a hydrocarbon
    [ ] Synthesis of a hydrocarbon
    [ ] Single replacement in organic chemistry
    Explanation: This is the general template for complete combustion: a hydrocarbon plus excess oxygen yields carbon dioxide and water.

  Q17. What is 'activation energy' in the context of starting a fire?
    [*] The minimum energy needed to initiate the combustion reaction
    [ ] The total energy released by the fire
    [ ] The temperature of the products
    [ ] The mass of fuel required
    Explanation: A spark or match provides the activation energy to start combustion. Once the reaction begins, it releases enough heat to sustain itself.

  Q18. What is the meaning of 'ignition temperature'?
    [*] The minimum temperature at which a fuel begins to burn in air
    [ ] The maximum temperature a flame can reach
    [ ] The boiling point of the fuel
    [ ] The temperature at which oxygen liquefies
    Explanation: Ignition temperature is the threshold temperature at which a substance begins sustained combustion without needing a continued external heat source.

  Q19. Define 'incomplete combustion' in your own scientific terms.
    [*] Burning with insufficient O₂, yielding CO, C (soot), or both instead of only CO₂
    [ ] A reaction that never finishes
    [ ] Burning that produces no heat
    [ ] A reaction where the fuel is left over
    Explanation: Incomplete combustion happens when O₂ is limited. Carbon is only partially oxidized, producing toxic CO or solid soot rather than harmless CO₂.

  Q20. What does 'flammable' mean?
    [*] Easily ignited and capable of burning rapidly
    [ ] Unable to catch fire
    [ ] Dissolves in water
    [ ] Reacts only with acids
    Explanation: Flammable substances have low ignition temperatures and readily undergo combustion when exposed to a spark or flame in the presence of oxygen.

  Q21. A gas stove has a yellow, sooty flame instead of a blue flame. What does this indicate?
    [*] Incomplete combustion due to insufficient air supply
    [ ] The gas is of higher quality
    [ ] Complete combustion is occurring
    [ ] The stove is overheating
    Explanation: A yellow, sooty flame means the gas isn't getting enough O₂ for complete combustion. Soot (carbon particles) glows yellow. A blue flame indicates complete combustion.

  Q22. A CO detector alarm goes off in a home with a gas furnace. What is likely happening?
    [*] The furnace is undergoing incomplete combustion, producing carbon monoxide
    [ ] The furnace is producing too much CO₂
    [ ] The detector is sensing water vapor
    [ ] Complete combustion is occurring normally
    Explanation: CO detectors sense carbon monoxide. If CO is accumulating, the furnace's air intake is likely restricted, causing incomplete combustion and producing toxic CO instead of CO₂.

  Q23. Propane (C₃H₈) is burned in a camping stove. How many moles of O₂ are needed for complete combustion of 1 mole of propane? C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
    [*] 5 moles O₂
    [ ] 3 moles O₂
    [ ] 4 moles O₂
    [ ] 8 moles O₂
    Explanation: The balanced equation shows a 1:5 mole ratio of C₃H₈ to O₂. One mole of propane requires five moles of oxygen for complete combustion.

  Q24. A candle burns in a sealed jar and eventually goes out. Why?
    [*] The oxygen in the jar is consumed, stopping combustion
    [ ] The wax becomes too cold to melt
    [ ] CO₂ is flammable and extinguishes the flame
    [ ] The jar absorbs all the heat
    Explanation: Combustion requires a continuous supply of O₂. In a sealed jar, the oxygen is used up and replaced by CO₂ and H₂O, so the flame cannot continue.

  Q25. Electric cars produce no tailpipe emissions. What does this tell you about combustion in electric vehicles?
    [*] No combustion reaction occurs in the vehicle's motor
    [ ] They use perfect complete combustion
    [ ] They combust water instead of gasoline
    [ ] They use nitrogen as fuel
    Explanation: Electric vehicles use electric motors powered by batteries — there is no combustion of fuel. Hence, there are no CO₂ or CO emissions from the vehicle itself.

  Q26. A Bunsen burner has an adjustable air hole. Opening it wider changes the flame from yellow to blue. Why?
    [*] More air (O₂) enters, shifting from incomplete to complete combustion
    [ ] The gas gets hotter but burns the same way
    [ ] The flame color is due to different gas types
    [ ] Closing the air hole would also give a blue flame
    Explanation: Opening the air hole increases O₂ supply, promoting complete combustion (blue flame). A restricted air hole limits O₂, causing incomplete combustion (yellow, sooty flame).

  Q27. Charcoal grills produce CO when the lid is closed too tightly. Explain why.
    [*] Restricted airflow limits O₂, causing incomplete combustion
    [ ] Charcoal decomposes in the heat rather than burning
    [ ] The lid catalyzes CO production
    [ ] Closing the lid increases O₂ concentration
    Explanation: Closing the lid reduces O₂ supply. With limited oxygen, carbon in the charcoal is only partially oxidized to CO instead of fully to CO₂.

  Q28. Jet fuel (kerosene) is a hydrocarbon that powers aircraft engines. What are the main products when kerosene undergoes complete combustion at altitude?
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    [ ] CO and soot
    [ ] Nitrogen gas and water
    [ ] Ozone and hydrogen
    Explanation: Kerosene is a hydrocarbon. Complete combustion in a jet engine (with excess O₂) produces CO₂ and H₂O, same as any hydrocarbon combustion.

  Q29. A survivalist lights a fire in a cave. The fire starts well but flames become small and orange. What should the survivalist do?
    [*] Increase ventilation to provide more O₂ for complete combustion
    [ ] Add more fuel to make the fire bigger
    [ ] Seal the cave entrance to contain heat
    [ ] Pour water on the fire and restart
    Explanation: The orange flame indicates incomplete combustion (low O₂). Improving ventilation brings in fresh air, supplying more O₂ and shifting toward complete, efficient combustion.

  Q30. A chemistry teacher burns magnesium ribbon in air: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO. Is this a combustion reaction even though Mg is not a hydrocarbon?
    [*] Yes — any rapid reaction with O₂ producing heat and light is combustion
    [ ] No — only hydrocarbons can undergo combustion
    [ ] No — this is a synthesis reaction only
    [ ] Yes — but only because MgO is a gas
    Explanation: Combustion includes any vigorous reaction with O₂ that produces heat and light. Metals like Mg burn brightly in air, qualifying as combustion even though they are not hydrocarbons.

--- Lesson 6.3:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Redox Reactions
Core Definitions

  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons (or gain of oxygen); oxidation number increases.
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons (or loss of oxygen); oxidation number decreases.
  • Redox Reaction: A reaction where oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously.
  • Oxidizing Agent: The substance that is reduced (gains electrons); causes oxidation in another substance.
  • Reducing Agent: The substance that is oxidized (loses electrons); causes reduction in another substance.
  • OIL RIG: Memory aid: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

--- Lesson 6.3:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Oxidation is defined as the:
    [*] Loss of electrons
    [ ] Gain of electrons
    [ ] Loss of protons
    [ ] Gain of neutrons
    Explanation: Oxidation means losing electrons. The mnemonic OIL (Oxidation Is Loss) helps remember this. When a species loses electrons, its oxidation number increases.

  Q2. Reduction is defined as the:
    [*] Gain of electrons
    [ ] Loss of electrons
    [ ] Gain of protons
    [ ] Loss of mass
    Explanation: Reduction means gaining electrons. The mnemonic RIG (Reduction Is Gain) helps. When a species gains electrons, its oxidation number decreases (is 'reduced').

  Q3. In a redox reaction, oxidation and reduction occur:
    [*] Simultaneously
    [ ] Only oxidation occurs
    [ ] In separate containers
    [ ] At different times
    Explanation: In every redox reaction, one species is oxidized while another is simultaneously reduced. Electrons lost by one are gained by the other.

  Q4. The substance that is reduced in a redox reaction is called the:
    [*] Oxidizing agent
    [ ] Reducing agent
    [ ] Catalyst
    [ ] Spectator ion
    Explanation: The oxidizing agent gains electrons (is reduced) and thereby causes another species to be oxidized. It is called 'oxidizing' because it oxidizes the other substance.

  Q5. The substance that is oxidized in a redox reaction is called the:
    [*] Reducing agent
    [ ] Oxidizing agent
    [ ] Product
    [ ] Inhibitor
    Explanation: The reducing agent loses electrons (is oxidized) and donates them to the oxidizing agent, causing it to be reduced.

  Q6. In Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu, which species is oxidized?
    [*] Zn (zinc)
    [ ] Cu²⁺ (copper ion)
    [ ] Zn²⁺
    [ ] Cu (copper metal)
    Explanation: Zinc goes from 0 to +2 oxidation state, meaning it lost 2 electrons. Losing electrons = oxidation. Zn is the reducing agent.

  Q7. OIL RIG stands for:
    [*] Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain
    [ ] Oxygen Is Lost, Reduction Is Gained
    [ ] Only Ions Lose, Reactants In Gain
    [ ] Oxidation Is Last, Reduction Is Great
    Explanation: OIL RIG is a popular mnemonic: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). It helps students remember which process involves which electron change.

  Q8. When an atom's oxidation number increases, the atom has been:
    [*] Oxidized
    [ ] Reduced
    [ ] Neutralized
    [ ] Catalyzed
    Explanation: An increase in oxidation number means the atom lost electrons (became more positive). Losing electrons = oxidation.

  Q9. In the reaction 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl, sodium is:
    [*] Oxidized (loses an electron to become Na⁺)
    [ ] Reduced (gains an electron)
    [ ] Unchanged
    [ ] Acting as a catalyst
    Explanation: Sodium goes from 0 to +1 — it loses one electron per atom. Losing electrons is oxidation. Chlorine gains those electrons (reduction).

  Q10. The oxidation number of any pure, uncombined element is:
    [*] 0
    [ ] +1
    [ ] -1
    [ ] +2
    Explanation: By convention, any element in its free, uncombined state (e.g., Fe, O₂, N₂, Na) has an oxidation number of zero.

  Q11. What does 'oxidation number' (oxidation state) represent?
    [*] The apparent charge on an atom in a compound, tracking electron distribution
    [ ] The number of protons in an atom
    [ ] The mass of the atom
    [ ] The number of bonds an atom forms
    Explanation: Oxidation numbers are bookkeeping tools that assign an apparent charge to each atom in a compound, helping track which atoms lose or gain electrons in redox reactions.

  Q12. Define 'oxidizing agent.'
    [*] The substance that is reduced and causes another substance to be oxidized
    [ ] The substance that is oxidized and donates electrons
    [ ] A substance that prevents oxidation
    [ ] A substance unchanged by the reaction
    Explanation: The oxidizing agent accepts electrons (is itself reduced), which forces the other reactant to lose electrons (be oxidized). It 'causes' oxidation in another species.

  Q13. Define 'reducing agent.'
    [*] The substance that is oxidized and donates electrons to reduce another substance
    [ ] The substance that gains electrons
    [ ] A catalyst that speeds up reduction
    [ ] An inert gas
    Explanation: The reducing agent donates electrons (is itself oxidized), supplying the electrons needed for the other species to be reduced.

    [ ] A reaction that is only half complete
    [ ] A reaction involving half the atoms
    [ ] The point where equilibrium is reached
    Explanation: Half-reactions separate the electron loss (oxidation half) and electron gain (reduction half) so each process can be analyzed independently.

    [*] Electrons move from the reducing agent to the oxidizing agent
    [ ] Protons move between atoms
    [ ] Neutrons are shared between molecules
    [ ] Energy transfers but no particles move
    Explanation: Redox reactions fundamentally involve electrons physically moving from one species (reducing agent) to another (oxidizing agent), changing their oxidation states.

    [*] The reduced species gains electrons, lowering its oxidation number
    [ ] The reduced species gains mass
    [ ] The reduced species gains protons
    [ ] The reduced species gains energy only

    [*] Reduction-oxidation
    [ ] Reactive-deoxidation
    [ ] Reduced oxygen
    [ ] Reverse oxidation
    Explanation: Redox is a portmanteau of 'reduction' and 'oxidation.' It describes any reaction in which electron transfer occurs between species.

    [*] It reveals which atoms lost or gained electrons, confirming a redox reaction
    [ ] It tells us the reaction temperature
    [ ] It measures the mass change

--- Lesson 6.4:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Activation Energy
Core Definitions

  • Activation Energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for a chemical reaction to begin.
  • Energy Diagram: A graph showing energy changes during a reaction; Ea is the 'hill' reactants must overcome.
  • Exothermic Reaction: Products have less energy than reactants; energy is released.
  • Endothermic Reaction: Products have more energy than reactants; energy is absorbed.
  • Activated Complex: The unstable, high-energy transition state at the top of the energy barrier.
  • Catalyst: A substance that lowers activation energy without being consumed in the reaction.

--- Lesson 6.4:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Activation energy is the minimum energy needed to:
    [*] Start a chemical reaction
    [ ] End a chemical reaction
    [ ] Create an element
    [ ] Cool down products
    Explanation: Activation energy (Ea) is the energy barrier that reactant molecules must overcome for a reaction to begin. Without enough Ea, no reaction occurs.

  Q2. On an energy diagram, activation energy is represented by the:
    [*] Height of the energy barrier between reactants and the peak
    [ ] Difference between products and reactants
    [ ] Total energy of the products
    [ ] Width of the graph
    Explanation: On an energy diagram, Ea is measured from the energy level of the reactants up to the peak (transition state). It's the 'hill' the reactants must climb.

  Q3. A catalyst speeds up a reaction by:
    [*] Lowering the activation energy
    [ ] Increasing the temperature
    [ ] Adding more reactant
    [ ] Changing the products
    Explanation: A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, allowing more molecules to react at a given temperature without the catalyst being consumed.

  Q4. In an exothermic reaction, the products have:
    [*] Lower energy than the reactants
    [ ] Higher energy than the reactants
    [ ] Equal energy to the reactants
    [ ] No energy
    Explanation: Exothermic reactions release energy to the surroundings. The products sit at a lower energy level than the reactants on the energy diagram.

  Q5. In an endothermic reaction, the products have:
    [*] Higher energy than the reactants
    [ ] Lower energy than the reactants
    [ ] The same energy as the reactants
    [ ] No activation energy
    Explanation: Endothermic reactions absorb energy from the surroundings. Products are at a higher energy level than reactants, and ΔH is positive.

  Q6. The activated complex (transition state) exists at which point on the energy diagram?
    [*] At the highest energy point (the peak)
    [ ] At the lowest energy point
    [ ] At the reactant energy level
    [ ] At the product energy level
    Explanation: The activated complex is the unstable, high-energy transition state at the top of the energy barrier. It exists momentarily before forming products.

  Q7. Without sufficient activation energy, a reaction will:
    [*] Not occur
    [ ] Proceed faster
    [ ] Release more energy
    [ ] Become endothermic
    Explanation: If reactant molecules don't possess enough kinetic energy to overcome the activation energy barrier, effective collisions won't occur, and no reaction takes place.

  Q8. A catalyst is NOT consumed in a reaction, meaning it:
    [*] Can be recovered unchanged after the reaction
    [ ] Becomes one of the products
    [ ] Is always destroyed
    [ ] Changes into a different element
    Explanation: A catalyst participates mechanistically in the reaction but is regenerated. It emerges unchanged, ready to catalyze additional reaction cycles.

  Q9. What does ΔH represent on an energy diagram?
    [*] The energy difference between products and reactants
    [ ] The activation energy
    [ ] The speed of the reaction
    [ ] The temperature at which reaction occurs
    Explanation: ΔH (enthalpy change) is the vertical distance between reactant and product energy levels. Negative ΔH = exothermic; positive ΔH = endothermic.

  Q10. Adding a catalyst changes the activation energy but does NOT change:
    [*] The overall energy change (ΔH) of the reaction
    [ ] The reaction rate
    [ ] The pathway of the reaction
    [ ] The activation energy
    Explanation: A catalyst lowers Ea and speeds the reaction, but the starting and ending energy levels remain the same. Therefore, ΔH is unchanged.

  Q11. What is the 'activated complex' (transition state)?
    [*] An unstable, high-energy arrangement of atoms at the top of the energy barrier
    [ ] The final stable product of a reaction
    [ ] A type of catalyst
    [ ] The initial reactant mixture
    Explanation: The activated complex is a fleeting, high-energy intermediate where old bonds are partly broken and new bonds partly formed. It cannot be isolated.

  Q12. Define 'activation energy' in your own words.
    [*] The minimum kinetic energy colliding molecules need to initiate bond breaking and reaction
    [ ] The total energy of all products
    [ ] The mass of the catalyst
    [ ] The temperature of the surroundings
    Explanation: Activation energy is the energy threshold that reacting molecules must reach during a collision for bonds to break and rearrange into products.

  Q13. What does 'exothermic' mean?
    [*] A reaction that releases energy to the surroundings (ΔH < 0)
    [ ] A reaction that absorbs energy from the surroundings
    [ ] A reaction that requires a catalyst
    [ ] A reaction at constant temperature
    Explanation: 'Exo' means out, 'thermic' means heat. Exothermic reactions release heat (energy flows out), and the products are at lower energy than reactants.

  Q14. What does 'endothermic' mean?
    [*] A reaction that absorbs energy from the surroundings (ΔH > 0)
    [ ] A reaction that always produces gas
    [ ] A reaction that releases energy
    [ ] A reaction with no energy change
    Explanation: 'Endo' means in, 'thermic' means heat. Endothermic reactions absorb heat from the surroundings, so the products are at higher energy.

  Q15. What is a 'catalyst' in chemistry?
    [*] A substance that lowers activation energy and speeds up a reaction without being consumed
    [ ] A reactant that is used up during the reaction
    [ ] A product formed at the end of the reaction
    [ ] A substance that increases activation energy
    Explanation: A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower Ea. It participates mechanistically but is regenerated, emerging unchanged after the reaction.

  Q16. What is an 'energy diagram' (potential energy diagram)?
    [*] A graph plotting potential energy vs. reaction progress, showing Ea and ΔH
    [ ] A table listing all known elements
    [ ] A drawing of molecular structures

    Explanation: An energy diagram shows energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis. It visually displays reactant/product energy levels, Ea, and the transition state.

  Q18. Define 'transition state' in the context of a chemical reaction.
    [*] The highest-energy, most unstable arrangement of atoms during the conversion of reactants to products
    [ ] The energy level of the final products

    Explanation: The transition state is the fleeting configuration at the peak of the energy barrier. Bonds are partially broken and partially formed — it cannot be isolated or observed directly.

--- Lesson 6.5:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Balancing Equations
Core Definitions

  • Law of Conservation of Mass: Matter cannot be created or destroyed; atoms must balance on both sides.
  • Coefficient: A number placed in front of a formula to balance atoms (e.g., 2H₂O).
  • Subscript: A number within a formula showing how many atoms of an element are in a molecule; NEVER change subscripts.
  • Balancing Steps: 1) Write the unbalanced equation. 2) Count atoms on each side. 3) Add coefficients to balance. 4) Verify.
  • Diatomic Elements: Elements that exist as pairs: H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ (remember HONClBrIF).
  • Balanced Equation: An equation with equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides.

--- Lesson 6.5:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A balanced equation must have equal numbers of each type of:
    [*] Atom on both sides
    [ ] Molecule on both sides
    [ ] Compound on both sides
    [ ] Bond on both sides
    Explanation: The law of conservation of mass requires equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides of the equation. Coefficients are adjusted to achieve this balance.

  Q2. Coefficients in a balanced equation represent the number of:
    [*] Molecules or moles of each substance
    [ ] Atoms within a single molecule
    [ ] Electrons transferred
    [ ] Charges on each ion
    Explanation: Coefficients are placed in front of chemical formulas and indicate how many units (molecules, formula units, or moles) of that substance participate in the reaction.

  Q3. The law of conservation of mass states that:
    [*] Matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction
    [ ] Energy is always conserved but mass is not
    [ ] All reactions produce water
    [ ] Atoms can appear or disappear during reactions
    Explanation: Conservation of mass means the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. No atoms are created or destroyed — only rearranged.

  Q4. The balanced form of H₂ + O₂ → H₂O is:
    [*] 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
    [ ] H₂ + O₂ → H₂O₂
    [ ] H₂ + 2O₂ → 2H₂O
    [ ] H₂ + O₂ → H₂O
    Explanation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O gives 4 H and 2 O on each side. The other options either change subscripts (illegal) or have unequal atom counts.

  Q5. When balancing equations, you should NEVER change the:
    [*] Subscripts in chemical formulas
    [ ] Coefficients
    [ ] Order of substances
    [ ] State symbols
    Explanation: Changing subscripts alters the identity of the compound (e.g., H₂O vs. H₂O₂ are different substances). Only coefficients may be adjusted to balance.

  Q6. The balanced equation for Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃ is:
    [*] 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃
    [ ] 2Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃
    [ ] Fe + O₂ → FeO₂
    [ ] 2Fe + 3O₂ → Fe₂O₃
    Explanation: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃: Left side has 4 Fe and 6 O; right side has 4 Fe (2×2) and 6 O (2×3). Balanced!

  Q7. The arrow (→) in a chemical equation means:
    [*] Yields / produces
    [ ] Equals
    [ ] Is greater than
    [ ] Reacts backwards
    Explanation: The arrow separates reactants (left) from products (right) and reads 'yields' or 'produces.' A double arrow (⇌) indicates a reversible reaction.

  Q8. Which group of elements always exists as diatomic molecules?
    [*] H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ (HONClBrIF)
    [ ] He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe
    [ ] Na, K, Ca, Mg
    [ ] C, Si, Ge, Sn
    Explanation: Seven elements naturally exist as diatomic pairs: H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂. The mnemonic HONClBrIF helps remember them.

  Q9. What is the first step in balancing a chemical equation?
    [*] Write the unbalanced (skeleton) equation with correct formulas
    [ ] Add random coefficients
    [ ] Change the subscripts to match
    [ ] Remove all diatomic molecules
    Explanation: Step 1: Write the correct formulas for all reactants and products. Then count atoms, add coefficients, and verify the balance.

  Q10. In the equation 2H₂O, the '2' is a:
    [*] Coefficient
    [ ] Subscript
    [ ] Superscript
    [ ] Charge
    Explanation: The number in front of a formula (2H₂O) is the coefficient. It multiplies all atoms in the formula: 2 × H₂O = 4 H atoms and 2 O atoms total.

  Q11. What is a 'coefficient' in a chemical equation?
    [*] A number placed before a formula to indicate how many units of that substance
    [ ] A number inside a formula showing atoms per molecule
    [ ] The charge on an ion
    [ ] The mass of a compound
    Explanation: A coefficient is the whole number placed before a chemical formula (e.g., the 3 in 3H₂O). It tells how many molecules or moles of that substance participate.

  Q12. What is a 'subscript' in a chemical formula?
    [*] A small number within a formula indicating how many atoms of an element are in one molecule
    [ ] A number placed before the formula
    [ ] The charge on the molecule
    [ ] The state of matter symbol
    Explanation: Subscripts are the small numbers after element symbols (e.g., the 2 in H₂O means 2 hydrogen atoms). Changing a subscript changes what compound it is.

  Q13. Define 'balanced equation.'
    [*] A chemical equation with equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides
    [ ] An equation with the same number of molecules on each side
    [ ] An equation where all coefficients are 1
    [ ] An equation with equal masses of each compound
    Explanation: A balanced equation satisfies conservation of mass — every element has the same atom count on the reactant side and the product side.

  Q14. What does 'diatomic' mean?
    [*] Consisting of two atoms bonded together (e.g., O₂, N₂, Cl₂)
    [ ] Having two electrons
    [ ] Having two energy levels
    [ ] Being a two-step reaction
    Explanation: 'Di' means two, 'atomic' means atoms. Diatomic elements exist naturally as molecules of two identical atoms bonded together.

  Q15. What does 'conservation of mass' require in balancing?
    [*] Total atoms of each element must be equal on both sides of the equation
    [ ] The equation must have the same number of compounds
    [ ] Energy input must equal energy output
    [ ] Coefficients must all be the same number
    Explanation: Conservation of mass means no atoms are created or destroyed. Balancing ensures every atom accounted for on the reactant side appears on the product side.

  Q16. What does the mnemonic 'HONClBrIF' help you remember?

    [ ] The noble gases
    [ ] The alkali metals
    [ ] The steps for balancing equations

    [ ] An equation missing products
    [ ] An equation with only element symbols
    [ ] A completed, balanced equation
    Explanation: A skeleton equation shows the correct chemical formulas for reactants and products but hasn't had coefficients adjusted yet — it's the starting point for balancing.

  Q18. What do the state symbols (s), (l), (g), and (aq) mean?

    [ ] Speed, length, gravity, and acceleration

    [ ] They have no specific meaning

  Q19. Why is the 'verify' step important when balancing equations?
    [*] To confirm that every element has equal atom counts on both sides
    [ ] To check the temperature of reactants
    [ ] To make sure all coefficients are prime numbers
    [ ] To ensure the arrow points right
    Explanation: Verification is the final step: count each element on both sides to confirm the equation is truly balanced. Mistakes in one coefficient can propagate.

    [ ] Remove all coefficients from the equation
    [ ] Set all coefficients to 1
    [ ] Change subscripts to match coefficients
    Explanation: Balanced equations conventionally use the smallest set of whole-number coefficients. If all coefficients share a common factor, divide through to simplify.

  Q21. A student writes: N₂ + H₂ → NH₃. She counts 2 N on the left but only 1 N on the right. What coefficient should she put before NH₃?
    [*] 2 (giving 2NH₃ with 2 N atoms on the right)
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 1 — it's already balanced
    [ ] 4
    Explanation: With N₂ on the left (2 N), putting 2 before NH₃ gives 2 N on the right. Still need to balance H: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (6 H each side).

  Q22. A classmate says: 'I balanced H₂O by changing it to H₂O₂.' Is this correct?
    [*] No — changing subscripts creates a different compound (hydrogen peroxide), which is not the same substance
    [ ] Yes — it's a valid way to balance
    [ ] Yes — subscripts and coefficients serve the same purpose
    [ ] No — but only because H₂O₂ doesn't exist
    Explanation: H₂O is water; H₂O₂ is hydrogen peroxide — a completely different compound. Subscripts define the substance and must never be changed during balancing.

  Q23. Balance: CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. What coefficients balance this equation?
    [*] 1 CH₄ + 2 O₂ → 1 CO₂ + 2 H₂O
    [ ] 2 CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
    [ ] CH₄ + 3 O₂ → CO₂ + 3 H₂O
    [ ] CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (already balanced)
    Explanation: C: 1=1 ✓; H: 4=4 (2×2) ✓; O: 4=4 (2+2) ✓. The equation CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O is balanced.

  Q24. A student balances an equation and gets: 6Na + 3Cl₂ → 6NaCl. Is this correct? Can it be simplified?
    [*] It is balanced but should be reduced: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
    [ ] It is not balanced at all
    [ ] It cannot be simplified
    [ ] The correct form is Na + Cl₂ → NaCl
    Explanation: 6:3:6 can all be divided by 3, giving 2:1:2. Convention uses the lowest whole-number coefficients: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl.

  Q25. A recipe calls for baking soda to react with vinegar. The equation is: NaHCO₃ + CH₃COOH → NaCH₃COO + H₂O + CO₂. Is it balanced as written?
    [*] Yes — counting each element shows equal atoms on both sides
    [ ] No — there are more oxygen atoms on the right
    [ ] No — carbon is not balanced
    [ ] Cannot tell without knowing the state symbols
    Explanation: Count: Na 1=1, H 5=5, C 3=3, O 5=5. All elements balance with coefficients of 1. The equation is already balanced.

  Q26. A lab report says: 'Mg + O₂ → MgO.' The student needs to balance it. What is the balanced equation?
    [*] 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
    [ ] Mg + O₂ → MgO₂
    [ ] Mg + O → MgO
    [ ] Mg₂ + O₂ → 2MgO
    Explanation: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO: 2 Mg and 2 O on each side. Options B and D illegally change subscripts or element formulas.

  Q27. Why can't you balance C + O₂ → CO₂ by writing C + O → CO₂?
    [*] Oxygen naturally exists as O₂ (diatomic); writing O alone misrepresents the actual reactant
    [ ] It would still be balanced
    [ ] The equation is already balanced with O
    [ ] Oxygen is not diatomic
    Explanation: Oxygen exists as O₂ under standard conditions. The correct skeleton equation must use O₂. In this case, C + O₂ → CO₂ is already balanced (1 C, 2 O each side).

  Q28. Balance the equation: Al + HCl → AlCl₃ + H₂
    [*] 2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂
    [ ] Al + 3HCl → AlCl₃ + H₂
    [ ] Al + 2HCl → AlCl₂ + H₂
    [ ] 2Al + 3HCl → 2AlCl₃ + H₂
    Explanation: 2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂: Al 2=2 ✓, H 6=6 ✓, Cl 6=6 ✓. Option B has 3 H left but 2 right.

  Q29. A student performs a reaction and finds the total mass of products is less than the total mass of reactants. Assuming no measurement error, what likely happened?
    [*] A gaseous product escaped the container and wasn't measured
    [ ] Atoms were destroyed during the reaction
    [ ] The law of conservation of mass doesn't apply here
    [ ] The coefficients were wrong
    Explanation: Conservation of mass always applies. If measured mass decreases, a gas (like CO₂ or H₂) likely escaped the open container, carrying mass with it.

  Q30. Balance: C₃H₈ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (propane combustion). What are the correct coefficients?
    [*] C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
    [ ] C₃H₈ + 3O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
    [ ] C₃H₈ + 4O₂ → 3CO₂ + 3H₂O
    [ ] 2C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 6CO₂ + 8H₂O
    Explanation: C: 3=3 ✓; H: 8=8 (4×2) ✓; O: 10=10 (6+4) ✓. C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O is balanced with smallest whole-number coefficients.

--- Lesson 6.6:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Reaction Rates & Catalysts

Core Definitions

  • Reaction Rate: The speed at which reactants are converted to products.
  • Collision Theory: Particles must collide with enough energy and proper orientation to react.
  • Temperature Effect: Increasing temperature increases particle speed and collision frequency → faster rate.
  • Concentration Effect: Higher concentration means more particles → more collisions → faster rate.
  • Surface Area Effect: Smaller particles (greater surface area) react faster.
  • Catalyst: Speeds up a reaction by lowering activation energy; is not consumed.

--- Lesson 6.6:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Increasing temperature generally causes a reaction to:
    [*] Speed up
    [ ] Slow down
    [ ] Stop completely
    [ ] Have no change in rate
    Explanation: Higher temperature means greater kinetic energy for particles. They move faster, collide more often, and collide with more energy — all increasing the reaction rate.

  Q2. A catalyst speeds up a reaction by:
    [*] Providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy
    [ ] Increasing the temperature
    [ ] Adding more reactant
    [ ] Changing the products formed
    Explanation: A catalyst lowers the activation energy by offering a different mechanistic pathway. More molecules can then overcome the barrier at the same temperature.

  Q3. Increasing the concentration of reactants will:
    [*] Increase the reaction rate
    [ ] Decrease the reaction rate
    [ ] Stop the reaction
    [ ] Have no effect
    Explanation: Higher concentration means more reactant particles per unit volume, leading to more collisions per second and a faster reaction rate.

  Q4. Increasing the surface area of a solid reactant:
    [*] Increases the reaction rate
    [ ] Decreases the reaction rate
    [ ] Has no effect on rate
    [ ] Changes the products
    Explanation: Breaking a solid into smaller pieces exposes more surface area for reactant particles to collide with, increasing the frequency of effective collisions.

  Q5. According to collision theory, for a reaction to occur particles must:
    [*] Collide with sufficient energy and correct orientation
    [ ] Simply be in the same container
    [ ] Be heated to extreme temperatures
    [ ] Be stationary
    Explanation: Collision theory states two conditions: molecules must collide with kinetic energy ≥ activation energy, AND they must be oriented correctly so the right bonds can break/form.

  Q6. An enzyme is a type of:
    [*] Biological catalyst
    [ ] Inhibitor
    [ ] Reactant
    [ ] Product
    Explanation: Enzymes are proteins that act as biological catalysts — they lower activation energy for biochemical reactions and are highly specific to their substrates.

  Q7. A catalyst can be recovered unchanged after the reaction because it:
    [*] Is not consumed — it participates but is regenerated
    [ ] Never interacts with reactants
    [ ] Becomes a product
    [ ] Is always a noble gas
    Explanation: Catalysts participate in intermediate steps of the reaction mechanism but are regenerated by the end. They emerge chemically unchanged.

  Q8. What does 'reaction rate' measure?
    [*] The speed at which reactants are converted to products
    [ ] The total mass of the products
    [ ] The temperature change during reaction
    [ ] The number of products formed
    Explanation: Reaction rate is the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time. It tells how fast the reaction proceeds.

  Q9. Powdering a solid reactant increases rate because it increases:
    [*] Surface area exposed to other reactants
    [ ] The mass of the solid
    [ ] The temperature of the solid
    [ ] The concentration of the solid
    Explanation: Grinding a solid into powder dramatically increases the total surface area available for collisions with other reactant molecules, greatly accelerating the reaction.

  Q10. Which of these would NOT increase the rate of a reaction?
    [*] Decreasing the concentration of reactants
    [ ] Adding a catalyst
    [ ] Increasing temperature
    [ ] Increasing surface area
    Explanation: Decreasing concentration means fewer particles per volume, fewer collisions, and a slower rate. All other options increase rate.

  Q11. What does 'collision theory' state?
    [*] Reactions occur when particles collide with enough energy and correct orientation
    [ ] All collisions between particles lead to reactions
    [ ] Reactions happen without any particle contact
    [ ] Only gas-phase particles can collide
    Explanation: Collision theory explains that only a fraction of collisions are 'effective' — those with sufficient kinetic energy (≥ Ea) and the right geometric orientation.

  Q12. Define 'catalyst' in chemistry.
    [*] A substance that increases reaction rate by lowering activation energy without being consumed
    [ ] A substance that slows down a reaction
    [ ] A reactant that is used up
    [ ] A product of the reaction
    Explanation: A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower Ea. It speeds the reaction and is regenerated — appearing unchanged at the end.

  Q13. What is 'activation energy' in the context of collision theory?
    [*] The minimum kinetic energy colliding particles need for a successful reaction
    [ ] The total energy of all particles
    [ ] The energy released by the reaction
    [ ] The temperature of the surroundings
    Explanation: For a collision to be effective, the combined kinetic energy of the colliding molecules must equal or exceed the activation energy barrier.

  Q14. What does 'effective collision' mean?
    [*] A collision with enough energy and proper orientation to result in a reaction
    [ ] Any collision between two particles
    [ ] A collision that produces sound
    [ ] A collision between molecules and the container wall
    Explanation: Most collisions are ineffective (wrong orientation or insufficient energy). Only effective collisions lead to bond breaking/forming and product formation.

  Q15. What is an 'inhibitor'?
    [*] A substance that slows down or prevents a reaction
    [ ] A substance that speeds up a reaction
    [ ] A type of reactant
    [ ] A diatomic element
    Explanation: An inhibitor is the opposite of a catalyst — it slows or stops a reaction, often by blocking the active site of a catalyst or interfering with the reaction mechanism.

  Q16. What does 'concentration effect' mean for reaction rate?
    [*] Higher concentration → more particles per volume → more collisions → faster rate
    [ ] Higher concentration always decreases rate
    [ ] Concentration has no effect on rate
    [ ] Concentration only affects gases
    Explanation: More particles in the same space means a greater probability of collisions per second, directly increasing the rate of reaction.

  Q17. What does 'surface area effect' mean for reaction rate?
    [*] Greater exposed surface area of solid reactants → more collisions → faster rate
    [ ] Larger chunks react faster
    [ ] Surface area only matters for liquids
    [ ] Surface area does not affect rate
    Explanation: Smaller particles have a much larger total surface area exposed to reactant molecules. This allows more simultaneous collisions and a faster reaction.

  Q18. Define 'reaction rate' precisely.
    [*] The change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time
    [ ] The total number of collisions in a reaction
    [ ] The temperature at which a reaction starts
    [ ] The amount of product formed overall
    Explanation: Reaction rate = Δ[concentration] / Δtime. It can be measured as the rate of disappearance of reactant or the rate of appearance of product.

  Q19. What is 'temperature effect' on reaction rate?
    [*] Higher temperature increases kinetic energy and collision frequency, speeding up the reaction
    [ ] Temperature has no effect on reaction rate
    [ ] Higher temperature always slows reactions
    [ ] Temperature only affects catalysts
    Explanation: Raising temperature increases average kinetic energy. More molecules exceed the activation energy threshold, and collisions happen more frequently — both increase rate.

  Q20. What does 'orientation' mean in collision theory?
    [*] The spatial arrangement of molecules during collision — they must align correctly for bonds to form
    [ ] The direction the container is facing
    [ ] The mass of the molecules
    [ ] The charge of the particles
    Explanation: Even with sufficient energy, molecules must collide with the reactive parts facing each other. Poor orientation leads to an ineffective collision.

  Q21. A student dissolves an antacid tablet whole in water and times the reaction. Then she crushes an identical tablet and repeats. The crushed tablet reacts faster. Why?
    [*] Crushing increased the surface area, allowing more contact with water molecules
    [ ] Crushing changed the chemical formula
    [ ] Crushing added a catalyst
    [ ] The crushed tablet had more mass
    Explanation: Crushing the tablet breaks it into smaller pieces with much more total surface area. More surface means more simultaneous collisions with water, speeding up the reaction.

  Q22. Food spoils faster at room temperature than in the refrigerator. Explain using collision theory.
    [*] Higher temperature gives bacteria/enzymes more kinetic energy, speeding up decomposition reactions
    [ ] Refrigerators remove all bacteria
    [ ] Cold food has more surface area
    [ ] Room temperature adds a catalyst to food
    Explanation: At higher temperatures, molecules (including enzymes) move faster and collide more frequently with enough energy. Refrigeration slows microbial and enzymatic reactions.

  Q23. A car's catalytic converter reduces harmful exhaust emissions. What is the converter doing to the reactions?
    [*] Lowering the activation energy so toxic CO and NOₓ convert to CO₂ and N₂ faster
    [ ] Heating the exhaust to extreme temperatures
    [ ] Adding oxygen to the exhaust
    [ ] Removing all gases from the exhaust
    Explanation: Platinum and palladium catalysts in the converter lower Ea for oxidizing CO and reducing NOₓ, converting them into less harmful CO₂ and N₂.

  Q24. A steel wool ball burns slowly in air but rapidly when placed in pure oxygen. What factor explains the difference?

    [ ] Pure oxygen is at a higher temperature
    [ ] The steel wool has different surface area in pure oxygen
    [ ] Pure oxygen acts as a catalyst
    Explanation: Air is only ~21% O₂. In pure oxygen (100%), there are ~5× more O₂ molecules per volume, dramatically increasing collision frequency and reaction rate.

  Q25. Hydrogen peroxide in a brown bottle decomposes slowly. Adding a pinch of MnO₂ causes vigorous bubbling. What happened?
    [*] MnO₂ catalyzed the decomposition, lowering Ea and greatly increasing the rate
    [ ] MnO₂ is a reactant that was consumed
    [ ] MnO₂ heated the hydrogen peroxide
    [ ] MnO₂ increased the concentration of H₂O₂
    Explanation: MnO₂ is a classic catalyst for H₂O₂ decomposition. It dramatically lowers the activation energy, causing rapid release of O₂ gas (the bubbles).

  Q26. Grain dust explosions in silos are devastating. Why does finely powdered grain explode but a pile of grain does not?
    [*] Fine dust has enormously greater surface area, allowing rapid reaction with O₂ in air
    [ ] Dust contains different chemicals than grain
    [ ] Dust is at a higher temperature
    [ ] The concentration of grain increases
    Explanation: Finely ground particles suspended in air have massive surface area in contact with O₂. A single spark can ignite nearly all particles at once, creating an explosion.

  Q27. A chemistry student places a glow stick in hot water and another in ice water. The one in hot water glows brighter. Why?
    [*] Higher temperature increases the rate of the chemiluminescent reaction, producing more light
    [ ] Hot water acts as a catalyst
    [ ] Cold water inhibits light emission but not the reaction
    [ ] The glow stick in ice water has run out of chemicals
    Explanation: Higher temperature increases molecular kinetic energy and collision frequency, speeding up the reaction. Faster reaction → more light produced per unit time → brighter glow.

  Q28. Meat tenderizer contains the enzyme papain, which breaks down proteins. Adding tenderizer to meat speeds up cooking. What role does papain play?
    [*] It is a biological catalyst that lowers the activation energy for breaking protein bonds
    [ ] It increases the temperature of the meat

    [ ] It acts as an inhibitor
    Explanation: Papain is an enzyme (biological catalyst) from papaya. It lowers the activation energy for hydrolyzing peptide bonds in meat proteins, tenderizing the meat faster.

    [*] Doubled concentration means twice as many HCl molecules collide with Zn per second
    [ ] The activation energy was halved
    [ ] Zinc's surface area doubled
    [ ] Temperature increased due to the extra acid
    Explanation: Doubling concentration doubles the number of reactant particles per volume. This proportionally increases the collision frequency and thus the reaction rate.

  Q30. A pressure cooker cooks food much faster than an open pot. Using collision theory, explain why.
    [*] Higher pressure raises the boiling point of water, increasing temperature and kinetic energy of molecules
    [ ] The sealed pot acts as a catalyst
    [ ] Pressure removes the need for activation energy
    [ ] Food has more surface area under pressure
    Explanation: In a pressure cooker, water boils at ~120°C instead of 100°C. The higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy, increasing collision energy and frequency — food cooks faster.

--- Lesson 6.7:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Chemical Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle

Core Definitions

  • Chemical Equilibrium: A state where the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; concentrations remain constant.
  • Reversible Reaction: A reaction that can proceed in both forward and reverse directions (⇌).
  • Le Chatelier's Principle: If a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to relieve the stress.
  • Adding Reactant: Shifts equilibrium toward products (forward).
  • Increasing Temperature: Shifts equilibrium in the endothermic direction.
  • Changing Pressure: Increasing pressure shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas.

--- Lesson 6.7:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Chemical equilibrium is reached when:
    [*] The rates of forward and reverse reactions are equal
    [ ] All reactants are used up
    [ ] The reaction stops completely
    [ ] All products are formed
    Explanation: At equilibrium the forward and reverse reactions proceed at equal rates. The reaction has NOT stopped — it is dynamic. Concentrations remain constant because the two rates are balanced.

  Q2. At equilibrium, the concentrations of reactants and products:
    [*] Remain constant but are not necessarily equal to each other
    [ ] Continue to change
    [ ] Are always equal
    [ ] Both equal zero
    Explanation: Equilibrium means the rates are balanced, so concentrations stay constant over time. However, the actual concentrations of products and reactants are rarely equal to each other.

  Q3. Le Chatelier's principle predicts that when a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system will:
    [*] Shift to counteract the stress
    [ ] Always shift to the right
    [ ] Always shift to the left
    [ ] Stop reacting entirely
    Explanation: Le Chatelier's principle states that a system at equilibrium responds to any disturbance (concentration, pressure, temperature) by shifting in the direction that partially relieves that stress.

  Q4. Adding more reactant to a system at equilibrium causes the equilibrium to shift:
    [*] Toward the products (right)
    [ ] Toward the reactants (left)
    [ ] In both directions equally
    [ ] Not at all
    Explanation: Adding more reactant increases reactant concentration. To reduce that stress, the system consumes extra reactant by shifting toward products (forward/right).

  Q5. In an exothermic reaction, increasing the temperature shifts equilibrium:
    [*] Toward the reactants (left)
    [ ] Toward the products (right)
    [ ] Not at all — temperature does not affect equilibrium
    [ ] Causes an explosion
    Explanation: For an exothermic reaction (heat is a product), adding heat is like adding a product. The system shifts left (toward reactants) to absorb the excess heat.

  Q6. Removing a product from a reaction at equilibrium will:
    [*] Shift equilibrium toward products (right) to replace what was removed
    [ ] Shift equilibrium toward reactants (left)
    [ ] Have no effect on equilibrium
    [ ] Stop the reaction completely
    Explanation: Removing product decreases its concentration. To counteract this stress, the forward reaction speeds up, producing more product and shifting equilibrium to the right.

  Q7. The equilibrium constant (K) expresses the ratio of:
    [*] Products to reactants at equilibrium
    [ ] Reactants to products
    [ ] Temperature to pressure
    [ ] Energy to mass
    Explanation: K = [products]^coefficients / [reactants]^coefficients. A large K (>1) means products are favored; a small K (<1) means reactants are favored.

  Q8. If K >> 1 for a reaction, what does that indicate?
    [*] Products are heavily favored at equilibrium
    [ ] Reactants are heavily favored
    [ ] The reaction cannot reach equilibrium
    [ ] The reaction rate is very fast
    Explanation: A very large K means the numerator (products) is much larger than the denominator (reactants), so the mixture at equilibrium is mostly products.

  Q9. Which of these is true about equilibrium?
    [*] Forward and reverse reactions are still occurring
    [ ] The reaction has completely stopped
    [ ] Only the forward reaction occurs
    [ ] Only the reverse reaction occurs
    Explanation: Equilibrium is dynamic — both forward and reverse reactions continue simultaneously. The rates are equal so there is no net change in concentrations.

  Q10. Increasing pressure on a gaseous equilibrium shifts the reaction toward:
    [*] The side with fewer moles of gas
    [ ] The side with more moles of gas
    [ ] Always left
    [ ] Pressure has no effect on equilibrium
    Explanation: By Le Chatelier's principle, increasing pressure favors the side with fewer gas molecules, reducing the total number of moles and thus partially relieving the pressure stress.

  Q11. Define 'chemical equilibrium.'
    [*] A dynamic state where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal and concentrations remain constant
    [ ] A state where the reaction stops and no molecules move
    [ ] When all reagents have been consumed
    [ ] When only products remain in the container
    Explanation: Chemical equilibrium is dynamic — molecular-level reactions continue in both directions but at equal rates, resulting in constant macroscopic concentrations.

  Q12. What is a 'reversible reaction'?
    [*] A reaction that can proceed in both forward and reverse directions (⇌)
    [ ] A reaction that only goes in one direction
    [ ] A reaction that produces heat
    [ ] A reaction that cannot be observed
    Explanation: Reversible reactions proceed in both directions: reactants → products AND products → reactants. The double arrow ⇌ distinguishes them from irreversible reactions (→).

  Q13. Define 'Le Chatelier's principle.'
    [*] If a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to partially counteract that stress
    [ ] Equilibrium always shifts to the right
    [ ] Pressure never affects equilibrium
    [ ] Catalysts change the position of equilibrium
    Explanation: Le Chatelier's principle is a qualitative prediction tool: the equilibrium responds to a stress (change in concentration, temperature, or pressure) by shifting to offset that change.

  Q14. What is the 'equilibrium constant' (Keq)?
    [*] The ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, raised to stoichiometric powers
    [ ] The speed of the forward reaction
    [ ] The temperature at which equilibrium occurs
    [ ] The total mass of products formed
    Explanation: Keq = [products]^coefficients / [reactants]^coefficients. It is a fixed value at a given temperature and tells how far the reaction proceeds toward products.

  Q15. What does 'dynamic equilibrium' mean?
    [*] Both forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates, with no net change in concentrations
    [ ] The reaction has completely stopped
    [ ] The products are continuously removed
    [ ] The temperature is constantly changing
    Explanation: Dynamic means ongoing motion. At equilibrium, molecules are still reacting (it is not static), but the forward rate equals the reverse rate, so concentrations appear unchanged.

  Q16. What is a 'stress' in the context of Le Chatelier's principle?
    [*] A change in concentration, temperature, or pressure that disturbs equilibrium
    [ ] A type of chemical bond
    [ ] Adding a catalyst
    [ ] The mass of a product
    Explanation: A stress is any change that disrupts equilibrium. Common stresses include adding/removing reactants or products, changing temperature, and changing pressure for gaseous systems.

  Q17. What does it mean when we say equilibrium 'shifts right'?
    [*] The forward reaction rate temporarily exceeds the reverse, producing more products
    [ ] All reactants turn into products permanently
    [ ] The reaction stops
    [ ] The reverse reaction speeds up
    Explanation: Shifting right means the forward reaction temporarily becomes faster than the reverse, consuming reactants and forming more products until a new equilibrium is established.

  Q18. What does K << 1 indicate about a reaction?
    [*] Reactants are favored — the mixture is mostly reactants at equilibrium
    [ ] Products are heavily favored
    [ ] The reaction does not occur
    [ ] The reaction is very fast
    Explanation: When K is much less than 1, the denominator (reactants) is larger than the numerator (products). The reaction barely proceeds toward products before equilibrium is reached.

  Q19. What is the 'reaction quotient' (Q)?
    [*] The ratio of products to reactants at any point in the reaction, used to predict which way the reaction will shift
    [ ] The same as the equilibrium constant at all times
    [ ] The temperature of the reaction
    [ ] The mass of products formed
    Explanation: Q has the same formula as K but is calculated at any moment, not just equilibrium. If Q < K the reaction shifts right; if Q > K it shifts left; if Q = K the system is at equilibrium.

  Q20. What does 'position of equilibrium' refer to?
    [*] The relative proportions of reactants and products at equilibrium
    [ ] The physical location of the reaction vessel
    [ ] The speed of the reaction
    [ ] The color of the solution
    Explanation: The position of equilibrium describes whether the mixture favors products (lies to the right) or reactants (lies to the left). It is reflected in the value of K.

  Q21. In the Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, exothermic), factories use high pressure. Why does this favor ammonia production?
    [*] High pressure shifts equilibrium toward fewer gas moles — 4 moles of gas become 2 moles of NH₃
    [ ] Pressure increases the temperature
    [ ] Pressure acts as a catalyst
    [ ] Pressure breaks the N≡N triple bond directly
    Explanation: The left side has 4 moles of gas (1 N₂ + 3 H₂) and the right side has 2 moles (2 NH₃). Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium toward fewer gas moles — right, favoring ammonia.

  Q22. Carbonated drinks go flat faster when opened and left at room temperature. Which equilibrium concept explains this?
    [*] Removing CO₂ gas (opening) and higher temperature both shift CO₂(aq) ⇌ CO₂(g) to the right
    [ ] A catalyst is added when the bottle is opened
    [ ] The drink reaches a new equilibrium with more dissolved CO₂
    [ ] Sugar decomposes at room temperature
    Explanation: When the cap is removed, CO₂ escapes, lowering its partial pressure above the liquid. By Le Chatelier's principle, more dissolved CO₂ comes out of solution. Warmth further decreases gas solubility.

  Q23. An industrial chemist wants to increase the yield of product C in the reaction: A + B ⇌ C + heat. Which action would help?
    [*] Lower the temperature to shift equilibrium toward the exothermic (product) side
    [ ] Raise the temperature
    [ ] Remove A from the mixture
    [ ] Add more product C
    Explanation: Since the forward reaction is exothermic (produces heat), lowering temperature is like removing heat — a product. The system shifts right to replace the lost heat, making more C.

  Q24. A sealed container holds the equilibrium N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2 NO₂(g). The colorless N₂O₄ is on the left and brown NO₂ on the right. If the container is placed in ice, the gas becomes lighter in color. What happened?
    [*] Cooling shifted equilibrium left toward colorless N₂O₄, decreasing the brown NO₂
    [ ] The cold destroyed the NO₂ molecules
    [ ] Ice added a catalyst
    [ ] The equilibrium shifted right
    Explanation: The forward reaction (N₂O₄ → 2 NO₂) is endothermic. Cooling removes heat, so the system shifts in the exothermic direction (left), forming more colorless N₂O₄ and less brown NO₂.

  Q25. In a closed limekiln, CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g). A worker opens the kiln to vent CO₂. What happens to the equilibrium?
    [*] Equilibrium shifts right — more CaCO₃ decomposes to replace the lost CO₂
    [ ] Equilibrium shifts left
    [ ] No change — solids don't affect equilibrium
    [ ] The reaction stops
    Explanation: Removing CO₂ decreases product concentration. By Le Chatelier's principle, the system shifts right to produce more CO₂ (and CaO), consuming more CaCO₃.

  Q26. A student adds a catalyst to an equilibrium mixture and observes that concentrations don't change. Why?
    [*] A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally — it does not change the position of equilibrium
    [ ] The catalyst was defective
    [ ] Catalysts only affect the forward reaction
    [ ] The reaction was already at maximum rate
    Explanation: Catalysts lower the activation energy for both directions equally. They help the system reach equilibrium faster but do not change the value of K or the equilibrium position.

  Q27. Blood carries O₂ via hemoglobin: Hb + O₂ ⇌ HbO₂. At high altitude where O₂ is low, what happens?
    [*] Equilibrium shifts left — less HbO₂ forms, causing altitude sickness
    [ ] More HbO₂ is produced
    [ ] The equilibrium is unaffected
    [ ] Hemoglobin breaks down permanently
    Explanation: Low O₂ concentration at altitude decreases the reactant level. By Le Chatelier's, equilibrium shifts left — less HbO₂ forms, meaning less oxygen delivery to tissues.

  Q28. In the Contact process for sulfuric acid, 2 SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2 SO₃ (exothermic). Why is a moderate temperature (~450°C) used instead of a very low one?
    [*] Low temperature favors products but makes the reaction too slow — moderate temperature balances yield and rate
    [ ] Low temperature shifts equilibrium right so there is no need for a catalyst
    [ ] Higher temperature increases the value of K
    [ ] Temperature has no effect on this reaction
    Explanation: Le Chatelier's says low T favors the exothermic direction (more SO₃), but at very low T the reaction is impractically slow. Industrial conditions balance thermodynamic yield with kinetic speed.

  Q29. A beaker contains CoCl₄²⁻ (blue) ⇌ Co(H₂O)₆²⁺ (pink). Adding HCl turns the solution blue. Adding water turns it pink. Explain.
    [*] HCl adds Cl⁻ (reactant), shifting left toward blue; water dilutes Cl⁻, shifting right toward pink
    [ ] HCl is a catalyst for the blue form
    [ ] Water destroys the cobalt complex
    [ ] Temperature changes cause the color shift
    Explanation: Adding Cl⁻ ions increases reactant concentration for the CoCl₄²⁻ side, shifting left (blue). Adding water dilutes Cl⁻, shifting right toward the aqua complex (pink). Classic Le Chatelier demonstration.

  Q30. A reaction at equilibrium has K = 1. A student doubles the concentration of one reactant. What happens next?
    [*] Q < K, so the reaction shifts right (toward products) until a new equilibrium is established
    [ ] Nothing — K = 1 means the reaction cannot shift
    [ ] The reaction shifts left
    [ ] The value of K changes to 2
    Explanation: Adding reactant increases the denominator in Q, making Q < K. The system shifts right to restore equilibrium. K itself does not change (it is constant at a given temperature).
