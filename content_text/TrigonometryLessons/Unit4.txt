--- Lesson 4.1: Pythagorean Identities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Pythagorean Identities
Pythagorean Identities
Derived from sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 (the unit circle equation x² + y² = 1).
Three Forms  • sin²θ + cos²θ = 1  • Divide by cos²θ: tan²θ + 1 = sec²θ  • Divide by sin²θ: 1 + cot²θ = csc²θ
These identities are used to simplify expressions, verify other identities, and solve equations.

    [*] 1
    [ ] 2

  Q2. tan²θ + 1 =

    [ ] 4

  Q11. If cot θ = 1, then csc²θ =

  Q13. (1 − sin²θ)/cos θ =

--- Lesson 4.2: Reciprocal Identities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Reciprocal Identities
Reciprocal Identities
Each trig function has a reciprocal partner:
  • csc θ = 1/sin θ and sin θ = 1/csc θ  • sec θ = 1/cos θ and cos θ = 1/sec θ  • cot θ = 1/tan θ and tan θ = 1/cot θ
These identities let you convert between any trig function and its reciprocal to simplify expressions.
Usage
Replace less common functions (csc, sec, cot) with familiar ones (sin, cos, tan) when simplifying or verifying identities.

--- Lesson 4.3: Quotient Identities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Quotient Identities
Quotient Identities  • tan θ = sin θ / cos θ  • cot θ = cos θ / sin θ
These are the two quotient identities. They connect tangent and cotangent to sine and cosine.
Applications  • Rewrite complicated expressions in terms of sin and cos using the quotient identities.  • Combined with reciprocal and Pythagorean identities, quotient identities are powerful simplification tools.

--- Lesson 4.4: Cofunction Identities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cofunction Identities
Cofunction Identities
Cofunctions of complementary angles (adding to π/2 or 90°) are equal:
  • sin(π/2 − θ) = cos θ   and   cos(π/2 − θ) = sin θ  • tan(π/2 − θ) = cot θ   and   cot(π/2 − θ) = tan θ  • sec(π/2 − θ) = csc θ   and   csc(π/2 − θ) = sec θ
The prefix "co-" means complement: co-sine, co-tangent, co-secant.

--- Lesson 4.5: Verifying Identities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Verifying Identities
Verifying Trig Identities
To verify (prove) an identity, transform one side until it equals the other. Key techniques:
  • Work on the more complex side.  • Convert everything to sin and cos.  • Factor, combine fractions, or multiply by a conjugate.  • Use Pythagorean, reciprocal, quotient, and cofunction identities.
Important: Never cross-multiply or treat it as an equation — you're proving, not solving.

--- Lesson 4.6: Simplifying Expressions – Applications (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Simplifying Expressions – Applications
Simplifying Trig Expressions — Applications
Combining Pythagorean, reciprocal, quotient, and cofunction identities to simplify real-world and mathematical expressions.

--- Lesson 4.7: Physics Cases – Identities in Action (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Physics Cases – Identities in Action
Trig Identities in PhysicsWave Superposition
When two waves overlap: A sin(ωt) + B cos(ωt) = R sin(ωt + φ) where R = √(A² + B²) and φ = arctan(B/A).
Projectile Range
R = v₀² sin(2θ)/g. The identity sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ shows that max range occurs at θ = 45°.
Simple Harmonic Motion
x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ) can be rewritten using sum identities as A cos φ cos ωt − A sin φ sin ωt.
