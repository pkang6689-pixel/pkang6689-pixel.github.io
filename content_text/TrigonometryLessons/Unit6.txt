--- Lesson 6.1: Solving Linear Trig Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving Linear Trig Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'reference angle'?
A reference angle is the positive acute angle between the terminal side of the angle and the nearest part of the x-axis.
What is the 'general solution' of a trigonometric equation?
The general solution expresses every possible solution by adding full-period multiples (like + 2nπ) where n is any integer.
What is meant by the 'period' of a trigonometric function?
The period is the length of one complete cycle of the function before it begins repeating.
What is a 'linear' trigonometric equation?
A linear trig equation has the trigonometric function raised only to the first power, such as 2 sin θ + 1 = 0.
What is a 'coterminal angle'?
Coterminal angles differ by multiples of 2π (or 360°) and share the same terminal side on the unit circle.
What is the 'unit circle'?
The unit circle has radius 1 and center at the origin; coordinates on it give cosine and sine values for each angle.
A sound technician measures that the displacement of a speaker cone follows d(t) = 0.3 sin(440πt). She needs the cone to be at displacement d = 0.15 mm. What is the first positive time this occurs?
Set 0.3 sin(440πt) = 0.15, so sin(440πt) = 1/2. The first solution is 440πt = π/6, giving t = 1/2640 s.
A pendulum's angular displacement is θ(t) = 0.2 sin(3πt). An engineer needs to know when the pendulum first passes through θ = 0.1 radians. What is the time?
Set 0.2 sin(3πt) = 0.1, so sin(3πt) = 1/2. The first positive solution is 3πt = π/6, so t = 1/18 s.
A surveyor sights a tower and records the equation tan α = 2.5, where α is the angle of elevation. She then moves to a point where tan β = 1. What is the angle β?
tan β = 1 gives β = 45° (or π/4 radians), since tangent equals 1 at that angle.
A physics student launches a projectile. The horizontal range equation simplifies to sin α = 0.6, where α is the launch angle between 0° and 90°. What is α to the nearest degree?
α = arcsin(0.6) ≈ 36.87° ≈ 37°. Since 0 < α < 90°, this is the only solution in the given range.

--- Lesson 6.2: Solving Quadratic Trig Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving Quadratic Trig Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If the quadratic formula gives sin θ = 1.5, what is the conclusion?
Since 1.5 is outside the range [−1, 1] for sine, this root must be rejected.
What is a 'quadratic trigonometric equation'?
A quadratic trig equation contains a trigonometric function raised to the second power, like sin²θ or cos²θ.
What is the 'zero-product property'?
The zero-product property states that if AB = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0, which is essential for solving factored trig equations.
What is the 'Pythagorean identity' commonly used when solving quadratic trig equations?
The fundamental Pythagorean identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 allows substitution of sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ or vice versa.
What is the 'quadratic formula'?
The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a) solves any quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0.
Real-World Applications
Example: A physics student models oscillating voltage as V(t) = 10 sin(t). She needs to find when V² − 5V − 50 = 0. What values of V satisfy this equation?
Factor: (V − 10)(V + 5) = 0, giving V = 10 or V = −5. Since V = 10 sin(t), sin(t) = 1 or sin(t) = −1/2.

Overview

The fundamental period is the smallest positive T for which the function values repeat exactly.

Coterminal angles land on the same terminal side and differ by integer multiples of 2π (or 360°).

--- Lesson 6.5: Modeling with Trig Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Modeling with Trig Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Monthly temperatures are modeled by T(m) = 20 cos(πm/6) + 55°F. What is the average temperature?
The vertical shift D = 55 represents the average (midline) temperature.
A Ferris wheel's height is h(t) = 25 sin(πt/15) + 30. What is the maximum height?
Maximum = D + A = 30 + 25 = 55 ft.
A spring bounces with height y(t) = 4 sin(2πt) + 10. At t = 0.25, what is y?
y(0.25) = 4 sin(π/2) + 10 = 4(1) + 10 = 14.
A population model is P(t) = 500 sin(πt/6) + 2000. What is the population at t = 3?
P(3) = 500 sin(π/2) + 2000 = 500(1) + 2000 = 2500.
What is the 'amplitude' of a sinusoidal model?
Amplitude is half the total vertical range — the distance from the midline to a peak (or trough).
What is the 'midline' of a sinusoidal function?
The midline is the horizontal line y = D, the average value around which the function oscillates.
What is a 'phase shift' in a sinusoidal model?
Phase shift is the horizontal displacement, determined by C/B in y = A sin(Bx − C) + D.
What is a 'vertical shift' in a trig model?
The vertical shift D raises (positive D) or lowers (negative D) the entire sinusoidal graph.
What is meant by 'fitting a sinusoidal model' to data?
Fitting means choosing amplitude, period, phase shift, and vertical shift so the model curve approximates the data well.
What is meant by the 'period' in a real-world context like temperature modeling?
In temperature modeling, the period is typically 12 months (one year) for annual cycles.
What is the relationship between period (T) and angular frequency (B)?
The period and angular frequency are inversely related: T = 2π/B.
Blood pressure is modeled as P(t) = 20 sin(2.5πt) + 100 mmHg, where t is in seconds. A nurse needs the systolic pressure (maximum). What is it?
Max = D + A = 100 + 20 = 120 mmHg.
Real-World Applications
Example: Monthly temperatures are modeled by T(m) = 20 cos(πm/6) + 55°F. What is the average temperature?
The vertical shift D = 55 represents the average (midline) temperature.
Example: A city's average monthly temperature is modeled by T(m) = 22 cos(πm/6) + 58°F, where m = 0 is January. A construction company can only pour concrete when T ≥ 47°F. During which months (starting from m = 0) is the temperature first below 47°F?
Set T = 47: cos(πm/6) = −1/2, so πm/6 = 2π/3, giving m = 4. But cos decreases then, so T < 47 begins after m = 4. Solving more carefully: T drops below 47 at m = 8 (the second crossing going into cold months).
Example: A seasonal sales model is S(w) = 1500 sin(πw/26) + 5000 units, where w is the week of the year. When do sales first exceed 6000 units?
Set 1500 sin(πw/26) = 1000, sin(πw/26) = 2/3. Then πw/26 = arcsin(2/3) ≈ 0.7297, so w ≈ 26(0.7297)/π ≈ 6.04. Sales exceed 6000 slightly before week 6, so approximately week 5.
Important Concepts
What does 'frequency' represent in a real-world sinusoidal model
Frequency is the reciprocal of the period — it tells how many complete oscillations happen per unit interval.
What is the relationship between period (T) and angular frequency (B)
The period and angular frequency are inversely related: T = 2π/B.

--- Lesson 6.6: Physics Cases – Oscillations & Waves (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Physics Cases – Oscillations & Waves
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A mass on a spring has x(t) = 0.1 cos(10t) m. What is the period?
Period T = 2π/ω = 2π/10 = π/5 s.
A wave is described by y = 3 sin(2πx/λ − 2πft). If f = 440 Hz, what is the angular frequency ω?
ω = 2πf = 2π(440) = 880π rad/s.
A damped oscillation is x(t) = 5e^(−0.1t) cos(4t). What is the initial amplitude?
At t = 0, the envelope is 5e^0 = 5, so the initial amplitude is 5.
What is 'simple harmonic motion' (SHM)?
SHM occurs when a restoring force proportional to displacement drives the object back and forth sinusoidally.
What is 'angular frequency' (ω)?
Angular frequency ω = 2πf measures how fast the phase angle changes, in rad/s.
What is a 'node' in a standing wave?
A node is a fixed point on a standing wave where destructive interference causes zero displacement permanently.
What is an 'antinode' in a standing wave?
An antinode is a point on a standing wave where constructive interference produces the largest oscillation amplitude.
What is 'wavelength' (λ)?
Wavelength λ is the distance between two consecutive points in phase (e.g., crest to crest).
What is 'superposition' of waves?
Superposition states that when waves overlap, the net displacement equals the algebraic sum of individual displacements.
What is the 'phase' of a wave?
The phase is the total angle argument (like ωt + φ) that determines where in its cycle the wave is at any time/position.
What is 'resonance' in an oscillating system?
Resonance occurs when an external force oscillates at the system's natural frequency, producing dramatically larger oscillations.
What is the 'wave number' (k)?
The wave number k = 2π/λ tells how many radians of phase change occur per unit distance.
A tuning fork produces pressure waves p(t) = 0.003 sin(2π · 256 · t) Pa. A lab report requires the time between consecutive zero crossings (half-period). What is this time?
Period T = 1/256 s. Half-period = 1/512 s ≈ 1.95 ms.
An ocean wave has the equation y(x,t) = 1.5 sin(0.5x − 2t) m. A surfer wants to know the wave speed. What is it?
Wave speed v = ω/k = 2/0.5 = 4 m/s.
Important Concepts
A wave is described by y = 3 sin(2πx/λ − 2πft). If f = 440 Hz, what is the angular frequency ω
ω = 2πf = 2π(440) = 880π rad/s.
The frequency of a pendulum is f = (1/2π)√(g/L). If L = 1 m and g = 9.8 m/s², the period is approximately:
T = 2π√(L/g) = 2π√(1/9.8) ≈ 2π(0.3194) ≈ 2.0 s.
What is 'angular frequency' (ω)
Angular frequency ω = 2πf measures how fast the phase angle changes, in rad/s.
A mass-spring system has natural frequency ω₀ = 5 rad/s and is driven at ω = 5 rad/s. This scenario illustrates:
When the driving frequency equals the natural frequency, resonance occurs and the amplitude grows dramatically.
A physics student combines two waves: y₁ = 3 sin(10t) and y₂ = 3 sin(10.5t). The resulting 'beat frequency' heard is:
Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂| = |10/(2π) − 10.5/(2π)| = 0.5/(2π) ≈ 0.08 Hz.
