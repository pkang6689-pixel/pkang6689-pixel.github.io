--- Lesson 6.1: Solving Linear Trig Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving Linear Trig Equations
Solving Linear Trig Equations
A linear trig equation has the form a · trig(θ) + b = 0.
Steps  • 1. Isolate the trig function.  • 2. Use inverse trig to find the principal solution.  • 3. Identify all solutions in the given interval using quadrant analysis.Example
2 sin θ − 1 = 0 → sin θ = 1/2 → θ = π/6, 5π/6 (in [0, 2π)).

--- Lesson 6.2: Solving Quadratic Trig Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving Quadratic Trig Equations
Solving Quadratic Trig Equations
Equations like 2sin²θ − sinθ − 1 = 0 are quadratic in the trig function.
Strategy  • Let u = sin θ (or cos θ, tan θ) to get a standard quadratic: au² + bu + c = 0.  • Factor or use the quadratic formula.  • Solve each factor: u = k₁ or u = k₂ → trig θ = k → find θ.  • Reject solutions outside the trig function's range.

--- Lesson 6.3: Solving Multiple-Angle Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving Multiple-Angle Equations
Solving Multiple-Angle Equations
Equations like sin(2θ) = √3/2 or cos(3θ) = −1/2 involve a multiple of the angle.
Method  • Let u = 2θ (or 3θ, etc.).  • Solve for u in the expanded interval: if θ ∈ [0, 2π), then u ∈ [0, 4π) for 2θ.  • Find all u values in the expanded interval.  • Divide by the multiplier to get θ.

--- Lesson 6.4: General Solutions & Periodicity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: General Solutions & Periodicity
General Solutions & Periodicity
When no interval is specified, express all solutions using the period:
  • sin θ = k: θ = arcsin(k) + 2nπ or θ = (π − arcsin(k)) + 2nπ.  • cos θ = k: θ = ±arccos(k) + 2nπ.  • tan θ = k: θ = arctan(k) + nπ.
Here n is any integer. These formulas capture every solution by exploiting periodicity.

--- Lesson 6.5: Modeling with Trig Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Modeling with Trig Equations
Modeling with Trig Equations
Many real-world phenomena are periodic and modeled by sinusoidal equations:
Examples  • Temperature: T(t) = A sin(Bt − C) + D models seasonal temperature variation.  • Tides: h(t) = A cos(Bt − C) + D models water height over time.  • Daylight hours: periodic with a 365-day cycle.  • Setting the model equal to a value and solving gives the time(s) when that value occurs.

--- Lesson 6.5: Modeling with Trig Equations (Quiz) ---

--- Lesson 6.6: Physics Cases – Oscillations & Waves (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Physics Cases – Oscillations & Waves
Physics Cases: Oscillations & WavesSimple Harmonic Motion
x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ). Setting x = x₀ and solving for t gives the time(s) the object reaches position x₀.
Resonance
When driving frequency matches natural frequency, amplitude increases dramatically — found by solving ω_d = ω₀.
Standing Waves
Nodes occur where sin(nπx/L) = 0, i.e., at x = kL/n for integer k.
