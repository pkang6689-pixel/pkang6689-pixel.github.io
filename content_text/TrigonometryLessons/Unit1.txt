--- Lesson 1.1: Angles in Degrees & Radians (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Angles in Degrees & Radians
Angles in Degrees & Radians
An angle is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (vertex). Angles can be measured in degrees or radians.
Degrees  • A full rotation = 360°.  • A straight angle = 180°; a right angle = 90°.Radians  • A radian measures the angle subtended by an arc equal in length to the radius.  • A full rotation = 2π radians ≈ 6.283 rad.  • π radians = 180°.Conversion Formulas  • Degrees → Radians: multiply by π/180.  • Radians → Degrees: multiply by 180/π.
Example: 90° × π/180 = π/2 rad. Example: π/3 rad × 180/π = 60°.

--- Lesson 1.2: Arc Length & Sector Area (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Arc Length & Sector Area
Arc Length & Sector Area
When an angle is measured in radians, there are elegant formulas connecting the angle to the arc and sector of a circle.
Arc Length  • s = rθ where s = arc length, r = radius, θ = central angle in radians.Sector Area  • A = ½r²θ where A = area of the sector, r = radius, θ = central angle in radians.
Note: If the angle is in degrees, first convert to radians or use: s = (θ/360)·2πr and A = (θ/360)·πr².

The relationship s = rθ shows why radians are natural: arc length is simply radius × angle.

--- Lesson 1.3: Right Triangle Ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Right Triangle Ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA)
Right Triangle Ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA)
In a right triangle, the three primary trigonometric ratios relate the sides to an acute angle θ:
  • sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse  • cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse  • tan θ = opposite / adjacent
The mnemonic SOH-CAH-TOA helps remember these:
  • Sine = Opposite / Hypotenuse  • Cosine = Adjacent / Hypotenuse  • Tangent = Opposite / Adjacent
These ratios depend only on the angle, not on the size of the triangle (since all right triangles with the same acute angle are similar).

--- Lesson 1.4: Special Triangles (30°–60°–90°, 45°–45°–90°) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Special Triangles (30°–60°–90°, 45°–45°–90°)
Special Triangles
Two right triangles have exact, memorizable side ratios:
45°–45°–90° Triangle  • Both legs are equal: ratio 1 : 1 : √2.  • If each leg = a, then hypotenuse = a√2.  • sin 45° = cos 45° = √2/2 ≈ 0.707; tan 45° = 1.30°–60°–90° Triangle  • Side ratio: 1 : √3 : 2 (short leg : long leg : hypotenuse).  • Short leg is opposite 30°; long leg is opposite 60°; hypotenuse is opposite 90°.  • sin 30° = ½, cos 30° = √3/2, tan 30° = √3/3.  • sin 60° = √3/2, cos 60° = ½, tan 60° = √3.
These triangles come from splitting a square diagonally (45-45-90) or an equilateral triangle in half (30-60-90).

--- Lesson 1.5: Unit Circle Basics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Unit Circle Basics
Unit Circle Basics
The unit circle is a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin of the coordinate plane.
  • Any point on the unit circle can be written as (cos θ, sin θ) where θ is the angle from the positive x-axis.  • The x-coordinate = cos θ, the y-coordinate = sin θ.Key Angles on the Unit Circle  • 0° (0): (1, 0)  • 30° (π/6): (√3/2, 1/2)  • 45° (π/4): (√2/2, √2/2)  • 60° (π/3): (1/2, √3/2)  • 90° (π/2): (0, 1)  • 180° (π): (−1, 0)  • 270° (3π/2): (0, −1)
The unit circle extends trig functions beyond acute angles to all real numbers.

--- Lesson 1.6: Coterminal Angles & Reference Angles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Coterminal Angles & Reference Angles
Coterminal Angles & Reference AnglesCoterminal Angles  • Angles that share the same terminal side are coterminal.  • Add or subtract 360° (or 2π) to find coterminal angles: θ ± 360°n.  • Example: 30° and 390° are coterminal (390 − 360 = 30).Reference Angles  • The reference angle is the acute angle between the terminal side and the x-axis.  • Q I: ref = θ. Q II: ref = 180° − θ. Q III: ref = θ − 180°. Q IV: ref = 360° − θ.  • Trig values of any angle can be found using its reference angle and the sign for that quadrant.
ASTC rule: All trig functions positive in Q I; Sine in Q II; Tangent in Q III; Cosine in Q IV.

--- Lesson 1.7: Applications in Geometry (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Geometry
Applications of Trigonometry in Geometry
Trigonometric ratios and radian measure have wide applications in geometry:
Finding Lengths and Angles  • Use right-triangle trig to find unknown sides or angles in any geometric figure that contains right triangles.  • A diagonal of a rectangle creates two right triangles — trig finds the diagonal length or angle.Area Using Trig  • Area of a triangle: A = ½ab sin C (two sides and the included angle).  • Area of a sector: A = ½r²θ (central angle in radians).Regular Polygons  • For a regular n-gon inscribed in a circle of radius r, the side length = 2r sin(π/n) and the area = ½nr² sin(2π/n).
Trigonometry bridges the gap between angle measures and linear/area measures throughout geometry.
