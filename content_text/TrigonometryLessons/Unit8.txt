--- Lesson 8.1: The Polar Coordinate System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Polar Coordinate System
The Polar Coordinate System
A point is represented as (r, θ) where r is the distance from the origin (pole) and θ is the angle from the positive x-axis (polar axis).
Key Facts  • Unlike Cartesian coordinates, polar representations are not unique: (r, θ) = (r, θ+2nπ) = (−r, θ+π).  • Negative r means the point is in the opposite direction.  • The origin has r = 0 for any θ.

  Q5. How many polar representations does a point have?

    [ ] None

    [ ] (2, 0) in Cartesian
    [*] The point (−2, 0) in Cartesian

--- Lesson 8.2: Graphing Polar Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphing Polar Equations
Graphing Polar EquationsCommon Polar Curves  • Circle: r = a (constant), r = a cos θ, r = a sin θ.  • Cardioid: r = a ± a cos θ or r = a ± a sin θ.  • Limaçon: r = a ± b cos θ (different a, b).  • Rose: r = a cos(nθ) — n petals if n odd, 2n petals if n even.  • Spiral: r = aθ (Archimedean).

--- Lesson 8.3: Converting Polar & Rectangular Coordinates (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Converting Polar & Rectangular Coordinates
Converting Between Polar & RectangularPolar → Rectangular
x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ
Rectangular → Polar
r = √(x² + y²), θ = arctan(y/x) (adjusted for quadrant).
Equation Conversion
Substitute x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ, x²+y² = r² to convert equations between systems.

--- Lesson 8.4: Complex Numbers in Polar Form (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Complex Numbers in Polar Form
Complex Numbers in Polar Form
Any complex number z = a + bi can be written as:

z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = r · cis θ

where r = |z| = √(a²+b²) is the modulus and θ = arg(z) is the argument.
Multiplication & Division  • z₁z₂ = r₁r₂ · cis(θ₁+θ₂) — multiply moduli, add arguments.  • z₁/z₂ = (r₁/r₂) · cis(θ₁−θ₂) — divide moduli, subtract arguments.

--- Lesson 8.5: De Moivre's Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: De Moivre's Theorem
De Moivre's Theorem
[r(cos θ + i sin θ)]ⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ)
Powers
Raise modulus to the nth power and multiply the argument by n.
Roots
The nth roots of z = r cis θ are: r^(1/n) · cis((θ + 2kπ)/n) for k = 0, 1, …, n−1.

The n roots are equally spaced around a circle of radius r^(1/n).

--- Lesson 8.6: Electrical Engineering Applications (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electrical Engineering Applications
Electrical Engineering: Phasors & AC CircuitsPhasors
AC voltages and currents are sinusoidal: v(t) = V₀ cos(ωt + φ). Each is represented by a phasor V = V₀ cis φ in the complex plane.
Impedance
Z = R + jX (j = i in EE). |Z| = √(R²+X²), θ_Z = arctan(X/R).
Ohm's Law for AC
V = I · Z (phasor multiplication: multiply magnitudes, add phases).

--- Lesson 8.7: Physics Cases – Waves & Quantum Mechanics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Physics Cases – Waves & Quantum Mechanics
Physics: Waves & Quantum MechanicsWave Superposition
Adding sinusoidal waves with different phases is simplified by converting to phasors (complex exponentials) and adding the complex numbers.
Quantum Mechanics
The wave function ψ is complex-valued. Born's rule: P = |ψ|² gives the probability density, using the modulus squared of a complex number.
