--- Lesson 7.1: Law of Sines (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Law of Sines
Law of Sines
For any triangle with sides a, b, c opposite angles A, B, C:

a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C
When to Use  • AAS: Two angles and a non-included side.  • ASA: Two angles and the included side.  • SSA: Two sides and a non-included angle (ambiguous case).Derivation
Drop an altitude h from vertex C: h = a sin B = b sin A → a/sin A = b/sin B.

--- Lesson 7.2: The Ambiguous Case (SSA) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Ambiguous Case (SSA)
The Ambiguous Case (SSA)
When two sides and a non-included angle are given (SSA), there may be 0, 1, or 2 triangles.
Analysis (Given a, b, A)  • Compute sin B = b sin A / a.  • If sin B > 1 → no triangle.  • If sin B = 1 → one triangle (right angle at B).  • If sin B two triangles; if not, one triangle.

--- Lesson 7.3: Law of Cosines (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Law of Cosines
Law of Cosines
c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C

Generalizes the Pythagorean theorem (when C = 90°, cos C = 0).
When to Use  • SAS: Two sides and the included angle → find the third side.  • SSS: Three sides → find each angle.Finding Angles from SSS
cos C = (a² + b² − c²) / (2ab). Then C = arccos(…).

--- Lesson 7.4: Area of Triangles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Area of Triangles
Area of TrianglesSine Area Formula
Area = ½ ab sin C — uses two sides and the included angle.
Heron's Formula
Given all three sides a, b, c: compute s = (a+b+c)/2, then

Area = √(s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c))

--- Lesson 7.5: Navigation & Bearing Applications (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Navigation & Bearing Applications
Navigation & Bearing Applications
Bearings give direction as an angle from north (clockwise). Trig laws solve real navigation problems.
Typical Problems  • Two observers measure bearings to a target → find the distance using Law of Sines.  • A ship travels on two bearings and known distances → find displacement using Law of Cosines.  • Pilots correct for wind drift using vector addition and trig.

--- Lesson 7.6: Engineering & Surveying Cases (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A truss member is in tension at 500 N at 30° to horizontal. Horizontal component:
    [ ] 500 N
    [*] 500 cos 30° ≈ 433 N
    [ ] 500 sin 30° = 250 N
    [ ] 500 tan 30°
    Explanation: Horizontal = F cos θ.

  Q2. Same member: vertical component:
    [ ] 500 N
    [ ] 433 N
    [*] 500 sin 30° = 250 N
    [ ] 500 cos 30°
    Explanation: Vertical = F sin θ.

  Q3. Two forces of 100 N and 150 N act at 60° apart. Resultant magnitude:
    [ ] 250 N
    [ ] 50 N
    [*] √(100²+150²+2·100·150·cos60°) = √(10000+22500+15000) = √47500 ≈ 217.9 N
    [ ] 100 N
    Explanation: Law of Cosines for vector addition.

  Q4. A surveyor measures two sides of a field: 80m, 120m, with included angle 75°. Area:
    [ ] 9600 m²
    [*] ½(80)(120)sin75° ≈ 4636 m²
    [ ] 4800 m²
    [ ] 2400 m²
    Explanation: ½ ab sin C.

  Q5. A roof has a 6:12 pitch. The angle of elevation:
    [ ] 30°
    [ ] 45°
    [*] arctan(6/12) = arctan(0.5) ≈ 26.57°
    [ ] 60°
    Explanation: 6:12 means rise 6, run 12.

  Q6. An arch spans 20 m with a rise of 5 m. The radius of a circular arch:
    [ ] 5 m
    [ ] 20 m
    [*] R = (s²/(8h)) + h/2 = (400/40) + 2.5 = 12.5 m
    [ ] 10 m
    Explanation: Using the sagitta formula: R = s²/(8h) + h/2.

  Q7. A bridge cable makes angles of 25° and 35° with the towers. If the span is 100 m:
    [*] Use Law of Sines with the triangle (angles 25°, 35°, 120°) to find cable lengths
    [ ] Cable = 100 m
    [ ] Use Pythagorean theorem
    [ ] Not enough info
    Explanation: The remaining angle = 180° − 25° − 35° = 120°.

  Q8. A surveyor at point A sights point C at 40° from the baseline AB. At B, C is at 60° from BA. AB = 200 m. Find AC:
    [ ] 200 m
    [*] Using Law of Sines: angle C = 80°, AC = 200 sin 60° / sin 80° ≈ 175.9 m
    [ ] 200 sin 40°
    [ ] 300 m
    Explanation: C = 180°−40°−60° = 80°. AC/sinB = AB/sinC.

  Q9. Breaking an irregular plot into triangles is a standard technique in:
    [ ] Physics only
    [*] Land surveying and cartography

    [ ] 1

    [ ] Only the largest triangle
    Explanation: Add up all triangle areas.

  Q12. A crane boom is 30 m long at 55° from horizontal. Its tip height:
    [ ] 30 m

  Q13. The horizontal reach of the same boom:
    [ ] 30 m
    [ ] 24.57 m

  Q14. Two walls meet at 110°. A diagonal brace across the gap (walls 6m and 8m) has length:
    [ ] 14 m

  Q15. Surveying accuracy depends on:

  Q16. A tunnel must be drilled from both ends. Engineers use:

  Q18. In structural design, force triangles help determine:

--- Lesson 7.7: Astronomy Applications (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Astronomy Applications
Astronomy ApplicationsStellar Parallax
The distance to nearby stars is found using Earth's orbital diameter as a baseline and measuring the tiny angle of parallax — a direct application of the Law of Sines.
Orbital Calculations
Planetary positions, satellite orbits, and lunar distance calculations all involve oblique triangles.
Angular Separation
The angle between two celestial objects as seen from Earth.

--- Lesson 7.7: Astronomy Applications (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Stellar parallax uses Earth's orbit as:
    [ ] A telescope
    [*] A baseline (diameter ≈ 2 AU)
    [ ] A magnifier
    [ ] A filter
    Explanation: The baseline is the diameter of Earth's orbit.

  Q2. If a star has a parallax angle of 0.5 arcseconds, its distance is:
    [ ] 0.5 parsecs
    [*] 2 parsecs
    [ ] 1 parsec
    [ ] 5 parsecs
    Explanation: Distance (pc) = 1 / parallax (arcsec) = 1/0.5 = 2 pc.

  Q3. 1 parsec ≈
    [ ] 1 light-year
    [*] 3.26 light-years
    [ ] 10 light-years
    [ ] 0.1 light-years
    Explanation: 1 pc ≈ 3.26 ly.

  Q4. The parallax method uses which trig law?
    [ ] Only Pythagorean theorem
    [ ] Law of Cosines
    [*] Law of Sines (very thin triangle)
    [ ] No trig
    Explanation: The triangle is extremely elongated; Law of Sines is used with small angles.

  Q5. For very small angles, sin θ ≈
    [ ] cos θ
    [ ] tan θ
    [*] θ (in radians)
    [ ] 1
    Explanation: Small-angle approximation: sin θ ≈ θ.

  Q6. The Moon's distance from Earth was first calculated using:
    [ ] Radar
    [*] Triangulation from two cities (different viewing angles)
    [ ] GPS
    [ ] Starlight
    Explanation: Different observation points on Earth give slightly different angles → distance.

  Q7. Two observers 1000 km apart see the Moon at slightly different positions. This is:
    [ ] An illusion
    [*] Lunar parallax
    [ ] A phase change
    [ ] An eclipse
    Explanation: Different baselines give different apparent positions.

  Q8. In the Sun-Earth-Mars triangle during opposition:
    [ ] All angles are 90°
    [*] The Sun-Earth-Mars angle can be measured, and Kepler's laws give distances
    [ ] No triangle exists
    [ ] Only the Earth-Mars distance matters

  Q9. The angular separation between two stars as seen from Earth is measured in:
    [ ] Meters
    [ ] Light-years
    [*] Degrees or arcminutes/arcseconds
    [ ] Kilograms
    Explanation: Angles on the sky.

  Q10. A satellite at altitude h is seen at elevation angle θ from the ground. The slant range R:
    [ ] R = h
    [*] R = h / sin θ (simplified; exact formula uses Earth's curvature)
    [ ] R = h cos θ
    [ ] R = h tan θ
    Explanation: For flat-Earth approx, R = h / sin θ.

  Q11. If the Moon subtends an angle of about 0.5° and its distance is 384,400 km, its diameter is:

    [ ] 3,474 km (exact)
    [ ] 500 km
    [ ] 1 km

  Q12. Kepler's equation relates orbital position to time using:
    [ ] Only algebra
    [*] An equation involving sine (M = E − e sin E)
    [ ] No trig
    [ ] Only logarithms
    Explanation: Kepler's equation: M = E − e sin E (transcendental equation requiring numerical methods).

  Q13. The ecliptic is tilted 23.5° relative to the celestial equator because:
    [ ] The Moon orbits at 23.5°
    [*] Earth's axis is tilted 23.5° from perpendicular to its orbital plane

    [ ] Stars move

  Q14. To find the distance between two planets at known orbital radii and angular separation:
    [ ] Add the radii

    [ ] Subtract the radii
    [ ] Multiply

  Q15. Mars orbits at ~1.52 AU, Earth at 1 AU. When 60° apart from the Sun:

  Q16. The transit of Venus was historically used to determine:
    [ ] Venus's color

  Q17. Radio telescopes measure angular position to determine:
    [ ] Color
    [*] Precise coordinates and separation of celestial objects

    [ ] Temperature directly
    Explanation: Angular resolution and position measurement.

  Q18. The small-angle formula d = Dθ assumes:
    [ ] θ in degrees
