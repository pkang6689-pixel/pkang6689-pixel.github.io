--- Lesson 7.1: Solving by Factoring — GCF &amp; Grouping (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving by Factoring — GCF &amp; Grouping
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of a set of terms?
The GCF is the largest monomial (including coefficients and variables) that divides evenly into every term of the expression.
What is the Zero Product Property?
The Zero Product Property states that if AB = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0, which is the fundamental principle that allows us to solve equations by factoring.
What is a 'common binomial factor'?
A common binomial factor is a binomial (like (x + 3)) that appears in every term after initial grouping, allowing it to be factored out to complete the factoring process.
What is a 'monomial'?
A monomial is a single-term algebraic expression consisting of a constant, a variable, or a product of constants and variables with non-negative integer exponents.
A student needs to solve 7x³ + 14x² + 7x = 0. What is the first step?
The GCF of all terms is 7x, so factor it out first: 7x(x² + 2x + 1) = 7x(x + 1)² = 0, giving x = 0 or x = −1.

--- Lesson 7.2: Solving by Factoring — Trinomials (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving by Factoring — Trinomials
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'trinomial'?
A trinomial is a polynomial expression with exactly three terms, such as x² + 5x + 6 or ax² + bx + c.
What is the 'AC method' for factoring trinomials?
The AC method multiplies a and c, finds two numbers that multiply to ac and add to b, rewrites the middle term, and then factors by grouping.
What is a 'quadratic trinomial'?
A quadratic trinomial specifically has degree 2 and three terms, written as ax² + bx + c where a is not zero.
What is the 'product-sum method' for factoring x² + bx + c?
For x² + bx + c (where a = 1), find two numbers p and q such that p × q = c and p + q = b, then write the factored form as (x + p)(x + q).
A rectangular pool has an area of x² + 8x + 15 square feet. If the length is (x + 5), what is the width?
Factor x² + 8x + 15 = (x + 3)(x + 5), so since the length is (x + 5), the width must be (x + 3) feet.
A picture frame has a total area of 2x² + 11x + 12 square inches. If the frame factors as (2x + 3)(x + 4), and x = 5, what is the area?
Using the factored form: (2(5) + 3)(5 + 4) = (13)(9) = 117 square inches, which matches 2(25) + 11(5) + 12 = 50 + 55 + 12 = 117.
A rectangular field has length (x + 7) meters and width (x − 3) meters. If the area is 75 square meters, what is x?
Area = (x + 7)(x − 3) = x² + 4x − 21 = 75, so x² + 4x − 96 = 0 → (x + 12)(x − 8) = 0, giving x = 8 (since x must make width positive).
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'check by multiplying' mean after factoring?
After factoring, always expand (multiply out) the result using FOIL or distribution to confirm it matches the original expression, ensuring no errors were made.

    [ ] (x + 2)(x + 6)

--- Lesson 7.3: Special Products &amp; Factoring Patterns (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Special Products &amp; Factoring Patterns
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference of squares pattern?
The difference of squares is a factoring pattern where a² − b² equals (a + b)(a − b). It requires two perfect square terms separated by subtraction.
What is a perfect square trinomial?
A perfect square trinomial is a trinomial of the form a² + 2ab + b² or a² − 2ab + b², which factors as (a + b)² or (a − b)² respectively. The middle term is always twice the product of the square roots of the first and last terms.
What is meant by factoring completely?
Factoring completely means breaking an expression down until every factor is either a prime polynomial or a monomial. This may require multiple steps, such as factoring out a GCF first and then applying a special pattern.
What is a conjugate pair?
A conjugate pair consists of two binomials that are identical except for the sign between their terms, such as (a + b) and (a − b). Multiplying conjugates produces a difference of squares: (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b².
What is a special product in the context of factoring?
Special products are polynomial expressions that result from specific multiplication patterns, including the difference of squares a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b) and perfect square trinomials a² ± 2ab + b² = (a ± b)². Recognizing these patterns makes factoring faster.
What is a prime polynomial?
A prime polynomial (also called irreducible) is one that cannot be written as a product of two or more polynomials of lower degree with integer coefficients. For example, x² + 1 is prime over the reals.
What is the expanded form of (a + b)²?
The square of a binomial (a + b)² expands to a² + 2ab + b². A common mistake is to write only a² + b², forgetting the crucial middle term 2ab.
What is the expanded form of (a − b)²?
The square of a binomial (a − b)² expands to a² − 2ab + b². Note that the last term b² is always positive because squaring a negative gives a positive result.
A landscaper has a rectangular garden that is (x + 6) meters long and (x − 6) meters wide. What is the area in factored form, and what special product pattern does the expanded area represent?
Multiplying (x + 6)(x − 6) using the difference of squares pattern gives x² − 36. This demonstrates that when conjugate pairs are multiplied, the cross terms cancel out.

A decorator is comparing two square rugs. One has area x² square feet and the other has area 100 square feet. The difference in their areas, x² − 100, needs to be factored for a pricing formula. What is the factored form?
Since x² − 100 = x² − 10², this is a difference of squares. Applying the pattern gives (x + 10)(x − 10). Note that 100 = 10², so the terms inside each binomial are x and 10.

First apply difference of squares: (x²)² − 9² = (x² + 9)(x² − 9). Then factor x² − 9 again as (x + 3)(x − 3). The factor x² + 9 is a sum of squares and cannot be factored further over the reals.

--- Lesson 7.4: Completing the Square (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Completing the Square
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is completing the square?
Completing the square is an algebraic technique where you manipulate a quadratic expression of the form x² + bx by adding (b/2)² to create a perfect square trinomial that can be written as (x + b/2)².
What is vertex form of a quadratic function?
Vertex form is y = a(x − h)² + k, where the vertex of the parabola is at the point (h, k). Completing the square converts standard form into vertex form, making it easy to identify the vertex.
What is a perfect square trinomial?
A perfect square trinomial takes the form a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)² or a² − 2ab + b² = (a − b)². Completing the square creates a perfect square trinomial by adding the right constant.
What is the relationship between completing the square and the quadratic formula?
The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) is derived by applying the completing the square process to the general quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0. This shows the two methods are fundamentally connected.
What is standard form of a quadratic equation?
Standard form of a quadratic equation is ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are real numbers and a ≠ 0. This is the starting form before converting to vertex form by completing the square.
What is the leading coefficient in a quadratic expression?
The leading coefficient is the number multiplied by the highest-degree term, which in a quadratic is the x² term. When completing the square, the leading coefficient must equal 1, so you divide through by it first if needed.
What is the vertex of a parabola?
The vertex is the maximum or minimum point of a parabola, depending on whether it opens down or up. In vertex form y = a(x − h)² + k, the vertex is the point (h, k). Completing the square reveals the vertex directly.
A ball is thrown upward and its height in feet is modeled by h = −t² + 6t + 2. A student completes the square to find the maximum height. What is the vertex form of this equation?
Factor out −1 from the quadratic terms: h = −(t² − 6t) + 2. Complete the square inside: h = −(t² − 6t + 9 − 9) + 2 = −(t − 3)² + 9 + 2 = −(t − 3)² + 11. The maximum height is 11 feet at t = 3 seconds.
An architect designs a parabolic arch described by y = −x² + 12x − 32. To find the peak of the arch, she completes the square. What is the peak height?
Factor out −1: y = −(x² − 12x) − 32. Complete the square: y = −(x² − 12x + 36 − 36) − 32 = −(x − 6)² + 36 − 32 = −(x − 6)² + 4. The peak height is 4 units at x = 6.
A landscape designer models a fountain's water arc as y = −x² + 10x − 21. She needs to find the vertex to position the fountain. What is the vertex?
Factor out −1: y = −(x² − 10x) − 21. Complete the square: y = −(x² − 10x + 25 − 25) − 21 = −(x − 5)² + 25 − 21 = −(x − 5)² + 4. The vertex is (5, 4).

--- Lesson 7.5: The Quadratic Formula (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Quadratic Formula
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For 2x² − 4x − 6 = 0, what is a?
In the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, the value of a is the coefficient of x², which is 2. The values b = −4 and c = −6 are the other coefficients.
What is the quadratic formula?
The quadratic formula x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a) is a universal formula that provides the solutions (roots) of any quadratic equation in standard form. It is derived by completing the square on the general equation ax² + bx + c = 0.
What is the discriminant in the quadratic formula?
The discriminant is the expression b² − 4ac found under the square root in the quadratic formula. Its value determines the number and type of solutions: positive means two real solutions, zero means one, and negative means no real solutions.
What is a root of a quadratic equation?
A root (also called a solution or zero) of a quadratic equation is a value of x that satisfies the equation ax² + bx + c = 0. Graphically, roots correspond to the x-intercepts where the parabola crosses the x-axis.
What is a repeated root?
A repeated root (also called a double root) occurs when b² − 4ac = 0, making the ± portion equal to zero. Both solutions collapse to one value: x = −b/(2a). Graphically, the parabola just touches the x-axis at its vertex.
What is a coefficient in the context of ax² + bx + c = 0?
A coefficient is the numerical factor in front of a variable term. In ax² + bx + c = 0, a is the coefficient of x², b is the coefficient of x, and c is the constant term. Correctly identifying coefficients is essential for using the quadratic formula.
Real-World Applications
Example: A football is kicked and follows the path h = −4.9t² + 14.7t + 1, where h is meters and t is seconds. A goalpost crossbar is at h = 3.05 m. Using the quadratic formula, does the ball clear the crossbar at t = 0.15 seconds?
Plug in t = 0.15: h = −4.9(0.0225) + 14.7(0.15) + 1 = −0.11 + 2.205 + 1 = 3.095 ≈ 3.1 m. Since 3.1 > 3.05, the ball clears the crossbar. The quadratic formula can also be used to find the exact times when h = 3.05.

--- Lesson 7.6: The Discriminant (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Discriminant
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the discriminant of a quadratic equation?
The discriminant, denoted D or Delta, is the expression b² − 4ac found under the square root in the quadratic formula. It acts as a diagnostic tool: D > 0 means two real roots, D = 0 means one repeated root, and D < 0 means no real roots.
What is a rational root?
A rational root is a solution that can be written as a ratio of two integers, such as 3, −1/2, or 7/4. The discriminant produces rational roots when it is a non-negative perfect square.
What is an irrational root?
An irrational root contains a radical that cannot be simplified to a whole number or fraction. This occurs when the discriminant is positive but not a perfect square, producing values like √2, √5, or √13 in the solution.
What is a complex solution?
Complex solutions contain the imaginary unit i = √(−1) and take the form a + bi. They arise when b² − 4ac < 0, meaning the value under the square root is negative. In Algebra 1, we identify these as 'no real solutions.'
What is a perfect square in the context of the discriminant?
A perfect square is a number that is the square of an integer (0, 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ...). When the discriminant is a positive perfect square, the roots are rational and the quadratic can be factored over the integers.
What is a double root?
A double root (also called a repeated root) occurs when the discriminant equals zero. The quadratic touches the x-axis at exactly one point, its vertex. The single solution is x = −b/(2a).
What is the relationship between the discriminant and the graph of a parabola?
The discriminant directly tells you how many x-intercepts the parabola has. D > 0 means two x-intercepts (two crossings), D = 0 means one x-intercept (vertex touches the axis), and D < 0 means no x-intercepts.
What is the x-intercept of a parabola?
An x-intercept is a point where the parabola meets the x-axis, meaning y = 0. Each x-intercept corresponds to a real root of the quadratic equation. The number of x-intercepts is determined by the discriminant.

--- Lesson 7.7: Systems with Quadratics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Systems with Quadratics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a system of equations?
A system of equations is a collection of two or more equations involving the same set of variables. A solution to the system is an ordered pair (or set of values) that makes every equation in the system true at the same time.
What is the substitution method for solving systems?
The substitution method involves isolating one variable in one equation (e.g., y = x + 2), then replacing that variable in the other equation. For linear-quadratic systems, this typically means setting the two expressions for y equal to each other.
What is a linear-quadratic system?
A linear-quadratic system consists of one linear equation (degree 1, such as y = mx + b) and one quadratic equation (degree 2, such as y = ax² + bx + c). This type of system can have 0, 1, or 2 solutions.
What is a secant line in relation to a parabola?
A secant line is a line that crosses through a curve at two distinct points. When a secant line intersects a parabola, the linear-quadratic system has two solutions, corresponding to a positive discriminant.
What is an intersection point?
An intersection point is an ordered pair (x, y) that lies on the graphs of all equations in a system. Graphically, it is where the curves cross or touch each other. Each intersection point is a solution to the system.
What is a quadratic-quadratic system?
A quadratic-quadratic system involves two equations that are both quadratic (degree 2). Two parabolas can intersect at 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4 points, depending on their orientations and positions.
What is the elimination method?
The elimination method (also called the addition method) involves combining equations so that one variable cancels out. For quadratic-quadratic systems, subtracting one equation from the other can eliminate the x² terms, leaving a simpler equation to solve.
Real-World Applications

--- Lesson 7.8: Quadratic Inequalities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Quadratic Inequalities
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a quadratic inequality?
A quadratic inequality is an inequality involving a quadratic expression, such as ax² + bx + c > 0 or ax² + bx + c ≤ 0. Instead of finding exact values (as with equations), you find ranges of values that satisfy the inequality.
What is a test point in the context of solving inequalities?
A test point is any value selected from within an interval to check the sign of the quadratic expression. For example, if the roots are 2 and 5, you might test x = 0, x = 3, and x = 6 to determine where the expression is positive or negative.
What is a sign chart (or sign analysis)?
A sign chart is a visual tool that marks the roots on a number line and labels each interval with + or − to show where the expression is positive or negative. This helps identify which intervals satisfy the given inequality.
What is the difference between a strict inequality and a non-strict inequality?
A strict inequality (< or >) excludes the boundary values (roots), shown with open circles or parentheses. A non-strict inequality (≤ or ≥) includes the boundary values, shown with closed circles or brackets.
What is a compound inequality?
A compound inequality combines two inequality statements. For quadratic inequalities, solutions often take the form of a compound inequality (like −3 ≤ x ≤ 5 for 'between the roots') or a disjunction (like x < −3 or x > 5 for 'outside the roots').

    Explanation: Set h &gt; 48: −16t² + 64t &gt; 48, so −16t² + 64t − 48 &gt; 0. Divide by −16 (flip inequality): t² − 4t + 3 &lt; 0. Factor: (t − 1)(t − 3) &lt; 0. The solution is 1 &lt; t &lt; 3 seconds.

  Q22. A farmer wants the area of a rectangular pen to be at least 96 square feet. If one side is x and the other is (20 − x), solve x(20 − x) ≥ 96 to find valid widths.
    [*] 8 ≤ x ≤ 12
    [ ] 0 &lt; x &lt; 20
    [ ] x ≤ 8 or x ≥ 12
    [ ] 4 ≤ x ≤ 16
    Explanation: Expand: −x² + 20x ≥ 96, so x² − 20x + 96 ≤ 0. Factor: (x − 8)(x − 12) ≤ 0. Roots at x = 8 and x = 12. Between the roots: 8 ≤ x ≤ 12. The width must be between 8 and 12 feet.

  Q23. A bridge can handle stress modeled by S = x² − 10x + 24. The bridge is safe when S ≤ 0. For what values of x is the bridge safe?
    [ ] x ≤ 24
    [ ] x ≤ 4 or x ≥ 6
    [ ] 0 ≤ x ≤ 10
    [*] 4 ≤ x ≤ 6
    Explanation: Solve x² − 10x + 24 ≤ 0. Factor: (x − 4)(x − 6) ≤ 0. The roots are x = 4 and x = 6. Since the parabola opens upward, the expression is non-positive between the roots: 4 ≤ x ≤ 6.

  Q24. A company's profit is P = −x² + 12x − 20. For what range of production levels (x) is the profit positive (P &gt; 0)?
    [ ] x &gt; 20
    [ ] 0 &lt; x &lt; 12
    [*] 2 &lt; x &lt; 10
    [ ] x &lt; 2 or x &gt; 10
    Explanation: Set P &gt; 0: −x² + 12x − 20 &gt; 0, or x² − 12x + 20 &lt; 0. Factor: (x − 2)(x − 10) &lt; 0. The roots are x = 2 and x = 10. The expression is negative between the roots, so the profit is positive for 2 &lt; x &lt; 10.

  Q25. A firework must stay above 80 feet for the display to be visible. Its height is h = −16t² + 96t. During what time interval is the firework above 80 feet?
    [*] 1 &lt; t &lt; 5 seconds
    [ ] 2 &lt; t &lt; 4 seconds
    [ ] 0 &lt; t &lt; 6 seconds
    [ ] t &lt; 1 or t &gt; 5 seconds
    Explanation: Set −16t² + 96t &gt; 80: −16t² + 96t − 80 &gt; 0. Divide by −16 (flip): t² − 6t + 5 &lt; 0. Factor: (t − 1)(t − 5) &lt; 0. Between the roots: 1 &lt; t &lt; 5 seconds.

  Q26. A safety zone requires that the value of x² − 6x + 5 remain non-positive. What range of x values satisfies this condition?
    [ ] x ≤ 1 or x ≥ 5
    [ ] −1 ≤ x ≤ 5
    [ ] 0 ≤ x ≤ 6
    [*] 1 ≤ x ≤ 5
    Explanation: Solve x² − 6x + 5 ≤ 0. Factor: (x − 1)(x − 5) ≤ 0. Roots at x = 1 and x = 5. Since the parabola opens upward, the expression is non-positive between the roots: 1 ≤ x ≤ 5.

  Q27. A swimmer dives from a board and her height is h = −t² + 4t + 5. During what time interval is she above the water (h &gt; 0)?
    [ ] 1 &lt; t &lt; 5 seconds
    [ ] 0 &lt; t &lt; 4 seconds
    [*] −1 &lt; t &lt; 5, so for valid times: 0 ≤ t &lt; 5 seconds
    [ ] t &gt; 5 seconds
    Explanation: Set −t² + 4t + 5 &gt; 0, or t² − 4t − 5 &lt; 0. Factor: (t − 5)(t + 1) &lt; 0. The roots are t = −1 and t = 5. Between the roots: −1 &lt; t &lt; 5. Since time starts at 0, the swimmer is above water for 0 ≤ t &lt; 5 seconds.

  Q28. A theater charges x dollars per ticket. Revenue is R = −x² + 20x. For what ticket prices does the revenue exceed $75?
    [ ] $10 &lt; x &lt; $20
    [ ] $0 &lt; x &lt; $20
    [ ] x &lt; $5 or x &gt; $15
    [*] $5 &lt; x &lt; $15
    Explanation: Set R &gt; 75: −x² + 20x &gt; 75, so −x² + 20x − 75 &gt; 0, or x² − 20x + 75 &lt; 0. Factor: (x − 5)(x − 15) &lt; 0. Between the roots: 5 &lt; x &lt; 15. Revenue exceeds $75 when the ticket price is between $5 and $15.

  Q29. An engineer needs to ensure that stress values modeled by S = x² − 8x + 12 stay positive. For what values of x is S &gt; 0?
    [ ] 0 &lt; x &lt; 12
    [*] x &lt; 2 or x &gt; 6
    [ ] x &gt; 8
    [ ] 2 &lt; x &lt; 6
    Explanation: Factor: (x − 2)(x − 6) &gt; 0. Roots at x = 2 and x = 6. Since the parabola opens upward, the expression is positive outside the roots: x &lt; 2 or x &gt; 6.

  Q30. A tennis ball is hit with height h = −5t² + 20t. The net is 1 meter high. During what time interval does the ball clear the net (h &gt; 1)?
    [ ] t &gt; 1 second
    [*] Approximately 0.05 &lt; t &lt; 3.95 seconds
    [ ] 0 &lt; t &lt; 4 seconds
    [ ] 1 &lt; t &lt; 3 seconds
    Explanation: Set −5t² + 20t &gt; 1: −5t² + 20t − 1 &gt; 0, or 5t² − 20t + 1 &lt; 0. Using the quadratic formula: t = (20 ± √(400 − 20))/10 = (20 ± √380)/10 = 2 ± √(19/5). Since √(19/5) ≈ 1.949, the ball clears the net from approximately 0.05 to 3.95 seconds.

--- Lesson 7.8: Quadratic Inequalities (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: What is a quadratic inequality?
     A: An inequality with a quadratic expression: ax² + bx + c > 0 (or <, ≤, ≥)

  2. Q: What are the steps to solve?
     A: Solve the related equation = 0, find boundary points, test intervals, write solution

  3. Q: Solve: x² − 4 > 0
     A: x < −2 or x > 2 (values outside the roots)

  4. Q: Solve: x² − 9 ≤ 0
     A: −3 ≤ x ≤ 3 (values between the roots, inclusive)

  5. Q: What is the first step in solving x² − 5x + 6 < 0?
     A: Solve x² − 5x + 6 = 0 to find boundary points

  6. Q: Open circle on a boundary means:
     A: The value is NOT included (< or >)

  7. Q: x² + 1 > 0 has what solution?
     A: All real numbers (x² + 1 is always positive)

  8. Q: How do you test intervals?
     A: Pick a test point in each interval and check if the inequality is true

  9. Q: Solve: x² − 1 ≥ 0
     A: x ≤ −1 or x ≥ 1

  10. Q: Graph of x² − 4 < 0 on a number line:
     A: Open circles at −2 and 2, shade between them
