--- Lesson 5.1: Exponential Growth (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential Growth
0 and b > 1.">An exponential growth function has the form f(x) = a · bˣ where a > 0 and b > 1.

The base b is the growth factor. The growth rate r is found from b = 1 + r.

Exponential growth increases slowly at first, then rapidly — it eventually outpaces any linear or polynomial growth.

Real-world examples: population growth, compound interest, viral spread.

--- Lesson 5.3: Exponential vs. Linear Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential vs. Linear Models
Linear growth: constant rate of change (add/subtract the same amount). Graph is a straight line.

Exponential growth: constant percent/multiplicative change (multiply by the same factor). Graph is a curve.

Linear: y = mx + b. Exponential: y = a · bˣ.

To identify: check if consecutive y-values have a common difference (linear) or common ratio (exponential).

--- Lesson 5.4: Geometric Sequences (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Geometric Sequences
A geometric sequence has a common ratio (r) between consecutive terms.

The explicit formula is: a(n) = a(1) · r^(n−1).

If |r| > 1, the sequence grows; if 0 < |r| < 1, the sequence shrinks.

Example: 3, 6, 12, 24, ... has r = 2 and a(n) = 3 · 2^(n−1).

--- Lesson 5.5: Compound Interest (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Compound Interest
The compound interest formula: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where P = principal, r = annual rate, n = compoundings per year, t = time in years.

Common compounding: annually (n=1), semi-annually (n=2), quarterly (n=4), monthly (n=12), daily (n=365).

Continuous compounding: A = Pe^(rt).

Compound interest grows faster than simple interest (I = Prt) because interest earns interest.

--- Lesson 5.6: Exponential Equations from Tables &amp; Graphs (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Exponential Equations from Tables & Graphs

To find an exponential equation from a table: (1) Find the common ratio r. (2) Find the initial value a (when x = 0). (3) Write y = a · rˣ.

From a graph: read the y-intercept (a) and use two points to calculate the ratio r.

If x-values in a table are not consecutive, compute the ratio using r = (y₂/y₁)^(1/(x₂−x₁)).

Always verify by checking that the equation produces the correct values for all given data points.

--- Lesson 5.7: Transformations of Exponential Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Transformations of Exponential Functions
y = a · b^(x−h) + k: h shifts horizontally, k shifts vertically, a stretches/reflects.

The horizontal asymptote shifts from y = 0 to y = k.

Reflection: −a reflects over the x-axis; b^(−x) reflects over the y-axis.

Transformations of exponential functions follow the same rules as other function transformations.

--- Lesson 5.9: Interpreting Exponential Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interpreting Exponential Models
In y = a · bˣ, a is the initial value and b is the growth/decay factor.

 0. Decay: r The growth/decay rate r = b − 1. Growth: r > 0. Decay: r < 0.

Interpret slope-like meaning from context: 'increases by r% per unit of x.'

Compare models by comparing their growth factors or initial values in context.

--- Lesson 5.10: Exponential Regression (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential Regression
Exponential regression uses technology to find the best-fit exponential equation y = a · bˣ for a data set.

Use a graphing calculator or software: enter data, run ExpReg to get a and b.

The correlation coefficient r (or r²) indicates how well the model fits. Closer to ±1 (or 1) means a better fit.

Use the regression equation to make predictions, but be cautious with extrapolation.
