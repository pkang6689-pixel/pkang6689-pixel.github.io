--- Lesson 5.1: Exponential Growth (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential Growth
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In y = 5 · 4ˣ, what is y when x = 2?
Substitute x = 2: y = 5 · 4² = 5 · 16 = 80. You raise the base to the exponent first, then multiply by the initial value.
What is an exponential function?
An exponential function is specifically defined as y = a · bˣ where the base b is a positive constant not equal to 1, and 'a' is a nonzero constant. The variable appears in the exponent, which distinguishes it from linear and quadratic functions.
What is a growth factor?
The growth factor is the base b in an exponential function y = a · bˣ. It represents the constant multiplier applied to the output each time x increases by 1. For growth, b > 1.
What is a growth rate in the context of exponential functions?
The growth rate r is the decimal or percentage by which the quantity increases each period. It is found by subtracting 1 from the growth factor: r = b − 1. For example, b = 1.08 means an 8% growth rate.
What is an asymptote?
An asymptote is a line that a graph gets closer and closer to but never actually reaches. For basic exponential growth functions y = a · bˣ, the horizontal asymptote is y = 0.
What is the domain of an exponential function?
The domain of any exponential function y = a · bˣ is all real numbers, meaning x can be any value from negative infinity to positive infinity. There is no restriction on the input.
What is the range of y = a · bˣ when a > 0?
When a > 0, the output of y = a · bˣ is always positive because a positive number times a positive power is always positive. The function approaches but never reaches zero, so the range is y > 0.
What is the difference between exponential and linear growth?
Linear growth increases by the same fixed amount each period (constant rate of change), while exponential growth increases by the same percentage or factor each period (constant multiplicative change). Eventually, exponential growth always surpasses linear growth.
A charity fundraiser collects $1,000 on the first day. Each subsequent day, donations increase by 20%. What is the total collected on day 4 alone?
Day 4 is 3 periods after day 1, so donations = 1000(1.20)³ = 1000 × 1.728 = $1,728. Each day the donations are multiplied by 1.20, and after three growth periods the amount has increased by 72.8%.

--- Lesson 5.2: Exponential Decay (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential Decay
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 500(0.8)³?
500(0.8)³ = 500 × 0.512 = 256. Calculate 0.8³ = 0.8 × 0.8 × 0.8 = 0.512, then multiply by 500.
What is exponential decay?
Exponential decay is a pattern where a quantity decreases by the same percentage or factor each time period. It is modeled by y = a · bˣ with 0 < b < 1, producing a curve that drops rapidly at first and then levels off toward zero.
What is a decay factor?
The decay factor is the base b in the exponential decay function y = a · bˣ, where 0 < b < 1. It represents the fraction of the quantity that remains after each time period.
What is a decay rate?
The decay rate r is the fraction or percentage of the quantity that is lost each period. It is found by r = 1 − b, where b is the decay factor. For example, b = 0.85 gives a decay rate of 15%.
What is a half-life?
A half-life is the time required for a quantity undergoing exponential decay to decrease to exactly half of its current value. It is commonly used in radioactive decay and pharmacology.
What is the horizontal asymptote of a basic exponential decay function?
For a basic exponential decay function y = a · bˣ with no vertical shift, the horizontal asymptote is y = 0. The function values get closer and closer to zero but never actually equal zero.
What is the range of y = a · bˣ when a > 0 and 0 < b < 1?
When a > 0 and 0 < b < 1, the function always produces positive outputs. As x increases, y approaches but never reaches 0, and as x decreases, y grows without bound. So the range is y > 0.
What is the difference between exponential decay and linear decrease?
Linear decrease subtracts the same fixed amount each period (constant rate of change), while exponential decay multiplies by the same fraction each period (constant multiplicative change). Exponential decay decreases quickly at first, then slows down.
A new car is purchased for $30,000 and depreciates at 12% per year. What is the car's value after 3 years?
V = 30000(0.88)³ = 30000 × 0.681472 ≈ $20,440.32. The decay factor is 1 − 0.12 = 0.88, and after 3 years the car retains about 68.1% of its original value.
A laptop purchased for $1,200 depreciates at 20% per year. What is its value after 4 years?
V = 1200(0.80)⁴ = 1200 × 0.4096 = $491.52. The decay factor is 1 − 0.20 = 0.80, and after 4 years the laptop has retained about 41% of its original value.
A cup of hot coffee at 180°F cools such that the temperature above room temperature (70°F) decreases by 15% each minute. What is the coffee's temperature after 2 minutes?
The excess temperature is 180 − 70 = 110°F. After 2 minutes: excess = 110(0.85)² = 110 × 0.7225 = 79.475°F. Total temperature = 70 + 79.475 ≈ 149.5°F.
A smartphone's resale value was $900 new. Two years later it is worth $576. Assuming exponential decay, what is the annual decay factor?
576 = 900 · b², so b² = 576/900 = 0.64, and b = √0.64 = 0.80. The phone retains 80% of its value each year, meaning a 20% annual depreciation rate.

--- Lesson 5.3: Exponential vs. Linear Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential vs. Linear Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a common difference?
A common difference is the constant value obtained by subtracting any term from the next term in a sequence. If the common difference is the same throughout, the pattern is linear.
What is a common ratio?
A common ratio is the constant multiplier found by dividing any term by the preceding term. If each ratio is the same, the data follows an exponential pattern.
What is a rate of change?
The rate of change measures how much the output (y) changes for each unit change in the input (x). For linear functions, this rate is constant and equals the slope.
What is slope in a linear function?
The slope m = (change in y)/(change in x) = rise/run. It tells how much the output changes per unit increase in x, and for a linear function this rate is the same everywhere.
What is the y-intercept in the context of linear vs. exponential models?
In both linear (y = mx + b) and exponential (y = a · bˣ) functions, the y-intercept is the output when x = 0. For linear it is b, and for exponential it is a.
What is additive change?
Additive change means a constant amount is added to (or subtracted from) the output for each unit increase in x. This describes a linear function with a constant rate of change.
What is the key way to distinguish linear from exponential data in a table?
To identify the model type from a table, compute differences between consecutive y-values. If the differences are constant, it is linear. If the ratios of consecutive y-values are constant, it is exponential.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'multiplicative change' mean?
Multiplicative change means that to go from one output to the next, you multiply by a constant factor rather than adding a constant amount. This is the defining characteristic of exponential functions.
Example: A store tracks weekly sales. Week 1: 200 units, Week 2: 250, Week 3: 300, Week 4: 350. Which model best fits, and what are predicted Week 5 sales?
The differences are constant: 250 − 200 = 50, 300 − 250 = 50, 350 − 300 = 50. This is linear with slope 50. Week 5: 350 + 50 = 400 units.

--- Lesson 5.4: Geometric Sequences (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Geometric Sequences
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a geometric sequence?
A geometric sequence is a sequence in which each term after the first is found by multiplying the previous term by a fixed nonzero number called the common ratio. This is in contrast to an arithmetic sequence, which uses addition.
What is the common ratio in a geometric sequence?
The common ratio r is the constant multiplier between successive terms. It is found by dividing any term by its predecessor: r = a(n+1)/a(n). If this ratio is the same for all pairs, the sequence is geometric.
What is an explicit formula?
An explicit formula lets you find any term directly from its position number n without computing all the terms before it. For a geometric sequence, the explicit formula is a(n) = a(1) · r^(n−1).
What is a recursive formula?
A recursive formula defines each term in relation to the previous term. For geometric sequences, it is a(n) = r · a(n−1), meaning you multiply the previous term by the common ratio to find the next one.
What is the difference between an arithmetic and a geometric sequence?
In an arithmetic sequence, you add the same number (common difference d) to get from one term to the next. In a geometric sequence, you multiply by the same number (common ratio r). This fundamental distinction separates the two types.
What is a finite geometric series?
A finite geometric series is the sum of the first n terms of a geometric sequence. Its formula is S(n) = a(1)(1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r), which adds up a specific number of terms.
What is the nth term of a geometric sequence?
The nth term is the specific value at position n in the sequence. It is calculated using the explicit formula a(n) = a(1) · r^(n−1), where a(1) is the first term and r is the common ratio.
A ball is dropped from 80 feet. Each bounce reaches 3/4 of the previous height. What is the height after the 3rd bounce?
This is a geometric sequence with a(1) = 80(3/4) = 60 for the first bounce. The height after the 3rd bounce: 80 · (3/4)³ = 80 · 27/64 = 80 · 0.421875 = 33.75 feet.
An investment of $5,000 earns 10% annually. The value each year forms a geometric sequence. What is the value after 3 years?
a(1) = 5000, r = 1.10. After 3 years: 5000 · (1.10)³ = 5000 · 1.331 = $6,655. Each year the investment grows by 10%, creating a geometric sequence.
Real-World Applications
Example: A runner's training plan starts at 2 miles in week 1. Each week the distance is multiplied by 1.25. What distance does the runner cover in week 5?
a(5) = 2 · (1.25)⁴ = 2 · 2.4414 ≈ 4.88 miles. The distance increases by 25% each week, forming a geometric sequence with a(1) = 2 and r = 1.25.

--- Lesson 5.5: Compound Interest (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Compound Interest
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is compound interest?
Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the interest that has already been added. This means you earn 'interest on interest,' which causes the balance to grow exponentially over time.
What is principal?
The principal is the initial amount of money that is invested, deposited, or borrowed. It is the starting point for all interest calculations, represented by P in the formulas.
What is the Annual Percentage Yield (APY)?
APY is the effective annual rate of return that accounts for compounding. It is always equal to or greater than the stated (nominal) rate, and it allows for fair comparison between accounts with different compounding frequencies.
What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 estimates the doubling time of an investment: years to double ≈ 72 / (interest rate in percent). For example, at 6% interest, money doubles in about 72/6 = 12 years.
What is simple interest?
Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal amount, not on any accumulated interest. The formula I = Prt gives the total interest earned, where P is principal, r is rate, and t is time.
What is the difference between nominal rate and effective rate?
The nominal rate is the stated annual interest rate without considering compounding. The effective rate (APY) accounts for compounding and is always equal to or greater than the nominal rate. They are equal only with annual compounding.
If $500 is invested at 6% compounded continuously for 10 years, what is the final amount?
A = 500e^(0.06×10) = 500e^0.6 ≈ 500 × 1.82212 ≈ $911.06. Continuous compounding uses A = Pe^(rt) and gives the maximum possible growth for a given rate.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the Annual Percentage Yield (APY)?
APY is the effective annual rate of return that accounts for compounding. It is always equal to or greater than the stated (nominal) rate, and it allows for fair comparison between accounts with different compounding frequencies.
Important Concepts
Which compounding frequency yields the most interest
More frequent compounding yields slightly more interest because interest is calculated on accumulated interest more often. Daily (n = 365) compounds more often than quarterly (n = 4), semi-annually (n = 2), or annually (n = 1).
What does 'compounding frequency' mean
Compounding frequency is how many times per year the interest is computed and added to the account balance. Common frequencies include annually (1), semi-annually (2), quarterly (4), monthly (12), and daily (365).

--- Lesson 5.6: Exponential Equations from Tables & Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential Equations from Tables & Graphs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the y-intercept of an exponential function?
The y-intercept is the point where the graph crosses the y-axis, occurring at x = 0. For y = a · bˣ, when x = 0, y = a · 1 = a. So the y-intercept is the point (0, a).
What is the growth/decay factor in an exponential equation?
The growth/decay factor is the base b in y = a · bˣ. If b > 1 it is a growth factor, and if 0 < b < 1 it is a decay factor. It represents the multiplier applied to the output for each unit increase in x.
What is interpolation?
Interpolation is estimating values that fall between the known data points. It is generally more reliable than extrapolation because you are working within the range where the model has been validated.
What is extrapolation?
Extrapolation is predicting values outside the range of the observed data. While it can be useful, it is less reliable because the real-world pattern may change beyond the data range.
What is a scatter plot in the context of exponential data?
A scatter plot displays individual data points as dots on a coordinate plane. By looking at the shape of the scatter plot, you can determine whether the data is linear (straight), exponential (curved), or follows another pattern.
What is a falling exponential curve on a graph?
A falling exponential curve shows that the function decreases as x increases, approaching but never reaching its horizontal asymptote. This occurs when 0 < b < 1 (decay factor), creating a curve that drops steeply at first then levels off.
A scientist collects temperature data as a substance cools: at time 0 it is 200°C, at time 1 it is 160°C, at time 2 it is 128°C. What is the exponential equation?
a = 200 (value at x = 0). b = 160/200 = 0.8. Verify: 200(0.8)² = 200 × 0.64 = 128, which matches the data point at x = 2. The linear model y = 200 − 40x would give 120 at x = 2, which does not match.
A graph passes through (0, 50) and curves upward through (2, 200). What is the exponential equation?
a = 50 (from x = 0). Then 50 · b² = 200, so b² = 4, b = 2. The equation is y = 50(2)ˣ. Verify: 50(2)² = 50 · 4 = 200.
A table shows x: 0, 1, 2, 3 and y: 1000, 800, 640, 512. A store manager uses this to model inventory decay. What is the equation?

--- Lesson 5.7: Transformations of Exponential Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Transformations of Exponential Functions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a vertical shift of an exponential function?
A vertical shift adds or subtracts a constant k to the entire function, moving every point up (k > 0) or down (k < 0). It also moves the horizontal asymptote from y = 0 to y = k.
What is a horizontal shift of an exponential function?
A horizontal shift replaces x with (x − h), moving the graph right by h units (or left if h is negative). Unlike vertical shifts, horizontal shifts do not change the asymptote.
What is a reflection in the context of exponential transformations?
A reflection flips the graph across an axis. Negating the output (y = −bˣ) reflects over the x-axis. Negating the input (y = b^(−x)) reflects over the y-axis.
What is a vertical stretch?
A vertical stretch occurs when the function is multiplied by a factor a where |a| > 1. Every y-value is scaled by this factor, making the graph taller and narrower. For example, y = 3 · 2ˣ stretches y = 2ˣ by 3.
What is a vertical compression?
A vertical compression occurs when the function is multiplied by a factor a where 0 < |a| < 1. This squeezes the graph toward the x-axis, making all y-values closer to zero.
What is the parent function of an exponential family?
The parent function is the simplest version of a function family, before any transformations are applied. For exponentials, the parent function y = bˣ has no shifts, stretches, or reflections.
What is the effect of replacing x with −x in an exponential function?
Replacing x with −x reflects the graph over the y-axis. For exponentials, this converts y = bˣ (growth if b > 1) into y = b^(−x) = (1/b)ˣ (decay), flipping the direction of the curve.
What is the transformation form of an exponential function?
The general transformation form y = a · b^(x−h) + k captures all possible transformations: a controls vertical stretch/compression/reflection, h controls horizontal shift, and k controls vertical shift and asymptote.
A heating system turns on at 6 AM (x = 0) and the temperature model is y = 68 − 20(0.5)ˣ. What is the room temperature as time goes on (the asymptote)?
As x → ∞, (0.5)ˣ → 0, so y → 68 − 0 = 68. The asymptote is y = 68, meaning the temperature approaches 68°F. At x = 0, the temperature is 68 − 20 = 48°F.
A medicine's effectiveness is modeled by y = −100(0.8)ˣ + 100, where y is the percent of symptoms relieved. What is the maximum relief possible?
As x → ∞, (0.8)ˣ → 0, so y → −100(0) + 100 = 100. The asymptote is y = 100, meaning the medicine approaches but never quite reaches 100% relief. At x = 0, relief is 0%.
An investment starts at $2,000 and doubles every year: y = 2000 · 2ˣ. If you graph profit instead (subtract the initial investment), the equation becomes y = 2000 · 2ˣ − 2000. What is the new asymptote?
In y = 2000 · 2ˣ − 2000, the vertical shift k = −2000 determines the asymptote: y = −2000. As x → −∞, 2ˣ → 0, so y → −2000. At x = 0, profit = 2000 − 2000 = 0.
A student transforms y = 3ˣ by reflecting it over the x-axis and shifting up 5. What is the new equation, and what is the y-intercept?
Reflecting over the x-axis: y = −3ˣ. Shifting up 5: y = −3ˣ + 5. At x = 0: y = −1 + 5 = 4. The asymptote is y = 5.
A cooling model is given by T = 25 + 75(0.9)ˣ, where T is temperature in °F and x is minutes. What is the initial temperature and what does the room temperature appear to be?
At x = 0: T = 25 + 75(1) = 100°F. As x → ∞, (0.9)ˣ → 0, so T → 25°F. The asymptote y = 25 represents the room temperature the object approaches.
Real-World Applications
Example: A marketing model predicts sales as y = 500 − 500(0.7)ˣ, where x is the number of ad campaigns. What are the initial sales (x=0) and the maximum sales the model predicts?
At x = 0: y = 500 − 500(1) = 0. As x → ∞, (0.7)ˣ → 0, so y → 500 − 0 = 500. The model starts at 0 and approaches the asymptote of 500 sales.

--- Lesson 5.8: Solving Exponential Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solving Exponential Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the one-to-one property of exponential functions?
The one-to-one property states that if the bases are equal and positive (not 1), then the exponents must be equal. This is the foundation for solving exponential equations by matching bases.
What is a logarithm?
A logarithm is the inverse of exponentiation. log_b(x) = y means b^y = x. It is used to solve exponential equations when the bases cannot easily be made equal.
What is the power rule of exponents?
The power rule states that when you raise a power to another power, you multiply the exponents: (bᵐ)ⁿ = bᵐⁿ. This is essential for rewriting expressions with the same base, like (3²)ˣ = 3²ˣ.
What is the product rule of exponents?
The product rule states that when multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents: bᵐ · bⁿ = bᵐ⁺ⁿ. This is useful for simplifying expressions before solving.
What is the quotient rule of exponents?
The quotient rule states that when dividing powers with the same base, subtract the exponents: bᵐ / bⁿ = bᵐ⁻ⁿ. For example, 2⁵ / 2² = 2³ = 8.

--- Lesson 5.9: Interpreting Exponential Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interpreting Exponential Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the domain of a real-world exponential model for time?
In real-world models where x represents time, the domain is typically restricted to t ≥ 0 because negative time usually has no meaning. The mathematical function exists for all real numbers, but the practical domain is limited.
What is the doubling time?
Doubling time is how long it takes for a quantity experiencing exponential growth to become twice as large. It depends on the growth rate and can be estimated using the Rule of 72: doubling time ≈ 72/rate.
What is the practical range of a decay model y = a · bˣ (where a > 0)?
For a decay model with a > 0 and 0 < b < 1, the output starts at a (when x = 0) and decreases toward 0 as x increases. The practical range for x ≥ 0 is 0 < y ≤ a.
What is the relationship between growth factor and growth rate?
The growth factor b = 1 + r, where r is the growth rate as a decimal. For growth, b > 1 and r > 0. For decay, the decay factor b = 1 − r, where r is the decay rate.
A town's population is modeled by P = 12000(1.04)ᵗ. Interpret this model: what is the population now and how fast is it growing?
The initial value a = 12,000 is the current population. The base 1.04 means b − 1 = 0.04 = 4% growth rate per year. This is percentage-based growth, not a fixed number.
A radioactive sample is modeled by R = 500(0.5)^(t/3), where t is in years. What is the half-life and how much remains after 9 years?
The exponent t/3 means one half-life is 3 years (when t = 3, exponent = 1, and amount halves). After 9 years: 500(0.5)³ = 500 × 0.125 = 62.5 grams (three half-lives).
A savings account is described by A = 10000(1.005)^(12t). What is the annual interest rate and how often is it compounded?
The base 1.005 = 1 + 0.005 means the monthly rate is 0.5%. Since there are 12 compounding periods per year (from 12t), the annual rate is 0.5% × 12 = 6%, compounded monthly.
Real-World Applications
Example: A real estate agent says a house valued at $300,000 appreciates at 4% annually. The model is V = 300000(1.04)ᵗ. What will the house be worth in 5 years?
V = 300000(1.04)⁵ = 300000 × 1.21665 ≈ $364,996. Simple 4% × 5 = 20% would give $360,000, but compound growth gives slightly more due to the compounding effect.

--- Lesson 5.10: Exponential Regression (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Exponential Regression
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is regression?
Regression is a statistical technique that determines the equation of the curve (or line) that best fits a given set of data points. It minimizes the differences between predicted and actual values.
What is the coefficient of determination (R²)?
R² measures the proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is explained by the model. An R² of 1 means every data point lies exactly on the curve; an R² of 0 means the model has no predictive power.
What is a residual?
A residual = actual value − predicted value. It measures how far off the model's prediction is for each data point. Ideally, residuals should be small and randomly scattered.
What is a residual plot?
A residual plot graphs the residuals against x-values. If the residuals are randomly scattered around zero, the model is a good fit. If they show a pattern (curve, trend), a different model may be needed.
What is the correlation coefficient (r)?
The correlation coefficient r ranges from −1 to 1. Values near ±1 indicate strong relationships; values near 0 indicate weak ones. For exponential growth, r is positive; for decay, it depends on the linearized data.
What is overfitting in regression?
Overfitting occurs when a model is too complex and captures random noise in the data rather than the underlying trend. An overfit model performs well on existing data but poorly on new data.
A real estate company tracks home prices over 10 years. The exponential regression gives P = 250000(1.035)ᵗ with R² = 0.94. What does this model predict about homes and what is the estimated price in year 15?
Growth rate = 1.035 − 1 = 0.035 = 3.5% per year. Year 15: 250000(1.035)¹⁵ ≈ 250000 × 1.6772 ≈ $419,295. Note: predicting 5 years beyond the data (year 15) involves some extrapolation risk.
Real-World Applications
Example: An ice cream shop tracks daily sales over 8 summer weeks. Exponential regression gives S = 120(1.08)ˣ with R² = 0.85. If R² is below 0.90, what should the shop owner consider?
R² = 0.85 means 15% of the variation is unexplained, suggesting the model may miss important factors. The owner should try other models (linear, quadratic) or consider variables like weather or promotions that affect sales.

    Explanation: 1000(1.25)ᵗ = 100000 → (1.25)ᵗ = 100. Using estimation: (1.25)²⁰ ≈ 86.7 and (1.25)²¹ ≈ 108.4. So the model predicts 100,000 users at approximately month 21, though real growth may slow before then.

  Q30. A student runs exponential regression on their data and gets y = 50(0.95)ˣ with R² = 0.99. Another student uses linear regression on the same data and gets y = −2.5x + 50 with R² = 0.97. Which model is better and why?
    [ ] Linear, because the R² difference is small
    [ ] They are identical in quality
    [ ] Linear, because it is simpler to understand
    [*] Exponential, because R² is higher and the residual pattern matters more than simplicity
    Explanation: The exponential model has R² = 0.99 vs 0.97 for linear, and for data that truly decays exponentially, the linear model will have systematic errors for extreme x-values. Also, the exponential model never predicts negative values, which is often more realistic.

--- Lesson 5.10: Exponential Regression (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: What is exponential regression?
     A: Using technology to find the best-fit equation y = a · bˣ for data

  2. Q: What does r² close to 1 indicate?
     A: A strong fit for the model

  3. Q: What tool is used for exponential regression?
     A: A graphing calculator or software (ExpReg)

  4. Q: What should you be cautious about?
     A: Extrapolation beyond the range of the data

  5. Q: When is exponential regression appropriate?
     A: When the data shows a multiplicative pattern

  6. Q: What does the 'a' value represent in the regression model?
     A: The estimated initial value

  7. Q: If r² = 0.98, is the model good?
     A: Yes — 0.98 is close to 1, indicating a strong fit

  8. Q: How do you evaluate if exponential is the right model?
     A: Check if the scatter plot shows a curve and ratios are roughly constant

  9. Q: What should you check before using exponential regression?
     A: That the data shows a multiplicative (curved) pattern, not linear

  10. Q: How is r² different from r?
     A: r² measures goodness of fit (0 to 1); r measures correlation strength and direction (−1 to 1)
