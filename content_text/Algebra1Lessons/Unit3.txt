--- Lesson 3.1: Scatter Plots &amp; Correlation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scatter Plots &amp; Correlation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In a scatter plot showing height vs. shoe size, most points trend upward. One point is far away from the rest. What is this distant point called?
A data point that is far away from the general trend or pattern of the other points in a scatter plot is called an outlier.
What is a scatter plot?
A scatter plot is a type of graph that displays paired numerical data as individual points on a coordinate plane, with one variable on each axis.
What is correlation?
Correlation is a statistical measure that describes both the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables, without implying causation.
What is the correlation coefficient (r)?
The correlation coefficient r is a numerical value ranging from -1 to 1 that quantifies how strongly and in what direction two variables are linearly related.
What is positive correlation?
Positive correlation occurs when both variables tend to increase together, meaning as one variable goes up, the other also goes up, producing an upward trend on a scatter plot.
What is negative correlation?
Negative correlation means that as one variable increases, the other tends to decrease, producing a downward trend from left to right on a scatter plot.
What is an outlier in a scatter plot?
An outlier in a scatter plot is a data point that falls far from the general trend or pattern formed by the other data points, and it can significantly affect the correlation.
What is a cluster in a scatter plot?
A cluster is a group of data points in a scatter plot that are closely gathered together, often suggesting a subgroup within the data that shares similar characteristics.
What is the independent variable in a scatter plot?
The independent variable is plotted on the x-axis and is the variable that is thought to influence or predict the other variable (the dependent variable on the y-axis).
Outliers and Resistant Measures
An outlier in a scatter plot is a data point that falls far from the general trend or pattern formed by the other data points, and it can significantly affect the correlation.
Following the pattern, 8 absences would predict a grade around 64-68. Scoring 75 is noticeably above the trend, making this point an outlier that deviates from the strong negative linear pattern.
Real-World Applications
Example: On a scatter plot, the x-axis represents hours studied and the y-axis represents test scores. A student studied 5 hours and scored 88. What ordered pair represents this point?
In a scatter plot, points are written as (x, y). Since hours studied is on the x-axis and test score is on the y-axis, the point is (5, 88).
Example: A scatter plot of age vs. reaction time shows points that fall from left to right. What type of association is this?
Points that fall from left to right indicate that as one variable increases, the other decreases, which is a negative linear association.
Example: A marketing team records advertising spending (in hundreds of dollars) and monthly sales (in thousands of dollars): (2, 15), (4, 22), (6, 28), (8, 35), (10, 40). The correlation coefficient is r = 0.99. What does this tell the team?
An r value of 0.99 is extremely close to 1, indicating a near-perfect positive linear relationship where increases in advertising spending are very strongly associated with increases in monthly sales.
Example: A teacher records students' absences and final grades: (0, 95), (2, 88), (4, 80), (6, 72), (10, 55), (12, 48). She estimates r is approximately -0.98. A student who was absent 8 days scored 75. Is this student's point an outlier?
Following the pattern, 8 absences would predict a grade around 64-68. Scoring 75 is noticeably above the trend, making this point an outlier that deviates from the strong negative linear pattern.
Example: A car dealership tracks the age of used cars (in years) and their selling prices (in thousands): (1, 28), (2, 24), (3, 22), (5, 16), (7, 10), (10, 5). What type of correlation exists, and approximately what would a 4-year-old car sell for?
As car age increases, price decreases, showing negative correlation. Between the 3-year ($22k) and 5-year ($16k) data points, a 4-year-old car would be approximately $19,000.
Example: A biologist measures the average daily temperature (F) and the number of cricket chirps per minute over several nights: (55, 65), (60, 82), (65, 100), (70, 115), (75, 132), (80, 148). The correlation is strong and positive. Based on the trend, approximately how many chirps per minute would occur at 85 degrees?
The chirps increase by roughly 16-18 per 5-degree increase. From 148 chirps at 80 degrees, adding approximately 16-17 gives about 165 chirps at 85 degrees.
Example: A school counselor surveys students on hours of sleep and GPA: (5, 2.1), (6, 2.5), (7, 3.0), (8, 3.4), (9, 3.7), (10, 3.9). She calculates r = 0.98. A new student sleeps 4 hours and has a 3.8 GPA. How does this affect the correlation?
The trend shows low sleep = low GPA. A student with only 4 hours of sleep but a 3.8 GPA strongly deviates from this pattern, so adding this outlier would pull r closer to 0 (decrease the strength).
Example: A retail analyst tracks the number of employees working and the average customer wait time (minutes) for a store: (2, 18), (3, 14), (4, 10), (5, 7), (6, 5), (8, 3). The store manager has a budget for either 7 employees or 9 employees. Based on the trend, what wait time difference would the manager expect between these two staffing levels?
The trend shows diminishing returns: from 6 employees (5 min) to 8 employees (3 min) is a drop of 2 minutes over 2 employees. At 7 employees, wait time would be about 4 minutes, and at 9 employees about 2 minutes, giving roughly a 2-minute difference.

--- Lesson 3.2: Line of Best Fit (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Line of Best Fit
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A scatter plot has the line of best fit y = 2x + 5. What is the predicted y-value when x = 4?
Substituting x = 4 into y = 2x + 5 gives y = 2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13.
The line of best fit for a data set is y = -3x + 20. What is the slope and what does it mean?
In y = -3x + 20, the slope is -3, which means for every 1-unit increase in x, the predicted y-value decreases by 3 units.
A line of best fit passes through the points (2, 10) and (6, 22). What is the slope of this line?
Slope = (22 - 10)/(6 - 2) = 12/4 = 3.
The line of best fit is y = 0.5x + 12. The actual data point at x = 10 has y = 18. What is the residual for this point?
Predicted value: y = 0.5(10) + 12 = 17. Residual = actual - predicted = 18 - 17 = 1.
Using the line y = 4x + 3, what is the y-intercept and what does it represent?
In the equation y = 4x + 3, the y-intercept is 3, which represents the predicted value of y when x is 0.
What is a line of best fit?
A line of best fit (also called a trend line or regression line) is a straight line drawn through a scatter plot that best represents the overall trend by minimizing the distances between the line and the data points.
What is linear regression?
Linear regression is a statistical method that calculates the line of best fit by minimizing the sum of the squared differences (residuals) between the actual data points and the predicted values on the line.
What is interpolation?
Interpolation is the process of using a line of best fit to predict a value that falls within the range of the observed data, which is generally more reliable than predicting outside the range.
What is extrapolation?
Extrapolation uses the line of best fit to predict values beyond the range of the observed data, which is less reliable because the linear trend may not continue outside the data range.
What is a trend line?
A trend line is another name for a line of best fit. It is drawn through a scatter plot to show the general direction and pattern of the data.
What is the least-squares regression line?
The least-squares regression line is the specific line of best fit that minimizes the sum of the squared residuals (vertical distances between each actual data point and its predicted value on the line).
What is the coefficient of determination (r-squared)?
The coefficient of determination (r-squared) is the square of the correlation coefficient and represents the proportion (or percentage) of the variance in the dependent variable that can be explained by the independent variable.
A car rental company finds that the line of best fit for rental days (x) and total cost in dollars (y) is y = 45x + 30. A customer rents a car for 5 days and pays $260. What is the residual for this customer?
Predicted cost: y = 45(5) + 30 = 225 + 30 = $255. Residual = actual - predicted = $260 - $255 = $5.
A fitness trainer records weeks of training (x) and the number of push-ups completed (y) for a client: (1, 12), (2, 18), (3, 22), (4, 28), (5, 33), (6, 38). The line of best fit is approximately y = 5.1x + 7.2. After 8 weeks, the client does 52 push-ups. What is the residual?
Predicted value at x = 8: y = 5.1(8) + 7.2 = 40.8 + 7.2 = 48.0. Residual = actual - predicted = 52 - 48 = 4.0.
A meteorologist uses the line of best fit y = -0.8x + 75 to model the relationship between altitude (in hundreds of feet, x) and temperature (F, y). A hiker at 5000 feet (x = 50) records a temperature of 32 degrees. What is the residual, and what does a negative residual here mean?
Predicted: y = -0.8(50) + 75 = -40 + 75 = 35 degrees. Residual = actual - predicted = 32 - 35 = -3. A negative residual means the actual temperature was 3 degrees lower than the model predicted.
A teacher uses the model y = 8x + 30 to predict final exam scores (y) based on homework completion rate (x, on a 0-10 scale). Two students with a rate of 7 each got scores of 86 and 90. What is the average residual for these two students?
At x = 7: predicted y = 8(7) + 30 = 56 + 30 = 86. Residual for student 1: 86 - 86 = 0. Residual for student 2: 90 - 86 = 4. Average residual: (0 + 4)/2 = 2.
Real-World Applications
Example: A line of best fit for data on study hours (x) and exam scores (y) is y = 5x + 50. What score does the model predict for someone who studies 0 hours?
Substituting x = 0 into y = 5(0) + 50 gives y = 50. The model predicts a score of 50 for zero study hours, which is the y-intercept.
Example: The equation y = -1.5x + 100 models the relationship between temperature (x, in degrees) and hot chocolate sales (y). How many sales are predicted at 40 degrees?
Substituting x = 40: y = -1.5(40) + 100 = -60 + 100 = 40 sales predicted.
Example: A coffee shop owner tracks the outside temperature (F) and daily iced coffee sales over 8 days. The line of best fit is y = 3.2x - 120, where x is temperature and y is number of iced coffees sold. On a day when the temperature is 85 degrees, how many iced coffees does the model predict will be sold?
Substituting x = 85: y = 3.2(85) - 120 = 272 - 120 = 152 iced coffees predicted.
Example: A school psychologist models the relationship between hours of screen time per day (x) and a concentration score (y) using y = -4x + 50. The data was collected for students with 1 to 6 hours of screen time. A parent asks about a child with 10 hours of screen time. What should the psychologist explain?
Since the data was collected for 1 to 6 hours, predicting at 10 hours is extrapolation (going beyond the observed range). While y = -4(10) + 50 = 10, this prediction is unreliable because the linear trend may not hold at extreme values.
Example: A real estate agent finds that the line of best fit for house size (in hundreds of sq ft, x) and selling price (in thousands of dollars, y) is y = 15x + 50. The r-squared value is 0.89. What percentage of the variation in selling price is explained by house size?
The r-squared value of 0.89 means that 89% of the variation in selling price can be explained by the house size. The remaining 11% is due to other factors.
Example: A tutoring center tracks the number of sessions attended (x) and improvement in test scores (y). The line of best fit is y = 3.5x + 2. A student who attended 10 sessions improved by 40 points. The center claims their average improvement matches the model. Is this correct?
Predicted improvement: y = 3.5(10) + 2 = 35 + 2 = 37 points. The actual improvement of 40 is close (residual of 3), so the claim is approximately correct.
Example: A teacher uses the model y = 8x + 30 to predict final exam scores (y) based on homework completion rate (x, on a 0-10 scale). Two students with a rate of 7 each got scores of 86 and 90. What is the average residual for these two students?
At x = 7: predicted y = 8(7) + 30 = 56 + 30 = 86. Residual for student 1: 86 - 86 = 0. Residual for student 2: 90 - 86 = 4. Average residual: (0 + 4)/2 = 2.

--- Lesson 3.3: Residuals &amp; Residual Plots (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Residuals &amp; Residual Plots
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
The line of best fit predicts y = 25, but the actual value is 28. What is the residual?
Residual = actual - predicted = 28 - 25 = 3. A positive residual means the actual value is above the predicted value.
A data set has residuals: -2, 1, -1, 3, -1. What is the sum of these residuals?
Sum = -2 + 1 + (-1) + 3 + (-1) = 0. For a least-squares regression line, the sum of the residuals is always approximately zero.
What is a residual?
A residual is the difference between an actual observed value and the corresponding predicted value from the regression model, calculated as residual = actual - predicted.
What is a residual plot?
A residual plot is a graph where the residuals are plotted on the y-axis against either the independent variable or predicted values on the x-axis, used to assess how well a model fits the data.
What is the formula for calculating a residual?
The residual is calculated as the actual observed value minus the predicted value from the model: residual = actual - predicted.
What is the purpose of creating a residual plot?
The primary purpose of a residual plot is to evaluate whether a linear model is a good fit for the data by checking whether the residuals show random scatter or a systematic pattern.
An online retailer models shipping time (days, y) based on distance (hundreds of miles, x) with y = 0.3x + 1. For orders shipped 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25 hundred miles, actual delivery times were 2.5, 4.2, 5.3, 7.1, and 8.6 days. What is the largest absolute residual?
Predicted: 0.3(5)+1=2.5, 0.3(10)+1=4, 0.3(15)+1=5.5, 0.3(20)+1=7, 0.3(25)+1=8.5. Residuals: 2.5-2.5=0, 4.2-4=0.2, 5.3-5.5=-0.2, 7.1-7=0.1, 8.6-8.5=0.1. The largest absolute residual is 0.2.
A city planner models the relationship between distance from downtown (miles, x) and average rent (hundreds of dollars, y) using a least-squares line. She gets residuals of 1.2, -0.8, 0.3, -1.5, and 0.8 for five data points. What is the sum of these residuals, and is this consistent with a properly fitted least-squares line?
Sum = 1.2 + (-0.8) + 0.3 + (-1.5) + 0.8 = 0. For a least-squares regression line, the sum of all residuals is always zero, so this is consistent with a properly fitted model.

--- Lesson 3.4: Correlation vs. Causation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Correlation vs. Causation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A researcher finds a strong positive correlation between shoe size and reading ability in children. What is the most likely explanation?
Age is a lurking variable. As children get older, both their feet and their reading skills develop. The correlation between shoe size and reading is not causal but is driven by the confounding variable of age.
A study shows that cities with more police officers have higher crime rates. A politician claims that hiring police causes crime. What is wrong with this claim?
This is an example of reverse causation. Cities with higher crime rates hire more police to address the problem, not the other way around. The politician confused the direction of the causal relationship.
What is causation?
Causation (or a causal relationship) exists when a change in one variable directly brings about or produces a change in another variable, not merely an association between them.
What is a lurking variable?
A lurking variable (also called a confounding variable) is a variable not included in the analysis that affects both variables being studied, potentially creating a misleading appearance of a direct relationship between them.
What is a confounding variable?
A confounding variable is an extraneous variable that correlates with both the independent and dependent variables, making it hard to determine whether the observed relationship is truly causal or driven by the confounder.
What is a spurious correlation?
A spurious correlation is a statistical relationship between two variables that appears to be meaningful but is actually due to coincidence, a confounding variable, or some other factor unrelated to a true causal link.
What is an observational study?
An observational study involves collecting data by observing subjects without intervening or manipulating variables. It can identify correlations but generally cannot establish causation.
What is reverse causation?
Reverse causation occurs when the presumed direction of a causal relationship is backwards. What is thought to be the cause is actually the effect, and what is thought to be the effect is actually the cause.
What is a controlled experiment?
A controlled experiment compares outcomes between a treatment group (receiving the intervention) and a control group (not receiving it), while other variables are held constant to isolate the effect of the treatment.
What is a placebo in an experiment?
A placebo is an inactive treatment (such as a sugar pill) given to the control group in an experiment. It looks identical to the real treatment, helping researchers determine whether observed effects are due to the actual treatment or psychological factors.
A school district notices that schools with larger libraries have higher average test scores. The superintendent proposes spending $5 million to expand all school libraries. A data analyst warns this may not improve scores. What is the analyst's likely reasoning?
The analyst likely recognizes that school district wealth is a confounding variable. Wealthier districts can afford both larger libraries and other educational resources (better teachers, technology, etc.) that actually drive higher test scores.
A researcher finds that countries that consume more chocolate per capita win more Nobel Prizes. She wants to publish a paper claiming chocolate consumption boosts intelligence. What is wrong with this conclusion?
Wealthier nations can afford both more luxury goods like chocolate and better education systems that produce Nobel laureates. National wealth and educational investment are confounding variables that explain the correlation without a causal link between chocolate and intelligence.
A fitness app company finds that users who log meals daily lose more weight than those who do not. They claim their meal logging feature causes weight loss. A researcher points out a concern. What is it?
People who bother to log every meal are likely already more committed to weight loss. Motivation and discipline are confounding variables. Without random assignment, we cannot conclude the logging feature itself causes weight loss.
Real-World Applications
Example: A study finds that ice cream sales and drowning incidents both increase in the summer. Does ice cream cause drowning?
This is a classic example of a lurking (confounding) variable. Hot weather causes both more ice cream consumption and more swimming, leading to more drownings. Correlation does not imply causation.
Example: There is a strong correlation between the number of Nicolas Cage movies released in a year and the number of drownings in swimming pools. What does this demonstrate?
This is a famous example of a spurious correlation, where two completely unrelated variables appear correlated purely by coincidence. It powerfully illustrates that correlation does not imply causation.
Example: A school district notices that schools with larger libraries have higher average test scores. The superintendent proposes spending $5 million to expand all school libraries. A data analyst warns this may not improve scores. What is the analyst's likely reasoning?
The analyst likely recognizes that school district wealth is a confounding variable. Wealthier districts can afford both larger libraries and other educational resources (better teachers, technology, etc.) that actually drive higher test scores.
Example: A city finds a positive correlation between the number of fire trucks sent to a fire and the dollar amount of damage. A citizen argues that sending fewer fire trucks would reduce damage. Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
Fire severity is the confounding variable. Larger, more severe fires cause both more damage and require more fire trucks. Sending fewer trucks would not reduce damage; it would likely increase it.
Example: A university study randomly assigns first-year students to either attend weekly study groups or study independently. After one semester, the study group students have GPAs 0.3 points higher on average. Can the university conclude that study groups cause higher GPAs?
Because students were randomly assigned to groups, confounding variables (like prior academic ability or motivation) are balanced across both groups. This controlled experimental design allows the university to conclude that study groups caused the GPA improvement.

Key Definitions
In a survey of 200 students, 80 prefer basketball. What is the relative frequency of students who prefer basketball?
Relative frequency = frequency / total = 80/200 = 0.40 = 40%.
A joint relative frequency in a two-way table is 0.15. If the total number of observations is 400, what is the joint frequency?
Joint frequency = joint relative frequency x total = 0.15 x 400 = 60.
A class of 50 students is surveyed. 15 are male and ride the bus. What is the joint relative frequency of male bus riders?
Joint relative frequency = joint frequency / grand total = 15/50 = 0.30 or 30%.
In a two-way table, 60 out of 100 males prefer action movies. What is the conditional relative frequency of preferring action movies given that the person is male?
Conditional relative frequency = joint frequency / marginal frequency of the condition = 60/100 = 0.60 or 60% of males prefer action movies.
A two-way table has a joint relative frequency of 0.12 for 'Male and Plays Soccer.' The marginal relative frequency for 'Male' is 0.48. What is the conditional relative frequency of playing soccer given male?

What is relative frequency?
Relative frequency is the ratio of a particular frequency to the total number of observations. It expresses what proportion or percentage of the total a particular category represents.
What is a joint relative frequency?
A joint relative frequency is found by dividing the frequency in a specific cell (the intersection of two categories) by the grand total of all observations in the table.
What is a marginal relative frequency?
A marginal relative frequency is the ratio of a row total or column total to the grand total, representing the proportion of observations that fall into that single category regardless of the other variable.
What is a conditional relative frequency?
A conditional relative frequency is found by dividing a joint frequency by a marginal frequency (row or column total), showing the proportion of one category within a specific subgroup of the other variable.
What is the difference between a frequency table and a relative frequency table?
A frequency table displays raw counts of observations, while a relative frequency table converts those counts to proportions or percentages by dividing each frequency by the total number of observations.
What is the difference between row conditional relative frequencies and column conditional relative frequencies?
Row conditional relative frequencies divide each cell by its row total (conditioning on the row variable), while column conditional relative frequencies divide each cell by its column total (conditioning on the column variable).

--- Lesson 3.7: Association in Categorical Data (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Association in Categorical Data
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is association in the context of categorical data?
Association between categorical variables exists when the distribution (or conditional relative frequencies) of one variable changes depending on which category of the other variable you examine.
What is a conditional distribution?
A conditional distribution shows how one variable is distributed within a particular category of another variable. For example, the distribution of ice cream preferences among females only.
What is a segmented bar graph?
A segmented bar graph (or stacked bar graph) divides each bar into segments that represent the proportions of a second categorical variable. It is useful for visually comparing conditional distributions.
What is the difference between association and independence in categorical data?
Association means that the conditional distributions of one variable differ across categories of the other, indicating a relationship. Independence means the conditional distributions are the same, indicating no relationship.
What is the role of sample size when determining association?
With small sample sizes, observed differences in conditional frequencies may be due to random chance. Larger samples provide more reliable evidence, making it easier to distinguish a real association from random variation.
Real-World Applications
Example: A survey shows that 40% of children, 60% of teenagers, and 55% of adults prefer chocolate ice cream. Is there an association between age group and ice cream preference?
Because the conditional relative frequencies differ across age groups (40%, 60%, 55%), there appears to be an association between age group and ice cream preference.
Example: A marketing agency surveys 800 consumers about shopping channel (Online, In-Store) and age group (18-35, 36-55, 56+). 18-35: Online=180, In-Store=70. 36-55: Online=150, In-Store=150. 56+: Online=50, In-Store=200. Create a segmented bar chart mentally and determine which age group has the strongest preference for in-store shopping.
18-35: In-Store = 70/250 = 28%. 36-55: In-Store = 150/300 = 50%. 56+: In-Store = 200/250 = 80%. The 56+ age group has the strongest preference for in-store shopping at 80%.
Example: A school surveys 400 students about after-school activity (Sports, Clubs, None) and academic performance (Above Average, Average, Below Average). Sports: Above=60, Avg=40, Below=20. Clubs: Above=70, Avg=50, Below=10. None: Above=30, Avg=60, Below=60. Is there an association between participation and academic performance?
Sports Above Avg: 60/120 = 50%. Clubs Above Avg: 70/130 = 53.8%. None Above Avg: 30/150 = 20%. The large differences in conditional distributions show a clear association between participation and academic performance.
Example: A university examines the relationship between major (Science, Arts, Business) and employment within 6 months of graduation (Employed, Not Employed). Science: Employed=180, Not=20. Arts: Employed=100, Not=50. Business: Employed=160, Not=40. A parent argues that major choice does not affect employment. Does the data support this?
Science: 180/200 = 90%. Arts: 100/150 = 66.7%. Business: 160/200 = 80%. The different employment rates across majors show an association. The parent's claim is not supported by the data.
Important Concepts
A health clinic surveys 500 patients about exercise frequency (Regular, Occasional, None) and flu occurrence last year (Yes, No). Regular: Yes=15, No=185. Occasional: Yes=30, No=120. None: Yes=45, No=105. Is there evidence of an association between exercise and flu
Regular: 15/200 = 7.5% flu. Occasional: 30/150 = 20% flu. None: 45/150 = 30% flu. The conditional flu rates differ substantially across exercise groups, showing a clear association.

    Explanation: When there is no association, the conditional distributions are the same for each category, so the segments within each bar have approximately the same proportions regardless of which bar you look at.

    [ ] Association applies to numerical data; independence applies to categorical data
    Explanation: Association means that the conditional distributions of one variable differ across categories of the other, indicating a relationship. Independence means the conditional distributions are the same, indicating no relationship.

  Q23. A college admissions office tracks acceptance by gender (Male, Female) and major (STEM, Non-STEM). Overall, 40% of males and 30% of females are accepted. --- Lesson 3.7: Association in Categorical Data (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: When are two categorical variables associated?
     A: When the conditional distributions differ across categories

  2. Q: If conditional relative frequencies are approximately equal across groups, there is:
     A: No association

  3. Q: How do you check for association?
     A: Compare conditional relative frequencies across groups

  4. Q: Does association imply causation?
     A: No — association is not the same as causation

  5. Q: What is a mosaic plot?
     A: A visual display of association between two categorical variables

  6. Q: What would no association look like in a table?
     A: The conditional distributions are approximately the same for all groups

  7. Q: What would association look like?
     A: Noticeable differences in conditional distributions across groups

  8. Q: How can you establish causation from an observed association?
     A: Through a controlled experiment

  9. Q: What is a segmented bar graph?
     A: A bar graph where each bar is divided into segments showing conditional relative frequencies

  10. Q: How do you determine the strength of an association?
     A: By how different the conditional distributions are across groups — larger differences = stronger association
