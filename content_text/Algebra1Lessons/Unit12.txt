--- Lesson 12.1: Sample Spaces & Basic Probability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sample Spaces & Basic Probability
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the sample space for rolling a standard die?
A standard die has six faces numbered 1 through 6, so the sample space is {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.
What is a sample space?
A sample space is the complete set of all possible outcomes of a probability experiment. It is usually denoted by S and must include every possible result.
What is probability?
Probability is a numerical measure of how likely an event is to occur. It is calculated as the ratio of favorable outcomes to total outcomes and always falls between 0 and 1.
What is an event in probability?
An event is a specific outcome or set of outcomes from the sample space that we are interested in. For example, when rolling a die, 'rolling an even number' is the event {2, 4, 6}.
What is the complement of an event?
The complement of event E (written E' or not E) contains all outcomes not in E. The probability of the complement is P(not E) = 1 − P(E).
What is an outcome?
An outcome is a single possible result of a probability experiment. For example, when flipping a coin, 'Heads' is one outcome. The sample space lists all possible outcomes.
What is theoretical probability?
Theoretical probability is calculated by reasoning about the equally likely outcomes: P(E) = (number of favorable outcomes) / (total number of outcomes). No experiment needs to be performed.
What is experimental probability?
Experimental probability is determined by conducting an experiment and calculating the ratio of times the event occurred to the total number of trials. It may differ from theoretical probability.
What is a favorable outcome?
A favorable outcome is any outcome that matches the event of interest. For example, if the event is 'rolling an even number,' then 2, 4, and 6 are the favorable outcomes.
What is the complement rule?
The complement rule states that the probability of an event not occurring equals 1 minus the probability of it occurring: P(not E) = 1 − P(E). This is useful when it is easier to find what you do not want.
A weather forecast says there is a 65% chance of sunshine. If you are planning an outdoor event, what is the probability it will not be sunny?
Using the complement rule: P(not sunny) = 1 − 0.65 = 0.35 or 35%.
A jar contains 4 red, 6 blue, and 10 white jelly beans. A child picks one at random. What is the probability of picking a red jelly bean?
Total jelly beans = 4 + 6 + 10 = 20. P(red) = 4/20 = 1/5.
A class has 15 boys and 12 girls. If a student is chosen at random to present, what is the probability a girl is chosen?
Total students = 15 + 12 = 27. P(girl) = 12/27 = 4/9.
A lottery has 1,000 tickets and you buy 5. What is the probability you win?
P(win) = favorable/total = 5/1000 = 1/200.
A deck of 52 cards is shuffled. What is the probability of drawing a heart?
There are 13 hearts in a 52-card deck. P(heart) = 13/52 = 1/4.
A basketball player makes 72 out of 90 free throws. What is the experimental probability of making the next free throw?
Experimental probability = successes/trials = 72/90 = 4/5 or 80%.
A quality inspector finds 3 defective items in a batch of 200. What is the probability that the next item is defective?
Based on experimental data, P(defective) = 3/200 = 0.015 or 1.5%.
A traffic light is green for 30 seconds, yellow for 5 seconds, and red for 25 seconds in each cycle. What is the probability a car arrives during a green light?
Total cycle = 30 + 5 + 25 = 60 seconds. P(green) = 30/60 = 1/2.
A phone company surveyed 500 customers and found 350 were satisfied. What is the probability that a randomly chosen customer is satisfied?
P(satisfied) = 350/500 = 7/10 = 0.7 or 70%.

--- Lesson 12.2: Independent & Dependent Events (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Independent & Dependent Events
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the multiplication rule for probability?
The multiplication rule finds the probability of both events occurring. For independent events use P(A) × P(B); for dependent events use P(A) × P(B|A).
What is conditional probability?
Conditional probability, written P(B|A), is the probability of event B occurring given that event A has already occurred. It is essential for calculating probabilities of dependent events.
What is a compound event?
A compound event consists of two or more simple events. Examples include flipping a coin AND rolling a die, or drawing two cards in succession.
What is a simple event?
A simple event is an event consisting of a single outcome. For example, rolling a 3 on a die is a simple event. Simple events are the building blocks of compound events.
What is the counting principle (fundamental counting principle)?
The fundamental counting principle states that if one event can occur in m ways and a second event can occur in n ways, the total number of ways both events can occur together is m × n.
You flip a coin twice. What is the probability of getting heads both times?
Independent events: P(H and H) = P(H) × P(H) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4.
A box has 6 chocolates and 4 mints. You take one candy, eat it, then take another. What is the probability both are chocolates?
Without replacement (dependent): P(choc then choc) = 6/10 × 5/9 = 30/90 = 1/3.
You roll two dice. What is the probability of getting a sum of 7?
There are 36 total outcomes. Pairs summing to 7: (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1) = 6 outcomes. P = 6/36 = 1/6.
A shelf has 8 mystery novels and 4 science fiction novels. You pick two books at random without replacement. What is the probability both are mystery novels?
Without replacement: P(mystery then mystery) = 8/12 × 7/11 = 56/132 = 14/33.
A spinner with sections 1-5 is spun twice. What is the probability of getting 3 on both spins?
Independent events: P(3 and 3) = 1/5 × 1/5 = 1/25.
In a class of 30 students, 18 passed the test. If two students are randomly selected without replacement, what is the probability both passed?
Without replacement: P = 18/30 × 17/29 = 306/870 = 51/145.
A weather forecaster says there is a 40% chance of rain on Saturday and a 30% chance on Sunday, independently. What is the probability it rains on both days?
Independent events: P(rain Sat and rain Sun) = 0.4 × 0.3 = 0.12 = 12%.
A teacher randomly picks 2 students from a group of 5 girls and 7 boys for a project. What is the probability both are girls?
Without replacement: P(girl then girl) = 5/12 × 4/11 = 20/132 = 5/33.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the multiplication rule for probability?
The multiplication rule finds the probability of both events occurring. For independent events use P(A) × P(B); for dependent events use P(A) × P(B|A).

--- Lesson 12.3: Conditional Probability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conditional Probability
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is conditional probability?
Conditional probability measures the likelihood of an event B happening when we already know that event A has occurred. It is written P(B|A) and calculated as P(A and B)/P(A).
What is a two-way frequency table?
A two-way frequency table organizes data into rows and columns based on two categorical variables. It shows joint frequencies (both conditions), marginal totals, and is used to compute conditional probabilities.
What is joint probability?
Joint probability P(A and B) is the probability of both events A and B occurring together. In a two-way table, joint probabilities come from the interior cells.
What is a marginal probability?
Marginal probability is the probability of a single event calculated from the row or column totals (margins) of a two-way table, without considering any conditions.
What is the multiplication rule using conditional probability?
The general multiplication rule states P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A). This works for both independent and dependent events. For independent events, P(B|A) = P(B), simplifying to P(A) × P(B).
What is a relative frequency?
Relative frequency is a count divided by the total number of observations. It provides an experimental estimate of probability and is often expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.
What is the addition rule for probability?
The addition rule finds the probability of A or B (or both): P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B). The subtraction prevents double-counting outcomes in both events.
In a school survey, 60% of students play sports, 40% are in clubs, and 25% do both. What is the probability a student is in a club, given they play sports?
P(club|sports) = P(club and sports)/P(sports) = 0.25/0.60 = 5/12 ≈ 0.417.
At a restaurant, 70% of customers order an entree, 50% order a drink, and 40% order both. Given that a customer orders a drink, what is the probability they also ordered an entree?
P(entree|drink) = P(entree and drink)/P(drink) = 0.40/0.50 = 4/5 = 0.8.
A college admissions office finds that 60% of admitted students enroll. Of those who enroll, 85% return for a second year. What is the probability a randomly admitted student enrolls AND returns?
P(enroll and return) = P(enroll) × P(return|enroll) = 0.60 × 0.85 = 0.51 or 51%.
In a game show, there are 3 doors. Behind one is a prize. You pick door 1, and the host (who knows what is behind each door) opens door 3 to reveal no prize. Should you switch to door 2?
This is the Monty Hall problem. Initially P(prize behind door 1) = 1/3 and P(prize behind door 2 or 3) = 2/3. After the host reveals door 3 is empty, that 2/3 probability shifts entirely to door 2. Switching gives you a 2/3 chance of winning.
Real-World Applications

The general multiplication rule states P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A). This works for both independent and dependent events. For independent events, P(B|A) = P(B), simplifying to P(A) × P(B).

P(butter|bread) = 0.80 and P(bread) = 0.25. Using the multiplication rule: P(bread and butter) = P(bread) × P(butter|bread) = 0.25 × 0.80 = 0.20 = 20%.

--- Lesson 12.4: Probability with Combinatorics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability with Combinatorics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a factorial?
A factorial, written n!, is the product of all positive integers from n down to 1. For example, 4! = 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24. By convention, 0! = 1.
What is a permutation?
A permutation is an ordered arrangement of items. The order matters, so AB is different from BA. The formula is P(n,r) = n!/(n−r)!.
What is a combination?
A combination is a selection of items where order does not matter. Choosing AB is the same as choosing BA. The formula is C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!).
What is the fundamental counting principle?
The fundamental counting principle states that if one task can be done m ways and another task n ways, the total number of ways to do both tasks is m × n.
What is the difference between a permutation and a combination?
In permutations, the order of selection matters (ABC ≠ BAC), while in combinations it does not (ABC = BAC). This is why P(n,r) ≥ C(n,r) for the same n and r.
What is the formula for permutations?
The permutation formula P(n,r) = n!/(n−r)! counts the number of ordered arrangements of r items chosen from n items.
What is the formula for combinations?
The combination formula C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!) counts the number of ways to select r items from n items when order does not matter.
What is the relationship between P(n, r) and C(n, r)?
Since C(n,r) = P(n,r)/r!, combinations divide out the r! arrangements of the selected items. This makes C(n,r) ≤ P(n,r) for any given n and r.
A bag has 5 red, 4 blue, and 3 green balls. If you pick 3 balls at random, what is the probability all 3 are red?
Total balls = 12. Ways to choose 3 red from 5: C(5,3) = 10. Total ways to choose any 3 from 12: C(12,3) = 220. P = 10/220 = 1/22.

    Explanation: Since C(n,r) = P(n,r)/r!, combinations divide out the r! arrangements of the selected items. This makes C(n,r) ≤ P(n,r) for any given n and r.

  Q21. A pizza shop offers 8 toppings. How many different 3-topping pizzas can you make?
    [ ] 24
    [ ] 336
    [ ] 512
    [*] 56
    Explanation: Order of toppings does not matter, so use combinations: C(8,3) = 8!/(3!·5!) = (8×7×6)/(3×2×1) = 336/6 = 56.

  Q22. A race has 10 runners. How many ways can gold, silver, and bronze medals be awarded?
    [ ] 1000
    [ ] 30
    [*] 720
    [ ] 120
    Explanation: Order matters (gold, silver, bronze are different), so use permutations: P(10,3) = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720.

  Q23. A club with 12 members needs to choose a president, vice president, and secretary. How many ways can this be done?
    [ ] 1728
    [*] 1320
    [ ] 36
    [ ] 220
    Explanation: Order matters (different positions), so P(12,3) = 12 × 11 × 10 = 1320.

  Q24. A lottery requires choosing 6 numbers from 1 to 49. How many possible ticket combinations exist?
    [*] 13,983,816
    [ ] 294
    [ ] 10,068,347,520
    [ ] 49
    Explanation: Order does not matter in a lottery: C(49,6) = 49!/(6!·43!) = 13,983,816. This explains why winning the lottery is extremely unlikely.

  Q25. A teacher wants to arrange 5 books on a shelf. How many arrangements are possible?
    [ ] 25
    [ ] 10
    [ ] 5
    [*] 120
    Explanation: Arranging all 5 books is 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120 possible arrangements.

  Q26. A committee of 4 is to be chosen from 10 volunteers. How many different committees can be formed?
    [ ] 5040

    [ ] 10000
    [*] 210
    Explanation: Order does not matter (a committee is a group): C(10,4) = 10!/(4!·6!) = (10×9×8×7)/(4×3×2×1) = 5040/24 = 210.

    [ ] 10000

    Explanation: Order matters and no repetition: P(10,4) = 10 × 9 × 8 × 7 = 5040 passwords.

  Q30. A bag has 5 red, 4 blue, and 3 green balls. If you pick 3 balls at random, what is the probability all 3 are red?

--- Lesson 12.4: Probability with Combinatorics (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: What is the Counting Principle?
     A: If event A has m outcomes and B has n outcomes, there are m × n total outcomes

  2. Q: What are permutations?
     A: Arrangements where ORDER matters: P(n,r) = n!/(n−r)!

  3. Q: What are combinations?
     A: Selections where ORDER does NOT matter: C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!)

  4. Q: P(5, 2) = ?
     A: 5!/3! = 20

  5. Q: C(5, 2) = ?
     A: 5!/(2!·3!) = 10

  6. Q: 3 shirts and 4 pants = how many outfits?
     A: 3 × 4 = 12

  7. Q: How many ways can 3 people line up?
     A: 3! = 6

  8. Q: When do you use permutations vs. combinations?
     A: Permutations when order matters; combinations when it doesn't

  9. Q: C(10, 3) = ?
     A: 10!/(3!·7!) = 120
