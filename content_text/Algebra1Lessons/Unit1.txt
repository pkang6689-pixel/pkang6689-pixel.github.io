--- Lesson 1.1: Measures of Center (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Center
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the mean of the data set {2, 4, 6, 8, 10}?
The mean is calculated by adding all values and dividing by the count: (2 + 4 + 6 + 8 + 10) / 5 = 30 / 5 = 6.
What is the mode of {5, 5, 8, 10, 12}?
The mode is the value that appears most frequently. Since 5 appears twice and all other values appear once, the mode is 5.
What is the mean of {10, 20, 30}?
The mean is (10 + 20 + 30) / 3 = 60 / 3 = 20.
If the data set {1, 3, 5, 7, x} has a mean of 5, what is x?
Mean = sum / count, so 5 = (1 + 3 + 5 + 7 + x) / 5. Solving: 25 = 16 + x, so x = 9.
What is the mean of a data set?
The mean is the arithmetic average, calculated by adding all values in the data set and dividing by the total number of values.
What is the median?
The median is the middle value when all data points are sorted from least to greatest. For an even number of values, it is the average of the two middle values.
What is the mode?
The mode is the value (or values) that appear most often in a data set. A data set can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes.
What is an outlier?
An outlier is an extreme value that falls far from the rest of the data and can significantly affect the mean while having little effect on the median.
What is a weighted mean?
A weighted mean assigns different importance (weights) to data values, computed as the sum of (value times weight) divided by the sum of all weights.
What is a 'resistant' measure of center?
A resistant (or robust) measure is one that does not change much when outliers are present. The median is resistant, while the mean is not.
What is the 'frequency' of a data value?
Frequency is the count of how many times a particular value occurs in a data set. It is used to determine the mode.
A teacher records the following test scores for 5 students: 72, 85, 90, 68, and 95. What is the mean test score for the class?
The mean is (72 + 85 + 90 + 68 + 95) / 5 = 410 / 5 = 82. This represents the average performance of the class.
A bakery sells the following numbers of cupcakes each day over 6 days: 30, 45, 30, 50, 30, 55. What is the mode, and what does it tell the baker?
The mode is 30 because it appears three times, more than any other value. This tells the baker that 30 cupcakes is the most frequently occurring daily sales figure.
A gym owner records the ages of members in an evening class: 22, 25, 27, 30, 31, 33, 35. What is the median age of the class?
The data is already in order with 7 values, so the median is the 4th value: 30. The gym owner can use this to understand that the typical member in the class is about 30 years old.
A soccer coach tracks the number of goals scored per game over 8 games: 1, 0, 3, 2, 1, 1, 4, 0. What is the mean number of goals per game?
The mean is (1 + 0 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 1 + 4 + 0) / 8 = 12 / 8 = 1.5 goals per game.
A student earns the following scores on four exams weighted differently: Exam 1 (weight 10%) = 80, Exam 2 (weight 20%) = 90, Exam 3 (weight 30%) = 70, Exam 4 (weight 40%) = 85. What is the weighted mean?
Weighted mean = (80 x 0.10) + (90 x 0.20) + (70 x 0.30) + (85 x 0.40) = 8 + 18 + 21 + 34 = 81.0.

Outliers and Resistant Measures
The mean uses every value in its calculation, so extreme values (outliers) pull the mean toward them, while the median and mode remain relatively unaffected.
An outlier is an extreme value that falls far from the rest of the data and can significantly affect the mean while having little effect on the median.
A resistant (or robust) measure is one that does not change much when outliers are present. The median is resistant, while the mean is not.

--- Lesson 1.2: Measures of Spread (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Spread
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the range of {3, 7, 10, 15, 20}?
The range is the difference between the maximum and minimum values: 20 - 3 = 17.
For the data set {2, 4, 6, 8, 10}, what is Q1?
Q1 is the median of the lower half {2, 4}. The median of these two values is (2 + 4) / 2 = 3.
What is the range of a data set?
The range is the simplest measure of spread, calculated by subtracting the smallest value from the largest value in the data set.
What is the interquartile range (IQR)?
The IQR is Q3 minus Q1 and measures how spread out the middle half of the data is, making it resistant to outliers.
What is standard deviation?
Standard deviation measures the typical distance between each data point and the mean. A larger standard deviation means more spread out data.
What is variance?
Variance is the average of the squared deviations from the mean. The standard deviation is the square root of the variance.
What is a quartile?
Quartiles are values that divide ordered data into four equal groups. Q1, Q2 (the median), and Q3 mark the boundaries of each quarter.
What is the five-number summary?
The five-number summary provides a quick snapshot of a data set's distribution using the minimum, Q1, median, Q3, and maximum values.
What is the 'spread' of a data set?
Spread describes the variability or dispersion of data values. Greater spread means values are more widely distributed, while less spread means they are closer together.
A farmer measures the heights (in cm) of 7 tomato plants: 30, 32, 35, 33, 31, 34, 36. What is the range of the plant heights?
The range is the maximum minus the minimum: 36 - 30 = 6 cm. This tells the farmer that plant heights vary by 6 cm.
A teacher gives a test and the scores are {55, 60, 65, 70, 72, 75, 78, 80, 85, 90, 92, 95}. What is the IQR of the test scores?
Q1 is the median of the lower half {55,60,65,70,72,75} = (65+70)/2 = 67.5. Q3 is the median of the upper half {78,80,85,90,92,95} = (85+90)/2 = 87.5. IQR = 87.5 - 67.5 = 20.
A coach records 100-meter dash times (in seconds) for a sprinter over 6 races: 11.2, 11.5, 11.3, 11.4, 11.1, 11.5. What is the range of the sprinter's times?
Range = max - min = 11.5 - 11.1 = 0.4 seconds. This small range indicates the sprinter is very consistent.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The IQR only measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data, so outliers in the tails do not affect it.
Real-World Applications
Example: If every value in a data set is multiplied by 3, the standard deviation:
Multiplying every value by a constant c multiplies the standard deviation by |c|. So multiplying by 3 triples the standard deviation.
Example: A teacher gives a test and the scores are {55, 60, 65, 70, 72, 75, 78, 80, 85, 90, 92, 95}. What is the IQR of the test scores?
Q1 is the median of the lower half {55,60,65,70,72,75} = (65+70)/2 = 67.5. Q3 is the median of the upper half {78,80,85,90,92,95} = (85+90)/2 = 87.5. IQR = 87.5 - 67.5 = 20.
Example: A delivery company tracks package weights (in lbs): 2, 3, 5, 8, 10, 12, 15, 50. An analyst uses the IQR instead of the range to describe spread. Why is the IQR a better choice here?
The 50-lb package is an outlier that inflates the range to 48. The IQR focuses on the middle 50% of the data, providing a spread measure that is not distorted by extremes.

--- Lesson 1.5: Box Plots &amp; Dot Plots (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Box Plots &amp; Dot Plots
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a box plot (box-and-whisker plot)?
A box plot is a graphical display that uses a rectangular box and extending lines (whiskers) to show the min, Q1, median, Q3, and max of a data set.
What is a dot plot?
A dot plot places a dot for each data value above a number line. Multiple dots are stacked when values repeat, making it easy to see the distribution of small data sets.
What is the 'whisker' on a box plot?
Whiskers are the lines that extend from each end of the box to the smallest and largest data values that are not classified as outliers.
What is a 'modified box plot'?
A modified box plot uses the 1.5 x IQR rule to identify outliers and plots them as individual points. The whiskers stop at the last non-outlier value.
What is a 'fence' in the context of box plots?
Fences are calculated as Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and Q3 + 1.5(IQR). Any data point beyond a fence is classified as an outlier.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
In a modified box plot, outliers are plotted as individual dots or asterisks beyond the whiskers, which stop at the fences (1.5 x IQR from Q1 and Q3).
Q1 = 11.25, Q3 = 12.05, IQR = 0.8. Upper fence = 12.05 + 1.5(0.8) = 13.25. Since 15.0 > 13.25, it is an outlier.
On a modified box plot, the $250,000 CEO salary would appear as an individual point beyond the upper whisker, making it clearly visible as an outlier.
High-end outliers in Month 2 suggest a few exceptionally busy days, likely caused by holiday shopping, sales events, or other special circumstances.
Real-World Applications
Example: What percentage of data falls below Q1 in a box plot?
Q1 is the first quartile, meaning 25% of the data falls below it and 75% falls above it.
Example: A school counselor compares SAT scores for two classes using parallel box plots. Class A has a median of 1100 and IQR of 200, while Class B has a median of 1050 and IQR of 100. Which class performed more consistently?
Class B's IQR of 100 is half of Class A's IQR of 200, meaning the middle 50% of Class B's scores are packed into a smaller range. This indicates more consistency.
Example: A teacher makes a dot plot of quiz scores: 6, 7, 7, 8, 8, 8, 9, 9, 10. How many dots are stacked above the value 8?
The value 8 appears three times in the data set, so three dots are stacked above 8 on the dot plot.
Example: A sports analyst compares points scored per game for two basketball players using parallel box plots. Player X: median 18, IQR 6. Player Y: median 22, IQR 14. Which player would you describe as a more reliable scorer?
Player X's IQR of 6 is less than half of Player Y's IQR of 14, meaning Player X's scoring is more tightly concentrated and therefore more reliable, even though Player Y averages more.

What is a two-way table?
A two-way table organizes categorical data into rows and columns, where each cell shows the count for a specific combination of two categorical variables.
What is a 'joint frequency'?
A joint frequency is the count in an inner cell where a specific row category and column category intersect, showing how many subjects belong to both categories.
What is a 'marginal frequency'?
Marginal frequencies are the row totals and column totals that appear along the margins (edges) of the two-way table.
What is a 'conditional frequency'?
A conditional frequency gives the proportion within a specific group (row or column). It answers questions like 'Of the males, what fraction prefer action movies?'
What is a 'contingency table'?
A contingency table is another name for a two-way table, used to display the frequency distribution of two categorical variables simultaneously.
What is an 'association' between two categorical variables?
An association exists when the conditional distributions of one variable change depending on the category of the other variable, suggesting a relationship between them.

IQR = Q3 - Q1 = 40 - 20 = 20. Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5(IQR) = 40 + 1.5(20) = 40 + 30 = 70.
What is an outlier?
An outlier is an extreme value that lies far from the majority of the data. It can occur on either the high or low end of the distribution.
What is the '1.5 x IQR rule'?
The 1.5 x IQR rule establishes fences at Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and Q3 + 1.5(IQR). Data points beyond these fences are formally classified as outliers.
What is a 'fence' in outlier detection?
Fences are the boundary values calculated using the 1.5 x IQR rule: lower fence = Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and upper fence = Q3 + 1.5(IQR).
What is the 'effect of an outlier on the range'?
Since the range is max minus min, any outlier at the high or low end directly changes the range, often making it much larger than it would be without the outlier.
What is a 'deviation from the mean'?
A deviation from the mean is calculated as (data value - mean). Outliers have very large deviations, which inflate measures like variance and standard deviation.
