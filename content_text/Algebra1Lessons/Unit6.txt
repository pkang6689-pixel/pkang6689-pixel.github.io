--- Lesson 6.1: Key Features of Parabolas (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Key Features of Parabolas
A quadratic function has the form y = ax² + bx + c. Its graph is a parabola.

If a > 0, the parabola opens upward (minimum). If a downward (maximum).

The vertex is the highest or lowest point. The axis of symmetry is x = −b/(2a).

The y-intercept is c (set x = 0). The x-intercepts (zeros/roots) are where y = 0.

--- Lesson 6.2: Graphing in Standard Form (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphing in Standard Form
Standard form: y = ax² + bx + c. Steps: (1) Find the axis of symmetry x = −b/(2a). (2) Find the vertex by substituting. (3) Plot the y-intercept and its mirror point. (4) Plot additional points.

The y-intercept is the point (0, c).

Use symmetry about the axis to find mirror image points.

The larger |a|, the narrower the parabola. The smaller |a|, the wider.

--- Lesson 6.4: Transformations of Quadratic Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Transformations of Quadratic Functions
y = a(x − h)² + k: h shifts horizontally, k shifts vertically, a stretches/compresses and reflects.

|a| > 1: vertical stretch (narrower). 0 < |a| < 1: vertical compression (wider).

a < 0: reflection over the x-axis.

Combine transformations: e.g., y = −2(x − 3)² + 1 is a reflection, stretch by 2, right 3, up 1.

--- Lesson 6.6: Quadratic Regression (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Quadratic Regression
Quadratic regression finds the best-fit equation y = ax² + bx + c for data that follows a parabolic pattern.

Use a graphing calculator: enter data, run QuadReg.

Check r² (coefficient of determination) — closer to 1 means a better fit.

Use quadratic regression when the scatter plot shows a curved (parabolic) pattern and second differences are roughly constant.
