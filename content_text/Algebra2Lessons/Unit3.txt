--- Lesson 3.1: Polynomial Operations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Polynomial Operations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the degree of 7x^4 - 3x^2 + 5x - 1?
The degree of a polynomial is the highest power of the variable. The highest exponent here is 4 from the term 7x^4.
What is the leading coefficient of -4x^5 + 9x^3 - x + 2?
The leading coefficient is the coefficient of the term with the highest degree. The highest-degree term is -4x^5, so the leading coefficient is -4.
What is a polynomial?
A polynomial is an algebraic expression made up of terms with variables raised to non-negative integer exponents, combined with addition, subtraction, and multiplication by constants.
What is the 'degree' of a polynomial?
The degree of a polynomial is determined by the highest power of the variable present. For example, 5x^3 + 2x - 1 has degree 3.
What is a 'monomial'?
A monomial is a polynomial with exactly one term, such as 5x^3, -7, or 2xy^2. It consists of a coefficient multiplied by variables raised to non-negative integer powers.
What is the 'constant term' of a polynomial?
The constant term is the term with no variable attached (x^0 = 1). In 3x^2 + 5x - 7, the constant term is -7.
What is the 'distributive property' in polynomial multiplication?
The distributive property states that a(b + c) = ab + ac. When multiplying polynomials, each term of one polynomial must be multiplied by every term of the other.
What is FOIL?
FOIL stands for First, Outer, Inner, Last. It is a shortcut for multiplying two binomials by multiplying the first terms, outer terms, inner terms, and last terms, then combining.
What is 'closure' with respect to polynomial operations?
Polynomials are closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication, meaning the result of any of these operations on polynomials is always another polynomial.
A box has dimensions (x + 1), (x + 2), and (x + 3) centimeters. What is the volume of the box as a polynomial?
V = (x+1)(x+2)(x+3). First: (x+1)(x+2) = x^2 + 3x + 2. Then (x^2 + 3x + 2)(x+3) = x^3 + 3x^2 + 3x^2 + 9x + 2x + 6 = x^3 + 6x^2 + 11x + 6.
A company's revenue from two products is R1 = 3x^2 + 10x and R2 = x^2 - 4x + 50. What is the total revenue?
Total revenue = R1 + R2 = (3x^2 + x^2) + (10x - 4x) + 50 = 4x^2 + 6x + 50.
A building has a rectangular base of (4x - 1) by (3x + 2) meters and a triangular roof section. What is the area of just the base?
Area = (4x-1)(3x+2) = 12x^2 + 8x - 3x - 2 = 12x^2 + 5x - 2.
An investment returns (x^2 + 3x) dollars in the first year and (2x^2 - x + 500) dollars in the second year. If expenses are (x^2 + 100) each year, what is the total profit over two years?
Total returns = x^2 + 3x + 2x^2 - x + 500 = 3x^2 + 2x + 500. Total expenses = 2(x^2 + 100) = 2x^2 + 200. Profit = 3x^2 + 2x + 500 - 2x^2 - 200 = x^2 + 2x + 300.
Real-World Applications
Example: Multiply: (2x + 3)(x^2 - x + 4)
2x(x^2 - x + 4) + 3(x^2 - x + 4) = 2x^3 - 2x^2 + 8x + 3x^2 - 3x + 12 = 2x^3 + x^2 + 5x + 12.
Example: Multiply: (x - 1)(x + 1)(x + 3)
First multiply (x-1)(x+1) = x^2 - 1. Then (x^2 - 1)(x + 3) = x^3 + 3x^2 - x - 3.
Example: Multiply: (2x - 5)^2
Using (a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2: (2x)^2 - 2(2x)(5) + 5^2 = 4x^2 - 20x + 25.
Example: What is the 'distributive property' in polynomial multiplication?
The distributive property states that a(b + c) = ab + ac. When multiplying polynomials, each term of one polynomial must be multiplied by every term of the other.
Example: A storage container is a cube with side length (x + 5) feet. Express the surface area as a polynomial.
Surface area of a cube = 6s^2 = 6(x+5)^2 = 6(x^2 + 10x + 25) = 6x^2 + 60x + 150.
Example: Two competing stores have weekly sales modeled by S1 = 4x^2 + 20x + 100 and S2 = 2x^2 + 35x + 80. By how much does Store 1 outsell Store 2?
S1 - S2 = (4x^2 - 2x^2) + (20x - 35x) + (100 - 80) = 2x^2 - 15x + 20.

--- Lesson 3.2: Factoring Polynomials (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Factoring Polynomials
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'Greatest Common Factor' (GCF) in factoring?
The GCF is the largest factor (including both numerical and variable parts) that is common to every term in the polynomial. It should always be factored out first.
What is a 'difference of squares'?
A difference of squares is a^2 - b^2, which always factors as (a - b)(a + b). Note that a sum of squares a^2 + b^2 cannot be factored over the real numbers.
What is the 'sum of cubes' formula?
The sum of cubes formula is a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2). Notice the signs follow the pattern: Same, Opposite, Always Positive (SOAP).
What is the 'difference of cubes' formula?
The difference of cubes formula is a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2). The first factor matches the sign, the middle term of the trinomial is opposite.
What is 'factoring completely'?
Factoring completely means expressing the polynomial as a product of its GCF and irreducible polynomial factors. No factor should be factorable further.
What is a 'zero' of a polynomial in relation to factoring?
A zero of a polynomial is an x-value where f(x) = 0. By the zero product property, if a polynomial is in factored form, setting each factor equal to zero gives the zeros.
What is the 'zero product property'?
The zero product property states that if ab = 0, then a = 0 or b = 0. This principle is fundamental to solving polynomial equations by factoring.
An engineer needs to factor x^3 - 64 to analyze stress points. What is the factored form?
Difference of cubes with a = x and b = 4: x^3 - 64 = (x - 4)(x^2 + 4x + 16).
A rectangular garden has area x^2 + 5x + 6 square feet. If the width is (x + 2), what is the length?
Factor the area: x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3). Since width is (x + 2), the length must be (x + 3) feet.

--- Lesson 3.2: Factoring Polynomials (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Factor: x^2 - 9
    [ ] (x - 9)(x + 1)
    [ ] (x - 3)^2
    [*] (x - 3)(x + 3)
    [ ] (x + 3)^2
    Explanation: This is a difference of squares: a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b). Here a = x and b = 3, so x^2 - 9 = (x-3)(x+3).

  Q2. Factor completely: 2x^2 + 10x + 12
    [ ] 2(x + 6)(x + 1)
    [ ] (x + 2)(2x + 6)
    [*] 2(x + 2)(x + 3)
    [ ] (2x + 4)(x + 3)
    Explanation: First factor out the GCF of 2: 2(x^2 + 5x + 6). Then factor the trinomial: 2(x + 2)(x + 3).

  Q3. Factor: x^3 - 8
    [*] (x - 2)(x^2 + 2x + 4)
    [ ] (x - 2)(x^2 - 2x + 4)
    [ ] (x - 2)^3
    [ ] (x + 2)(x^2 - 2x + 4)
    Explanation: This is a difference of cubes: a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2). With a = x and b = 2: (x - 2)(x^2 + 2x + 4).

  Q4. Factor: x^3 + 27
    [ ] (x + 3)^3
    [ ] (x + 3)(x^2 + 3x + 9)
    [*] (x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9)
    [ ] (x - 3)(x^2 + 3x + 9)
    Explanation: Sum of cubes: a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2). With a = x and b = 3: (x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9).

  Q5. Factor by grouping: x^3 + 2x^2 + 3x + 6
    [*] (x^2 + 3)(x + 2)
    [ ] x(x^2 + 2x + 3) + 6
    [ ] (x^2 + 2)(x + 3)
    [ ] (x + 1)(x^2 + 6)
    Explanation: Group: x^2(x + 2) + 3(x + 2) = (x^2 + 3)(x + 2). The common factor (x + 2) is extracted from both groups.

  Q6. Factor: 6x^2 + 7x - 3
    [*] (3x - 1)(2x + 3)
    [ ] (6x - 1)(x + 3)
    [ ] (2x - 1)(3x + 3)
    [ ] (3x + 1)(2x - 3)
    Explanation: We need factors of 6*(-3) = -18 that add to 7: 9 and -2. Rewrite: 6x^2 + 9x - 2x - 3 = 3x(2x + 3) - 1(2x + 3) = (3x - 1)(2x + 3).

  Q7. Factor completely: x^4 - 16
    [*] (x^2 + 4)(x + 2)(x - 2)
    [ ] (x - 2)^2(x + 2)^2
    [ ] (x^2 - 4)(x^2 + 4)
    [ ] (x^2 + 4)(x^2 - 4)
    Explanation: First use difference of squares: (x^2 - 4)(x^2 + 4). Then factor x^2 - 4 again: (x - 2)(x + 2)(x^2 + 4). Note x^2 + 4 cannot be factored over the reals.

  Q8. Factor: 3x^3 - 12x
    [ ] 3x(x^2 - 4)
    [ ] x(3x^2 - 12)
    [ ] 3(x^3 - 4x)
    [*] 3x(x - 2)(x + 2)
    Explanation: Factor out GCF: 3x(x^2 - 4). Then apply difference of squares: 3x(x - 2)(x + 2). Always factor completely.

  Q9. Factor: x^2 - 7x + 12
    [ ] (x - 2)(x - 6)
    [ ] (x - 1)(x - 12)
    [*] (x - 3)(x - 4)
    [ ] (x + 3)(x + 4)
    Explanation: Find two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to -7: -3 and -4. So x^2 - 7x + 12 = (x - 3)(x - 4).

  Q10. Factor: 8x^3 + 1
    [ ] (2x + 1)(4x^2 + 2x + 1)
    [ ] (2x + 1)^3
    [ ] (8x + 1)(x^2 + 1)
    [*] (2x + 1)(4x^2 - 2x + 1)
    Explanation: Sum of cubes: (2x)^3 + 1^3 = (2x + 1)((2x)^2 - (2x)(1) + 1^2) = (2x + 1)(4x^2 - 2x + 1).

  Q11. What does it mean to 'factor' a polynomial?
    [ ] To find the value of x
    [ ] To simplify by combining like terms
    [*] To write it as a product of simpler polynomial expressions
    [ ] To divide by the highest power
    Explanation: Factoring a polynomial means expressing it as a product of two or more simpler polynomials. It is the reverse of multiplication (expanding).

  Q12. What is the 'Greatest Common Factor' (GCF) in factoring?
    [ ] The largest coefficient in the polynomial
    [ ] The product of all coefficients
    [ ] The highest-degree term
    [*] The largest expression that divides evenly into all terms of the polynomial
    Explanation: The GCF is the largest factor (including both numerical and variable parts) that is common to every term in the polynomial. It should always be factored out first.

  Q13. What is a 'difference of squares'?
    [ ] Any expression with two squared terms
    [*] An expression of the form a^2 - b^2 that factors as (a - b)(a + b)
    [ ] An expression of the form a^2 + b^2

--- Lesson 3.3: Synthetic Division (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Synthetic Division
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Use synthetic division to divide (x^3 + 3x^2 - 4x + 2) by (x - 1). What is the remainder?
Using synthetic division with 1: bring down 1, multiply and add through: 1, 4, 0, 2. The remainder is 2.
Divide (2x^3 - 5x^2 + 3x - 1) by (x - 2) using synthetic division. What is the quotient?
Synthetic division with 2: [2, -5, 3, -1]. Bring down 2; 2*2=4, -5+4=-1; 2*(-1)=-2, 3+(-2)=1; 2*1=2, -1+2=1. Quotient: 2x^2 - x + 1, remainder 1.
Divide (x^4 - 1) by (x - 1) using synthetic division. What is the quotient?
Write x^4 - 1 as x^4 + 0x^3 + 0x^2 + 0x - 1. Synthetic division with 1 gives coefficients 1, 1, 1, 1 with remainder 0. Quotient: x^3 + x^2 + x + 1.
Use synthetic division to divide (3x^3 + 2x - 5) by (x + 1). What is the remainder?
Use -1 (since dividing by x+1). Coefficients: [3, 0, 2, -5]. 3; -3, -3; 3, 5; -5, -10. Remainder is -10.
Divide (x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6) by (x - 1). What is the quotient?
Synthetic division with 1: [1, -6, 11, -6]. 1; 1, -5; -5, 6; 6, 0. Quotient: x^2 - 5x + 6, which factors further as (x-2)(x-3).
What is the remainder when x^4 + 3x^3 - x^2 + 2 is divided by (x - 1)?
By the Remainder Theorem, the remainder is f(1) = 1 + 3 - 1 + 0 + 2 = 5. Coefficients include 0 for the missing x term.
What is synthetic division?
Synthetic division is an efficient shorthand method for dividing a polynomial by a linear divisor of the form (x - c). It uses only the coefficients and the value c.
What is the 'divisor' in polynomial division?
The divisor is the polynomial you divide by. In synthetic division, the divisor must be linear (degree 1) in the form (x - c).
What is the 'quotient' in polynomial division?
The quotient is the polynomial result of the division. If f(x) = d(x)*q(x) + r, then q(x) is the quotient, always one degree less than the dividend.
What is the 'remainder' in polynomial division?
The remainder is what is left after dividing. When dividing by a linear factor, the remainder is a constant. If the remainder is 0, the divisor is a factor of the dividend.
What is the 'dividend' in polynomial division?
The dividend is the polynomial being divided. In f(x) / d(x), f(x) is the dividend. In synthetic division, its coefficients are listed in the top row.
What is 'polynomial long division'?
Polynomial long division follows the same process as long division of numbers: divide, multiply, subtract, bring down. It works with any polynomial divisor.
What is the division algorithm for polynomials?
The division algorithm states that dividend = divisor * quotient + remainder, where the remainder's degree is strictly less than the divisor's degree.

--- Lesson 3.4: Polynomial Graphs & Zeros (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Polynomial Graphs & Zeros
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the end behavior of f(x) = -x^3 + 2x?
A cubic with a negative leading coefficient rises to the left and falls to the right, since odd-degree polynomials have opposite end behaviors.
What is the degree of f(x) = 4x^5 - x^3 + 7?
The degree of a polynomial is the highest power of x. Here the highest power is x^5, so the degree is 5.
What is the y-intercept of f(x) = 2x^3 - x^2 + 5?
The y-intercept is f(0) = 2(0)^3 - (0)^2 + 5 = 5.
What is the leading coefficient of f(x) = -3x^6 + x^4 - 2?
The leading coefficient is the coefficient of the highest-degree term. The highest-degree term is -3x^6, so the leading coefficient is -3.
What is 'end behavior' of a polynomial?
End behavior describes the direction the graph heads as x goes to positive infinity or negative infinity, determined by the degree and leading coefficient.
What is the 'Fundamental Theorem of Algebra'?
The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra states that every polynomial of degree n >= 1 has at least one root in the complex numbers, guaranteeing n roots when counted with multiplicity.
What is a 'local maximum' of a polynomial?
A local maximum is a point where the function value is higher than at all nearby points, though it may not be the absolute highest value of the function.
What is the 'leading term' of a polynomial?
The leading term is the term with the highest exponent. It determines the end behavior and the degree of the polynomial.
A box manufacturer models the volume of a box as V(x) = x(10 - 2x)(8 - 2x), where x is the height of the cut corners in inches. What is the degree of this polynomial?
Expanding: x(10 - 2x)(8 - 2x) = x(80 - 36x + 4x^2) = 4x^3 - 36x^2 + 80x. The highest power is x^3, so degree 3.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'multiplicity' of a zero refer to?
Multiplicity is the number of times a particular factor appears in the factored form. For example, in (x - 2)^3, the zero x = 2 has multiplicity 3.
Example: What does it mean for a zero to have 'odd multiplicity'?
When a zero has odd multiplicity, the graph crosses through the x-axis at that point. When the multiplicity is even, the graph merely touches and turns around.

The quotient is the polynomial result you get when dividing, before accounting for any remainder. It satisfies: dividend = divisor times quotient + remainder.
What is a 'depressed polynomial'?
After dividing a polynomial by one of its factors, the resulting quotient is called the depressed polynomial. Its degree is one less than the original.
What is the 'dividend' in polynomial division?
The dividend is the polynomial that is being divided. In f(x) divided by (x - a), f(x) is the dividend.
What is 'polynomial long division'?
Polynomial long division is a step-by-step algorithm that divides polynomials in a manner analogous to long division of integers, producing a quotient and remainder.

What is the value of i^2?

What is the conjugate of 4 - 5i?
The conjugate of a + bi is a - bi. So the conjugate of 4 - 5i is 4 + 5i.
What is the product (2 + i)(2 - i)?
Using the difference of squares pattern: (2 + i)(2 - i) = 4 - i^2 = 4 - (-1) = 5.
What is an 'imaginary number'?
An imaginary number has the form bi, where b is a nonzero real number and i is the imaginary unit defined as sqrt(-1).
What is a 'complex number'?
A complex number combines a real part (a) and an imaginary part (bi) in the form a + bi, where a and b are real numbers.
What is the 'complex conjugate' of a number?
The complex conjugate of a + bi is a - bi. Multiplying a complex number by its conjugate yields a real number: (a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 + b^2.
What is the 'imaginary unit'?
The imaginary unit i is defined by the property that i^2 = -1. It allows us to take square roots of negative numbers.
What is the 'complex plane'?
The complex plane (or Argand diagram) is a two-dimensional plane where the horizontal axis represents real parts and the vertical axis represents imaginary parts.
What is a 'pure imaginary number'?
A pure imaginary number has the form bi where b is nonzero and the real part is zero. Examples include 2i, -5i, and i.

Total impedance in series is the sum: (2 + 3i) + (4 - i) = 6 + 2i ohms.
A signal processing engineer multiplies two signals represented by (1 + 2i) and (3 - i). What is the product?
(1 + 2i)(3 - i) = 3 - i + 6i - 2i^2 = 3 + 5i + 2 = 5 + 5i.
An engineer finds the conjugate of impedance Z = 5 - 7i for a circuit calculation. What is the conjugate?
The conjugate of a + bi is a - bi. So the conjugate of 5 - 7i is 5 + 7i.

    [ ] 4, 1
    [ ] 1, 2
    [ ] 2, -2
    [*] 1, -1, 2, -2
    Explanation: Let u = x^2: u^2 - 5u + 4 = (u - 1)(u - 4) = 0. So x^2 = 1 or x^2 = 4, giving x = +-1, +-2.

  Q11. What does 'degree of a polynomial' mean?
    [ ] The constant term
    [ ] The number of terms
    [ ] The leading coefficient
    [*] The highest power of the variable in the polynomial
    Explanation: The degree is the largest exponent on the variable. It determines the polynomial's end behavior and the maximum number of roots.

  Q12. What is the 'Rational Root Theorem'?
    [ ] A method for graphing, particularly regarding states following
    [ ] A theorem that finds all roots in most standard conditions
    [ ] A theorem about irrational numbers following the established framework
    [*] A theorem that lists all possible rational roots of a polynomial with integer coefficients
    Explanation: The Rational Root Theorem states that any rational root p/q of a polynomial with integer coefficients must have p dividing the constant term and q dividing the leading coefficient.

  Q13. What does 'Descartes' Rule of Signs' determine?
    [ ] The end behavior
    [ ] The degree of a polynomial
    [*] The possible number of positive and negative real roots
    [ ] The exact roots of a polynomial
    Explanation: Descartes' Rule of Signs uses the number of sign changes in f(x) and f(-x) to determine the possible number of positive and negative real roots.

  Q14. What is a 'higher-degree polynomial'?
    [ ] A constant polynomial
    [ ] A polynomial of degree 2
    [*] A polynomial of degree 3 or greater
    [ ] A linear polynomial
    Explanation: Higher-degree polynomials typically refer to those of degree 3 (cubic) or higher, which can have more complex behavior than quadratics.

  Q15. What does 'irreducible polynomial' mean?
    [ ] A polynomial equal to zero, particularly regarding does as predicted by standard models
    [ ] A polynomial with no terms, particularly regarding system as predicted by standard models
    [ ] A polynomial of degree 1, particularly regarding system following conventional understanding
    [*] A polynomial that cannot be factored into polynomials of lower degree over the given number system
    Explanation: An irreducible polynomial cannot be written as a product of two non-constant polynomials over the specified number system (e.g., reals or rationals).

  Q16. What is 'synthetic division'?
    [ ] A method for multiplying polynomials
    [*] A shortcut method for dividing a polynomial by a linear binomial (x - a)
    [ ] A way to find the degree
    [ ] A graphing technique
    Explanation: Synthetic division is an efficient algorithm for dividing a polynomial by (x - a) using only the coefficients, avoiding the full long division process.

  Q17. What does 'multiplicity' of a root mean?
    [*] The number of times a root appears as a factor
    [ ] The sign of the root
    [ ] The degree of the polynomial
    [ ] The size of the root
    Explanation: If (x - r)^k is a factor of a polynomial, then r is a root with multiplicity k. Multiplicity affects whether the graph crosses or touches the x-axis at that root.

  Q18. What is 'polynomial long division'?
    [*] A method of dividing polynomials similar to long division of numbers
    [ ] A method for finding the degree
    [ ] A way to multiply polynomials
    [ ] A graphing method
    Explanation: Polynomial long division systematically divides one polynomial by another, producing a quotient and remainder, analogous to integer long division.

  Q19. What are 'complex roots'?
    [ ] Roots that are very large
    [ ] Only real number roots
    [ ] Roots of linear polynomials
    [*] Roots that involve the imaginary unit i
    Explanation: Complex roots are solutions of the form a + bi where b is nonzero. They always occur in conjugate pairs for polynomials with real coefficients.

  Q20. What does 'factor theorem' state?
    [*] (x - a) is a factor of f(x) if and only if f(a) = 0
    [ ] All polynomials can be factored
    [ ] The factor equals the degree
    [ ] Factoring is impossible for degree > 4
    Explanation: The Factor Theorem connects roots and factors: (x - a) divides f(x) exactly when substituting x = a gives zero.

  Q21. A company models its revenue as R(x) = x^4 - 10x^3 + 35x^2 - 50x + 24 in thousands, where x is years. The Rational Root Theorem suggests testing x = 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, etc. Which of these is a root?
    [ ] x = 6
    [*] x = 1
    [ ] x = 10
    [ ] x = 5
    Explanation: R(1) = 1 - 10 + 35 - 50 + 24 = 0. So x = 1 is a root. The polynomial factors as (x-1)(x-2)(x-3)(x-4).

  Q22. A biologist models a population as P(t) = t^5 - t. She factors this to find when the population is zero. What are the real roots?
    [ ] t = 0 and 1
    [ ] t = 0 only
    [*] t = 0, 1, and -1
    [ ] t = 1 and -1
    Explanation: Factor: t(t^4 - 1) = t(t^2 - 1)(t^2 + 1) = t(t - 1)(t + 1)(t^2 + 1). Real roots are 0, 1, and -1.

  Q23. An engineer models vibration as V(x) = x^4 - 13x^2 + 36. What are the real values of x where vibration is zero?
    [*] x = 2, -2, 3, -3
    [ ] x = 6, -6
    [ ] x = 4, 9
    [ ] x = 2, 3
    Explanation: Let u = x^2: u^2 - 13u + 36 = (u - 4)(u - 9) = 0. So x^2 = 4 or x^2 = 9, giving x = +-2 and x = +-3.

  Q24. A drug's concentration is modeled by C(t) = t^3 - 9t^2 + 26t - 24 mg/L, where t is hours. Using the Rational Root Theorem, find one root.
    [ ] t = 12
    [ ] t = 5
    [ ] t = 7
    [*] t = 2
    Explanation: C(2) = 8 - 36 + 52 - 24 = 0. So t = 2 is a root. Factoring gives (t-2)(t-3)(t-4), so all roots are 2, 3, and 4.

  Q25. A storage tank's volume function is V(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 - 4x + 12 cubic meters, where x is a dimension in meters. Factor this polynomial completely.
    [*] (x - 3)(x - 2)(x + 2)
    [ ] (x - 6)(x - 2)
    [ ] (x + 3)(x - 2)(x + 2)
    [ ] (x - 3)(x + 2)^2
    Explanation: V(3) = 27 - 27 - 12 + 12 = 0. Dividing by (x - 3): x^2 - 4 = (x - 2)(x + 2). So V(x) = (x - 3)(x - 2)(x + 2).

  Q26. A projectile's path is modeled by h(t) = -t^4 + 5t^2 - 4. When does the projectile hit the ground (h = 0)?
    [*] t = 1, -1, 2, -2
    [ ] t = 0 and 5
    [ ] t = 4 and 1
    [ ] t = 1 and 2
    Explanation: -(t^4 - 5t^2 + 4) = -(t^2 - 1)(t^2 - 4) = -(t-1)(t+1)(t-2)(t+2) = 0. The zeros are t = +-1 and +-2.

  Q27. A financial model uses P(x) = x^3 - x^2 - 4x + 4. An analyst tests x = 1. Is it a root, and what are all the roots?
    [ ] Yes; roots are 1, -1, 4
    [ ] Yes; roots are 1 and 4
    [*] Yes; roots are 1, 2, -2
    [ ] No; x = 1 is not a root
    Explanation: P(1) = 1 - 1 - 4 + 4 = 0. Factoring: (x-1)(x^2-4) = (x-1)(x-2)(x+2). Roots are 1, 2, and -2.

  Q28. A bridge load function is L(x) = x^4 - 17x^2 + 16. What are the real roots?
    [ ] x = 1, 16
    [ ] x = 2, 8
    [*] x = 1, -1, 4, -4
    [ ] x = 4, -4
    Explanation: Let u = x^2: u^2 - 17u + 16 = (u - 1)(u - 16) = 0. So x^2 = 1 or x^2 = 16, giving x = +-1 and x = +-4.

  Q29. A chemical process is modeled by C(t) = t^3 + 6t^2 + 11t + 6. Using rational root testing, find all roots.
    [*] t = -1, -2, -3
    [ ] t = -1, -6
    [ ] t = 1, 2, 3
    [ ] t = -2, -3
    Explanation: C(-1) = -1 + 6 - 11 + 6 = 0. Dividing: (t + 1)(t^2 + 5t + 6) = (t + 1)(t + 2)(t + 3). Roots are -1, -2, -3.

  Q30. An architect models a curved roof as f(x) = x^4 - 10x^2 + 9. Where does the roof meet the base (f = 0)?
    [ ] x = 9, -9

    Explanation: Factor: (x^2 - 1)(x^2 - 9) = (x-1)(x+1)(x-3)(x+3) = 0. The roof meets the base at x = +-1 and x = +-3.

  1. Q: Rational Root Theorem: possible rational roots of 2x³ - 3x + 1?
     A: ±1, ±1/2. Factors of constant 1: ±1. Factors of leading 2: ±1, ±2.

  2. Q: Test x = 1/2 in 2x³ + x² - 5x + 2
     A: f(1/2) = 1/4 + 1/4 - 5/2 + 2 = 0. Yes, x = 1/2 is a root.

  3. Q: Descartes' Rule of Signs: x³ - 2x² + x - 1, positive roots?
     A: 3 or 1 positive roots. Three sign changes: +x³ to -2x² to +x to -1.

  4. Q: Number of possible negative roots using Descartes' Rule: f(-x)?
     A: Count sign changes in f(-x). (-x)³ - 2(-x)² + (-x) - 1 = -x³ - 2x² - x - 1: 0 sign changes = 0 negative roots.

  5. Q: Test p/q = -3/2 in 2x³ + 5x² - 9x - 18
     A: f(-3/2) = -27/4 + 45/4 + 27/2 - 18 = ... (calculate).

  6. Q: If x³ - 5x² + 8x - 4 has factor (x - 1), other factors?
     A: (x - 1)² (x - 4). (x-1) has multiplicity 2.

  7. Q: Bounds for real roots: if f(1) > 0 and f(2) < 0
     A: Root between 1 and 2 (Intermediate Value Theorem).
