--- Lesson 6.1: Arithmetic Sequences (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Arithmetic Sequences
Arithmetic Sequence: Sequence with constant difference d between consecutive terms. a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d. Example: 2, 5, 8, 11, 14... has a_1 = 2, d = 3. The common difference can be negative or zero.

Finding Terms: Once a_1 and d known, any term can be found: a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d. To find d: d = (a_m - a_k)/(m - k). To insert arithmetic means between two terms, find common difference and generate intermediate terms.

Sum of Arithmetic Sequence: S_n = (n/2)(a_1 + a_n) = (n/2)(2a_1 + (n-1)d). Gauss's formula: sum of first n positive integers is n(n+1)/2. Sum is always the average of first and last term, times the count.

Applications: Linear patterns in data. Counting: total items in stepped arrangement. Time intervals: events at regular intervals. Savings plans: deposit fixed amount each period.

--- Lesson 6.2: Geometric Sequences (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Geometric Sequences
Geometric Sequence: Sequence with constant ratio r between consecutive terms. a_n = a_1 · r^{n-1}. Example: 2, 6, 18, 54... has a_1 = 2, r = 3. Each term is r times the previous term.

Finding Terms: Given a_1 and r, find any term using a_n = a_1 · r^{n-1}. Common ratio: r = a_{n+1}/a_n. Handle negative ratios (alternating sign sequences). To insert geometric means between two terms, compute appropriate r and generate intermediates.

Sum of Geometric Sequence: If r ≠ 1: S_n = a_1(1 - r^n)/(1 - r) = a_1(r^n - 1)/(r - 1). If r = 1: S_n = na_1. The formula works even with negative r.

Applications: Exponential growth/decay (bacteria, investments). Fractals and self-similar structures. Bouncing ball problems (heights form geometric sequence). Depreciation: asset value decreases by constant percentage.

--- Lesson 6.3: Series & Summation Notation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Series & Summation Notation
Series: Sum of terms in a sequence. Arithmetic series: a_1 + a_2 + ... + a_n. Geometric series: a_1 + a_1r + a_1r^2 + ... + a_1r^{n-1}. Infinite series: sum of infinitely many terms (may or may not converge).

Summation Notation: Σ symbol denotes sum. Σ_{i=1}^{n} a_i means a_1 + a_2 + ... + a_n. Index i is the summation variable. Lower limit (below Σ) and upper limit (above Σ) define the range. Rules: Σ c·a_i = cΣ a_i, Σ(a_i + b_i) = Σ a_i + Σ b_i.

Special Sums: Σ_{i=1}^{n} 1 = n, Σ_{i=1}^{n} i = n(n+1)/2, Σ_{i=1}^{n} i^2 = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6, Σ_{i=1}^{n} i^3 = [n(n+1)/2]^2. These formulas allow closed forms for many series.

Telescoping Series: Series where consecutive terms cancel: Σ (a_i - a_{i+1}) = a_1 - a_{n+1}. Useful for partial fractions decomposition.

--- Lesson 6.5: Applications & Mathematical Induction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications & Mathematical Induction (AP Prep)
Practical Applications: Loan amortization uses series formulas. Monthly payment computed using present value of annuity. Stock dividend accumulation. Pendulum oscillation amplitudes (geometric decay). Recursive sequences define many real systems.

Mathematical Induction: Proof technique for statements about all positive integers. Two steps: (1) Base case: prove statement true for n = 1. (2) Inductive step: assume true for n = k, prove it's true for n = k+1. Proves infinitely many cases with finite work.

Induction Examples: Prove Σ_{i=1}^{n} i = n(n+1)/2. Prove geometric sequence formula a_n = a_1 · r^{n-1}. Prove statements about divisibility: 3^n - 1 is always divisible by 2.

AP Connections: Mathematical induction foundation for many calculus proofs and theorem verification. Recursive functions and sequences central to analysis. The principle of induction connects to the completeness axiom in real number system, essential for rigorous calculus development.

--- Lesson 6.5: Applications & Mathematical Induction (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Mathematical induction: prove n! > 2ⁿ for n ≥ 5
     A: Base: 5! = 120 > 32. Step: if k!>2^k, then (k+1)! > 2^(k+1).

  2. Q: Induction steps?
     A: 1) Base case (n=1). 2) Inductive step: assume true for k, prove k+1.

  3. Q: Prove Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (2i-1) = n²
     A: Base: 1 = 1². Step: if k², then k² + 2(k+1)-1 = (k+1)².

  4. Q: Annuity future value?
     A: FV = PMT·((1+r)ⁿ - 1)/r. Regular deposits earning interest.

  5. Q: Annuity present value?
     A: PV = PMT·(1 - (1+r)^(-n))/r. Lump sum for sequence.

  6. Q: Simple vs compound interest?
     A: Simple: I = Prt once. Compound: A = P(1+r/n)^(nt) repeated.

  7. Q: Loan amortization: equal payments over time?
     A: Uses PV annuity formula. Payment = PV·r/[1-(1+r)^(-n)].

  8. Q: Sinking fund: future amount goal, regular deposits?
     A: Uses FV annuity formula backward.

  9. Q: Prove: 1 + r + r² + ... + r^(n-1) = (r^n - 1)/(r - 1)?
     A: Geometric series sum formula.

  10. Q: Investment doubling formula: t = ln(2)/r?
     A: True for continuous A = Pe^(rt). t = ln(2)/r years.
