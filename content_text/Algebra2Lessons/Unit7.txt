--- Lesson 7.1: Counting Principles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Counting Principles
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 0! equal to?
By definition, 0! = 1. This is necessary for consistency in combinatorial formulas.
What is the Fundamental Counting Principle?
The Fundamental Counting Principle states that if event A has m outcomes and event B has n outcomes, the total number of combined outcomes is m × n.
What is a 'tree diagram' used for in counting?
A tree diagram shows branching paths for each stage of an experiment, making it easy to list and count all possible outcomes.
What is the Addition Principle in counting?
The Addition Principle says that if events A and B cannot happen simultaneously (mutually exclusive), the number of ways for A or B is |A| + |B|.
Real-World Applications
Example: A car comes in 6 colors, 3 engine types, and 2 transmission types. A customer can also choose one of 4 interior packages. How many unique car configurations exist?
6 × 3 × 2 × 4 = 144 unique configurations.

--- Lesson 7.2: Permutations & Combinations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Permutations & Combinations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a permutation?
A permutation is an ordered arrangement. The order of selection matters, so AB is different from BA.
What is a combination?
A combination is a selection where order doesn't matter. Choosing {A, B, C} is the same as {C, B, A}.
What is the formula for P(n, r)?
P(n,r) = n!/(n−r)! counts ordered arrangements of r items chosen from n.
What is the formula for C(n, r)?
C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!) counts unordered selections of r items from n. It equals P(n,r)/r!.
What is a 'permutation with repetition'?
When some items are identical (repeated), the number of distinguishable arrangements is n!/(n₁! · n₂! · ...) where nᵢ counts each group of identical items.
What is the relationship between P(n, r) and C(n, r)?
C(n,r) = P(n,r)/r! because combinations remove the r! orderings of each selection that permutations count separately.
What is a 'circular permutation'?
In circular permutations, arrangements that differ only by rotation are identical. The number of circular permutations of n items is (n−1)!.

--- Lesson 7.3: Probability Basics & Events (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability Basics & Events
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A standard die is rolled. What is P(even)?
Even outcomes: {2, 4, 6} = 3 out of 6. P(even) = 3/6 = 1/2.
A card is drawn from a standard deck. What is P(heart)?
There are 13 hearts in 52 cards. P(heart) = 13/52 = 1/4.
What is P(A or B) if A and B are mutually exclusive with P(A) = 0.3 and P(B) = 0.4?
For mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) = 0.3 + 0.4 = 0.7.
If P(A) = 0.6, what is P(not A)?
P(not A) = 1 − P(A) = 1 − 0.6 = 0.4.
Two dice are rolled. What is the probability of getting a sum of 7?
There are 6 outcomes summing to 7: (1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1). P = 6/36 = 1/6.
A bag has 5 red and 3 blue marbles. One marble is drawn. What is P(red)?
P(red) = 5/(5+3) = 5/8.
What is the probability of drawing an ace from a standard 52-card deck?
There are 4 aces in 52 cards: P(ace) = 4/52 = 1/13.
A spinner has 4 equal sections: red, blue, green, yellow. What is P(not green)?
P(not green) = 1 − P(green) = 1 − 1/4 = 3/4.
What is 'probability' of an event?
Probability is a number between 0 and 1 (inclusive) that measures the likelihood of an event. P = 0 means impossible; P = 1 means certain.
What is the 'Addition Rule' for probability?
The Addition Rule accounts for overlap: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B). For mutually exclusive events, P(A and B) = 0.
What is an 'event' in probability?
An event is any subset of the sample space. For example, 'rolling an even number' is the event {2, 4, 6} from rolling a die.
What is a 'simple event'?
A simple event has a single outcome, such as rolling a 3 on a die. A compound event has multiple outcomes.
A quality inspector checks products from a factory. Out of 500 items, 15 are defective. If one item is randomly selected, what is the probability it is defective?
P(defective) = 15/500 = 3/100 = 0.03.
A weather forecast says there is a 30% chance of rain. What is the probability it does NOT rain?
P(no rain) = 1 − P(rain) = 1 − 0.30 = 0.70 = 70%.
In a class of 30 students, 12 play soccer and 10 play basketball. If 4 play both, what is the probability a randomly chosen student plays soccer or basketball?
P(S or B) = (12 + 10 − 4)/30 = 18/30 = 3/5.
A bag contains 10 marbles: 4 red, 3 blue, 2 green, 1 yellow. If two marbles are drawn without replacement, what is the probability both are red?
P(both red) = (4/10)(3/9) = 12/90 = 2/15.
A medical test is positive for 95% of people who have a disease and for 3% of people who don't (false positive). If 1% of the population has the disease, what is the probability a random person tests positive?
P(+) = P(+|disease)·P(disease) + P(+|no disease)·P(no disease) = (0.95)(0.01) + (0.03)(0.99) = 0.0095 + 0.0297 = 0.0392.
A game involves rolling two dice. You win if the sum is 7 or 11. What is the probability of winning on a single roll?
Sum of 7: 6 ways. Sum of 11: 2 ways. Events are mutually exclusive. P = (6+2)/36 = 8/36 = 2/9.
Three coins are flipped. What is the probability of getting exactly 2 heads?
Total outcomes = 2³ = 8. Exactly 2 heads: {HHT, HTH, THH} = 3 outcomes. P = 3/8.
A survey shows 45% of people prefer coffee, 35% prefer tea, and 20% prefer juice. If two people are chosen independently, what is the probability both prefer coffee?
Since the choices are independent: P(both coffee) = 0.45 × 0.45 = 0.2025.
A student takes a 4-question multiple choice test (each with 4 options). If the student guesses randomly on every question, what is the probability of getting all 4 correct?
P(all correct) = (1/4)⁴ = 1/256.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student takes a 4-question multiple choice test (each with 4 options). If the student guesses randomly on every question, what is the probability of getting all 4 correct?
P(all correct) = (1/4)⁴ = 1/256.

--- Lesson 7.4: Conditional Probability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conditional Probability
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A bag has 6 red and 4 blue balls. Two are drawn without replacement. What is P(2nd is red | 1st is red)?
After drawing 1 red, 5 red remain out of 9 total. P = 5/9.
A coin is flipped twice. What is P(2nd is heads | 1st is heads)?
Coin flips are independent, so P(2nd H | 1st H) = P(H) = 1/2.
In a class, 40% are female and 25% of females earn an A. What is P(female AND earns A)?
P(F and A) = P(F) · P(A|F) = 0.40 × 0.25 = 0.10.
Two cards are drawn without replacement from a standard deck. What is the probability both are kings?
P = (4/52)(3/51) = 12/2652 = 1/221.
A fair die is rolled. Given that the result is at least 3, what is P(5)?
Given ≥ 3 means sample space is {3,4,5,6}. P(5 | ≥3) = 1/4.
What is 'conditional probability'?
Conditional probability P(B|A) is the probability of event B given that event A has occurred. It narrows the sample space to only outcomes in A.
What is the 'Multiplication Rule' for probability?
The Multiplication Rule states P(A and B) = P(A) · P(B|A). For independent events, this simplifies to P(A) · P(B).
What is 'Bayes' Theorem' used for?
Bayes' Theorem lets you compute P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A)/P(B), reversing the conditioning.
What is a 'two-way table' used for in conditional probability?
A two-way table displays frequencies for two categories, making it easy to read joint probabilities P(A and B) and compute conditional probabilities.
What is the 'Law of Total Probability'?
The Law of Total Probability computes P(B) by summing over all mutually exclusive scenarios that cover the sample space.
What is a 'tree diagram' in the context of conditional probability?
A probability tree diagram branches at each stage, labeling branches with conditional probabilities. Multiplying along a path gives the joint probability.
A company has two factories. Factory A produces 60% of items (with 5% defect rate) and Factory B produces 40% (with 3% defect rate). If a defective item is found, what is the probability it came from Factory A?
P(defective) = 0.6(0.05) + 0.4(0.03) = 0.042. P(A|def) = 0.03/0.042 = 5/7 ≈ 0.714 by Bayes' Theorem.
A school reports that 70% of students pass math. Of those who pass math, 80% pass science. What is the probability a student passes both math and science?
P(M and S) = P(M) · P(S|M) = 0.70 × 0.80 = 0.56.
An insurance company knows that 20% of drivers are high-risk. High-risk drivers have a 40% claim rate; low-risk drivers have a 10% claim rate. What is the probability a randomly selected driver will file a claim?
P(claim) = 0.20(0.40) + 0.80(0.10) = 0.08 + 0.08 = 0.16.
In a drawer with 8 black socks and 6 white socks, two socks are drawn without replacement. What is the probability of getting a matching pair (both same color)?
P(both black) = (8/14)(7/13) = 56/182. P(both white) = (6/14)(5/13) = 30/182. P(match) = 86/182 = 43/91.
A test is 99% accurate for detecting a disease that affects 0.1% of the population. If a person tests positive, what is the approximate probability they actually have the disease?
P(+) = 0.001(0.99) + 0.999(0.01) = 0.00099 + 0.00999 = 0.01098. P(disease|+) = 0.00099/0.01098 ≈ 0.09 = 9%.
A jar has 5 red and 7 green marbles. Three marbles are drawn without replacement. What is the probability all three are green?
P = (7/12)(6/11)(5/10) = 210/1320 = 7/44.
A biased coin lands heads 60% of the time. If it is flipped 3 times, what is the probability of getting heads on the first flip and tails on the second and third?
P(H,T,T) = 0.6 × 0.4 × 0.4 = 0.096 (independent flips).
In a survey, 50% of respondents like brand A, 30% like brand B, and 10% like both. Given that a person likes brand A, what is the probability they also like brand B?
P(B|A) = P(A and B)/P(A) = 0.10/0.50 = 0.20 = 1/5.
Two machines produce parts. Machine 1 produces 70% of parts with a 2% defect rate. Machine 2 produces 30% with a 5% defect rate. A defective part is found. What is the probability it came from Machine 2?
P(def) = 0.70(0.02) + 0.30(0.05) = 0.014 + 0.015 = 0.029. P(M2|def) = 0.015/0.029 = 15/29 ≈ 0.517.
A box has 3 gold coins and 2 silver coins. You draw two coins without replacement. Given the first coin is gold, what is the probability the second is also gold?
After drawing 1 gold, 2 gold remain out of 4 coins. P(2nd gold | 1st gold) = 2/4 = 1/2.
Real-World Applications

The Multiplication Rule states P(A and B) = P(A) · P(B|A). For independent events, this simplifies to P(A) · P(B).

--- Lesson 7.6: Hypothesis Testing & Confidence Intervals (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hypothesis Testing & Confidence Intervals
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A sample mean is 50 with a 95% CI of (48, 52). What is the margin of error?
Margin of error = (upper − lower)/2 = (52 − 48)/2 = 2.
A sample of 64 has mean 82 and σ = 16. What is the 95% confidence interval for the population mean?
E = 1.96(16/√64) = 1.96(2) = 3.92. CI = (82 − 3.92, 82 + 3.92) = (78.08, 85.92).
What is a 'confidence interval'?
A confidence interval gives a range (lower bound, upper bound) that is likely to contain the true population parameter at a given confidence level.
What is a 'null hypothesis' (H₀)?
The null hypothesis is the starting assumption of no effect or no difference. We test whether data provides enough evidence to reject it.
What is a 'p-value'?
The p-value measures how surprising the observed data is under H₀. A small p-value suggests the data is unlikely if H₀ were true.
What is a 'Type II error'?
A Type II error (false negative) occurs when we fail to detect a real effect — we don't reject H₀ even though it's false.
What is the 'alternative hypothesis' (H₁ or Hₐ)?
The alternative hypothesis is what we conclude if we reject H₀. It states there is a real effect, difference, or relationship.
A pharmaceutical company tests a new drug. H₀: The drug has no effect. After testing 500 patients, p-value = 0.002. At α = 0.05, what is the conclusion?
p-value (0.002) < α (0.05), so we reject H₀. There is statistically significant evidence the drug has an effect.
A study found that students who ate breakfast scored 5 points higher on a test (p-value = 0.04). A skeptic says the sample was only 20 students. What is the main concern?
With n = 20, the margin of error is large and the result may not replicate. Small samples increase uncertainty even when p < α.
Real-World Applications
Example: A school administrator claims the average test score is 75. A sample of 36 students has a mean of 78 with σ = 12. Is there evidence to reject the claim at α = 0.05?
z = (78−75)/(12/√36) = 3/2 = 1.5. For a two-tailed test, p ≈ 0.134 > 0.05, so we fail to reject H₀.
Example: A city claims average commute time is 30 minutes. A sample of 100 commuters has mean 32 min with σ = 8 min. At α = 0.01, is the claim rejected?
z = (32−30)/(8/10) = 2.5. Two-tailed p ≈ 0.012 > 0.01. We barely fail to reject at the stricter α = 0.01 level.
Example: A study found that students who ate breakfast scored 5 points higher on a test (p-value = 0.04). A skeptic says the sample was only 20 students. What is the main concern?
With n = 20, the margin of error is large and the result may not replicate. Small samples increase uncertainty even when p < α.
Example: An energy company claims its wind turbines produce an average of 2 MW. A sample of 25 turbines averages 1.85 MW with s = 0.3 MW. At α = 0.05, does the sample contradict the claim?
z = (1.85 − 2)/(0.3/√25) = −0.15/0.06 = −2.5. The two-tailed p ≈ 0.012 < 0.05, so we reject H₀.

--- Lesson 7.7: Correlation & Linear Regression (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Correlation & Linear Regression
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the range of the correlation coefficient r?
The correlation coefficient r always falls between −1 and 1 inclusive.
What is 'correlation'?
Correlation measures how strongly and in what direction two variables are linearly associated, quantified by the correlation coefficient r.
What is the 'correlation coefficient' (r)?
r ranges from −1 (perfect negative) to 1 (perfect positive). r = 0 means no linear correlation.
What is 'linear regression'?
Linear regression finds the equation ŷ = mx + b of the line that best fits the data by minimizing the sum of squared residuals.
What is a 'residual' in regression?
A residual measures the vertical distance between an actual data point and the regression line's predicted value.
What is an 'outlier' in the context of regression?
An outlier is a data point that deviates significantly from the trend. It can disproportionately affect the slope and intercept of the regression line.

Hot weather is a confounding variable that increases both ice cream sales and swimming (and thus drowning risk). Correlation does not imply causation.

ŷ = 3,000(10) + 35,000 = 30,000 + 35,000 = $65,000.
A data set has r = −0.15. What is the best interpretation?
r = −0.15 is close to 0, indicating a very weak negative linear association between the variables.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
