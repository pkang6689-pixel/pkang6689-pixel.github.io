--- Lesson 3.1: Muscle Tissue Types (Skeletal, Cardiac, Smooth) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Muscle Tissue Types (Skeletal, Cardiac, Smooth)
Three Types of Muscle Tissue  • Skeletal muscle: Voluntary, striated, multinucleated; attached to bones for movement.  • Cardiac muscle: Involuntary, striated, branching cells with intercalated discs; found only in the heart.  • Smooth muscle: Involuntary, nonstriated, spindle-shaped; found in walls of hollow organs (GI tract, blood vessels, bladder).Key Differences
Skeletal is the only type under conscious control. Cardiac muscle is self-exciting (autorhythmicity). Smooth muscle sustains long, slow contractions.

--- Lesson 3.2: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology
Skeletal Muscle AnatomyOrganizational Hierarchy  • Muscle → Fascicle → Muscle fiber (cell) → Myofibril → Sarcomere  • Each muscle fiber is a single multinucleated cell wrapped in sarcolemma (cell membrane).  • Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) stores Ca²⁺ ions.Connective Tissue Wrappings  • Endomysium: Around each fiber. Perimysium: Around each fascicle. Epimysium: Around the entire muscle.Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)
Motor neuron meets muscle fiber → acetylcholine (ACh) released → triggers contraction.

--- Lesson 3.3: Sliding Filament Theory (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sliding Filament Theory
The Sliding Filament Theory
Muscle contraction occurs when thin filaments (actin) slide over thick filaments (myosin), shortening the sarcomere.
Steps of the Cross-Bridge Cycle  • 1. Ca²⁺ released from SR binds to troponin → tropomyosin shifts → exposes binding sites on actin.  • 2. Myosin head (already energized by ATP hydrolysis) binds actin → cross-bridge forms.  • 3. Power stroke: myosin head pivots, pulling actin toward the center (ADP + Pᵢ released).  • 4. New ATP binds myosin → detachment from actin.  • 5. ATP hydrolyzed → myosin re-cocks. Cycle repeats as long as Ca²⁺ is present.

--- Lesson 3.3: Sliding Filament Theory (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The sliding filament theory states that:
    [ ] Filaments shorten
    [*] Thin filaments slide over thick filaments, shortening the sarcomere
    [ ] Muscles grow

    Explanation: Filaments slide; they don't shorten themselves.

    [*] Myosin
    [ ] Tropomyosin
    Explanation: Myosin = thick filament.

  Q3. Thin filaments are primarily:
    [ ] Myosin
    [*] Actin (with troponin and tropomyosin)
    [ ] Collagen
    [ ] Elastin
    Explanation: Actin + regulatory proteins.

  Q4. Contraction begins when Ca²⁺ binds to:
    [ ] Myosin
    [ ] Actin directly
    [*] Troponin
    [ ] Tropomyosin
    Explanation: Ca²⁺ → troponin → conformational change.

  Q5. Troponin-Ca²⁺ binding causes:
    [ ] Actin to shorten
    [ ] Myosin to dissolve
    [*] Tropomyosin to shift, exposing actin binding sites
    [ ] ATP production
    Explanation: Removing the tropomyosin block.

  Q6. The cross-bridge forms when:
    [ ] ATP binds myosin
    [ ] Ca²⁺ leaves the fiber
    [*] Myosin head binds to the exposed actin site
    [ ] Tropomyosin covers actin
    Explanation: Cross-bridge = myosin-actin attachment.

  Q7. The power stroke is powered by:
    [ ] New ATP binding
    [ ] Calcium directly
    [*] Energy stored in the myosin head (from prior ATP hydrolysis)
    [ ] Sodium ions
    Explanation: ATP was already hydrolyzed to 'cock' the head.

  Q8. During the power stroke, _____ are released.
    [ ] ATP
    [ ] Ca²⁺
    [*] ADP and Pᵢ (inorganic phosphate)
    [ ] Troponin
    Explanation: Products of the previous ATP hydrolysis.

  Q9. Detachment of myosin from actin requires:
    [ ] Ca²⁺
    [ ] ADP
    [*] A new ATP molecule binding to the myosin head
    [ ] Nothing

  Q10. Rigor mortis occurs because:
    [ ] Muscles relax after death
    [*] No ATP is available to detach myosin from actin → muscles stiffen
    [ ] Ca²⁺ is absent
    [ ] Muscles contract faster
    Explanation: ATP depletion → permanent cross-bridges.

  Q11. Ca²⁺ is released from the:
    [ ] Mitochondria
    [ ] Nucleus
    [*] Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR)
    [ ] Golgi apparatus
    Explanation: SR stores and releases Ca²⁺.

  Q12. Ca²⁺ is pumped back into the SR by:
    [ ] Passive diffusion
    [ ] Myosin
    [*] Ca²⁺-ATPase pumps (requires ATP)
    [ ] Gravity
    Explanation: Active transport returns Ca²⁺ → relaxation.

  Q13. When Ca²⁺ is pumped back, the muscle:
    [ ] Contracts harder
    [*] Relaxes (tropomyosin re-covers actin binding sites)
    [ ] Dies
    [ ] Stays contracted
    Explanation: Removing Ca²⁺ ends contraction.

  Q14. ATP is needed for:
    [ ] Contraction only
    [ ] Relaxation only
    [*] Both contraction (cocking myosin) and relaxation (detachment and Ca²⁺ removal)
    [ ] Neither
    Explanation: ATP powers multiple steps.

  Q15. The cross-bridge cycle repeats as long as:
    [ ] Myosin is present
    [ ] ATP runs out
    [*] Ca²⁺ is available and ATP is supplied
    [ ] The nerve fires once
    Explanation: Sustained Ca²⁺ and ATP keep the cycle going.

  Q16. How many times can a myosin head cycle per second?
    [ ] Once
    [*] About 5 times
    [ ] 100 times
    [ ] 1,000 times
    Explanation: Approximately 5 cycles per second during contraction.

  Q17. The sarcomere shortens because:
    [ ] I bands expand
    [ ] A bands lengthen
    [*] Z lines are pulled closer together as actin slides inward
    [ ] Myosin shortens
    Explanation: Actin slides → Z lines approach each other.

  Q18. During maximal contraction, the H zone:
    [ ] Widens
    [ ] Stays the same
    [*] Disappears (actin filaments overlap in the center)
    [ ] Doubles
    Explanation: Thin filaments completely overlap the thick.

  Q19. A muscle cramp may result from:
    [ ] Too much ATP
    [*] Failure to pump Ca²⁺ back into the SR (sustained contraction)
    [ ] Too much relaxation
    [ ] No nerve signal
    Explanation: Prolonged Ca²⁺ in cytoplasm → continuous contraction.

  Q20. Understanding the sliding filament theory is key to:
    [ ] Only anatomy class
    [*] Understanding muscle diseases, pharmacology, exercise physiology, and cardiac function
    [ ] Nothing practical
    [ ] Only sports
    Explanation: Foundation for all clinical muscle science.

--- Lesson 3.4: Muscle Contraction & Energy Use (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Muscle Contraction & Energy Use
Muscle Contraction & Energy SystemsTypes of Contraction  • Isotonic: Muscle changes length (concentric = shortening, eccentric = lengthening).  • Isometric: Muscle generates force without changing length (holding a weight still).Energy Sources  • Immediate: ATP (2–3 sec) → Creatine phosphate (5–8 sec).  • Short-term: Anaerobic glycolysis (30–60 sec) — glucose → lactic acid + 2 ATP.  • Long-term: Aerobic (oxidative) metabolism — glucose/fat + O₂ → 36 ATP (sustains long activity).Muscle Fatigue
Decline in force due to ATP depletion, lactic acid buildup, and electrolyte imbalance.

  Q20. Understanding energy metabolism is important for:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Exercise science, treating metabolic disorders, and nutrition
    [ ] Only athletes
    [ ] Only diabetics
    Explanation: Energy systems underpin all muscle function and clinical conditions.

--- Lesson 3.5: Major Muscle Groups (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Major Muscle Groups
Major Muscle GroupsUpper Body  • Deltoid: Shoulder abduction. Biceps brachii: Elbow flexion/forearm supination. Triceps brachii: Elbow extension.  • Pectoralis major: Arm flexion/adduction/medial rotation. Latissimus dorsi: Arm extension/adduction.Core  • Rectus abdominis: Trunk flexion. External/internal obliques: Trunk rotation.  • Erector spinae: Back extension and posture.Lower Body  • Quadriceps femoris: Knee extension. Hamstrings: Knee flexion/hip extension. Gluteus maximus: Hip extension. Gastrocnemius: Plantar flexion (calf).

--- Lesson 3.6: Disorders of the Muscular System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Muscular System
Disorders of the Muscular System  • Muscular dystrophy (MD): Genetic; progressive muscle degeneration. Duchenne MD is the most common childhood form (dystrophin gene mutation).  • Myasthenia gravis: Autoimmune; antibodies attack ACh receptors at the NMJ → progressive weakness.  • Fibromyalgia: Chronic widespread muscle pain; cause unknown.  • Rhabdomyolysis: Rapid muscle breakdown releasing myoglobin → kidney damage.  • Strains: Overstretched or torn muscle fibers (different from sprains, which involve ligaments).

--- Lesson 3.6: Disorders of the Muscular System (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Duchenne muscular dystrophy is caused by:
    [ ] Vitamin deficiency
    [ ] Infection
    [*] A genetic mutation in the dystrophin gene
    [ ] Overuse
    Explanation: X-linked recessive dystrophin gene mutation.

  Q2. Myasthenia gravis affects the:
    [ ] Bones
    [ ] Heart
    [*] Neuromuscular junction (ACh receptors)
    [ ] Intestines
    Explanation: Autoantibodies destroy ACh receptors.

  Q3. Myasthenia gravis is classified as a(n):
    [ ] Genetic disorder
    [ ] Infectious disease
    [*] Autoimmune disorder
    [ ] Nutritional deficiency
    Explanation: The immune system attacks self-tissues.

  Q4. Fibromyalgia is characterized by:
    [ ] Muscle wasting
    [*] Chronic widespread pain and fatigue
    [ ] Joint destruction
    [ ] Bone loss
    Explanation: Pain without identifiable tissue damage.

  Q5. Rhabdomyolysis can cause:
    [ ] Improved strength
    [*] Acute kidney failure (from myoglobin release)
    [ ] Better endurance
    [ ] No complications
    Explanation: Myoglobin clogs kidney tubules.

  Q6. A muscle strain involves:
    [ ] Bone fracture
    [ ] Ligament tear
    [*] Overstretched or torn muscle fibers/tendons
    [ ] Joint dislocation
    Explanation: Strain = muscle/tendon injury.

  Q7. Duchenne MD primarily affects:
    [ ] Girls
    [ ] Adults
    [*] Boys (X-linked recessive)
    [ ] The elderly
    Explanation: Males are primarily affected.

  Q8. A common symptom of muscular dystrophy is:
    [ ] Increased strength
    [*] Progressive muscle weakness and loss of function
    [ ] Better coordination
    [ ] No symptoms
    Explanation: Muscles gradually weaken over time.

  Q9. DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness) occurs:
    [ ] During exercise
    [*] 24-72 hours after unaccustomed or intense exercise
    [ ] Only in athletes
    [ ] Never
    Explanation: Microtrauma and inflammation cause delayed soreness.

  Q10. Tetanus (the disease) causes:
    [ ] Muscle wasting
    [*] Sustained muscle contraction/spasm (lockjaw)
    [ ] Muscle relaxation
    [ ] No effect
    Explanation: Clostridium tetani toxin prevents relaxation.

  Q11. A torn ACL is classified as a:
    [ ] Strain
    [*] Sprain (ligament injury, not muscle)
    [ ] Fracture
    [ ] Dislocation
    Explanation: ACL = ligament → sprain, not strain.

  Q12. Compartment syndrome involves:
    [ ] Bone fracture
    [*] Increased pressure in a muscle compartment cutting off blood flow
    [ ] Joint infection
    [ ] Nerve disease
    Explanation: Swelling within a fascial compartment → tissue ischemia.

  Q13. Treatment for mild strains includes:
    [ ] Surgery immediately
    [*] RICE: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation
    [ ] Vigorous exercise
    [ ] Apply heat immediately and resume activity
    Explanation: RICE protocol for acute muscle injuries.

  Q14. Botulinum toxin (Botox) works by:
    [ ] Stimulating ACh release
    [*] Blocking ACh release at the NMJ → muscle paralysis
    [ ] Strengthening muscles
    [ ] Producing ACh
    Explanation: Prevents neurotransmitter release → relaxation.

  Q15. Atrophy refers to:
    [ ] Muscle growth
    [*] Decrease in muscle size from disuse, denervation, or disease
    [ ] Normal tone
    [ ] Hypertrophy
    Explanation: Muscles shrink without stimulation.

  Q16. Hypertrophy refers to:
    [ ] Muscle shrinkage
    [*] Increase in muscle size (from exercise/use)
    [ ] Cell multiplication
    [ ] Fat gain
    Explanation: Individual fibers get larger with training.

  Q17. Cramps are involuntary:
    [ ] Relaxations
    [*] Sustained, painful contractions
    [ ] Bone movements
    [ ] Nerve signals only
    Explanation: Involuntary tetanic-like contraction.

  Q18. Multiple sclerosis (MS) causes muscle weakness because:
    [ ] Muscles are diseased
    [*] The immune system destroys myelin on motor nerves → impaired signal transmission
    [ ] Bones weaken
    [ ] The heart fails
    Explanation: Demyelination slows or blocks nerve impulses.

  Q19. Prevention of muscle injuries includes:
    [ ] No warm-up
    [*] Proper warm-up, stretching, gradual progression, and adequate hydration
    [ ] Maximal effort immediately
    [ ] Skipping rest days
    Explanation: Preparation reduces injury risk.

  Q20. Understanding muscular disorders helps in:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and prevention
    [ ] Only surgery
    [ ] Only research
    Explanation: Clinical knowledge for comprehensive patient care.

--- Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Sports Medicine (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Sports Medicine
Case Studies in Sports MedicineCase 1: ACL Tear
A basketball player lands awkwardly → knee gives out. Diagnosis: Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tear. Treatment: surgery (graft) + physical therapy rehabilitation.
Case 2: Rotator Cuff Injury
A swimmer experiences shoulder pain with overhead motion. Rotator cuff muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) stabilize the shoulder.
Case 3: Achilles Tendon Rupture
Sudden calf pain during sprinting → unable to plantar flex. The Achilles tendon (calcaneal tendon) connects gastrocnemius/soleus to the calcaneus.

    [*] Anterior (forward) translation of the tibia relative to the femur

--- Lesson 3.8: AP Prep: Muscle Physiology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Muscle Physiology
AP Prep: Muscle Physiology
High-yield review of concepts commonly tested on AP and standardized anatomy exams.
Must-Know Concepts  • Sliding filament mechanism and the role of Ca²⁺, ATP, actin, myosin.  • NMJ: ACh release → depolarization → Ca²⁺ release from SR → contraction.  • Muscle fiber types: Type I (slow oxidative) vs. Type IIa/IIb (fast glycolytic).  • Length-tension relationship: maximal force at optimal sarcomere overlap.  • Graded responses: recruitment, frequency summation, tetanus.

--- Lesson 3.8: AP Prep: Muscle Physiology (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The sequence of events at the NMJ is:
    [ ] Ca²⁺ → ACh → contraction → AP
    [*] AP arrives → ACh released → binds receptors → muscle AP → Ca²⁺ released → contraction
    [ ] Contraction → ACh → Ca²⁺
    [ ] Random
    Explanation: Standard NMJ → contraction chain.

  Q2. Ca²⁺ in muscle contraction is released from the:
    [ ] Mitochondria
    [ ] Nerve terminal
    [*] Sarcoplasmic reticulum
    [ ] T-tubules
    Explanation: SR is the Ca²⁺ store.

  Q3. The size principle of motor unit recruitment states:
    [ ] Large units fire first
    [*] Smaller motor units are recruited first; larger ones as more force is needed
    [ ] All fire simultaneously
    [ ] Random recruitment
    Explanation: Orderly recruitment from small to large.

  Q4. Tetanus (physiological) results from:
    [ ] Single stimuli
    [*] Rapid, repeated stimuli preventing relaxation between contractions
    [ ] Muscle disease
    [ ] Bacterial infection
    Explanation: Fused tetanus = smooth, sustained contraction.

  Q5. The length-tension relationship shows that maximum force occurs when:
    [*] Sarcomeres are at optimal actin-myosin overlap
    [ ] Muscle is fully stretched
    [ ] Muscle is completely contracted
    [ ] There is no overlap
    Explanation: Too stretched or too compressed = less force.

  Q6. Type I (slow-twitch) fibers are rich in:
    [ ] Glycogen only
    [*] Mitochondria and myoglobin (red fibers)
    [ ] Fast glycolytic enzymes
    [ ] No special features
    Explanation: Aerobic machinery for endurance.

  Q7. Type IIb fibers are best for:
    [ ] Marathons
    [ ] Posture
    [*] Short, explosive bursts of power
    [ ] Swimming long distances
    Explanation: Fast glycolytic fibers fatigue quickly.

  Q8. Wave summation occurs when a second stimulus arrives:
    [ ] During relaxation
    [*] Before the muscle fully relaxes from the first stimulus
    [ ] Hours later
    [ ] The next day
    Explanation: Overlapping twitches = increased force.

  Q9. Treppe (staircase effect) is:
    [ ] Muscle fatigue
    [*] Progressive increase in contraction force with repeated stimuli (warm-up)
    [ ] Immediate maximal force
    [ ] Relaxation
    Explanation: Successive twitches become stronger.

  Q10. Complete tetanus vs. incomplete tetanus:
    [ ] No difference
    [*] Complete = smooth sustained contraction; incomplete = partial relaxation between stimuli
    [ ] Complete is weaker
    [ ] Incomplete involves no contraction
    Explanation: Frequency determines which type.

  Q11. Muscle produces maximum force at:
    [ ] Full stretch
    [ ] Full compression
    [*] Optimal resting length (about 2.0-2.6 μm sarcomere length)
    [ ] Zero length
    Explanation: Where actin-myosin overlap is optimal.

  Q12. If a sarcomere is overstretched:
    [ ] Force increases
    [*] Force decreases because fewer cross-bridges can form
    [ ] No change
    [ ] Muscle tears
    Explanation: Less overlap = fewer cross-bridges.

  Q13. Acetylcholinesterase is important because it:
    [ ] Produces force
    [*] Quickly degrades ACh to prevent continuous stimulation
    [ ] Releases Ca²⁺
    [ ] Stores ATP
    Explanation: Without it, muscles would stay contracted.

  Q14. Curare blocks ACh receptors, causing:
    [ ] Increased contraction
    [*] Muscle paralysis (especially respiratory muscles)
    [ ] No effect
    [ ] Spasms
    Explanation: Competitive inhibitor at the NMJ.

  Q15. Succinylcholine causes:
    [ ] Permanent paralysis
    [*] Initial depolarization then desensitization (used in surgical anesthesia)
    [ ] No effect
    [ ] Increased strength
    Explanation: Depolarizing neuromuscular blocker.

  Q16. The power stroke is the _____ step of the cross-bridge cycle.
    [ ] First
    [ ] Second
    [*] Third (myosin pivots, pulling actin)
    [ ] Last
    Explanation: After attachment, the head pivots.

  Q17. During rigor mortis:
    [ ] Muscles are relaxed
    [*] Myosin stays bound to actin because no ATP is available to break the bond
    [ ] ATP is abundant
    [ ] Only smooth muscle is affected
    Explanation: ATP depletion → locked cross-bridges.

  Q18. AP exam free-response may ask you to:
    [ ] Only label diagrams
    [*] Explain the mechanism of contraction, energy systems, or neuromuscular signaling
    [ ] Only define terms
    [ ] Only list muscles
    Explanation: Expect mechanism-based questions.

  Q19. Integration question: If Ca²⁺ cannot be released from the SR, what happens?
    [ ] Normal contraction
    [*] No contraction (troponin remains blocking binding sites)
    [ ] Constant contraction
    [ ] Muscle death
    Explanation: Ca²⁺ is the trigger for exposing actin binding sites.

  Q20. Quick review: the correct order is:
    [ ] ACh → Ca²⁺ → cross-bridge → AP
    [*] Nerve AP → ACh → muscle AP → Ca²⁺ → cross-bridge → contraction
    [ ] Ca²⁺ → ACh → AP → contraction
    [ ] Cross-bridge → AP → ACh → Ca²⁺
    Explanation: Complete signaling pathway.
