--- Lesson 3.1: Muscle Tissue Types (Skeletal, Cardiac, Smooth) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Muscle Tissue Types (Skeletal, Cardiac, Smooth)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is autorhythmicity in the context of muscle tissue?
Autorhythmicity (also called automaticity) refers to the intrinsic ability of cardiac pacemaker cells to spontaneously depolarize and generate action potentials, allowing the heart to beat without nervous system input.
What is myology?
Myology derives from the Greek root 'myo-' meaning muscle and '-logy' meaning study of, making it the branch of anatomy dedicated to the scientific study of muscles.
What is a gap junction in cardiac muscle?
Gap junctions are specialized protein channels found within intercalated discs that create direct cytoplasmic connections between adjacent cardiac cells, enabling rapid electrical signal transmission so the heart contracts as a coordinated unit.
What is contractility?
Contractility is the defining property of muscle tissue, referring to its ability to actively shorten and generate pulling force, which no other tissue type can do.
What is elasticity as a functional property of muscle?
Elasticity is the ability of muscle to spring back to its original length after being stretched, much like a rubber band, which helps maintain normal muscle tone and readiness for contraction.
A pregnant woman is told that the smooth muscle of her uterus will contract during labor to deliver the baby. She asks why she cannot consciously control these contractions. What is the best explanation?
Smooth muscle is involuntary, meaning it operates under the control of the autonomic nervous system and hormones (such as oxytocin during labor) rather than conscious thought, which is why uterine contractions cannot be voluntarily started or stopped.
Real-World Applications
Example: Muscle tissue accounts for approximately what percentage of total body mass?
Skeletal muscle alone constitutes approximately 40% of body weight, and when cardiac and smooth muscle are included, muscle tissue represents about 40-50% of total body mass.
Example: A medical student is examining a tissue slide under the microscope and sees long, cylindrical cells with multiple nuclei pushed to the periphery and clear striations. Which type of muscle tissue is the student most likely observing?
Skeletal muscle is identified microscopically by its long, cylindrical fibers with multiple peripherally located nuclei and prominent cross-striations, distinguishing it from cardiac muscle (branching, central nuclei, intercalated discs) and smooth muscle (spindle-shaped, no striations).

--- Lesson 3.1: Muscle Tissue Types (Skeletal, Cardiac, Smooth) (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which muscle type is under voluntary (conscious) control?
    [ ] All three types are voluntary
    [ ] Cardiac muscle
    [ ] Smooth muscle
    [*] Skeletal muscle
    Explanation: Skeletal muscle is the only muscle type controlled by the somatic (voluntary) nervous system, allowing conscious decisions to initiate or stop movement.

  Q2. Cardiac muscle is found exclusively in the:
    [ ] Walls of blood vessels
    [*] Heart
    [ ] Walls of the intestines
    [ ] Biceps and triceps
    Explanation: Cardiac muscle tissue is found only in the myocardium (heart wall), where it pumps blood continuously throughout a person's lifetime.

  Q3. Smooth muscle is located in the walls of which structures?
    [ ] The heart only
    [*] Hollow organs such as the stomach, blood vessels, and bladder
    [ ] Bones and cartilage
    [ ] Skeletal attachments and joints
    Explanation: Smooth muscle lines the walls of hollow visceral organs including the GI tract, blood vessels, urinary bladder, and airways, controlling involuntary functions like peristalsis and vasoconstriction.

  Q4. Which two muscle types display visible striations under a microscope?
    [ ] None of the muscle types are striated
    [ ] Smooth and cardiac
    [*] Skeletal and cardiac
    [ ] Smooth and skeletal
    Explanation: Both skeletal and cardiac muscle cells contain highly organized sarcomeres with alternating actin and myosin filaments, creating the characteristic banded (striated) appearance.

  Q5. Intercalated discs are a unique structural feature of which muscle type?
    [ ] Skeletal muscle
    [ ] Smooth muscle
    [ ] All three muscle types
    [*] Cardiac muscle
    Explanation: Intercalated discs are specialized cell-to-cell junctions found only in cardiac muscle, containing gap junctions and desmosomes that allow synchronized contraction of the heart.

  Q6. Smooth muscle cells are best described as:
    [ ] Flat and disc-shaped with no nucleus
    [*] Spindle-shaped (fusiform) with a single central nucleus
    [ ] Long, cylindrical, and multinucleated
    [ ] Branching with one or two central nuclei
    Explanation: Smooth muscle cells are small, tapered at both ends (spindle-shaped), and each contains a single, centrally located nucleus with no visible striations.

  Q7. Skeletal muscle fibers are characterized by being:
    [*] Long, cylindrical, and multinucleated
    [ ] Small and round with one nucleus
    [ ] Branching with intercalated discs
    [ ] Spindle-shaped and involuntary
    Explanation: Skeletal muscle fibers are among the largest cells in the body, being very long and cylindrical with multiple peripherally located nuclei resulting from the fusion of many myoblasts during development.

  Q8. Which muscle type contracts most slowly but can sustain contractions for the longest duration?
    [ ] Skeletal muscle
    [*] Smooth muscle
    [ ] Cardiac muscle
    [ ] All three contract at the same speed
    Explanation: Smooth muscle contracts slowly but can maintain prolonged, tonic contractions with very little energy expenditure, which is essential for functions like maintaining blood vessel tone and bladder storage.

  Q9. Peristalsis in the gastrointestinal tract is carried out by:
    [*] Smooth muscle
    [ ] Cardiac muscle
    [ ] Skeletal muscle
    [ ] Connective tissue
    Explanation: Smooth muscle in the walls of the digestive tract produces rhythmic, wave-like contractions called peristalsis that propel food from the esophagus through the intestines.

  Q10. Muscle tissue accounts for approximately what percentage of total body mass?
    [ ] 70-80%
    [ ] 20-25%
    [*] 40-50%
    [ ] 10-15%
    Explanation: Skeletal muscle alone constitutes approximately 40% of body weight, and when cardiac and smooth muscle are included, muscle tissue represents about 40-50% of total body mass.

  Q11. What is autorhythmicity in the context of muscle tissue?
    [ ] The ability of skeletal muscle to contract without fatigue
    [*] The ability of cardiac muscle cells to generate their own rhythmic electrical impulses without external nerve stimulation
    [ ] The process of smooth muscle digesting food
    [ ] The reflex contraction of any muscle when stretched
    Explanation: Autorhythmicity (also called automaticity) refers to the intrinsic ability of cardiac pacemaker cells to spontaneously depolarize and generate action potentials, allowing the heart to beat without nervous system input.

  Q12. What does the term 'striated' mean when describing muscle tissue?
    [ ] The muscle is involuntary and controlled by hormones
    [*] The muscle displays alternating light and dark bands due to the organized arrangement of myofilaments
    [ ] The muscle has a smooth, uniform appearance under the microscope
    [ ] The muscle is spindle-shaped and tapered at both ends
    Explanation: The term 'striated' means having a striped or banded pattern, which results from the highly organized, repeating arrangement of actin (thin) and myosin (thick) filaments within sarcomeres.

  Q13. What is myology?
    [*] The study of muscles and muscle tissue
    [ ] The study of connective tissue
    [ ] The study of nerve tissue
    [ ] The study of bone tissue
    Explanation: Myology derives from the Greek root 'myo-' meaning muscle and '-logy' meaning study of, making it the branch of anatomy dedicated to the scientific study of muscles.

  Q14. What does 'involuntary muscle' mean?
    [ ] Muscle that never contracts unless stimulated by a physician
    [ ] Muscle that is found only in the limbs
    [ ] Muscle that can only contract during sleep
    [*] Muscle that operates without conscious control, regulated by the autonomic nervous system and hormones
    Explanation: Involuntary muscle refers to muscle tissue that contracts without conscious thought, including cardiac muscle in the heart and smooth muscle in visceral organs, both regulated by the autonomic nervous system.

  Q15. What is a gap junction in cardiac muscle?
    [ ] The point where a nerve meets a muscle fiber
    [ ] A space between muscle fibers where nutrients are stored

    Explanation: Gap junctions are specialized protein channels found within intercalated discs that create direct cytoplasmic connections between adjacent cardiac cells, enabling rapid electrical signal transmission so the heart contracts as a coordinated unit.

  Q16. What does 'excitability' mean as a property of muscle tissue?

    [ ] The ability of muscle to produce heat during rest
    [ ] The ability of muscle to stretch without tearing
    [ ] The ability of muscle to return to its original shape after being stretched
    Explanation: Excitability (also called irritability or responsiveness) is the ability of muscle cells to respond to chemical or electrical stimuli by producing an action potential, which is the essential first step in triggering contraction.

  Q17. What is contractility?
    [ ] The ability of muscle to resist fatigue during prolonged activity
    [*] The unique ability of muscle tissue to shorten and generate force when adequately stimulated
    [ ] The ability of muscle to stretch when force is applied

    Explanation: Contractility is the defining property of muscle tissue, referring to its ability to actively shorten and generate pulling force, which no other tissue type can do.

  Q18. What does 'extensibility' mean in muscle physiology?
    [*] The ability of muscle to be stretched beyond its resting length without being damaged
    [ ] The ability of muscle to store calcium ions

    [ ] The ability of muscle to conduct electrical impulses
    Explanation: Extensibility refers to the capacity of muscle tissue to be stretched or extended beyond its normal resting length, which is important for allowing opposing muscles to lengthen during movement.

  Q19. What is elasticity as a functional property of muscle?

  Q20. What does 'visceral muscle' mean?

    [ ] Another name for cardiac muscle in the heart

    [ ] The fusiform shape of cardiac muscle cells

  Q26. A physiology student observes that a donated heart continues to beat in a transport container before transplantation. The student is amazed that the heart beats without being attached to a body. Which characteristic of cardiac muscle best explains this observation?

  Q30. A histology student is given three unlabeled tissue slides: one from the heart, one from the biceps, and one from the intestinal wall. She must identify each using only a microscope. Which set of features would correctly match all three slides?

--- Lesson 3.2: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the sarcolemma?
The sarcolemma is the specialized plasma membrane of a muscle fiber (cell), which receives action potentials at the motor end plate and transmits them along the surface and into T-tubules to initiate contraction.
What is a myofibril?
A myofibril is a cylindrical organelle running the length of a muscle fiber, composed of thousands of sarcomeres arranged end-to-end; each muscle fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils that produce the actual force of contraction.
What is the neuromuscular junction (NMJ)?
The neuromuscular junction is the specialized chemical synapse where a motor neuron's axon terminal communicates with a skeletal muscle fiber via the release of acetylcholine, converting a neural signal into a muscular response.
What is the motor end plate?
The motor end plate is the folded region of the muscle fiber's sarcolemma directly opposite the motor neuron terminal, densely packed with nicotinic acetylcholine receptors that allow the muscle to respond rapidly to neural signals.
What is acetylcholinesterase?
Acetylcholinesterase is an enzyme bound to the basal lamina in the synaptic cleft that rapidly hydrolyzes acetylcholine into acetate and choline, ensuring that each nerve impulse produces only one brief muscle response and preventing continuous stimulation.
What is the A band of a sarcomere?
The A band is the dark-staining region of a sarcomere that corresponds to the full length of the thick myosin filaments, including the zones where thin actin filaments overlap with myosin; importantly, the A band does not change in length during contraction.
What is the I band?
The I band is the light-staining region of a sarcomere containing only thin (actin) filaments with no overlap by thick (myosin) filaments; it spans two adjacent sarcomeres and is bisected by the Z line.
What is the H zone?
The H zone is the lighter central portion of the A band where only thick myosin filaments are present and no thin actin filaments reach; during contraction, the H zone narrows and can disappear completely.
Real-World Applications
Example: During a laboratory practical, a student examines a sarcomere diagram and notices that after contraction, the I band and H zone have narrowed but the A band remains the same width. Which principle of muscle contraction does this observation support?
The sliding filament theory predicts that the A band (length of myosin) stays constant while the I band and H zone narrow because actin slides inward over myosin, bringing Z lines closer together without any filament changing its own length.
Example: A surgeon is repairing a severed nerve to a patient's quadriceps muscle. She explains that without nerve supply, the muscle will waste away. Which component of the motor unit is damaged, and what condition will develop?
When the motor neuron component of a motor unit is severed, the muscle fibers it innervates lose their neural stimulation and undergo denervation atrophy, progressively shrinking in size and losing function until the nerve is repaired or reinnervation occurs.
Example: A pharmacology student is learning about nerve agent poisoning. The instructor explains that nerve agents inhibit acetylcholinesterase, causing ACh to accumulate at the NMJ. Based on knowledge of NMJ physiology, what cascade of events would occur in the victim's muscles?
Inhibition of acetylcholinesterase causes acetylcholine to accumulate in the synaptic cleft, leading to continuous stimulation of the motor end plate, initial muscle fasciculations and spasms, followed by depolarization block and flaccid paralysis as receptors become desensitized.

--- Lesson 3.2: Muscle Anatomy & Physiology (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The basic contractile unit of skeletal muscle is the:
    [*] Sarcomere
    [ ] Fascicle
    [ ] Muscle fiber
    [ ] Myofibril
    Explanation: The sarcomere is the smallest functional contractile unit of a muscle fiber, extending from one Z line to the next and containing the organized actin and myosin filaments responsible for contraction.

  Q2. The correct structural hierarchy from largest to smallest in skeletal muscle is:
    [*] Muscle, Fascicle, Fiber, Myofibril, Sarcomere
    [ ] Sarcomere, Myofibril, Fiber, Fascicle, Muscle
    [ ] Myofibril, Fiber, Muscle, Fascicle, Sarcomere
    [ ] Fiber, Fascicle, Muscle, Sarcomere, Myofibril
    Explanation: The organizational hierarchy runs from the whole muscle down to fascicles (bundles of fibers), individual muscle fibers (cells), myofibrils (contractile strands within each fiber), and finally sarcomeres (the repeating units within myofibrils).

  Q3. The sarcoplasmic reticulum in a muscle fiber stores:
    [ ] ATP molecules
    [ ] Glycogen granules
    [ ] Sodium ions
    [*] Calcium ions (Ca2+)
    Explanation: The sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) is a specialized smooth endoplasmic reticulum in muscle fibers that sequesters and releases calcium ions, which are essential for initiating the contraction cycle.

  Q4. Which connective tissue layer wraps around a single muscle fiber?
    [ ] Deep fascia
    [ ] Epimysium
    [ ] Perimysium
    [*] Endomysium
    Explanation: The endomysium is the thin connective tissue sheath that surrounds each individual muscle fiber, providing structural support and carrying capillaries and nerve fibers to the cell.

  Q5. Perimysium surrounds which structure?
    [ ] A single muscle fiber
    [ ] A single sarcomere
    [ ] The entire muscle
    [*] A fascicle (bundle of muscle fibers)
    Explanation: The perimysium is a connective tissue sheath that wraps around each fascicle, which is a bundle of muscle fibers grouped together, and it carries the larger blood vessels and nerves that supply the fascicle.

  Q6. The epimysium is the connective tissue that surrounds:
    [ ] A single muscle fiber
    [ ] A fascicle
    [ ] A single myofibril
    [*] The entire muscle
    Explanation: The epimysium is the dense, irregular connective tissue layer that encases the entire muscle, separating it from surrounding tissues and organs while also being continuous with tendons at the muscle's ends.

  Q7. At the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), the neurotransmitter released is:
    [ ] Dopamine
    [ ] Epinephrine
    [ ] Serotonin
    [*] Acetylcholine (ACh)
    Explanation: Acetylcholine is the specific neurotransmitter used at all neuromuscular junctions between somatic motor neurons and skeletal muscle fibers, binding to nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate to trigger muscle contraction.

  Q8. T-tubules in skeletal muscle function to:
    [ ] Store calcium ions for contraction
    [ ] Produce ATP for energy
    [ ] Remove metabolic waste products
    [*] Conduct the action potential deep into the interior of the muscle fiber
    Explanation: Transverse tubules (T-tubules) are invaginations of the sarcolemma that extend deep into the muscle fiber, ensuring that the action potential reaches the interior of the cell quickly so that all myofibrils contract nearly simultaneously.

  Q9. ACh binds to receptors located on the:
    [ ] Nerve terminal membrane
    [ ] Sarcoplasmic reticulum
    [ ] Myofibril surface
    [*] Sarcolemma at the motor end plate
    Explanation: Acetylcholine crosses the synaptic cleft and binds to nicotinic receptors concentrated on the junctional folds of the motor end plate, a specialized region of the sarcolemma (muscle cell membrane).

  Q10. A motor unit consists of:
    [ ] The entire somatic nervous system
    [ ] A single sarcomere and its Z lines
    [ ] One entire muscle and its blood supply
    [*] One motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates
    Explanation: A motor unit is the functional unit of motor control, comprising a single motor neuron and every muscle fiber it innervates; when the neuron fires, all fibers in that motor unit contract together.

  Q11. What is the sarcolemma?
    [ ] The contractile protein within sarcomeres
    [*] The plasma membrane of a muscle fiber
    [ ] The connective tissue wrapping a fascicle
    [ ] The cytoplasm of a muscle fiber
    Explanation: The sarcolemma is the specialized plasma membrane of a muscle fiber (cell), which receives action potentials at the motor end plate and transmits them along the surface and into T-tubules to initiate contraction.

  Q12. What is a myofibril?
    [*] A long, thread-like contractile organelle within a muscle fiber, composed of repeating sarcomeres

    [ ] The nerve fiber that stimulates a muscle

    Explanation: A myofibril is a cylindrical organelle running the length of a muscle fiber, composed of thousands of sarcomeres arranged end-to-end; each muscle fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils that produce the actual force of contraction.

  Q13. What is the neuromuscular junction (NMJ)?

    [ ] The junction between two adjacent sarcomeres
    Explanation: The neuromuscular junction is the specialized chemical synapse where a motor neuron's axon terminal communicates with a skeletal muscle fiber via the release of acetylcholine, converting a neural signal into a muscular response.

  Q14. What is the motor end plate?
    [*] The specialized region of the sarcolemma containing a high concentration of ACh receptors at the NMJ
    [ ] The tip of the motor neuron where neurotransmitters are synthesized

  Q15. What is acetylcholinesterase?

    Explanation: Acetylcholinesterase is an enzyme bound to the basal lamina in the synaptic cleft that rapidly hydrolyzes acetylcholine into acetate and choline, ensuring that each nerve impulse produces only one brief muscle response and preventing continuous stimulation.

    Explanation: The A band is the dark-staining region of a sarcomere that corresponds to the full length of the thick myosin filaments, including the zones where thin actin filaments overlap with myosin; importantly, the A band does not change in length during contraction.

--- Lesson 3.3: Sliding Filament Theory (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sliding Filament Theory
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a cross-bridge in muscle contraction?
A cross-bridge refers to the temporary bond formed when the globular head of a myosin molecule attaches to a binding site on an actin filament, creating the structural link through which force is transmitted to pull actin inward during the power stroke.
What is the power stroke?
The power stroke is the force-generating step of the cross-bridge cycle in which the myosin head pivots from its cocked position to a bent position, pulling the attached actin filament approximately 10 nanometers toward the center of the sarcomere.
What is the role of tropomyosin in muscle contraction?
Tropomyosin is a long, rod-shaped regulatory protein that winds along the actin filament groove, physically blocking the myosin-binding sites when the muscle is relaxed and shifting position when calcium binds to troponin to permit cross-bridge formation.
What is the role of troponin in the contraction cycle?
Troponin is a three-subunit regulatory complex (troponin C binds calcium, troponin T binds tropomyosin, troponin I inhibits actin-myosin interaction) that acts as the calcium sensor on the thin filament, initiating contraction when calcium binds to troponin C.
What is excitation-contraction coupling?
Excitation-contraction coupling is the physiological process that converts an electrical signal (action potential on the sarcolemma and T-tubules) into a mechanical response (muscle contraction) through the release of calcium from the sarcoplasmic reticulum.
What is the Ca2+-ATPase pump?
The Ca2+-ATPase (SERCA) pump is an active transport protein embedded in the sarcoplasmic reticulum membrane that uses ATP to transport calcium ions from the sarcoplasm back into the SR lumen, lowering cytoplasmic calcium and allowing tropomyosin to re-cover actin binding sites for relaxation.
During a physiology exam, a student is asked: 'If a myosin head could not hydrolyze ATP, what would happen to the cross-bridge cycle?' What is the correct answer?
ATP hydrolysis by the myosin ATPase is essential for re-energizing the myosin head; if hydrolysis cannot occur, the myosin head cannot return to its cocked (high-energy) position after detachment, meaning it cannot perform another power stroke and the cross-bridge cycle would stall.
Real-World Applications
Example: A forensic scientist examining a body found in a warm apartment notices the muscles are very stiff. The estimated time of death is 8 hours prior. Using knowledge of the sliding filament theory, which molecular event best explains the stiffness?
After death, cellular metabolism stops and ATP is rapidly depleted; without ATP to bind myosin and break the actin-myosin cross-bridges, the myosin heads remain locked to actin, causing the characteristic stiffness of rigor mortis that typically begins 2-6 hours after death.

    [ ] ADP binding to the myosin head

--- Lesson 3.4: Muscle Contraction & Energy Use (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Muscle Contraction & Energy Use
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is oxygen debt (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption)?
Oxygen debt (now more accurately called excess post-exercise oxygen consumption, or EPOC) refers to the elevated oxygen consumption after exercise that is needed to replenish ATP and creatine phosphate stores, convert lactic acid back to glucose in the liver, and restore normal metabolic conditions.
What is a muscle twitch?
A muscle twitch is the simplest muscle response, consisting of a single, brief contraction followed by relaxation in response to a single threshold stimulus, and it includes three phases: the latent period, contraction phase, and relaxation phase.
What is muscle tone?
Muscle tone refers to the continuous, partial contraction of muscles maintained by alternating activation of different motor units, keeping muscles firm and ready for action, maintaining posture, and stabilizing joints even when the body is at rest.
What is anaerobic glycolysis?
Anaerobic glycolysis is a metabolic pathway that occurs in the cytoplasm without oxygen, breaking glucose into pyruvate and then converting it to lactic acid, producing 2 net ATP per glucose molecule rapidly enough to power intense activities lasting 30 seconds to 2 minutes.
What is aerobic metabolism (oxidative phosphorylation)?
Aerobic metabolism includes glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain in the mitochondria, requiring oxygen as the final electron acceptor and producing approximately 36 ATP per glucose molecule, making it the most efficient energy system for sustained activities.
What is the creatine phosphate system?
The creatine phosphate (phosphocreatine) system is the fastest way to regenerate ATP, using the enzyme creatine kinase to transfer a high-energy phosphate from creatine phosphate to ADP, providing energy for about 5-8 seconds of maximum effort before the stores are depleted.
A marathon runner has been running for 2 hours and is primarily using aerobic metabolism. She asks her coach why she cannot sprint at full speed for the entire marathon. What is the best physiological explanation?
Aerobic metabolism generates a large total amount of ATP but the rate of production is slower than anaerobic systems; sprinting requires extremely rapid ATP turnover that only the creatine phosphate and anaerobic glycolysis systems can provide, but these deplete quickly, so sustained running must rely on the slower but inexhaustible aerobic pathway.
After a heavy weightlifting session, a personal trainer notices her client breathing heavily even though he has stopped exercising. She explains this is 'oxygen debt.' What is the physiological purpose of this elevated breathing?
The elevated post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC/oxygen debt) provides the oxygen needed for aerobic metabolism to restore depleted ATP and creatine phosphate reserves, convert accumulated lactate back to pyruvate or glucose in the liver, and bring elevated body temperature and heart rate back to resting levels.
A fitness instructor tells a client to slowly lower a heavy barbell from shoulder height to waist height, emphasizing control on the way down. The client asks why the lowering phase feels more difficult and causes more soreness than the lifting phase. What is the biomechanical explanation?
Eccentric contractions during the lowering phase produce greater mechanical stress on fewer active cross-bridges because the muscle is being lengthened while trying to resist the load, causing more microscopic damage to muscle fibers and resulting in greater delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS).

    [ ] The muscle shortening significantly
    [ ] No force being produced at all
    Explanation: During an isometric contraction, the muscle generates force and cross-bridges cycle, but the muscle does not change length because the force produced equals the resistance, like pushing against an immovable wall or holding a plank position.

  Q4. The immediate energy source used directly by the myosin head during contraction is:
    [ ] Creatine phosphate
    [*] ATP (adenosine triphosphate)
    [ ] Glucose
    [ ] Fatty acids
    Explanation: ATP is the only molecule that can be directly used by the myosin ATPase to power the cross-bridge cycle; however, stored ATP in muscle fibers lasts only about 2-3 seconds, so it must be continuously regenerated from other energy sources.

  Q5. Creatine phosphate provides energy for approximately how long during intense activity?
    [ ] 1 second
    [ ] 30 minutes
    [*] 5-8 seconds
    [ ] 5 minutes
    Explanation: Creatine phosphate rapidly donates its phosphate group to ADP via the enzyme creatine kinase to regenerate ATP, providing energy for approximately 5-8 additional seconds of maximal effort beyond the initial stored ATP.

  Q6. Anaerobic glycolysis produces a net yield of:
    [ ] 100 ATP per glucose molecule
    [ ] 36 ATP per glucose molecule
    [*] 2 ATP per glucose molecule plus lactic acid
    [ ] 0 ATP
    Explanation: Anaerobic glycolysis breaks down one glucose molecule into two pyruvate molecules (which are then converted to lactic acid without oxygen), yielding a net gain of only 2 ATP, making it fast but energetically inefficient.

  Q7. Aerobic metabolism can produce approximately how many ATP per glucose molecule?
    [*] 36 ATP
    [ ] 100 ATP
    [ ] 2 ATP
    [ ] 5 ATP
    Explanation: Aerobic metabolism (including glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation) produces approximately 36 ATP per glucose molecule, making it about 18 times more efficient than anaerobic glycolysis, though it requires a continuous supply of oxygen.

  Q8. Lactic acid buildup is primarily associated with:
    [ ] Fat metabolism during long-distance running
    [ ] Complete rest and recovery
    [*] Anaerobic glycolysis during intense, short-duration exercise
    [ ] Low-intensity aerobic exercise
    Explanation: When oxygen supply cannot meet demand during intense exercise, pyruvate from glycolysis is converted to lactic acid (lactate) to regenerate NAD+ and keep glycolysis running, leading to accumulation that contributes to the burning sensation and fatigue.

  Q9. Slow-twitch (Type I) muscle fibers are best suited for:
    [ ] Short, explosive bursts of power like sprinting
    [ ] Only activities performed during sleep
    [*] Endurance activities like marathon running and maintaining posture
    [ ] Rapid, powerful weightlifting movements
    Explanation: Type I (slow oxidative) fibers are rich in mitochondria and myoglobin, use aerobic metabolism predominantly, fatigue slowly, and are ideal for sustained, endurance-type activities like long-distance running, cycling, and maintaining upright posture.

  Q10. Muscle fatigue results primarily from:
    [ ] Increased bone density
    [*] ATP depletion, lactic acid accumulation, and electrolyte imbalances
    [ ] Excessive rest between contractions
    [ ] Excess oxygen delivery to the muscle
    Explanation: Muscle fatigue is a multifactorial condition resulting from depletion of ATP and creatine phosphate, accumulation of lactic acid and other metabolic byproducts, disruption of ion concentrations, and impaired calcium handling in the SR.

  Q11. What is oxygen debt (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption)?
    [ ] The amount of oxygen used during the exercise itself
    [ ] The inability to breathe during exercise
    [*] The extra oxygen consumed after exercise to restore ATP, creatine phosphate, and clear lactic acid from the body
    [ ] A medical condition where the lungs cannot absorb oxygen
    Explanation: Oxygen debt (now more accurately called excess post-exercise oxygen consumption, or EPOC) refers to the elevated oxygen consumption after exercise that is needed to replenish ATP and creatine phosphate stores, convert lactic acid back to glucose in the liver, and restore normal metabolic conditions.

  Q12. What is a muscle twitch?
    [ ] The permanent shortening of a muscle due to disease
    [ ] A sustained, prolonged contraction lasting several minutes
    [ ] The resting tone of a muscle between contractions
    [*] A single, brief contraction-relaxation cycle in response to one stimulus
    Explanation: A muscle twitch is the simplest muscle response, consisting of a single, brief contraction followed by relaxation in response to a single threshold stimulus, and it includes three phases: the latent period, contraction phase, and relaxation phase.

  Q13. What does tetanus (physiological) mean in muscle physiology?
    [ ] The death of muscle fibers due to oxygen deprivation
    [ ] A bacterial infection that causes lockjaw
    [ ] The complete relaxation of a muscle after exercise
    [*] A sustained, smooth muscle contraction resulting from stimuli arriving so rapidly that the fiber cannot relax between them
    Explanation: Physiological tetanus (different from the disease tetanus) occurs when high-frequency stimuli cause successive contractions to fuse into a smooth, sustained contraction because the muscle fiber does not have time to fully relax between stimuli, producing maximum force output.

  Q14. What is muscle tone?
    [ ] The sound produced when a muscle contracts
    [ ] Complete muscle relaxation with zero tension
    [*] Constant, low-level involuntary contractions that keep muscles firm and ready for action even at rest
    [ ] The maximum force a muscle can produce during a single contraction
    Explanation: Muscle tone refers to the continuous, partial contraction of muscles maintained by alternating activation of different motor units, keeping muscles firm and ready for action, maintaining posture, and stabilizing joints even when the body is at rest.

  Q15. What does 'fast-twitch' (Type II) mean when describing muscle fibers?
    [*] Fibers that contract rapidly and powerfully, relying primarily on anaerobic glycolysis, but fatigue quickly
    [ ] Fibers that cannot generate any significant force
    [ ] Fibers found only in cardiac muscle
    [ ] Fibers that contract slowly and resist fatigue for endurance activities
    Explanation: Fast-twitch (Type II) muscle fibers have rapid myosin ATPase activity, large glycogen stores, and rely heavily on anaerobic glycolysis for quick energy, making them ideal for short, powerful bursts like sprinting or jumping but prone to rapid fatigue.

  Q16. What is anaerobic glycolysis?
    [ ] The transfer of phosphate from creatine phosphate to ADP
    [*] The partial breakdown of glucose to pyruvate (then lactic acid) without oxygen, yielding 2 ATP rapidly
    [ ] The complete breakdown of glucose using oxygen in the mitochondria
    [ ] The breakdown of fatty acids for long-duration energy
    Explanation: Anaerobic glycolysis is a metabolic pathway that occurs in the cytoplasm without oxygen, breaking glucose into pyruvate and then converting it to lactic acid, producing 2 net ATP per glucose molecule rapidly enough to power intense activities lasting 30 seconds to 2 minutes.

  Q17. What is aerobic metabolism (oxidative phosphorylation)?
    [ ] The direct transfer of phosphate from creatine phosphate to ADP
    [ ] A process that only occurs during sleep and rest
    [ ] A quick anaerobic process that produces 2 ATP without oxygen
    [*] The oxygen-dependent metabolic pathway in mitochondria that completely oxidizes glucose (or fats) to produce approximately 36 ATP per glucose
    Explanation: Aerobic metabolism includes glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain in the mitochondria, requiring oxygen as the final electron acceptor and producing approximately 36 ATP per glucose molecule, making it the most efficient energy system for sustained activities.

  Q18. What does 'wave summation' mean in muscle physiology?
    [ ] The recruitment of all motor units at once for maximum force
    [ ] The decrease in force during prolonged stimulation
    [*] An increase in contraction force that occurs when a second stimulus arrives before the muscle fully relaxes from the first, causing overlapping responses
    [ ] The resting state between two separate twitches
    Explanation: Wave summation (or temporal summation) occurs when successive stimuli arrive before complete relaxation, causing each new contraction to build on the residual force of the previous one, resulting in progressively greater total force production.

  Q19. What is the creatine phosphate system?
    [ ] A system that only functions during rest and recovery
    [ ] The aerobic breakdown of glucose in the mitochondria
    [*] A rapid, anaerobic system where creatine phosphate donates its phosphate group to ADP to regenerate ATP within seconds
    [ ] A long-term energy system that uses fat for fuel
    Explanation: The creatine phosphate (phosphocreatine) system is the fastest way to regenerate ATP, using the enzyme creatine kinase to transfer a high-energy phosphate from creatine phosphate to ADP, providing energy for about 5-8 seconds of maximum effort before the stores are depleted.

  Q20. What does 'isotonic contraction' mean?
    [ ] A contraction where the muscle generates no force at all
    [ ] A contraction that occurs only in smooth muscle
    [*] A contraction where the muscle changes length while maintaining relatively constant tension, including both concentric and eccentric types
    [ ] A contraction where force is produced but the muscle does not change length
    Explanation: Isotonic (meaning 'same tension') contraction occurs when a muscle changes length against a constant load, encompassing both concentric contractions (muscle shortens) and eccentric contractions (muscle lengthens), and is the most common type of contraction during daily movement.

  Q21. A 100-meter sprinter explodes out of the starting blocks. During the first 10 seconds of the race, which energy systems would primarily fuel her muscles, and in what order?
    [ ] Aerobic metabolism first, then anaerobic glycolysis
    [ ] Protein catabolism provides all the energy for sprinting
    [*] Stored ATP first (2-3 seconds), then creatine phosphate (5-8 seconds), then anaerobic glycolysis kicks in
    [ ] Fat oxidation exclusively for the entire sprint
    Explanation: During a short, maximal sprint, the muscles first use their small stored ATP reserves (lasting 2-3 seconds), then rapidly regenerate ATP via the creatine phosphate system (5-8 seconds), and as phosphocreatine depletes, anaerobic glycolysis becomes the dominant energy source.

  Q22. A marathon runner has been running for 2 hours and is primarily using aerobic metabolism. She asks her coach why she cannot sprint at full speed for the entire marathon. What is the best physiological explanation?
    [ ] Her skeletal muscles cannot contract at all during aerobic metabolism
    [*] Aerobic metabolism produces abundant ATP but too slowly for maximum-intensity effort; sprinting requires the faster but quickly depleted anaerobic systems
    [ ] Aerobic metabolism only works in cardiac muscle, not skeletal muscle
    [ ] Marathons use only creatine phosphate for energy
    Explanation: Aerobic metabolism generates a large total amount of ATP but the rate of production is slower than anaerobic systems; sprinting requires extremely rapid ATP turnover that only the creatine phosphate and anaerobic glycolysis systems can provide, but these deplete quickly, so sustained running must rely on the slower but inexhaustible aerobic pathway.

  Q23. After a heavy weightlifting session, a personal trainer notices her client breathing heavily even though he has stopped exercising. She explains this is 'oxygen debt.' What is the physiological purpose of this elevated breathing?
    [ ] The body is simply anxious and hyperventilating with no metabolic purpose
    [*] The extra oxygen consumed is used to replenish ATP and creatine phosphate stores, convert lactic acid back to glucose, and restore normal metabolic conditions
    [ ] The breathing is caused by muscle damage, not metabolic needs
    [ ] The body needs to expel excess ATP that built up during exercise
    Explanation: The elevated post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC/oxygen debt) provides the oxygen needed for aerobic metabolism to restore depleted ATP and creatine phosphate reserves, convert accumulated lactate back to pyruvate or glucose in the liver, and bring elevated body temperature and heart rate back to resting levels.

  Q24. A physical therapist asks a patient to hold a 10-pound weight at arm's length for as long as possible. After 2 minutes, the patient's arm begins to shake and eventually drops. What type of contraction was being performed, and why did fatigue occur?
    [ ] Eccentric contraction; fatigue occurred from excess oxygen delivery
    [*] Isometric contraction; fatigue occurred from ATP depletion, metabolite accumulation, and motor unit fatigue while maintaining constant muscle length
    [ ] No contraction was occurring because the arm was not moving
    [ ] Concentric contraction; fatigue occurred from muscle tearing
    Explanation: Holding a weight stationary is an isometric contraction where force is produced without muscle length change; fatigue develops because sustained cross-bridge cycling depletes local ATP, metabolic byproducts accumulate, and motor units that have been continuously active become exhausted.

  Q25. A sports science student notices that long-distance runners tend to have thin, lean legs while sprinters have muscular, powerful legs. How do muscle fiber types explain this difference?
    [ ] The difference is entirely due to genetics and has nothing to do with fiber types
    [*] Distance runners have a higher proportion of Type I (slow-twitch) fibers that are smaller but fatigue-resistant, while sprinters have more Type II (fast-twitch) fibers that are larger and more powerful
    [ ] Both athletes have the same fiber type composition; the visual difference is only due to body fat
    [ ] Sprinters have more Type I fibers and distance runners have more Type II fibers
    Explanation: Distance runners' muscles are dominated by Type I fibers that are smaller in diameter, rich in mitochondria, and highly fatigue-resistant, while sprinters' muscles contain a higher proportion of Type II fibers that are larger, generate more power through anaerobic metabolism, and respond more dramatically to hypertrophy training.

  Q26. A patient with McArdle's disease cannot break down glycogen in their muscles. During exercise, they experience early fatigue and cramping. Based on knowledge of muscle energy systems, which metabolic pathway is most directly impaired?
    [ ] Fat metabolism only, because glycogen is a type of fat
    [ ] The creatine phosphate system, because it relies on glycogen
    [ ] No metabolic pathway is affected because glycogen is not used for energy
    [*] Glycolysis (both anaerobic and aerobic), because glucose from glycogen breakdown is unavailable as a fuel source for ATP production
    Explanation: McArdle's disease (glycogen storage disease type V) impairs the enzyme myophosphorylase needed to break glycogen into glucose-1-phosphate, directly limiting the substrate available for glycolysis, which drastically reduces ATP production during exercise and causes premature fatigue and painful cramping.

  Q27. A fitness instructor tells a client to slowly lower a heavy barbell from shoulder height to waist height, emphasizing control on the way down. The client asks why the lowering phase feels more difficult and causes more soreness than the lifting phase. What is the biomechanical explanation?
    [ ] The lowering phase involves isometric contraction with no muscle length change
    [ ] Gravity does all the work during the lowering phase, so the muscles are resting
    [*] The lowering phase involves eccentric contraction, which generates more muscle damage and DOMS because the muscle is lengthened under tension with fewer motor units sharing the load
    [ ] The lowering phase uses no muscle activation at all
    Explanation: Eccentric contractions during the lowering phase produce greater mechanical stress on fewer active cross-bridges because the muscle is being lengthened while trying to resist the load, causing more microscopic damage to muscle fibers and resulting in greater delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS).

  Q28. A nurse is caring for a patient in the ICU who has been on a ventilator and sedated for 3 weeks. The doctor mentions that the patient's respiratory muscles and limb muscles will have significantly weakened. Using knowledge of muscle physiology, what has happened to these muscles?
    [ ] The muscles are unchanged because sedation preserves muscle mass
    [*] The muscles have undergone disuse atrophy, losing protein content and decreasing in size due to prolonged inactivity and lack of neural stimulation
    [ ] The muscles have undergone hypertrophy from the constant sedation
    [ ] The muscles have converted entirely from skeletal to smooth muscle tissue
    Explanation: Prolonged immobility leads to disuse atrophy, where muscle fibers shrink due to increased protein degradation and decreased protein synthesis; the diaphragm and limb muscles are particularly affected in ventilated ICU patients, often requiring extensive rehabilitation to regain strength and function.

  Q29. An exercise physiologist is testing an athlete on a treadmill with gradually increasing speed. She notices that at a certain intensity, the athlete's blood lactate levels begin to rise sharply. What does this threshold represent in terms of energy metabolism?
    [ ] The point where creatine phosphate stores are fully depleted
    [ ] The point where the athlete's muscles switch entirely to aerobic metabolism
    [ ] A sign that the athlete should stop exercising immediately due to muscle damage
    [*] The lactate threshold, where anaerobic glycolysis begins contributing significantly because aerobic metabolism alone cannot meet the ATP demand
    Explanation: The lactate threshold represents the exercise intensity at which the rate of lactate production from anaerobic glycolysis exceeds the rate of lactate clearance, indicating that aerobic metabolism can no longer fully meet the muscles' ATP demands and anaerobic pathways are increasingly recruited.

  Q30. A medical student is studying a patient who experiences severe muscle cramps and dark brown urine after an intense CrossFit workout. The attending physician suspects rhabdomyolysis. How do muscle energy depletion and contraction physiology contribute to this dangerous condition?
    [ ] Rhabdomyolysis is caused by too much ATP production, not too little
    [*] Extreme energy depletion and sustained contraction damage muscle cell membranes, causing intracellular contents including myoglobin to leak into the bloodstream, which can damage the kidneys
    [ ] The excessive exercise has no relationship to muscle cell damage
    [ ] The dark urine is caused by dehydration alone and has nothing to do with muscle damage
    Explanation: During extreme exercise, severe ATP depletion and sustained forceful contractions can overwhelm the muscle fiber's ability to maintain membrane integrity, leading to rhabdomyolysis where cell contents (myoglobin, creatine kinase, potassium) leak into the blood, and myoglobin can clog renal tubules, potentially causing acute kidney failure.

--- Lesson 3.5: Major Muscle Groups (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Major Muscle Groups
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an agonist (prime mover)?
An agonist (or prime mover) is the muscle that bears the primary responsibility for a particular movement, such as the biceps brachii being the agonist for elbow flexion or the quadriceps being the agonist for knee extension.
What is an antagonist muscle?
An antagonist is a muscle that opposes the action of the agonist, providing controlled resistance and smooth movement; for example, the triceps brachii is the antagonist to the biceps brachii during elbow flexion, relaxing to allow flexion and contracting to produce extension.
What is a synergist muscle?
A synergist is a muscle that assists the prime mover by contributing to the same movement, stabilizing joints to prevent unwanted motions, or neutralizing undesired actions of the agonist, such as the brachialis assisting the biceps during elbow flexion.
What is the latissimus dorsi?
The latissimus dorsi ('lat') is the broadest muscle of the back, extending from the lower spine and iliac crest to the humerus, responsible for powerful arm extension, adduction, and medial rotation, as used in pull-ups, swimming, and rowing.
What is the pectoralis major?
The pectoralis major is a large, fan-shaped muscle covering the upper anterior chest, originating from the clavicle, sternum, and ribs and inserting on the humerus; it is the primary muscle for chest press movements, push-ups, and bringing the arms across the body.
What is the erector spinae?
The erector spinae is a group of three paired muscle columns (iliocostalis, longissimus, and spinalis) running along the length of the vertebral column that are the primary extensors of the spine, maintaining upright posture and controlling trunk flexion.
What is the sternocleidomastoid (SCM)?
The sternocleidomastoid is a large, strap-like neck muscle named for its attachments (sternum, clavicle, and mastoid process of the temporal bone) that, when contracting unilaterally, rotates the head to the opposite side, and bilaterally, flexes the neck.
A bodybuilder is performing a bench press. During the upward pressing phase, which major muscle group acts as the primary agonist, and what is the type of contraction?
During the pressing (upward) phase of the bench press, the pectoralis major is the primary agonist performing a concentric contraction, shortening to push the barbell upward through horizontal adduction and flexion of the shoulder, assisted by the anterior deltoid and triceps.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The deltoid is the primary abductor of the shoulder, and damage to the motor cortex area controlling it would prevent the patient from raising her arm laterally, making overhead activities like hair brushing, reaching shelves, and dressing extremely difficult.
Real-World Applications
Example: A patient who had a stroke has difficulty raising her left arm above her head to brush her hair. The neurologist explains that the stroke damaged the motor cortex area controlling the left shoulder. Which muscle is primarily affected, preventing arm abduction?
The deltoid is the primary abductor of the shoulder, and damage to the motor cortex area controlling it would prevent the patient from raising her arm laterally, making overhead activities like hair brushing, reaching shelves, and dressing extremely difficult.
Example: A respiratory therapist is treating a patient with a spinal cord injury at the C3-C5 level. The therapist explains that the patient cannot breathe independently because the phrenic nerve is damaged. Which muscle is paralyzed, and why is it so critical?
The diaphragm is the primary muscle of inspiration, responsible for about 75% of air movement during quiet breathing, and it is innervated by the phrenic nerve originating from C3-C5; damage to these spinal cord segments paralyzes the diaphragm and requires mechanical ventilation.
Example: A patient complains of a stiff neck after sleeping in an awkward position. The doctor diagnoses a strained sternocleidomastoid muscle. The patient notices she can only turn her head to the right comfortably. On which side is the SCM most likely strained?
The sternocleidomastoid rotates the head to the opposite side when contracting unilaterally; if the left SCM is strained, turning the head to the right (which requires left SCM contraction) would be more comfortable since the strained muscle is already shortened, while turning left would painfully stretch it.

--- Lesson 3.6: Disorders of the Muscular System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Muscular System
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is muscular dystrophy?
Muscular dystrophy encompasses a group of more than 30 inherited genetic disorders in which mutations in genes encoding structural muscle proteins (like dystrophin) lead to progressive skeletal muscle weakness, degeneration, and replacement with fibrotic and fatty tissue.
What is atrophy in the context of muscular disorders?
Atrophy is the wasting or reduction in muscle size that occurs when muscle fibers shrink due to decreased protein synthesis and increased protein degradation, caused by factors such as prolonged immobilization, nerve damage, aging (sarcopenia), or chronic disease.
What is hypertrophy?
Hypertrophy is the increase in skeletal muscle size that results from enlargement of existing muscle fibers (not creation of new ones) through increased synthesis of myofilaments and myofibrils, typically stimulated by resistance training and adequate nutrition.
What is compartment syndrome?
Compartment syndrome occurs when swelling or bleeding within a closed fascial compartment raises the pressure to a level that compromises blood flow and nerve function, leading to ischemia and potential permanent tissue damage if not treated with emergency fasciotomy.
What is dystrophin?
Dystrophin is a large, rod-shaped cytoskeletal protein that forms a crucial link between the intracellular actin cytoskeleton and the extracellular matrix through the dystrophin-associated glycoprotein complex, protecting the sarcolemma from mechanical stress during contraction; its absence causes Duchenne muscular dystrophy.
What is a muscle spasm?
A muscle spasm (or cramp) is an involuntary, sustained, and often painful contraction that occurs without conscious control, commonly caused by electrolyte imbalances, dehydration, muscle fatigue, or nerve irritation.
A nurse is caring for an ICU patient who develops sudden, severe pain and swelling in his right lower leg after a tibial fracture was casted. The limb appears tense, and the patient reports numbness in the foot. The surgeon is urgently called. What is the likely diagnosis, and why is emergency surgery needed?
Acute compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency where swelling within a rigid fascial compartment raises pressure above capillary perfusion pressure, cutting off blood flow to muscles and nerves; without fasciotomy (surgical opening of the compartment) within 6 hours, irreversible muscle necrosis and nerve damage occur.
A dermatologist is using Botox injections to treat a patient with chronic migraines. The patient asks how a toxin can be a medicine. Based on knowledge of muscular disorders, what is the therapeutic mechanism of Botox?
Therapeutic Botox uses tiny, precisely targeted doses of botulinum toxin to block ACh release at selected neuromuscular junctions, causing localized, temporary muscle paralysis that reduces the excessive muscle tension contributing to chronic migraines, cervical dystonia, and spasticity.
A physical therapist is treating a patient with multiple sclerosis (MS) who is experiencing increasing weakness and spasticity in her legs. The patient asks why her muscles are getting weaker even though the disease affects nerves, not muscles. What is the best explanation?
In MS, the autoimmune destruction of myelin in the central nervous system slows or blocks motor signals traveling from the brain and spinal cord to skeletal muscles; although the muscles themselves are initially structurally normal, impaired innervation causes weakness, and damaged inhibitory pathways lead to spasticity.
An elderly patient has been hospitalized for pneumonia and confined to bed for 3 weeks. Upon discharge, she is unable to walk unassisted. Her doctor explains that her leg muscles have atrophied. The patient asks if the muscle loss is permanent. What is the most accurate response?
Disuse atrophy is generally reversible because the muscle fibers shrink but are not destroyed; with progressive resistance exercise and physical therapy, protein synthesis increases, myofibrils are rebuilt, and muscle size and strength can be largely restored, though recovery may take longer in elderly patients.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Duchenne MD is X-linked recessive; female carriers have two X chromosomes, and the normal copy on one X produces sufficient dystrophin through random X-inactivation to maintain muscle function, while males have only one X chromosome and therefore express the full disease phenotype when the dystrophin gene is mutated.
Real-World Applications
Example: A muscle strain involves damage to:
A muscle strain is an injury to muscle fibers or their associated tendons caused by overstretching or excessive force, ranging from mild microscopic fiber damage (Grade I) to complete rupture (Grade III) of the muscle-tendon unit.
Example: What does 'demyelination' mean in the context of multiple sclerosis and muscle weakness?
Demyelination is the loss or destruction of the myelin sheath that normally insulates nerve fibers and speeds up signal conduction; in multiple sclerosis, the immune system attacks myelin on central nervous system neurons, slowing or blocking motor signals and causing muscle weakness, spasticity, and coordination problems.
Example: A physical therapist is treating a patient with multiple sclerosis (MS) who is experiencing increasing weakness and spasticity in her legs. The patient asks why her muscles are getting weaker even though the disease affects nerves, not muscles. What is the best explanation?
In MS, the autoimmune destruction of myelin in the central nervous system slows or blocks motor signals traveling from the brain and spinal cord to skeletal muscles; although the muscles themselves are initially structurally normal, impaired innervation causes weakness, and damaged inhibitory pathways lead to spasticity.

  Q1. Duchenne muscular dystrophy is caused by:
    [ ] Muscle overuse from excessive exercise

    [ ] A vitamin deficiency
    [*] A genetic mutation in the dystrophin gene (X-linked recessive)
    Explanation: Duchenne muscular dystrophy is caused by mutations in the DMD gene on the X chromosome that lead to the absence of dystrophin, a critical protein that anchors the muscle fiber's cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix, resulting in progressive muscle degeneration.

    [ ] The intestinal smooth muscle
    [ ] Bones and joints
    [ ] The brain's motor cortex
    [*] Neuromuscular junction, where autoantibodies destroy ACh receptors
    Explanation: Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder in which the immune system produces antibodies that attack and destroy nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, reducing the number of available receptors and causing progressive muscle weakness.

    [ ] Bone fractures throughout the body
    [ ] Visible muscle wasting and tissue destruction
    [*] Chronic widespread musculoskeletal pain and fatigue without identifiable tissue damage

    Explanation: Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition characterized by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbances, and cognitive difficulties, with no identifiable structural tissue damage on examination or imaging, thought to involve abnormal central pain processing.

  Q4. A muscle strain involves damage to:
    [*] Overstretched or torn muscle fibers and/or tendons
    [ ] A crack or break in the periosteum and bone matrix
    [ ] An overstretched or torn ligament connecting bones
    [ ] A displacement of bones from their normal joint alignment
    Explanation: A muscle strain is an injury to muscle fibers or their associated tendons caused by overstretching or excessive force, ranging from mild microscopic fiber damage (Grade I) to complete rupture (Grade III) of the muscle-tendon unit.

  Q5. Duchenne muscular dystrophy primarily affects:
    [ ] Adult men and women equally
    [ ] Only newborn females
    [*] Boys, because it is X-linked recessive
    [ ] Elderly women
    Explanation: Because Duchenne MD is X-linked recessive, males (who have only one X chromosome) are predominantly affected, while females typically serve as carriers with two X chromosomes providing a backup copy of the dystrophin gene.

  Q6. Rhabdomyolysis can lead to which serious complication?
    [ ] Stronger bones
    [ ] Improved athletic performance
    [ ] No significant complications
    [*] Acute kidney failure due to myoglobin release into the bloodstream
    Explanation: When muscle fibers are severely damaged and break down (rhabdomyolysis), large quantities of myoglobin are released into the bloodstream; this protein can precipitate in and clog the renal tubules, leading to acute kidney injury or failure.

  Q7. Tetanus (the disease) is caused by a toxin from which organism?
    [ ] Streptococcus pyogenes
    [ ] Escherichia coli
    [*] Clostridium tetani
    [ ] Staphylococcus aureus
    Explanation: Tetanus is caused by the neurotoxin tetanospasmin produced by the anaerobic bacterium Clostridium tetani, which blocks inhibitory neurotransmitter release in the spinal cord, resulting in uncontrolled muscle contraction and spasms (lockjaw).

    [*] 24-72 hours after unaccustomed or intense exercise
    [ ] During exercise
    [ ] Immediately and resolves within minutes
    [ ] Only in professional athletes
    Explanation: DOMS is delayed-onset muscle soreness that typically peaks 24-72 hours after exercise, especially eccentric exercise, and is caused by microscopic damage to muscle fibers that triggers an inflammatory repair response.

  Q9. Botulinum toxin (Botox) works by:
    [ ] Stimulating acetylcholine release for stronger contractions
    [*] Blocking ACh release at the neuromuscular junction, causing localized muscle paralysis
    [ ] Increasing calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum
    [ ] Strengthening muscle fibers through protein synthesis
    Explanation: Botulinum toxin blocks the release of acetylcholine from the presynaptic terminal at the NMJ by cleaving SNARE proteins required for vesicle fusion, causing temporary, localized muscle paralysis used therapeutically for spasticity, dystonia, and cosmetically for wrinkle reduction.

  Q10. The RICE protocol for acute muscle injuries stands for:
    [ ] Resist, Inflate, Compress, Extend
    [ ] Run, Ice, Contract, Elevate
    [ ] Rehab, Inject, Cool, Exercise
    [*] Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation
    Explanation: RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) is the standard first-aid protocol for acute musculoskeletal injuries that helps reduce swelling, inflammation, and pain in the initial 48-72 hours after injury.

  Q11. What is muscular dystrophy?
    [ ] A condition where muscles grow too large from overuse

    [ ] An infection of the muscle tissue by bacteria

    Explanation: Muscular dystrophy encompasses a group of more than 30 inherited genetic disorders in which mutations in genes encoding structural muscle proteins (like dystrophin) lead to progressive skeletal muscle weakness, degeneration, and replacement with fibrotic and fatty tissue.

    [ ] The multiplication of muscle cells to create new fibers
    [ ] The shrinking of muscle fibers due to inactivity
    Explanation: Hypertrophy is the increase in skeletal muscle size that results from enlargement of existing muscle fibers (not creation of new ones) through increased synthesis of myofilaments and myofibrils, typically stimulated by resistance training and adequate nutrition.

  Q14. What is compartment syndrome?

    Explanation: A muscle spasm (or cramp) is an involuntary, sustained, and often painful contraction that occurs without conscious control, commonly caused by electrolyte imbalances, dehydration, muscle fatigue, or nerve irritation.

--- Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Sports Medicine (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Sports Medicine
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the PCL (posterior cruciate ligament)?

--- Lesson 3.8: AP Prep: Muscle Physiology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Muscle Physiology
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the length-tension relationship in muscle physiology?
The length-tension relationship describes how the isometric force a muscle can produce depends on its initial sarcomere length; at optimal length, maximum actin-myosin overlap allows the most cross-bridges to form, producing peak force, while overstretched or over-compressed sarcomeres produce less force.
What is motor unit recruitment?
Motor unit recruitment is the nervous system's primary mechanism for grading whole-muscle force by activating increasing numbers of motor units; since individual fibers follow the all-or-none law, force gradation depends on how many motor units are activated simultaneously.
What is a depolarizing neuromuscular blocker?
Depolarizing neuromuscular blockers like succinylcholine mimic ACh by binding to nicotinic receptors and causing initial depolarization (producing brief fasciculations), but because they resist breakdown by acetylcholinesterase, they produce sustained depolarization that desensitizes the receptors, resulting in flaccid paralysis used during surgical intubation.
What is the 'size principle' (Henneman's size principle)?
Henneman's size principle states that motor neurons are recruited in an orderly fashion based on their size (and therefore threshold): small-diameter alpha motor neurons (innervating Type I fibers) are recruited first at low force levels, and progressively larger neurons (innervating Type II fibers) are added as more force is needed.
What is the difference between isometric and isotonic contractions?
Isometric ('same length') contractions generate force without changing muscle length (like holding a plank), while isotonic ('same tension') contractions involve muscle length changes against a constant load, including concentric (shortening) and eccentric (lengthening) subtypes.
What is the latent period of a muscle twitch?
The latent period is the short delay (typically 1-2 milliseconds) between the delivery of the stimulus and the beginning of visible contraction, during which the action potential propagates along the sarcolemma and T-tubules, Ca2+ is released from the SR, and cross-bridges begin to form.
What is the role of the dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR) in excitation-contraction coupling?
The dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR) is a voltage-gated calcium channel/sensor embedded in the T-tubule membrane that undergoes a conformational change when the action potential arrives, mechanically triggering the associated ryanodine receptor (RyR) on the SR membrane to open and release stored calcium into the sarcoplasm.
What is the ryanodine receptor (RyR)?
The ryanodine receptor is a large calcium release channel embedded in the SR membrane that opens when activated by the DHPR on the adjacent T-tubule, allowing the rapid efflux of stored calcium from the SR into the sarcoplasm to initiate the contraction process.
A student is studying for the AP exam and encounters this question: 'A patient is given a drug that blocks voltage-gated sodium channels on the sarcolemma. Predict the effect on muscle contraction.' What is the correct answer and reasoning?
Blocking voltage-gated sodium channels prevents action potential generation and propagation on the sarcolemma; without an action potential reaching the T-tubules, DHPRs cannot activate, RyRs remain closed, calcium stays sequestered in the SR, and no cross-bridge cycling occurs, resulting in muscle paralysis.
A pharmacology question on the AP exam asks: 'An organophosphate pesticide inhibits acetylcholinesterase. Trace the cascade of events from the NMJ to the muscle fiber and predict the clinical outcome.' What is the correct analysis?
Acetylcholinesterase inhibition causes ACh to accumulate, producing continuous receptor activation, sustained end-plate depolarization, initial muscle fasciculations and cramping, followed by depolarization block (receptor desensitization) leading to flaccid paralysis; this sequence affects all cholinergic synapses and is life-threatening when respiratory muscles are paralyzed.
An AP integration question asks: 'Compare and contrast the mechanisms by which curare and botulinum toxin both cause muscle paralysis, despite working at different sites of the NMJ.' What is the correct comparison?
Though both produce flaccid paralysis, they act at different points: botulinum toxin acts presynaptically by cleaving SNARE proteins to prevent ACh vesicle fusion and release, while curare acts postsynaptically as a competitive antagonist that occupies nicotinic receptors without activating them, preventing ACh from binding.
For AP exam preparation, a student must explain why the warmup treppe effect demonstrates the importance of temperature on enzyme activity in muscle physiology. What is the correct molecular explanation?
The treppe effect demonstrates that mild increases in temperature from repeated contractions enhance enzyme kinetics (myosin ATPase cycles faster, Ca2+-ATPase pumps more efficiently) and increase calcium availability in the sarcoplasm, resulting in progressively stronger twitches until a steady state is reached, illustrating why athletes warm up before performance.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student is studying for the AP exam and encounters this question: 'A patient is given a drug that blocks voltage-gated sodium channels on the sarcolemma. Predict the effect on muscle contraction.' What is the correct answer and reasoning?
Blocking voltage-gated sodium channels prevents action potential generation and propagation on the sarcolemma; without an action potential reaching the T-tubules, DHPRs cannot activate, RyRs remain closed, calcium stays sequestered in the SR, and no cross-bridge cycling occurs, resulting in muscle paralysis.
Example: An AP exam free-response question asks: 'Explain why a muscle produces less force when it is either overstretched or over-compressed compared to its optimal resting length.' How should a student structure their answer using the length-tension relationship?
The length-tension relationship is based on the sliding filament theory: at optimal resting length, the maximum number of myosin heads can interact with actin; when overstretched, reduced overlap means fewer cross-bridges; when over-compressed, thick filaments crumple against Z-lines and thin filaments overlap each other, sterically hindering cross-bridge formation.
Example: A pharmacology question on the AP exam asks: 'An organophosphate pesticide inhibits acetylcholinesterase. Trace the cascade of events from the NMJ to the muscle fiber and predict the clinical outcome.' What is the correct analysis?
Acetylcholinesterase inhibition causes ACh to accumulate, producing continuous receptor activation, sustained end-plate depolarization, initial muscle fasciculations and cramping, followed by depolarization block (receptor desensitization) leading to flaccid paralysis; this sequence affects all cholinergic synapses and is life-threatening when respiratory muscles are paralyzed.
Example: An AP exam scenario describes an athlete who can pick up a feather gently and also lift a 200-pound barbell. The question asks: 'Since individual muscle fibers follow the all-or-none principle, explain how the nervous system produces such dramatically different force levels.'
Although each fiber contracts maximally once threshold is reached, the nervous system controls whole-muscle force through spatial summation (recruiting few motor units for light tasks and many for heavy ones, following the size principle) and temporal summation (increasing firing frequency to achieve tetanus), enabling smooth force gradation from feather-light to maximum effort.
Example: For AP exam preparation, a student must explain why the warmup treppe effect demonstrates the importance of temperature on enzyme activity in muscle physiology. What is the correct molecular explanation?
The treppe effect demonstrates that mild increases in temperature from repeated contractions enhance enzyme kinetics (myosin ATPase cycles faster, Ca2+-ATPase pumps more efficiently) and increase calcium availability in the sarcoplasm, resulting in progressively stronger twitches until a steady state is reached, illustrating why athletes warm up before performance.

  Q1. The correct sequence of events at the neuromuscular junction leading to contraction is:
    [ ] Contraction, ACh release, Ca2+ release, action potential
    [ ] Ca2+ release, ACh release, contraction, action potential
    [*] Nerve AP arrives, ACh released, binds receptors, muscle AP generated, Ca2+ released from SR, contraction
    [ ] The events occur randomly with no specific order
    Explanation: The complete signaling pathway proceeds in a strict sequence: a nerve action potential triggers ACh release, ACh binds to receptors on the motor end plate, generating a muscle action potential that travels along the sarcolemma and into T-tubules, triggering Ca2+ release from the SR to initiate cross-bridge cycling.

  Q2. Calcium ions for muscle contraction are released from the:
    [ ] The nerve terminal
    [*] Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR)
    [ ] Mitochondria
    [ ] T-tubules
    Explanation: The sarcoplasmic reticulum stores calcium in its terminal cisternae at high concentrations; when an action potential reaches the T-tubule, it activates voltage-sensitive dihydropyridine receptors that trigger ryanodine receptors on the SR to open, releasing Ca2+ into the sarcoplasm.

  Q3. The size principle of motor unit recruitment states that:
    [ ] All motor units fire simultaneously regardless of force needed
    [*] Smaller motor units are recruited first, with progressively larger units added as more force is needed
    [ ] The largest motor units are always recruited first
    [ ] Motor units are recruited randomly
    Explanation: The size principle (Henneman's principle) states that motor neurons are recruited in order of increasing size: small motor neurons with low thresholds innervating slow-twitch fibers are recruited first for fine motor control, and larger motor neurons innervating fast-twitch fibers are added only when greater force is required.

  Q4. The length-tension relationship shows maximum force production occurs when:
    [ ] There is no actin-myosin overlap
    [*] Sarcomeres are at their optimal resting length with maximal actin-myosin overlap
    [ ] The muscle is stretched to its maximum length
    [ ] The muscle is completely compressed
    Explanation: The length-tension relationship demonstrates that maximum isometric force is generated when sarcomeres are at their optimal resting length (approximately 2.0-2.6 micrometers), where the greatest number of cross-bridges can form between overlapping actin and myosin filaments.

  Q5. Type I (slow-twitch) muscle fibers are rich in:
    [*] Mitochondria and myoglobin, making them appear red
    [ ] Fast glycolytic enzymes
    [ ] Glycogen stores only
    [ ] No distinguishing features
    Explanation: Type I fibers contain abundant mitochondria for aerobic ATP production and high concentrations of myoglobin (an oxygen-storing protein), giving them their characteristic red color and making them highly fatigue-resistant for endurance activities.

  Q6. Type IIb (fast-twitch glycolytic) fibers are best suited for:
    [ ] Maintaining upright posture through prolonged low-intensity contractions
    [*] Short, explosive bursts of power such as sprinting or jumping
    [ ] Long-distance aerobic swimming and sustained endurance efforts
    [ ] Marathon running and other sustained oxidative endurance activities
    Explanation: Type IIb (or IIx) fibers have the fastest contraction speed, largest diameter, and greatest force production, relying primarily on anaerobic glycolysis; they fatigue rapidly but are ideal for activities requiring brief, maximal power output like sprinting, jumping, and throwing.

  Q7. Wave summation occurs when:
    [ ] Stimuli are separated by hours or days
    [ ] The muscle fully relaxes between each stimulus
    [*] A second stimulus arrives before the muscle fully relaxes from the first, producing greater total force
    [ ] Only one motor unit is activated
    Explanation: Wave summation (temporal summation) occurs when successive stimuli arrive at a frequency that does not allow complete relaxation between twitches, so each new contraction builds upon the residual tension of the previous one, producing progressively greater total force.

  Q8. Treppe (the staircase effect) describes:
    [ ] The immediate maximal force from the first contraction
    [ ] A decrease in force with each successive contraction
    [ ] Complete tetanus from rapid stimulation
    [*] A progressive increase in contraction strength with repeated stimuli at low frequency, as the muscle warms up
    Explanation: Treppe is the phenomenon where successive muscle twitches at the same stimulus intensity produce progressively stronger contractions, resulting from increased availability of Ca2+ in the sarcoplasm, increased enzyme activity from warming, and improved muscle fiber compliance, which is why athletes warm up before competition.

  Q9. Complete (fused) tetanus differs from incomplete (unfused) tetanus in that:
    [ ] Complete tetanus is weaker than incomplete tetanus because the sustained fusion of stimuli fatigues the available cross-bridges over time
    [ ] Incomplete tetanus involves no meaningful contraction because the gaps between stimuli prevent any useful summation of force
    [*] Complete tetanus produces a smooth, sustained contraction with no relaxation between stimuli, while incomplete tetanus shows partial relaxation between stimuli
    [ ] There is no physiological difference between complete and incomplete tetanus; both produce the same contractile force and relaxation pattern
    Explanation: In incomplete (unfused) tetanus, the stimulus frequency allows brief partial relaxation between contractions, producing a wavy force tracing; in complete (fused) tetanus, the frequency is so high that no relaxation occurs, producing a smooth, plateau of maximum sustainable force.

  Q10. Curare causes muscle paralysis by:
    [*] Competitively blocking nicotinic ACh receptors on the motor end plate, preventing depolarization
    [ ] Increasing ACh release at the NMJ
    [ ] Increasing calcium release from the SR
    [ ] Destroying the sarcoplasmic reticulum
    Explanation: Curare is a competitive antagonist that binds to nicotinic ACh receptors at the NMJ without activating them, blocking acetylcholine from binding and preventing depolarization of the motor end plate, resulting in flaccid paralysis, particularly dangerous when it affects the respiratory muscles.

  Q11. What is the length-tension relationship in muscle physiology?
    [*] The relationship between a muscle's resting sarcomere length and the maximum isometric force it can generate, determined by the degree of actin-myosin overlap
    [ ] The relationship between the number of muscle fibers and total body weight
    [ ] The relationship between nerve length and signal speed
    [ ] The relationship between muscle length and the speed of contraction
    Explanation: The length-tension relationship describes how the isometric force a muscle can produce depends on its initial sarcomere length; at optimal length, maximum actin-myosin overlap allows the most cross-bridges to form, producing peak force, while overstretched or over-compressed sarcomeres produce less force.

  Q12. What is motor unit recruitment?
    [*] The process of activating progressively more motor units to increase the force of whole-muscle contraction
    [ ] The process of building new motor neurons through exercise
    [ ] The replacement of damaged motor neurons after injury
    [ ] The deactivation of motor units during rest
    Explanation: Motor unit recruitment is the nervous system's primary mechanism for grading whole-muscle force by activating increasing numbers of motor units; since individual fibers follow the all-or-none law, force gradation depends on how many motor units are activated simultaneously.

  Q13. What is a depolarizing neuromuscular blocker?
    [ ] A drug that permanently increases muscle contraction strength by enhancing the sensitivity of actin-myosin cross-bridges to calcium, producing larger forces with each cycle
    [*] A drug like succinylcholine that initially activates (depolarizes) the motor end plate, then causes sustained depolarization and receptor desensitization, leading to temporary paralysis
    [ ] A drug that selectively blocks voltage-gated calcium channels in cardiac muscle only, reducing heart rate and contraction strength without any effect on skeletal muscle
    [ ] A vitamin supplement that enhances nerve conduction velocity at the neuromuscular junction, improving the speed and reliability of signal transmission to muscle fibers
    Explanation: Depolarizing neuromuscular blockers like succinylcholine mimic ACh by binding to nicotinic receptors and causing initial depolarization (producing brief fasciculations), but because they resist breakdown by acetylcholinesterase, they produce sustained depolarization that desensitizes the receptors, resulting in flaccid paralysis used during surgical intubation.

  Q14. What is the 'size principle' (Henneman's size principle)?
    [ ] Muscle fiber size determines the speed of contraction but not force
    [ ] Larger muscles always produce more force than smaller muscles
    [*] Motor neurons are recruited in order of increasing size: smaller, slow-twitch motor units first, then larger, fast-twitch motor units as greater force is demanded
    [ ] The largest motor units are always active, regardless of force requirement
    Explanation: Henneman's size principle states that motor neurons are recruited in an orderly fashion based on their size (and therefore threshold): small-diameter alpha motor neurons (innervating Type I fibers) are recruited first at low force levels, and progressively larger neurons (innervating Type II fibers) are added as more force is needed.

  Q15. What is the difference between isometric and isotonic contractions?
    [ ] They are the same type of contraction with different names
    [ ] Isometric means the muscle lengthens; isotonic means it shortens
    [*] In isometric contractions, force is generated without muscle length change; in isotonic contractions, the muscle changes length while maintaining relatively constant tension
    [ ] Isometric contractions only occur in smooth muscle; isotonic only in skeletal
    Explanation: Isometric ('same length') contractions generate force without changing muscle length (like holding a plank), while isotonic ('same tension') contractions involve muscle length changes against a constant load, including concentric (shortening) and eccentric (lengthening) subtypes.

  Q16. What does 'threshold stimulus' mean in muscle physiology?
    [ ] The stimulus needed to recruit every motor unit in the body simultaneously
    [ ] The maximum stimulus a muscle can receive before damage occurs
    [*] The minimum stimulus strength required to depolarize a muscle fiber enough to generate an action potential and trigger contraction
    [ ] The stimulus that causes a muscle to relax completely
    Explanation: The threshold stimulus is the minimum voltage (or stimulus strength) needed to depolarize a muscle fiber to the critical level that opens enough voltage-gated sodium channels to generate a self-propagating action potential, which then triggers the full contraction response according to the all-or-none principle.

  Q17. What is the latent period of a muscle twitch?
    [ ] The relaxation phase after contraction
    [ ] The recovery period between two separate exercise sessions
    [ ] The period of maximum force generation
    [*] The brief delay between the stimulus and the onset of contraction, during which excitation-contraction coupling occurs
    Explanation: The latent period is the short delay (typically 1-2 milliseconds) between the delivery of the stimulus and the beginning of visible contraction, during which the action potential propagates along the sarcolemma and T-tubules, Ca2+ is released from the SR, and cross-bridges begin to form.

  Q18. What is the role of the dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR) in excitation-contraction coupling?
    [ ] It serves as an active calcium pump on the sarcoplasmic reticulum membrane that resequesters calcium ions during the relaxation phase of muscle contraction
    [ ] It functions as an enzyme at the neuromuscular junction that rapidly breaks down acetylcholine after it has bound to the motor end plate receptors
    [*] It is a voltage-sensitive receptor on the T-tubule membrane that detects the action potential and triggers the ryanodine receptor on the SR to release calcium
    [ ] It forms the structural cross-bridge connection between the actin thin filament and the myosin thick filament during the power stroke of contraction
    Explanation: The dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR) is a voltage-gated calcium channel/sensor embedded in the T-tubule membrane that undergoes a conformational change when the action potential arrives, mechanically triggering the associated ryanodine receptor (RyR) on the SR membrane to open and release stored calcium into the sarcoplasm.

  Q19. What is the ryanodine receptor (RyR)?
    [ ] A protein that breaks down ATP to power the myosin head
    [ ] A regulatory protein on the thin filament
    [*] A calcium release channel on the sarcoplasmic reticulum membrane that opens in response to DHPR activation, flooding the sarcoplasm with Ca2+
    [ ] A receptor on the motor end plate that binds ACh
    Explanation: The ryanodine receptor is a large calcium release channel embedded in the SR membrane that opens when activated by the DHPR on the adjacent T-tubule, allowing the rapid efflux of stored calcium from the SR into the sarcoplasm to initiate the contraction process.

  Q20. What does 'summation of force' mean in whole-muscle physiology?
    [ ] The progressive decrease in force-generating capacity during repeated contractions, caused by the accumulation of metabolic waste products in the sarcoplasm
    [*] The combining of forces from multiple motor units (spatial summation) and/or from rapid successive stimuli (temporal summation) to produce graded force in a whole muscle
    [ ] The generation of contractile force by a single motor unit functioning in isolation, independent of any recruitment of additional motor units or frequency changes
    [ ] The baseline resting tension present in a completely relaxed muscle due to the inherent elasticity of the connective tissue elements and passive sarcolemma resistance
    Explanation: Force summation in whole muscles occurs through two mechanisms: spatial summation (recruiting more motor units to increase total force) and temporal summation (increasing stimulus frequency so twitches overlap and add together), allowing smooth, graded control of muscle force from delicate tasks to maximal efforts.
