--- Lesson 1.1: Levels of Organization (Cells → Tissues → Organs → Systems) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Levels of Organization (Cells → Tissues → Organs → Systems)
Levels of Structural Organization
The human body is organized from the smallest to largest levels:
  • Chemical level: Atoms combine into molecules (e.g., H₂O, DNA).  • Cellular level: Cells are the basic units of life (e.g., neurons, muscle cells).  • Tissue level: Groups of similar cells (4 types: epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous).  • Organ level: Two or more tissues form a functional unit (e.g., heart, liver).  • Organ system level: Organs work together (11 major systems).  • Organismal level: All systems cooperate to maintain life.

  Key Concepts: Anatomical Terminology (Planes, Directions, Cavities)
Anatomical TerminologyAnatomical Position
Standing erect, feet forward, palms facing anteriorly. All directional terms reference this position.
Directional Terms  • Superior/Inferior: Above/below.  • Anterior (ventral)/Posterior (dorsal): Front/back.  • Medial/Lateral: Toward/away from the midline.  • Proximal/Distal: Closer/farther from the trunk (for limbs).  • Superficial/Deep: Toward/away from the body surface.Body Planes  • Sagittal: Divides left/right. Midsagittal = equal halves.  • Frontal (coronal): Divides anterior/posterior.  • Transverse (horizontal): Divides superior/inferior (cross-section).Body Cavities
Dorsal cavity (cranial + vertebral) and ventral cavity (thoracic + abdominopelvic).

--- Lesson 1.3: Homeostasis & Feedback Mechanisms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Homeostasis & Feedback Mechanisms
Homeostasis & Feedback
Homeostasis: The body's ability to maintain a relatively stable internal environment despite external changes.
Components of a Feedback Loop  • Receptor (sensor): Detects a stimulus/change.  • Control center (integrator): Processes the signal, determines the response (often the brain).  • Effector: Carries out the response (muscles, glands).Types  • Negative feedback: Reverses the change (most common). Example: thermoregulation.  • Positive feedback: Amplifies the change until the process completes. Example: childbirth (oxytocin).

--- Lesson 1.4: Basic Histology (Tissue Types) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Basic Histology (Tissue Types)
Basic Histology — The Four Tissue Types1. Epithelial Tissue  • Covers body surfaces, lines cavities, forms glands.  • Classified by layers (simple vs. stratified) and cell shape (squamous, cuboidal, columnar).2. Connective Tissue  • Supports, protects, binds. Contains cells in an extracellular matrix.  • Types: loose (areolar), dense (tendons), cartilage, bone, blood.3. Muscle Tissue  • Generates force. Three types: skeletal (voluntary, striated), cardiac (involuntary, striated), smooth (involuntary, nonstriated).4. Nervous Tissue  • Transmits electrical signals. Neurons + supporting neuroglia (glial cells).

--- Lesson 1.5: Medical Imaging Techniques (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Medical Imaging Techniques
Medical Imaging TechniquesX-Ray
Uses ionizing radiation. Best for bones and detecting fractures. Low cost, fast.
CT (Computed Tomography)
Multiple X-ray images processed by computer to create cross-sectional slices. Good for soft tissue and bone.
MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
Uses strong magnets and radio waves. Superior soft-tissue contrast. No ionizing radiation.
Ultrasound
Uses sound waves. No radiation. Common for fetal imaging, heart (echocardiogram), and abdominal organs.
PET (Positron Emission Tomography)
Uses radioactive tracers to detect metabolic activity. Used in cancer diagnosis and brain studies.

--- Lesson 1.6: Case Studies in Homeostasis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Homeostasis
Case Studies in HomeostasisCase 1 — Dehydration
A runner loses excessive water through sweat. Osmoreceptors detect increased blood osmolarity → hypothalamus signals ADH release → kidneys reabsorb more water → blood osmolarity returns to normal.
Case 2 — Diabetes (Type 1)
Autoimmune destruction of beta cells → no insulin → blood glucose stays elevated → homeostatic failure without treatment.
Case 3 — Heatstroke
Body's cooling mechanisms overwhelmed → core temp rises above 40°C → positive feedback cycle (enzyme dysfunction worsens temp control) → medical emergency.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Anatomical Terminology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Anatomical Terminology
AP Prep: Anatomical Terminology
This lesson reviews high-yield anatomical terms and concepts that frequently appear on standardized exams.
Key AP Review Points  • Know all directional terms relative to anatomical position.  • Identify body regions: cranial, cervical, thoracic, abdominal, pelvic, upper/lower extremities.  • Serous membranes: parietal (lines wall), visceral (covers organ). Examples: pleura, pericardium, peritoneum.  • Abdominopelvic quadrants: RUQ, LUQ, RLQ, LLQ.

--- Lesson 1.7: AP Prep: Anatomical Terminology (Quiz) ---

  Q1. RUQ stands for:
    [ ] Right Upper Quadricep
    [*] Right Upper Quadrant
    [ ] Right Under Quarter
    [ ] Right Upper Quarter
    Explanation: Abdominopelvic quadrant: right upper quadrant.

  Q2. The liver is primarily in which quadrant?
    [ ] LUQ
    [ ] RLQ
    [*] RUQ
    [ ] LLQ
    Explanation: The liver occupies most of the right upper quadrant.

  Q3. The appendix is in the:
    [ ] RUQ
    [ ] LUQ
    [*] RLQ
    [ ] LLQ
    Explanation: Right lower quadrant — appendicitis pain localizes here.

  Q4. Serous membranes produce:
    [ ] Blood
    [ ] Mucus
    [*] Serous fluid (reduces friction)
    [ ] Bile
    Explanation: Serous fluid lubricates organs to reduce friction during movement.

  Q5. The visceral pericardium:
    [ ] Lines the chest wall
    [*] Directly covers the heart surface
    [ ] Surrounds the lungs
    [ ] Lines the abdomen
    Explanation: Visceral = adhered to the organ itself.

  Q6. The parietal pleura:
    [ ] Covers the lung surface
    [*] Lines the wall of the thoracic cavity
    [ ] Covers the heart
    [ ] Lines the abdomen
    Explanation: Parietal = attached to the body wall.

  Q7. The cervical region refers to the:
    [ ] Chest
    [ ] Abdomen
    [*] Neck

    Explanation: Cervical = neck region.

  Q8. The lumbar region is the:

    [*] Lower back

  Q9. The brachial region refers to the:
    [ ] Forearm

  Q10. The femoral region is the:
