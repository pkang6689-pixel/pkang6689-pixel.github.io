--- Lesson 9.1: Anatomy of the Urinary System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Anatomy of the Urinary System
Anatomy of the Urinary SystemComponents  • Kidneys (2): Bean-shaped, retroperitoneal. Cortex (outer), medulla (inner pyramids), pelvis (collects urine).  • Ureters: Transport urine from kidneys to bladder via peristalsis.  • Urinary Bladder: Stores urine; detrusor muscle; trigone (triangle between two ureteral openings and internal urethral orifice).  • Urethra: Carries urine from bladder to exterior. Female ~4 cm; male ~20 cm (passes through prostate).Nephron — Functional Unit
~1 million per kidney. Parts: glomerulus (Bowman's capsule), PCT, loop of Henle, DCT, collecting duct.

--- Lesson 9.2: Kidney Function & Filtration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Kidney Function & Filtration
Kidney Function & FiltrationThree Processes of Urine Formation  • Glomerular filtration: Passive, pressure-driven. GFR ~125 mL/min = ~180 L/day. Filters water, ions, glucose, amino acids, urea; retains proteins and cells.  • Tubular reabsorption: PCT (65% of filtrate), loop of Henle (water & ions), DCT & collecting duct (hormone-regulated). >99% of filtrate reabsorbed.  • Tubular secretion: H⁺, K⁺, drugs, toxins secreted from blood into tubular fluid.RAAS
Low BP → renin → angiotensinogen → angiotensin I → (ACE) → angiotensin II → vasoconstriction + aldosterone → Na⁺/H₂O retention → BP ↑.

--- Lesson 9.3: Fluid & Electrolyte Balance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fluid & Electrolyte Balance
Fluid & Electrolyte BalanceBody Fluid Compartments
Total body water ~60% of body weight. ICF (intracellular fluid) ~2/3; ECF (extracellular fluid) ~1/3 (plasma + interstitial).
Key Electrolytes  • Na⁺: Major ECF cation; regulates osmolarity and fluid volume.  • K⁺: Major ICF cation; essential for membrane potential, cardiac function.  • Ca²⁺: Muscle contraction, nerve signaling, clotting, bone.  • Acid-base: pH 7.35–7.45. Buffers (bicarbonate), lungs (CO₂), kidneys (H⁺/HCO₃⁻).

--- Lesson 9.4: Male Reproductive System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Male Reproductive System
Male Reproductive SystemAnatomy  • Testes: Produce sperm (seminiferous tubules) and testosterone (Leydig cells). Located in scrotum (cooler temperature).  • Epididymis: Sperm maturation and storage.  • Vas deferens → ejaculatory duct → urethra: Sperm transport pathway.  • Accessory glands: Seminal vesicles (fructose, prostaglandins), prostate (alkaline fluid), bulbourethral glands (pre-ejaculate).Spermatogenesis
Spermatogonia (2n) → primary spermatocyte → meiosis I → secondary → meiosis II → spermatids → spermatozoa. ~74 days. FSH + testosterone drive the process.

--- Lesson 9.5: Female Reproductive System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Female Reproductive System
Female Reproductive SystemAnatomy  • Ovaries: Produce oocytes and hormones (estrogen, progesterone). Oogenesis begins prenatally.  • Uterine (fallopian) tubes: Site of fertilization; fimbriae sweep oocyte in.  • Uterus: Endometrium (inner lining, sheds during menstruation), myometrium (smooth muscle).  • Vagina: Birth canal, receives penis.Menstrual Cycle (~28 days)
Follicular phase (FSH → follicle growth → estrogen ↑) → LH surge → ovulation → luteal phase (corpus luteum → progesterone ↑) → if no fertilization → corpus luteum degenerates → menstruation.

--- Lesson 9.6: Hormonal Regulation of Reproduction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hormonal Regulation of Reproduction
Hormonal Regulation of ReproductionHypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) Axis
GnRH (hypothalamus) → FSH + LH (anterior pituitary) → Gonads (testes/ovaries) → sex hormones → negative feedback to hypothalamus/pituitary.
Males
GnRH → LH → testosterone (Leydig); GnRH → FSH → spermatogenesis (Sertoli). Testosterone + inhibin = negative feedback.
Females
GnRH → FSH → follicle growth → estrogen. Mid-cycle: estrogen surge → positive feedback → LH surge → ovulation. Luteal: progesterone → negative feedback.

--- Lesson 9.7: Disorders of the Urinary & Reproductive Systems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Urinary & Reproductive Systems
Disorders of the Urinary & Reproductive SystemsUrinary Disorders  • UTI: Bacterial infection (often E. coli); more common in females (shorter urethra).  • Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis): Calcium oxalate most common. Severe flank pain (renal colic).  • Acute kidney injury (AKI): Sudden loss of function (prerenal, intrarenal, postrenal). Oliguria, ↑ creatinine/BUN.  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): Progressive loss of nephrons; stages 1-5; stage 5 = end-stage → dialysis or transplant.Reproductive Disorders  • STIs: Chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis, HPV, HIV, herpes.  • Infertility: ~15% of couples; multifactorial.

AP Prep: Renal PhysiologyExam Focus Areas  • Nephron structure and function (filtration, reabsorption, secretion).  • GFR regulation: myogenic mechanism, tubuloglomerular feedback, RAAS.  • Countercurrent multiplier and concentrating mechanism.  • Hormonal regulation: ADH, aldosterone, ANP, PTH.  • Acid-base balance: respiratory vs. metabolic; compensation.  • Urinalysis interpretation.

--- Lesson 9.8: AP Prep: Renal Physiology (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The countercurrent multiplier operates in the:

    [ ] DCT
    [*] Loop of Henle (descending and ascending limbs)

    [ ] Absent

  Q10. Inulin clearance measures:
