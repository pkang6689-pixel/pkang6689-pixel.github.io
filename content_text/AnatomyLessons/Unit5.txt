--- Lesson 5.1: Hormones & Chemical Signaling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hormones & Chemical Signaling
Hormones & Chemical SignalingWhat Are Hormones?
Chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands into the bloodstream; they act on distant target cells.
Types of Hormones  • Amino acid-derived: Thyroid hormones, catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine).  • Peptide/protein: Insulin, growth hormone, ADH, oxytocin.  • Steroid: Cortisol, estrogen, testosterone, aldosterone (derived from cholesterol).Mechanisms of Action  • Water-soluble hormones → bind surface receptors → second messenger (e.g., cAMP).  • Lipid-soluble (steroid/thyroid) → pass through membrane → bind intracellular/nuclear receptors → alter gene expression.

--- Lesson 5.2: Major Endocrine Glands (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Major Endocrine Glands
Major Endocrine Glands  • Pituitary (hypophysis): 'Master gland'; anterior (GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, prolactin), posterior (ADH, oxytocin — made in hypothalamus).  • Thyroid: T3, T4 (metabolism); calcitonin (lowers blood Ca²⁺).  • Parathyroids (4): PTH — raises blood Ca²⁺.  • Adrenals: Cortex (cortisol, aldosterone, androgens); Medulla (epinephrine, norepinephrine).  • Pancreas (endocrine): Islets of Langerhans — β-cells (insulin↓glucose), α-cells (glucagon↑glucose).  • Gonads: Ovaries (estrogen, progesterone); Testes (testosterone).  • Pineal gland: Melatonin (circadian rhythm).  • Thymus: Thymosin (T-cell maturation).

--- Lesson 5.3: Hormonal Regulation of Homeostasis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hormonal Regulation of Homeostasis
Hormonal Regulation of HomeostasisBlood Glucose Regulation
High glucose → β-cells release insulin → cells absorb glucose → blood glucose drops. Low glucose → α-cells release glucagon → liver releases glucose → blood glucose rises.
Calcium Homeostasis
High Ca²⁺ → calcitonin → Ca²⁺ into bone. Low Ca²⁺ → PTH → Ca²⁺ out of bone + ↑intestinal absorption + ↑renal reabsorption.
Water Balance
Dehydration → hypothalamus detects ↑osmolarity → ADH released → kidneys reabsorb more water.

--- Lesson 5.4: Feedback Loops in the Endocrine System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Feedback Loops in the Endocrine System
Feedback Loops in the Endocrine SystemNegative Feedback (Most Common)
Output reduces the stimulus. Example: ↑cortisol → inhibits hypothalamus (CRH) and anterior pituitary (ACTH) → less cortisol produced.
Positive Feedback (Rare)
Output amplifies the stimulus. Examples: oxytocin during labor (uterine contractions ↑ → more oxytocin → stronger contractions until delivery) and LH surge triggering ovulation.
Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axes
HPT axis (thyroid), HPA axis (adrenal), HPG axis (gonadal) — all use negative feedback to maintain set points.

--- Lesson 5.5: Disorders of the Endocrine System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Endocrine System
Disorders of the Endocrine System  • Diabetes mellitus: Type 1 (autoimmune β-cell destruction) and Type 2 (insulin resistance). Hyperglycemia → complications (neuropathy, retinopathy, nephropathy).  • Hyperthyroidism: Graves' disease (autoimmune → excess T3/T4). Symptoms: weight loss, tachycardia, exophthalmos.  • Hypothyroidism: Hashimoto's (autoimmune thyroid destruction). Symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance.  • Cushing's syndrome: Excess cortisol. Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency.  • Gigantism/Acromegaly: Excess GH before/after bone growth plate closure.

--- Lesson 5.6: Case Studies in Endocrinology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Endocrinology
Case Studies in EndocrinologyCase 1: A Patient with Uncontrolled Blood Sugar
A 45-year-old presents with polyuria, polydipsia, and weight loss despite increased appetite. Fasting glucose 280 mg/dL, HbA1c 10.5%. Diagnosis: Type 2 diabetes mellitus. Management: lifestyle changes + metformin + monitoring.
Case 2: Thyroid Dysfunction
A 30-year-old female with weight gain, fatigue, constipation, cold intolerance. TSH elevated, T4 low. Anti-TPO antibodies positive. Diagnosis: Hashimoto's thyroiditis. Treatment: levothyroxine.
Case 3: Adrenal Crisis
A patient on chronic prednisone suddenly stops medication → collapse, hypotension, hypoglycemia. Adrenal crisis due to HPA axis suppression. Treatment: IV hydrocortisone, fluid resuscitation.

--- Lesson 5.7: AP Prep: Hormone Pathways (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Hormone Pathways
AP Prep: Hormone PathwaysEssential Pathways for the Exam  • HPT axis: Hypothalamus (TRH) → Anterior pituitary (TSH) → Thyroid (T3/T4) → Negative feedback to hypothalamus/pituitary.  • HPA axis: Hypothalamus (CRH) → Anterior pituitary (ACTH) → Adrenal cortex (cortisol) → Negative feedback.  • HPG axis: Hypothalamus (GnRH) → Anterior pituitary (FSH/LH) → Gonads (estrogen/testosterone) → Negative feedback.  • Blood glucose: Insulin/glucagon antagonism.  • Calcium: PTH/calcitonin/vitamin D interplay.

--- Lesson 5.7: AP Prep: Hormone Pathways (Quiz) ---

  Q1. In the HPT axis, TRH is released by the:
    [ ] Thyroid
    [ ] Pituitary
    [*] Hypothalamus
    [ ] Adrenal gland
    Explanation: TRH = thyrotropin-releasing hormone from hypothalamus.

  Q2. TSH acts on the:
    [ ] Adrenals
    [*] Thyroid gland to produce T3/T4
    [ ] Pancreas
    [ ] Gonads
    Explanation: TSH stimulates thyroid hormone production.

  Q3. Negative feedback in the HPT axis: high T3/T4 inhibits:
    [ ] The thyroid directly
    [*] TRH (hypothalamus) and TSH (anterior pituitary)
    [ ] The gonads
    [ ] The adrenals
    Explanation: Long and short loop feedback.

  Q4. In the HPA axis, ACTH acts on the:
    [ ] Thyroid
    [*] Adrenal cortex to produce cortisol
    [ ] Medulla
    [ ] Pancreas
    Explanation: ACTH → cortisol (zona fasciculata).

  Q5. CRH release increases during:
    [ ] Sleep
    [*] Stress (physical, emotional, or physiological)
    [ ] Relaxation
    [ ] Low cortisol only
    Explanation: Stress activates the HPA axis.

  Q6. In the HPG axis, FSH stimulates:
    [ ] Cortisol production
    [*] Follicle development (females) and spermatogenesis (males)
    [ ] Thyroid activity
    [ ] Calcium release
    Explanation: FSH = follicle-stimulating hormone.

  Q7. LH triggers:
    [ ] Thyroid function
    [*] Ovulation (females) and testosterone production (males)
    [ ] Cortisol release
    [ ] Calcium reabsorption
    Explanation: Luteinizing hormone.

  Q8. A tropic hormone is one that:
    [ ] Acts on muscles
    [*] Stimulates another endocrine gland to secrete its hormone
    [ ] Is always a steroid
    [ ] Is produced by the gonads
    Explanation: TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH are tropic.

  Q9. The permissive effect means:
    [ ] Hormones block each other
    [*] One hormone makes target cells more responsive to another (e.g., thyroid potentiates catecholamines)
    [ ] No interaction
    [ ] Hormones are identical
    Explanation: Thyroid hormones enable catecholamine effects.

  Q10. Antagonistic hormones:
    [ ] Work together
    [*] Oppose each other (e.g., insulin ↓glucose vs. glucagon ↑glucose)
    [ ] Are identical
    [ ] Don't exist
    Explanation: Opposing effects maintain balance.

  Q11. Synergistic hormones:
    [ ] Oppose each other
    [*] Work together to produce a greater combined effect
    [ ] Cancel each other
    [ ] Don't exist
    Explanation: Combined effect > individual effects.

  Q12. If the anterior pituitary is destroyed, which would increase?
    [ ] TSH
    [ ] ACTH
    [*] None of the anterior pituitary hormones would increase; all would drop
    [ ] FSH
    Explanation: No pituitary = no tropic hormones.

  Q13. A patient with primary hypothyroidism has:
    [ ] Low TSH
    [*] High TSH, low T3/T4
    [ ] Normal labs
    [ ] Low ACTH
    Explanation: Pituitary compensates by raising TSH.

  Q14. A patient with secondary hypothyroidism (pituitary failure) has:
    [ ] High TSH
    [*] Low TSH, low T3/T4
    [ ] High T4
    [ ] Normal labs
    Explanation: Pituitary can't produce TSH.

  Q15. Identifying primary vs. secondary endocrine failure is important for:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Determining the location of the problem and guiding treatment
    [ ] Only research
    [ ] Only surgery
    Explanation: Primary = gland failure. Secondary = pituitary. Tertiary = hypothalamus.

  Q16. Water-soluble hormones have a _____ half-life compared to steroids.
    [ ] Longer
    [*] Shorter
    [ ] Equal
    [ ] No half-life

  Q17. Steroid hormones affect gene expression by:
    [ ] Using cAMP
    [*] Binding intracellular receptors that act as transcription factors
    [ ] Binding surface receptors
    [ ] Being enzymes
    Explanation: Steroid-receptor complex → alters DNA transcription.

  Q18. For the AP exam, you should be able to:
    [ ] Only memorize gland names
    [*] Trace complete hormone pathways, predict effects of disruptions, and interpret clinical data
    [ ] Only name hormones
    [ ] Only identify glands
    Explanation: Application-level understanding required.

  Q19. A question asks: 'What happens if the adrenal glands are removed?' The answer involves:

  Q20. Integrating endocrine pathways is essential because:
    [ ] Systems are isolated

    Explanation: Hormones regulate nearly all physiological processes.
