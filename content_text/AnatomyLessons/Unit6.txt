--- Lesson 6.1: Structure of the Heart (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Structure of the Heart
Structure of the HeartChambers & Valves  • 4 chambers: Right atrium (RA), right ventricle (RV), left atrium (LA), left ventricle (LV).  • AV valves: Tricuspid (right) and mitral/bicuspid (left) — prevent backflow from ventricles to atria.  • Semilunar valves: Pulmonary (RV → pulmonary artery) and aortic (LV → aorta).Layers & Coverings  • Pericardium: Protective sac. Epicardium: Outer. Myocardium: Thick muscular middle. Endocardium: Inner lining.Blood Flow Path
Body → vena cavae → RA → tricuspid → RV → pulmonary valve → lungs → pulmonary veins → LA → mitral → LV → aortic valve → aorta → body.

--- Lesson 6.2: Blood Vessels & Circulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Blood Vessels & Circulation
Blood Vessels & CirculationVessel Types  • Arteries: Thick walls, carry blood AWAY from the heart. Elastic (aorta) and muscular (distributing).  • Arterioles: Small arteries that regulate blood flow to capillary beds.  • Capillaries: Thin-walled (one cell thick), site of gas/nutrient/waste exchange.  • Venules → Veins: Return blood to the heart. Thinner walls; valves prevent backflow.Circulatory Routes
Pulmonary: RV → lungs → LA. Systemic: LV → body → RA. Hepatic portal: GI tract → liver → hepatic veins → IVC.

--- Lesson 6.3: Blood Composition & Function (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Blood Composition & Function
Blood Composition & FunctionPlasma (~55%)
Water (~92%), proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), electrolytes, nutrients, waste, gases, hormones.
Formed Elements (~45%)  • Red blood cells (RBCs/erythrocytes): Carry O₂ (hemoglobin); no nucleus; ~4.5–5.5 million/µL.  • White blood cells (WBCs/leukocytes): Immune defense; 5,000–10,000/µL. Types: neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils.  • Platelets (thrombocytes): Cell fragments; essential for clotting; 150,000–400,000/µL.

--- Lesson 6.4: Cardiac Cycle & Heartbeat Regulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cardiac Cycle & Heartbeat Regulation
Cardiac Cycle & Heartbeat RegulationCardiac Cycle
Systole: Contraction phase (ventricles pump blood). Diastole: Relaxation phase (ventricles fill).
Conduction System  • SA node (sinoatrial): 'Pacemaker'; initiates each heartbeat (~60-100 bpm).  • AV node: Delays signal briefly → ensures atria finish emptying.  • Bundle of His → bundle branches → Purkinje fibers: Conduct impulse rapidly through ventricles.ECG/EKG
P wave = atrial depolarization. QRS complex = ventricular depolarization. T wave = ventricular repolarization.

  Q1. Systole refers to:

    [*] Heart contraction (ventricles pumping blood)

  Q2. Diastole refers to:

  Q3. The SA node is called the pacemaker because it:
    [ ] Is artificial

--- Lesson 6.5: Blood Pressure & Homeostasis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Blood Pressure & Homeostasis
Blood Pressure & HomeostasisBlood Pressure Basics
Systolic pressure: Pressure during ventricular contraction (~120 mmHg). Diastolic: Pressure during relaxation (~80 mmHg). Normal = 120/80.
Regulation  • Short-term (neural): Baroreceptors in carotid sinus/aortic arch → ANS adjusts HR and vessel diameter.  • Long-term (renal/hormonal): RAAS system, ADH, ANP — regulate blood volume.Hypertension
Persistently elevated BP (≥130/80). Major risk for heart attack, stroke, kidney disease.

--- Lesson 6.5: Blood Pressure & Homeostasis (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Normal blood pressure is approximately:
    [ ] 140/90
    [*] 120/80 mmHg
    [ ] 100/60
    [ ] 160/100
    Explanation: 120 systolic / 80 diastolic.

  Q2. Systolic pressure measures pressure during:
    [ ] Ventricular relaxation
    [*] Ventricular contraction
    [ ] Atrial filling
    [ ] Sleep
    Explanation: Systole = contraction.

  Q3. Diastolic pressure measures pressure during:
    [ ] Ventricular contraction
    [*] Ventricular relaxation
    [ ] Exercise
    [ ] Atrial contraction
    Explanation: Diastole = relaxation.

  Q4. Baroreceptors detect:
    [ ] Blood oxygen
    [*] Changes in blood pressure (stretch of vessel walls)
    [ ] Temperature
    [ ] Glucose levels
    Explanation: Pressure sensors.

  Q5. When baroreceptors detect high BP, the response is:
    [ ] Increased HR
    [*] Decreased HR and vasodilation (parasympathetic activation)
    [ ] No response
    [ ] Increased vasoconstriction
    Explanation: Lower BP back to normal.

  Q6. The RAAS system is activated by:
    [ ] High BP
    [*] Low blood pressure (kidneys release renin)
    [ ] High glucose
    [ ] High calcium
    Explanation: Low BP → renin → angiotensin II → aldosterone.

  Q7. Angiotensin II causes:
    [ ] Vasodilation
    [*] Potent vasoconstriction and stimulates aldosterone release → ↑BP
    [ ] Lower BP
    [ ] Increased urination
    Explanation: Powerful vasoconstrictor.

  Q8. Aldosterone increases BP by:
    [ ] Dilating vessels
    [*] Causing kidneys to retain sodium and water → ↑blood volume
    [ ] Decreasing HR
    [ ] Reducing blood volume
    Explanation: Na⁺/H₂O retention → volume expansion.

  Q9. ADH (vasopressin) increases BP by:
    [ ] Dilating vessels
    [*] Causing kidneys to retain water → ↑blood volume
    [ ] Increasing urine output
    [ ] Reducing HR
    Explanation: Water retention.

  Q10. ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide) is released when:
    [ ] BP is low
    [*] Atria are stretched (high blood volume) → promotes sodium/water excretion → ↓BP
    [ ] Heart rate is low
    [ ] Dehydration occurs
    Explanation: Counter-regulatory to RAAS.

  Q11. Hypertension is defined as BP consistently at or above:
    [ ] 120/80
    [*] 130/80 mmHg
    [ ] 140/90
    [ ] 160/100
    Explanation: Current guidelines: ≥130/80.

  Q12. Hypertension is called the 'silent killer' because:
    [ ] It's not dangerous
    [*] It often has no symptoms until serious damage occurs (heart, brain, kidneys)
    [ ] It's always detected early
    [ ] It causes pain
    Explanation: Asymptomatically damages organs.

  Q13. Risk factors for hypertension include:
    [ ] Only genetics
    [*] Obesity, high sodium diet, sedentary lifestyle, smoking, genetics, and stress
    [ ] Only age
    [ ] Only stress
    Explanation: Multifactorial.

  Q14. Untreated hypertension can lead to:
    [ ] No complications
    [*] Heart failure, stroke, kidney disease, and retinopathy
    [ ] Only headaches
    [ ] Only dizziness
    Explanation: End-organ damage.

  Q15. ACE inhibitors treat hypertension by:
    [ ] Increasing angiotensin II
    [*] Blocking conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II → vasodilation → ↓BP
    [ ] Increasing HR
    [ ] Increasing salt retention
    Explanation: Block the angiotensin-converting enzyme.

  Q16. Hypotension (low BP) can cause:
    [ ] Hypertension
    [*] Dizziness, fainting (syncope), and organ underperfusion
    [ ] No symptoms ever
    [ ] High blood sugar
    Explanation: Insufficient blood flow to brain.

  Q17. Orthostatic hypotension is:
    [ ] High BP when standing
    [*] A sudden BP drop when standing up (gravity pools blood in legs)
    [ ] Normal BP change
    [ ] Chronic hypertension
    Explanation: Common in elderly and dehydrated patients.

  Q18. Mean arterial pressure (MAP) is important because it:
    [ ] Is irrelevant
    [*] Reflects the average pressure driving blood to organs (must stay above ~60 mmHg)
    [ ] Only matters in surgery
    [ ] Is the same as systolic
    Explanation: MAP ≈ diastolic + 1/3(systolic − diastolic).

  Q19. Lifestyle modifications for hypertension include:
    [ ] None
    [*] DASH diet (low sodium), regular exercise, weight loss, limiting alcohol, stress management
    [ ] Only medication
    [ ] Only surgery
    Explanation: First-line treatment approach.

  Q20. Understanding BP regulation is essential because:
    [ ] It's not important
    [*] Hypertension is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease worldwide
    [ ] Only for cardiologists
    [ ] Only for research
    Explanation: Global health priority.

--- Lesson 6.6: Disorders of the Circulatory System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Circulatory System
Disorders of the Circulatory System  • Coronary artery disease (CAD): Atherosclerosis of coronary arteries → angina, myocardial infarction (heart attack).  • Heart failure: Heart can't pump effectively; systolic (reduced ejection fraction) or diastolic (preserved EF).  • Valvular disease: Stenosis (narrowing) or regurgitation (leaking).  • Peripheral artery disease (PAD): Atherosclerosis in limb arteries → claudication.  • Aneurysm: Weakened arterial wall bulges → risk of rupture.

--- Lesson 6.6: Disorders of the Circulatory System (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Coronary artery disease is caused primarily by:
    [ ] Genetics alone
    [*] Atherosclerosis (plaque buildup) in the coronary arteries
    [ ] Valve disease
    [ ] Infection
    Explanation: Plaque narrows coronary lumens.

  Q2. A myocardial infarction occurs when:
    [ ] The heart slows
    [*] A coronary artery is blocked, causing death of heart muscle tissue

  Q5. Heart failure means:
    [ ] The heart stops completely
    [*] The heart cannot pump efficiently enough to meet the body's needs
    [ ] A heart attack

  Q6. Symptoms of heart failure include:
    [ ] No symptoms

  Q11. Valvular regurgitation means:
    [ ] A blocked valve

  Q17. A pacemaker is used to treat:
    [ ] Heart failure

  Q18. Heart valve replacement may be needed for:
    [ ] Normal valves
    [*] Severe stenosis or regurgitation that cannot be managed medically
    [ ] Mild murmurs
    [ ] Hypertension
    Explanation: Mechanical or biological valve prostheses.

  Q19. Prevention of cardiovascular disease focuses on:
    [ ] Nothing

    [ ] Only medication

  Q20. Cardiovascular disease is the _____ cause of death worldwide.

    Explanation: CVD = #1 killer globally.

--- Lesson 6.7: Case Studies in Cardiovascular Health (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Cardiovascular Health
Case Studies in Cardiovascular HealthCase 1: Acute MI
A 62-year-old male with chest pain radiating to left arm, diaphoresis, ECG showing ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF. Troponin elevated. Diagnosis: inferior STEMI. Treatment: emergent PCI (percutaneous coronary intervention).
Case 2: Congestive Heart Failure
A 70-year-old with progressive dyspnea, pedal edema, and pulmonary crackles. Echocardiogram shows ejection fraction 25%. Diagnosis: systolic heart failure. Management: ACE inhibitor, beta-blocker, diuretic.
Case 3: Hypertensive Emergency
BP 220/130 with headache and blurred vision. Requires IV antihypertensives with careful BP reduction.

--- Lesson 6.7: Case Studies in Cardiovascular Health (Quiz) ---

  Q1. ST elevation on an ECG indicates:
    [ ] Normal heart
    [*] Acute, complete coronary artery occlusion (STEMI)
    [ ] A slow heart rate

    [ ] Normal heart health

    [ ] 25%

  Q5. In the heart failure case, EF of 25% indicates:
    [ ] Normal function
    [*] Severe systolic dysfunction (reduced pumping ability)

  Q7. Diuretics in heart failure help by:
    [ ] Increasing blood volume

  Q11. In the MI case, aspirin is given to:

  Q12. After an MI, long-term medications typically include:
    [ ] Nothing

    [ ] Only a statin

  Q13. B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) is elevated in:
    [ ] Normal health

--- Lesson 6.8: AP Prep: ECG Interpretation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: ECG Interpretation
AP Prep: ECG InterpretationECG Waveforms Review  • P wave: Atrial depolarization (atrial contraction).  • PR interval: Time from atrial depolarization to ventricular depolarization (0.12–0.20 s).  • QRS complex: Ventricular depolarization (0.06–0.10 s).  • T wave: Ventricular repolarization.  • QT interval: Total ventricular electrical activity.Common Findings
Normal sinus rhythm, sinus bradycardia, sinus tachycardia, atrial fibrillation (no P waves, irregular), ST elevation (MI), heart block.

--- Lesson 6.8: AP Prep: ECG Interpretation (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The P wave on an ECG represents:
    [ ] Ventricular contraction
    [*] Atrial depolarization
    [ ] Ventricular relaxation
    [ ] Heart failure
    Explanation: P = atria depolarize.

  Q2. A normal PR interval is:
    [ ] 0.04-0.08 s
    [*] 0.12-0.20 s
    [ ] 0.30-0.40 s
    [ ] 0.50-0.60 s
    Explanation: Represents conduction through AV node.

  Q3. A prolonged PR interval (>0.20 s) suggests:
    [ ] Normal conduction
    [*] First-degree AV block (delayed AV conduction)
    [ ] Atrial fibrillation
    [ ] Ventricular tachycardia
    Explanation: AV node conducts slowly.

  Q4. The QRS complex represents:
    [ ] Atrial activity
    [*] Ventricular depolarization
    [ ] Atrial repolarization
    [ ] Valve closure
    Explanation: Largest deflection on ECG.

  Q5. A wide QRS (>0.12 s) may indicate:
    [ ] Normal conduction
    [*] Bundle branch block or ventricular origin of the rhythm
    [ ] Fast heart rate
    [ ] Atrial fibrillation
    Explanation: Abnormal ventricular conduction.

  Q6. The T wave represents:
    [ ] Atrial depolarization
    [*] Ventricular repolarization
    [ ] Atrial repolarization
    [ ] Ventricular depolarization
    Explanation: T = ventricles resetting.

  Q7. ST elevation on an ECG suggests:
    [ ] Normal finding
    [*] Acute myocardial injury (STEMI)
    [ ] Bradycardia
    [ ] Atrial fibrillation
    Explanation: ST elevation = urgent.

  Q8. ST depression may indicate:
    [ ] Normal
    [*] Myocardial ischemia (reduced blood flow without complete blockage)
    [ ] MI
    [ ] Normal variant
    Explanation: Ischemia without infarction.

  Q9. In normal sinus rhythm:
    [ ] There are no P waves
    [*] Each P wave is followed by a QRS, at a regular rate of 60-100 bpm
    [ ] QRS is absent
    [ ] T waves are absent
    Explanation: Normal pattern.

  Q10. Sinus bradycardia shows:
    [ ] Rate >100
    [*] Normal P-QRS pattern but rate <60 bpm
    [ ] No P waves
    [ ] Wide QRS
    Explanation: Slow but normal rhythm.

  Q11. Sinus tachycardia shows:
    [ ] Rate <60
    [*] Normal P-QRS pattern but rate >100 bpm
    [ ] No P waves
    [ ] ST elevation
    Explanation: Fast but normal rhythm.

  Q12. Atrial fibrillation on ECG shows:
    [ ] Regular P waves
    [*] No identifiable P waves, irregularly irregular rhythm
    [ ] Wide QRS
    [ ] ST elevation
    Explanation: Chaotic atrial activity.

  Q13. In second-degree AV block (Type I/Wenckebach):
    [ ] PR is constant
    [*] PR interval progressively lengthens until a QRS is dropped
    [ ] There are no P waves
    [ ] QRS is absent
    Explanation: Progressive PR prolongation then dropped beat.

  Q14. In third-degree (complete) heart block:
    [ ] Normal conduction
    [*] P waves and QRS complexes are independent (no AV conduction)
    [ ] No P waves
    [ ] No QRS
    Explanation: Atria and ventricles beat independently.

  Q15. Ventricular tachycardia (VT) shows:
    [ ] Narrow QRS
    [*] Wide, regular QRS complexes at a fast rate; life-threatening
    [ ] Normal P waves
    [ ] Slow rate
    Explanation: Can deteriorate to VFib.

  Q16. Ventricular fibrillation shows:
    [ ] Normal rhythm
    [*] Chaotic, irregular waveform with no identifiable P, QRS, or T; cardiac arrest
    [ ] Slow regular rhythm
    [ ] ST elevation only
    Explanation: No effective cardiac output.

  Q17. The QT interval represents:
    [ ] Atrial activity only
    [*] Total duration of ventricular depolarization + repolarization
    [ ] P wave duration
    [ ] Only QRS
    Explanation: Prolonged QT increases arrhythmia risk.

  Q18. A 12-lead ECG provides:
    [ ] One view
    [*] Multiple views of the heart's electrical activity from different angles
    [ ] Only heart rate
    [ ] Only rhythm
    Explanation: Helps localize ischemia/injury.

  Q19. To interpret an ECG systematically:
    [ ] Guess the rhythm
    [*] Assess rate, rhythm, P waves, PR interval, QRS width, ST/T changes systematically
    [ ] Only look at one lead
    [ ] Only check heart rate
    Explanation: Systematic approach prevents missed findings.

  Q20. ECG interpretation skills are essential for:
    [ ] Only cardiologists
    [*] All healthcare providers in emergency and acute care settings
    [ ] Only researchers
    [ ] Nobody
    Explanation: Basic ECG is a core clinical skill.
