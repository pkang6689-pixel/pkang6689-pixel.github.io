--- Lesson 8.1: Anatomy of Digestive Organs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Anatomy of Digestive Organs
Anatomy of Digestive OrgansGI Tract (Alimentary Canal)  • Mouth → pharynx → esophagus → stomach → small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum) → large intestine (cecum, colon, rectum) → anus.GI Wall Layers  • Mucosa: Innermost; epithelium, secretion/absorption. Submucosa: Connective tissue, blood vessels, nerves.  • Muscularis: Smooth muscle (circular + longitudinal) for peristalsis. Serosa: Outermost covering (visceral peritoneum).Accessory Organs
Salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, pancreas — secrete into the GI tract via ducts.

--- Lesson 8.2: Mechanical & Chemical Digestion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mechanical & Chemical Digestion
Mechanical & Chemical DigestionMechanical Digestion
Mastication (chewing), churning in the stomach, segmentation in the small intestine. Increases surface area for enzymes.
Chemical Digestion  • Carbohydrates: Salivary amylase (mouth) → pancreatic amylase (SI) → maltase, sucrase, lactase (brush border) → monosaccharides.  • Proteins: Pepsin (stomach, pH 2) → pancreatic trypsin, chymotrypsin (SI) → peptidases (brush border) → amino acids.  • Fats: Bile salts emulsify → pancreatic lipase → monoglycerides + fatty acids.

--- Lesson 8.3: Absorption of Nutrients (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Absorption of Nutrients
Absorption of NutrientsSmall Intestine = Primary Absorption Site  • Villi and microvilli provide ~200 m² surface area.  • Monosaccharides & amino acids: Absorbed into capillaries → portal vein → liver.  • Fatty acids & monoglycerides: Absorbed into enterocytes → re-esterified to triglycerides → packaged into chylomicrons → enter lacteals (lymphatic) → eventually blood.  • Vitamins: Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) absorbed with fats; water-soluble absorbed with water.Large Intestine
Absorbs remaining water and electrolytes; houses gut microbiome; forms and stores feces.

--- Lesson 8.4: Accessory Organs of Digestion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Accessory Organs of Digestion
Accessory Organs of DigestionLiver
Largest internal organ. Functions: bile production, detoxification, protein synthesis (albumin, clotting factors), glycogen storage, bilirubin metabolism.
Gallbladder
Stores and concentrates bile; releases it into the duodenum upon CCK stimulation.
Pancreas (Exocrine)
Produces pancreatic juice: bicarbonate (neutralizes acid), amylase, lipase, trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, nucleases.
Salivary Glands
Parotid, submandibular, sublingual — produce saliva (water, amylase, lysozyme, mucin).

--- Lesson 8.5: Disorders of the Digestive System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Digestive System
Disorders of the Digestive System  • GERD: Gastroesophageal reflux — acid damages esophagus. Treatment: PPIs, lifestyle changes.  • Peptic ulcer: Mucosal erosion in stomach/duodenum; often H. pylori or NSAID-related.  • IBD: Crohn's disease (any GI segment, transmural) vs. ulcerative colitis (colon only, mucosal).  • IBS: Functional disorder — altered motility without structural cause.  • Colorectal cancer: Third most common cancer; screening via colonoscopy.  • Celiac disease: Autoimmune response to gluten → villous atrophy → malabsorption.

--- Lesson 8.6: Case Studies in Nutrition & GI Health (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Nutrition & GI Health
Case Studies in Nutrition & GI HealthCase 1: Iron-Deficiency Anemia
A 28-year-old vegetarian female with fatigue, pallor, low Hb, low ferritin, microcytic hypochromic RBCs. Diagnosis: iron-deficiency anemia. Treatment: iron supplementation + vitamin C to enhance absorption + dietary counseling.
Case 2: Acute Pancreatitis
A 50-year-old male with epigastric pain radiating to back after heavy alcohol use. Elevated lipase. Diagnosis: acute pancreatitis. Management: NPO (nothing by mouth), IV fluids, pain control, alcohol cessation.
Case 3: Celiac Disease
A child with failure to thrive, chronic diarrhea, and abdominal distension. Anti-tTG antibodies positive; duodenal biopsy shows villous atrophy. Treatment: strict gluten-free diet.

  Q1. Iron-deficiency anemia presents with:
    [ ] Only jaundice
    [*] Fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath, and microcytic hypochromic RBCs

  Q2. Ferritin levels in iron-deficiency anemia are:
    [ ] High

    [ ] Amylase
    [*] Lipase (elevated 3× or more above normal)

    [ ] Bilirubin

  Q5. NPO (nil per os) in pancreatitis aims to:
    [ ] Reduce weight
    [*] Rest the pancreas by stopping oral intake (reduces enzyme secretion)
    [ ] Cure the disease

  Q6. The most common causes of acute pancreatitis are:
    [ ] Diet and exercise
    [*] Gallstones and alcohol

    [ ] Genetics

  Q7. In the celiac case, anti-tTG antibodies are:
    [ ] Normal

    [ ] Normal growth
    [*] Malabsorption (e.g., celiac disease) or inadequate nutrition

  Q11. Chronic alcohol use damages the pancreas by:

  Q12. Gluten-free alternatives include:
    [ ] Only lettuce

    [ ] Nothing
    Explanation: Many naturally gluten-free grains exist.

  Q13. Malabsorption from celiac disease can cause deficiency of:
    [ ] Only iron

AP Prep: Digestive PhysiologyKey Topics for Exam  • GI tract anatomy and wall layers.  • Enzymes: source, substrate, product for carbohydrates, proteins, fats.  • Absorption: mechanisms (active transport, facilitated diffusion, osmosis); site specificity.  • Hormonal regulation: gastrin, CCK, secretin, GIP.  • Liver functions and accessory organ roles.  • Common disorders: GERD, ulcers, IBD, celiac.

--- Lesson 8.7: AP Prep: Digestive Physiology (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Salivary amylase digests _____ to _____.
    [ ] Fat to fatty acids
    [*] Starch to maltose
    [ ] Protein to amino acids
    [ ] Sucrose to glucose
    Explanation: Begins carb digestion in mouth.

  Q2. Pepsin digests _____ in the _____.
    [ ] Fat in the duodenum
    [*] Protein in the stomach
    [ ] Starch in the mouth
    [ ] Nucleic acids in the ileum
    Explanation: Pepsin + HCl in stomach.

  Q3. Pancreatic juice enters the duodenum via the:
    [ ] Stomach
    [*] Hepatopancreatic ampulla (ampulla of Vater)
    [ ] Ileum
    [ ] Jejunum
    Explanation: Common bile duct + pancreaticduct open here.

  Q4. Which hormone stimulates HCl production?
    [ ] Secretin
    [ ] CCK
    [*] Gastrin
    [ ] GIP
    Explanation: Gastrin from G cells.

  Q5. CCK stimulates:
    [ ] Gastric acid secretion
    [*] Gallbladder contraction + pancreatic enzyme release
    [ ] Salivary secretion
    [ ] Colonic motility
    Explanation: Response to fat and protein.

  Q6. Secretin stimulates:
    [ ] Acid secretion
    [*] Pancreatic bicarbonate secretion (neutralizes acid)
    [ ] Bile production
    [ ] Gastric motility
    Explanation: Response to acid in duodenum.

  Q7. GIP inhibits:
    [ ] Insulin secretion
    [*] Gastric acid secretion and motility; also stimulates insulin release
    [ ] Bile secretion
    [ ] Pancreatic function
    Explanation: Gastric Inhibitory Peptide.

  Q8. Glucose is absorbed in the SI by:
    [ ] Simple diffusion
    [*] Sodium-dependent co-transport (SGLT1) and facilitated diffusion (GLUT2)
    [ ] Osmosis
    [ ] Endocytosis
    Explanation: Active transport on apical, facilitated on basolateral.

  Q9. Amino acids are absorbed by:
    [ ] Passive diffusion only
    [*] Active transport and co-transport mechanisms
    [ ] Osmosis
    [ ] Through lacteals
    Explanation: Multiple amino acid transporters.

  Q10. Fatty acids are absorbed into enterocytes by:
    [ ] Active transport
    [ ] Endocytosis
    [*] Simple diffusion (lipid-soluble) then repackaged into chylomicrons
    [ ] Facilitated diffusion
    Explanation: Lipids cross membranes easily.

  Q11. The liver converts excess glucose to _____ for storage.
    [ ] Fat only
    [*] Glycogen (glycogenesis)
    [ ] Protein
    [ ] Amino acids
    Explanation: Glycogen = liver and muscle glucose reserve.

  Q12. Gluconeogenesis in the liver is:
    [ ] Making glycogen
    [*] Making new glucose from non-carbohydrate sources (amino acids, lactate, glycerol)
    [ ] Glycogen breakdown
    [ ] Fat synthesis
    Explanation: Maintains blood glucose during fasting.

  Q13. The cephalic phase of digestion is triggered by:
    [ ] Food in the stomach
    [*] Sight, smell, thought of food (vagus nerve stimulation)
    [ ] Food in the intestines
    [ ] Chyme in the colon
    Explanation: Brain anticipates food → prepares digestion.

  Q14. The gastric phase of digestion occurs when:
    [ ] Food enters the mouth
    [*] Food enters the stomach (stretch + chemical stimulation → gastrin release)
    [ ] Food enters the intestines
    [ ] Food is being chewed
    Explanation: Stomach distension triggers acid and enzyme release.

  Q15. The intestinal phase of digestion:
    [ ] Increases gastric activity
    [*] Mostly inhibits gastric activity (so the duodenum isn't overwhelmed)
    [ ] Is involuntary only
    [ ] Doesn't exist
    Explanation: Slow gastric emptying to match intestinal capacity.

  Q16. Bilirubin in feces gives them their:
    [ ] Red color
    [*] Brown color (bilirubin → stercobilin by gut bacteria)
    [ ] Yellow color
    [ ] Green color
    Explanation: Bacterial bile pigment metabolism.

  Q17. Short bowel syndrome results in:
    [ ] Improved absorption
    [*] Malabsorption due to insufficient intestinal length (e.g., after surgical resection)
    [ ] Constipation
    [ ] No symptoms
    Explanation: Less SI = less absorptive surface.

  Q18. For the AP exam, you should be able to:
    [ ] Only list enzymes

    [ ] Only name organs
    [ ] Memorize pH values only

  Q19. The vagus nerve's role in digestion includes:
    [ ] No role
    [*] Stimulating gastric secretion, motility, and pancreatic output (parasympathetic)
    [ ] Only inhibiting digestion
    [ ] Only sensing pain
    Explanation: Parasympathetic = rest and digest.

  Q20. Digestive physiology integrates with:
    [ ] No other systems
    [*] Endocrine (hormones), nervous (vagal control), circulatory (nutrient transport), and immune systems
    [ ] Only muscles
    [ ] Only the brain
    Explanation: Whole-body integration.
