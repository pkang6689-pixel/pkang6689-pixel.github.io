--- Lesson 2.1: Bone Structure & Function (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bone Structure & Function
Bone Structure & FunctionFunctions of Bone  • Support, protection, movement (lever for muscles), mineral storage (Ca²⁺, PO₄³⁻), blood cell formation (hematopoiesis), energy storage (yellow marrow fat).Gross Anatomy of a Long Bone  • Diaphysis: Shaft; compact bone surrounding the medullary cavity (yellow marrow).  • Epiphysis: Ends; spongy (cancellous) bone covered by articular cartilage.  • Epiphyseal plate/line: Growth plate (cartilage in children; ossified line in adults).  • Periosteum: Outer fibrous membrane covering bone; rich in blood vessels and nerves.  • Endosteum: Thin membrane lining the medullary cavity.

--- Lesson 2.2: Types of Bones (Long, Short, Flat, Irregular) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Bones (Long, Short, Flat, Irregular)
Classification of Bones by Shape  • Long bones: Longer than wide; shaft + two ends. Examples: femur, humerus, phalanges.  • Short bones: Roughly cube-shaped. Examples: carpals (wrist), tarsals (ankle).  • Flat bones: Thin, flattened, often curved. Examples: skull bones, sternum, scapula, ribs.  • Irregular bones: Complex shapes. Examples: vertebrae, hip bones, some facial bones.  • Sesamoid bones: Embedded in tendons. Example: patella (kneecap).

--- Lesson 2.3: Axial Skeleton (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Axial Skeleton
The Axial Skeleton
The axial skeleton (80 bones) forms the central axis: skull, vertebral column, thoracic cage.
Skull (22 bones)  • Cranial bones (8): frontal, parietal (2), temporal (2), occipital, sphenoid, ethmoid.  • Facial bones (14): mandible, maxilla (2), zygomatic (2), nasal (2), lacrimal (2), palatine (2), inferior nasal conchae (2), vomer.Vertebral Column (26 bones)  • Cervical (7), Thoracic (12), Lumbar (5), Sacrum (1, fused 5), Coccyx (1, fused 3–5).Thoracic Cage  • Sternum (manubrium, body, xiphoid), 12 pairs of ribs: true (1–7), false (8–10), floating (11–12).

--- Lesson 2.4: Appendicular Skeleton (Summary) ---

The Appendicular Skeleton

--- Lesson 2.5: Joints & Movement (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Joints & Movement
Joints (Articulations) & MovementClassification by Structure  • Fibrous: No joint cavity; bones joined by fibers (e.g., skull sutures).  • Cartilaginous: Bones joined by cartilage (e.g., intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis).  • Synovial: Joint cavity with synovial fluid; most movable (e.g., knee, shoulder, hip).Types of Synovial Joints  • Plane (gliding), Hinge (elbow), Pivot (atlas/axis), Condyloid (wrist), Saddle (thumb), Ball-and-socket (hip, shoulder).Movements
Flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, rotation, circumduction, pronation/supination.

--- Lesson 2.6: Bone Growth & Remodeling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bone Growth & Remodeling
Bone Growth & RemodelingOssification  • Intramembranous: Bone forms directly from mesenchyme (flat bones of skull).  • Endochondral: Bone replaces a hyaline cartilage model (most bones).Long Bone Growth  • Length: Epiphyseal plate cartilage proliferates → ossifies progressively.  • Width: Appositional growth — osteoblasts add bone to the periosteal surface.Remodeling
Continuous process: osteoclasts resorb old bone, osteoblasts deposit new bone. Regulated by hormones (PTH, calcitonin) and mechanical stress (Wolff's law).

--- Lesson 2.7: Disorders of the Skeletal System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Skeletal System
Disorders of the Skeletal SystemCommon Conditions  • Osteoporosis: Bone mass loss → fragile bones; common in postmenopausal women.  • Osteoarthritis (OA): Wear-and-tear degeneration of articular cartilage.  • Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA): Autoimmune destruction of joint synovial membranes.  • Fractures: Complete/incomplete, open/closed, stress, pathological.  • Osteogenesis imperfecta: Genetic disorder; brittle bones (collagen defect).  • Paget's disease: Excessive/disorganized bone remodeling.

--- Lesson 2.7: Disorders of the Skeletal System (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Osteoporosis is characterized by:
    [ ] Excess bone
    [*] Decreased bone density and increased fracture risk
    [ ] Joint inflammation
    [ ] Muscle weakness only
    Explanation: Bone resorption outpaces formation.

  Q2. The population most at risk for osteoporosis:
    [ ] Young men

  Q11. Rickets in children is caused by:

  Q12. DEXA scans are used to diagnose:

--- Lesson 2.8: AP Prep: Bone Identification (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Bone Identification
AP Prep: Bone Identification
Review of high-yield bones, landmarks, and markings frequently tested on AP and standardized exams.
Key Bone Landmarks (Markings)  • Foramen: Opening for blood vessels or nerves (e.g., foramen magnum).  • Process: Bony projection (e.g., spinous process of vertebrae).  • Fossa: Depression (e.g., glenoid fossa of scapula).  • Condyle: Rounded articular surface (e.g., femoral condyles).  • Tuberosity: Roughened area for muscle/ligament attachment (e.g., tibial tuberosity).
