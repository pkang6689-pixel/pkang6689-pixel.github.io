--- Lesson 4.1: Neurons & Neuroglia (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Neurons & Neuroglia
Neurons & NeurogliaNeuron Structure  • Cell body (soma): Contains the nucleus; integrates incoming signals.  • Dendrites: Short, branching extensions that receive signals.  • Axon: Single long process that conducts impulses away from the cell body.  • Myelin sheath: Insulating lipid layer (Schwann cells in PNS, oligodendrocytes in CNS); speeds conduction.  • Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps in myelin where saltatory (jumping) conduction occurs.Neuroglia (Glial Cells)  • Astrocytes: Support neurons, blood-brain barrier. Oligodendrocytes: CNS myelin. Schwann cells: PNS myelin.  • Microglia: Immune defense in CNS. Ependymal cells: Line ventricles, produce CSF.

--- Lesson 4.2: Action Potentials & Synaptic Transmission (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Action Potentials & Synaptic Transmission
Action Potentials & Synaptic TransmissionResting Membrane Potential
At rest: −70 mV (inside negative). Maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in).
Action Potential Phases  • Depolarization: Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes in → membrane becomes positive (~+30 mV).  • Repolarization: Na⁺ channels close, K⁺ channels open → K⁺ exits → membrane returns to negative.  • Hyperpolarization: Brief overshoot below −70 mV before return to rest.Synaptic Transmission
AP reaches axon terminal → Ca²⁺ enters → vesicles release neurotransmitter → binds postsynaptic receptors → excitatory or inhibitory response.

--- Lesson 4.2: Action Potentials & Synaptic Transmission (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The resting membrane potential is approximately:
    [ ] 0 mV
    [*] −70 mV
    [ ] +30 mV
    [ ] −120 mV
    Explanation: Inside of neuron is negative at rest.

  Q2. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump moves:
    [ ] 3 K⁺ out, 2 Na⁺ in
    [*] 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in
    [ ] Equal amounts
    [ ] No ions
    Explanation: 3 sodium out, 2 potassium in per cycle.

  Q3. During depolarization:
    [ ] K⁺ channels open
    [*] Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes into the cell
    [ ] The cell becomes more negative
    [ ] Nothing changes
    Explanation: Na⁺ influx makes interior positive.

  Q4. The threshold potential is approximately:
    [ ] −70 mV
    [ ] 0 mV
    [*] −55 mV
    [ ] +30 mV
    Explanation: Must reach ~−55 mV to trigger an AP.

  Q5. During repolarization:
    [ ] Na⁺ continues entering
    [*] K⁺ channels open → K⁺ leaves the cell
    [ ] Ca²⁺ enters
    [ ] The cell stays positive
    Explanation: K⁺ efflux restores negative potential.

  Q6. Action potentials follow the all-or-none principle, meaning:
    [ ] Partial APs are possible
    [*] Either a full AP fires or none at all
    [ ] All neurons fire simultaneously
    [ ] There is no threshold
    Explanation: Once threshold is reached, full AP always occurs.

  Q7. Saltatory conduction occurs in:
    [ ] Unmyelinated axons
    [*] Myelinated axons (impulse jumps between nodes of Ranvier)

    [ ] Cell bodies
    Explanation: Myelin enables jumping conduction.

  Q8. At the synapse, the neurotransmitter is released from:
    [ ] Dendrites
    [*] The presynaptic axon terminal (vesicles)

  Q9. Before neurotransmitter release, _____ ions enter the axon terminal.
    [ ] Na⁺

  Q10. An excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP):
    [ ] Hyperpolarizes the membrane
    [*] Depolarizes the membrane (makes AP more likely)
    [ ] Has no effect
    [ ] Destroys the synapse

  Q11. An inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP):
    [ ] Depolarizes the membrane
    [*] Hyperpolarizes the membrane (makes AP less likely)
    [ ] Triggers an AP
    [ ] Releases Ca²⁺
    Explanation: IPSP pushes away from threshold.

  Q12. Neurotransmitter is removed from the synaptic cleft by:
    [ ] Leaving permanently
    [*] Reuptake, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion
    [ ] Nothing
    [ ] Electrical current
    Explanation: Multiple mechanisms terminate the signal.

  Q13. The refractory period:
    [ ] Allows continuous firing
    [*] Prevents a new AP from occurring immediately (ensures one-way propagation)
    [ ] Is very long
    [ ] Does not exist
    Explanation: Ensures directionality and limits firing rate.

  Q14. Conduction velocity increases with:
    [ ] Smaller axon diameter
    [*] Larger axon diameter and myelination
    [ ] No myelin
    [ ] Fewer nodes
    Explanation: Larger and myelinated = faster.

  Q15. Common neurotransmitters include:
    [ ] Only ACh
    [*] ACh, norepinephrine, dopamine, serotonin, GABA, glutamate
    [ ] Only dopamine
    [ ] Only serotonin
    Explanation: Many different neurotransmitters exist.

  Q16. GABA is the main _____ neurotransmitter in the brain.
    [ ] Excitatory
    [*] Inhibitory
    [ ] Modulatory
    [ ] None
    Explanation: GABA inhibits postsynaptic neurons.

  Q17. Glutamate is the main _____ neurotransmitter.
    [ ] Inhibitory
    [*] Excitatory
    [ ] Modulatory
    [ ] None
    Explanation: Glutamate excites postsynaptic neurons.

  Q18. Drugs that affect synaptic transmission include:
    [ ] None
    [ ] Only illegal drugs
    [*] Antidepressants, anesthetics, anti-anxiety meds, and many others
    [ ] Only pain relievers
    Explanation: Many drugs target neurotransmitter systems.

  Q19. The absolute refractory period means:
    [ ] The neuron can fire if stimulated strongly
    [*] No new AP can be generated regardless of stimulus strength
    [ ] The neuron is dead
    [ ] Normal firing
    Explanation: Na⁺ channels are inactivated.

  Q20. Understanding APs and synapses is crucial for:
    [ ] Only neuroscientists
    [*] All medicine (anesthesia, psychiatry, neurology, pharmacology)
    [ ] Only anatomy class
    [ ] Nothing practical
    Explanation: Foundation of neuroscience and pharmacology.

--- Lesson 4.3: Central Nervous System (Brain & Spinal Cord) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Central Nervous System (Brain & Spinal Cord)
Central Nervous SystemBrain Regions  • Cerebrum: Largest part; cerebral cortex handles higher functions (thought, memory, language, motor control). Four lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital.  • Cerebellum: Coordination, balance, fine motor skills.  • Brainstem: Midbrain, pons, medulla oblongata — vital functions (breathing, heart rate, sleep/wake).  • Diencephalon: Thalamus (relay center), hypothalamus (homeostasis, hormones).Spinal Cord
31 pairs of spinal nerves. Conducts signals between brain and body; integrates reflexes.

--- Lesson 4.4: Peripheral Nervous System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Peripheral Nervous System
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
All neural tissue outside the brain and spinal cord.
Divisions  • Sensory (afferent): Carries information from receptors TO the CNS.  • Motor (efferent): Carries commands FROM the CNS to effectors.Motor Subdivision  • Somatic nervous system: Voluntary control of skeletal muscles.  • Autonomic nervous system: Involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands.Cranial Nerves
12 pairs of cranial nerves (I–XII) emerge from the brain. Important examples: vagus nerve (X) — extensive autonomic functions.

--- Lesson 4.5: Autonomic Nervous System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Autonomic Nervous System
Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)
Involuntary control of internal organs, smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.
Two Divisions  • Sympathetic ('fight or flight'): Increases heart rate, dilates bronchioles, redirects blood to muscles, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion.  • Parasympathetic ('rest and digest'): Decreases heart rate, constricts bronchioles, stimulates digestion, constricts pupils.Dual Innervation
Most organs receive both sympathetic and parasympathetic input, maintaining a dynamic balance.

--- Lesson 4.6: Reflex Arcs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Reflex Arcs
Reflex Arcs
A reflex is a rapid, involuntary, predictable response to a stimulus.
Components of a Reflex Arc (5 steps)  • 1. Receptor: Detects the stimulus.  • 2. Sensory (afferent) neuron: Transmits impulse to the CNS.  • 3. Integration center: CNS processes the information (may involve interneurons).  • 4. Motor (efferent) neuron: Carries the response command.  • 5. Effector: Muscle or gland that carries out the response.Examples
Patellar (knee-jerk) reflex: Stretch receptor → sensory neuron → spinal cord → motor neuron → quadriceps contracts. This is a monosynaptic reflex (no interneuron).

--- Lesson 4.6: Reflex Arcs (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A reflex is:
    [ ] A voluntary action
    [*] A rapid, involuntary, predictable response to a stimulus
    [ ] A learned behavior
    [ ] A conscious decision
    Explanation: Reflexes are automatic.

  Q2. The correct order of a reflex arc is:
    [ ] Motor → sensory → effector → receptor → CNS
    [*] Receptor → sensory neuron → CNS → motor neuron → effector
    [ ] Effector → CNS → receptor → motor → sensory
    [ ] Random order
    Explanation: Standard 5-component pathway.

  Q3. The receptor in a reflex arc:
    [ ] Carries out the response
    [*] Detects the stimulus
    [ ] Processes the information
    [ ] Is always in the brain
    Explanation: Receptor = sensor.

  Q4. The effector in a reflex arc:
    [ ] Detects the stimulus
    [*] Carries out the response (muscle or gland)
    [ ] Transmits the signal
    [ ] Processes information
    Explanation: Effector = response executor.

  Q5. The patellar reflex is monosynaptic because:
    [ ] It has many synapses
    [*] There is only one synapse (sensory to motor, no interneuron)
    [ ] It involves the brain
    [ ] It is voluntary
    Explanation: Simplest reflex pathway.

  Q6. In the withdrawal reflex (e.g., touching a hot stove):
    [ ] Only one synapse is involved
    [*] Multiple synapses are involved (interneurons relay the signal)
    [ ] The brain processes it first
    [ ] No motor neuron is used
    Explanation: Polysynaptic reflex.

  Q7. The integration center for most reflexes is in the:
    [ ] Brain only
    [ ] Muscle
    [*] Spinal cord (or brainstem)
    [ ] Skin
    Explanation: Most reflexes are processed at the spinal level.

  Q8. Reflexes are faster than voluntary responses because:
    [ ] They use bigger neurons
    [*] They bypass the cerebral cortex (no conscious processing needed)
    [ ] They use hormones
    [ ] They don't exist
    Explanation: Direct spinal cord processing = fast.

  Q9. The knee-jerk reflex tests:
    [ ] Brain function
    [*] The integrity of the spinal cord and nerves at the L2-L4 level
    [ ] Muscle strength only
    [ ] Pain sensation
    Explanation: Clinically tests the femoral nerve/L2-L4 segments.

  Q10. An absent patellar reflex may indicate:
    [ ] Normal function
    [*] Nerve or spinal cord damage at L2-L4
    [ ] Excess strength
    [ ] No medical significance
    Explanation: Reflex testing diagnoses neural pathology.

  Q11. A hyperactive (exaggerated) reflex may suggest:
    [ ] Normal function
    [*] Upper motor neuron damage (loss of inhibitory brain control)
    [ ] Peripheral nerve damage
    [ ] Muscle weakness
    Explanation: Uninhibited spinal reflexes = upper motor neuron lesion.

  Q12. The Babinski reflex (toe fanning up on sole stimulation) is:
    [ ] Always normal in adults
    [*] Normal in infants but abnormal in adults (suggests corticospinal tract damage)
    [ ] Never tested
    [ ] Voluntary
    Explanation: Babinski in adults = concerning sign.

  Q13. Crossed extensor reflex:
    [ ] Only flexes one leg
    [*] Extends the opposite leg while the injured leg flexes (maintains balance)
    [ ] Is voluntary
    [ ] Doesn't exist
    Explanation: Stepping on a tack: lift injured foot, push down with the other.

  Q14. Somatic reflexes involve:
    [ ] Smooth muscle
    [ ] Glands
    [*] Skeletal muscle
    [ ] Cardiac muscle
    Explanation: Somatic = skeletal muscle responses.

  Q15. Autonomic reflexes involve:
    [ ] Skeletal muscle only
    [*] Smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands
    [ ] Only the brain
    [ ] No muscles
    Explanation: Autonomic reflexes regulate internal organs.

  Q16. An example of an autonomic reflex:
    [ ] Knee-jerk
    [*] Baroreceptor reflex (blood pressure regulation)
    [ ] Withdrawal reflex
    [ ] Patellar reflex
    Explanation: Baroreceptors detect BP changes → autonomic adjustment.

  Q17. Sensory neurons in a reflex arc are also called:
    [ ] Efferent
    [ ] Motor
    [*] Afferent
    [ ] Interneurons
    Explanation: Afferent = sensory = toward CNS.

  Q18. Motor neurons in a reflex arc are also called:
    [ ] Afferent
    [ ] Sensory
    [*] Efferent
    [ ] Association neurons
    Explanation: Efferent = motor = away from CNS.

  Q19. Reflexes are important for:
    [ ] Nothing
    [*] Protecting the body from injury and maintaining homeostasis rapidly
    [ ] Only neurological exams
    [ ] Only anatomy class
    Explanation: Survival mechanism.

  Q20. Testing reflexes is a critical part of:
    [*] A neurological examination to assess nervous system integrity
    [ ] Only school labs
    [ ] Only physical therapy
    [ ] Nothing clinical
    Explanation: Reflex testing is routine in neurological assessment.

--- Lesson 4.7: Disorders of the Nervous System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Disorders of the Nervous System
Disorders of the Nervous System  • Alzheimer's disease: Progressive memory loss and cognitive decline; amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles.  • Parkinson's disease: Loss of dopamine-producing neurons in substantia nigra → tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia.  • Epilepsy: Abnormal, excessive electrical activity → seizures.  • Multiple sclerosis: Autoimmune demyelination in the CNS.  • Stroke: Ischemic (blockage) or hemorrhagic (bleed) → sudden neurological deficits.  • Meningitis: Infection/inflammation of the meninges (bacterial or viral).

--- Lesson 4.7: Disorders of the Nervous System (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Alzheimer's disease primarily affects:
    [ ] Movement
    [*] Memory and cognitive function
    [ ] Vision
    [ ] Hearing
    Explanation: Progressive memory loss and dementia.

  Q2. Hallmarks of Alzheimer's pathology include:
    [ ] Normal brain tissue
    [*] Amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles
    [ ] Excess myelin
    [ ] Too many neurons
    Explanation: Protein deposits damage neurons.

  Q3. Parkinson's disease involves loss of:
    [ ] Serotonin neurons
    [*] Dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra
    [ ] ACh neurons
    [ ] GABA neurons
    Explanation: Dopamine deficiency → motor symptoms.

  Q4. Cardinal symptoms of Parkinson's include:
    [*] Tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (slow movement), postural instability
    [ ] Blindness
    [ ] Paralysis
    [ ] Seizures
    Explanation: TRAP mnemonic.

  Q5. Epilepsy is characterized by:
    [ ] Constant pain
    [*] Recurrent seizures from abnormal electrical brain activity
    [ ] Memory loss only
    [ ] Paralysis
    Explanation: Seizure disorder.

  Q6. An ischemic stroke is caused by:
    [ ] A bleed
    [*] A blood clot blocking a cerebral artery
    [ ] Trauma
    [ ] Infection
    Explanation: ~87% of strokes are ischemic.

  Q7. A hemorrhagic stroke involves:
    [ ] A clot
    [*] Rupture of a blood vessel in the brain
    [ ] Infection
    [ ] Dehydration
    Explanation: Bleeding into brain tissue.

  Q8. Stroke symptoms include:
    [ ] No symptoms
    [*] Sudden weakness, speech difficulty, facial drooping, confusion
    [ ] Only headache
    [ ] Only dizziness
    Explanation: FAST: Face, Arms, Speech, Time.

  Q9. Multiple sclerosis involves:
    [ ] Muscle wasting
    [*] Autoimmune destruction of CNS myelin
    [ ] Bone loss
    [ ] Liver disease

  Q10. Meningitis symptoms include:
    [ ] Joint pain only
    [*] Severe headache, stiff neck, fever, photophobia
    [ ] Muscle cramps only
    [ ] Rash only
    Explanation: Classic meningitis triad.

  Q11. Bacterial meningitis is a:
    [ ] Minor illness
    [*] Medical emergency requiring immediate antibiotics
    [ ] Chronic condition
    [ ] Non-infectious disease
    Explanation: Can be fatal within hours without treatment.

  Q12. A concussion is a:
    [ ] Fracture
    [*] Mild traumatic brain injury (TBI)
    [ ] Stroke
    [ ] Seizure
    Explanation: Brain shaken within the skull.

  Q13. Traumatic brain injury (TBI) can result from:
    [*] Falls, vehicle accidents, sports impacts, and violence
    [ ] Infection only
    [ ] Genetics only
    [ ] Aging only
    Explanation: Physical trauma to the head.

  Q14. Sciatica involves irritation of the:
    [ ] Cranial nerves
    [*] Sciatic nerve (often from herniated disc)
    [ ] Vagus nerve
    [ ] Optic nerve
    Explanation: Lower back → buttock → leg pain.

  Q15. Neuropathy in diabetes results from:
    [*] Chronic high blood sugar damaging peripheral nerves

    [ ] Low blood sugar
    [ ] No connection
    Explanation: Diabetic neuropathy = common complication.

  Q16. ALS (Lou Gehrig's disease) involves:
    [ ] Only sensory neurons
    [*] Progressive degeneration of motor neurons
    [ ] Only the brain
    [ ] Only autonomic neurons
    Explanation: Motor neuron death → progressive paralysis.

  Q17. Treatment for Parkinson's often includes:
    [*] Levodopa (L-DOPA) to increase dopamine levels
    [ ] Surgery only
    [ ] No treatment exists
    [ ] Antibiotics
    Explanation: L-DOPA is converted to dopamine in the brain.

  Q18. Neuroplasticity refers to:
    [ ] Brain rigidity
    [*] The brain's ability to reorganize and form new neural connections
    [ ] Brain atrophy
    [ ] Nerve death
    Explanation: Basis for rehabilitation after brain injury.

  Q19. Prevention of neurological disorders includes:
    [ ] Nothing can be done
    [*] Protecting the head, managing cardiovascular risk factors, staying active, and early detection
    [ ] Only genetics determine outcomes
    [ ] Only medication
    Explanation: Lifestyle factors significantly affect neurological health.

  Q20. Why is understanding neurological disorders important?
    [ ] It's not
    [*] They are among the leading causes of disability worldwide
    [ ] Only for neurologists
    [ ] Only for research
    Explanation: Neurological conditions affect millions globally.

  Key Concepts: AP Prep: Neurophysiology
AP Prep: NeurophysiologyKey Topics for Exam  • Resting potential, action potential phases, ion channels.  • Synaptic transmission: neurotransmitter release, receptor binding, signal termination.  • Reflex arc components and clinical testing.  • ANS: sympathetic vs. parasympathetic effects on target organs.  • Brain regions and their functions.

--- Lesson 4.8: AP Prep: Neurophysiology (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Graded potentials are:
    [ ] All-or-none like APs
    [*] Local, decremental potentials that can be excitatory or inhibitory
    [ ] Only inhibitory
    [ ] Found only in muscle
    Explanation: EPSPs and IPSPs are graded.

  Q2. Temporal summation occurs when:
    [ ] Many neurons fire at once
    [*] One neuron fires rapidly, signals overlap
    [ ] No signals arrive
    [ ] Muscles contract
    Explanation: Rapid successive stimuli from the same source.

  Q3. Spatial summation occurs when:
    [ ] One neuron fires repeatedly
    [*] Multiple neurons stimulate the same postsynaptic cell simultaneously
    [ ] Muscles contract
    [ ] No stimuli arrive
    Explanation: Convergence of signals from different sources.

  Q4. An EPSP _____ the chance of an AP.
    [ ] Decreases
    [*] Increases (depolarizes toward threshold)
    [ ] Eliminates
    [ ] Has no effect on
    Explanation: Excitatory = moves toward threshold.

  Q5. An IPSP _____ the chance of an AP.
    [ ] Increases
    [*] Decreases (hyperpolarizes away from threshold)
    [ ] Eliminates
    [ ] Has no effect on
    Explanation: Inhibitory = moves away from threshold.

  Q6. During the absolute refractory period:
    [ ] A normal stimulus triggers an AP
    [*] No AP can be generated, regardless of stimulus strength
    [ ] The neuron is at rest
    [ ] The neuron is dead
    Explanation: Na⁺ channels are inactivated.

  Q7. The relative refractory period:
    [ ] No AP is possible
    [*] A stronger-than-normal stimulus can trigger an AP
    [ ] Is the same as the absolute
    [ ] Does not exist
    Explanation: Some Na⁺ channels have recovered.

  Q8. Sympathetic stimulation of the heart:
    [ ] Decreases HR
    [*] Increases heart rate and contractility
    [ ] Has no effect
    [ ] Stops the heart
    Explanation: β₁ receptors → ↑HR, ↑force.

  Q9. Parasympathetic stimulation of the GI tract:
    [ ] Inhibits motility
    [*] Increases motility and secretion (digestion)
    [ ] Has no effect
    [ ] Causes pain
    Explanation: Rest and digest.

  Q10. The neurotransmitter for the somatic NMJ is always:
    [ ] Norepinephrine
    [*] Acetylcholine
    [ ] Dopamine
    [ ] Serotonin
    Explanation: ACh at all skeletal NMJs.

  Q11. Serotonin is involved in regulating:
    [ ] Only motor function
    [*] Mood, sleep, appetite, and pain
    [ ] Only digestion
    [ ] Only vision
    Explanation: Low serotonin linked to depression.

  Q12. Dopamine pathways are involved in:
    [ ] Only Parkinson's
    [*] Reward, motivation, motor control, and addiction
    [ ] Only sleep
    [ ] Only vision
    Explanation: Multiple brain functions.

  Q13. The hypothalamus connects the nervous and endocrine systems by:
    [ ] Having no connection
    [*] Controlling the pituitary gland (the 'master' endocrine gland)
    [ ] Being a muscle
    [ ] Producing CSF
    Explanation: Hypothalamus-pituitary axis.

  Q14. A nerve impulse travels fastest in:
    [ ] Small, unmyelinated fibers
    [*] Large, myelinated fibers
    [ ] Dendrites
    [ ] Cell bodies
    Explanation: Diameter and myelin both increase speed.

  Q15. In clinical practice, EEG measures:
    [ ] Heart activity
    [*] Brain electrical activity (useful for diagnosing epilepsy, sleep disorders)
    [ ] Muscle activity
    [ ] Nerve speed
    Explanation: Electroencephalogram.

  Q16. Nerve conduction studies test:
    [ ] Brain waves
    [*] The speed and strength of electrical signals in peripheral nerves
    [ ] Heart rhythm
    [ ] Muscle size
    Explanation: Useful for diagnosing neuropathy.

  Q17. A key concept for the AP exam: the Na⁺/K⁺ pump is:
    [ ] Passive transport
    [*] Active transport (uses ATP to maintain the resting potential)
    [ ] Channel-mediated
    [ ] Not energy-requiring
    Explanation: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in — requires ATP.

  Q18. The direction of an AP along an axon is determined by:
    [ ] Random chance
    [*] The refractory period (prevents backward propagation)
    [ ] The myelin sheath direction
    [ ] Gravity
    Explanation: Refractory zones ensure one-way travel.

  Q19. If all Na⁺ channels were blocked:
    [ ] Normal function
    [*] No action potentials could be generated (nerve block)
    [ ] Faster conduction
    [ ] Stronger signals
    Explanation: Local anesthetics block Na⁺ channels.

  Q20. Neurophysiology connects to all body systems because:
    [ ] It doesn't
    [*] The nervous system controls and coordinates virtually every body function
    [ ] Only muscles matter
    [ ] Only hormones matter
    Explanation: The nervous system is the master coordinator.
