--- Lesson 5.1: Bisectors of Triangles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bisectors of Triangles
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is true about the circumcenter?
The circumcenter is equidistant from all three vertices. The other options — 'It is equidistant from all three sides', 'It is always inside the triangle', and 'It divides medians in a 2:1 ratio' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

--- Lesson 5.1: Bisectors of Triangles (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The circumcenter of a triangle is the point where which lines intersect?
    [ ] The three angle bisectors
    [ ] The three medians
    [ ] The three altitudes
    [*] The three perpendicular bisectors
    Explanation: Circumcenter The three perpendicular bisectors of a Triangle are concurrent (meet at one Point ). Perpendicular Bisector A perpendicular bisector of a side of a Triangle is a Line , segment, or ray that is perpendicular to that side at its midpoint. The other options — 'The three medians', 'The three altitudes', and 'The three angle bisectors' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q2. What is true about the circumcenter?
    [ ] It is equidistant from all three sides
    [*] It is equidistant from all three vertices
    [ ] It divides medians in a 2:1 ratio
    [ ] It is always inside the triangle
    Explanation: The circumcenter is equidistant from all three vertices. The other options — 'It is equidistant from all three sides', 'It is always inside the triangle', and 'It divides medians in a 2:1 ratio' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q3. The incenter is the center of which circle?
    [ ] The circumscribed circle
    [ ] The great circle
    [*] The inscribed circle
    [ ] The unit circle
    Explanation: It is the center of the inscribed Circle (the Circle that fits inside the Triangle and touches all three sides). It is the center of the circumscribed Circle (the Circle passing through all three vertices). The other options — 'The circumscribed circle', 'The unit circle', and 'The great circle' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q4. Where is the circumcenter of a right triangle?
    [ ] Inside the triangle
    [ ] Outside the triangle
    [ ] At the right-angle vertex
    [*] At the midpoint of the hypotenuse
    Explanation: In a right Triangle , the circumcenter is on the hypotenuse (its midpoint). Perpendicular Bisector A perpendicular bisector of a side of a Triangle is a Line , segment, or ray that is perpendicular to that side at its midpoint. The other options — 'At the right-angle vertex', 'Inside the triangle', and 'Outside the triangle' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q5. Any point on an angle bisector is equidistant from:
    [ ] The midpoints of the sides
    [*] The two sides of the angle
    [ ] All three vertices
    [ ] The two vertices
    Explanation: Angle Bisector Theorem — Any Point on the Angle bisector is equidistant from the two sides of the Angle. The incenter is equidistant from all three sides. The other options — 'The two vertices', 'All three vertices', and 'The midpoints of the sides' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q6. The incenter of a triangle is always located:
    [ ] Outside the triangle
    [*] Inside the triangle
    [ ] At the midpoint of a side
    [ ] On the longest side
    Explanation: The incenter is always inside the Triangle. In an acute Triangle , the circumcenter is inside the Triangle. The other options — 'Outside the triangle', 'On the longest side', and 'At the midpoint of a side' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

  Q7. A circumscribed circle passes through:
    [ ] The centroid
    [ ] Only two vertices
    [ ] The midpoints of all three sides
    [*] All three vertices of the triangle
    Explanation: It is the center of the circumscribed Circle (the Circle passing through all three vertices). It is the center of the inscribed Circle (the Circle that fits inside the Triangle and touches all three sides). The other options — 'The midpoints of all three sides', 'Only two vertices', and 'The centroid' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
