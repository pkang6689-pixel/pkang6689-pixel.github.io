--- Lesson 11.1: Areas of Parallelograms and Triangles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Areas of Parallelograms and Triangles
Area of a Parallelogram

A = bh

where b is the base and h is the height (perpendicular to the base).

Area of a Triangle

A = ½bh

where b is the base and h is the corresponding height.

Key Ideas

  • The height must be perpendicular to the base — it is not necessarily a side of the figure.
  • Any side can be chosen as the base; the height changes accordingly.
  • A diagonal divides a parallelogram into two congruent triangles.

--- Lesson 11.2: Areas of Trapezoids, Rhombi, and Kites (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Areas of Trapezoids, Rhombi, and Kites
Area of a Trapezoid

A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h

where b₁ and b₂ are the parallel bases and h is the height.

Area of a Rhombus or Kite

A = ½d₁d₂

where d₁ and d₂ are the lengths of the diagonals.

Key Ideas

  • The diagonals of a rhombus are perpendicular bisectors of each other.
  • The diagonals of a kite are perpendicular, but only one is bisected.

--- Lesson 11.3: Areas of Circles and Sectors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Areas of Circles and Sectors
Area of a Circle

A = πr²

Area of a Sector

A sector is a "pie slice" of a circle defined by a central angle θ.

A = (θ/360°) × πr²

Area of a Segment

A segment is the region between a chord and its arc.

A(segment) = A(sector) − A(triangle)

Key Ideas

  • The ratio of a sector's area to the circle's area equals the ratio of the central angle to 360°.

--- Lesson 11.4: Areas of Regular Polygons and Composite Figures (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Areas of Regular Polygons and Composite Figures
Area of a Regular Polygon

A = ½aP

where a is the apothem (distance from center to a side) and P is the perimeter.

Key Definitions

  • Center: equidistant from all vertices.
  • Radius: segment from center to a vertex.
  • Apothem: perpendicular segment from center to a side.
  • Central angle of a regular n-gon = 360°/n.

Composite Figures

Break a composite figure into non-overlapping simple shapes. Find each area and add (or subtract for holes).

--- Lesson 11.5: Areas of Similar Figures (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Areas of Similar Figures
Area Ratio Theorem

If two similar figures have a scale factor of a : b, then the ratio of their areas is a² : b².

Example

If two similar triangles have sides in ratio 3 : 5, their areas are in ratio 9 : 25.

Key Skills

  • Find the ratio of areas given the scale factor.
  • Find the scale factor given the ratio of areas (take the square root).
  • Use area ratios to find unknown areas of similar figures.

--- Lesson 11.6: Integration in Area Calculations ⭐ (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Integration in Area Calculations ⭐

Area Under a Curve

The area between a curve y = f(x) and the x-axis from x = a to x = b can be approximated using geometric methods.

Riemann Sums

  • Divide the interval [a, b] into n equal subintervals of width Δx = (b − a)/n.
  • Approximate area = sum of rectangle areas = Σ f(xᵢ) × Δx.
  • As n → ∞, the approximation approaches the exact area.

Connection to Geometry

Area formulas for trapezoids and triangles are special cases of integration for linear functions. Circles and sectors relate to integration of curves.
