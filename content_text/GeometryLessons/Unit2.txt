Negation (~p or ¬p) reverses the truth value: if p is true, ~p is false, and vice versa. Negation simply adds 'not' to the statement. The answer is 'False'. The other options — 'True', 'Unknown', and 'Undefined' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.

--- Lesson 2.3: Conditional Statements (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conditional Statements
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In 'If p, then q,' what is p called?
The hypothesis is p and the conclusion is q. A conditional is false ONLY when the hypothesis is true and the conclusion is false. The other options — 'The conclusion', 'The converse', and 'The inverse' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the contrapositive of 'If p, then q'?
Contrapositive — If not q, then not p (~q → ~p) — swap AND negate. The other options — 'If q, then p', 'If not p, then not q', and 'If p, then not q' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the converse of 'If it rains, then the ground is wet'?
The converse of 'If p, then q' is 'If q, then p.' The converse is NOT logically equivalent to the original; it may be true or false independently. The answer is 'If the ground is wet, then it rains'. The other options — 'If it does not rain, the ground is not wet', 'If the ground is not wet, then it does not rain', and 'It rains and the ground is wet' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
What is the inverse of 'If p, then q'?
Inverse — If not p, then not q (~p → ~q) — negate both. The other options — 'If q, then p', 'If not q, then not p', and 'If p, then not q' — are related concepts but do not correctly answer this specific question.
