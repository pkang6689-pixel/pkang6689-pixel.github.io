--- Lesson 12.1: Sample Spaces &amp; Basic Probability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sample Spaces &amp; Basic Probability
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A coin is flipped. What is P(heads)?
A fair coin has 2 equally likely outcomes. P(heads) = 1/2.
A standard die is rolled. What is P(rolling a 3)?
There are 6 equally likely outcomes on a standard die. P(3) = 1/6.
What is P(rolling an even number) on a standard die?
Even numbers: {2, 4, 6} = 3 outcomes out of 6. P = 3/6 = 1/2.
A bag has 3 red, 2 blue, and 5 green marbles. What is P(blue)?
Total marbles = 10. P(blue) = 2/10 = 1/5.
What is P(not rolling a 6) on a standard die?
P(not 6) = 1 − P(6) = 1 − 1/6 = 5/6.
A card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. What is P(heart)?
There are 13 hearts in a 52-card deck. P(heart) = 13/52 = 1/4.
What is the probability of drawing a king from a standard deck?
There are 4 kings in a 52-card deck. P(king) = 4/52 = 1/13.
What is a 'sample space'?
The sample space is the complete set of all possible outcomes. For a coin flip: {H, T}. For a die roll: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.
What is 'probability'?
Probability is a number from 0 to 1 (or 0% to 100%) that quantifies the likelihood of an event. P = favorable outcomes / total outcomes.
What is an 'event' in probability?
An event is a subset of the sample space — one or more outcomes we are interested in. 'Rolling an even number' is an event: {2, 4, 6}.
What is the 'complement' of an event?
The complement of event A (written A') includes everything not in A. P(A') = 1 − P(A).
What is 'theoretical probability'?
Theoretical probability is calculated mathematically: P(A) = number of favorable outcomes / total number of outcomes.
What is 'experimental probability'?
Experimental probability is based on actual data from performing trials: P(A) = times A occurred / total trials.
What is the 'fundamental counting principle'?
The fundamental counting principle states that if event A has m outcomes and event B has n outcomes, the total combined outcomes is m × n.
A weather forecast says there is a 30% chance of rain. What is the probability it will NOT rain?
P(no rain) = 1 − P(rain) = 1 − 0.30 = 0.70 = 70%.
A class has 12 boys and 18 girls. If a student is randomly selected, what is the probability of selecting a boy?
Total students = 30. P(boy) = 12/30 = 2/5.
A jar contains 8 red, 5 blue, and 7 green candies. You pick one at random. What is the probability of NOT picking green?
Total = 20. P(not green) = 1 − 7/20 = 13/20.
A lottery requires choosing 1 number from 1-50 and 1 color from 5 options. What is the total sample space?
50 × 5 = 250 total outcomes.
A teacher randomly assigns 30 students to groups. If 6 students are in the honors track, what is the probability the first student assigned is from honors?
P(honors) = 6/30 = 1/5.
A game board has 20 spaces: 5 are 'bonus' spaces. What is the probability of landing on a bonus space?
P(bonus) = 5/20 = 1/4.
You roll two dice. How many total outcomes are in the sample space, and what is the probability of rolling a sum of 7?
Two dice: 6 × 6 = 36 outcomes. Sums of 7: (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1) = 6 ways. P = 6/36 = 1/6.

--- Lesson 12.2: Independent &amp; Dependent Events (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Independent &amp; Dependent Events
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A coin is flipped and a die is rolled. What is P(heads AND 4)?
Independent events: P = 1/2 × 1/6 = 1/12.
A bag has 5 red and 3 blue marbles. You draw one, do NOT replace it, then draw another. What is P(red then blue)?
Without replacement (dependent): P = 5/8 × 3/7 = 15/56.
A spinner has 4 equal sections. You spin twice. What is P(red both times)?
Independent: P = 1/4 × 1/4 = 1/16.
From a deck of 52 cards, you draw 2 cards without replacement. What is P(ace then ace)?
Dependent: P = 4/52 × 3/51 = 12/2652 = 1/221.
Flipping a coin 3 times: what is P(all heads)?
Independent: P = 1/2 × 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/8.
What is the 'multiplication rule' for probability?
The multiplication rule finds P(A and B). For independent events: multiply directly. For dependent events: use conditional probability P(B|A).
What is the 'addition rule' for mutually exclusive events?
For mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B). Since they can't overlap, there's no double-counting.
What is a 'tree diagram' used for in probability?
A tree diagram branches out at each stage of an experiment, showing all possible paths and helping organize compound probability calculations.
A box contains 6 red and 4 blue socks. You pick two socks without looking and without replacement. What is P(both red)?

A student guesses on two independent multiple-choice questions, each with 4 choices. What is P(both correct)?

Independent: P = 0.40 × 0.40 = 0.16 = 16%.

  Key Concepts: Probability with Combinatorics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 4! (4 factorial)?
4! = 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24.
What is 0! ?
By definition, 0! = 1. This is a convention that makes many formulas work correctly.
What is a 'factorial'?
n! (n factorial) = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ... × 2 × 1. For example, 5! = 120.
What is a 'permutation'?
A permutation is an ordered arrangement. ABC, ACB, and BAC are all different permutations of the same three letters.
What is a 'combination'?
A combination selects items without regard to order. Choosing {A, B, C} is the same as choosing {C, B, A}.
What is the difference between a permutation and a combination?
In permutations, AB ≠ BA (order matters). In combinations, {A,B} = {B,A} (order doesn't matter). Permutations are always ≥ combinations.
What is the formula for permutations P(n, r)?
P(n, r) = n!/(n−r)! counts the number of ways to arrange r items from n distinct items.
What is the formula for combinations C(n, r)?
C(n, r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!) counts the number of ways to choose r items from n without regard to order.
What is 'counting probability'?
Counting probability uses permutations or combinations to systematically count favorable outcomes and total outcomes, then applies P = favorable/total.
A raffle has 100 tickets. You buy 5. What is the probability of winning if 1 ticket is drawn?
P = 5/100 = 1/20.
A phone password requires 4 distinct digits (0-9). How many passwords are possible, and what is the probability of guessing correctly on one try?
Order matters, no repetition: P(10,4) = 10 × 9 × 8 × 7 = 5040. P(guess) = 1/5040.

  Q26. A license plate has 3 letters followed by 3 digits. Letters and digits can repeat. How many plates are possible?
    [*] 17,576,000
    [ ] 1,757,600
    [ ] 36³
    [ ] 78
    Explanation: 26 × 26 × 26 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 17,576 × 1,000 = 17,576,000.

  Q27. A store has 6 different shirts. You want to buy 2 as gifts. The probability of randomly choosing the 2 specific shirts you want is:
    [*] 1/15
    [ ] 1/36
    [ ] 1/6
    [ ] 2/6
    Explanation: Total ways to choose 2 from 6: C(6,2) = 15. Favorable = 1 specific pair. P = 1/15.

  Q28. A raffle has 100 tickets. You buy 5. What is the probability of winning if 1 ticket is drawn?
    [ ] 5/100
    [ ] 1/5
    [*] 1/20
    [ ] 1/100
    Explanation: P = 5/100 = 1/20.

  Q29. A phone password requires 4 distinct digits (0-9). How many passwords are possible, and what is the probability of guessing correctly on one try?
    [*] 5040 passwords; P = 1/5040
    [ ] 40 passwords; P = 1/40
    [ ] 210 passwords; P = 1/210
    [ ] 10000 passwords; P = 1/10000
    Explanation: Order matters, no repetition: P(10,4) = 10 × 9 × 8 × 7 = 5040. P(guess) = 1/5040.

  Q30. A club has 10 members. If a 4-person relay team is formed where each person runs a specific leg, how many teams are possible?
    [ ] 10000
    [*] 5040
    [ ] 210
    [ ] 40
    Explanation: Order matters (different legs): P(10,4) = 10 × 9 × 8 × 7 = 5040.

--- Lesson 12.4: Probability with Combinatorics (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: What is the Counting Principle?
     A: If event A has m outcomes and B has n outcomes, there are m × n total outcomes

  2. Q: What are permutations?
     A: Arrangements where ORDER matters: P(n,r) = n!/(n−r)!

  3. Q: What are combinations?
     A: Selections where ORDER does NOT matter: C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!)

  4. Q: P(5, 2) = ?
     A: 5!/3! = 20

  5. Q: C(5, 2) = ?
     A: 5!/(2!·3!) = 10

  6. Q: 3 shirts and 4 pants = how many outfits?
     A: 3 × 4 = 12

  7. Q: How many ways can 3 people line up?
     A: 3! = 6

  8. Q: When do you use permutations vs. combinations?
     A: Permutations when order matters; combinations when it doesn't

  9. Q: C(10, 3) = ?
     A: 10!/(3!·7!) = 120
