--- Lesson 1.1: Measures of Center (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Center
The mean (average) is found by adding all values and dividing by the number of values: mean = Î£x / n.

The median is the middle value when data is ordered from least to greatest. If there is an even number of values, the median is the average of the two middle values.

The mode is the value that appears most frequently. A data set can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes.

The mean is sensitive to outliers while the median is resistant to extreme values. Choose the median when data is skewed.

--- Lesson 1.2: Measures of Spread (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Spread
The range is the difference between the maximum and minimum values: Range = Max − Min.

The interquartile range (IQR) measures the spread of the middle 50% of data: IQR = Q3 − Q1. It is resistant to outliers.

The standard deviation measures how far data values typically are from the mean. A small standard deviation means data is clustered near the mean.

The variance is the square of the standard deviation: σ² = Σ(x − x̄)² / n.

--- Lesson 1.3: Data Distributions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Data Distributions
A symmetric distribution has data evenly spread on both sides of the center. The mean and median are approximately equal.

A skewed left (negatively skewed) distribution has a long tail extending to the left. The mean is less than the median.

A skewed right (positively skewed) distribution has a long tail extending to the right. The mean is greater than the median.

A uniform distribution has all values occurring with roughly equal frequency.

--- Lesson 1.4: Describing Distributions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Describing Distributions
When describing a distribution, discuss its shape (symmetric, skewed left, skewed right), center (mean or median), and spread (range, IQR, or standard deviation).

Also note any unusual features such as outliers, gaps, or clusters in the data.

Use the mean and standard deviation for symmetric distributions. Use the median and IQR for skewed distributions.

Always describe distributions in context — relate numbers back to what the data represents.

--- Lesson 1.6: Two-Way Tables (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Two-Way Tables
A two-way table (contingency table) displays data for two categorical variables.

Each cell shows the frequency (count) or relative frequency (proportion/percentage) for a combination of categories.

Marginal frequencies are the totals in the last row and last column, showing the distribution of each variable separately.

Joint frequencies are the counts inside the table where two categories intersect.

--- Lesson 1.7: Outliers &amp; Their Effects (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Outliers & Their Effects

An outlier is a data value that is significantly higher or lower than the rest of the data.

A common rule: a value is an outlier if it is below Q1 − 1.5 × IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 × IQR.

Outliers can significantly affect the mean and standard deviation, pulling them toward the extreme value.

The median and IQR are resistant to outliers and are preferred when outliers are present.

--- Lesson 2.4: Literal Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Literal Equations
A literal equation contains two or more variables. You solve for one variable in terms of the others.

Use the same inverse operations as with regular equations — just treat the other variables as constants.

Examples: Solve d = rt for r → r = d/t. Solve A = ½bh for h → h = 2A/b.

Literal equations are common in science and geometry formulas.

--- Lesson 2.8: Systems of Equations — Substitution &amp; Elimination (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Systems of Equations — Substitution & Elimination

A system of equations is a set of two or more equations with the same variables. The solution is the point(s) that satisfy all equations.

Substitution: Solve one equation for a variable, then substitute into the other.

Elimination: Add or subtract equations to eliminate a variable. Multiply first if needed.

Systems can have one solution (intersecting lines), no solution (parallel lines), or infinitely many solutions (same line).

--- Lesson 3.1: Scatter Plots &amp; Correlation (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Scatter Plots & Correlation

A scatter plot displays pairs of numerical data as points on a coordinate plane to show relationships between two variables.

Positive correlation: as x increases, y increases. Negative correlation: as x increases, y decreases. No correlation: no apparent pattern.

The strength of correlation can be described as strong, moderate, or weak, depending on how closely points follow a pattern.

Correlation does not imply causation — just because two variables are correlated does not mean one causes the other.

--- Lesson 3.4: Correlation vs. Causation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Correlation vs. Causation
Correlation means two variables have a statistical association — they change together in a predictable way.

Causation means one variable directly causes a change in another.

Correlation does NOT imply causation. A lurking variable (confounding variable) may explain the association.

To establish causation, you need a controlled experiment, not just an observational study.

--- Lesson 3.7: Association in Categorical Data (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Association in Categorical Data
Two categorical variables have an association if the distribution of one variable differs across categories of the other.

Compare conditional relative frequencies to check for association. If they are approximately equal across categories, there is no association.

If conditional distributions differ noticeably, there is an association between the variables.

Association in categorical data does NOT imply causation — the same rule applies as with numerical data.
