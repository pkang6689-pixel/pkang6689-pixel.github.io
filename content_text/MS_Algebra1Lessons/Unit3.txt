--- Lesson 6.1: Key Features of Parabolas (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Key Features of Parabolas
A quadratic function has the form y = ax² + bx + c. Its graph is a parabola.

If a > 0, the parabola opens upward (minimum). If a downward (maximum).

The vertex is the highest or lowest point. The axis of symmetry is x = −b/(2a).

The y-intercept is c (set x = 0). The x-intercepts (zeros/roots) are where y = 0.

--- Lesson 6.2: Graphing in Standard Form (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphing in Standard Form
Standard form: y = ax² + bx + c. Steps: (1) Find the axis of symmetry x = −b/(2a). (2) Find the vertex by substituting. (3) Plot the y-intercept and its mirror point. (4) Plot additional points.

The y-intercept is the point (0, c).

Use symmetry about the axis to find mirror image points.

The larger |a|, the narrower the parabola. The smaller |a|, the wider.

--- Lesson 6.3: Graphing in Vertex Form (Summary) ---

Vertex form: y = a(x − h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex.

The axis of symmetry is x = h.

a > 0: opens up (minimum at vertex). a complete the square.

--- Lesson 6.4: Transformations of Quadratic Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Transformations of Quadratic Functions
y = a(x − h)² + k: h shifts horizontally, k shifts vertically, a stretches/compresses and reflects.

|a| > 1: vertical stretch (narrower). 0 < |a| < 1: vertical compression (wider).

a < 0: reflection over the x-axis.

Combine transformations: e.g., y = −2(x − 3)² + 1 is a reflection, stretch by 2, right 3, up 1.

--- Lesson 7.1: Solving by Factoring — GCF &amp; Grouping (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Solving by Factoring — GCF & Grouping

Always look for a Greatest Common Factor (GCF) first. Factor it out before doing anything else.

Example: 6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3). Set each factor equal to 0.

Factoring by grouping: for four terms, group into pairs, factor each pair, then factor the common binomial.

The Zero Product Property: if AB = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0.

--- Lesson 7.3: Special Products &amp; Factoring Patterns (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Special Products & Factoring Patterns

Difference of squares: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b).

Perfect square trinomial: a² + 2ab + b² = (a + b)² and a² − 2ab + b² = (a − b)².

Sum/Difference of cubes: a³ + b³ = (a + b)(a² − ab + b²); a³ − b³ = (a − b)(a² + ab + b²).

Recognizing these patterns makes factoring much faster.

--- Lesson 7.4: Completing the Square (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Completing the Square
Completing the square rewrites ax² + bx + c in vertex form a(x − h)² + k.

Steps: (1) Move c to the other side. (2) Take half of b, square it, add to both sides. (3) Factor the perfect square. (4) Solve.

Example: x² + 6x = 7 → x² + 6x + 9 = 16 → (x+3)² = 16 → x + 3 = ±4 → x = 1 or x = −7.

If a ≠ 1, divide the entire equation by a first.

--- Lesson 7.6: The Discriminant (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: The Discriminant
The discriminant is the expression under the square root in the quadratic formula: D = b² − 4ac.

If D > 0: two distinct real solutions. If D = 0: one repeated real solution. If D no real solutions (two complex solutions).

The discriminant tells you how many times the parabola crosses the x-axis.

Example: For x² + 4x + 5 = 0, D = 16 − 20 = −4 < 0 → no real solutions.

--- Lesson 8.1: Adding &amp; Subtracting Polynomials (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Adding & Subtracting Polynomials

A polynomial is a sum of terms with whole-number exponents. The degree is the highest exponent.

To add polynomials, combine like terms (same variable and exponent).

To subtract, distribute the negative sign to every term in the second polynomial, then combine like terms.

Arrange terms in descending order of degree (standard form).

--- Lesson 8.4: Polynomial Identities (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Polynomial Identities
A polynomial identity is an equation that is true for all values of the variables.

Key identities: (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b², (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b², (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b².

Binomial theorem (introduction): (a + b)ⁿ can be expanded using patterns from Pascal's Triangle.

Identities are useful for mental math, simplification, and proofs.
