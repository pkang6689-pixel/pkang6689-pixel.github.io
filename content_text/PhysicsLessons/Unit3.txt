--- Lesson 3.1: Force & Interaction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Force & Interaction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a force?
A force is an interaction that, when unbalanced, causes a change in an object's velocity (acceleration). Forces can also deform objects.
What is the SI unit of force?
The SI unit of force is the newton (N), defined as 1 N = 1 kg·m/s². It is named after Sir Isaac Newton.
What is a contact force?
Contact forces require direct physical contact between the interacting objects. Examples include friction, normal force, tension, and applied forces.
What is a non-contact force?
Non-contact (field) forces act at a distance through fields. Examples include gravitational, electromagnetic, and nuclear forces.
What is a net force?
The net force (resultant force) is the vector sum of all individual forces. An object accelerates only when the net force is nonzero.
Two teams play tug-of-war. Team A pulls with 500 N and Team B pulls with 450 N. What is the net force and which direction does the rope accelerate?
Net force = 500 − 450 = 50 N toward Team A (the stronger puller). The rope (and both teams) will accelerate in Team A's direction.
A person pushes two stacked boxes (10 kg and 5 kg) with 30 N of force on a frictionless surface. What is the acceleration of the system?
The total mass of the system is 10 + 5 = 15 kg. Since the surface is frictionless: a = F_net/m = 30/15 = 2 m/s².
A parachutist falls at constant velocity. Her weight is 700 N. What is the upward drag force?
Constant velocity means zero acceleration (ΣF = 0). Therefore drag force upward must equal weight downward: F_drag = 700 N.
Three forces act on an object: 10 N east, 6 N west, and 8 N north. What is the magnitude of the net force?
Horizontal: 10 − 6 = 4 N east. Vertical: 8 N north. Net force = √(4² + 8²) = √80 ≈ 8.94 N at arctan(8/4) ≈ 63.4° north of east.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a non-contact force?
Gravity acts between any two masses without requiring contact — it operates through the gravitational field. Friction, tension, and normal force all require contact.

--- Lesson 3.1: Force & Interaction (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is a force?

    [*] A push or pull that can change an object's motion, shape, or direction
    [ ] The mass of an object

    Explanation: A force is an interaction that, when unbalanced, causes a change in an object's velocity (acceleration). Forces can also deform objects.

  Q2. What is the SI unit of force?
    [*] Newton (N)
    [ ] Joule (J)
    [ ] Kilogram (kg)
    [ ] Meter per second (m/s)
    Explanation: The SI unit of force is the newton (N), defined as 1 N = 1 kg·m/s². It is named after Sir Isaac Newton.

  Q3. Forces always occur in what type of arrangement?
    [ ] Isolation — forces act independently
    [ ] Random groupings
    [ ] Triplets — every force produces two reactions
    [*] Pairs — every force has an equal and opposite reaction force
    Explanation: Newton's third law states that forces always come in interaction pairs. When object A pushes on object B, B pushes back on A with equal magnitude and opposite direction.

  Q4. What is a contact force?
    [ ] Only the gravitational force
    [ ] A force that only exists in a vacuum
    [*] A force that requires physical touching between objects
    [ ] A force that acts through empty space
    Explanation: Contact forces require direct physical contact between the interacting objects. Examples include friction, normal force, tension, and applied forces.

  Q5. What is a non-contact force?
    [*] A force that acts on an object without physical contact, through a field
    [ ] A force that only acts when objects touch
    [ ] A force that has no effect on motion
    [ ] A force that exists only in theory
    Explanation: Non-contact (field) forces act at a distance through fields. Examples include gravitational, electromagnetic, and nuclear forces.

  Q6. Which of the following is an example of a non-contact force?
    [*] Gravity
    [ ] Tension
    [ ] Normal force
    [ ] Friction
    Explanation: Gravity acts between any two masses without requiring contact — it operates through the gravitational field. Friction, tension, and normal force all require contact.

  Q7. What is a net force?
    [ ] Any single force applied to an object
    [*] The vector sum of all forces acting on an object
    [ ] The largest force acting on an object
    [ ] The force of gravity on an object
    Explanation: The net force (resultant force) is the vector sum of all individual forces. An object accelerates only when the net force is nonzero.

  Q8. When the net force on an object is zero, the object is said to be in:
    [ ] Free fall
    [ ] Maximum acceleration
    [ ] Terminal velocity
    [*] Equilibrium
    Explanation: When the net force is zero, the object is in equilibrium — it either remains at rest or moves at constant velocity (Newton's first law).

  Q9. What happens to an object when an unbalanced (net) force acts on it?
    [ ] It moves at constant velocity
    [ ] It remains at rest
    [*] It accelerates in the direction of the net force
    [ ] It immediately stops
    Explanation: An unbalanced net force causes acceleration in the direction of that force (Newton's second law: F_net = ma).

  Q10. Is force a scalar or vector quantity?
    [ ] Scalar — it has only magnitude
    [*] Vector — it has both magnitude and direction
    [ ] Neither — it is dimensionless
    [ ] It depends on the type of force
    Explanation: Force is a vector quantity. Both the magnitude (how strong) and the direction (which way) of a force are essential for describing its effect on motion.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes a force?
    [ ] The energy an object possesses due to its elevated position relative to a reference point, also known as gravitational potential energy measured in joules
    [ ] A scalar measure of how fast an object is moving at a given instant, calculated as the distance covered per unit time in meters per second
    [ ] The continuous displacement of an object through space from one location to another, characterized by a trajectory path and an elapsed travel time
    [*] An interaction between two objects that can cause acceleration, deformation, or a change in direction — characterized by magnitude, direction, and point of application
    Explanation: A force is a vector interaction between two objects. It is fully described by its magnitude (size), direction, and point of application on the object.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes a contact force?
    [ ] A force that acts between objects regardless of distance
    [ ] A force produced by electric charges at a distance
    [*] A force that arises from the direct physical interaction between two objects that are touching
    [ ] The gravitational attraction between two objects
    Explanation: Contact forces result from the electromagnetic interactions at the surfaces where objects meet. They include normal force, friction, tension, air resistance, and applied forces.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes a field force?
    [ ] A mathematical concept with no real-world effects
    [*] A force that acts on an object through a field, without requiring physical contact between the interacting objects
    [ ] A force that acts only on the surface of objects
    [ ] A force exerted only by magnets
    Explanation: Field forces (non-contact forces) are transmitted through fields that permeate space. Gravitational, electric, and magnetic forces are all field forces.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes net force?
    [*] The single resultant force obtained by taking the vector sum of all individual forces acting on an object
    [ ] The force applied by the hand only
    [ ] The strongest individual force on an object
    [ ] The average of all forces on an object
    Explanation: Net force = ΣF = F₁ + F₂ + F₃ + ... (vector sum). It determines the acceleration of the object: a = F_net/m.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes equilibrium?
    [ ] A state in which the object is perfectly still and has no forces acting on it at all, since the absence of motion implies the absence of all applied forces
    [*] A state in which the net force on an object is zero, resulting in no acceleration — the object either remains at rest or moves at constant velocity
    [ ] A state that can only be achieved in the microgravity environment of outer space, where gravitational forces are absent and no weight is experienced by objects
    [ ] A state in which all individual forces acting on the object have exactly equal magnitudes, even if they do not point in opposing directions to cancel out
    Explanation: Equilibrium (ΣF = 0) means no acceleration. Static equilibrium: object at rest. Dynamic equilibrium: object moves at constant velocity. In both cases, net force is zero.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes a free-body diagram?
    [ ] A graph that plots the magnitude of force acting on an object against elapsed time, showing how the applied force changes throughout the duration of motion
    [*] A simplified drawing that shows an object isolated from its surroundings with all external forces acting on it represented as labeled arrows
    [ ] A detailed diagram of an object falling freely under gravity alone, showing its trajectory path, velocity at each point, and the acceleration due to gravity
    [ ] A complete sketch of the entire experimental setup in a physics laboratory, including all equipment, instruments, measuring devices, and surrounding furniture
    Explanation: A free-body diagram (FBD) isolates an object and shows every external force as a vector arrow originating from the object's center. It is the primary tool for force analysis.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes a fundamental force?
    [ ] Any force whose magnitude is measured in the standard SI unit of newtons, regardless of its physical origin or the type of interaction that produces it
    [ ] The precise amount of force needed to accelerate a one-kilogram mass by exactly one meter per second squared, which defines the SI unit called the newton
    [ ] A force that acts only on fundamental subatomic particles such as quarks and leptons, and has no measurable effect on larger everyday macroscopic objects
    [*] One of the four basic forces of nature — gravitational, electromagnetic, strong nuclear, and weak nuclear — from which all other forces are derived
    Explanation: The four fundamental forces are: gravitational (between masses), electromagnetic (between charges), strong nuclear (binds quarks/nucleons), and weak nuclear (responsible for radioactive decay).

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes the normal force?
    [ ] The friction force that acts along the interface between two surfaces in contact, opposing the relative sliding motion or tendency of motion between them
    [ ] The gravitational force that the Earth exerts on an object, pulling it downward toward the center of the Earth with a magnitude equal to mass times g
    [*] The perpendicular contact force exerted by a surface on an object resting on or pressing against it, preventing the object from passing through the surface
    [ ] A force whose magnitude is always exactly equal to the weight of the object, regardless of the surface orientation, applied forces, or acceleration present
    Explanation: The normal force acts perpendicular to the contact surface. On a horizontal surface it often equals the object's weight, but on inclines or when additional vertical forces act, it differs.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes tension?
    [ ] The gravitational attraction between two objects with mass, acting at a distance through the gravitational field without any physical contact being required
    [ ] The restoring force produced by a spring when it is compressed below its natural equilibrium length, pushing outward on whatever compresses it at both ends
    [*] The pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or similar object when it is pulled taut by forces acting at each end
    [ ] A repulsive force that acts to push two objects apart from each other, operating through electromagnetic repulsion between like charges at the atomic level
    Explanation: Tension is a pulling force along the length of a flexible connector. In an ideal (massless, inextensible) string, tension is the same throughout.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes an interaction pair?
    [ ] Two identical forces acting in the same direction
    [ ] Two forces acting on the same object in opposite directions
    [*] Two forces of equal magnitude and opposite direction that act on two different objects as described by Newton's third law
    [ ] A pair of objects that never interact
    Explanation: An interaction pair (action-reaction pair) consists of two forces: F_AB on B by A, and F_BA on A by B. They are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects.

  Q21. A book rests on a table. Identify all forces acting on the book.
    [ ] Only the upward normal force from the table acts on the book, since gravity is canceled out by the table's presence and does not count as a separate force
    [*] Weight (gravitational force downward) and normal force from the table (upward) — these two forces are balanced, keeping the book in equilibrium
    [ ] Friction and tension are the only forces acting on the book, because the book must be held in place by friction to prevent it from sliding off the table
    [ ] Only gravity acts on the book pulling it downward, since the table merely blocks the book's path and does not exert any actual upward force on its surface
    Explanation: Two forces act on the book: (1) Weight (mg downward) from Earth's gravitational pull, and (2) Normal force (N upward) from the table surface. Since the book is at rest, N = mg.

  Q22. A student pushes a box across the floor at constant velocity by applying 50 N of force. What can you conclude about friction?
    [ ] Friction is zero because the box is currently in motion, and kinetic friction only exists when objects are stationary and about to start moving from rest
    [ ] Friction is greater than 50 N since the box requires extra force beyond the push to overcome the initial resistance and maintain its constant sliding speed
    [*] Friction equals 50 N — since velocity is constant, net force is zero, so friction must exactly balance the applied 50 N push
    [ ] Friction is less than 50 N because some of the applied pushing force goes into maintaining the box's velocity while only the remainder opposes friction
    Explanation: Constant velocity means zero acceleration, which means ΣF = 0. Therefore friction = applied force = 50 N. The forces are balanced.

  Q23. Two teams play tug-of-war. Team A pulls with 500 N and Team B pulls with 450 N. What is the net force and which direction does the rope accelerate?
    [ ] 0 N total — the two forces completely cancel each other since both teams pull on the same rope and opposite forces on the same object always sum to zero
    [ ] 950 N total — the net force is the sum of both pulls: 500 + 450 = 950 N, because all forces on a rope add together regardless of direction to give the total
    [ ] 50 N toward Team B — the net force points toward the weaker team because the stronger team's force pushes the rope away from themselves toward the opposition
    [*] 50 N toward Team A — net force = 500 − 450 = 50 N in Team A's direction
    Explanation: Net force = 500 − 450 = 50 N toward Team A (the stronger puller). The rope (and both teams) will accelerate in Team A's direction.

  Q24. An apple hangs from a tree branch by its stem. Draw the free-body diagram mentally. How many forces act on the apple?
    [ ] One — only gravity
    [ ] Zero — it is in equilibrium so no forces act
    [ ] Three — gravity, tension, and normal force
    [*] Two — weight (gravity pulling down) and tension in the stem (pulling up)
    Explanation: The apple has weight (mg downward) and tension in the stem (T upward). Since the apple is in equilibrium: T = mg. There is no normal force since the apple hangs freely.

  Q25. You stand on a bathroom scale in an elevator. The scale reads your normal weight. What can you say about the elevator's motion?
    [ ] The elevator must be completely stationary on a floor, because the scale can only display your true weight when there is absolutely zero velocity in the system
    [*] The elevator is either at rest or moving at constant velocity — zero acceleration means the scale reads your true weight
    [ ] The elevator is currently in free fall, because weightlessness and normal weight are indistinguishable from each other when measured by a standard bathroom scale
    [ ] The elevator must be accelerating upward, because only upward acceleration can produce a scale reading that matches your actual gravitational weight value exactly
    Explanation: A scale measures normal force. When N = mg (your true weight), the net force is zero, meaning the elevator has zero acceleration — it could be at rest or moving at constant velocity.

  Q26. A person pushes two stacked boxes (10 kg and 5 kg) with 30 N of force on a frictionless surface. What is the acceleration of the system?
    [ ] 6 m/s² — using only the lighter mass: a = F/m = 30/5 = 6 m/s², because the force acts primarily on the smaller box which then pushes the larger one forward
    [ ] 3 m/s² — using only the heavier mass: a = F/m = 30/10 = 3 m/s², because the acceleration depends on the larger box since it carries most of the system's weight
    [ ] 0.5 m/s² — dividing total mass by force: a = m/F = 15/30 = 0.5 m/s², because the formula for acceleration requires mass divided by force not the reverse
    [*] 2 m/s² — a = F/m_total = 30/15 = 2 m/s²
    Explanation: The total mass of the system is 10 + 5 = 15 kg. Since the surface is frictionless: a = F_net/m = 30/15 = 2 m/s².

  Q27. A parachutist falls at constant velocity. Her weight is 700 N. What is the upward drag force?
    [ ] 0 N — since she is falling there cannot be any upward force acting on her, as falling objects by definition experience only the downward pull of gravity alone
    [ ] 1400 N — the drag force must be double her weight to slow her descent, since the parachute needs to both support her weight and actively reduce her speed
    [*] 700 N — at constant velocity (terminal velocity), drag force equals weight so net force is zero
    [ ] 350 N — the drag force equals half her weight because the parachute only partially resists gravity, leaving the other half as a net downward force on her body
    Explanation: Constant velocity means zero acceleration (ΣF = 0). Therefore drag force upward must equal weight downward: F_drag = 700 N.

  Q28. Three forces act on an object: 10 N east, 6 N west, and 8 N north. What is the magnitude of the net force?
    [ ] 4 N — only the east-west forces matter for the resultant: 10 minus 6 = 4 N, and the northward force is perpendicular so it does not contribute to the net total
    [ ] 8 N — only the northward component matters for the resultant: the east and west forces cancel each other out, leaving just the 8 N north as the entire net force
    [*] √(4² + 8²) = √80 ≈ 8.94 N — horizontal net = 10 − 6 = 4 N east; vertical = 8 N north; resultant = √(16 + 64)
    [ ] 24 N — adding all three force magnitudes together: 10 + 6 + 8 = 24 N, since the net force is the scalar sum of all individual force magnitudes acting on the object
    Explanation: Horizontal: 10 − 6 = 4 N east. Vertical: 8 N north. Net force = √(4² + 8²) = √80 ≈ 8.94 N at arctan(8/4) ≈ 63.4° north of east.

  Q29. A spacecraft far from any planet drifts at 100 m/s with no forces acting on it. A student says it will eventually slow down. Is the student correct?
    [ ] Yes the student is correct — all moving objects eventually slow down and stop because motion naturally dissipates over time even without any external resistance present
    [ ] Yes the student is correct — interstellar space contains enough residual friction from gas molecules to gradually slow any spacecraft to zero velocity over time
    [ ] No the student is wrong — without a net force the spacecraft will actually speed up continuously, gaining velocity from the residual energy stored in its mass
    [*] No — with no net force, the spacecraft maintains constant velocity indefinitely (Newton's first law applies in the zero-force environment of deep space)
    Explanation: Newton's first law: without a net force, an object's velocity remains unchanged. In deep space, with negligible gravitational and drag forces, the spacecraft continues at 100 m/s forever.

  Q30. You are in a car that suddenly brakes. You feel pushed forward. Is there a real forward force on you?
    [ ] Yes — the dashboard or windshield exerts a real forward force on your body, actively pushing you toward the front of the car during the rapid braking event
    [ ] Yes — inertia itself is a real physical force that pushes your body forward whenever the car decelerates, acting in the opposite direction of the braking force
    [ ] No — the only force you feel is the seatbelt pulling you back, which creates the illusion of forward motion even though no other forces are present on your body
    [*] No — your body tends to continue forward at the car's original speed (inertia); the sensation of being pushed forward is due to the car decelerating beneath you, not a real forward force
    Explanation: When the car brakes, there is no forward force on you. Your body simply continues at the original velocity due to inertia (Newton's first law), while the car slows. The seatbelt provides the true backward force that decelerates you.

--- Lesson 3.2: Newton's First Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Newton's First Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Newton's first law of motion?
Newton's first law (law of inertia) states that an object's velocity remains constant (including zero) unless a net external force acts on it.
What is inertia?
Inertia is the natural resistance of an object to any change in its velocity. An object with more mass has more inertia.
If all forces on an object are balanced, what is its acceleration?
Balanced forces mean net force = 0. By Newton's second law (or first law), the acceleration is zero.

--- Lesson 3.2: Newton's First Law (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is Newton's first law of motion?
    [ ] The net force on an object equals its mass multiplied by its acceleration, describing how force produces proportional changes in an object's velocity over time
    [ ] For every action force there is an equal and opposite reaction force acting on a different object, as formalized in Newton's third law of interaction pairs
    [*] An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force
    [ ] Energy cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system but can only be transformed from one form to another, conserving the total energy at all times
    Explanation: Newton's first law (law of inertia) states that an object's velocity remains constant (including zero) unless a net external force acts on it.

  Q2. Newton's first law is also known as the law of:
    [ ] Acceleration
    [ ] Action and reaction
    [*] Inertia
    [ ] Conservation of energy
    Explanation: Newton's first law is called the law of inertia because it describes inertia — the tendency of objects to resist changes in their state of motion.

  Q3. What is inertia?
    [*] The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion
    [ ] The speed at which objects naturally move
    [ ] The force that keeps objects moving
    [ ] A type of friction
    Explanation: Inertia is the natural resistance of an object to any change in its velocity. An object with more mass has more inertia.

  Q4. Which property of an object determines how much inertia it has?
    [ ] Weight
    [ ] Speed
    [*] Mass
    [ ] Volume
    Explanation: Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia. A more massive object has greater inertia and is harder to accelerate or decelerate.

  Q5. A hockey puck slides on frictionless ice. What happens to its velocity?
    [ ] It speeds up over time due to the momentum stored inside it, because an object in motion naturally gains additional velocity even without an applied force
    [*] It remains constant — with no net force, the puck continues at the same velocity forever
    [ ] It eventually comes to a complete stop after a certain time, because all moving objects naturally lose their velocity and return to a state of rest on any surface
    [ ] It gradually slows down over time, because objects naturally lose velocity even in the absence of friction due to the inherent dissipation of motion energy
    Explanation: On frictionless ice, there is no net force on the puck (gravity and normal force cancel vertically). Newton's first law dictates that the puck maintains its velocity.

  Q6. If all forces on an object are balanced, what is its acceleration?
    [ ] Equal to the applied force divided by mass
    [ ] It depends on the object's speed
    [*] Zero
    [ ] 9.8 m/s²
    Explanation: Balanced forces mean net force = 0. By Newton's second law (or first law), the acceleration is zero.

  Q7. A ball rolls along a flat surface and eventually stops. Why does it stop?
    [ ] It runs out of force
    [ ] Gravity pulls it to a halt
    [*] Friction and air resistance (unbalanced forces) slow it down
    [ ] Motion naturally stops on its own
    Explanation: The ball decelerates because friction and air resistance provide a net backward force. Without these forces, it would continue rolling indefinitely (Newton's first law).

  Q8. A passenger in a car lurches forward when the car suddenly stops. This happens because of:
    [ ] Gravity pulling the passenger forward
    [*] Inertia — the passenger's body tends to continue moving forward at the car's original velocity
    [ ] A forward force from the engine
    [ ] Air pressure inside the car
    Explanation: The passenger's body was moving at the car's speed. When the car stops, the body continues forward due to inertia until a force (seatbelt, dashboard) stops it.

  Q9. Newton's first law applies in which type of reference frame?
    [ ] Only rotating reference frames
    [ ] Only the Earth's reference frame
    [ ] All reference frames equally
    [*] Inertial reference frames — frames that are not accelerating
    Explanation: Newton's first law is valid only in inertial (non-accelerating) reference frames. In fact, an inertial frame is defined as one where Newton's first law holds.

  Q10. An object is moving at constant velocity. Must there be a net force on it?
    [ ] Yes — friction must be overcome
    [ ] Only if the object is heavy
    [*] No — constant velocity means zero acceleration, which means zero net force
    [ ] Yes — a force is needed to maintain motion
    Explanation: Newton's first law: no net force is needed to maintain constant velocity. Only to change velocity (accelerate).

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes Newton's first law?
    [ ] The principle that F = ma
    [ ] The principle that force is proportional to displacement
    [*] The principle that an object will not change its velocity unless a net external force causes it to accelerate
    [ ] The principle that for every action there is a reaction
    Explanation: Newton's first law states that velocity is naturally constant — only a nonzero net force can change it. This applies to objects at rest (v = 0) and objects in motion (v ≠ 0).

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes inertia?
    [ ] The kinetic energy stored in a moving object due to its speed and mass, calculated as one-half times mass times velocity squared in the standard formula
    [ ] The frictional resistance between two surfaces in contact, which depends on the coefficient of friction and the normal force pressing the surfaces together
    [*] The intrinsic property of matter that resists any change in velocity — an object's tendency to maintain its current state of rest or uniform motion
    [ ] A force that actively opposes an object's motion, pushing back against the direction of travel and gradually reducing the object's speed until it comes to rest
    Explanation: Inertia is not a force but a property of matter. Greater mass means greater inertia. A bowling ball has more inertia than a tennis ball — it is harder to start, stop, or redirect.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes mass in the context of Newton's first law?
    [*] A quantitative measure of an object's inertia — the resistance to changes in motion
    [ ] The same as weight
    [ ] The amount of space an object occupies
    [ ] The gravitational pull on an object
    Explanation: Mass measures inertia. Unlike weight (which depends on g), mass is the same everywhere. A 10 kg object has the same inertia on Earth, the Moon, or in deep space.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes a balanced force system?
    [ ] Forces that are all the same magnitude
    [*] A set of forces acting on an object whose vector sum is zero, resulting in no acceleration
    [ ] Forces that all point in the same direction
    [ ] A system with only one force
    Explanation: When forces are balanced (ΣF = 0), there is no net force and no acceleration. The object is in equilibrium — either at rest or moving at constant velocity.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes an unbalanced force system?
    [ ] Forces that are all oriented perpendicular to each other, forming right angles at the point of application and creating a complex multi-directional force pattern
    [ ] A system containing exactly two forces of any magnitude and direction, since having precisely two forces is the defining characteristic of an unbalanced arrangement
    [*] A set of forces acting on an object whose vector sum is nonzero, resulting in acceleration in the direction of the net force
    [ ] Forces in which one individual force is stronger in magnitude than the others, which always guarantees the presence of a nonzero net force on the object in question
    Explanation: Unbalanced forces produce a nonzero net force, which causes acceleration (a = F_net/m). The acceleration is in the direction of the net force.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes the natural state of motion according to Newton?
    [*] Constant velocity (including rest) — an object free from net forces maintains its velocity indefinitely
    [ ] Objects naturally accelerate on their own over time, building up speed spontaneously without requiring any external force to drive the increase in velocity
    [ ] Objects naturally move in circular paths unless straightened out by an applied force, since circular motion is the default state of all matter in the universe
    [ ] Everything in the universe naturally comes to rest eventually, because all motion gradually dies out on its own even when no retarding forces are present
    Explanation: Before Newton, Aristotle believed rest was the natural state. Newton showed that constant velocity (including rest) is natural — only forces change velocity.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes an external force?
    [ ] A force that only acts on the surface of an object
    [*] A force applied to a system from outside that system, capable of changing the system's momentum or energy
    [ ] A force that originates inside the system
    [ ] Any force that is very large
    Explanation: External forces come from outside the defined system. Internal forces (within the system) cancel out. Only external forces can change the motion of the system's center of mass.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes static equilibrium?
    [ ] A state where an object oscillates back and forth
    [ ] A state where an object moves at constant velocity
    [*] A state in which an object is at rest and all forces acting on it are balanced (net force = 0, net torque = 0)
    [ ] A state where all forces are zero individually
    Explanation: Static equilibrium: the object is at rest (v = 0) and has zero acceleration (ΣF = 0). For extended objects, the net torque must also be zero.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes dynamic equilibrium?
    [ ] A state where the forces acting on an object fluctuate rapidly in magnitude and direction, causing the object to vibrate or oscillate around a central position
    [ ] A state where an object accelerates uniformly under a constant net force, gaining equal amounts of velocity during each equal time interval of the motion period
    [ ] A state that can only be achieved in a perfect vacuum, since air molecules always prevent any real object from maintaining truly constant velocity in practice
    [*] A state in which an object moves at constant velocity with zero acceleration because all forces acting on it are balanced
    Explanation: Dynamic equilibrium: the object moves at constant nonzero velocity (ΣF = 0, a = 0). A car cruising at constant speed on a straight road (engine force = friction + drag) is in dynamic equilibrium.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes the law of inertia historically?
    [ ] A law stating that heavier objects always move slower than lighter ones under the same conditions, because mass and speed are inversely proportional at all times
    [ ] A law discovered by Albert Einstein as part of his theory of general relativity, describing how mass curves spacetime and affects the motion of nearby objects

--- Lesson 3.3: Newton's Second Law (F = ma) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Newton's Second Law (F = ma)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the acceleration of a 5 kg object with a net force of 20 N acting on it?
a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s².
What is the weight of a 10 kg object on Earth (g = 9.8 m/s²)?
Weight = mg = 10 × 9.8 = 98 N. Weight is the gravitational force on an object.
A 2000 kg car accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s in 10 seconds. What is the net force on the car?
a = Δv/Δt = 20/10 = 2 m/s². F = ma = 2000 × 2 = 4000 N.
Two forces act on a 5 kg object: 30 N east and 10 N west. What is the acceleration?
F_net = 30 − 10 = 20 N east. a = F_net/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s² east.
A 70 kg skydiver experiences a downward weight of 686 N and an upward air resistance of 400 N. What is her acceleration?
F_net = 686 − 400 = 286 N downward. a = F_net/m = 286/70 ≈ 4.09 m/s² downward. She is still accelerating but slower than free fall.
A 1500 kg car engine produces 6000 N of forward force. Friction and air resistance total 1500 N. What is the car's acceleration?
F_net = 6000 − 1500 = 4500 N forward. a = 4500/1500 = 3 m/s².
A student pushes a 20 kg box with 100 N horizontally. Friction is 40 N. The box moves on a level surface. What is the box's acceleration?
F_net = F_applied − f = 100 − 40 = 60 N. a = F_net/m = 60/20 = 3 m/s².
A 500 g toy car (0.5 kg) accelerates at 2 m/s² on a frictionless track then enters a rough patch and decelerates at 1 m/s². What is the friction force on the rough patch?
On the rough patch, the only horizontal force is friction. F_friction = ma = 0.5 × 1 = 0.5 N, directed opposite to the car's motion.

    [ ] 1 N — friction equals mass times the original acceleration: f = 0.5 times 2 = 1 N, because friction must match the driving force that was present on the smooth track
    [*] 0.5 N — friction = ma = 0.5 × 1 = 0.5 N (opposing motion)
    [ ] 0.25 N — friction equals mass times deceleration divided by 2: f = 0.5 times 1 / 2 = 0.25 N, because only half the deceleration comes from the friction force
    [ ] 2 N — friction equals mass times the sum of both accelerations: f = 0.5 times (2 + 1) = 1.5, rounded to about 2 N since both phases contribute to friction
    Explanation: On the rough patch, the only horizontal force is friction. F_friction = ma = 0.5 × 1 = 0.5 N, directed opposite to the car's motion.

--- Lesson 3.4: Newton's Third Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Newton's Third Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
When a gun fires a bullet, what is the reaction force?

--- Lesson 3.4: Newton's Third Law (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What does Newton's third law state?
    [*] For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force
    [ ] The equation F = ma, which relates the net force acting on an object to the product of its mass and the resulting acceleration it experiences
    [ ] The principle that energy can never be created or destroyed in any process, but can only be converted from one form to another within a closed system
    [ ] The principle that objects at rest will remain at rest and objects in motion stay in motion at constant velocity unless acted on by an external net force
    Explanation: Newton's third law: when object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

  Q2. Newton's third law forces act on how many objects?
    [ ] Three objects
    [ ] No objects — they cancel out
    [ ] The same object
    [*] Two different objects
    Explanation: Third law pairs always act on two DIFFERENT objects. This is key — they never cancel because they act on different things.

  Q3. You push a wall with 50 N. How much force does the wall push back on you?
    [*] 50 N — equal and opposite to your push
    [ ] 0 N — walls are rigid structures that cannot push, so no reaction force is generated
    [ ] 100 N — the wall pushes back twice as hard to compensate for its rigidity
    [ ] 25 N — only half the force is returned because the wall absorbs the other half
    Explanation: By Newton's third law, if you push the wall with 50 N, the wall pushes back on you with exactly 50 N in the opposite direction.

  Q4. When you walk, what force actually propels you forward?
    [ ] Your muscles pushing you forward through the air
    [*] The friction force from the ground pushing forward on your foot
    [ ] The normal force from the ground
    [ ] The force of gravity
    Explanation: Your foot pushes backward on the ground (action). By Newton's third law, the ground pushes forward on your foot (reaction). This friction force is what propels you.

  Q5. A swimmer pushes water backward with her arms. What propels her forward?
    [ ] Buoyancy created by the water's displaced volume lifts her body upward and forward, providing the propulsion needed for horizontal movement through the pool
    [ ] Her muscles directly push her body forward through the water by internal force generation, without any interaction between her limbs and the surrounding fluid
    [*] The water pushes her forward — Newton's third law reaction to her pushing the water backward
    [ ] The natural current and wave motion of the water carries her forward in the desired direction, independent of any forces her arms apply to the surrounding water
    Explanation: The swimmer pushes water backward (action). The water pushes the swimmer forward (reaction). This third law pair is what enables swimming.

  Q6. Do action-reaction force pairs ever cancel each other out?
    [*] No — they act on different objects, so they cannot cancel
    [ ] Yes — they always cancel, which is why nothing moves
    [ ] Only in space
    [ ] Only when the forces are equal
    Explanation: Action-reaction pairs act on DIFFERENT objects and therefore cannot cancel. Force cancellation only happens when multiple forces act on the SAME object.

  Q7. A horse pulls a cart. The cart pulls back on the horse with equal force. How does the system move forward?
    [*] The horse pushes backward on the ground, and the ground pushes the horse forward — this external friction force exceeds the cart's friction, so the system accelerates
    [ ] Newton's third law does not apply to this particular situation, because biological systems like horses and carts are too complex for the simple interaction law
    [ ] The cart does not actually pull back on the horse at all, because inanimate objects like carts cannot generate forces of their own against a living animal
    [ ] The horse's pull on the cart is actually larger in magnitude than the cart's pull back on the horse, which creates the net forward force for the system
    Explanation: The horse-ground interaction provides a forward force on the horse. The cart's rolling friction is less than this forward force. The net external force on the system (horse + cart) is forward, so the system accelerates.

  Q8. When a gun fires a bullet, what is the reaction force?
    [ ] There is no reaction — the bullet just goes forward
    [ ] Gravity pulls the bullet down
    [*] The bullet pushes the gun backward (recoil) with equal force
    [ ] Air resistance on the bullet
    Explanation: The gun pushes the bullet forward (action). The bullet pushes the gun backward (reaction) — this is experienced as recoil. The forces are equal in magnitude but act on different objects.

  Q9. A ball sits on a table. Are the weight of the ball and the normal force from the table a Newton's third law pair?
    [ ] Yes they are a third law pair because all balanced forces acting in opposite directions on a single object are automatically classified as Newton's third law pairs
    [*] No — they are both forces on the ball. The third law pair to the ball's weight is the ball pulling Earth upward; the pair to the normal force is the ball pushing down on the table
    [ ] Yes they are a third law pair since the two forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, which is the only requirement for being a third law pair
    [ ] No they are not a third law pair because they are different types of forces — gravity is a field force while the normal force is a contact force between surfaces
    Explanation: Weight (Earth pulls ball) pairs with the ball pulling Earth. Normal force (table pushes ball) pairs with ball pushing table. Two forces on the same object are NOT a third law pair.

  Q10. A rocket works in space where there is no air to push against. How does it accelerate?
    [ ] It pushes against the vacuum of space, because even empty space has enough substance for Newton's third law to produce a forward reaction force on the vehicle
    [ ] It cannot work in space at all, because rocket engines fundamentally require an atmosphere or solid ground to push against in order to generate forward thrust
    [ ] The solar wind provides a steady forward push that propels the spacecraft through the solar system, similar to how wind pushes a sailboat across the ocean
    [*] It pushes exhaust gases backward, and by Newton's third law, the gases push the rocket forward
    Explanation: The rocket expels exhaust backward at high speed (action). The exhaust pushes the rocket forward (reaction). No external medium is needed — Newton's third law works between the rocket and its exhaust.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes Newton's third law?
    [ ] The principle that objects at rest stay at rest and objects in motion stay in motion unless acted on by an external net force, also called the law of inertia
    [ ] The principle that energy is conserved in all collisions and interactions, meaning the total energy before an event always equals the total energy after the event
    [ ] The principle that the net force on an object equals the product of its mass and acceleration, expressed by the mathematical equation F equals m times a
    [*] The principle that whenever one body exerts a force on a second body, the second body simultaneously exerts a force that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction on the first body
    Explanation: Newton's third law: F_AB = −F_BA. These interaction forces are simultaneous, equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes an action-reaction pair?
    [ ] Two forces that are always gravitational in nature, because Newton's third law was originally formulated exclusively for the gravitational attraction between masses
    [ ] Two forces that cancel each other out when acting on the same object, resulting in zero net force and zero acceleration for that particular single object
    [ ] Two forces that combine additively to create motion, adding their magnitudes together to produce a larger resultant force that drives the object forward
    [*] Two forces that are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, of the same type, and act on two different objects simultaneously
    Explanation: Action-reaction pairs are the hallmark of Newton's third law. They are always: (1) equal magnitude, (2) opposite direction, (3) same type of force, and (4) on different objects.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes the term 'action force'?
    [*] Either one of the two forces in a Newton's third law interaction pair — the designation of 'action' versus 'reaction' is arbitrary
    [ ] The larger of the two forces in a Newton's third law interaction pair, since the action force is defined as whichever force has the greater numerical magnitude
    [ ] A force that can only be exerted by a living being such as a person or animal, since only biological organisms are capable of initiating an action force
    [ ] The force that always comes first in time before the corresponding reaction force follows, since there is always a measurable delay between the action and reaction
    Explanation: There is no physical distinction between action and reaction — the labels are arbitrary. Both forces in the pair are simultaneous and equivalent. Either can be called the 'action.'

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes recoil?
    [ ] The gravitational force that the Earth exerts on a firearm, pulling it downward with a force equal to the weapon's mass multiplied by the gravitational acceleration
    [*] The backward motion of an object that results from the reaction force when it propels or ejects another object forward
    [ ] The elastic restoring force produced by a spring when it returns to its natural equilibrium length after being stretched or compressed by an external applied force
    [ ] The forward motion of a projectile through the air after being launched, which is the initial kinematic effect of the launching force applied to the projectile
    Explanation: Recoil is a direct consequence of Newton's third law. When a gun fires a bullet forward, the equal and opposite reaction force pushes the gun backward.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes thrust?
    [ ] The gravitational pull of the Moon on a spacecraft passing through cislunar space, which varies inversely with the square of the distance between the two objects
    [ ] The drag force experienced by an airplane as it moves through the atmosphere, created by the friction and pressure of air molecules impacting the aircraft surfaces
    [ ] The total weight of a rocket sitting on the launch pad before ignition, equal to the entire mass of the rocket and fuel multiplied by the gravitational acceleration
    [*] The forward force on a rocket or jet produced by the reaction to ejecting mass (exhaust) at high velocity backward
    Explanation: Thrust = reaction force from expelled exhaust (Newton's third law). Thrust = dm/dt × v_exhaust (rate of mass ejection × exhaust velocity).

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes an interaction?
    [ ] A high-speed collision between two or more objects, where significant forces are exchanged during a brief contact period that alters each object's velocity
    [*] A mutual exchange of forces between two objects — each object exerts a force on the other simultaneously
    [ ] A force that acts on only one object in complete isolation, without any corresponding force being exerted on any other object anywhere in the physical system
    [ ] Any event that involves motion of any kind, including rotation, translation, vibration, or oscillation of one or more objects within a defined spatial region
    Explanation: An interaction is the fundamental concept behind Newton's third law. Forces never exist in isolation — they always arise from interactions between pairs of objects.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes an internal force?
    [ ] A force applied from outside the defined system boundary by an external agent, capable of changing the total momentum and kinetic energy of the entire system
    [ ] Any force whose magnitude is smaller than 10 newtons, since forces below this threshold are classified differently from larger forces in standard physics notation
    [ ] A gravitational force acting within the interior of a planet, directed toward the center of mass and responsible for compressing the planet's internal material
    [*] A force between objects within a defined system that does not change the system's total momentum
    Explanation: Internal forces are third law pairs within a system. They cancel when calculating the system's total force, which is why only external forces change a system's momentum.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes an external force?
    [ ] A force acting between two objects that are both contained within the defined system boundary, forming a Newton's third law pair that sums to zero internally
    [*] A force exerted on a system by an object outside that system, capable of changing the system's total momentum
    [ ] Only the gravitational force, because gravity is the sole example of a force that originates from outside a system and can influence the system's total momentum
    [ ] A force that performs no work on the system regardless of the displacement, because its direction is always perpendicular to the motion of the system at every point
    Explanation: External forces come from outside the defined system boundary. They change the system's center-of-mass motion and total momentum, while internal forces do not.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes the normal force as a reaction force?
    [ ] The direct reaction to gravity pulling the object downward, since the normal force is simply gravity's Newton's third law partner acting in the opposite direction
    [*] When an object pushes on a surface, the surface pushes back perpendicular to itself with equal magnitude — this is the Newton's third law reaction to the object's push on the surface
    [ ] A force that is always exactly equal to the weight of the object in every situation, regardless of whether the object is on a flat surface, incline, or accelerating
    [ ] A force that only acts in the horizontal direction along a surface, providing the friction needed to prevent objects from sliding sideways across the contact area
    Explanation: The normal force is the third law reaction to the object pressing on the surface. Note: it is NOT the third law pair of the object's weight (which pairs with the object pulling Earth).

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes force pairs in collisions?
    [ ] Forces that exist only after the collision has ended, arising from the deformation and rebound of the objects once they have separated from their initial contact
    [*] During a collision, the forces between two colliding objects are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction throughout the contact — consistent with Newton's third law
    [ ] The stronger or more massive object always exerts a greater force on the weaker object, because force depends on the mass and strength of the object producing it
    [ ] The lighter object always experiences a greater force during the collision, because less massive objects absorb more impact energy and therefore feel stronger forces
    Explanation: During any collision, Newton's third law guarantees that both objects experience equal and opposite forces at every instant. The accelerations differ because their masses differ (F = ma).

  Q21. A large truck collides with a small car. Which vehicle experiences the greater force during the collision?
    [*] Both experience the same magnitude of force — Newton's third law guarantees equal and opposite forces regardless of mass
    [ ] The truck exerts a much greater force on the car than the car exerts on the truck, because the truck has significantly more mass and is therefore much more powerful
    [ ] The heavier vehicle always experiences the greater impact force in any collision, because a larger mass means a larger force is required to change its velocity
    [ ] The car exerts a greater force on the truck than the truck exerts on the car, because the smaller object concentrates its force into a smaller area upon impact
    Explanation: Newton's third law: F_truck→car = −F_car→truck. The forces are equal. However, the car has more acceleration (a = F/m) because of its smaller mass, which is why small cars sustain more damage.

  Q22. When you jump, you push down on the ground. How does this make you go up?
    [*] The ground pushes up on you with a force equal to your push — this upward reaction force (greater than your weight) accelerates you off the ground

    [ ] The surrounding air pushes you upward when you crouch and spring up, because compressing air beneath your body creates enough upward pressure to lift your weight
    [ ] Your muscles directly lift your body mass upward by generating internal forces, without any interaction between your feet and the ground being necessary at all
    Explanation: You push the ground down (action). Ground pushes you up (reaction). When this upward force exceeds your weight, you have a net upward force and you accelerate upward.

  Q24. A student says: 'If every force has an equal and opposite reaction, how can anything ever accelerate? All forces should cancel!' Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
    [ ] Reaction forces are always slightly smaller in magnitude than the corresponding action forces, which creates a small net force that allows objects to accelerate forward
    [ ] Reaction forces are slightly delayed in time after the action forces, and this brief temporal gap creates a window during which the object can accelerate before the reaction arrives
    [ ] The student is completely correct in their reasoning — since all forces truly do cancel in pairs, no object should ever be able to accelerate under any circumstances whatsoever
    [*] The flaw is that action-reaction forces act on DIFFERENT objects — to determine acceleration, only consider forces on ONE object. The net force on a single object can be nonzero
    Explanation: Third law pairs act on DIFFERENT objects. For object A, we sum all forces ON A (including B's reaction) to find A's net force. The force by A on B is irrelevant to A's motion.

    Explanation: There are two main pairs: (1) gravitational interaction between Earth and person, (2) contact interaction between person and scale. Weight and normal force act on the same object (person), so they are NOT a third law pair.

--- Lesson 3.5: Friction & Tension (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Friction & Tension
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between static and kinetic friction?
Static friction acts when there is no relative motion (it prevents sliding). Kinetic friction acts during relative motion (sliding). Static friction has a higher maximum value.
What is the formula for kinetic friction?
Kinetic friction = μ_k N. It is approximately constant regardless of the sliding speed (to first approximation).
What is tension in a rope?
Tension is the force transmitted through a rope, string, or cable when pulled taut. In an ideal (massless) rope, tension is the same at every point along it.
In an ideal (massless, inextensible) rope, what is true about tension throughout the rope?
For a massless rope, Newton's second law requires that the net force on any segment is zero (since m = 0). This means tension must be constant throughout.
A 10 kg box sits on a surface with μ_s = 0.4. What is the maximum static friction? (g = 10 m/s²)
N = mg = 100 N. f_s,max = μ_s × N = 0.4 × 100 = 40 N. You need more than 40 N of horizontal force to start the box sliding.
A box slides across a floor with μ_k = 0.3 and a normal force of 50 N. What is the kinetic friction force?
f_k = μ_k × N = 0.3 × 50 = 15 N, opposing the direction of sliding.
A 20 kg crate sits on a floor (μ_s = 0.5, μ_k = 0.3). You push horizontally with 80 N. Does the crate move? What is the friction force? (g = 10 m/s²)
N = mg = 200 N. f_s,max = μ_s N = 0.5 × 200 = 100 N. Since the applied 80 N < 100 N, static friction adjusts to exactly 80 N (opposing the push), and the crate does not move.
A 5 kg mass hangs from a rope attached to the ceiling. What is the tension in the rope? (g = 10 m/s²)
The mass is static. Forces on it: tension T upward, weight mg = 50 N downward. Equilibrium: T = mg = 50 N.
Two blocks (3 kg and 5 kg) are connected by a rope on a frictionless surface. A 24 N force pulls the 5 kg block. What is the acceleration and the tension in the rope between them?
System: a = F/(m₁ + m₂) = 24/8 = 3 m/s². For the 3 kg block (only force is tension): T = m₁ × a = 3 × 3 = 9 N.
A person pulls a sled (15 kg) with a rope at 30° above horizontal. The rope tension is 60 N. μ_k = 0.2. What is the acceleration? (g = 10 m/s²)
F_x = 60cos30° ≈ 52 N. N = mg − 60sin30° = 150 − 30 = 120 N. f_k = 0.2 × 120 = 24 N. F_net = 52 − 24 = 28 N. a = 28/15 ≈ 1.87 m/s².
An Atwood machine has masses of 4 kg and 6 kg connected by a rope over a frictionless pulley. What is the acceleration and tension? (g = 10 m/s²)
a = (m₂ − m₁)g/(m₁ + m₂) = 20/10 = 2 m/s². For the 4 kg mass: T − 4g = 4a → T = 4(10 + 2) = 48 N.
A car's tires have μ_s = 0.8 with the road. The car has mass 1500 kg. What is the maximum braking force before the tires start to skid? (g = 10 m/s²)
N = mg = 1500 × 10 = 15000 N. f_s,max = μ_s N = 0.8 × 15000 = 12000 N. Beyond this force, the tires lose grip and start skidding (switching to kinetic friction).

    [ ] f = the coefficient of friction times mg only, because the normal force is always equal to the weight and contact area determines the actual friction value

    [ ] f_k = the coefficient of kinetic friction times the contact area, since kinetic friction depends primarily on how much surface area is touching between objects

    [ ] In the direction of motion
    [ ] Toward the center of the Earth
    [ ] Perpendicular to the surface

  Q6. What is tension in a rope?

    [ ] The weight of the rope
    [ ] The stretching limit of the rope
    [ ] The friction between the rope and an object

--- Lesson 3.6: Normal Force & Inclined Planes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Normal Force & Inclined Planes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
On an inclined plane, what is the component of gravity parallel to the surface?
The weight component along (parallel to) the incline = mg sinθ. This is what tends to make objects slide down.
On an inclined plane, what is the normal force?
The normal force on an incline = mg cosθ. It equals the perpendicular component of gravity on the incline surface.
A 5 kg block sits on a 30° incline. What is the normal force? (g = 10 m/s²)
N = mg cosθ = 5 × 10 × cos30° = 50 × 0.866 ≈ 43.3 N.
A 10 kg box sits on a frictionless 30° incline. What is its acceleration down the slope? (g = 10 m/s²)
On a frictionless incline: a = g sinθ = 10 × sin30° = 5 m/s² down the slope. The normal force and perpendicular gravity cancel.
A 20 kg box is on a 45° incline with μ_k = 0.3. It slides down. What is its acceleration? (g = 10 m/s²)
a = g(sinθ − μ_k cosθ) = 10(0.707 − 0.3 × 0.707) = 10(0.707 − 0.212) = 10 × 0.495 = 4.95 m/s².
A child slides down a playground slide (incline angle 35°) at constant velocity. What is the coefficient of kinetic friction?
Constant velocity → a = 0 → mg sinθ = μ_k mg cosθ → μ_k = sinθ/cosθ = tan35° ≈ 0.700.
You place a coin on a book and slowly tilt the book. At 22° the coin starts to slide. What is the coefficient of static friction?
At the critical angle: mg sinθ = μ_s mg cosθ → μ_s = tanθ_c = tan22° ≈ 0.404.
A 50 kg person stands at the center of a 4 m long plank resting on two supports at each end. What is the normal force at each support? (g = 10 m/s²)
By symmetry (person at center), each support bears half the weight. N₁ = N₂ = mg/2 = 500/2 = 250 N.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which definition BEST describes mechanical advantage of an inclined plane?
For a frictionless incline: Force needed = mg sinθ, which is less than mg. The longer the incline (smaller θ), the smaller the force needed, but the greater the distance traveled. Work = same.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes the free-body diagram for an object on an incline?

--- Lesson 3.7: Circular Motion & Centripetal Force (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Circular Motion & Centripetal Force
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is centripetal acceleration?
Centripetal acceleration always points toward the center of the circle, continuously changing the direction of velocity to produce circular motion.
What is the formula for centripetal acceleration?
Centripetal acceleration = v²/r. A higher speed or smaller radius produces greater centripetal acceleration.
What is the formula for centripetal force?
F_c = ma_c = m(v²/r) = mv²/r. The centripetal force is the net force directed toward the center.
A car moves around a circular track of radius 50 m at 20 m/s. What is its centripetal acceleration?
a_c = v²/r = 20²/50 = 400/50 = 8 m/s² directed toward the center of the track.
A 0.5 kg ball on a 1 m string moves in a horizontal circle at 4 m/s. What is the tension in the string?
T = mv²/r = 0.5 × 4²/1 = 0.5 × 16 = 8 N. Tension provides the centripetal force.
A 1000 kg car rounds a flat curve (r = 100 m). The coefficient of static friction is 0.6. What is the maximum safe speed? (g = 10 m/s²)
μ_s mg = mv²/r → v² = μ_s gr = 0.6 × 10 × 100 = 600 → v = √600 ≈ 24.5 m/s.
A stone on a string moves in a vertical circle with radius 0.8 m. What is the minimum speed at the top for the string to remain taut?
At the top, minimum tension = 0: mg = mv²/r → v² = gr → v = √(9.8 × 0.8) = √7.84 ≈ 2.8 m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: A fan blade rotates at 120 RPM. A point on the tip (r = 0.3 m) has what centripetal acceleration?
120 RPM = 2 Hz. ω = 2π × 2 = 4π rad/s. a_c = ω²r = (4π)² × 0.3 = 16π² × 0.3 ≈ 47.4 m/s².
Important Concepts
Which definition BEST describes frequency in circular motion
Frequency f = 1/T = ω/(2π). It counts how many full circles per second. 1 Hz = 1 revolution per second.

    [*] Circular motion where the speed changes — the object has both centripetal acceleration (toward center) and tangential acceleration (along the path)
    Explanation: In non-uniform circular motion, the speed changes, producing tangential acceleration. The total acceleration is the vector sum of centripetal and tangential components.

  Q21. A car moves around a circular track of radius 50 m at 20 m/s. What is its centripetal acceleration?
    [ ] 1000 m/s squared — using a_c = v times r = 20 times 50 = 1000 m/s squared, because centripetal acceleration equals the product of velocity and radius
    [*] 8 m/s² — a_c = v²/r = 400/50 = 8 m/s² toward the center
    [ ] 0.4 m/s squared — using a_c = v divided by r = 20/50 = 0.4 m/s squared, because centripetal acceleration equals velocity divided by radius without squaring

    Explanation: a_c = v²/r = 20²/50 = 400/50 = 8 m/s² directed toward the center of the track.

  Q22. A 0.5 kg ball on a 1 m string moves in a horizontal circle at 4 m/s. What is the tension in the string?

    Explanation: T = mv²/r = 0.5 × 4²/1 = 0.5 × 16 = 8 N. Tension provides the centripetal force.

  Q23. A satellite orbits Earth at radius r with speed v. If it moves to an orbit with radius 2r, how does the required centripetal acceleration change?

--- Lesson 3.8: Non-Inertial Frames & Pseudo-Forces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Non-Inertial Frames & Pseudo-Forces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a non-inertial reference frame?
A non-inertial frame accelerates or rotates. In such frames, Newton's laws don't hold in their usual form — pseudo-forces must be introduced.
What is a pseudo-force (fictitious force)?
Pseudo-forces arise because the reference frame itself is accelerating. They have no agent (no third law partner) and disappear when viewed from an inertial frame.
When you are in a car that accelerates forward, you feel pushed backward. What is this 'force'?
In the car's (accelerating) frame, a pseudo-force pushes you backward. In an inertial frame view, your body simply maintains its velocity while the car accelerates forward beneath you.

    [ ] To the left or west — in a counterclockwise rotating frame the Coriolis force deflects all northward-moving objects to the left of their initial direction of travel
    [ ] Straight up off the surface of the turntable, because the Coriolis force acts perpendicular to both the velocity and the rotation axis in the vertical direction only
    Explanation: For counterclockwise rotation, F_Cor = −2m(ω × v) deflects objects to the right of their velocity direction. This is analogous to the Northern Hemisphere's Coriolis effect.

  Q27. An astronaut in a freely falling spacecraft releases a ball. The ball appears to float motionless beside her. Explain using pseudo-forces.
    [ ] The ball is held in place by the air pressure inside the sealed spacecraft cabin, which creates an equilibrium zone that prevents any object from drifting in any direction
    [*] In the spacecraft's non-inertial frame (free-fall), the gravitational pseudo-force exactly cancels real gravity, making the effective gravity zero — everything inside appears weightless
    [ ] There is no gravity present in outer space at the spacecraft's altitude, so the ball simply has no force acting on it and naturally remains stationary beside the astronaut
    [ ] The ball has no mass while inside the spacecraft because objects lose their mass in microgravity environments, making them immune to all forces including gravity
    Explanation: In the free-falling frame, the pseudo-force is mg upward, exactly canceling the real gravitational force mg downward. The net effective force is zero, producing apparent weightlessness.

  Q28. Earth rotates. At the equator, how does this affect your apparent weight compared to the poles?
    [ ] There is no difference between the equator and poles, because Earth's rotation has no measurable effect on the apparent weight of objects at any latitude on the surface
    [ ] Apparent weight drops to exactly zero at the equator because the centrifugal pseudo-force completely cancels gravity, making all objects weightless at equatorial latitudes
    [*] Your apparent weight is slightly less at the equator because the centrifugal pseudo-force (pointing outward) partially opposes gravity — the effect is about 0.3%
    [ ] Apparent weight is actually greater at the equator than at the poles, because the centrifugal pseudo-force adds to gravity rather than opposing it at lower latitudes
    Explanation: At the equator, centrifugal acceleration = ω²R ≈ 0.034 m/s² outward. This reduces apparent g by about 0.3%. At the poles, there is no centrifugal effect (r = 0).

  Q29. A helicopter drops supplies to a ship. If the ship is at latitude 60°N and the supplies fall for 10 seconds, roughly which direction do they deflect due to the Coriolis effect?
    [*] The supplies deflect to the east — falling objects in the Northern Hemisphere are deflected eastward by the Coriolis effect because they move from a region of lower to higher angular velocity
    [ ] The supplies deflect to the north, because the Coriolis force always pushes falling objects toward the nearest geographic pole in both hemispheres of the rotating Earth
    [ ] There is no measurable deflection of the falling supplies, because the Coriolis effect only acts on objects moving horizontally and has zero effect on vertically falling items
    [ ] The supplies deflect to the west, because falling objects in the Northern Hemisphere always drift westward due to the Coriolis force acting opposite to Earth's rotation
    Explanation: A falling object at 60°N moves from a higher altitude (slower Earth rotation speed) to a lower altitude (faster rotation at the ground). In the Earth's frame, this appears as an eastward deflection.

  Q30. A double-decker bus rounds a curve. Passengers on the upper deck feel more 'pushed outward' than those on the lower deck. Why?
    [ ] Gravity is stronger at greater heights above the road surface, pulling upper-deck passengers outward with more force during the turn than passengers closer to the ground
    [*] The upper deck is farther from the road surface and closer to the center of the curve doesn't explain it. Actually, passengers on the upper deck are at a greater height, and the bus tilts slightly, creating a larger effective pseudo-force displacement. More precisely, at greater height the tangential speed is slightly higher during body sway, amplifying the sensation
    [ ] The upper deck has less friction between the seats and passengers, allowing passengers to slide more easily and therefore feel a stronger sensation of being pushed outward
    [ ] The upper deck passengers experience greater centrifugal pseudo-force because they are farther from the center of the curve, since F = m times omega squared times r increases with larger r and greater height amplifies the tipping
    Explanation: At the upper deck, passengers are farther from the center of the curve (larger effective radius), so the centrifugal pseudo-force (mω²r) is slightly larger. Additionally, the greater height amplifies the tipping sensation.
