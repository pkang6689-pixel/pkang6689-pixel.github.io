--- Lesson 4.1: Work & Energy Transfer (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Work & Energy Transfer
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is work in physics?
Work is defined as W = F × d × cos(θ), where force and displacement are in the same direction.
If a 10 N force moves an object 5 meters, what is the work done?
Work = Force × Distance = 10 N × 5 m = 50 J.
What is energy?
Energy is the ability to perform work or cause change.
A car traveling at 20 m/s has a mass of 1500 kg. What is its kinetic energy?
KE = ½mv² = ½ × 1500 × (20)² = 300,000 J.
If 200 J of work is done on a 5 kg object initially at rest, what is its final velocity?
Using work-energy theorem: W = ½mv² → 200 = ½ × 5 × v² → v² = 80 → v ≈ 8.94 m/s.
A motor does 5000 J of work in 10 seconds. What is its power?
Power = Work / Time = 5000 J / 10 s = 500 W.
A spring is compressed, storing 1000 J of elastic potential energy. If released, what is the maximum speed of a 4 kg object attached to it?
PE = KE → 1000 = ½ × 4 × v² → v² = 500 → v ≈ 22.4 m/s.
An object experiences both an applied force of 50 N and friction of 10 N over 8 meters. What is the net work done?
Net force = 50 - 10 = 40 N. Net work = 40 N × 8 m = 320 J.

--- Lesson 4.2: Kinetic Energy & Potential Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Kinetic Energy & Potential Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for kinetic energy?
Kinetic energy is the energy of motion: KE = ½ × mass × velocity².
What is the formula for gravitational potential energy?
Gravitational potential energy is PE = mass × g × height.
What is the kinetic energy of an object at rest?
An object at rest has zero velocity, so KE = ½m(0)² = 0.
A 3 kg object is moving at 4 m/s. What is its kinetic energy?
KE = ½mv² = ½ × 3 × (4)² = ½ × 3 × 16 = 24 J.
A 5 kg object is at a height of 6 meters above the ground. What is its gravitational potential energy? (g = 10 m/s²)
PE = mgh = 5 × 10 × 6 = 300 J.
An object with a kinetic energy of 100 J has a mass of 2 kg. What is its velocity?
100 = ½ × 2 × v² → 100 = v² → v = 10 m/s.
An object at rest on a cliff 40 meters high has a mass of 2 kg. What is its total mechanical energy relative to the ground? (g = 10 m/s²)
E_total = KE + PE = 0 + 2 × 10 × 40 = 800 J.
A 1.5 kg ball is thrown horizontally at 12 m/s from a 25-meter platform. What is its total mechanical energy at release? (g = 10 m/s²)
E = KE + PE = ½ × 1.5 × (12)² + 1.5 × 10 × 25 = 108 + 375 = 483 J. (Recalculated: 108 + 375 = 483 J, closest is given setup)
A spring with spring constant k = 200 N/m is compressed 0.5 m. What is its elastic potential energy?
PE_elastic = ½kx² = ½ × 200 × (0.5)² = ½ × 200 × 0.25 = 25 J.
A 2 kg object falls freely from rest. After falling 5 meters, what is its kinetic energy? (g = 10 m/s²)
The loss in PE becomes KE: mgh = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 J.
An archer pulls back an arrow (mass 0.1 kg) on a bow, storing 20 J of elastic energy. If the arrow is released horizontally, what is its velocity?
Elastic PE = KE → 20 = ½ × 0.1 × v² → v² = 400 → v = 20 m/s.

--- Lesson 4.3: Conservation of Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conservation of Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the velocity of an object dropped from rest at height h when it reaches the ground? (assuming no air resistance)
Using energy conservation: mgh = ½mv² → v = √(2gh).
In an idealized system with no losses, what is the total energy at any time?
In a conservative system, E_total = KE + PE = constant.
A 2 kg ball is dropped from rest at a height of 10 meters. Ignoring air resistance, what is its velocity just before hitting the ground? (g = 10 m/s²)
Using energy conservation: mgh = ½mv² → 2 × 10 × 10 = ½ × 2 × v² → v = √200 ≈ 14.1 m/s.
A 1 kg object is launched upward with an initial velocity of 30 m/s. What is the maximum height it reaches? (g = 10 m/s²)
Using energy conservation: ½mv² = mgh → ½ × 1 × (30)² = 1 × 10 × h → h = 45 m.
A 3 kg block slides down a frictionless incline from rest, descending 5 meters vertically. What is its speed at the bottom?
Using energy conservation: mgh = ½mv² → 3 × 10 × 5 = ½ × 3 × v² → v = √100 = 10 m/s.
A 2 kg block starts at rest on a table 2 meters high and slides off. Ignoring friction, what is its total mechanical energy? (g = 10 m/s²)
E_total = PE + KE = 2 × 10 × 2 + 0 = 40 J (conserved throughout the fall).
A pendulum swings with a bob of mass 1 kg. At the lowest point, its velocity is 4 m/s. What is the maximum height the bob rises to? (g = 10 m/s²)
At lowest point (KE max) to highest point (PE max): ½mv² = mgh → ½ × 1 × (4)² = 1 × 10 × h → h = 0.8 m.
A coaster car (mass 1000 kg) starts from rest at height 50 m. Ignoring friction, what is its speed at height 20 m?
Energy at start: mgh_1 = ½mv² + mgh_2 → 1000 × 10 × 50 = ½ × 1000 × v² + 1000 × 10 × 20 → v ≈ 24.5 m/s.
A spring stores 60 J of elastic potential energy. A 2 kg object attached to it is released. What is its maximum velocity?
PE_spring = KE_max → 60 = ½ × 2 × v² → v² = 60 → v ≈ 7.75 m/s.
A ball is tossed upward at 12 m/s from a 5-meter-high window. Ignoring air resistance, what is its velocity when it reaches the ground? (g = 10 m/s²)
E = KE_initial + PE_initial = ½ × m × 12² + m × 10 × 5 = ½m × 144 + 50m; at ground: ½mv² = E → v ≈ 15.6 m/s.

  Key Concepts: Power & Efficiency
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for power?
Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.
What is the SI unit for power?
Power is measured in Watts, where 1 W = 1 J/s.
If work is 500 J and it takes 10 seconds, what is the power?
P = W/t = 500 J / 10 s = 50 W.
What is the relationship between power and velocity?
Instantaneous power is the force applied times the velocity: P = Fv (when force is in direction of motion).
What is the formula for efficiency?
Efficiency compares useful output to total input and is expressed as a percentage.
A water pump moves 1000 liters of water 3 meters high in 5 minutes. What is the power output? (g = 10 m/s², 1 liter ≈ 1 kg)
Work = mgh = 1000 × 10 × 3 = 30,000 J. Power = 30,000 / 300 s = 100 W.
An elevator raises 10 people (average mass 70 kg each) 20 meters in 5 seconds. What is the power required? (g = 10 m/s²)
Total mass = 700 kg. Work = 700 × 10 × 20 = 140,000 J. Power = 140,000 / 5 = 28,000 W.
A machine has an input power of 2000 W and an efficiency of 75%. What is the useful output power?
Output = Efficiency × Input = 0.75 × 2000 = 1500 W.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which definition BEST describes 'average power'?
Average power = Total work / Total time.
Example: Which definition BEST describes 'mechanical advantage'?
Mechanical advantage = Output force / Input force; it indicates how much a machine multiplies force.

Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

What is the work-energy theorem?
W_net = ΔKE = KE_final - KE_initial.
What is the mathematical form of the work-energy theorem?
The work-energy theorem states that net work equals the change in kinetic energy.

W = ΔKE → 75 = KE_f - 0 → KE_f = 75 J.

    [ ] 1000 J
    [*] 100 J
    Explanation: W = F × d = 20 × 5 = 100 J.

  Q26. A baseball (mass 0.15 kg) pitched at 40 m/s is caught and stops. How much work was done by the catching force?
    [ ] -60 J
    [ ] 120 J
    [*] -120 J
    [ ] 240 J
    Explanation: W = ΔKE = 0 - ½ × 0.15 × (40)² = -120 J.

  Q27. A 3 kg object starting at rest has 108 J of net work done on it. What is its final velocity?
    [ ] 4 m/s
    [ ] 6 m/s
    [*] √72 ≈ 8.49 m/s
    [ ] 12 m/s
    Explanation: W = ½mv_f² → 108 = ½ × 3 × v_f² → v_f² = 72 → v_f ≈ 8.49 m/s.

  Q28. An object of mass 4 kg changes from 3 m/s to 7 m/s. What is the net work done on it?
    [ ] 40 J
    [*] 80 J
    [ ] 160 J
    [ ] 120 J
    Explanation: W = ½ × 4 × (7² - 3²) = 2 × (49 - 9) = 2 × 40 = 80 J.

  Q29. A force does 500 J of work on a 10 kg object initially at rest. If friction does -100 J of work, what is the final kinetic energy?
    [ ] 500 J
    [ ] 100 J
    [*] 400 J
    [ ] 600 J
    Explanation: Net work = 500 - 100 = 400 J. W_net = ΔKE = 400 J.

--- Lesson 4.6: Energy in Collisions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy in Collisions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between elastic and inelastic collisions?
In elastic collisions, KE is conserved. In inelastic, some KE is lost (to heat, sound, deformation).
What is a perfectly inelastic collision?
In a perfectly inelastic collision, objects merge and move together — maximum KE loss.
What is the coefficient of restitution?
e = relative velocity after / relative velocity before. e = 1 is perfectly elastic; e = 0 is perfectly inelastic.
A 2 kg ball moving at 5 m/s collides with a 3 kg stationary ball. They stick together. What is their final velocity?
p = (2)(5) + (3)(0) = 10 kg·m/s. Final velocity = 10/(2+3) = 2 m/s.
A 1 kg ball at 10 m/s collides with a 9 kg stationary ball. If perfectly inelastic, what is the final velocity?
p = 1 × 10 = 10 kg·m/s. Final v = 10/10 = 1 m/s.
Two equal masses move toward each other at 5 m/s each. If they collide perfectly inelastically, what is their final velocity?
p_total = mv - mv = 0. Since v_f = p_total/(2m) = 0 m/s.
A 5 kg object moving at 4 m/s collides elastically with a 15 kg stationary object. What is the velocity of the 5 kg object after the collision?
Using elastic collision formulas: v_1f = (m_1 - m_2)/(m_1 + m_2) × v_1i = (5-15)/20 × 4 = -2 m/s.
A 3 kg object at 8 m/s and a 2 kg object at 2 m/s move in the same direction and collide perfectly inelastically. What is their final velocity?
p = 3×8 + 2×2 = 24 + 4 = 28 kg·m/s. v_f = 28/5 = 5.6 m/s.
A 1200 kg car moving at 15 m/s rear-ends a 800 kg stationary car. They stick together. What is their combined velocity?
p = 1200 × 15 = 18,000 kg·m/s. v_f = 18,000 / (1200 + 800) = 18,000/2000 = 9 m/s.

    Explanation: In elastic collisions, KE is conserved. In inelastic, some KE is lost (to heat, sound, deformation).

    [*] Yes, in an isolated system momentum is always conserved
    [ ] Only in perfectly inelastic collisions
    [ ] No, only in elastic collisions
    [ ] Only when masses are equal
    Explanation: The law of conservation of momentum applies to all collisions when no external forces act.

  Q3. What is a perfectly inelastic collision?
    [ ] Energy increases during collision
    [*] Objects stick together after collision (maximum kinetic energy lost)
    [ ] Objects bounce off each other perfectly
    [ ] Both objects disappear
    Explanation: In a perfectly inelastic collision, objects merge and move together — maximum KE loss.

  Q4. In an elastic collision between equal masses where one is initially stationary, what happens?
    [*] The moving object stops and the stationary one moves at the original speed
    [ ] Both objects move together
    [ ] Both objects continue in the same direction
    [ ] Both objects stop
    Explanation: In a perfectly elastic collision between equal masses, momentum and KE transfer completely.

  Q5. In a collision, is kinetic energy always conserved?
    [ ] Only when masses are equal
    [ ] Yes, always
    [ ] Only in perfectly inelastic collisions
    [*] No, only in elastic collisions
    Explanation: KE is conserved only in elastic collisions. Inelastic collisions lose KE to other forms of energy.

  Q6. What happens to the lost kinetic energy in an inelastic collision?
    [ ] It stays as kinetic energy
    [ ] It increases as potential energy
    [ ] It disappears permanently
    [*] It converts to heat, sound, or deformation energy
    Explanation: Energy conservation means the 'lost' KE transforms to other forms: internal energy, sound, etc.

  Q7. What is the coefficient of restitution?
    [ ] The ratio of kinetic energy before and after
    [*] A ratio measuring the elasticity of a collision (0 = perfectly inelastic, 1 = perfectly elastic)
    [ ] A measure of friction in a collision
    [ ] The ratio of final to initial mass
    Explanation: e = relative velocity after / relative velocity before. e = 1 is perfectly elastic; e = 0 is perfectly inelastic.

  Q8. Can an object gain kinetic energy from a collision?
    [*] Yes, if another object does positive work on it
    [ ] Only in perfectly inelastic collisions
    [ ] No, collisions only remove kinetic energy
    [ ] Only if the objects have equal mass
    Explanation: In elastic and some inelastic collisions, one object can gain KE from another.

  Q9. What distinguishes a 'super-elastic' (explosive) collision from an elastic collision?
    [*] Kinetic energy increases (e.g., from stored chemical or spring energy)
    [ ] Momentum is not conserved

    Explanation: Explosive collisions release stored energy, increasing total KE above the initial KE.

  Q10. In a two-body elastic collision, what two quantities are always conserved?
    [ ] Velocity and acceleration
    [ ] Mass and velocity
    [ ] Force and distance
    [*] Momentum and kinetic energy
    Explanation: In elastic collisions, both p = mv (momentum) and KE = ½mv² are conserved.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes 'impulse'?

    [*] The product of force and the time duration it acts (J = FΔt = Δp)

    [ ] Momentum is always zero

    [*] The total momentum of an isolated system remains constant

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes 'center of mass velocity'?
