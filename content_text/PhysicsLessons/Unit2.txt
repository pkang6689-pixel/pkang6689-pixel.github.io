--- Lesson 2.1: Distance, Displacement, Speed, Velocity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Distance, Displacement, Speed, Velocity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between distance and displacement?
Distance measures the total length of the path traveled (scalar, always positive). Displacement measures the straight-line distance from start to finish with direction (vector, can be positive, negative, or zero).
What is average speed?
Average speed = total distance / total time. It is a scalar quantity and is always positive.
What is average velocity?
Average velocity = Δx / Δt (displacement / time). It is a vector quantity and can be positive, negative, or zero.
A car travels 100 km north then 100 km south. What is its distance and displacement?
Distance is the total path: 100 + 100 = 200 km. Displacement is the net change in position: 100 km north − 100 km south = 0 km (back to start).
What is instantaneous speed?
Instantaneous speed is the rate of distance change at a particular instant. It corresponds to the magnitude of the slope of a position-time graph at that point.
What is the SI unit of speed?
The SI unit of speed (and velocity) is meters per second (m/s). Other units like km/h can be converted: divide by 3.6 to convert km/h to m/s.
A jogger runs 5 km in 25 minutes. What is their average speed in km/h?
Average speed = distance / time = 5 km / (25/60 h) = 5 / 0.417 = 12 km/h.
An object moves 30 m east in 5 s. What is its velocity?
Velocity = displacement / time = 30 m east / 5 s = 6 m/s east. Since velocity is a vector, the direction must be included.
Two trains leave the same station. Train A travels north at 80 km/h and Train B travels south at 60 km/h. After 2 hours, what is the distance between them and why does this involve vectors?
Train A displacement: 160 km north. Train B displacement: 120 km south. Since they move in opposite directions, the separation = 160 + 120 = 280 km. Vector addition: 160 north − (−120 south) = 160 + 120 = 280 km.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is average speed?
Average speed = total distance / total time. It is a scalar quantity and is always positive.
Example: What is average velocity?
Average velocity = Δx / Δt (displacement / time). It is a vector quantity and can be positive, negative, or zero.
Example: A jogger runs 5 km in 25 minutes. What is their average speed in km/h?
Average speed = distance / time = 5 km / (25/60 h) = 5 / 0.417 = 12 km/h.
Example: A delivery driver drives 30 km in 30 minutes, stops for 15 minutes for a delivery, then drives 30 km back in 30 minutes. Calculate the average speed and average velocity for the entire trip.
Total distance = 30 + 30 = 60 km. Total time = 30 + 15 + 30 = 75 min = 1.25 h. Average speed = 60/1.25 = 48 km/h. Displacement = 0 (returned to start), so average velocity = 0/1.25 = 0.
Example: A sprinter runs 100 m in 10.0 s. Her coach says her average speed was 10 m/s but her instantaneous speed at the 50 m mark was 11.5 m/s. How is this possible?
Average speed = total distance / total time = 100/10 = 10 m/s. But she started from rest and accelerated, so she was slower at the start and faster later. At the 50 m mark, her instantaneous speed exceeded the average. Instantaneous ≠ average unless speed is constant.
Example: A race car on a 5 km circular track completes exactly 3 laps in 6 minutes. What are its average speed and average velocity?
Distance = 3 × 5 = 15 km. Time = 6 min = 0.1 h. Average speed = 15/0.1 = 150 km/h. After 3 complete laps, the car is back at the starting line, so displacement = 0 and average velocity = 0.

    [*] Meters per second (m/s)
    [ ] Kilometers per hour (km/h)
    [ ] Miles per hour (mph)
    [ ] Feet per second (ft/s)
    Explanation: The SI unit of speed (and velocity) is meters per second (m/s). Other units like km/h can be converted: divide by 3.6 to convert km/h to m/s.

  Q8. A jogger runs 5 km in 25 minutes. What is their average speed in km/h?
    [*] 12 km/h
    [ ] 5 km/h
    [ ] 0.2 km/h

  Q9. What does the slope of a position-time graph represent?
    [ ] Acceleration
    [ ] Distance
    [*] Velocity
    [ ] Force
    Explanation: The slope of a position-time graph gives velocity. A steeper slope means higher velocity; a horizontal line means zero velocity (stationary). A negative slope indicates motion in the negative direction.

  Q10. An object moves 30 m east in 5 s. What is its velocity?
    [ ] 150 m/s east
    [ ] 25 m/s east
    [*] 6 m/s east
    [ ] 6 m/s (direction unknown)
    Explanation: Velocity = displacement / time = 30 m east / 5 s = 6 m/s east. Since velocity is a vector, the direction must be included.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes distance?
    [ ] The straight-line separation between two points
    [ ] Displacement measured without direction
    [*] The total length of the path traveled by an object, regardless of direction — a scalar quantity
    [ ] The area under a velocity-time graph
    Explanation: Distance is scalar (no direction) and equals the sum of all path segments. It is always positive or zero and can never decrease during motion.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes displacement?
    [ ] The total length of the path an object travels during its motion, measured along every twist and turn — a scalar quantity
    [*] The change in position of an object measured as a straight line from initial to final position, with direction — a vector quantity
    [ ] The rate at which an object changes its location over a given time interval, expressed in meters per second — a kinematic rate
    [ ] The product of an object's average speed and the elapsed travel time, yielding the total ground covered — measured in meters
    Explanation: Displacement = Δx = x_final − x_initial. It has magnitude (shortest distance between start and end) and direction. It can be positive, negative, or zero.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes speed?
    [*] The rate at which an object covers distance — a scalar quantity equal to distance divided by time
    [ ] The magnitude of displacement per unit time — a vector quantity that includes the direction of travel along the path
    [ ] The product of distance and the time elapsed during motion, measured in meter-seconds as a compound kinematic unit
    [ ] The instantaneous rate of change of displacement, accounting for both the magnitude and the direction of the motion
    Explanation: Speed = distance/time. It is always positive or zero, has no direction, and tells how fast (but not in what direction) an object is moving.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes velocity?
    [ ] Acceleration multiplied by time
    [ ] Speed with an arbitrary direction assigned
    [ ] The rate at which distance is covered
    [*] The rate of change of displacement with respect to time — a vector quantity with both magnitude and direction
    Explanation: Velocity = Δx/Δt. As a vector, it includes direction. Positive and negative signs indicate opposite directions along a chosen axis.

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes instantaneous velocity?
    [*] The velocity of an object at a single specific moment in time, equal to the derivative of position with respect to time
    [ ] The peak velocity an object reaches during its entire journey, representing the single fastest point along its motion path
    [ ] The total displacement divided by the total elapsed time of travel, giving the overall rate of position change for the trip
    [ ] The reading obtained exclusively from a calibrated speedometer device, which only measures how fast the vehicle is currently going
    Explanation: Instantaneous velocity v = dx/dt, the limit of Δx/Δt as Δt → 0. It is the slope of the tangent to the position-time curve at any given instant.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes uniform motion?
    [*] Motion at constant velocity — traveling in a straight line at an unchanging speed with no acceleration
    [ ] Motion in a circular path at constant speed
    [ ] Motion that gradually speeds up
    [ ] Any motion along a straight line
    Explanation: Uniform motion means constant velocity: zero acceleration, constant speed, and constant direction. On a position-time graph, it appears as a straight line.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes a reference frame?
    [*] A coordinate system used to define positions and describe the motion of objects relative to an observer
    [ ] The fixed physical boundary that separates one region of measurable space from another, often established by a visible landmark
    [ ] The designated initial position from which all timed experiments begin, serving as the universal agreed-upon zero point
    [ ] The downward vertical direction established by the local gravitational field, which determines how all free objects accelerate
    Explanation: A reference frame provides the axes and origin from which positions, velocities, and accelerations are measured. Motion is always described relative to a chosen reference frame.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes a position-time graph?
    [ ] A graph showing the distance between two objects over time
    [ ] A map showing where an object is located
    [ ] A graph with velocity on the y-axis and time on the x-axis
    [*] A graph that plots an object's position on the vertical axis against time on the horizontal axis, where the slope represents velocity
    Explanation: Position-time graphs encode motion information: slope = velocity, horizontal line = stationary, curved line = changing velocity. They are fundamental tools in kinematics.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes the odometer vs. speedometer distinction?
    [ ] An odometer measures displacement while a speedometer measures velocity
    [*] An odometer measures total distance traveled (scalar), while a speedometer measures instantaneous speed (scalar)
    [ ] Both measure the same quantity in different units
    [ ] An odometer measures average speed while a speedometer measures maximum speed
    Explanation: The odometer accumulates total path length (distance — always increasing). The speedometer shows how fast the car is going right now (instantaneous speed). Neither provides directional information.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes relative velocity?
    [ ] The total velocity of all objects in a system
    [ ] The maximum velocity difference between two objects
    [*] The velocity of one object as observed from the reference frame of another moving object
    [ ] The average of two objects' velocities
    Explanation: Relative velocity v_A relative to B = v_A − v_B. If two cars move in the same direction at 60 km/h and 40 km/h, the relative velocity of the first from the second's frame is 20 km/h.

  Q21. A student walks 4 blocks north to school, then 3 blocks east to the library after school. Her pedometer (step counter) shows she walked 700 m total. What are her distance and displacement from home to the library?

    [ ] She must have been running downhill at the 50 m mark which artificially raised her instantaneous speed beyond the average — on flat ground they would be equal values
    [ ] The coach made a measurement error because instantaneous speed at any point during a uniform-distance run must exactly equal the average speed over the whole race

  Q26. A car's speedometer reads 90 km/h due north. At that instant, the driver turns onto an eastbound highway and maintains 90 km/h. Has the velocity changed?
    [ ] No — speedometers measure velocity directly
    [ ] Yes — the speed increased because east is a 'faster' direction

    [ ] No — the speed stayed the same so the velocity is unchanged

  Q30. Two ships leave a port simultaneously. Ship A sails 100 km east, Ship B sails 100 km north. Both take 5 hours. They have the same speed but a sailor says they have different velocities. Explain.

    [ ] The velocities differ only because the ships have different masses and hull designs — a heavier ship would have lower velocity even when traveling at the same measured speed
    Explanation: Speed (scalar) = 100 km / 5 h = 20 km/h for both. Velocity (vector) = 20 km/h east for A, 20 km/h north for B. Since velocity includes direction, these are different velocity vectors despite identical speeds.

--- Lesson 2.2: Acceleration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acceleration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is acceleration?
Acceleration a = Δv/Δt, measuring how quickly velocity changes. Its SI unit is m/s².
What is the SI unit of acceleration?
Acceleration has units of velocity divided by time: (m/s)/s = m/s².
If a car increases its velocity from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 seconds, what is its acceleration?
a = Δv/Δt = (20 − 0)/5 = 4 m/s².
What is deceleration?
Deceleration occurs when the acceleration vector opposes the velocity vector, reducing speed. It is not simply 'negative acceleration' — the sign depends on the chosen coordinate system.
An object has a positive velocity and a negative acceleration. What is happening?
When acceleration is opposite to velocity, the speed decreases. A positive velocity with negative acceleration means the object moves in the positive direction but is decelerating.
A car accelerates from rest at 3 m/s² for 10 seconds. How far does it travel and what is its final speed?
v = u + at = 0 + 3(10) = 30 m/s. s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(3)(10²) = 150 m. The car reaches 30 m/s (108 km/h) having traveled 150 m.
A rocket accelerates upward at 20 m/s². If the rocket starts from rest, what is its velocity after 5 seconds?
v = u + at = 0 + 20 × 5 = 100 m/s upward. The rocket gains 20 m/s of velocity every second it accelerates.
A train's velocity-time graph shows a straight line from (0, 0) to (10, 20). What is the train's acceleration and displacement during this interval?
Slope = Δv/Δt = 20/10 = 2 m/s². Area = ½ × base × height = ½ × 10 × 20 = 100 m. This confirms s = ½at² = ½(2)(100) = 100 m.
A elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s² for 3 seconds, then moves at constant velocity for 5 seconds, then decelerates at 2 m/s² for 3 seconds. What is the total displacement?
Phase 1: v = 0 + 2(3) = 6 m/s. s₁ = ½(2)(3²) = 9 m. Phase 2: constant 6 m/s for 5 s. s₂ = 30 m. Phase 3: s₃ = 6(3) − ½(2)(3²) = 18 − 9 = 9 m. Total: 9 + 30 + 9 = 48 m.
An athlete practices sprints. Run 1: 0 to 8 m/s in 2 seconds. Run 2: 0 to 8 m/s in 4 seconds. Both reach the same final speed. What is different?
Run 1: a = 8/2 = 4 m/s², s = ½(4)(4) = 8 m. Run 2: a = 8/4 = 2 m/s², s = ½(2)(16) = 16 m. Despite the same final speed, the acceleration differs and Run 2 covers more distance because it takes longer to reach the same speed.

--- Lesson 2.2: Acceleration (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is acceleration?
    [ ] The speed of an object at one instant
    [ ] The rate of change of distance
    [*] The rate of change of velocity with respect to time
    [ ] The total displacement of an object
    Explanation: Acceleration a = Δv/Δt, measuring how quickly velocity changes. Its SI unit is m/s².

  Q2. What is the SI unit of acceleration?
    [ ] Newtons (N)
    [*] Meters per second squared (m/s²)
    [ ] Meters per second (m/s)
    [ ] Kilometers per hour (km/h)
    Explanation: Acceleration has units of velocity divided by time: (m/s)/s = m/s².

  Q3. If a car increases its velocity from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 seconds, what is its acceleration?
    [ ] 100 m/s²
    [*] 4 m/s²
    [ ] 20 m/s²
    [ ] 0.25 m/s²
    Explanation: a = Δv/Δt = (20 − 0)/5 = 4 m/s².

  Q4. Can an object accelerate while maintaining constant speed?
    [ ] Only if the object has zero mass, because massless particles are the sole exception to the rule that speed must change for acceleration
    [ ] Only in outer space where there is no gravity, since gravitational fields prevent acceleration at constant speed under normal conditions
    [ ] No — maintaining a constant speed always guarantees the velocity is also constant, so no acceleration can occur in that situation
    [*] Yes — if the direction of motion changes (e.g., circular motion), velocity changes even at constant speed
    Explanation: Acceleration is a change in velocity (a vector). Since velocity has both magnitude and direction, changing direction at constant speed still constitutes acceleration.

  Q5. What is deceleration?
    [*] Acceleration in the opposite direction to the velocity, causing the object to slow down
    [ ] Any acceleration that carries a negative sign in the chosen coordinate system, regardless of whether the object speeds up or slows down
    [ ] A state of zero acceleration where the object maintains a perfectly constant velocity with no change in speed or direction at all
    [ ] Acceleration that always equals exactly negative 9.8 m/s squared, since that is the only value at which objects can decelerate on Earth
    Explanation: Deceleration occurs when the acceleration vector opposes the velocity vector, reducing speed. It is not simply 'negative acceleration' — the sign depends on the chosen coordinate system.

  Q6. An object has a positive velocity and a negative acceleration. What is happening?
    [ ] The object is moving backward
    [ ] The object is speeding up in the negative direction
    [ ] The object is stationary
    [*] The object is slowing down — the acceleration opposes the direction of motion
    Explanation: When acceleration is opposite to velocity, the speed decreases. A positive velocity with negative acceleration means the object moves in the positive direction but is decelerating.

  Q7. What does the slope of a velocity-time graph represent?
    [*] Acceleration
    [ ] Displacement
    [ ] Velocity
    [ ] Speed
    Explanation: On a v-t graph, slope = Δv/Δt = acceleration. A steeper slope means greater acceleration. A horizontal line means constant velocity (zero acceleration).

  Q8. What does a horizontal line on a velocity-time graph mean?
    [ ] The object is stationary
    [ ] The object is accelerating at a constant rate
    [ ] The object is decelerating
    [*] The object is moving at constant velocity (zero acceleration)
    Explanation: A horizontal line means velocity is not changing with time, so acceleration is zero. The object moves at constant velocity (uniform motion).

  Q9. What does the area under a velocity-time graph represent?
    [ ] Force
    [ ] Acceleration
    [*] Displacement
    [ ] Speed
    Explanation: The area under a v-t graph equals displacement: ∫v dt = Δx. This works for any shape of v-t graph.

  Q10. An object has zero velocity but nonzero acceleration. Is this possible?
    [ ] Only if the object has infinite mass, because extremely massive objects can maintain zero velocity while forces still produce acceleration
    [*] Yes — for example, a ball thrown upward has zero velocity at its highest point but still has gravitational acceleration downward
    [ ] No — whenever an object has zero velocity it must also have zero acceleration, since both quantities always equal zero simultaneously
    [ ] Only in a perfect vacuum where air resistance is absent, since air molecules prevent acceleration from existing without velocity present
    Explanation: At the peak of a vertical throw, v = 0 momentarily but a = −g = −9.8 m/s² (gravity never stops). This is why the ball immediately begins falling back down.

  Q11. Which definition BEST describes acceleration?
    [ ] The total change in position of an object
    [ ] How fast an object is moving at one instant
    [*] The rate at which velocity changes with respect to time — a vector quantity with SI unit m/s²
    [ ] The rate at which distance changes with time
    Explanation: a = Δv/Δt (or dv/dt). Acceleration is a vector, having both magnitude and direction. It tells us how quickly and in what direction the velocity is changing.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes constant acceleration?
    [ ] Any acceleration value that is positive in sign, since only positive accelerations can remain constant while negative ones always fluctuate
    [ ] Acceleration whose magnitude steadily increases over time, growing larger and larger with each passing second as the object gains speed
    [ ] Acceleration that is always equal to exactly 9.8 m/s squared, because gravitational acceleration is the only type that remains constant
    [*] Acceleration that has the same magnitude and direction at every instant, producing a uniformly changing velocity
    Explanation: Under constant acceleration, velocity changes by the same amount each second. This produces a straight line on a v-t graph and a parabola on a position-time graph.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes instantaneous acceleration?
    [ ] The maximum acceleration experienced
    [*] The acceleration of an object at a specific instant, equal to the derivative of velocity with respect to time (dv/dt)
    [ ] Acceleration measured with a stopwatch
    [ ] The average acceleration over the entire trip
    Explanation: Instantaneous acceleration is the limit of Δv/Δt as Δt → 0. It equals the slope of the velocity-time graph at that instant and can differ from the average acceleration.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes free fall?
    [*] Motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone, with no air resistance, where all objects accelerate at g ≈ 9.8 m/s² regardless of mass
    [ ] The descent of an object from a very high altitude through the atmosphere, where air resistance plays a significant role in its motion
    [ ] Motion of an object through space with absolutely no forces acting on it at all, including the complete absence of gravitational attraction
    [ ] Any motion in which an object moves in the downward direction, whether it is powered, thrown, dropped, or sliding along an inclined plane
    Explanation: Free fall occurs when gravity is the only force. All objects — regardless of mass, shape, or composition — experience the same acceleration g ≈ 9.8 m/s² downward (ignoring air resistance).

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes a velocity-time graph?
    [*] A graph plotting velocity on the vertical axis against time on the horizontal axis, where slope equals acceleration and area equals displacement
    [ ] A graph that only applies to situations with constant velocity, since changing velocities cannot be accurately represented on such a plot
    [ ] A diagram showing the direct mathematical relationship between the distance an object covers and its instantaneous speed at every point
    [ ] A graph that plots an object's position on the vertical axis against time on the horizontal axis, showing how location changes with time
    Explanation: V-t graphs encode motion completely: slope gives acceleration, area under the curve gives displacement, and horizontal intercepts show when the object reverses direction.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes uniform acceleration?
    [ ] Motion at a constant speed
    [ ] Motion with continuously increasing acceleration
    [ ] Motion in a straight line at any speed
    [*] Motion in which velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals — the acceleration remains constant
    Explanation: Uniform acceleration means a = constant. Velocity increases (or decreases) linearly with time: v = v₀ + at. This is the simplest type of accelerated motion and is described by the SUVAT equations.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes non-uniform acceleration?
    [*] Acceleration that changes in magnitude and/or direction over time, making velocity change at a varying rate
    [ ] Acceleration whose value is always negative throughout the entire motion, never reaching zero or becoming positive at any point in time
    [ ] An extremely large but constant acceleration that stays the same at every instant but simply has a very high numerical magnitude value
    [ ] Acceleration that can only exist during curved motion along a circular or elliptical path, and never occurs during straight-line travel
    Explanation: Non-uniform acceleration means a ≠ constant. Examples include a car in traffic (varying throttle) and SHM (acceleration proportional to displacement). Calculus (derivatives/integrals) is needed to analyze such motion.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes the acceleration due to gravity (g)?
    [ ] The maximum speed limit that any falling object can reach, beyond which no further increase in downward velocity is physically possible
    [*] The acceleration experienced by objects in free fall near Earth's surface, approximately 9.8 m/s² directed downward
    [ ] The gravitational force that Earth exerts on an object, measured in newtons and directly dependent on the mass of the object in question
    [ ] The weight of an object divided by its total volume, giving a density-like quantity that determines how quickly the object accelerates
    Explanation: g ≈ 9.81 m/s² at Earth's surface. It varies slightly with altitude and latitude. On the Moon, g ≈ 1.6 m/s². It is the same for all objects regardless of mass (in the absence of air resistance).

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes jerk?
    [ ] The maximum acceleration that any mechanical system can achieve, representing the upper bound on how quickly velocity can ever change
    [ ] A sudden and complete stop in an object's motion, where velocity drops instantly to zero without any intermediate deceleration phase
    [ ] An error or uncertainty introduced during the measurement of acceleration, caused by imprecise instruments or incorrect timing methods
    [*] The rate of change of acceleration with respect to time (da/dt), with SI units of m/s³
    Explanation: Jerk = da/dt = d³x/dt³. It describes how smoothly acceleration changes. High jerk means abrupt changes in acceleration, which feels uncomfortable in vehicles and can damage machinery.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes terminal velocity?
    [*] The constant maximum velocity reached by a falling object when air resistance equals gravitational force, resulting in zero net force and zero acceleration
    [ ] The fastest speed theoretically possible in the universe, representing an absolute upper limit that no material object can ever exceed
    [ ] The final velocity an object has at the exact moment of impact with the ground, determined solely by the height from which it was dropped
    [ ] The minimum launch velocity required for an object to escape Earth's atmosphere and enter outer space by overcoming gravitational pull
    Explanation: Terminal velocity occurs when drag force balances weight: mg = F_drag. At this point a = 0 and velocity remains constant. A skydiver's terminal velocity is about 55 m/s (200 km/h) in a spread position.

  Q21. A car accelerates from rest at 3 m/s² for 10 seconds. How far does it travel and what is its final speed?
    [ ] Final speed = 30 m/s, distance = 300 m
    [ ] Final speed = 30 m/s, distance = 30 m
    [ ] Final speed = 10 m/s, distance = 150 m

    Explanation: v = u + at = 0 + 3(10) = 30 m/s. s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(3)(10²) = 150 m. The car reaches 30 m/s (108 km/h) having traveled 150 m.

  Q22. A ball is thrown straight up at 20 m/s. Ignoring air resistance, how high does it go before momentarily stopping?
    [ ] Approximately 40 m — using the simplified formula s = v times t with an estimated flight time of about 2 seconds gives 40 meters
    [*] Approximately 20.4 m — using v² = u² + 2as: 0 = 20² + 2(−9.8)s, s = 400/19.6 ≈ 20.4 m
    [ ] Approximately 200 m — using s = u squared divided by g without the factor of two gives 400 divided by 2 equals 200 meters height
    [ ] Approximately 10 m — using the formula s = u times t squared with t = 1 second gives half the initial velocity as the peak height
    Explanation: At the peak v = 0. Using v² = u² + 2as with a = −9.8 m/s²: 0 = 400 − 19.6s → s = 400/19.6 ≈ 20.4 m. Gravity decelerates the ball at 9.8 m/s² until it stops.

  Q23. A bicycle goes from 5 m/s to 15 m/s in 4 seconds. Then from 15 m/s to 35 m/s in 4 seconds. Is the acceleration constant?
    [ ] Cannot determine without knowing the position data, because calculating acceleration requires distance measurements not velocity and time

    [ ] Yes the acceleration is constant — since both time intervals are exactly 4 seconds long, the rate of velocity change must be identical
    [*] No — the first interval has a = (15−5)/4 = 2.5 m/s² and the second has a = (35−15)/4 = 5 m/s², showing non-uniform acceleration
    Explanation: First interval: a₁ = 10/4 = 2.5 m/s². Second interval: a₂ = 20/4 = 5.0 m/s². Since 2.5 ≠ 5.0, the acceleration is not constant — it increased, indicating non-uniform acceleration.

  Q24. Two balls are dropped from the same height. Ball A has mass 1 kg and Ball B has mass 5 kg. Ignoring air resistance, which hits the ground first?
    [*] They hit at the same time — in free fall, all objects accelerate at g regardless of mass
    [ ] It depends on the height
    [ ] Ball B hits first because it is heavier
    [ ] Ball A hits first because it is lighter
    Explanation: Galileo's principle: in the absence of air resistance, all objects fall with the same acceleration g ≈ 9.8 m/s². Mass does not affect free-fall acceleration. Both balls experience identical motion.

  Q25. A car traveling at 30 m/s applies brakes and decelerates at 5 m/s². How far does it travel before stopping?
    [ ] 150 m — using the formula s = u times t with stopping time t = 30/5 = 6 seconds: s = 30 times 6 = 180, adjusted for braking gives 150 m
    [ ] 6 m — dividing the initial velocity by the deceleration gives the stopping time of 6 seconds, which also equals the stopping distance in meters
    [*] 90 m — using v² = u² + 2as: 0 = 900 + 2(−5)s → s = 900/10 = 90 m
    [ ] 45 m — using the formula s = half times u times t with t = 3 seconds: s = 0.5 times 30 times 3 = 45 m of total braking distance traveled
    Explanation: v² = u² + 2as. With v = 0, u = 30, a = −5: 0 = 900 − 10s → s = 90 m. Alternatively, average velocity = (30 + 0)/2 = 15 m/s, time = 30/5 = 6 s, distance = 15 × 6 = 90 m.

  Q26. A rocket accelerates upward at 20 m/s². If the rocket starts from rest, what is its velocity after 5 seconds?
    [ ] 4 m/s upward — dividing the acceleration by the elapsed time gives 20 divided by 5 = 4 m/s as the final upward velocity of the rocket
    [ ] 100 m/s downward — the magnitude is 100 m/s but directed downward because gravity opposes the upward thrust during the launch phase
    [ ] 25 m/s upward — adding the acceleration and the time together gives 20 + 5 = 25 m/s as the approximate final velocity of the rocket
    [*] 100 m/s upward — v = u + at = 0 + 20(5) = 100 m/s
    Explanation: v = u + at = 0 + 20 × 5 = 100 m/s upward. The rocket gains 20 m/s of velocity every second it accelerates.

  Q27. A skydiver jumps from a plane and initially accelerates at 9.8 m/s². After some time, she reaches a terminal velocity of 55 m/s. What happened to her acceleration?
    [ ] She experienced negative acceleration throughout the entire fall, decelerating from the moment she left the plane all the way until reaching the ground below

  Q28. A train's velocity-time graph shows a straight line from (0, 0) to (10, 20). What is the train's acceleration and displacement during this interval?

--- Lesson 2.3: Graphical Analysis of Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphical Analysis of Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A student has a position-time graph showing a straight line from (0 s, 0 m) to (5 s, 20 m). What is the velocity?
Velocity = slope of the x-t graph = Δx/Δt = 20 m / 5 s = 4 m/s. The constant slope indicates uniform velocity.
A velocity-time graph shows a horizontal line at v = 8 m/s from t = 0 to t = 6 s. What is the displacement?
Displacement = area under v-t graph = rectangle area = v × t = 8 m/s × 6 s = 48 m.
A velocity-time graph shows a straight line from (0 s, 0 m/s) to (4 s, 12 m/s). What is the acceleration and displacement?
Acceleration = slope = 12/4 = 3 m/s². Displacement = area of triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × 4 × 12 = 24 m.
A car's velocity-time graph shows v = 10 m/s from t = 0 to t = 3 s, then v increases linearly from 10 m/s to 25 m/s between t = 3 s and t = 8 s. What is the total displacement?
Phase 1 (rectangle): 10 × 3 = 30 m. Phase 2 (trapezoid): ½(10 + 25) × 5 = 87.5 m. Total = 30 + 87.5 = 117.5 m.
A velocity-time graph shows a line going from +10 m/s at t = 0 to −10 m/s at t = 4 s. At what time does the object momentarily stop, and what is the total displacement?
The line crosses v = 0 at t = 2 s (by symmetry or linear interpolation). Positive area (0 to 2 s): ½ × 2 × 10 = 10 m. Negative area (2 to 4 s): ½ × 2 × (−10) = −10 m. Net displacement = 10 + (−10) = 0 m.
A physics teacher draws an acceleration-time graph showing a constant value of 3 m/s² from t = 0 to t = 6 s. If the initial velocity is 2 m/s, what is the final velocity?
Area under a-t graph = Δv. Area = 3 m/s² × 6 s = 18 m/s. Final velocity = v₀ + Δv = 2 + 18 = 20 m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which definition BEST describes average velocity?
Average velocity = Δx/Δt. On a position-time graph, it equals the slope of the straight line (secant) connecting the initial and final points.

--- Lesson 2.4: Equations of Motion (constant acceleration) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Equations of Motion (constant acceleration)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
An object starts from rest and accelerates at 4 m/s² for 3 seconds. What is its final velocity?
Using v = u + at with u = 0: v = 0 + 4(3) = 12 m/s.
A car traveling at 20 m/s brakes and stops in 4 seconds. What is the acceleration?
Using v = u + at: 0 = 20 + a(4) → a = −20/4 = −5 m/s². The negative sign indicates deceleration.
An object has initial velocity 10 m/s and accelerates at 3 m/s² for 4 seconds. What is the displacement?
s = ut + ½at² = 10(4) + ½(3)(4²) = 40 + 24 = 64 m.
A car is traveling at 25 m/s and brakes uniformly to a stop over a distance of 62.5 m. What is the deceleration?
v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 625 + 125a → a = −625/125 = −5 m/s². The negative sign correctly indicates deceleration.
A motorcycle accelerates from 10 m/s to 30 m/s over a distance of 200 m. What is the acceleration?
v² = u² + 2as: 30² = 10² + 2a(200) → 900 = 100 + 400a → a = 800/400 = 2 m/s².
A rocket accelerates at 15 m/s² from rest for 10 seconds, then its engines cut out and it decelerates at 10 m/s² until it stops. What is the total distance traveled?
Phase 1: v₁ = 0 + 15(10) = 150 m/s; s₁ = ½(15)(10²) = 750 m. Phase 2: s₂ = (0 − 22500)/(−20) = 1125 m. Total = 750 + 1125 = 1875 m.
An elevator starts from rest, accelerates upward at 1.5 m/s² for 4 s, moves at constant velocity for 8 s, then decelerates at 1.5 m/s² for 4 s. What is the total distance?
Phase 1: v = 1.5(4) = 6 m/s, s₁ = ½(1.5)(16) = 12 m. Phase 2: s₂ = 6(8) = 48 m. Phase 3: s₃ = 6(4) − ½(1.5)(16) = 24 − 12 = 12 m. Total = 72 m.

--- Lesson 2.5: Free Fall & Projectile Motion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Free Fall & Projectile Motion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the acceleration due to gravity near Earth's surface?
Near Earth's surface, all objects in free fall accelerate at g ≈ 9.8 m/s² directed downward, regardless of their mass (ignoring air resistance).
What is the vertical acceleration of a projectile at the top of its trajectory?
At the peak, the vertical velocity is zero, but the acceleration is still g = 9.8 m/s² downward. Gravity acts continuously throughout the entire trajectory.
In projectile motion, what is the horizontal acceleration (ignoring air resistance)?
In ideal projectile motion, the only force is gravity (vertical). With no horizontal force, horizontal acceleration is zero, and horizontal velocity remains constant.
A ball is dropped from rest. After 2 seconds, what is its velocity? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
v = u + gt = 0 + 10(2) = 20 m/s downward.
A ball is thrown straight up. What is its velocity at the highest point?
At the peak of vertical motion, the ball's velocity is instantaneously zero. It then begins falling back down under gravity.
A cannonball is fired at 50 m/s at an angle of 30° above horizontal. What is its horizontal range? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
R = u²sin(2θ)/g = 50² × sin(60°)/10 = 2500 × 0.866/10 ≈ 216.5 m.
A soccer ball is kicked at 20 m/s at 45° on level ground. What is the maximum height and total time of flight? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
v_y = 20sin45° = 10√2 ≈ 14.14 m/s. H = v_y²/(2g) = 200/20 = 10 m. T = 2v_y/g = 2(14.14)/10 ≈ 2.83 s.
Real-World Applications
Example: A package is dropped from a plane flying horizontally at 100 m/s at an altitude of 500 m. Describe the path of the package as seen from the ground.
The package retains the plane's horizontal velocity (100 m/s) and gains vertical velocity from gravity. The combination of constant horizontal v and accelerating vertical v produces a parabolic trajectory.

--- Lesson 2.6: Relative Motion & Reference Frames (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Relative Motion & Reference Frames
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a reference frame?
A reference frame is a coordinate system (with an origin and axes) relative to which we measure positions, velocities, and accelerations of objects.
If you are sitting on a moving train, what is your velocity relative to the train?
In the reference frame of the train, you are not moving — your velocity relative to the train is zero. You are at rest in that frame.
A car moves east at 60 km/h and a truck moves east at 40 km/h. What is the velocity of the car relative to the truck?
v_car relative to truck = v_car − v_truck = 60 − 40 = 20 km/h east. From the truck's perspective, the car moves ahead at 20 km/h.
Two cars approach each other, each moving at 50 km/h. What is their relative velocity?
When objects move toward each other, relative speed = sum of speeds. If car A moves east at 50 and car B west at 50, v_A relative to B = 50 − (−50) = 100 km/h.
A person walks at 1 m/s on a conveyor belt moving at 2 m/s in the same direction. What is their speed relative to the ground?
When moving in the same direction, velocities add: v_person/ground = v_person/belt + v_belt/ground = 1 + 2 = 3 m/s.
What is an inertial reference frame?
An inertial reference frame is one in which Newton's first law holds — objects at rest stay at rest and moving objects maintain constant velocity unless acted upon by a force. It is non-accelerating.
A boat crosses a river at 5 m/s relative to the water. The river flows at 3 m/s. What is the boat's speed relative to the ground?
Since the boat moves perpendicular to the river flow, the velocities add as vectors: v = √(5² + 3²) = √34 ≈ 5.83 m/s.
If you throw a ball forward at 10 m/s while riding in a car moving at 20 m/s, what is the ball's speed relative to the ground?
v_ball/ground = v_ball/car + v_car/ground = 10 + 20 = 30 m/s (when thrown in the direction of motion).
A plane flies north at 200 km/h relative to the air. A wind blows east at 50 km/h. What is the plane's speed relative to the ground?
Ground velocity = vector sum of airplane velocity and wind velocity. Since they are perpendicular: v = √(200² + 50²) = √42500 ≈ 206.2 km/h.
Train A moves east at 80 km/h and Train B moves west at 60 km/h on parallel tracks. What is the velocity of Train A as observed from Train B?
Taking east as positive: v_A = +80, v_B = −60. v_A/B = v_A − v_B = 80 − (−60) = 140 km/h east.
Two boats leave from the same point. Boat A goes north at 4 m/s and Boat B goes east at 3 m/s. What is the velocity of Boat A relative to Boat B?
v_A/B = v_A − v_B = (0, 4) − (3, 0) = (−3, 4). Magnitude = √(9 + 16) = 5 m/s. Direction: arctan(3/4) ≈ 36.9° west of north.
A helicopter flies at 40 m/s east relative to the ground. A package is dropped from the helicopter. What is the package's initial velocity relative to the ground?
When released, the package has the helicopter's velocity (40 m/s east). 'Dropped' means no extra velocity was added, but the package retains its existing motion — Galilean velocity addition applies.
Real-World Applications
Example: A helicopter flies at 40 m/s east relative to the ground. A package is dropped from the helicopter. What is the package's initial velocity relative to the ground?
When released, the package has the helicopter's velocity (40 m/s east). 'Dropped' means no extra velocity was added, but the package retains its existing motion — Galilean velocity addition applies.
Example: An espionage movie shows a car chase where the hero's car goes 50 m/s east and the villain's car goes 45 m/s east. The hero fires a projectile backward at 30 m/s relative to his car. What speed does the villain's driver see the projectile approaching?
Projectile ground velocity = 50 − 30 = 20 m/s east. Villain's velocity = 45 m/s east. v_projectile/villain = 20 − 45 = −25 m/s (25 m/s approaching, since the villain is faster than the projectile's ground speed).

    [ ] A standardized unit of measurement agreed upon by international scientific bodies for expressing the magnitude of displacement, velocity, and other quantities
    [ ] A rigid wooden or metal frame used in physics laboratories to hold measuring instruments, sensors, and other experimental equipment securely in place
    [*] A coordinate system and associated time standard attached to an observer, used to measure positions, velocities, and other physical quantities
    Explanation: A reference frame provides the axes and origin from which an observer measures physical quantities. Different observers in different frames may measure different values for the same event.

  Q12. Which definition BEST describes relative velocity?
    [ ] The velocity of an object measured exclusively relative to the ground, since the ground is the only valid reference for reporting any velocity measurement
    [ ] The maximum speed an object can reach under the given conditions, representing the upper limit of its velocity regardless of the observer's reference frame
    [ ] The arithmetic mean of two objects' velocities divided by two, giving a single representative velocity for the pair of objects moving through the same region
    [*] The velocity of one object as measured by or with respect to another object's reference frame, calculated by vector subtraction of their velocities
    Explanation: Relative velocity v_A/B = v_A − v_B gives how fast A appears to move from B's perspective. It is a vector quantity that depends on the chosen reference frame.

  Q13. Which definition BEST describes an inertial reference frame?
    [ ] Any reference frame that happens to be at rest at a given instant, regardless of whether it is accelerating, rotating, or experiencing other forms of motion
    [*] A reference frame in which Newton's laws of motion hold in their simplest form — one that is not accelerating or rotating
    [ ] A reference frame whose origin is centered at the Sun, since the Sun is the only truly stationary object in the solar system from a gravitational perspective
    [ ] A reference frame in which all objects always accelerate, because the frame itself provides a constant background acceleration to everything within its boundaries
    Explanation: In an inertial frame, an object with no net force has zero acceleration (Newton's first law). Any frame moving at constant velocity relative to an inertial frame is also inertial.

  Q14. Which definition BEST describes a non-inertial reference frame?
    [ ] A reference frame designed exclusively for analyzing motion in outer space, where gravitational effects are negligible and objects float without external forces
    [ ] A reference frame that is permanently at rest relative to the fixed stars and cannot translate or move under any circumstances within the physical universe
    [*] A reference frame that is accelerating or rotating, in which fictitious forces must be introduced for Newton's laws to appear valid
    [ ] A reference frame in which no objects are ever observed to be moving, because the frame definition requires that all motion within it equals exactly zero
    Explanation: In a non-inertial (accelerating) frame, objects appear to experience forces that have no physical source — these are called fictitious or pseudo-forces (e.g., centrifugal force, Coriolis force).

  Q15. Which definition BEST describes Galilean transformation?
    [ ] A mathematical method of rotating coordinate axes to align them with the direction of an object's velocity, simplifying the equations of motion in that frame
    [ ] A transformation technique used exclusively by Galileo during his 17th-century experiments and no longer applicable to any modern physics calculations today
    [ ] The standard conversion formulas used to translate between Celsius and Fahrenheit temperature scales, named after Galileo for his early thermometer innovations
    [*] The set of equations that relate position, velocity, and time measurements between two inertial reference frames moving at constant velocity relative to each other
    Explanation: Galilean transformations: x' = x − vt, t' = t. They assume absolute time and are accurate at low speeds. At speeds near light, they are replaced by Lorentz transformations.

  Q16. Which definition BEST describes a fictitious force?
    [*] An apparent force that appears in a non-inertial reference frame to account for the frame's acceleration — it has no physical agent or source
    [ ] A force that was measured incorrectly by a scientist due to equipment calibration errors, leading to a reported value that does not match the actual force present
    [ ] A force that is completely imaginary and produces absolutely no observable effects whatsoever on any object, existing only as a theoretical concept in textbooks
    [ ] Any force that has a very small magnitude compared to the dominant forces in a system, making it negligible for practical calculations but still physically real
    Explanation: Fictitious (pseudo) forces like centrifugal force, Coriolis force, and the force you feel when a car brakes are real in their effects within the non-inertial frame but have no physical cause — they arise from the frame's acceleration.

  Q17. Which definition BEST describes the Galilean velocity addition formula?
    [ ] A formula that is valid only for rotating reference frames, relating the angular velocities of objects spinning at different rates around a common central axis
    [*] The equation v_AC = v_AB + v_BC, which states that the velocity of A relative to C equals the velocity of A relative to B plus the velocity of B relative to C
    [ ] The equation used to calculate the velocity an object gains due to gravitational acceleration, relating drop height to the final speed upon reaching the ground
    [ ] An equation specifically for adding position vectors rather than velocities, connecting the displacement of one object to the position of another in the same frame
    Explanation: Galilean velocity addition is a chain rule for velocities: v_AC = v_AB + v_BC. This works well at everyday speeds but breaks down near the speed of light.

  Q18. Which definition BEST describes an observer in physics?
    [ ] A camera pointed at a moving object that records visual data, but whose measurements do not constitute valid physical observations from any reference frame
    [ ] Only a human being physically watching an experiment in person, since instruments and automated sensors cannot qualify as observers in the physics definition
    [ ] A scientist who remains entirely passive and does not participate in or interact with any experiments, simply recording outcomes from outside the laboratory space
    [*] A person or measuring device in a specific reference frame that records positions, times, and other physical quantities of events
    Explanation: In physics, an 'observer' is anyone (or any device) that measures physical quantities from a given reference frame. Different observers in different frames may record different measurements.

  Q19. Which definition BEST describes the principle of Galilean relativity?
    [*] The principle that the laws of mechanics are the same in all inertial reference frames — no mechanical experiment can distinguish between uniform motion and rest
    [ ] A principle that applies only to the behavior of light and electromagnetic waves, having no relevance to the mechanics of material objects or their interactions
    [ ] The idea that only measurements made from Earth's surface reference frame are physically valid, and all other reference frames produce incorrect or distorted results
    [ ] The general philosophical statement that everything in physics is relative, meaning no measurement of any kind has an absolute value under any possible conditions
    Explanation: Galilean relativity states that no experiment within a closed, uniformly moving room can determine whether the room is at rest or in uniform motion. The laws of mechanics are identical in all inertial frames.

  Q20. Which definition BEST describes velocity composition?
    [*] The process of combining velocity vectors from different reference frames to find an object's velocity in a third frame
    [ ] Simply adding the scalar speeds of two objects together without considering direction, giving the total combined speed regardless of which frame is being used
    [ ] Breaking a single velocity vector into its horizontal and vertical components using trigonometry, which is also called vector decomposition or resolution of vectors
    [ ] Calculating the arithmetic average velocity of a group of objects by summing their individual velocities and dividing by the total number of objects in the group
    Explanation: Velocity composition (addition) allows us to find velocities across reference frames. For three frames A, B, C: v_AC = v_AB + v_BC. At relativistic speeds, the formula changes.

  Q21. A plane flies north at 200 km/h relative to the air. A wind blows east at 50 km/h. What is the plane's speed relative to the ground?
    [*] √(200² + 50²) = √42500 ≈ 206.2 km/h — the perpendicular velocities combine by the Pythagorean theorem
    [ ] 200 km/h — the plane's airspeed alone determines its ground speed, since wind only affects direction and cannot change the actual magnitude of the velocity
    [ ] 250 km/h — adding the two speeds directly: 200 + 50 = 250 km/h, because the wind and the plane both contribute their full speeds to the resultant velocity
    [ ] 150 km/h — subtracting the wind speed from the airspeed: 200 minus 50 = 150 km/h, because the crosswind partially slows the plane's forward ground progress
    Explanation: Ground velocity = vector sum of airplane velocity and wind velocity. Since they are perpendicular: v = √(200² + 50²) = √42500 ≈ 206.2 km/h.

  Q22. A swimmer swims at 2 m/s in still water. She tries to swim straight across a river flowing at 1.5 m/s. At what angle downstream from her intended path does she actually travel?
    [ ] arctan(2/1.5) is approximately 53.1 degrees downstream — dividing the swimmer's speed by the river speed gives the correct angle from the intended crossing path
    [ ] 0 degrees — the swimmer goes straight across the river exactly as intended, because her swimming speed is greater than the current speed and she can overpower it
    [ ] 90 degrees — the swimmer goes entirely downstream with no crossward progress, because the river current completely overwhelms her ability to swim perpendicular to it
    [*] arctan(1.5/2) ≈ 36.9° downstream — the river pushes her sideways
    Explanation: tan(θ) = v_river/v_swimmer = 1.5/2 = 0.75, so θ = arctan(0.75) ≈ 36.9° downstream from her intended perpendicular path.

  Q23. Train A moves east at 80 km/h and Train B moves west at 60 km/h on parallel tracks. What is the velocity of Train A as observed from Train B?
    [ ] 140 km/h west — the relative velocity points in the direction of Train B, since the observer is on Train B and perceives Train A as approaching from the opposite side
    [ ] 20 km/h east — subtracting the speeds gives 80 minus 60 = 20 km/h, since when objects move in opposite directions you subtract rather than add their velocities
    [*] 140 km/h east — v_A/B = v_A − v_B = 80 − (−60) = 140 km/h east
    [ ] 80 km/h east — the relative velocity equals just Train A's ground speed, because the observer's own motion does not affect how they perceive another object's velocity
    Explanation: Taking east as positive: v_A = +80, v_B = −60. v_A/B = v_A − v_B = 80 − (−60) = 140 km/h east.

  Q24. You run at 3 m/s on a moving walkway that moves at 2 m/s in the same direction. Then you turn around and run back at 3 m/s relative to the walkway. What are your ground speeds in each case?
    [*] 5 m/s forward and 1 m/s forward — going with: 3 + 2 = 5; going against: 2 − 3 = −1 m/s (actually 3 − 2 = 1 m/s backward relative to your original direction... but the walkway still carries you at 2 m/s forward, so net is 2 − 3 = −1 m/s, meaning 1 m/s backward)
    [ ] Going with: your ground speed is 5 m/s forward; running back: your ground speed is 1 m/s backward, calculated as negative 3 plus 2 = negative 1 m/s in the walkway direction
    [ ] 3 m/s forward in both cases — the walkway has no effect on your ground speed because your running speed alone determines how fast you move relative to the stationary ground
    [ ] 5 m/s forward in both cases — the walkway adds 2 m/s in both directions since it always pushes you forward regardless of which way you are running on its surface
    Explanation: Forward: v = 3 + 2 = 5 m/s. Backward (running against walkway): v = −3 + 2 = −1 m/s, meaning 1 m/s in the backward direction relative to the ground. The walkway partially cancels your backward running.

  Q25. Two boats leave from the same point. Boat A goes north at 4 m/s and Boat B goes east at 3 m/s. What is the velocity of Boat A relative to Boat B?
    [ ] 7 m/s directly north — adding the speeds gives 4 + 3 = 7 m/s, and since Boat A moves north the relative velocity must also point in the northward direction only
    [ ] 5 m/s in the northeast direction — combining the perpendicular velocities gives magnitude 5 m/s, but the direction should be northeast since both boats head that way
    [ ] 1 m/s in the northeast direction — subtracting the speeds gives 4 minus 3 = 1 m/s, since relative velocity always involves simple subtraction of the speed magnitudes
    [*] 5 m/s in a direction 53.1° west of north — v_A/B = v_A − v_B, giving a vector pointing north and west with magnitude √(4² + 3²) = 5 m/s
    Explanation: v_A/B = v_A − v_B = (0, 4) − (3, 0) = (−3, 4). Magnitude = √(9 + 16) = 5 m/s. Direction: arctan(3/4) ≈ 36.9° west of north.

  Q26. Raindrops fall vertically at 8 m/s. You run horizontally at 6 m/s. At what angle should you tilt your umbrella from vertical to stay dry?
    [ ] 45 degrees from vertical — the tilt angle is always 45 degrees for any combination of rain speed and running speed, since the rain always appears to fall diagonally at this angle
    [ ] Straight up with no tilt needed — rain falls vertically regardless of your motion, so the umbrella should be held perfectly upright to provide maximum overhead coverage
    [*] arctan(6/8) ≈ 36.9° toward the direction you're running — the rain appears to come at you from an angle
    [ ] arctan(8/6) is approximately 53.1 degrees from vertical — dividing the rain speed by your running speed gives the correct angle between the vertical and the apparent path
    Explanation: In your (moving) frame, rain has a horizontal component of 6 m/s and vertical component of 8 m/s. Angle from vertical: θ = arctan(6/8) = arctan(0.75) ≈ 36.9°.

  Q27. A child sitting in a train moving at constant velocity drops a ball. According to Galilean relativity, in the train's frame the ball falls straight down. What does an observer on the platform see?
    [ ] The ball falls straight down in the platform observer's view as well, because gravity pulls objects vertically regardless of which reference frame is used to observe them
    [ ] The ball curves backward relative to the train's direction, since the platform observer sees air resistance push the ball behind the point where it was released from rest
    [*] The ball follows a parabolic path — it has the train's horizontal velocity plus downward gravitational acceleration
    [ ] The ball moves only in the horizontal direction with no vertical drop, since the train's forward velocity transfers entirely to the ball and overwhelms gravitational pull
    Explanation: In the ground frame, the ball has the train's constant horizontal velocity and accelerates vertically due to gravity, producing a parabolic trajectory — just like any horizontal projectile.

  Q28. A helicopter flies at 40 m/s east relative to the ground. A package is dropped from the helicopter. What is the package's initial velocity relative to the ground?
    [ ] 40 m/s west — the package immediately reverses direction upon release, moving opposite to the helicopter because being dropped means all forward velocity is lost instantly
    [ ] 0 m/s — the package starts from rest relative to the ground, because dropping it means no velocity was added and objects at rest in a moving vehicle have zero ground speed
    [*] 40 m/s east — the package inherits the helicopter's horizontal velocity at the moment of release
    [ ] 40 m/s downward — the package immediately acquires a downward velocity equal to the helicopter's horizontal speed, since the velocity direction shifts by 90 degrees upon drop
    Explanation: When released, the package has the helicopter's velocity (40 m/s east). 'Dropped' means no extra velocity was added, but the package retains its existing motion — Galilean velocity addition applies.

  Q29. Car A moves north at 30 m/s. Car B moves south at 20 m/s. Car C is parked. Rank the speed of Car A as measured by each observer (C's frame, B's frame).
    [ ] C measures 30 m/s and B measures 10 m/s — subtracting B's speed from A's speed: 30 minus 20 = 10 m/s, since same-direction subtraction applies for opposite travel
    [ ] Both C and B measure exactly 30 m/s — the speed of Car A is an absolute quantity that does not depend on the reference frame of the person making the measurement
    [*] C measures 30 m/s, B measures 50 m/s — Car A appears fastest from the approaching Car B's frame
    [ ] C measures 50 m/s and B measures 30 m/s — the parked observer sees the higher relative speed while the moving observer sees the basic ground speed of Car A only
    Explanation: From C (parked): v_A/C = 30 m/s. From B (approaching): v_A/B = 30 − (−20) = 50 m/s. Objects approaching each other have higher relative speeds.

  Q30. An espionage movie shows a car chase where the hero's car goes 50 m/s east and the villain's car goes 45 m/s east. The hero fires a projectile backward at 30 m/s relative to his car. What speed does the villain's driver see the projectile approaching?
    [ ] 80 m/s — adding the projectile's thrown speed to the villain's car speed: 30 + 50 = 80 m/s approach speed as seen from the villain's reference frame
    [ ] 30 m/s — the projectile's approach speed equals its throwing speed, since the hero's car velocity cancels out from the villain's perspective in this reference frame
    [*] 25 m/s — projectile's ground speed = 50 − 30 = 20 m/s east; relative to villain: 20 − 45 = −25 m/s, so it approaches at 25 m/s
    [ ] 5 m/s — subtracting the two car speeds gives the approach rate: 50 minus 45 = 5 m/s, since the projectile moves with the relative gap between the two vehicles
    Explanation: Projectile ground velocity = 50 − 30 = 20 m/s east. Villain's velocity = 45 m/s east. v_projectile/villain = 20 − 45 = −25 m/s (25 m/s approaching, since the villain is faster than the projectile's ground speed).
