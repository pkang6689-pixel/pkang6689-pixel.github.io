--- Lesson 3.1: Measures of Central Tendency (mean, median, mode) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Central Tendency (mean, median, mode)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the mean of a data set?
The mean is the arithmetic average: sum of all data values divided by the total count.
What is the median?
The median is the center value in an ordered data set, splitting it into two equal halves.
What is the mode?
The mode is the data value with the highest frequency of occurrence.
What is a weighted mean?
A weighted mean assigns different weights to values, computed as the sum of (weight times value) divided by the sum of weights.
What is a trimmed mean?
A trimmed mean removes a set percentage of extreme values from both ends before computing the average, reducing the influence of outliers.
What is the midrange?
The midrange = (max + min) / 2. It is a simple but outlier-sensitive measure of center.
A student's grades are: Homework (weight 20%) = 90, Midterm (weight 30%) = 78, Final (weight 50%) = 84. What is the student's weighted mean grade?
Weighted mean = 0.20(90) + 0.30(78) + 0.50(84) = 18 + 23.4 + 42 = 83.4.
How to Calculate
Example: For the data set {2, 4, 6, 8}, the mean is:
Solution: Mean = (2 + 4 + 6 + 8) / 4 = 20 / 4 = 5.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The mean includes every value in its calculation, so a single extreme outlier can pull it substantially higher or lower.
The median is a resistant measure because it depends on position rather than magnitude, making it robust against outliers.
Real-World Applications
Example: A teacher records test scores for 7 students: 68, 72, 75, 78, 80, 85, 92. She wants to report a single number that best represents typical performance. Which value is the mean, and is it a reasonable summary here?
Mean = (68+72+75+78+80+85+92)/7 = 550/7 ≈ 78.6. With no extreme outliers and a roughly symmetric spread, the mean is a good summary.
Example: A real estate website lists home prices in a neighborhood: $180k, $195k, $210k, $215k, $220k, $225k, and $1.2 million. A buyer asks for the 'average' home price. Why would the median be a better measure than the mean?
The mean = (180+195+210+215+220+225+1200)/7 ≈ $349k, inflated by the outlier. The median ($215k) is resistant to the outlier and more representative.
Example: A shoe store tracks daily sales of shoe sizes: 7, 7, 8, 8, 8, 9, 9, 10, 11. The manager wants to know which size to stock the most. Which measure of central tendency should the manager use?
The mode (size 8, appearing 3 times) tells the manager which size is in highest demand, making it the most useful measure for inventory decisions.
Example: An ice cream shop tracks daily sales of five flavors over a month. Vanilla sold 450 units, Chocolate 430, Strawberry 200, Mint 180, and Mango 140. The owner wants to determine the most popular flavor and the average sales per flavor. What are the mode and the mean?
The mode is Vanilla with the highest sales (450). Mean = (450+430+200+180+140)/5 = 1400/5 = 280 units per flavor.
Important Concepts
What does 'bimodal' mean
Bimodal means a distribution has two peaks or two values tied for the highest frequency.
What is a weighted mean
A weighted mean assigns different weights to values, computed as the sum of (weight times value) divided by the sum of weights.
What does 'skewed distribution' mean
In a skewed distribution, data trail off more in one direction. Right-skewed distributions have the mean pulled above the median; left-skewed have it pulled below.
A student's grades are: Homework (weight 20%) = 90, Midterm (weight 30%) = 78, Final (weight 50%) = 84. What is the student's weighted mean grade
Weighted mean = 0.20(90) + 0.30(78) + 0.50(84) = 18 + 23.4 + 42 = 83.4.

--- Lesson 3.2: Measures of Spread (range, variance, standard deviation) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Spread (range, variance, standard deviation)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For the data set {2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9}, the mean is 5. What is the population variance?
Deviations: (−3)²+(−1)²+(−1)²+(−1)²+(0)²+(0)²+(2)²+(4)² = 9+1+1+1+0+0+4+16 = 32. Variance = 32/8 = 4.
What is the range of a data set?
The range is the simplest measure of spread: maximum minus minimum.
What is variance?
Variance quantifies spread by averaging the squared differences between each data point and the mean.
What is standard deviation?
Standard deviation is the square root of variance, bringing the spread measure back to the original measurement units.
What is Bessel's correction?
Bessel's correction divides by n−1 rather than n when computing sample variance, correcting the systematic underestimate of population variance.
What is the sum of squared deviations (SS)?
SS = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)². Dividing SS by N gives population variance; dividing by n−1 gives sample variance.
A sample of 4 daily high temperatures (°F) is: 60, 65, 70, 75. What is the sample variance (using n − 1)?
Mean = 270/4 = 67.5. Deviations: −7.5, −2.5, 2.5, 7.5. SS = 56.25+6.25+6.25+56.25 = 125. Sample variance = 125/3 ≈ 41.67.
A data set of exam scores has a population variance of 225. What is the population standard deviation?
Standard deviation = √variance = √225 = 15.
How to Calculate
Example: For the data set {2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9}, the mean is 5. What is the population variance?
Solution: Deviations: (−3)²+(−1)²+(−1)²+(−1)²+(0)²+(0)²+(2)²+(4)² = 9+1+1+1+0+0+4+16 = 32. Variance = 32/8 = 4.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The range dropped from 150 ms to 25 ms by removing one value, showing that the range depends entirely on the extremes and is very sensitive to outliers.
Real-World Applications
Example: If every value in a data set is multiplied by 3, the standard deviation will:
Multiplying all values by a constant c multiplies the standard deviation by |c|. Variance is multiplied by c².
Example: A teacher gives a quiz to two classes. Class A scores: 70, 75, 78, 80, 82. Class B scores: 50, 65, 78, 90, 100. Both classes have the same mean (77). Which class has greater variability, and how can you tell?
Class A range = 82−70 = 12. Class B range = 100−50 = 50. Despite equal means, Class B's scores are far more spread out.
Example: An investor tracks monthly returns (%) for two stocks over 6 months. Stock X: 2, 3, 2, 4, 3, 4. Stock Y: −5, 10, −3, 15, 1, 12. Both average 3% per month. Which stock is riskier, and why?
Stock X's returns cluster tightly around 3% (low SD), while Stock Y swings wildly from −5% to 15% (high SD). Greater standard deviation indicates higher risk.
Example: A data analyst finds that a company's daily sales figures have a mean of $500 and a standard deviation of $120. If the company converts all sales records to euros at a fixed rate of 0.90 euros per dollar, what will the new standard deviation be?
Multiplying all values by 0.90 multiplies the standard deviation by 0.90: 120 × 0.90 = 108 euros.
Example: A data set of exam scores has a population variance of 225. What is the population standard deviation?
Standard deviation = √variance = √225 = 15.

--- Lesson 3.3: Percentiles & Quartiles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Percentiles & Quartiles
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a percentile?
The kth percentile is the value at or below which k% of the observations in a data set fall.
What is a quartile?
The three quartiles Q1, Q2, and Q3 split an ordered data set into four groups, each containing about 25% of the data.
What is the interquartile range (IQR)?
IQR = Q3 − Q1. It captures the spread of the central 50% of the data and is resistant to outliers.
What is the five-number summary?
The five-number summary (Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max) provides a compact description of the distribution and is used to construct boxplots.
What is a decile?
Deciles divide data into 10 equal groups. The first decile equals the 10th percentile, the fifth decile equals the median, etc.
What is the difference between a percentile and a percentage?
A percentile indicates relative standing (e.g., 'this score is at the 75th percentile'), while a percentage simply represents a fraction of 100 (e.g., '75% correct').
What is meant by 'outlier fence' in the context of quartiles?
Outlier fences are computed from the quartiles and IQR. Points beyond these fences are flagged as potential outliers.
In a class of 200 students, a student scored 156th when ranked from lowest to highest. What is the student's approximate percentile rank?
Percentile rank ≈ (156/200) × 100 = 78th percentile. The student scored higher than about 78% of the class.
A marathon records finishing times (minutes) for 1,000 runners. The five-number summary is: Min=145, Q1=210, Median=240, Q3=275, Max=420. The race director wants to award medals to runners finishing in the top 25%. What is the approximate cutoff time?
The fastest 25% are the runners with the lowest times, i.e., below Q1 = 210 minutes. Q1 marks the boundary below which 25% of runners fall.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Because the IQR only involves Q1 and Q3, extreme outliers do not change its value.
Outlier fences are computed from the quartiles and IQR. Points beyond these fences are flagged as potential outliers.
Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5×IQR = 200 − 150 = 50. Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5×IQR = 300 + 150 = 450. Since 400 < 450, the $400k home is not an outlier.
IQR = 72,000 − 45,000 = 27,000. Upper fence = 72,000 + 1.5(27,000) = 72,000 + 40,500 = $112,500. Since $150,000 > $112,500, it is an outlier.
IQR = 38−22 = 16. Extreme outlier fence = Q3 + 3×IQR = 38 + 48 = 86. Since 55 < 86, the value is not an extreme outlier (though it may be a mild outlier by the 1.5×IQR rule since 38+24=62 > 55... actually 55 < 62, so it's not even a mild outlier).
Real-World Applications
Example: A student scores at the 90th percentile on a standardized test. This means:
The 90th percentile means the student's score is at or above 90% of all scores.
Example: What does 'cumulative percentage' mean in a percentile context?
Cumulative percentage is the running total of relative frequencies, showing what fraction of data falls at or below each value.
Example: What is the difference between a percentile and a percentage?
A percentile indicates relative standing (e.g., 'this score is at the 75th percentile'), while a percentage simply represents a fraction of 100 (e.g., '75% correct').

Being at the 60th percentile means the baby's weight exceeds or equals that of 60% of same-age babies.

Percentile rank ≈ (156/200) × 100 = 78th percentile. The student scored higher than about 78% of the class.

Percentiles reflect rank position, not score magnitude. Near the extremes, a small percentile difference may correspond to a large or small score gap depending on the distribution.

--- Lesson 3.4: Z-Scores & Standardization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Z-Scores & Standardization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a z-score?
A z-score transforms a raw value into units of standard deviations above or below the mean.
What is the standard normal distribution?
The standard normal distribution is a special case of the normal distribution with μ = 0 and σ = 1. Z-scores follow this distribution.
What is the 'z-table' used for?
A z-table provides cumulative probabilities for the standard normal distribution, telling you the proportion of data below any given z-score.
A factory produces light bulbs with mean lifetime 1200 hours and SD 100 hours. The manager defines 'defective' as any bulb with z-score below −2. What is the maximum lifetime of a defective bulb?
x = mean + z × SD = 1200 + (−2)(100) = 1200 − 200 = 1000 hours. Any bulb lasting 1000 hours or less is considered defective.
An IQ test has mean = 100 and SD = 15. Mensa requires an IQ at or above the 98th percentile, which corresponds to z ≈ 2.05. What is the minimum IQ score for Mensa membership?
x = mean + z × SD = 100 + 2.05(15) = 100 + 30.75 ≈ 131.
A statistics class has exam scores with mean = 74 and SD = 6. The professor wants to assign A grades to students with z-scores of 1.5 or above. What is the minimum raw score for an A?
x = mean + z × SD = 74 + 1.5(6) = 74 + 9 = 83.
Real-World Applications
Example: A z-score tells you:
A z-score = (x − mean) / standard deviation, expressing how far a value is from the mean in units of standard deviations.
Example: If a data value equals the mean, its z-score is:
z = (x − mean)/SD. When x = mean, z = 0/SD = 0.
Example: A score has z = −2.0. This means the score is:
A negative z-score indicates the value is below the mean. z = −2 means it is 2 standard deviations below.
Example: The formula for a z-score is:
The z-score formula is z = (x − μ)/σ for a population, or z = (x − x̄)/s for a sample.
Example: A test has mean = 80 and SD = 10. A student scores 95. The z-score is:
z = (95 − 80)/10 = 15/10 = 1.5.
Example: After standardizing a data set (converting all values to z-scores), the new mean and standard deviation are:
Standardization always produces a distribution with mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1.
Example: A z-score of +3.5 would typically be considered:
Values with |z| > 2 or 3 are typically considered unusual. A z-score of 3.5 is far from the mean and suggests a possible outlier.
Example: Z-scores allow you to compare values from:
By converting to a common scale (mean 0, SD 1), z-scores enable direct comparison across different distributions.
Example: If mean = 50 and SD = 5, the raw score corresponding to z = −1.4 is:
x = mean + z × SD = 50 + (−1.4)(5) = 50 − 7 = 43.
Example: According to the Empirical Rule for normal distributions, approximately what percentage of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean?
The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7 rule) states about 68% of data in a normal distribution falls within ±1 SD of the mean.
Example: What is a z-score?
A z-score transforms a raw value into units of standard deviations above or below the mean.
Example: What does it mean if a z-score is negative?
A negative z-score means x < mean, so the data value lies below the mean of the distribution.
Example: What does 'unusual value' mean in z-score context?
Values with |z| > 2 are generally considered unusual because they occur in less than about 5% of a normal distribution.
Example: What does it mean to 'convert back from a z-score to a raw score'?
To reverse the z-score transformation: x = μ + zσ (or x̄ + zs for samples).
Example: On a biology exam (mean = 72, SD = 8), a student scores 88. On a chemistry exam (mean = 65, SD = 5), the same student scores 80. On which exam did the student perform better relative to classmates?
Biology z = (88−72)/8 = 2.0. Chemistry z = (80−65)/5 = 3.0. The higher z-score in chemistry means the student was further above average relative to peers.
Example: The heights of adult men in a city are normally distributed with mean 70 inches and SD 3 inches. A man is 76 inches tall. Using the Empirical Rule, approximately what percentage of men are shorter than him?
z = (76−70)/3 = 2.0. By the Empirical Rule, about 95% falls within ±2 SD, so about 2.5% is above z=2 and about 97.5% is below.
Example: A factory produces light bulbs with mean lifetime 1200 hours and SD 100 hours. The manager defines 'defective' as any bulb with z-score below −2. What is the maximum lifetime of a defective bulb?
x = mean + z × SD = 1200 + (−2)(100) = 1200 − 200 = 1000 hours. Any bulb lasting 1000 hours or less is considered defective.
Example: An IQ test has mean = 100 and SD = 15. Mensa requires an IQ at or above the 98th percentile, which corresponds to z ≈ 2.05. What is the minimum IQ score for Mensa membership?
x = mean + z × SD = 100 + 2.05(15) = 100 + 30.75 ≈ 131.
Example: Daily coffee consumption at an office is normally distributed with mean = 3.2 cups and SD = 0.8 cups. Using Chebyshev's Theorem, at least what percentage of employees drink between 1.6 and 4.8 cups?
The range 1.6 to 4.8 is 3.2 ± 1.6 = mean ± 2 SD (k=2). Chebyshev's: at least 1 − 1/2² = 1 − 0.25 = 75%.
Example: A statistics class has exam scores with mean = 74 and SD = 6. The professor wants to assign A grades to students with z-scores of 1.5 or above. What is the minimum raw score for an A?

--- Lesson 3.5: Coefficient of Variation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Coefficient of Variation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the coefficient of variation (CV)?
The CV = (SD/mean) × 100% is a standardized measure of dispersion that allows comparison across different scales.
What is a 'ratio scale' and why does it matter for the CV?
The CV divides by the mean, so the mean must be meaningful (not arbitrary). Ratio scales (e.g., weight, height, time) have a true zero, making the CV valid.
What is 'reproducibility' and how does the CV measure it?
In laboratory and manufacturing settings, the CV is commonly used to quantify reproducibility. Lower CV values indicate more consistent, reproducible results.
Real-World Applications
Example: The main advantage of the CV over the standard deviation is that the CV:
Because the CV is a ratio of SD to mean, the units cancel, making it ideal for comparing variability across different measurement scales.
Example: A teacher compares test score variability between two courses. Statistics: mean = 78, SD = 6. Art History: mean = 82, SD = 10. A parent asks which course has more uniform grading. What does the CV reveal?
CV_Stats = (6/78)×100% ≈ 7.7%. CV_Art = (10/82)×100% ≈ 12.2%. Statistics scores are more uniform relative to the mean.
Example: A quality control team measures the weight of cereal boxes. Line 1: mean = 500g, SD = 5g. Line 2: mean = 250g, SD = 3g. Management wants to identify which production line needs improvement. What does the CV suggest?
CV_1 = (5/500)×100% = 1.0%. CV_2 = (3/250)×100% = 1.2%. Line 2 has slightly greater relative variability, indicating it needs improvement.
Example: A sports analyst compares the consistency of two basketball players' scoring. Player 1: mean = 22 points, SD = 4 points. Player 2: mean = 14 points, SD = 3 points. Which player is the more consistent scorer?
CV_1 = (4/22)×100% ≈ 18.2%. CV_2 = (3/14)×100% ≈ 21.4%. Player 1 is relatively more consistent despite having a higher SD.

--- Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Sports & Medicine (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Sports & Medicine
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'sabermetrics'?
Sabermetrics (SABR = Society for American Baseball Research) applies statistical analysis to baseball data, pioneered by Bill James and popularized by Moneyball.
What is a 'randomized controlled trial' (RCT)?
RCTs are the gold standard in medical research. Random assignment ensures treatment groups are comparable, allowing causal conclusions.
What is 'regression to the mean' in sports context?
Regression to the mean explains why a player who has an exceptional season often performs closer to their career average the following year.
What is 'Wins Above Replacement' (WAR) in sports analytics?
WAR estimates a player's total contribution by measuring how many additional wins they provide over what a freely available replacement player would contribute.
What is 'load management' in sports, from a statistical perspective?
Load management uses statistical models of injury risk, cumulative workload, and performance trends to decide when to rest players.
A vaccine trial reports: Treatment group (10,000 people): 5 got the disease. Placebo group (10,000 people): 100 got the disease. What is the vaccine's efficacy?
Efficacy = 1 − (treatment rate / placebo rate) = 1 − (5/10000)/(100/10000) = 1 − (0.0005/0.01) = 1 − 0.05 = 95%.
A clinical study reports that a new diabetes medication reduces HbA1c by 0.3% compared to placebo (p < 0.001). Doctors consider a 0.5% reduction clinically meaningful. What is the correct interpretation?
A very low p-value means the effect is unlikely due to chance, but the effect size (0.3%) is too small to be clinically useful. Context matters.
Real-World Applications
Example: In sports analytics, a player's batting average is an example of a:
Batting average = hits / at-bats, which is a proportion or rate, not a measure of central tendency like the arithmetic mean.
Example: In basketball, a player's 'Player Efficiency Rating' (PER) combines multiple stats. This is an example of:
PER is a composite statistic that weights multiple performance categories (points, rebounds, assists, etc.) to summarize overall player effectiveness.
Example: What does 'on-base percentage' (OBP) measure in baseball?
OBP = (Hits + Walks + HBP) / (At-bats + Walks + HBP + Sacrifice flies). It is more comprehensive than batting average.
Example: What is 'load management' in sports, from a statistical perspective?
Load management uses statistical models of injury risk, cumulative workload, and performance trends to decide when to rest players.
Example: A baseball player hit .350 in April (50 at-bats) but only .260 for the full season (500 at-bats). A fan argues April showed his 'true talent.' Why is the full-season average more reliable?
With only 50 at-bats, random variation can produce extreme batting averages. Over 500 at-bats, the average converges closer to true ability (regression to the mean).

    Explanation: Survival rate is typically expressed as a percentage surviving after a defined period (e.g., 5-year survival rate for cancer patients).

  Q15. What is 'regression to the mean' in sports context?
    [ ] The team's win percentage always approaching 50%
    [ ] A player's performance always getting worse over time
    [*] The tendency for extreme performances to be followed by more typical ones closer to the player's long-term average
    [ ] A statistical formula for predicting home runs
    Explanation: Regression to the mean explains why a player who has an exceptional season often performs closer to their career average the following year.

  Q16. What does 'relative risk' mean in medical studies?
    [*] The ratio of the probability of an event in the treatment group to its probability in the control group
    [ ] The total number of side effects reported during the clinical trial observation period
    [ ] The cost of medical treatment, including drugs, procedures, and all associated expenses
    [ ] A patient's personal risk tolerance, which reflects their willingness to accept side effects
    Explanation: Relative risk compares the incidence of an outcome between two groups. RR = 2.0 means the treatment group is twice as likely to experience the outcome.

  Q17. What is 'Wins Above Replacement' (WAR) in sports analytics?
    [ ] A player's total number of wins accumulated over the course of an entire career or season
    [ ] The number of wars a country has fought, which is a historical measure unrelated to sports
    [ ] The difference between wins and losses, also known as a team's overall margin of victory
    [*] A composite statistic estimating how many more wins a player contributes compared to a replacement-level (average) player
    Explanation: WAR estimates a player's total contribution by measuring how many additional wins they provide over what a freely available replacement player would contribute.

  Q18. What does 'number needed to treat' (NNT) mean?
    [*] The number of patients who must receive a treatment for one additional patient to benefit compared to the control group
    [ ] The total number of patients in a hospital at any given point during the study period
    [ ] The number of doses required to complete the full prescribed course of treatment
    [ ] The number of doctors per patient, which reflects the staffing ratio at a medical facility
    Explanation: NNT = 1 / (absolute risk reduction). A lower NNT indicates a more effective treatment. NNT = 10 means treating 10 patients benefits 1 additional patient.

  Q19. What is 'load management' in sports, from a statistical perspective?
    [ ] Loading equipment onto trucks for transportation to the stadium before game day events
    [*] Using data on player fatigue, injury risk, and performance metrics to strategically rest players for optimal long-term performance
    [ ] Reducing the weight of player uniforms to improve comfort and range of motion on field
    [ ] Managing the team's budget and financial resources to stay within the salary cap limits
    Explanation: Load management uses statistical models of injury risk, cumulative workload, and performance trends to decide when to rest players.

  Q20. What does 'absolute risk reduction' (ARR) mean?
    [ ] The relative risk divided by the sample size
    [ ] The standard deviation of treatment outcomes
    [ ] The total number of adverse events
    [*] The difference in event rates between the control group and the treatment group
    Explanation: ARR = control event rate − treatment event rate. If the control has 20% events and treatment has 15%, ARR = 5 percentage points.

  Q21. A baseball player hit .350 in April (50 at-bats) but only .260 for the full season (500 at-bats). A fan argues April showed his 'true talent.' Why is the full-season average more reliable?
    [*] Larger sample sizes produce more reliable estimates; the hot April likely reflected random variation (regression to the mean)
    [ ] April data should be excluded because it's spring training
    [ ] Both averages are equally reliable
    [ ] Full-season averages are always lower
    Explanation: With only 50 at-bats, random variation can produce extreme batting averages. Over 500 at-bats, the average converges closer to true ability (regression to the mean).

  Q22. A pharmaceutical company advertises that its new statin reduces heart attack risk by 50%. The actual data: 2 out of 100 patients on placebo had a heart attack vs. 1 out of 100 on the statin. Is '50% reduction' misleading?
    [ ] No, 50% is accurate and impressive enough to communicate the benefit to patients clearly
    [ ] The study is invalid because the sample is too large and may detect trivially small effects
    [ ] No, relative risk always tells the full story and absolute risk adds no useful information
    [*] Yes, the relative risk reduction is 50%, but the absolute risk reduction is only 1 percentage point (from 2% to 1%), which is less dramatic
    Explanation: Relative risk reduction (50%) sounds dramatic, but the absolute risk reduction (2%→1% = 1 percentage point) shows the actual benefit is modest. NNT = 100.

  Q23. A sports team's analytics department tracks a midfielder's running distance, sprint count, pass accuracy, and tackles per game. Over 10 games, pass accuracy drops from 88% to 79% while running distance stays constant. What might the data suggest?
    [ ] The player is performing equally well since distance is unchanged
    [*] The player may be fatigued or injured: maintaining distance but losing precision suggests declining effectiveness that a single stat would miss
    [ ] The analytics are wrong because running distance didn't change
    [ ] Pass accuracy is irrelevant for midfielders
    Explanation: Multiple metrics tell a richer story. A decline in precision alongside stable endurance metrics may indicate fatigue, minor injury, or tactical problems.

  Q24. A vaccine trial reports: Treatment group (10,000 people): 5 got the disease. Placebo group (10,000 people): 100 got the disease. What is the vaccine's efficacy?
    [ ] 5%
    [*] 95%
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 99%
    Explanation: Efficacy = 1 − (treatment rate / placebo rate) = 1 − (5/10000)/(100/10000) = 1 − (0.0005/0.01) = 1 − 0.05 = 95%.

  Q25. A basketball analyst compares two players. Player A: 25 points per game, 3 turnovers per game. Player B: 18 points per game, 1 turnover per game. The analyst says Player A is clearly better. What statistical context is being overlooked?
    [ ] Player A plays more minutes so the comparison is always valid and no adjustment is needed
    [*] Efficiency: Player B may have a higher points-per-possession ratio or fewer turnovers per minute, meaning Player B uses possessions more effectively
    [ ] Nothing; more points always means better performance regardless of efficiency or context
    [ ] Only rebounds matter when evaluating overall basketball player effectiveness and value
    Explanation: Raw totals don't account for usage, minutes played, or efficiency. Rate statistics and composite metrics provide better context for player comparison.

  Q26. A hospital implements a new hand-washing protocol. Before the protocol, the infection rate was 8 per 1,000 patients. After, it dropped to 3 per 1,000. The NNT (number needed to treat) for the hand-washing protocol is:
    [ ] 5
    [*] 200
    [ ] 100
    [ ] 500
    Explanation: ARR = 8/1000 − 3/1000 = 5/1000 = 0.005. NNT = 1/0.005 = 200. For every 200 patients exposed to the protocol, one infection is prevented.

  Q27. A marathon runner's finishing times over 10 races are: 3:10, 3:08, 3:15, 3:12, 3:11, 3:09, 3:14, 3:10, 3:13, 3:11 (hours:minutes). The coach says the runner is 'very consistent.' Which statistic best supports this claim?
    [ ] The mean time of about 3:11, which simply reports the average without any variability context
    [ ] The fastest time of 3:08, which is a single extreme value and does not represent consistency
    [*] The small standard deviation (about 2 minutes) and low CV relative to the mean
    [ ] The range of 7 minutes, which only captures the gap between the fastest and slowest races
    Explanation: Consistency is measured by low variability. A standard deviation of only ~2 minutes across 10 races demonstrates high consistency.

  Q28. A clinical study reports that a new diabetes medication reduces HbA1c by 0.3% compared to placebo (p < 0.001). Doctors consider a 0.5% reduction clinically meaningful. What is the correct interpretation?
    [ ] Statistical significance always implies clinical significance so both conditions are always met
    [ ] The drug should be approved immediately since p < 0.001 proves it is effective for patients
    [ ] The drug is highly effective because p < 0.001 is an extremely strong level of significance
    [*] The result is statistically significant but NOT clinically significant, as the 0.3% reduction falls below the 0.5% threshold doctors consider meaningful
    Explanation: A very low p-value means the effect is unlikely due to chance, but the effect size (0.3%) is too small to be clinically useful. Context matters.

  Q29. A sports journalist claims a quarterback had a 'career year' based solely on total passing yards. However, the QB also had the most interceptions in the league. What principle of data interpretation does this illustrate?
    [ ] Total yards is always the most important stat and overrides all other performance measures
    [ ] One statistic tells the complete story and multiple metrics are unnecessary for evaluation
    [*] Evaluating performance requires multiple metrics; cherry-picking one favorable stat while ignoring negatives creates a misleading narrative
    [ ] Interceptions don't matter in football because they are not part of overall quarterback ratings
    Explanation: Comprehensive evaluation requires looking at multiple dimensions. High yards with high turnovers suggests volume without efficiency.

  Q30. A health study compares mortality rates in two groups: exercisers (n=5,000) and non-exercisers (n=5,000). Exercisers had a 2% mortality rate vs. 5% for non-exercisers. Before concluding that exercise prevents death, what must be considered?
    [ ] The sample size is too large, which means the study detected only trivially small effects
    [*] This is observational, so confounders (age, baseline health, socioeconomic status, diet) may explain the difference; exercisers may be healthier to begin with
    [ ] Nothing; the data prove exercise prevents death and no further interpretation is required
    [ ] Only a p-value is needed to make a causal claim about the benefits of regular exercise
    Explanation: Observational studies cannot establish causation. People who exercise regularly may also eat better, be younger, or have better access to healthcare—all confounding variables.
