--- Lesson 3.1: Measures of Central Tendency (mean, median, mode) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Central Tendency (mean, median, mode)
Measures of Central Tendency
Central tendency measures identify a single value that best represents the 'center' of a dataset.
  • Mean (x̄): The arithmetic average — sum of all values divided by the number of values. Sensitive to outliers.  • Median: The middle value when data are ordered. If n is even, it is the average of the two middle values. Resistant to outliers.  • Mode: The most frequently occurring value. A dataset can be unimodal (one mode), bimodal (two modes), multimodal, or have no mode.
Choosing the right measure:
  • Use the mean for roughly symmetric distributions without extreme outliers.  • Use the median for skewed distributions or when outliers are present (e.g., income data).  • Use the mode for categorical data or to identify the most common category.
In a right-skewed distribution: mean > median. In a left-skewed distribution: mean symmetric distribution: mean ≈ median.

--- Lesson 3.2: Measures of Spread (range, variance, standard deviation) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Measures of Spread (range, variance, standard deviation)
Measures of Spread
Spread (variability) measures describe how spread out or dispersed data values are around the center.
  • Range: Maximum − Minimum. Simple but sensitive to outliers.  • Variance (s²): The average of the squared deviations from the mean. Uses n−1 in the denominator for sample variance (Bessel's correction).  • Standard Deviation (s): The square root of the variance. It is in the same units as the data and measures typical distance from the mean.  • IQR (Interquartile Range): Q3 − Q1. Resistant to outliers; measures spread of the middle 50%.
A low standard deviation means data are clustered near the mean; a high standard deviation means data are widely spread.

The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7 rule) for approximately normal distributions: about 68% of data fall within 1 SD of the mean, 95% within 2 SD, and 99.7% within 3 SD.

--- Lesson 3.3: Percentiles & Quartiles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Percentiles & Quartiles
Percentiles & Quartiles
Percentiles divide a dataset into 100 equal parts. The k-th percentile is the value below which k% of the data fall.
  • 25th percentile (Q1): 25% of data fall below this value.  • 50th percentile (Q2 / Median): 50% of data fall below.  • 75th percentile (Q3): 75% of data fall below.
Quartiles divide data into four quarters:
  • Q1 separates the lowest 25%.  • Q2 (median) separates the lower and upper halves.  • Q3 separates the lowest 75%.
Percentiles are widely used in standardized testing (e.g., 'You scored in the 90th percentile means you scored higher than 90% of test-takers'), growth charts, and income reporting.

The percentile rank of a value tells you what percentage of data falls at or below that value.

--- Lesson 3.4: Z-Scores & Standardization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Z-Scores & Standardization
Z-Scores & Standardization
A z-score (standard score) tells you how many standard deviations a data value is from the mean.

Formula: z = (x − x̄) / s
  • z = 0 means the value equals the mean.  • z > 0 means the value is above the mean.  • z   • z = 2 means the value is 2 standard deviations above the mean.
Standardization converts data to z-scores, creating a distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1. This allows comparison of values from different datasets with different units or scales.

Example: Who performed better — a student with a score of 85 (class mean 75, SD 5) or a score of 90 (class mean 82, SD 6)? Their z-scores: (85−75)/5 = 2.0 vs. (90−82)/6 ≈ 1.33. The first student is relatively higher.

Z-scores are also used to identify unusual values: typically, |z| > 2 is considered unusual, and |z| > 3 is very unusual (potential outlier).

--- Lesson 3.5: Coefficient of Variation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Coefficient of Variation
Coefficient of Variation
The Coefficient of Variation (CV) expresses the standard deviation as a percentage of the mean, allowing comparison of variability between datasets with different units or scales.

Formula: CV = (s / x̄) × 100%
  • A higher CV indicates greater relative variability.  • A lower CV indicates less relative variability (more consistent data).  • The CV is unitless, making it ideal for comparing variability across datasets measured in different units.
Example: Dataset A has mean 50, SD 10 → CV = 20%. Dataset B has mean 200, SD 30 → CV = 15%. Although B has a larger SD, it has less relative variability.

Limitations: The CV is not meaningful when the mean is zero or near zero, or for data measured on an interval scale (like temperature in °C, where 0 is not a true zero).

--- Lesson 3.6: Interpreting Data in Context (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interpreting Data in Context
Interpreting Data in Context
Statistical values are meaningless without context. Interpreting data means connecting numerical results back to the real-world situation they describe.
  • Context includes: Who or what was studied, what was measured, when and where the data were collected, and why the study was done.  • Interpreting the mean: 'The average commute time for employees at Company X is 28 minutes' — not just 'x̄ = 28.'  • Interpreting the SD: 'Commute times typically vary by about 12 minutes from the average' — not just 's = 12.'  • Interpreting a z-score: 'A commute of 52 minutes is 2 SDs above the mean, which is unusually long compared to peers.'
Always answer in complete sentences that reference the variable, units, and context. Avoid naked numbers.

Common interpretations on AP exams:
  • Mean: 'On average, [variable] is [value] [units].'  • SD: '[Variable] values typically differ from the mean by about [value] [units].'  • Percentile: '[Value] is at the [k]th percentile, meaning [k]% of [subjects] have a [variable] at or below [value].'

--- Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Sports & Medicine (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Sports & Medicine
Case Studies in Sports & Medicine
Descriptive statistics play a critical role in both sports analytics and medical research.

Sports Analytics:
  • Batting averages, ERA, player efficiency ratings are all descriptive statistics used to evaluate performance.  • Moneyball: The Oakland A's used statistical analysis (OBP, SLG) rather than traditional scouting to build a competitive team on a small budget. This revolutionized baseball and spread to other sports.  • Modern sports use tracking data (player movement, speed, distance covered) to optimize training and strategy.
Medical Research:
  • Clinical trials use means, medians, and survival rates to compare treatments.  • Vital statistics: Life expectancy, infant mortality rate, and disease prevalence are descriptive measures reported by public health agencies.  • BMI (Body Mass Index): A widely used (though debated) descriptive measure: weight(kg) / height(m)².  • Epidemiological studies: Track disease incidence (new cases) and prevalence (total cases) to guide public health policy.
