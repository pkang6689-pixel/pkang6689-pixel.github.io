--- Lesson 6.1: Sampling Methods (systematic, stratified, cluster) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sampling Methods (systematic, stratified, cluster)
Sampling Methods
Good sampling is essential for valid statistical inference. Different methods suit different situations.
  • Simple Random Sample (SRS): Every individual has an equal chance of selection. Use a random number generator or table.  • Systematic sampling: Select every k-th item from a list after a random start. Easy to implement but can be biased if the list has a periodic pattern.  • Stratified sampling: Divide the population into homogeneous strata (e.g., age groups), then take an SRS from each stratum. Ensures representation of key subgroups.  • Cluster sampling: Divide the population into clusters (e.g., schools, neighborhoods), randomly select some clusters, and sample ALL individuals in chosen clusters. Cost-effective for geographically spread populations.  • Convenience sampling: Selecting whoever is easiest to reach — prone to bias; not recommended for formal studies.  • Multi-stage sampling: Combines methods (e.g., cluster then SRS within clusters).

--- Lesson 6.2: Bias in Sampling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bias in Sampling
Bias in Sampling
Bias is a systematic error that causes sample results to consistently differ from the true population value.
  • Selection bias: Certain groups are systematically excluded from the sample (e.g., undercoverage, convenience sampling).  • Response bias: Respondents give inaccurate answers due to question wording, social desirability, or misunderstanding.  • Nonresponse bias: People who don't respond may differ systematically from those who do.  • Voluntary response bias: Only strongly opinionated people choose to participate.  • Leading questions: Questions worded to push respondents toward a particular answer.  • Measurement bias: Faulty instruments or procedures consistently produce inaccurate measurements.
Bias cannot be eliminated by increasing sample size — only by improving methodology.

--- Lesson 6.3: Designing Surveys & Experiments (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Designing Surveys & Experiments
Designing Surveys & Experiments
Proper study design determines whether conclusions are valid.
  • Observational study: Researchers observe and measure without intervening. Can identify associations but NOT causation.  • Experiment: Researchers actively impose treatments on subjects. Can establish cause and effect when properly designed.  • Key elements of an experiment:  • Treatment: The condition applied to experimental units.  • Control group: Receives no treatment or a placebo for comparison.  • Random assignment: Subjects are randomly assigned to treatment/control groups to balance confounding variables.  • Replication: Using enough subjects for reliable results.  • Blinding: Single-blind (subject doesn't know), double-blind (neither subject nor researcher knows).  • Confounding variable: A variable that influences both the explanatory and response variables, making it hard to determine the true cause.

--- Lesson 6.4: Randomization & Control Groups (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Randomization & Control Groups
Randomization & Control Groups
Randomization and control groups are the twin pillars of good experimental design.
  • Randomization: Assigning subjects to groups using a random process ensures that differences between groups are due to chance, not systematic factors.  • Why randomize? Balances both known and unknown confounding variables across groups. Without randomization, groups may differ in ways that affect the outcome.  • Control group: The group that does NOT receive the treatment, providing a baseline for comparison. May receive a placebo or standard treatment.  • Types of control:  • Placebo control: receives an inactive look-alike treatment.  • Active control: receives the current standard treatment (common in medical trials).  • No-treatment control: receives nothing (when ethically appropriate).  • Statistical significance compares the observed effect to what could be expected by random chance alone.

--- Lesson 6.5: Simulation Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Simulation Models
Simulation Models
Simulation uses random number generation to model real-world processes and estimate probabilities or outcomes that are difficult to compute analytically.
  • Steps: (1) Define the model and assumptions. (2) Generate random outcomes using a random number generator. (3) Repeat many times (trials). (4) Analyze the distribution of results.  • Monte Carlo simulation: A general method using repeated random sampling. Named after casino randomness.  • Applications: Estimating probabilities, modeling financial risk, testing statistical methods, understanding complex systems.  • Law of Large Numbers: As the number of simulation trials increases, the estimated probability approaches the true probability.  • Random number tables and generators: Sources of random digits used to simulate chance processes.
Simulations provide approximate answers when exact calculation is impractical.

--- Lesson 6.7: Ethics in Data Collection (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ethics in Data Collection
Ethics in Data Collection
Ethical considerations are fundamental to conducting responsible statistical research.
  • Informed consent: Participants must understand the study's purpose, procedures, risks, and benefits before voluntarily agreeing to participate.  • Privacy and confidentiality: Personal data must be protected. Researchers should anonymize data when possible.  • Institutional Review Board (IRB): A committee that reviews research involving human subjects to ensure ethical standards are met.  • Do no harm: Research should minimize physical and psychological risks to participants.  • Data integrity: Researchers must report data honestly — fabrication, falsification, and selective reporting are serious ethical violations.  • Historical violations: The Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932–1972) denied treatment to Black men without consent — a landmark case leading to modern ethical guidelines.  • Vulnerable populations: Children, prisoners, and others with limited autonomy require extra protections.
