--- Lesson 6.1: Sampling Methods (systematic, stratified, cluster) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sampling Methods (systematic, stratified, cluster)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a sampling frame?
A sampling frame is the complete list of population members that are eligible for selection; ideally it matches the entire target population.
What is a voluntary response sample?
In a voluntary response sample, people choose whether to respond, which tends to over-represent those with strong opinions.
What is the sampling interval in systematic sampling?
The sampling interval k is calculated by dividing the population size N by the desired sample size n, and then every k-th individual is selected.
What is meant by a 'representative sample'?
A representative sample accurately reflects the demographics and attributes of the population, allowing generalizations to be made.
What is proportionate stratified sampling?
In proportionate stratified sampling, each stratum contributes to the sample in proportion to its size in the overall population.
What is a census?
A census attempts to gather information from every individual in the entire population rather than from a sample.
A city has 200 apartment buildings. A researcher randomly selects 10 buildings and surveys every tenant in those buildings. She finds that average rent in the sample is higher than the city average. What is the most likely explanation?
Cluster sampling can produce estimates that differ from the population value because entire clusters may be more or less expensive; sampling variability between clusters is a known limitation.
A population of 10,000 is listed alphabetically. A systematic sample with k = 50 and a random start of 23 would include individuals 23, 73, 123, ... What is the total sample size?
The sample size is N/k = 10,000/50 = 200. Starting at 23, the sequence is 23, 73, 123, ... up to 9,973, giving exactly 200 individuals.
Real-World Applications
Example: Selecting every 10th person on a list is an example of:
Using a fixed interval (every 10th person) after a random start is the hallmark of systematic sampling.
Example: Multi-stage sampling:
Multi-stage sampling applies different sampling techniques at each level, such as first selecting clusters and then taking an SRS within each cluster.
Example: What does undercoverage mean in sampling?
Undercoverage occurs when certain groups in the population are not represented in the sampling frame, leading to potential bias.
Example: A health agency wants to study nutrition habits across a large country. They randomly select 20 towns and survey every household in those towns. What sampling method is being used?
The towns are naturally occurring clusters; randomly selecting entire towns and surveying all households within them is cluster sampling.
Example: A city has 200 apartment buildings. A researcher randomly selects 10 buildings and surveys every tenant in those buildings. She finds that average rent in the sample is higher than the city average. What is the most likely explanation?
Cluster sampling can produce estimates that differ from the population value because entire clusters may be more or less expensive; sampling variability between clusters is a known limitation.

    [ ] A probability of 0

  Q2. Stratified sampling divides the population into:
    [ ] Paired samples

    [ ] Random groups

    Explanation: Strata are subgroups whose members share similar characteristics, and a random sample is then drawn from each stratum.

  Q3. In cluster sampling, you:
    [ ] Sample one person per group
    [ ] Divide into similar groups
    [ ] Select the first available person
    [*] Randomly select whole clusters and sample everyone in them
    Explanation: Cluster sampling randomly picks entire groups (clusters), and every member within those selected clusters is included in the sample.

  Q4. Systematic sampling selects:
    [ ] The first n individuals on the list
    [ ] Only outliers in the population
    [ ] Random individuals without a pattern
    [*] Every k-th individual from a list after a random start
    Explanation: A random starting point is chosen, and then every k-th individual is selected, where k equals the population size divided by the desired sample size.

  Q5. Convenience sampling is problematic because it:
    [ ] Is too expensive
    [ ] Produces too much data
    [ ] Requires stratification
    [*] Introduces selection bias
    Explanation: Convenience samples over-represent easily accessible individuals and systematically exclude hard-to-reach parts of the population.

  Q6. Which sampling method ensures representation of key subgroups?
    [ ] Simple random sampling
    [*] Stratified sampling
    [ ] Voluntary response sampling
    [ ] Convenience sampling
    Explanation: Because a random sample is drawn from each stratum, stratified sampling guarantees that every important subgroup is represented.

  Q7. Cluster sampling is most cost-effective when the population is:
    [ ] Small and accessible

    [*] Geographically spread out
    [ ] Completely homogeneous
    Explanation: Sampling entire clusters in a few locations reduces travel and administrative costs when the population is widely dispersed.

  Q8. Selecting every 10th person on a list is an example of:

  Q9. Which of the following is NOT a probability sampling method?

  Q10. Multi-stage sampling:
    [*] Combines multiple sampling methods in successive stages
    [ ] Uses only one method throughout
    [ ] Is the same as SRS
    [ ] Is always biased
    Explanation: Multi-stage sampling applies different sampling techniques at each level, such as first selecting clusters and then taking an SRS within each cluster.

    [ ] A perfectly representative sample

--- Lesson 6.2: Bias in Sampling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bias in Sampling
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is voluntary response bias?
In voluntary response surveys, individuals self-select to participate, and those with intense feelings are overrepresented.
What is social desirability bias?
Social desirability bias occurs when people over-report positive behaviors and under-report negative ones to appear more favorable.
What is interviewer bias?
The interviewer's demeanor, phrasing, or personal characteristics can subtly influence how subjects respond.
What is survivorship bias?
Survivorship bias occurs when only successes or survivors are studied, leading to overly optimistic conclusions because failures are invisible.
What is undercoverage bias?
Undercoverage means the sampling frame does not include certain segments of the population, so their views are systematically excluded.
What is recall bias?
Recall bias occurs when subjects cannot accurately remember past experiences, often systematically over- or under-reporting certain behaviors.
What is the difference between bias and variability?
Bias consistently pushes results away from the truth, while variability describes how spread out repeated estimates are; ideally both are minimized.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
With only a 15% response rate and younger, less-supportive people not responding, the sample over-represents supporters, so 60% is likely an overestimate.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is undercoverage bias?
Undercoverage means the sampling frame does not include certain segments of the population, so their views are systematically excluded.
Example: A phone survey about internet usage only uses landline numbers. Critics point out that younger adults who use only cell phones are excluded. What type of bias does this illustrate?
Cell-phone-only households are systematically excluded from the sampling frame, which is undercoverage -- a form of selection bias.

--- Lesson 6.3: Designing Surveys & Experiments (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Designing Surveys & Experiments
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a confounding variable?
A confounding variable is associated with both the explanatory and response variables, making it difficult to determine which variable is truly responsible for the observed effect.
What is the response variable in an experiment?
The response variable (dependent variable) is the outcome measured to determine whether the explanatory variable had an effect.
What is meant by 'blinding' in an experiment?
Blinding prevents expectations from influencing the results by hiding group assignments from subjects, researchers, or both.
What is a treatment group?
The treatment group receives the active drug, intervention, or condition being tested in the experiment.
What is the meaning of 'statistical significance'?
Statistical significance indicates that the observed effect is large enough that it is unlikely to be due to random chance, typically evaluated using a p-value threshold.
A researcher notices that students who eat breakfast tend to have higher GPAs than those who skip breakfast. She concludes that eating breakfast causes better academic performance. What is the main flaw in this reasoning?
Without random assignment, confounding variables such as household income, parental involvement, or sleep habits could explain the observed association between breakfast and GPA.
A city health department surveys 5,000 residents and finds that people who live near parks exercise more and have lower rates of heart disease. A newspaper headline reads: 'Living near parks prevents heart disease.' What is wrong with this headline?
This is an observational study, so confounders such as income, lifestyle, and access to healthcare could explain the association. Only a randomized experiment could support a causal claim.
Real-World Applications
Example: A pharmaceutical company wants to test whether a new migraine medication reduces headache duration. They recruit 200 migraine sufferers and randomly assign 100 to receive the medication and 100 to receive a sugar pill. Neither the patients nor the doctors know who receives which pill. After 8 weeks, the average headache duration is 2.1 hours shorter in the medication group. What type of study design is this?
This is a double-blind randomized controlled experiment because subjects are randomly assigned to treatment and control groups, a placebo is used, and neither patients nor doctors know the assignments.
Example: A school principal wants to test whether a new after-school tutoring program improves math scores. She has 60 students volunteer and lets them choose whether to attend tutoring or not. Students who attend tutoring score 15 points higher on average. Can she conclude the tutoring caused the improvement?
Self-selection means more motivated students may have chosen tutoring, and their motivation (not the tutoring itself) could explain the higher scores. Random assignment would be needed for a causal claim.
Example: A fitness company claims their new protein shake builds muscle faster. They recruit 40 gym members and randomly assign 20 to drink the shake daily and 20 to drink a similar-tasting placebo shake. After 3 months, the protein shake group gains an average of 3.2 pounds of muscle while the placebo group gains 2.9 pounds. The difference is not statistically significant (p = 0.42). What should you conclude?

--- Lesson 6.4: Randomization & Control Groups (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Randomization & Control Groups
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a control group?
A control group does not receive the active treatment and serves as a comparison baseline, showing what happens in the absence of the treatment.
What is a completely randomized design?
In a completely randomized design, every subject has an equal chance of being assigned to any group, without any preliminary blocking or stratification.
What is an 'active control' in a clinical trial?
An active control receives an existing effective treatment, allowing researchers to compare the new treatment against the current standard of care.
What is the difference between random sampling and random assignment?
Random sampling determines who is in the study (generalizability), while random assignment determines who gets which treatment (causation).
What is meant by 'ethical concerns with control groups'?
When effective treatments exist, denying them to a control group raises ethical concerns, especially for serious or life-threatening conditions.
What is a 'placebo-controlled trial'?
A placebo-controlled trial compares the active treatment against a placebo (inactive substance), allowing researchers to distinguish genuine treatment effects from psychological responses.
A gym offers a free trial of a new workout program. Of the 300 members who sign up, 200 attend regularly and 100 drop out early. The 200 who stayed show significant fitness improvements. The gym advertises: 'Our program improves fitness in 100% of participants!' What is the problem with this claim?
By ignoring the 100 dropouts, the gym only reports results from the most committed members. This survivorship bias and self-selection make the claim misleading.
Real-World Applications
Example: A researcher wants to test the effect of study music on exam performance. She has 100 students from two dorms: 50 freshmen in Dorm A and 50 seniors in Dorm B. She randomly assigns half the freshmen to music and half to silence, and does the same for seniors. What advantage does this approach have over a completely randomized design?
By blocking on class year, the researcher controls for potential differences in academic experience between freshmen and seniors, reducing variability and increasing the power to detect a music effect.
Example: An education researcher randomly assigns 200 students to either a traditional lecture format or a flipped classroom format. At the end of the semester, the flipped classroom group scores 8 points higher on the final exam (p = 0.003). Can the researcher conclude that the flipped classroom caused the higher scores?
Because students were randomly assigned to groups and the result is statistically significant (p = 0.003), the researcher can reasonably conclude that the flipped classroom format caused the improvement in scores.
Example: A cereal company claims that children who eat their cereal every morning score higher on standardized tests. They surveyed 10,000 families and found a positive association. A statistician points out that this study lacks random assignment. What does this mean for the company's claim?
Without random assignment, confounders like family income, parental education, and access to resources could explain the association. A large sample does not compensate for lack of random assignment.

--- Lesson 6.5: Simulation Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Simulation Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a Monte Carlo simulation?
Monte Carlo simulation uses repeated random sampling to model complex systems and estimate probabilities, named after the famous casino in Monaco to evoke the idea of randomness.
What is a 'trial' in a simulation?
A trial is one execution of the random process being modeled, producing a single outcome. Many trials are combined to estimate probabilities.
What is the 'law of large numbers' as it relates to simulation?
The law of large numbers states that as the number of independent trials increases, the sample mean approaches the true expected value, which is why simulations become more accurate with more trials.
What is 'standard error' in the context of simulation estimates?
Standard error measures how much the simulation estimate varies from the true value. It decreases proportionally to the square root of the number of trials.
What is a 'what-if scenario' in simulation?
What-if scenarios allow researchers to explore hypothetical situations by changing input parameters and observing how outcomes are affected, without real-world costs or risks.
A basketball player makes 70% of her free throws. Her coach wants to estimate the probability that she makes at least 8 out of 10 free throws in a game. Using a random number generator with digits 0-9 (where 0-6 represent a make and 7-9 represent a miss), the coach runs 1,000 simulated games. In 383 of them, she makes 8 or more. What is the estimated probability?
The estimated probability is 383/1000 = 0.383. The simulation assigns digits 0-6 (7 out of 10 digits = 70%) to makes and runs 1,000 trials to approximate the probability.
A restaurant manager wants to know the probability that at least 2 of 5 randomly selected customers order dessert, given that 30% of all customers do. She runs a simulation with 5,000 trials using random digits 0-9 (0-2 = dessert, 3-9 = no dessert). In 2,370 trials, at least 2 customers ordered dessert. What is the estimated probability, and would more trials likely improve the estimate?
The estimated probability is 2370/5000 = 0.474. More trials would further reduce the variability of the estimate, making it closer to the true theoretical value.
A scientist wants to estimate the value of pi using simulation. She generates random (x, y) coordinates where both x and y are between 0 and 1. She counts the proportion of points that fall inside a quarter circle of radius 1 (where x^2 + y^2 <= 1). After 50,000 points, 39,280 fall inside. What is her estimate of pi?
The ratio of points inside the quarter circle to total points approximates pi/4. So pi is approximately 4 x (39280/50000) = 4 x 0.7856 = 3.1424.
A teacher wants to demonstrate the birthday paradox. She simulates random groups of 23 people and checks whether at least two share a birthday. After 10,000 simulated groups, 5,073 have at least one shared birthday. What is the estimated probability, and how does it compare to the theoretical answer of about 50.7%?
The simulation estimate is 5073/10000 = 50.73%, which closely matches the theoretical probability of approximately 50.7% for 23 people sharing a birthday.
A manufacturing company simulates their assembly line to predict defect rates. They model each of 5 quality checkpoints as independent, each catching 95% of defects. After 100,000 simulated products, only 3 defective products pass all 5 checkpoints. What is the simulated probability that a defective product passes all checkpoints?
The simulated probability is 3/100,000 = 0.00003. Theoretically, the probability of passing all 5 independent checkpoints is 0.05^5 = 0.0000003125, but the simulation with finite trials gives a close approximation.

The estimated probability of overbooking is 4250/50000 = 0.085 or 8.5%. The airline can use this to weigh the revenue from extra tickets against the cost of bumping passengers.
A student is told to simulate the probability of getting exactly 3 heads in 5 fair coin flips. She uses random digits 0-9 from a table, letting even digits (0, 2, 4, 6, 8) represent heads and odd digits (1, 3, 5, 7, 9) represent tails. After 2,000 trials, she gets exactly 3 heads in 621 of them. What is her estimate, and how does it compare to the theoretical value of 10/32 = 0.3125?
Her estimate is 621/2000 = 0.3105, which is close to the theoretical probability of C(5,3)/2^5 = 10/32 = 0.3125. The small difference is due to random variation in a finite number of trials.

--- Lesson 6.6: Case Studies in Political Polling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Political Polling
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'push poll'?
Push polls are not real surveys; they are campaign tools designed to plant negative information about an opponent under the guise of asking survey questions.
What is a 'tracking poll'?
Tracking polls survey at regular intervals (often daily or weekly) to follow trends and detect shifts in public opinion over time.
What is 'selection bias' in polling?
Selection bias occurs when the sampling method systematically includes or excludes certain types of people, making the sample unrepresentative of the population.
What is a 'likely voter screen'?
A likely voter screen uses questions about past voting behavior, interest in the election, and intention to vote to filter respondents and focus on those most likely to actually cast ballots.
An online news website posts a poll asking readers 'Do you support the new highway project?' Of 50,000 responses, 78% say no. The site reports that 'the vast majority of citizens oppose the project.' What is the primary problem with this conclusion?
Voluntary response polls attract people with strong opinions, typically opponents of a proposal. The 50,000 respondents self-selected, making the results unrepresentative of the general population.
In the weeks before an election, five different polls show Candidate X with support of 42%, 45%, 44%, 43%, and 46%. A poll aggregator averages these to get 44%. What is the primary advantage of this aggregation approach?
Aggregating multiple polls averages out the random sampling error in each individual poll, producing a more stable estimate. However, if all polls share a common bias, aggregation will not eliminate it.
During a presidential election, a tracking poll surveys 500 new voters each night and reports a 3-day rolling average. On Monday through Wednesday, the results are 48%, 50%, and 49%, giving a rolling average of 49%. On Thursday, the new result is 53%. What is the new 3-day rolling average (Tuesday-Thursday)?
The 3-day rolling average for Tuesday through Thursday is (50 + 49 + 53) / 3 = 152 / 3 = 50.67%. Rolling averages smooth out day-to-day variability while still capturing trends.

    Explanation: Selection bias occurs when the sampling method systematically includes or excludes certain types of people, making the sample unrepresentative of the population.

  Q18. What does 'margin of error' mean in a poll?
    [ ] The amount of money spent on the poll
    [*] The range within which the true population value is likely to fall, based on sampling variability
    [ ] The percentage of respondents who gave wrong answers
    [ ] The difference between two competing candidates
    Explanation: The margin of error quantifies the uncertainty in a poll estimate due to random sampling. It creates an interval around the reported value within which the true population value likely falls.

  Q19. What is a 'likely voter screen'?
    [ ] A method to prevent fraud in elections
    [ ] A way to increase the sample size of a poll
    [*] A set of questions used to identify respondents who are most likely to actually vote in the election
    [ ] A physical screen placed at voting locations
    Explanation: A likely voter screen uses questions about past voting behavior, interest in the election, and intention to vote to filter respondents and focus on those most likely to actually cast ballots.

  Q20. What does 'poll aggregation' mean?
    [ ] Discarding all polls except the most recent one
    [ ] Polling the same people multiple times
    [ ] Running one very large poll instead of many small ones
    [*] Combining results from multiple polls to create a more stable and accurate estimate
    Explanation: Poll aggregation (used by organizations like FiveThirtyEight and RealClearPolitics) combines data from many polls, reducing the impact of individual poll errors and producing a more reliable estimate.

  Q21. A polling firm surveys 1,200 likely voters and finds Candidate A at 48% and Candidate B at 46%, with a margin of error of plus or minus 3%. A news anchor says Candidate A is 'in the lead.' Is this claim statistically justified?
    [ ] Yes, because 1,200 respondents is a large sample
    [ ] Yes, because 48% is greater than 46%
    [ ] No, because polls are never accurate
    [*] No, because the 2-point difference is within the margin of error, making the race a statistical tie
    Explanation: With a margin of error of plus or minus 3%, both candidates' true support could range from 45% to 51% (for A) and 43% to 49% (for B). Since these ranges overlap substantially, the race is too close to call.

  Q22. An online news website posts a poll asking readers 'Do you support the new highway project?' Of 50,000 responses, 78% say no. The site reports that 'the vast majority of citizens oppose the project.' What is the primary problem with this conclusion?
    [ ] Online polls are always 100% accurate when the sample size exceeds ten thousand respondents
    [ ] The margin of error was not reported alongside the percentage, making the result hard to interpret
    [ ] The sample size is too small to make reliable inferences about the general population's preferences
    [*] The poll uses voluntary response, so only people with strong opinions (likely opponents) participated
    Explanation: Voluntary response polls attract people with strong opinions, typically opponents of a proposal. The 50,000 respondents self-selected, making the results unrepresentative of the general population.

  Q23. Two polls are conducted in the same week for a senate race. Poll A (n = 800) shows Candidate X at 51%, and Poll B (n = 1,500) shows Candidate X at 47%. Why might an aggregation model give more weight to Poll B?
    [ ] Because Poll A used better questions
    [*] Because Poll B has a larger sample size and therefore a smaller margin of error
    [ ] Because Poll B's result is lower
    [ ] Because aggregation models always prefer the most recent poll
    Explanation: A larger sample size produces a smaller margin of error, making the estimate more precise. Aggregation models typically weight polls by factors including sample size, methodology, and recency.

  Q24. A polling organization calls 10,000 landline phone numbers but only 600 people complete the survey (a 6% response rate). The results show strong support for a conservative candidate. A critic argues the poll is biased. Why might the critic be correct?
    [*] Landline-only sampling misses younger and more mobile demographics, and the 6% response rate introduces nonresponse bias
    [ ] 600 people is too few to draw any conclusions
    [ ] The poll should have called more numbers
    [ ] Conservative candidates always poll higher on landlines for no identifiable reason
    Explanation: Landline-only sampling systematically excludes younger, more urban, and cell-phone-only households. Combined with a very low response rate, the sample likely overrepresents older, more conservative voters.

  Q25. Before a city referendum, a local newspaper polls 1,000 residents using random digit dialing and finds 55% support with a margin of error of plus or minus 3.1%. A neighborhood association conducts its own informal survey of 200 members and finds only 30% support. Which result is more reliable for predicting the city-wide outcome, and why?
    [*] The newspaper poll, because it uses random sampling from the entire city and has a larger sample
    [ ] Both are equally reliable
    [ ] Neither is reliable because polls cannot predict referendums
    [ ] The neighborhood survey, because it has a stronger response
    Explanation: The newspaper poll uses random digit dialing to reach a representative sample of the entire city, while the neighborhood survey only captures one specific area's opinion, which may not reflect city-wide sentiment.

  Q26. A polling firm finds that Candidate A leads Candidate B by 6 points in a poll with a margin of error of plus or minus 4%. Can we be reasonably confident that Candidate A is actually ahead?
    [ ] No, because the margin of error is too large for any conclusion
    [ ] No, because margins of error apply only to the winner
    [ ] Yes, but only if the poll used a sample of at least 10,000
    [*] Yes, because the 6-point lead exceeds the margin of error of 4%, suggesting a real lead
    Explanation: When the lead (6 points) exceeds the margin of error (4 points), it suggests the lead is real and not just due to sampling variability. However, other sources of error could still affect the result.

  Q27. In the weeks before an election, five different polls show Candidate X with support of 42%, 45%, 44%, 43%, and 46%. A poll aggregator averages these to get 44%. What is the primary advantage of this aggregation approach?
    [ ] It always produces the exact true value
    [ ] It eliminates all sources of bias in the individual polls
    [ ] It makes the margin of error zero
    [*] It reduces random sampling variability by combining information from multiple independent polls
    Explanation: Aggregating multiple polls averages out the random sampling error in each individual poll, producing a more stable estimate. However, if all polls share a common bias, aggregation will not eliminate it.

  Q28. A state polls voters about a proposed tax increase. The poll question reads: 'Do you support the irresponsible plan to raise taxes on hardworking families?' The result shows 75% opposition. What type of polling problem does this illustrate?
    [ ] Nonresponse bias
    [ ] Voluntary response bias
    [ ] Undercoverage bias
    [*] Question wording bias (loaded or leading question)
    Explanation: The question uses loaded language ('irresponsible,' 'hardworking families') that pushes respondents toward opposition. A neutral question would produce more valid results.

  Q29. A national poll of 1,500 adults shows that 60% support stricter environmental regulations. However, the pollster notes that the response rate was only 8%. A statistician calculates that even with weighting adjustments, the effective sample may behave like a smaller sample. Why is the low response rate concerning despite the seemingly adequate 1,500 respondents?
    [ ] Because low response rates automatically invalidate any poll
    [*] Because the 92% who did not respond may hold different views, introducing nonresponse bias that cannot be fully corrected by weighting
    [ ] Because 1,500 is always too small for a national poll
    [ ] Because the margin of error is unaffected by response rates
    Explanation: When 92% of contacted people refuse to participate, those who do respond may be systematically different from those who do not. Weighting can only partially compensate if the nonresponse is related to the topic being measured.

  Q30. During a presidential election, a tracking poll surveys 500 new voters each night and reports a 3-day rolling average. On Monday through Wednesday, the results are 48%, 50%, and 49%, giving a rolling average of 49%. On Thursday, the new result is 53%. What is the new 3-day rolling average (Tuesday-Thursday)?
    [ ] 53.0%
    [ ] 49.0%
    [ ] 50.0%
    [*] 50.67%
    Explanation: The 3-day rolling average for Tuesday through Thursday is (50 + 49 + 53) / 3 = 152 / 3 = 50.67%. Rolling averages smooth out day-to-day variability while still capturing trends.

--- Lesson 6.7: Ethics in Data Collection (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ethics in Data Collection
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'conflict of interest' in research?
Conflicts of interest arise when researchers have financial ties, personal relationships, or other interests that could consciously or unconsciously bias their research.
Real-World Applications
Example: The Tuskegee Syphilis Study is an example of:
The Tuskegee study withheld effective treatment from African American men with syphilis for decades without informed consent, leading to the Belmont Report and modern ethical standards.
Example: A medical study in a developing country tests a new malaria treatment by giving the experimental drug to local villagers and comparing their outcomes to untreated villagers. The same study design would never be approved in the researchers' home country because a standard treatment exists. What ethical principle from the Belmont Report is being violated?
The principle of justice requires that research burdens and benefits be distributed fairly. Conducting a study in a developing country that would be considered unethical at home exploits a vulnerable population.

--- Lesson 6.7: Ethics in Data Collection (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Informed consent requires that participants:
    [ ] Know nothing about the study before agreeing
    [ ] Only give consent after the study is complete
    [*] Understand the study's purpose, risks, and procedures before agreeing to participate
    [ ] Are forced to participate by the researcher
    Explanation: Informed consent ensures that participants voluntarily agree after receiving full disclosure about the study's nature, risks, and procedures.

  Q2. An IRB (Institutional Review Board):
    [ ] Selects the sample for the study
    [ ] Publishes the research results
    [*] Reviews and approves research to ensure ethical standards are met
    [ ] Conducts the research itself
    Explanation: IRBs protect human subjects by reviewing study designs before data collection begins to ensure ethical treatment of participants.

  Q3. The Tuskegee Syphilis Study is an example of:
    [*] A severe ethical violation that led to modern research guidelines
    [ ] A study with excellent follow-up procedures
    [ ] Ethical research with proper informed consent
    [ ] A well-designed randomized controlled trial
    Explanation: The Tuskegee study withheld effective treatment from African American men with syphilis for decades without informed consent, leading to the Belmont Report and modern ethical standards.

  Q4. Data fabrication is:
    [ ] The same thing as rounding numbers
    [ ] Acceptable when deadlines are tight
    [*] A serious ethical violation involving inventing data that was never collected
    [ ] Common and accepted practice in research
    Explanation: Fabricating data is scientific fraud that undermines the integrity of research and can have serious real-world consequences.

  Q5. Participants can withdraw from a study:
    [ ] Never, once they have started
    [*] At any time without penalty
    [ ] Only with the researcher's permission
    [ ] Only before the study begins
    Explanation: Voluntary participation includes the absolute right to withdraw at any time without facing any penalty or loss of benefits.

  Q6. Selective reporting (cherry-picking data) is:
    [*] An ethical violation that misrepresents findings
    [ ] Required for peer-reviewed publications
    [ ] The same as thorough data analysis
    [ ] Good scientific practice
    Explanation: Reporting only favorable results while hiding unfavorable ones distorts the true findings and misleads other researchers and the public.

  Q7. Deception in research:
    [*] May be used in limited cases with IRB approval and must be followed by debriefing
    [ ] Is always acceptable in any study
    [ ] Requires no ethical review whatsoever
    [ ] Is never used under any circumstances
    Explanation: Deception must be justified as necessary, approved by an IRB, involve minimal risk, and always be followed by thorough debriefing of participants.

  Q8. The Belmont Report established:
    [ ] Statistical formulas for hypothesis testing
    [*] Ethical principles for research with human subjects: respect, beneficence, and justice
    [ ] Standards for manufacturing quality control charts
    [ ] Methods for stratified random sampling
    Explanation: The Belmont Report (1979) laid out three fundamental ethical principles: respect for persons, beneficence (minimizing harm), and justice (fair distribution of research burdens and benefits).

  Q9. P-hacking (trying many analyses until finding significance) is:
    [ ] Required by most IRBs
    [ ] Always acceptable if the sample is large
    [ ] A thorough approach to data analysis
    [*] An ethical and methodological problem that inflates false positives
    Explanation: P-hacking exploits random noise by testing many hypotheses until one produces a significant result, dramatically increasing the false positive rate.

  Q10. Ethical research ultimately:
    [*] Protects participants and enhances the credibility of science
    [ ] Is unnecessary in modern times
    [ ] Only applies to medical research
    [ ] Slows scientific progress unnecessarily
    Explanation: Ethical standards protect research participants from harm and maintain public trust in the scientific process.

  Q11. What does 'confidentiality' mean in research?
    [*] Protecting participants' identities and personal information from unauthorized disclosure
    [ ] Hiding the research results from the public
    [ ] Sharing data publicly with participants' full names
    [ ] Not collecting any data at all
    Explanation: Confidentiality means that researchers safeguard participants' personal information and do not reveal individual identities in publications or reports.

  Q12. What does 'anonymizing data' mean?
    [ ] Collecting and storing participants' names with their data
    [ ] Publishing all personal details for transparency
    [ ] Using only pseudonyms without removing other identifying details
    [*] Removing all identifying information so participants cannot be traced back to their responses
    Explanation: Anonymization removes names, addresses, and all other identifying information so that individual participants cannot be connected to their data.

  Q13. What are 'vulnerable populations' in research ethics?
    [ ] Only healthy adult volunteers who meet strict eligibility criteria for the specific study protocol
    [ ] No specific group requires extra protection because all participants have equal rights under federal research law
    [*] Groups with limited autonomy (such as children, prisoners, and cognitively impaired individuals) who need extra protections
    [ ] Only researchers who conduct studies and therefore face professional risks from negative research outcomes
    Explanation: Vulnerable populations include groups that may have reduced ability to provide fully voluntary informed consent, requiring additional safeguards in research.

  Q14. What does 'beneficence' mean as a research ethics principle?
    [ ] Researchers should maximize their own benefits from the study
    [ ] All research must generate financial profit
    [ ] Participants should pay for the cost of research
    [*] Researchers should minimize harm and maximize potential benefits to participants
    Explanation: Beneficence requires researchers to protect participants' well-being by minimizing risks and maximizing the potential benefits of the research.

  Q15. What does 'justice' mean in the Belmont Report?
    [ ] Minorities should be excluded from research for their protection
    [ ] Placebos must always be used in clinical trials
    [*] The benefits and burdens of research are distributed fairly across groups
    [ ] Only wealthy populations should be studied
    Explanation: The principle of justice requires that no group bears a disproportionate share of research risks, and that the benefits of research are available to all groups.

  Q16. What does 'debriefing' mean in research?
    [*] Explaining the true nature and purpose of the study to participants after their participation
    [ ] Concealing the true purpose of the study permanently
    [ ] Paying participants additional compensation
    [ ] Asking participants to leave the study immediately
    Explanation: Debriefing occurs after participation and ensures that participants understand the true purpose of the study, especially important when any deception was used.

  Q17. What is a 'conflict of interest' in research?
    [*] A situation where a researcher's personal or financial interests could bias the study results
    [ ] A disagreement between researchers about methods
    [ ] A required component of all research proposals
    [ ] A design that improves objectivity
    Explanation: Conflicts of interest arise when researchers have financial ties, personal relationships, or other interests that could consciously or unconsciously bias their research.

  Q18. What does 'reproducibility' mean in science?
    [*] Sharing methods, data, and procedures so others can independently verify results
    [ ] Destroying raw data after analysis is complete
    [ ] Keeping all methods secret from other researchers
    [ ] Never publishing research findings
    Explanation: Reproducibility requires transparent reporting of methods and data so that other researchers can repeat the study and verify the findings independently.

  Q19. What does 'data falsification' mean?
    [ ] Reporting all results, including negative findings
    [ ] Rounding numbers to appropriate decimal places
    [ ] Collecting data using proper scientific methods
    [*] Manipulating or altering actual research data to produce desired results
    Explanation: Data falsification involves changing or omitting data points to make results appear more favorable or significant, which is a form of scientific misconduct.

  Q20. What does 'respect for persons' mean in the Belmont Report?
    [ ] Researchers should always agree with participants' opinions
    [ ] All participants must be treated identically regardless of circumstances
    [ ] Researchers should be respected by their institutions
    [*] Individuals should be treated as autonomous agents and those with diminished autonomy deserve extra protection
    Explanation: Respect for persons means acknowledging individuals' right to make their own informed decisions about participation, and providing additional protections for those with diminished autonomy.

  Q21. A pharmaceutical company funds a study on its own drug and finds it is 30% more effective than competitors. However, the lead researcher owns $500,000 in company stock. An independent replication finds only a 5% difference. What ethical issue does this scenario illustrate?
    [ ] There is no ethical concern as long as the study was published
    [*] A conflict of interest where the researcher's financial stake may have biased the original results
    [ ] The independent study must be wrong because it is smaller
    [ ] The company properly disclosed the conflict
    Explanation: The researcher's significant financial interest in the company creates a conflict of interest that may have consciously or unconsciously influenced the study design, analysis, or reporting of results.

  Q22. A university psychology department wants to study how people respond to stress. They plan to tell participants they are taking a simple memory test, but will actually subject them to unexpected loud noises and time pressure to induce stress. Participants will be debriefed afterward. What ethical requirement must be met before this study can proceed?
    [ ] No requirements, since participants will eventually be debriefed
    [ ] Only the department chair needs to approve
    [ ] The study can proceed as long as participants sign a general consent form
    [*] IRB approval is required because the study uses deception and could cause psychological distress
    Explanation: Studies involving deception and potential psychological stress require IRB review to ensure the deception is justified, risks are minimized, and proper debriefing procedures are in place.

  Q23. A health researcher collects blood samples from 500 patients for a diabetes study. Five years later, she wants to use the stored samples to study a different genetic condition without contacting the patients for new consent. Is this ethically acceptable?
    [*] No, because using samples for a different purpose than originally consented to violates informed consent principles
    [ ] Yes, because five years is long enough to reuse any data
    [ ] Yes, as long as the new study is also about health
    [ ] Yes, because she already has the samples
    Explanation: Informed consent is specific to the study described to participants. Using samples for a different purpose without obtaining new consent or having a broad consent provision violates the principle of respect for persons.

  Q24. A researcher studying income inequality collects survey data including names, addresses, incomes, and social security numbers. The data is stored on an unencrypted laptop that is later stolen. Which ethical principle was most directly violated?
    [ ] Informed consent only
    [*] Confidentiality and data security obligations
    [ ] The principle of beneficence
    [ ] Justice
    Explanation: Researchers have an ethical obligation to protect participants' personal information through proper data security measures. Storing sensitive data unencrypted is a serious breach of confidentiality.

  Q25. A social media company uses data from its 2 billion users to conduct behavioral experiments without users' knowledge, changing what content they see to measure effects on mood. Users agreed to a general terms of service but were not specifically informed about the experiments. Is this ethically problematic?
    [*] Yes, because specific informed consent for experimentation was not obtained, and the experiment could cause psychological harm
    [ ] No, because users agreed to the terms of service
    [ ] No, because companies are exempt from research ethics
    [ ] Yes, but only if the company is publicly traded
    Explanation: General terms of service do not constitute informed consent for specific experiments, especially those that may affect participants' emotional well-being. This mirrors concerns raised by Facebook's 2014 emotional contagion study.

  Q26. A graduate student is running behind on her dissertation deadline. She has collected data from 180 of her planned 200 participants. Rather than recruit more participants, she fabricates data for 20 fictional participants with results similar to her existing data. What ethical violations has she committed?
    [*] Data fabrication, which is scientific fraud regardless of whether the fake data matches real patterns
    [ ] None, because the fabricated data is similar to the real data
    [ ] Only a minor procedural violation
    [ ] An ethical violation only if she gets caught
    Explanation: Fabricating data is scientific fraud regardless of the reason or how plausible the fake data appears. It undermines the integrity of the entire research enterprise and can lead to retraction, loss of credentials, and legal consequences.

  Q27. A clinical trial for a new cancer drug shows the drug is significantly extending lives compared to the placebo group. The trial is scheduled to run for two more years. An independent data safety monitoring board recommends stopping the trial early. Why would they make this recommendation?
    [ ] To prevent the drug from being approved by regulatory agencies before all required phases are completed
    [ ] To save money on the trial since the remaining two years of funding could be redirected to other research
    [ ] Because two more years is too long for any study and diminishing returns make extended data collection unnecessary
    [*] Because continuing to give placebos when the treatment is clearly effective would be unethical (denying effective treatment to the control group)
    Explanation: The principle of beneficence requires that once a treatment is clearly shown to be effective, it would be unethical to continue denying it to the control group. Data safety boards can recommend early termination.

  Q28. A researcher conducts 20 different statistical tests on the same dataset and finds one significant result (p = 0.04). She publishes only this one finding without mentioning the other 19 tests. What ethical and statistical problems does this present?
    [ ] The only problem is that she should have used a stricter threshold such as p = 0.01 to account for multiple comparisons
    [ ] This is standard practice in all scientific fields, since researchers routinely publish only their strongest individual findings
    [ ] No problem, because p = 0.04 is below the standard alpha level of 0.05 and therefore qualifies as statistically significant
    [*] P-hacking and selective reporting: with 20 tests, finding one significant result by chance is expected, and hiding the other tests misrepresents the findings
    Explanation: With 20 independent tests at alpha = 0.05, there is approximately a 64% chance of at least one false positive. Reporting only the significant result and hiding the failed tests is both p-hacking and selective reporting.

  Q29. A medical study in a developing country tests a new malaria treatment by giving the experimental drug to local villagers and comparing their outcomes to untreated villagers. The same study design would never be approved in the researchers' home country because a standard treatment exists. What ethical principle from the Belmont Report is being violated?
    [ ] Reproducibility, because the study design cannot be repeated under identical conditions in a different geographic location
    [ ] No principle is violated if the local government approves, since national sovereignty overrides international research guidelines
    [ ] Confidentiality, because villagers in small communities can easily be identified from the published research data
    [*] Justice, because the burdens of research are being placed on a vulnerable population that would not benefit from the research design used in wealthier countries
    Explanation: The principle of justice requires that research burdens and benefits be distributed fairly. Conducting a study in a developing country that would be considered unethical at home exploits a vulnerable population.

  Q30. A technology company collects location data, browsing history, and health app data from users' phones. They anonymize the data by removing names, then sell it to advertisers. A researcher later demonstrates that combining the 'anonymous' datasets can re-identify 87% of individuals. What does this reveal about anonymization?
    [ ] Companies have no obligation to protect user data
    [*] Removing names alone may not be sufficient for true anonymization when other data points can be combined to re-identify individuals
    [ ] Anonymization is always completely effective
    [ ] 87% re-identification is an acceptable rate
    Explanation: Simply removing names does not guarantee anonymity. Combining multiple data points (location patterns, browsing habits, health data) can uniquely identify individuals, demonstrating that true anonymization requires more sophisticated techniques.
