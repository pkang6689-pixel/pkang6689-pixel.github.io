--- Lesson 7.1: Confidence Intervals (mean, proportion) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Confidence Intervals (mean, proportion)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'point estimate'?
A point estimate is a single number (like a sample mean or sample proportion) that serves as our best guess for the population parameter.
What is the 'critical value' in a confidence interval?
The critical value (z* or t*) determines the width of the interval and depends on the confidence level chosen. Higher confidence levels require larger critical values.
What is a 'margin of error' in a confidence interval?
The margin of error equals the critical value times the standard error. It represents the half-width of the confidence interval.
What is a 'sampling distribution'?
A sampling distribution shows all possible values of a statistic and their frequencies when sampling is repeated many times from the same population.
What is the 'Central Limit Theorem' in the context of confidence intervals?
The CLT justifies using normal-based confidence intervals for means, because the sampling distribution of x-bar becomes approximately normal for large n, even if the population is not normal.
A researcher wants her 95% confidence interval for a mean to have a margin of error no larger than 2 units. She knows the population standard deviation is 12. What is the minimum sample size she needs?
n = (z* times sigma / ME)^2 = (1.96 times 12/2)^2 = (11.76)^2 = 138.3. Rounding up gives n = 139.
Real-World Applications
Example: A sample of 100 students has a mean test score of 78 with sigma = 10. The 95% confidence interval is:
The margin of error is 1.96 times (10/sqrt(100)) = 1.96 times 1 = 1.96. So the interval is 78 plus or minus 1.96, giving (76.04, 79.96).
Example: A hospital wants to estimate the average recovery time after a surgery. They sample 25 patients and find a mean of 14.2 days with a sample standard deviation of 3.5 days. Why should they use a t-interval rather than a z-interval?
When the population standard deviation is unknown (as is typical) and especially with a small sample (n = 25), the t-distribution accounts for the additional uncertainty from estimating sigma with s.
Example: A nutritionist surveys 250 adults and finds the average daily calorie intake is 2,150 calories with s = 400. She constructs a 99% confidence interval. Which of the following is closest to her interval?
SE = 400/sqrt(250) = 400/15.81 = 25.3. For 99% CI, z* = 2.576. ME = 2.576 times 25.3 = 65.2. Interval: 2150 plus or minus 65.2 = (2084.8, 2215.2).
Example: An online retailer surveys 1,600 customers and finds that 72% are satisfied with their service. The marketing team reports a 95% confidence interval of (0.698, 0.742). A manager asks: 'Does this mean there is a 95% probability that the true proportion is between 69.8% and 74.2%?' How should you respond?
The true proportion is a fixed (but unknown) value, not a random variable. The 95% confidence level means the procedure works 95% of the time, not that this specific interval has a 95% probability of containing the parameter.
Example: A school district wants to estimate the proportion of students who walk to school. They need a 95% CI with a margin of error no greater than 3 percentage points. With no prior estimate of the proportion, what minimum sample size is needed?
With no prior estimate, use p = 0.5 (worst case). n = (z*/ME)^2 times p(1-p) = (1.96/0.03)^2 times 0.25 = (65.33)^2 times 0.25 = 4268.4 times 0.25 = 1067.1. Round up to 1,068.

--- Lesson 7.2: Margin of Error (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Margin of Error
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'critical value'?
The critical value is the number of standard errors from the center needed to capture the desired percentage of the sampling distribution (e.g., z* = 1.96 for 95%).
What is 'systematic error' (bias)?
Systematic error (bias) pushes estimates consistently in one direction and cannot be fixed by simply increasing sample size, unlike random sampling error.
What is the 'half-width' of a confidence interval?
The half-width of a confidence interval is the margin of error. It is the distance from the point estimate (center) to either the upper or lower bound.
What is the 'worst-case' proportion for planning sample size?
When planning sample size with no prior estimate of the proportion, using p = 0.5 gives the most conservative (largest) sample size needed, ensuring the desired margin of error regardless of the true proportion.
What is the 'finite population correction factor'?
When the sample is more than about 5-10% of the population, the finite population correction factor sqrt((N-n)/(N-1)) reduces the standard error and narrows the confidence interval.
A news report states: 'According to our poll of 1,500 likely voters, the candidate has 47% support with a margin of error of plus or minus 2.5%.' A reader asks whether the candidate could actually have majority support (over 50%). What is the best answer?
The interval is (44.5%, 49.5%), which does not include 50%. Based on this data, majority support is unlikely, though the margin of error only accounts for sampling error, not other systematic issues.
A market research firm surveys 500 consumers about a new product. They find 35% would buy it, with a margin of error of plus or minus 4.2%. Their client asks: 'If we surveyed 2,000 consumers instead, what would the margin of error be?' What is the approximate answer?
Increasing from 500 to 2,000 is a factor of 4. Since ME is proportional to 1/sqrt(n), the new ME is approximately 4.2/sqrt(4) = 4.2/2 = 2.1%.
A 95% confidence interval based on a sample of 100 has a margin of error of 5.0. A colleague argues that using a 90% confidence level instead would give a margin of error of about 4.2. Is she correct, and what is the trade-off?
The ratio of critical values is 1.645/1.96 = 0.839, so the new ME would be about 5.0 times 0.839 = 4.2. The trade-off is that 10% of such intervals (vs. 5%) would miss the true parameter.
A researcher has a budget that allows surveying exactly 600 people. She estimates the population proportion is near 0.3. What is the approximate margin of error for a 95% confidence interval?
SE = sqrt(0.3 times 0.7 / 600) = sqrt(0.00035) = 0.0187. ME = 1.96 times 0.0187 = 0.0367, or approximately 3.7%.
Real-World Applications
Example: To cut the margin of error in half, you must multiply the sample size by:
Since ME is proportional to 1/sqrt(n), halving the ME requires multiplying n by 4 (because 1/sqrt(4n) = 1/(2*sqrt(n))).
Example: A university surveys 400 students and finds that 62% prefer online exams, with a margin of error of plus or minus 4.8% at 95% confidence. The administration wants the margin of error to be no more than plus or minus 2.4%. How many students should they survey?
To halve the margin of error from 4.8% to 2.4%, they need to quadruple the sample size: 400 times 4 = 1,600 students.

--- Lesson 7.3: Hypothesis Testing (null vs. alternative) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hypothesis Testing (null vs. alternative)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'null hypothesis'?
The null hypothesis (H0) is the default position asserting no effect or no difference. Hypothesis testing evaluates whether data provides enough evidence to reject this default.
What is the 'alternative hypothesis'?
The alternative hypothesis (Ha) states that there is an effect, difference, or change. It represents what the researcher suspects or hopes to demonstrate.
What is a 'test statistic'?
The test statistic (z, t, chi-square, etc.) standardizes the difference between the observed sample result and what is expected under H0, allowing comparison to known distributions.
What is a 'one-tailed test'?
A one-tailed test places all the rejection region in one tail (either left or right), used when the researcher has a specific directional alternative hypothesis.
What is a 'two-tailed test'?
A two-tailed test splits the rejection region between both tails of the distribution, detecting differences in either direction (Ha: parameter does not equal null value).
What is the 'rejection region' in a hypothesis test?
The rejection region consists of test statistic values that are extreme enough to lead to rejecting H0. Its size is determined by alpha.
A cereal company claims boxes contain an average of 500 grams. A consumer group suspects the boxes are underfilled. They sample 36 boxes and find a mean of 495 grams with sigma = 15 grams. At alpha = 0.05, test H0: mu = 500 vs. Ha: mu < 500. What is the z-statistic and conclusion?
z = (495 - 500)/(15/sqrt(36)) = -5/2.5 = -2.0. For a left-tailed test at alpha = 0.05, the critical value is -1.645. Since -2.0 < -1.645, we reject H0.
A teacher claims that her new teaching method raises average test scores above the district mean of 72. She tests 25 students and finds a mean of 75 with s = 10. At alpha = 0.05, using a t-test with Ha: mu > 72, what is the test statistic and conclusion?
t = (75 - 72)/(10/sqrt(25)) = 3/2 = 1.5. The critical t-value for df = 24 at alpha = 0.05 one-tailed is approximately 1.711. Since 1.5 < 1.711, we fail to reject H0.
A pharmaceutical company tests whether a new drug changes cholesterol levels from the current population mean of 200 mg/dL. After treating 64 patients, the mean cholesterol is 195 with sigma = 20. They use a two-tailed test at alpha = 0.01. What is the z-statistic, and what is their conclusion?
z = (195 - 200)/(20/sqrt(64)) = -5/2.5 = -2.0. For a two-tailed test at alpha = 0.01, the critical values are plus or minus 2.576. Since |-2.0| < 2.576, we fail to reject H0.
A politician claims that more than 60% of voters support her position. A poll of 500 voters finds 64% support. Test H0: p = 0.60 vs. Ha: p > 0.60 at alpha = 0.05. What is the z-statistic?
z = (0.64 - 0.60)/sqrt(0.60 times 0.40/500) = 0.04/sqrt(0.00048) = 0.04/0.0219 = 1.83. Since 1.83 > 1.645 (one-tailed critical value), we reject H0.
A study tests whether a coin is fair. After 200 flips, they get 115 heads. Test H0: p = 0.5 vs. Ha: p ≠ 0.5 at alpha = 0.05. What is the z-statistic, and is the coin likely fair?
p-hat = 115/200 = 0.575. z = (0.575 - 0.5)/sqrt(0.5 times 0.5/200) = 0.075/0.0354 = 2.12. Since |2.12| > 1.96, we reject H0 and conclude the coin may be biased.
A hospital wants to know if the average emergency room wait time has changed from the previous year's average of 45 minutes. They sample 100 patients and find a mean wait of 48 minutes with s = 12 minutes. The p-value for the two-tailed test is 0.012. At alpha = 0.05, what is their conclusion?
Since the p-value (0.012) is less than alpha (0.05), we reject H0. There is statistically significant evidence that the mean ER wait time differs from 45 minutes.
A website runs an A/B test comparing two homepage designs. Design B gets a 2.1% higher click-through rate than Design A. With 50,000 visitors per group, the p-value is 0.001. A manager asks: 'Is a 2.1% difference practically important, or just statistically significant?' What is the best answer?
With very large samples, even tiny real differences produce very small p-values. Statistical significance means the effect is real, but the 2.1% difference may or may not be worth acting on depending on costs and revenue impact.
A school district claims the dropout rate is 8%. A concerned parent group surveys 300 students and finds that 30 dropped out (10%). They conduct a one-tailed test with Ha: p > 0.08 and find z = 1.27 with p-value = 0.102. At alpha = 0.05, what is the conclusion, and what might the parent group do next?
Since p-value (0.102) > alpha (0.05), we fail to reject H0. The 10% sample rate is not significantly higher than 8%. The group could collect a larger sample to increase statistical power to detect a real difference if one exists.

--- Lesson 7.4: Type I & Type II Errors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Type I & Type II Errors
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'false positive' in hypothesis testing?
A false positive occurs when we incorrectly conclude there is an effect (reject H0) when none actually exists. This is a Type I error.
What is a 'false negative' in hypothesis testing?
A false negative occurs when we fail to detect a real effect (fail to reject H0 when it is false). This is a Type II error.
What is 'effect size'?
Effect size measures how large the true difference or effect is. Larger effects are easier to detect and require smaller samples for adequate power.
What is a 'power analysis'?
A power analysis is conducted before data collection to determine the minimum sample size needed to have a good chance (usually 80%) of detecting a meaningful effect.
What is a 'conservative test'?
A conservative test uses a strict (small) alpha, reducing false positives but potentially increasing false negatives. It requires very strong evidence to reject H0.
A pregnancy test has a false positive rate of 1% and a false negative rate of 5%. A woman who is not pregnant takes the test. What is the probability she gets a positive result?
For someone who is not pregnant, a false positive is a Type I error with rate 1%. There is a 1% chance the test incorrectly shows positive.
Real-World Applications
Example: A drug company tests whether a new drug lowers blood pressure more than a placebo. They set alpha = 0.05. The drug actually works (lowers blood pressure by 5 mmHg on average), but the study of only 20 patients fails to reject H0 (p = 0.12). What error has occurred?
Since the drug actually works but the study failed to detect it, this is a Type II error. The small sample of 20 gave the test low power to detect the 5 mmHg effect.
Example: An environmental agency tests water quality at 100 different sites, each at alpha = 0.05. All sites actually have safe water. How many false alarms (significant results) would you expect?
Even when all null hypotheses are true, we expect about 5% times 100 = 5 false positives. This is the multiple testing problem.
Example: A study with n = 500 finds a statistically significant difference (p = 0.03) showing that a new diet reduces weight by an average of 0.3 pounds over 6 months compared to a control. Is this result practically significant?
The large sample made even a tiny 0.3-pound difference statistically detectable, but losing 0.3 pounds in 6 months has no practical health benefit. Statistical significance does not equal practical importance.

--- Lesson 7.5: P-values & Significance Levels (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: P-values & Significance Levels
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'Bonferroni correction'?
The Bonferroni correction addresses the multiple testing problem by using alpha/k as the threshold for each of k tests, keeping the overall Type I error rate at alpha.
What is a 'borderline' p-value?
A borderline p-value is near alpha and should be interpreted cautiously. The difference between p = 0.048 (significant) and p = 0.052 (not significant) is trivial.
What is the 'multiple testing problem'?
When conducting many tests, the chance of at least one Type I error grows. With 20 tests at alpha = 0.05, the expected number of false positives is 1 even when all null hypotheses are true.
A student conducts a z-test and calculates z = 1.80 for a two-tailed test. Using a z-table, the area in one tail beyond 1.80 is 0.0359. What is the two-tailed p-value, and is it significant at alpha = 0.05?
For a two-tailed test, the p-value is twice the one-tail area: 2 times 0.0359 = 0.0718. Since 0.0718 > 0.05, the result is not significant at alpha = 0.05.
A company runs 20 A/B tests on website features at alpha = 0.05. They find 3 significant results and plan to implement those features. If all 20 features truly have no effect, what is the expected number of false positives?
With 20 tests at alpha = 0.05, we expect about 1 false positive. Finding 3 significant results suggests some may be real, but the company should consider a correction for multiple testing.
A researcher using the Bonferroni correction has 10 tests to perform at an overall alpha = 0.05. What is the adjusted significance level for each individual test?
The Bonferroni correction divides alpha by the number of tests: 0.05/10 = 0.005. Each individual test must meet this stricter threshold.
A student computes a p-value of 0.03 for a hypothesis test and writes: 'Since p = 0.03, there is a 97% chance that Ha is true.' What is wrong with this interpretation?
This is a fundamental misinterpretation. The p-value does not give the probability that either hypothesis is true. It tells us how surprising the data would be if H0 were true.
A marketing team tests 5 different email subject lines to see which produces the highest open rate. They test each against a baseline of 20% open rate at alpha = 0.05. Subject line D gives a p-value of 0.04. The team excitedly reports: 'Subject line D significantly outperforms our baseline!' Without any correction for multiple testing, what is the approximate probability that at least one of the 5 tests gives a significant result by chance when all subject lines perform the same as the baseline?
The probability of at least one false positive in 5 tests is 1 - (1 - 0.05)^5 = 1 - 0.9505^5 = 1 - 0.7738 = 0.226, or about 23%. Without correction, a seemingly significant result may well be a false positive.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the 'multiple testing problem'?
When conducting many tests, the chance of at least one Type I error grows. With 20 tests at alpha = 0.05, the expected number of false positives is 1 even when all null hypotheses are true.

A p-value of 0.052 is borderline. Failing to reject H0 does not prove the curriculum has no effect. The difference between p = 0.048 and p = 0.052 is trivially small and should not lead to dramatically different conclusions.

    [ ] Computing the p-value using a computer
    [ ] Using legitimate statistical methods to analyze data
    Explanation: P-hacking is the unethical practice of mining data through multiple analyses until a significant result appears, inflating the false positive rate far beyond the stated alpha level.

  Q21. A medical researcher tests a new treatment and obtains a p-value of 0.04. She says: 'There is only a 4% chance that the treatment does not work.' Is her interpretation correct?
    [ ] Yes, that is exactly what the p-value means
    [ ] Yes, because 0.04 < 0.05
    [ ] No; the correct interpretation is that there is a 96% chance the treatment works
    [*] No; the p-value is the probability of the data given H0, not the probability that H0 is true
    Explanation: This is a common misinterpretation. The p-value tells us P(data | H0 true), not P(H0 true | data). A p-value of 0.04 means there is a 4% chance of seeing results this extreme if the treatment has no effect.

  Q22. A student conducts a z-test and calculates z = 1.80 for a two-tailed test. Using a z-table, the area in one tail beyond 1.80 is 0.0359. What is the two-tailed p-value, and is it significant at alpha = 0.05?
    [ ] p = 0.9641; not significant
    [ ] p = 1.80; significant
    [*] p = 0.0718; not significant at alpha = 0.05
    [ ] p = 0.0359; significant at alpha = 0.05
    Explanation: For a two-tailed test, the p-value is twice the one-tail area: 2 times 0.0359 = 0.0718. Since 0.0718 > 0.05, the result is not significant at alpha = 0.05.

    [ ] All three provide equally strong evidence
    [ ] Study C, because p = 0.049 is closest to 0.05
    [ ] Study B, because it is in the middle
    Explanation: Smaller p-values indicate stronger evidence against H0. Study A's p = 0.001 means the observed data would be extremely rare under H0, providing the strongest evidence.

  Q24. A geneticist tests 1,000 genes to see which are associated with a disease, each at alpha = 0.05. She finds 63 significant results. If all genes are truly unassociated, about how many significant results would she expect by chance alone?
    [*] 50 (5% of 1,000)
    [ ] 100 (10% of 1,000)
    [ ] 0 (no false positives)
    [ ] 5 (0.5% of 1,000)
    Explanation: With 1,000 tests at alpha = 0.05, we expect about 0.05 times 1,000 = 50 false positives by chance. The 63 significant results are only slightly above this, suggesting many may be false positives.

  Q25. A teacher gets a p-value of 0.052 for a test of whether a new curriculum improves scores. She reports: 'The result was not significant (p = 0.052), so the curriculum has no effect.' Is this conclusion appropriate?
    [ ] Yes, because 0.052 is clearly far from 0.05

    [ ] 0 (no false positives)
    [ ] 3 (the observed number)
    [*] 1 (0.05 times 20)
    Explanation: With 20 tests at alpha = 0.05, we expect about 1 false positive. Finding 3 significant results suggests some may be real, but the company should consider a correction for multiple testing.

  Q27. A researcher using the Bonferroni correction has 10 tests to perform at an overall alpha = 0.05. What is the adjusted significance level for each individual test?
    [ ] 0.50
    [*] 0.005
    [ ] 0.01
    [ ] 0.05
    Explanation: The Bonferroni correction divides alpha by the number of tests: 0.05/10 = 0.005. Each individual test must meet this stricter threshold.

  Q28. A clinical trial has a p-value of 0.0001 for a blood pressure drug that lowers systolic BP by 2 mmHg compared to placebo. A doctor says: 'The incredibly small p-value proves this drug is very effective.' Is the doctor's reasoning correct?

  Q29. A student computes a p-value of 0.03 for a hypothesis test and writes: 'Since p = 0.03, there is a 97% chance that Ha is true.' What is wrong with this interpretation?
    [ ] Nothing is wrong; this interpretation correctly describes what a p-value of 0.03 represents
    [ ] The student should have said there is only a 3% chance that Ha is true instead of 97%

  Key Concepts: Interpreting Statistical Results
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'publication bias'?
Publication bias creates a distorted literature where positive findings are overrepresented, because null results are less likely to be submitted or accepted for publication.
What is 'Simpson's Paradox'?
Simpson's Paradox occurs when a trend seen in subgroups reverses when data is aggregated, usually due to a lurking variable that changes the group compositions.
What is 'cherry-picking' in the context of interpreting results?
Cherry-picking is the misleading practice of highlighting favorable evidence while omitting unfavorable evidence, creating a distorted picture of the research findings.
A news article reports: 'Eating chocolate is linked to lower heart disease risk, according to a study of 10,000 adults.' The study is observational. A reader concludes that chocolate prevents heart disease. What is the flaw in this reasoning?

This is an observational study, so families who can afford music lessons may also provide other educational advantages. Requiring music lessons for all students may not reproduce the 20-point benefit.

--- Lesson 7.7: Case Studies in Medical Trials (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Medical Trials
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'randomized controlled trial' (RCT)?
An RCT uses random assignment to allocate participants to treatment and control groups, allowing researchers to establish causal relationships between interventions and outcomes.
What is a 'placebo-controlled trial'?
A placebo-controlled trial includes a group receiving an inert substance, allowing researchers to separate the actual drug effect from the placebo effect and natural recovery.
What is a 'washout period'?
A washout period ensures that the effects of the first treatment have worn off before the second treatment begins, preventing carryover effects in crossover trials.
What is a 'Phase II' clinical trial?
Phase II trials test whether the drug works as intended in patients with the condition, evaluating efficacy and side effects in a medium-sized group.
What is 'informed consent' in a clinical trial?
Informed consent is a comprehensive process, not just a form, ensuring participants make a voluntary and informed decision about joining a clinical trial.
What is a 'Data Safety Monitoring Board' (DSMB)?
A DSMB is independent of the trial investigators and periodically reviews unblinded data to protect participants, recommending early termination if necessary.
A clinical trial for a new heart medication enrolls 2,000 patients. After randomization, 1,000 receive the drug and 1,000 receive a placebo. After 5 years, 80 patients in the drug group had heart attacks compared to 120 in the placebo group. What is the absolute risk reduction?
Drug group rate: 80/1000 = 8%. Placebo group rate: 120/1000 = 12%. ARR = 12% - 8% = 4 percentage points.
Using the data from the previous question (ARR = 4%), what is the Number Needed to Treat (NNT)?
NNT = 1/ARR = 1/0.04 = 25. This means 25 patients need to be treated for 5 years to prevent one additional heart attack.
A vaccine trial enrolls 30,000 participants: 15,000 receive the vaccine and 15,000 receive a placebo. After one year, 200 in the placebo group develop the disease compared to 10 in the vaccine group. What is the vaccine's efficacy?
Vaccine efficacy = (1 - (vaccine rate / placebo rate)) times 100 = (1 - (10/15000)/(200/15000)) times 100 = (1 - 10/200) times 100 = (1 - 0.05) times 100 = 95%.
A drug company reports that its new statin reduces heart attack risk by 36% compared to placebo (relative risk reduction). The actual event rates are 5.4% in the placebo group and 3.5% in the statin group. What is the NNT, and what does it tell a doctor?
ARR = 5.4% - 3.5% = 1.9% = 0.019. NNT = 1/0.019 = 52.6, rounded to 53. This means 53 patients must take the statin for one additional heart attack to be prevented.
Real-World Applications
Example: A Phase III trial for a cancer drug shows the drug extends median survival by 6 weeks (from 12 months to 13.5 months) with p < 0.001. However, 40% of patients experience severe nausea and 15% experience liver damage. Should the drug be approved?
Drug approval involves balancing benefits and risks. A statistically significant 6-week survival benefit must be weighed against severe side effects affecting a large proportion of patients.
Example: A clinical trial uses a crossover design to test two pain medications. Each of 50 patients receives Drug A for 4 weeks, then a 2-week washout, then Drug B for 4 weeks (or vice versa, randomly assigned). Why is this design advantageous?
Crossover designs reduce variability because each patient's response to both drugs is compared directly, eliminating differences in pain sensitivity between individuals.
Example: A trial comparing surgery versus medication for back pain has 150 patients in each group. After 1 year, 30% of surgery patients and 25% of medication patients report significant improvement. The p-value is 0.32. A surgeon argues: 'Surgery is 5 percentage points better.' Is this a valid conclusion?
With p = 0.32, the 5-percentage-point difference is not statistically significant. The observed difference could easily arise from random variation, and no advantage of surgery can be concluded.

  Q5. A Data Safety Monitoring Board (DSMB) can:
    [ ] Change the randomization scheme during the trial
    [ ] Only review data after the trial ends
    [*] Recommend stopping a trial early if the treatment shows clear benefit or harm
    [ ] Only observe but never intervene
    Explanation: DSMBs periodically review interim data and can recommend early termination if continuing would be unethical (clear benefit or unacceptable harm).

  Q6. The Salk polio vaccine trial (1954) was significant because:
    [ ] It was the first medical study ever conducted using standardized research protocols
    [ ] It used no control group, relying solely on before-and-after comparison of vaccinated children
    [ ] It was a small observational study that only followed a few hundred children over one summer
    [*] It was one of the largest randomized controlled trials ever conducted, with over 1 million children
    Explanation: The Salk trial was a landmark in clinical research, demonstrating the power of large-scale RCTs to establish vaccine efficacy.

  Q7. Crossover trials:
    [ ] Are always unethical because they expose every participant to all experimental treatments
    [*] Give each participant both treatments in sequence, with a washout period between
    [ ] Only test one treatment at a time, requiring separate trials for each comparison group
    [ ] Use no randomization and instead assign treatments based on participant preference or order
    Explanation: In crossover designs, each participant serves as their own control, reducing between-subject variability. A washout period allows the first treatment's effects to dissipate.

  Q8. Number Needed to Treat (NNT) of 20 means:
    [ ] The drug works on 20% of patients
    [ ] The drug costs $20 per treatment
    [*] 20 patients must be treated for one additional patient to benefit compared to placebo
    [ ] 20 patients were studied
    Explanation: NNT = 20 means that on average, 20 patients must receive the treatment for one additional patient to benefit beyond what would happen with placebo.

  Q9. Adverse events in clinical trials are:
    [ ] Never reported to regulators
    [*] Any undesirable medical occurrence in a participant, whether or not related to the treatment
    [ ] Reported only if they are life-threatening
    [ ] Only side effects directly caused by the drug
    Explanation: All adverse events are recorded and reported in clinical trials, regardless of whether they are believed to be related to the treatment.

  Q10. The FDA requires clinical trials before drug approval to:
    [*] Ensure drugs are safe and effective before reaching the public
    [ ] Increase the cost of medications
    [ ] Test drugs only on animals
    [ ] Delay treatments unnecessarily
    Explanation: The FDA approval process protects the public by requiring rigorous evidence of safety and efficacy through phased clinical trials.

  Q11. What is a 'randomized controlled trial' (RCT)?
    [ ] A study where participants choose their own treatment
    [*] An experiment where participants are randomly assigned to treatment or control groups to test the effect of an intervention
    [ ] An observational study with a large sample
    [ ] A study conducted randomly without any structure
    Explanation: An RCT uses random assignment to allocate participants to treatment and control groups, allowing researchers to establish causal relationships between interventions and outcomes.

  Q12. What does 'double-blind' mean in a clinical trial?
    [*] Neither the participants nor the researchers/evaluators know who receives the treatment vs. placebo
    [ ] Participants are tested twice to confirm the reproducibility of the initial results
    [ ] Two different drugs are tested simultaneously in the same group of study participants
    [ ] The study has two distinct phases separated by a washout period for each participant
    Explanation: Double-blinding prevents both participant expectations and researcher bias from influencing the results, since neither party knows which treatment is being administered.

  Q13. What is a 'placebo-controlled trial'?
    [ ] A trial where the placebo is the main treatment
    [ ] A trial where all patients receive a placebo
    [*] A trial comparing the active treatment to an inactive substance to isolate the treatment's true effect
    [ ] A trial with no control group
    Explanation: A placebo-controlled trial includes a group receiving an inert substance, allowing researchers to separate the actual drug effect from the placebo effect and natural recovery.

  Q14. What does 'intention-to-treat' (ITT) analysis mean?
    [ ] Analyzing participants based on which treatment they actually received
    [ ] Only analyzing participants with positive outcomes
    [ ] Analyzing only participants who finished the entire treatment protocol
    [*] Analyzing all participants based on their original group assignment, regardless of whether they completed treatment
    Explanation: ITT analysis includes all randomized participants in their originally assigned groups, preserving the integrity of randomization and providing a real-world estimate of treatment effectiveness.

  Q15. What is a 'washout period'?
    [ ] A period during which participants clean their equipment
    [ ] The time after the study ends
    [*] A period between treatments in a crossover trial allowing the first treatment's effects to dissipate
    [ ] The initial screening period before enrollment
    Explanation: A washout period ensures that the effects of the first treatment have worn off before the second treatment begins, preventing carryover effects in crossover trials.

  Q16. What does 'Number Needed to Treat' (NNT) mean?
    [ ] The total number of patients in the study
    [ ] The number of side effects observed
    [ ] The number of drugs tested in the trial
    [*] The number of patients who must receive treatment for one additional patient to benefit compared to the control
    Explanation: NNT provides a practical measure of treatment effectiveness. NNT = 1/(absolute risk reduction). A smaller NNT indicates a more effective treatment.

  Q17. What is a 'Phase II' clinical trial?
    [ ] Testing only in laboratory animals to establish dose ranges and identify potential toxicity
    [ ] Post-marketing surveillance after FDA approval to monitor long-term safety in the public
    [*] A trial testing the drug's effectiveness and side effects in a moderate number of patients (100-300) who have the target condition
    [ ] The first test of a drug in humans, focusing on basic safety and pharmacokinetic profiles
    Explanation: Phase II trials test whether the drug works as intended in patients with the condition, evaluating efficacy and side effects in a medium-sized group.

  Q18. What is 'informed consent' in a clinical trial?
    [ ] A form that participants sign without reading, serving only as a legal formality for records
    [ ] A requirement only for Phase III trials because earlier phases involve fewer health risks overall
    [*] A process ensuring participants understand the trial's purpose, procedures, risks, benefits, and alternatives before voluntarily agreeing to participate
    [ ] Permission from the drug company to participate, granted based on the patient's medical history
    Explanation: Informed consent is a comprehensive process, not just a form, ensuring participants make a voluntary and informed decision about joining a clinical trial.

  Q19. What is a 'Data Safety Monitoring Board' (DSMB)?
    [*] An independent group that reviews interim trial data to ensure participant safety and can recommend stopping the trial early
    [ ] The statistical team that analyzes the final results
    [ ] A committee that stores and backs up trial data
    [ ] A government agency that approves drugs
    Explanation: A DSMB is independent of the trial investigators and periodically reviews unblinded data to protect participants, recommending early termination if necessary.

  Q20. What does 'absolute risk reduction' (ARR) mean?
    [*] The difference in event rates between the control and treatment groups
    [ ] The p-value of the treatment effect
    [ ] The ratio of event rates between groups
    [ ] The total number of adverse events
    Explanation: ARR = (control event rate) - (treatment event rate). It directly shows how much the treatment reduces the probability of an event in absolute terms.

  Q21. A clinical trial for a new heart medication enrolls 2,000 patients. After randomization, 1,000 receive the drug and 1,000 receive a placebo. After 5 years, 80 patients in the drug group had heart attacks compared to 120 in the placebo group. What is the absolute risk reduction?
    [ ] 8 percentage points
    [ ] 33% relative reduction
    [*] 4 percentage points (12% - 8% = 4%)
    [ ] 40 patients
    Explanation: Drug group rate: 80/1000 = 8%. Placebo group rate: 120/1000 = 12%. ARR = 12% - 8% = 4 percentage points.

  Q22. Using the data from the previous question (ARR = 4%), what is the Number Needed to Treat (NNT)?
    [ ] 1,000 patients
    [*] 25 patients
    [ ] 100 patients
    [ ] 4 patients
    Explanation: NNT = 1/ARR = 1/0.04 = 25. This means 25 patients need to be treated for 5 years to prevent one additional heart attack.

  Q23. A Phase III trial for a cancer drug shows the drug extends median survival by 6 weeks (from 12 months to 13.5 months) with p < 0.001. However, 40% of patients experience severe nausea and 15% experience liver damage. Should the drug be approved?
    [*] The decision requires weighing the modest survival benefit (6 weeks) against serious side effects; statistical significance alone is not sufficient
    [ ] No, because 6 weeks is not statistically significant
    [ ] Yes, because any extension of life is worth any side effects
    [ ] Yes, because the result is highly significant (p < 0.001)
    Explanation: Drug approval involves balancing benefits and risks. A statistically significant 6-week survival benefit must be weighed against severe side effects affecting a large proportion of patients.

  Q24. A clinical trial reports that a new drug reduces the relative risk of stroke by 50%. The actual stroke rates are 2% in the control group and 1% in the treatment group. A patient reads '50% risk reduction' and thinks the drug would cut their personal stroke risk in half (from, say, 30% to 15%). Why is this misleading?
    [*] The 50% relative risk reduction sounds dramatic, but the absolute risk reduction is only 1 percentage point (2% to 1%), which is more informative for individual patients
    [ ] The patient's arithmetic is correct; a 50% reduction in stroke risk is a large individual benefit
    [ ] The drug does not work at all because the absolute numbers involved are too small to matter
    [ ] Relative risk reductions are more accurate than absolute reductions for evaluating treatment benefit
    Explanation: Relative risk reductions can sound impressive (50%) while the absolute benefit is small (1 percentage point). Patients should understand the absolute risk reduction to make informed decisions.

  Q25. During a clinical trial for a new antibiotic, interim data shows the antibiotic group has significantly higher mortality than the placebo group (p = 0.008). The DSMB recommends stopping the trial. Why?
    [ ] The trial has already collected enough data
    [*] Continuing to administer a treatment that appears to increase mortality would be unethical
    [ ] The p-value is too small
    [ ] To save money on the trial
    Explanation: When interim data shows significant harm, continuing the trial would expose more patients to a dangerous treatment. The ethical principle of beneficence requires stopping the trial.

  Q26. A vaccine trial enrolls 30,000 participants: 15,000 receive the vaccine and 15,000 receive a placebo. After one year, 200 in the placebo group develop the disease compared to 10 in the vaccine group. What is the vaccine's efficacy?
    [*] 95%
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 99%
    [ ] 5%
    Explanation: Vaccine efficacy = (1 - (vaccine rate / placebo rate)) times 100 = (1 - (10/15000)/(200/15000)) times 100 = (1 - 10/200) times 100 = (1 - 0.05) times 100 = 95%.

  Q27. A clinical trial uses a crossover design to test two pain medications. Each of 50 patients receives Drug A for 4 weeks, then a 2-week washout, then Drug B for 4 weeks (or vice versa, randomly assigned). Why is this design advantageous?
    [ ] It is faster than a parallel-group trial because each treatment period is shorter in duration
    [*] It requires fewer participants because each person serves as their own control, reducing between-subject variability
    [ ] It does not require randomization since participants receive every treatment in the same order
    [ ] It eliminates all side effects by allowing the washout period to clear residual drug activity
    Explanation: Crossover designs reduce variability because each patient's response to both drugs is compared directly, eliminating differences in pain sensitivity between individuals.

  Q28. A drug company reports that its new statin reduces heart attack risk by 36% compared to placebo (relative risk reduction). The actual event rates are 5.4% in the placebo group and 3.5% in the statin group. What is the NNT, and what does it tell a doctor?
    [ ] NNT = 100; this treatment is ineffective
    [ ] NNT cannot be calculated from these numbers
    [ ] NNT = 3; only 3 patients need treatment
    [*] NNT = 53; the doctor must treat 53 patients for about 5 years to prevent one heart attack
    Explanation: ARR = 5.4% - 3.5% = 1.9% = 0.019. NNT = 1/0.019 = 52.6, rounded to 53. This means 53 patients must take the statin for one additional heart attack to be prevented.

  Q29. A trial comparing surgery versus medication for back pain has 150 patients in each group. After 1 year, 30% of surgery patients and 25% of medication patients report significant improvement. The p-value is 0.32. A surgeon argues: 'Surgery is 5 percentage points better.' Is this a valid conclusion?
    [ ] Yes, because 30% > 25%
    [*] No, the 5-point difference is not statistically significant (p = 0.32), so it could easily be due to chance
    [ ] No, because back pain cannot be treated
    [ ] Yes, because surgery is inherently better than medication
    Explanation: With p = 0.32, the 5-percentage-point difference is not statistically significant. The observed difference could easily arise from random variation, and no advantage of surgery can be concluded.

  Q30. A COVID-19 vaccine trial reports 95% efficacy with a 95% CI of (90.3%, 97.6%). An anti-vaccine group claims: 'The vaccine might only be 90% effective based on the confidence interval, so the 95% efficacy is misleading.' How should you evaluate this claim?
    [ ] The claim is valid; the vaccine may not work
    [ ] The confidence interval proves the vaccine is exactly 95% effective
    [*] Even the lower bound of 90.3% represents extremely high efficacy, so the vaccine is clearly effective regardless of where the true efficacy falls within the CI
    [ ] A wider confidence interval would be more trustworthy
    Explanation: The entire confidence interval (90.3% to 97.6%) indicates very high efficacy. Even in the worst-case scenario suggested by the lower bound, the vaccine prevents over 90% of cases.
