--- Lesson 7.1: Confidence Intervals (mean, proportion) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Confidence Intervals (mean, proportion)
Confidence Intervals
A confidence interval (CI) provides a range of plausible values for a population parameter based on sample data.

General form: point estimate ± margin of error
  • CI for a mean (σ known): x̄ ± z* · (σ/√n)  • CI for a mean (σ unknown): x̄ ± t* · (s/√n) using the t-distribution with n−1 degrees of freedom.  • CI for a proportion: p̂ ± z* · √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)  • Confidence level (e.g., 95%): In repeated sampling, 95% of such intervals would contain the true parameter.  • Common z* values: 90% → 1.645, 95% → 1.960, 99% → 2.576.  • Conditions: Random sample, independence, normality (or large n via CLT), and for proportions: np̂ ≥ 10 and n(1−p̂) ≥ 10.

--- Lesson 7.2: Margin of Error (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Margin of Error
Margin of Error
The margin of error (ME) quantifies the precision of a sample estimate.
  • For a mean: ME = z* · (σ/√n) or t* · (s/√n).  • For a proportion: ME = z* · √(p̂(1−p̂)/n).  • Interpretation: We are [confidence level]% confident that the true parameter is within ME of our estimate.
Factors affecting ME:
  • ↑ Confidence level → ↑ ME (wider interval).  • ↑ Sample size (n) → ↓ ME (more precise).  • ↑ Variability (σ or s) → ↑ ME (less precise).
Sample size determination: To achieve a desired ME, solve for n. For a proportion: n = (z*/ME)² · p̂(1−p̂). When p̂ is unknown, use p̂ = 0.5 (maximum variability, most conservative).

--- Lesson 7.3: Hypothesis Testing (null vs. alternative) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hypothesis Testing (null vs. alternative)
Hypothesis Testing
Hypothesis testing is a formal procedure for using data to evaluate a claim about a population parameter.
  • Null hypothesis (H₀): The default claim — often 'no effect' or 'no difference.' Assumed true until evidence suggests otherwise.  • Alternative hypothesis (Hₐ): The claim that contradicts H₀ — what the researcher believes or wants to show.  • Test statistic: A z-score or t-score computed from the sample data measuring how far the sample result is from H₀.  • P-value: The probability of obtaining results as extreme (or more extreme) than observed, assuming H₀ is true.  • Decision: If p-value ≤ α, reject H₀ (statistically significant). If p-value > α, fail to reject H₀.  • Significance level (α): The threshold (usually 0.05) for deciding statistical significance.
Steps: (1) State H₀ and Hₐ. (2) Choose α. (3) Compute the test statistic. (4) Find the p-value. (5) Make a decision and state a conclusion in context.

--- Lesson 7.4: Type I & Type II Errors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Type I & Type II Errors
Type I & Type II Errors
When making decisions based on hypothesis tests, two types of errors can occur.
  • Type I error (false positive): Rejecting H₀ when H₀ is actually true. Probability = α (significance level).  • Type II error (false negative): Failing to reject H₀ when H₀ is actually false (Hₐ is true). Probability = β.  • Power = 1 − β: The probability of correctly rejecting a false H₀. Higher power = better ability to detect a real effect.
Trade-offs:
  • Decreasing α → decreases Type I error but increases Type II error (harder to reject H₀).  • Increasing sample size → increases power (reduces β) without changing α.  • Larger true effect size → easier to detect → higher power.H₀ TrueH₀ FalseReject H₀Type I error (α)Correct (Power = 1−β)Fail to reject H₀CorrectType II error (β)

--- Lesson 7.5: P-values & Significance Levels (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: P-values & Significance Levels
P-values & Significance Levels
The p-value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic as extreme (or more extreme) than the one observed, assuming H₀ is true.
  • Small p-value: The observed data is unlikely under H₀ → evidence against H₀.  • Large p-value: The observed data is consistent with H₀ → insufficient evidence against H₀.  • Decision rule: If p-value ≤ α, reject H₀. If p-value > α, fail to reject H₀.
Common misconceptions:
  • The p-value is NOT the probability that H₀ is true.  • The p-value is NOT the probability of making an error.  • A statistically significant result is not necessarily practically important.  • A non-significant result does not prove H₀ is true.
Effect size and confidence intervals should accompany p-values to provide a fuller picture.

    Explanation: A large p-value means the data is consistent with H₀.

  Q4. Statistical significance is achieved when:
    [ ] p > α
    [*] p ≤ α
    [ ] p = 1
    [ ] p = α/2
    Explanation: We reject H₀ when the p-value is at or below α.

  Q5. A p-value of 0.05 at α = 0.05:
    [ ] Fails to reject H₀
    [*] Rejects H₀ (borderline significant)
    [ ] Proves H₀
    [ ] Is invalid
    Explanation: p ≤ α, so we (barely) reject H₀.

  Q6. The p-value is NOT:
    [ ] A probability
    [ ] Dependent on data
    [*] The probability that H₀ is true
    [ ] Used in decision making
    Explanation: This is the most common misconception about p-values.

  Q7. A small p-value with a tiny effect size suggests:
    [ ] A large practical effect
    [*] Statistical significance but questionable practical importance

  Q8. p = 0.06 at α = 0.05 means:
    [ ] Reject H₀

    [ ] Half
    [*] Double
    [ ] Equal to
    [ ] Unrelated to

  Q10. Reporting only significant results (publication bias) can:
    [ ] Improve science
    [*] Distort the literature by hiding non-significant findings

    [ ] Increase power
    Explanation: Publishing only significant results gives a misleading picture.

  Q11. A confidence interval of (−0.5, 3.2) for a difference includes 0, suggesting:
    [*] The difference is not statistically significant at that confidence level
    [ ] The difference is significant
    [ ] The data is invalid
    [ ] The CI is wrong
    Explanation: If 0 is in the CI for a difference, we can't reject H₀: no difference.

  Q12. 'The p-value is the probability of making a Type I error' is:
    [ ] Correct
    [*] Incorrect — α is the Type I error rate, not the p-value
    [ ] Sometimes true
    [ ] Always true

  Q13. Effect size measures:
    [*] The magnitude of the observed effect
    [ ] The p-value
    [ ] The sample size
    [ ] The significance level
    Explanation: Effect size quantifies how large the difference or relationship is.

  Q14. A very large sample can produce a small p-value for:
    [ ] Only large effects
    [*] Even trivially small effects
    [ ] Only effects that are practically meaningful
    [ ] Only meaningful differences
    Explanation: Statistical power increases with n, so even tiny effects become 'significant.'

  Q15. Failing to reject H₀ proves:
    [ ] H₀ is true
    [*] Nothing — there may not be enough evidence to detect a real effect
    [ ] Hₐ is true
    [ ] The test was wrong
    Explanation: Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

  Q16. The p-value depends on:
    [ ] Only α
    [ ] Only the sample size

  Q17. Cohen's d is a measure of:
    [ ] P-value
    [ ] Significance

    Explanation: Cohen's d = (x̄₁ − x̄₂)/pooled SD; it quantifies the size of the difference.

  Q18. If α = 0.01, you need __ evidence to reject H₀ compared to α = 0.05:
    [ ] Less

    [ ] The same
    [ ] No

  Q19. Best practice: report both the p-value and:

  Q20. The American Statistical Association warns against:
    [ ] Using statistics
    [ ] Collecting data

--- Lesson 7.6: Interpreting Statistical Results (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interpreting Statistical Results
Interpreting Statistical Results
Correct interpretation of statistical results is just as important as the calculations themselves.
  • Context: Always state conclusions in the context of the problem, not just 'reject H₀.'  • Statistical vs. practical significance: A statistically significant result (small p) may have a trivially small effect. Always check effect size.  • Confidence interval interpretation: 'We are 95% confident that the true [parameter] is between [a] and [b].'  • Association vs. causation: Observational studies can show association but not cause-and-effect. Only randomized experiments establish causation.  • Generalizability: Results generalize only to the population from which the sample was drawn.  • Limitations: Acknowledge assumptions, potential biases, and the scope of conclusions.
Common mistakes: Confusing correlation with causation, misinterpreting p-values, ignoring assumptions, and overgeneralizing results.

--- Lesson 7.7: Case Studies in Medical Trials (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Medical Trials
Case Studies in Medical Trials
Medical trials (clinical trials) are among the most rigorous applications of statistical inference.
  • Phases:  • Phase I: Safety and dosage (small group).  • Phase II: Efficacy and side effects (larger group).  • Phase III: Compared to standard treatment or placebo (large RCT).  • Phase IV: Post-market surveillance.  • Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs): The gold standard. Random assignment + control group + blinding.  • Intention-to-treat (ITT): Analyzing participants in their originally assigned groups regardless of compliance — prevents bias.  • Key examples:  • Salk polio vaccine trial (1954): 1.8 million children, double-blind, proved vaccine efficacy.  • Framingham Heart Study: Long-running observational study identifying heart disease risk factors.  • COVID-19 vaccine trials: Phase III RCTs with 30,000+ participants establishing safety and efficacy.
