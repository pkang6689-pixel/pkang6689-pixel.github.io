--- Lesson 9.1: Law of Large Numbers (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Law of Large Numbers
Law of Large Numbers (LLN)
The Law of Large Numbers states that as the number of trials of a random experiment increases, the sample mean converges to the expected value (population mean).
  • Informal statement: The more you repeat an experiment, the closer the average of the results gets to the true expected value.  • Example: Flipping a fair coin many times — the proportion of heads approaches 0.5 as the number of flips grows.  • Weak LLN: The sample mean converges in probability to the population mean.  • Strong LLN: The sample mean converges almost surely to the population mean.  • Gambler's Fallacy: The mistaken belief that past outcomes affect future independent trials. The LLN says nothing about individual trials, only about long-run averages.  • Applications: Insurance (actuarial predictions), casinos (house edge realized over many bets), polling (larger samples → more accurate estimates).

--- Lesson 9.2: Expected Value (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Expected Value
Expected Value
The expected value (E(X) or μ) of a random variable is the long-run average value after many repetitions.
  • Discrete: E(X) = Σ xᵢ · P(xᵢ) — the sum of each value times its probability.  • Continuous: E(X) = ∫ x · f(x) dx over the range of X.  • Properties:  • E(aX + b) = aE(X) + b (linearity).  • E(X + Y) = E(X) + E(Y) (always, regardless of independence).  • If X and Y are independent: E(XY) = E(X) · E(Y).  • Variance: Var(X) = E(X²) − [E(X)]².  • Decision making: Expected value helps compare options (e.g., insurance, lottery, business decisions).

--- Lesson 9.3: Probability Distributions in Real Life (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability Distributions in Real Life
Probability Distributions in Real Life
Probability distributions model uncertainty in the real world.
  • Normal distribution: Heights, test scores, measurement errors, blood pressure — the bell curve appears when many small, independent factors combine.  • Binomial distribution: Number of successes in fixed trials — e.g., defective items in a batch, correct answers on a multiple-choice test.  • Poisson distribution: Rare events in a fixed interval — customer arrivals, typos per page, traffic accidents per day.  • Exponential distribution: Time between events — time between customer arrivals, lifetimes of products.  • Uniform distribution: All outcomes equally likely — random number generators, lottery draws.  • Choosing the right distribution: Match the real-world scenario to the distribution's assumptions — fixed trials (binomial), rare events (Poisson), continuous measurements (normal), etc.

--- Lesson 9.4: Chi-Square Tests (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Chi-Square Tests
Chi-Square Tests
The chi-square (χ²) test is used for categorical data.
  • Goodness-of-fit test: Tests whether observed frequencies match an expected distribution. χ² = Σ (O − E)²/E.  • Test of independence: Tests whether two categorical variables are independent using a contingency table.  • Test of homogeneity: Tests whether the distribution of a categorical variable is the same across different populations.  • Degrees of freedom:  • Goodness-of-fit: df = k − 1 (k = number of categories).  • Independence/homogeneity: df = (r − 1)(c − 1) (r rows, c columns).  • Conditions: All expected counts should be ≥ 5. Observations must be independent.  • χ² is always ≥ 0: Large χ² values suggest the observed data differ significantly from the expected.

--- Lesson 9.5: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)
ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)
ANOVA tests whether the means of three or more groups are all equal.
  • H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = … = μₖ (all group means are equal).  • Hₐ: At least one mean differs.  • F-statistic: F = (Between-group variability) / (Within-group variability) = MSB / MSW.  • MSB (Mean Square Between): Measures how much the group means differ from the overall mean.  • MSW (Mean Square Within): Measures the average variability within each group.  • If F is large: Between-group differences are large relative to within-group variability → evidence against H₀.  • Conditions: Independent random samples, approximately normal populations, equal variances across groups.  • Post-hoc tests: If ANOVA rejects H₀, use follow-up tests (e.g., Tukey's HSD) to identify which means differ.

--- Lesson 9.6: Nonparametric Tests (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nonparametric Tests
Nonparametric Tests
Nonparametric tests make fewer assumptions about the population distribution — they don't require normality.
  • When to use: Small samples, ordinal data, heavily skewed distributions, or when normality assumptions are clearly violated.  • Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test: Nonparametric alternative to the paired t-test. Uses ranks of differences.  • Mann-Whitney U Test: Nonparametric alternative to the two-sample t-test. Compares ranks between two independent groups.  • Kruskal-Wallis Test: Nonparametric alternative to one-way ANOVA. Compares medians of three or more groups.  • Spearman's Rank Correlation (rₛ): Measures monotonic (not necessarily linear) association between two variables.  • Trade-off: Nonparametric tests are more flexible but generally have less statistical power than parametric tests when assumptions are met.

--- Lesson 9.7: Case Studies in Psychology & Biology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Psychology & Biology
Case Studies in Psychology & Biology
Statistics is essential in psychology and biology research.
  • Clinical trials: Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) use hypothesis testing to determine if treatments work. Phases I–IV test safety, efficacy, and long-term effects. Statistics handles placebo effects, double-blinding, and intent-to-treat analysis.  • Psychology experiments: ANOVA compares groups (e.g., different therapy types). Effect sizes (Cohen's d) measure practical significance beyond p-values.  • Genetics: Chi-square tests verify if observed genetic ratios match Mendelian predictions. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium testing uses χ² goodness-of-fit.  • Ecology: Regression models species abundance vs. environmental factors. Mark-recapture methods estimate population size using the Lincoln-Petersen formula: N̂ = (M × C)/R.  • Replication crisis: Many published results have failed to replicate, highlighting the importance of proper statistical methods, effect sizes, and pre-registration.
