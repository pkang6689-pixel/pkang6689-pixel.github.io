--- Lesson 9.1: Law of Large Numbers (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Law of Large Numbers
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Gambler's Fallacy?
The Gambler's Fallacy is the mistaken belief that if an event has occurred more frequently in the past, it is less likely in the future (or vice versa) for independent events.
What is the difference between the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem?
LLN says the sample mean converges to the population mean. CLT says the distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal for large n. They are related but distinct.
What is meant by 'independent and identically distributed' (i.i.d.) in the context of LLN?
I.i.d. means each observation comes from the same distribution (identically distributed) and no observation influences another (independent). This is a key assumption of LLN.
What is the 'law of averages' misconception?
The 'law of averages' is a common misinterpretation that assumes short runs must balance out. LLN only applies as n approaches infinity, not over short sequences.
What is 'sampling variability'?
Sampling variability is the natural fluctuation in statistics from sample to sample. LLN tells us this variability decreases as sample size increases.
Real-World Applications
Example: If you roll a fair six-sided die many times, the average roll approaches:
The expected value of a fair die is (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 3.5, and LLN guarantees the average converges to this value.

--- Lesson 9.2: Expected Value (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Expected Value
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the expected value of a random variable?
The expected value (or mean) is the probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes: E(X) = sum of x_i times P(x_i).
What is the formula for E(X) when X is a continuous random variable?
For continuous random variables, the summation in the discrete formula is replaced by integration: E(X) = integral of x times f(x) dx over all possible values.
What is the relationship between expected value and variance?
Variance quantifies how much a random variable typically deviates from its expected value. It is defined as the expected squared deviation from the mean.
What is the 'law of the unconscious statistician' (LOTUS)?
LOTUS states that E[g(X)] = sum of g(x_i)·P(x_i) for discrete X, allowing you to compute the expected value of a function of X directly from X's distribution.
A lottery ticket costs $5. There is a 1/500 chance of winning $1,000 and a 1/100 chance of winning $50. What is the expected profit per ticket?
E(winnings) = (1/500)(1000) + (1/100)(50) = 2.00 + 0.50 = $2.50. E(profit) = $2.50 - $5.00 = -$2.50 per ticket.
An insurance company charges $600 per year for a home insurance policy. Data shows there is a 2% chance of a $15,000 claim and a 0.5% chance of a $50,000 claim in any given year (assume only one claim can occur). What is the company's expected profit per policy?
E(claims) = 0.02(15,000) + 0.005(50,000) = 300 + 250 = $550. E(profit) = $600 - $550 = $50 per policy.
A game costs $3 to play. You roll two fair dice and win $1 for every dot showing (sum of both dice). What is your expected profit per game?
E(sum of two dice) = 3.5 + 3.5 = 7. You win $7 on average. E(profit) = $7 - $3 = $4 per game.
A salesperson earns a $200 base salary per day plus a $50 commission for each sale. If the expected number of sales per day is 4, what is the expected daily income?
Let X = number of sales. Income = 200 + 50X. E(Income) = 200 + 50·E(X) = 200 + 50(4) = 200 + 200 = $400.
A factory produces widgets with the following defect distribution: 0 defects (probability 0.7), 1 defect (probability 0.2), and 2 defects (probability 0.1). Each defect costs $30 to repair. What is the expected repair cost per widget?
E(defects) = 0(0.7) + 1(0.2) + 2(0.1) = 0 + 0.2 + 0.2 = 0.4 defects per widget. E(repair cost) = 0.4 × $30 = $12.00.
A student's grade in a course depends on three exams. Exam 1 (weight 30%) has E(score) = 85, Exam 2 (weight 30%) has E(score) = 78, and the Final (weight 40%) has E(score) = 90. What is the expected weighted average?
E(grade) = 0.30(85) + 0.30(78) + 0.40(90) = 25.5 + 23.4 + 36.0 = 84.9.
A ride-share driver earns $12 per trip. On any given hour, the number of trips completed follows this distribution: 0 trips (10%), 1 trip (30%), 2 trips (40%), 3 trips (20%). What is the driver's expected hourly earnings?
E(trips) = 0(0.10) + 1(0.30) + 2(0.40) + 3(0.20) = 0 + 0.30 + 0.80 + 0.60 = 1.70 trips. E(earnings) = 1.70 × $12 = $20.40.
A venture capitalist invests $100,000 in a startup. There is a 70% chance the startup fails (returning $0), a 20% chance it returns $200,000, and a 10% chance it returns $1,000,000. What is the expected return on the investment?
E(return) = 0.70(0) + 0.20(200,000) + 0.10(1,000,000) = 0 + 40,000 + 100,000 = $140,000. The expected profit is $140,000 - $100,000 = $40,000.
A bakery makes a special cake each day. It costs $20 to make. If it sells (probability 0.8), the revenue is $55. If it does not sell, the baker salvages $5 worth of ingredients. What is the expected daily profit from this cake?
E(revenue) = 0.8($55) + 0.2($5) = $44 + $1 = $45. E(profit) = $45 - $20 = $25.00.
Real-World Applications
Example: Multiplying X by a constant a changes E(X) to:
E(aX) = a · E(X). Multiplying a random variable by a constant scales the expected value by that constant.

--- Lesson 9.3: Probability Distributions in Real Life (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability Distributions in Real Life
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between the Poisson and exponential distributions?
Poisson counts and exponential waiting times are mathematically linked. The Poisson describes event counts; the exponential describes the continuous time between those events.
What is a 'probability density function' (pdf)?
For continuous distributions, the pdf describes the relative likelihood of values. Probabilities are found by integrating the pdf over an interval, not by reading off individual point values.
What is a 'probability mass function' (pmf)?
The pmf assigns a probability P(X = x) to each possible value of a discrete random variable. The sum of all probabilities must equal 1.
A website receives an average of 3 server errors per day. The IT team wants to know the probability of having no errors tomorrow. Using a Poisson model with lambda = 3, what is P(X = 0)?
P(X = 0) = e^(-3) · 3^0 / 0! = e^(-3) = 0.0498, which rounds to approximately 0.050.
A mechanic knows that the time between breakdowns of a certain machine follows an exponential distribution with a mean of 50 hours. What is the probability the machine runs for more than 50 hours without breaking down?
For exponential with mean 50, rate lambda = 1/50. P(X > 50) = e^(-lambda · 50) = e^(-1) = 0.368. Note the exponential is not symmetric, so P(X > mean) is not 0.5.
A bus arrives at a stop at a random time uniformly distributed between 0 and 30 minutes after each hour. What is the probability a passenger who arrives at a random time waits less than 10 minutes?
With a uniform distribution on [0, 30], P(X < 10) = (10 - 0)/(30 - 0) = 10/30 = 1/3.
A pharmaceutical company tests 500 patients with a new drug. Each patient either responds to the drug (success) or does not. The response rate is estimated at 12%. A researcher asks: 'What is the probability that at least 70 patients respond?' Which distribution should she use?
Each patient is an independent trial with two outcomes and constant p = 0.12, so this is binomial (n = 500, p = 0.12). Since np = 60 and nq = 440 are both large, a normal approximation would also work.
Real-World Applications
Example: Typos per page in a long manuscript follow a:
Typos are rare events occurring at a roughly constant rate in a fixed interval (one page), which matches the Poisson model.
Example: A hospital emergency room receives an average of 8 patients per hour. A manager wants to estimate the probability of receiving exactly 12 patients in the next hour. Which distribution should be used?
Patient arrivals per hour are counts of events in a fixed time interval with a known average rate (8 per hour). This is a classic Poisson scenario with lambda = 8.
Example: A website receives an average of 3 server errors per day. The IT team wants to know the probability of having no errors tomorrow. Using a Poisson model with lambda = 3, what is P(X = 0)?
P(X = 0) = e^(-3) · 3^0 / 0! = e^(-3) = 0.0498, which rounds to approximately 0.050.
Example: Test scores on a standardized exam are normally distributed with mean 500 and standard deviation 100. A school wants to know what proportion of students score above 700. How many standard deviations above the mean is 700?
z = (700 - 500)/100 = 200/100 = 2.0. A score of 700 is exactly 2 standard deviations above the mean. About 2.3% of students score above this level.
Example: An ornithologist counts the number of a rare bird species spotted per day in a national park. Over many days, the average is 2.5 sightings per day. On a given day, what distribution should model the number of sightings?
Rare, independent sightings occurring at a constant average rate (2.5 per day) in a fixed time interval (one day) follow a Poisson distribution with lambda = 2.5.
Example: A fitness tracker measures daily step counts for a large population. The data is roughly bell-shaped with a mean of 7,500 steps and standard deviation of 2,000 steps. Using the empirical rule, approximately what percentage of people take between 5,500 and 9,500 steps?
5,500 and 9,500 are each 1 standard deviation from the mean (7,500 +/- 2,000). By the 68-95-99.7 empirical rule, about 68% of the data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean.
Example: A scientist records the time between earthquakes of magnitude 5.0 or greater in a certain region. The average time between such earthquakes is 120 days. She wants to model the probability that the next earthquake occurs within 60 days. Which distribution is most appropriate?
The time between random, independent events (earthquakes) occurring at a roughly constant rate is modeled by the exponential distribution. With mean = 120 days, rate lambda = 1/120 per day.
Important Concepts
What does it mean when we say a distribution is 'right-skewed'
A right-skewed distribution has most values concentrated on the left with a long tail stretching to the right. The mean is typically greater than the median in right-skewed distributions.

--- Lesson 9.4: Chi-Square Tests (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Chi-Square Tests
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a goodness-of-fit test?
A goodness-of-fit test compares observed category counts to the counts expected under a hypothesized distribution to see if the data 'fit' the model.
What is a test of independence?
A test of independence uses a contingency table to examine whether there is an association between two categorical variables from a single sample.
What is a test of homogeneity?
A homogeneity test compares the distribution of a categorical variable across two or more populations to see if they share the same proportions.
What is the difference between independence and homogeneity tests?
Both use the same chi-square formula and same df calculation. The difference is conceptual: independence examines two variables from one sample; homogeneity compares one variable's distribution across separate populations.
A geneticist crosses two heterozygous pea plants and observes 315 round-yellow, 108 round-green, 101 wrinkled-yellow, and 32 wrinkled-green offspring. The expected Mendelian ratio is 9:3:3:1. With 556 total offspring, what is the expected count for round-yellow?
The 9:3:3:1 ratio means 9/16 should be round-yellow. Expected count = 556 x (9/16) = 556 x 0.5625 = 312.75.
A hospital compares patient satisfaction (satisfied, neutral, dissatisfied) across three departments (surgery, ER, pediatrics). The row totals are Surgery: 200, ER: 150, Pediatrics: 100. The column totals are Satisfied: 250, Neutral: 120, Dissatisfied: 80. What is the expected count for the Surgery-Satisfied cell?
Grand total = 200 + 150 + 100 = 450. Expected = (Row total x Column total) / Grand total = (200 x 250) / 450 = 50,000 / 450 = 111.1.
A market researcher surveys 400 consumers about brand preference (A, B, C, D). She expects equal preference across all four brands. She observes: A = 120, B = 95, C = 90, D = 95. The chi-square value is 4.75. With df = 3 and a critical value of 7.815 at alpha = 0.05, what is the conclusion?
Chi-square = 4.75 < critical value 7.815, so we fail to reject H-null. Expected count for each brand = 400/4 = 100 (all >= 5). The observed differences are not statistically significant.
A school district examines whether school lunch choice (hot meal, sandwich, salad) is independent of grade level (elementary, middle, high). The chi-square test yields chi-square = 15.8 with df = 4. The critical value at alpha = 0.01 is 13.28. What is the conclusion?
Chi-square = 15.8 > critical value 13.28 at alpha = 0.01, so we reject H-null. Lunch choice and grade level are significantly associated even at the 1% significance level.
A die is rolled 120 times with results: 1 (25 times), 2 (17 times), 3 (19 times), 4 (22 times), 5 (18 times), 6 (19 times). If the die is fair, each expected count is 20. What is the chi-square contribution from face 1?
Chi-square contribution from face 1 = (O - E)^2 / E = (25 - 20)^2 / 20 = 25/20 = 1.25.
Real-World Applications
Example: A school district examines whether school lunch choice (hot meal, sandwich, salad) is independent of grade level (elementary, middle, high). The chi-square test yields chi-square = 15.8 with df = 4. The critical value at alpha = 0.01 is 13.28. What is the conclusion?

--- Lesson 9.5: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A farmer tests four fertilizers on crop yield. The ANOVA table shows SSB = 450, SSW = 900, with k = 4 groups and n = 40 total plants. What is the F-statistic?
MSB = SSB/(k-1) = 450/3 = 150. MSW = SSW/(n-k) = 900/36 = 25. F = MSB/MSW = 150/25 = 6.00.
A nutritionist compares calorie intake across three age groups. She has 15, 18, and 12 participants in the groups respectively (n = 45 total). If SSB = 12,000 and SSW = 36,000, what is the F-statistic?
MSB = 12,000/(3-1) = 6,000. MSW = 36,000/(45-3) = 36,000/42 = 857.14. F = 6,000/857.14 = 7.00.
An ANOVA with k = 4 groups and n = 60 yields SSB = 300 and SST = 1500. What is eta-squared, and how would you interpret it?
Eta-squared = SSB/SST = 300/1500 = 0.20. This means 20% of the total variance in the outcome is explained by group membership, which is a medium-to-large effect in many fields.
Real-World Applications
Example: Why is running multiple t-tests instead of ANOVA problematic?
With k groups, there are k(k-1)/2 possible pairwise comparisons. Each t-test has its own chance of a Type I error, and these compound, greatly inflating the overall false positive rate.
Example: A psychologist tests three therapy methods (CBT, medication, combined) on depression scores. With 20 patients per group (60 total) and k = 3 groups, how many degrees of freedom does MSB have?
df(between) = k - 1 = 3 - 1 = 2. The within-group df would be n - k = 60 - 3 = 57.
Example: A teacher compares test scores across four sections of the same course. ANOVA gives F = 1.12 with p = 0.35. At alpha = 0.05, what should the teacher conclude?
Since p = 0.35 > alpha = 0.05, we fail to reject H-null. F = 1.12 is close to 1, suggesting little difference between sections. Post-hoc tests are not warranted when ANOVA fails to reject.
Example: A nutritionist compares calorie intake across three age groups. She has 15, 18, and 12 participants in the groups respectively (n = 45 total). If SSB = 12,000 and SSW = 36,000, what is the F-statistic?
MSB = 12,000/(3-1) = 6,000. MSW = 36,000/(45-3) = 36,000/42 = 857.14. F = 6,000/857.14 = 7.00.
Example: A pharmaceutical company tests three drug dosages (low, medium, high) on blood pressure reduction. ANOVA yields p = 0.008. Tukey's HSD shows: Low vs. Medium (p = 0.42), Low vs. High (p = 0.005), Medium vs. High (p = 0.03). Which pairs differ significantly at alpha = 0.05?
Low vs. High (p = 0.005 < 0.05) and Medium vs. High (p = 0.03 < 0.05) are both significant. Low vs. Medium (p = 0.42 > 0.05) is not. The high dose produces significantly different results from both other doses.

Nonparametric tests are called 'distribution-free' because they do not assume the data come from any particular probability distribution family.

The sign test uses only the direction of differences (positive or negative), not their magnitude. It is one of the simplest nonparametric tests and is very robust but less powerful than the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

The Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test and the Mann-Whitney U Test are mathematically equivalent tests for comparing two independent groups using ranked data.

--- Lesson 9.7: Case Studies in Psychology & Biology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Psychology & Biology
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'p-hacking'?
P-hacking includes running many analyses and reporting only significant ones, removing data points to achieve significance, or testing many variables until one is significant. It inflates false positive rates.
What is the 'placebo effect'?
The placebo effect demonstrates that psychological expectations can produce real physiological improvements. This is why placebo-controlled trials are essential for separating true drug effects from psychological effects.
What is a 'confounding variable'?
A confounder is associated with both the independent and dependent variables, potentially making it appear that a treatment has an effect when the real cause is the confounding variable. Randomization helps control for confounders.
What is 'ecological validity' in psychological studies?
Ecological validity concerns whether findings from controlled laboratory experiments apply to real-world situations. High ecological validity means the results are meaningful outside the lab setting.
What is the importance of effect size in addition to p-values?
A p-value tells you whether an effect is statistically significant; effect size tells you how big the effect is. With large samples, even tiny, practically meaningless differences can reach statistical significance.
A psychologist conducts an RCT comparing CBT (cognitive behavioral therapy) to a waitlist control for treating anxiety. The CBT group (n = 40) shows a mean anxiety score of 12 (SD = 5) and the control group (n = 40) shows a mean of 18 (SD = 6). What is the approximate Cohen's d?
Pooled SD = sqrt[(5^2 + 6^2)/2] = sqrt[(25 + 36)/2] = sqrt(30.5) = 5.52. Cohen's d = (18 - 12)/5.52 = 6/5.52 = 1.09. This is a large effect.
A wildlife researcher marks 200 fish in a lake, releases them, and later captures 150 fish, of which 30 are marked. What is the estimated population size?
Using mark-recapture: N = (M x C) / R = (200 x 150) / 30 = 30,000 / 30 = 1,000 fish.
A genetics study tests Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium for a gene with two alleles (A, a) where p(A) = 0.6 and q(a) = 0.4. In a population of 500 individuals, the expected genotype counts under HWE are: AA = 180, Aa = 240, aa = 80. The observed counts are: AA = 200, Aa = 200, aa = 100. The chi-square value is 8.33 with df = 1 and the critical value at alpha = 0.05 is 3.84. What is the conclusion?
Since chi-square = 8.33 > critical value 3.84, we reject H-null. The population significantly deviates from HWE, suggesting evolutionary forces (selection, drift, mating patterns) may be acting on this gene.
A psychology lab runs 20 separate statistical tests on data from one experiment, each at alpha = 0.05. They find 3 significant results. A reviewer suggests the findings may be due to multiple testing. If a Bonferroni correction is applied, what is the new significance threshold per test?
Bonferroni correction: alpha_adjusted = 0.05 / 20 = 0.0025. Each individual test must achieve p < 0.0025 to be considered significant. Without knowing the exact p-values, the 3 'significant' results may no longer meet this stricter threshold.
A biology study uses ANOVA to compare the growth rates of bacteria under 4 antibiotic concentrations (0, low, medium, high). Results: F = 12.4, p < 0.001. Post-hoc tests show the high concentration group differs from all others, but the 0, low, and medium groups do not differ from each other. What is the practical conclusion?
ANOVA was significant (p < 0.001), and post-hoc tests revealed the source: only the high concentration group is different. This suggests a dose-response threshold where low and medium doses are insufficient to affect bacterial growth.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
With only 8 mice per group, highly skewed data, and an outlier, the t-test's normality assumption is violated and the CLT cannot compensate. The Mann-Whitney U Test is the robust nonparametric alternative.
Real-World Applications
Example: A psychologist conducts an RCT comparing CBT (cognitive behavioral therapy) to a waitlist control for treating anxiety. The CBT group (n = 40) shows a mean anxiety score of 12 (SD = 5) and the control group (n = 40) shows a mean of 18 (SD = 6). What is the approximate Cohen's d?
Pooled SD = sqrt[(5^2 + 6^2)/2] = sqrt[(25 + 36)/2] = sqrt(30.5) = 5.52. Cohen's d = (18 - 12)/5.52 = 6/5.52 = 1.09. This is a large effect.
Example: A psychology experiment tests whether background music affects memory. Participants are randomly assigned to silence, classical music, or pop music conditions. Memory scores are compared using ANOVA, yielding F = 6.45 and p = 0.003. Which follow-up is appropriate?
ANOVA tells us at least one group differs, but not which ones. Post-hoc tests like Tukey's HSD compare all pairs (silence vs. classical, silence vs. pop, classical vs. pop) to identify the specific differences.
Example: A psychology lab runs 20 separate statistical tests on data from one experiment, each at alpha = 0.05. They find 3 significant results. A reviewer suggests the findings may be due to multiple testing. If a Bonferroni correction is applied, what is the new significance threshold per test?
Bonferroni correction: alpha_adjusted = 0.05 / 20 = 0.0025. Each individual test must achieve p < 0.0025 to be considered significant. Without knowing the exact p-values, the 3 'significant' results may no longer meet this stricter threshold.
Important Concepts
A researcher studies whether a gene therapy affects tumor size in mice. She has a treatment group and a control group, each with 8 mice. The tumor size data are highly skewed with one extreme outlier. Which analysis approach is most appropriate
With only 8 mice per group, highly skewed data, and an outlier, the t-test's normality assumption is violated and the CLT cannot compensate. The Mann-Whitney U Test is the robust nonparametric alternative.

    [ ] Smaller samples
    [ ] Ignoring assumptions
    [*] Larger samples and stronger effect sizes
    Explanation: Larger sample sizes reduce sampling variability, and stronger effects are easier to detect. Both increase the probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis.

  Q10. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium is tested using:
    [ ] ANOVA
    [ ] Linear regression
    [ ] t-test
    [*] Chi-square goodness-of-fit
    Explanation: Chi-square goodness-of-fit tests compare observed genotype frequencies to the frequencies expected under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (p² + 2pq + q² = 1).

  Q11. What does 'Cohen's d' measure?
    [ ] The Type I error rate of the hypothesis test at the chosen significance level
    [*] The standardized difference between two group means, expressed in standard deviation units
    [ ] The sample size needed for a study to achieve a given level of statistical power
    [ ] The p-value of a t-test measuring the probability of obtaining the observed data
    Explanation: Cohen's d = (mean1 - mean2) / pooled SD. It quantifies the magnitude of the difference between two groups in standardized units, providing a measure of practical significance.

  Q12. What is 'p-hacking'?
    [*] Manipulating data analyses or selectively reporting results to obtain statistically significant p-values
    [ ] Publishing only null results while withholding all significant findings from the literature
    [ ] The correct calculation of p-values using established formulas for hypothesis testing in statistics
    [ ] A method for improving statistical power by increasing sample size or reducing measurement error
    Explanation: P-hacking includes running many analyses and reporting only significant ones, removing data points to achieve significance, or testing many variables until one is significant. It inflates false positive rates.

  Q13. What does 'pre-registration' of a study mean?
    [ ] Using larger significance levels
    [*] Publicly specifying hypotheses, methods, and analysis plans before data collection to prevent selective reporting
    [ ] Publishing results before peer review
    [ ] Registering patients before they enter a clinical trial
    Explanation: Pre-registration combats the replication crisis by locking in the research plan before data are collected, preventing researchers from adjusting hypotheses or analyses after seeing results.

  Q14. What is the 'placebo effect'?
    [ ] The worsening of symptoms in the control group due to disappointment about not receiving the drug
    [ ] A statistical error in analysis that inflates the apparent effect of the treatment beyond its true size
    [ ] The effect caused by the active drug ingredient interacting with the patient's existing medications
    [*] Improvement in patients' conditions caused by the belief that they are receiving treatment, even when the treatment is inactive
    Explanation: The placebo effect demonstrates that psychological expectations can produce real physiological improvements. This is why placebo-controlled trials are essential for separating true drug effects from psychological effects.

  Q15. What does 'intent-to-treat analysis' mean?
    [*] Analyzing all participants as originally assigned to groups, regardless of whether they completed treatment
    [ ] Analyzing only the control group
    [ ] Analyzing only patients who completed treatment
    [ ] Analyzing patients based on the treatment they actually received
    Explanation: Intent-to-treat (ITT) analysis preserves the benefits of randomization by analyzing every participant according to their original group assignment, even if they dropped out or switched treatments.

  Q16. What does 'publication bias' mean?
    [ ] Bias introduced by the publisher's political views
    [ ] All studies are equally likely to be published
    [ ] Only high-quality studies get published
    [*] Studies with significant results are more likely to be published than those with null results, skewing the literature
    Explanation: Publication bias means the published literature overrepresents positive findings because null results are less likely to be submitted or accepted for publication, creating a distorted view of the evidence.

  Q17. What is a 'confounding variable'?
    [ ] A variable that is measured very precisely
    [ ] A variable that has no effect on anything
    [*] A variable related to both the treatment and outcome that can create a spurious association
    [ ] The dependent variable in an experiment
    Explanation: A confounder is associated with both the independent and dependent variables, potentially making it appear that a treatment has an effect when the real cause is the confounding variable. Randomization helps control for confounders.

  Q18. What does 'Bonferroni correction' do?
    [ ] Eliminates the need for hypothesis testing altogether by providing direct probability estimates instead
    [*] Adjusts the significance level downward (alpha/m) when performing m multiple comparisons to control the family-wise error rate
    [ ] Replaces ANOVA with individual t-tests for each pair of groups in the analysis without any penalty
    [ ] Increases the significance level for each test to make it easier to detect smaller effects
    Explanation: The Bonferroni correction divides the desired alpha level by the number of tests (m) to maintain the overall Type I error rate. For example, with 10 tests at alpha = 0.05, each individual test uses alpha = 0.005.

  Q19. What is 'ecological validity' in psychological studies?
    [ ] Whether the study used ecological data
    [ ] Whether the study was conducted outdoors
    [ ] A measure of statistical power
    [*] The degree to which study findings generalize to real-world settings and behaviors
    Explanation: Ecological validity concerns whether findings from controlled laboratory experiments apply to real-world situations. High ecological validity means the results are meaningful outside the lab setting.

  Q20. What is the importance of effect size in addition to p-values?
    [ ] Effect size replaces all statistical tests
    [ ] P-values alone are always sufficient
    [ ] Effect size and p-value measure the same thing
    [*] Effect size shows practical significance: a small p-value can come from a trivially small effect in a very large sample
    Explanation: A p-value tells you whether an effect is statistically significant; effect size tells you how big the effect is. With large samples, even tiny, practically meaningless differences can reach statistical significance.

  Q21. A psychologist conducts an RCT comparing CBT (cognitive behavioral therapy) to a waitlist control for treating anxiety. The CBT group (n = 40) shows a mean anxiety score of 12 (SD = 5) and the control group (n = 40) shows a mean of 18 (SD = 6). What is the approximate Cohen's d?
    [ ] 0.20
    [ ] 6.00
    [*] 1.09
    [ ] 0.50
    Explanation: Pooled SD = sqrt[(5^2 + 6^2)/2] = sqrt[(25 + 36)/2] = sqrt(30.5) = 5.52. Cohen's d = (18 - 12)/5.52 = 6/5.52 = 1.09. This is a large effect.

  Q22. A biologist crosses heterozygous plants and obtains 120 offspring: 85 dominant phenotype and 35 recessive phenotype. The expected Mendelian ratio is 3:1. What are the expected counts and what test should be used?
    [ ] Expected: 85 dominant, 35 recessive; no test needed
    [ ] Expected: 100 dominant, 20 recessive; use ANOVA
    [*] Expected: 90 dominant, 30 recessive; use chi-square goodness-of-fit
    [ ] Expected: 60 dominant, 60 recessive; use a t-test
    Explanation: With a 3:1 ratio and 120 total, expected counts are 120 x 3/4 = 90 dominant and 120 x 1/4 = 30 recessive. A chi-square goodness-of-fit test compares observed (85, 35) to expected (90, 30).

  Q23. A wildlife researcher marks 200 fish in a lake, releases them, and later captures 150 fish, of which 30 are marked. What is the estimated population size?
    [*] 1,000
    [ ] 750
    [ ] 300
    [ ] 500
    Explanation: Using mark-recapture: N = (M x C) / R = (200 x 150) / 30 = 30,000 / 30 = 1,000 fish.

  Q24. A pharmaceutical company runs a Phase III trial with 1,000 patients. The drug group shows 3 points lower blood pressure than placebo (p = 0.001), but the clinical threshold for meaningful change is 10 points. What should be reported?
    [*] The result is statistically significant but the effect size (3 points) may not be clinically meaningful since it is below the 10-point threshold
    [ ] The study should be discarded because the effect is small
    [ ] The drug is highly effective because p < 0.001
    [ ] A p-value of 0.001 always means the treatment is practically important
    Explanation: This illustrates the difference between statistical and practical significance. With 1,000 patients, even a small 3-point difference can be statistically significant, but it falls short of the 10-point clinical threshold.

  Q25. A psychology experiment tests whether background music affects memory. Participants are randomly assigned to silence, classical music, or pop music conditions. Memory scores are compared using ANOVA, yielding F = 6.45 and p = 0.003. Which follow-up is appropriate?
    [*] Post-hoc pairwise comparisons (e.g., Tukey's HSD) to determine which specific conditions differ
    [ ] The study must be repeated before any conclusions can be drawn
    [ ] No follow-up is needed since ANOVA was significant
    [ ] A chi-square test to examine the same data
    Explanation: ANOVA tells us at least one group differs, but not which ones. Post-hoc tests like Tukey's HSD compare all pairs (silence vs. classical, silence vs. pop, classical vs. pop) to identify the specific differences.

  Q26. A researcher studies whether a gene therapy affects tumor size in mice. She has a treatment group and a control group, each with 8 mice. The tumor size data are highly skewed with one extreme outlier. Which analysis approach is most appropriate?
    [*] Mann-Whitney U Test because the data are non-normal and the sample is small
    [ ] Chi-square test of independence
    [ ] One-way ANOVA
    [ ] Independent t-test since there are two groups
    Explanation: With only 8 mice per group, highly skewed data, and an outlier, the t-test's normality assumption is violated and the CLT cannot compensate. The Mann-Whitney U Test is the robust nonparametric alternative.

  Q27. A double-blind placebo-controlled study of a new antidepressant finds that 60% of the drug group improved vs. 40% of the placebo group. With n = 100 per group, the result is significant (p = 0.004). A critic notes that 40% of the placebo group also improved. What does this highlight?
    [ ] Double-blinding failed because the placebo group showed improvement
    [ ] The drug does not work at all
    [ ] The study is invalid because the placebo group improved
    [*] The placebo effect is substantial (40% improvement), so the drug's true incremental benefit is 20 percentage points above placebo
    Explanation: The placebo effect is a real phenomenon. The drug's benefit must be evaluated relative to placebo: 60% - 40% = 20 percentage points of improvement attributable to the drug itself.

  Q28. A genetics study tests Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium for a gene with two alleles (A, a) where p(A) = 0.6 and q(a) = 0.4. In a population of 500 individuals, the expected genotype counts under HWE are: AA = 180, Aa = 240, aa = 80. The observed counts are: AA = 200, Aa = 200, aa = 100. The chi-square value is 8.33 with df = 1 and the critical value at alpha = 0.05 is 3.84. What is the conclusion?
    [ ] Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium cannot be tested with chi-square
    [*] The population deviates significantly from Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (chi-square = 8.33 > 3.84)
    [ ] The population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium
    [ ] The test is inconclusive
    Explanation: Since chi-square = 8.33 > critical value 3.84, we reject H-null. The population significantly deviates from HWE, suggesting evolutionary forces (selection, drift, mating patterns) may be acting on this gene.

  Q29. A psychology lab runs 20 separate statistical tests on data from one experiment, each at alpha = 0.05. They find 3 significant results. A reviewer suggests the findings may be due to multiple testing. If a Bonferroni correction is applied, what is the new significance threshold per test?
    [*] 0.0025
    [ ] 0.001
    [ ] 0.01
    [ ] 0.05
    Explanation: Bonferroni correction: alpha_adjusted = 0.05 / 20 = 0.0025. Each individual test must achieve p < 0.0025 to be considered significant. Without knowing the exact p-values, the 3 'significant' results may no longer meet this stricter threshold.

  Q30. A biology study uses ANOVA to compare the growth rates of bacteria under 4 antibiotic concentrations (0, low, medium, high). Results: F = 12.4, p < 0.001. Post-hoc tests show the high concentration group differs from all others, but the 0, low, and medium groups do not differ from each other. What is the practical conclusion?
    [*] Only the high antibiotic concentration significantly reduces bacterial growth; lower concentrations have no measurable effect compared to the control
    [ ] The antibiotic is completely ineffective at all concentrations
    [ ] All antibiotic concentrations reduce bacterial growth equally
    [ ] The ANOVA result is invalid because not all groups differ
    Explanation: ANOVA was significant (p < 0.001), and post-hoc tests revealed the source: only the high concentration group is different. This suggests a dose-response threshold where low and medium doses are insufficient to affect bacterial growth.
