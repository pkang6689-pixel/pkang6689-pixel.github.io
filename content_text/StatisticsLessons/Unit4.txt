--- Lesson 4.1: Basic Probability Rules (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Basic Probability Rules
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. What is P(heart)?
There are 13 hearts in a 52-card deck. P(heart) = 13/52 = 1/4.
If you roll a fair die, what is the probability of rolling an even number?
Even outcomes: {2, 4, 6} = 3 outcomes out of 6. P(even) = 3/6 = 1/2.
What is a 'sample space'?
The sample space (S) is the complete set of all possible outcomes. For a coin: S = {H, T}.
What is the 'complement' of an event?
The complement of event A (written A' or Ā) contains all outcomes in the sample space that are not in A. P(A') = 1 − P(A).
What is an 'event' in probability?
An event is any subset of the sample space. 'Rolling an even number' is the event {2, 4, 6}.
What is 'theoretical probability'?
Theoretical probability uses logical reasoning about the structure of the experiment: P(E) = (favorable outcomes) / (total outcomes), assuming equally likely outcomes.
What is an 'impossible event'?
An impossible event has P(E) = 0. Example: rolling a 7 on a standard six-sided die.
A bag contains 5 red, 3 blue, and 2 green marbles. If one marble is drawn at random, what is the probability of drawing a red or blue marble?
Total marbles = 10. Red or blue = 5 + 3 = 8 (mutually exclusive). P = 8/10 = 4/5.
In a class of 30 students, 18 play sports, 12 are in the band, and 5 do both. What is the probability that a randomly selected student plays sports OR is in the band?
P(Sports or Band) = P(S) + P(B) − P(S and B) = 18/30 + 12/30 − 5/30 = 25/30 = 5/6.
A weather forecast says there is a 40% chance of rain tomorrow. What is the probability it does NOT rain?
By the complement rule: P(no rain) = 1 − P(rain) = 1 − 0.40 = 0.60 = 60%.
A card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. What is the probability that the card is a king or a heart?
P(King or Heart) = P(King) + P(Heart) − P(King of Hearts) = 4/52 + 13/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13.
A company finds that 2% of its products are defective. If a customer buys one product, what is the probability it is NOT defective?
P(not defective) = 1 − P(defective) = 1 − 0.02 = 0.98.
A survey of 200 employees shows: 90 drink coffee only, 50 drink tea only, 40 drink both, and 20 drink neither. What is the probability a randomly selected employee drinks coffee or tea?
Coffee drinkers: 90 + 40 = 130. Tea drinkers: 50 + 40 = 90. P(C or T) = P(C) + P(T) − P(C and T) = 130/200 + 90/200 − 40/200 = 180/200 = 0.90.
A coin is flipped 1000 times and lands heads 520 times. What is the empirical probability of heads?
Empirical probability = 520/1000 = 0.52. As the number of flips increases, this should approach the theoretical 0.50 for a fair coin.
A spinner is divided into 5 equal sections: red, blue, green, yellow, and purple. What is the probability of landing on red or green?
Red and green are mutually exclusive. P(red or green) = 1/5 + 1/5 = 2/5.
In a hospital, records show that 15% of patients have diabetes, 10% have heart disease, and 4% have both. What is the probability that a randomly selected patient has diabetes or heart disease?
P(D or H) = P(D) + P(H) − P(D and H) = 0.15 + 0.10 − 0.04 = 0.21.
A school raffle sells 500 tickets. You buy 3 tickets. Your friend buys 7 tickets. What is the probability that either you or your friend wins the single prize?
Your winning and your friend winning are mutually exclusive (only one prize). P = 3/500 + 7/500 = 10/500 = 1/50.

--- Lesson 4.2: Counting Principles (permutations, combinations) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Counting Principles (permutations, combinations)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a factorial?
n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ... × 2 × 1. For example, 4! = 24.
What is a permutation?
A permutation is an arrangement where order matters. ABC is different from CBA.
What is a combination?
A combination counts selections without regard to order. Choosing {A, B, C} is the same selection as {C, A, B}.
What is a 'permutation with repetition'?
When some items are identical, we divide by the factorials of the repeat counts. Example: 'MISSISSIPPI' has 11!/(4!4!2!) arrangements.
What is a 'circular permutation'?
Circular permutations fix one object to eliminate rotational duplicates. n objects in a circle = (n−1)! arrangements.
What is the 'addition principle' in counting?
The addition principle is used when choosing between mutually exclusive alternatives: total ways = sum of ways for each alternative.

--- Lesson 4.3: Independent & Dependent Events (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Independent & Dependent Events
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A bag has 4 red and 6 blue marbles. Two marbles are drawn without replacement. What is P(both red)?
P(1st red) = 4/10. P(2nd red | 1st red) = 3/9. P(both red) = (4/10)(3/9) = 12/90 = 2/15.
Rolling a die twice: what is the probability of getting a 6 on both rolls?
Each roll is independent. P(6 and 6) = (1/6)(1/6) = 1/36.
What is the 'multiplication rule' for probability?
The general multiplication rule uses conditional probability. For independent events, P(B|A) = P(B), simplifying the formula.
What is the 'at least one' probability technique?
The complement method: P(at least one success) = 1 − P(zero successes). This is often easier to compute than adding all individual cases.
What is meant by 'testing for independence' using probability?
If P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B), the events satisfy the mathematical definition of independence.
What is the 'general multiplication rule'?
The general multiplication rule works for all event pairs. For independent events it simplifies since P(B|A) = P(B).
A drug test is 95% accurate. If two independent tests are administered to the same person, what is the probability both tests are correct?
P(both correct) = 0.95 × 0.95 = 0.9025. The tests are independent.
A jar contains 8 red and 12 blue candies. You eat one candy (without replacement), then pick another. What is the probability both are red?
P(1st red) = 8/20. P(2nd red|1st red) = 7/19. P(both red) = (8/20)(7/19) = 56/380 ≈ 0.147.
The probability of rain on Saturday is 0.3 and on Sunday is 0.5 (independently). What is the probability it rains on at least one day of the weekend?
P(no rain either day) = (1−0.3)(1−0.5) = 0.7 × 0.5 = 0.35. P(at least one day) = 1 − 0.35 = 0.65.

--- Lesson 4.4: Conditional Probability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conditional Probability
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A two-way table shows 200 students: 80 male athletes, 40 male non-athletes, 50 female athletes, 30 female non-athletes. Given a student is male, what is P(athlete | male)?
Males total = 80 + 40 = 120. P(athlete|male) = 80/120 = 2/3.
In a class, 60% of students passed the final. Of those who studied, 85% passed. Of those who didn't study, 30% passed. If 70% of students studied, what is P(studied | passed)?
P(passed) = P(passed|studied)P(studied) + P(passed|not studied)P(not studied) = 0.85(0.70)+0.30(0.30) = 0.595+0.09 = 0.685. P(studied|passed) = 0.595/0.685 ≈ 0.87. Actually recalculating: P(passed) = 0.60 given. But using Bayes: P(studied|passed) = P(passed|studied)×P(studied)/P(passed) = 0.85×0.70/0.60 = 0.595/0.60 ≈ 0.992.
A card is drawn from a standard deck and is known to be red. What is P(heart | red)?
Red cards = 26 (13 hearts + 13 diamonds). P(heart|red) = 13/26 = 1/2.
What is conditional probability?
Conditional probability restricts the sample space to cases where the given event occurred, then finds the probability of the event of interest.
What is Bayes' Theorem?
Bayes' Theorem enables updating the probability of a hypothesis based on new evidence.
What is a 'two-way table' (contingency table)?
A contingency table cross-tabulates two variables, making it easy to compute marginal, joint, and conditional probabilities.
What is 'sensitivity' of a test?
Sensitivity = P(positive test | condition present). High sensitivity means few false negatives.
What is 'specificity' of a test?
Specificity = P(negative test | condition absent). High specificity means few false positives.
What is the 'Law of Total Probability'?
The Law of Total Probability computes P(A) by summing over all mutually exclusive scenarios that partition the sample space.
A factory has two suppliers. Supplier X provides 70% of parts (3% defective). Supplier Y provides 30% (5% defective). A randomly selected part is defective. What is the probability it came from Supplier X?
P(def) = 0.70(0.03) + 0.30(0.05) = 0.021 + 0.015 = 0.036. P(X|def) = 0.021/0.036 ≈ 0.583.
In a school, 40% of students are in STEM. 80% of STEM students use the library weekly. Only 30% of non-STEM students do. If a student uses the library weekly, what is the probability they are in STEM?
P(library) = 0.40(0.80) + 0.60(0.30) = 0.32 + 0.18 = 0.50. P(STEM|library) = 0.32/0.50 = 0.64.
A medical test has 95% sensitivity and 90% specificity. The disease prevalence is 2%. If a person tests positive, what is the approximate probability they have the disease?
P(+) = 0.95(0.02) + 0.10(0.98) = 0.019 + 0.098 = 0.117. P(disease|+) = 0.019/0.117 ≈ 0.162 or about 16%.
An email filter classifies 95% of spam correctly and 98% of legitimate emails correctly. If 20% of all emails are spam, what is the probability an email flagged as spam is actually spam?
P(flagged) = 0.95(0.20) + 0.02(0.80) = 0.19 + 0.016 = 0.206. P(spam|flagged) = 0.19/0.206 ≈ 0.922.
A weather model predicts rain 80% of the time when it actually rains, and predicts no rain 90% of the time when it doesn't rain. It rains 30% of days. If the model predicts rain, what is the probability it actually rains?
P(predict rain) = 0.80(0.30) + 0.10(0.70) = 0.24 + 0.07 = 0.31. P(rain|predict rain) = 0.24/0.31 ≈ 0.774.
In a company, 60% of employees are full-time and 40% are part-time. 90% of full-time employees have health insurance, but only 20% of part-time employees do. Given an employee has health insurance, what is the probability they are full-time?
P(insured) = 0.90(0.60) + 0.20(0.40) = 0.54 + 0.08 = 0.62. P(FT|insured) = 0.54/0.62 ≈ 0.871.
A survey reveals: P(owns car) = 0.75, P(owns home) = 0.40, P(owns car and home) = 0.35. What is P(owns home | owns car)?
P(home|car) = P(home and car)/P(car) = 0.35/0.75 ≈ 0.467.
A genetics lab tests for a gene that 5% of the population carries. The test has 98% sensitivity and 97% specificity. If a person tests positive, what is the probability they carry the gene?
P(+) = 0.98(0.05) + 0.03(0.95) = 0.049 + 0.0285 = 0.0775. P(gene|+) = 0.049/0.0775 ≈ 0.632.

P(business) = 0.25(0.55) + 0.35(0.45) = 0.1375 + 0.1575 = 0.295. P(female|business) = 0.1375/0.295 ≈ 0.466.

A probability tree is a branching diagram where each branch represents a possible outcome with its associated probability.

Each branch represents one possible outcome at a decision point, labeled with its probability.
What is a 'node' in a probability tree?
Nodes are decision points where the experiment branches into different possible outcomes.

An endpoint is the termination of a path through the tree. Each endpoint corresponds to one complete outcome of the experiment.

--- Lesson 4.6: Venn Diagrams (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Venn Diagrams
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a Venn diagram?
Venn diagrams use circles (usually within a rectangle representing the universal set) to show logical relationships between sets.
What is the 'universal set' in a Venn diagram?
The universal set (U) contains every element relevant to the problem and is depicted as the enclosing rectangle.
What is De Morgan's Law?
De Morgan's Laws allow us to transform complements of compound sets: the complement of a union becomes an intersection of complements, and vice versa.
What is the 'empty set'?
The empty set has no members. It is a subset of every set and represents impossible events in probability.
A tech company surveyed 300 employees. 200 use Slack, 150 use Teams, and 100 use both. What is P(Slack or Teams) for a randomly selected employee?
P(S ∪ T) = P(S) + P(T) − P(S ∩ T) = 200/300 + 150/300 − 100/300 = 250/300 = 5/6.
Using a Venn diagram, if P(A) = 0.7, P(B) = 0.5, and P(A ∩ B) = 0.3, what is P(A only), i.e., P(A but not B)?
P(A only) = P(A) − P(A ∩ B) = 0.7 − 0.3 = 0.4.
A marketing team surveys 500 customers. 300 received email ads, 250 received social media ads, and 150 received both. If a customer from the survey is selected at random, what is the probability they received neither type of ad?
Union = 300 + 250 − 150 = 400. Neither = 500 − 400 = 100. P(neither) = 100/500 = 0.20.
A student club has members who play soccer (S), basketball (B), or both. |S| = 45, |B| = 30, |S ∩ B| = 12. A rival club claims the total membership is 45 + 30 = 75. Why is this wrong, and what is the correct total of members who play at least one sport?
Adding set sizes directly double-counts members in both sets. The inclusion-exclusion principle: |S ∪ B| = 45 + 30 − 12 = 63.

--- Lesson 4.7: Applications in Games of Chance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Games of Chance
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'expected value' in probability?
EV = Σ(xᵢ × P(xᵢ)). It represents the theoretical average if the experiment were repeated infinitely.
What is the Gambler's Fallacy?
Each spin, flip, or roll is independent. A roulette wheel doesn't remember previous results; thinking it does is the Gambler's Fallacy.
What is a 'fair game' in probability terms?
In a mathematically fair game, EV = 0. Most casino games are NOT fair because the house edge makes EV negative for the player.
What is 'independent trials' in the context of gambling?
In games like roulette and slots, each trial is independent. The wheel has no memory of previous spins.
What is a 'random number generator' (RNG) in gaming?
RNGs are used in online casinos, video slots, and digital card games to simulate randomness and ensure each outcome is independent.
In American roulette, a player bets $10 on a single number. The payout is 35:1. With 38 slots, what is the expected value of this bet?
EV = (1/38)(35×10) + (37/38)(−10) = 350/38 − 370/38 = −20/38 ≈ −$0.53.
You play a game where you flip a fair coin. Heads: you win $3. Tails: you lose $2. What is the expected value per flip?
EV = 0.5(3) + 0.5(−2) = 1.50 − 1.00 = +$0.50. This game favors the player.
A scratch-off lottery ticket costs $5. Prize structure: 1 in 4 tickets wins $5, 1 in 100 wins $50, 1 in 10,000 wins $10,000. What is the approximate expected value?
EV = (0.25)(5) + (0.01)(50) + (0.0001)(10,000) + (remaining prob)(0) − 5 = 1.25 + 0.50 + 1.00 − 5 = −$2.25.
Real-World Applications
Example: A slot machine has a 95% payback rate. A player inserts $100. On average, how much will the player have after one complete cycle of play?
A 95% payback rate means the machine returns $0.95 per $1 wagered on average. $100 × 0.95 = $95. House edge = 5%.

--- Lesson 4.8: Probability in Genetics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability in Genetics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Cystic fibrosis is autosomal recessive. Two carrier parents (Aa) have a child. What is the probability the child is a carrier but not affected?
Aa × Aa: P(Aa) = 2/4 = 1/2. Carriers (Aa) have the allele but don't show symptoms.
What is a Punnett square?
A Punnett square lists one parent's alleles along the top and the other along the side, showing all possible offspring combinations.
What is 'incomplete dominance'?
In incomplete dominance, Aa produces an intermediate phenotype (e.g., red × white = pink flowers).
What is the Hardy-Weinberg principle?
The Hardy-Weinberg principle (p² + 2pq + q² = 1) provides the expected genotype frequencies when no evolution is occurring.
A carrier mother (X^A X^a) and a normal father (X^A Y) have a son. What is the probability the son is color-blind (X^a Y)?
Sons get Y from father and either X^A or X^a (equally likely) from mother. P(X^a Y) = 1/2 = 50%.
In a population where q² = 0.04 (4% homozygous recessive), what is the frequency of carriers (2pq) under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium?
q² = 0.04, so q = 0.20 and p = 0.80. Carrier frequency = 2pq = 2(0.80)(0.20) = 0.32 = 32%.
A couple has 4 children. Each child independently has a 3/4 probability of having brown eyes (dominant) and 1/4 of having blue eyes (recessive). What is the probability all 4 have brown eyes?
P(all 4 brown) = (3/4)⁴ = 81/256 ≈ 0.316.
Huntington's disease is autosomal dominant (H). A father is Hh (affected) and the mother is hh (unaffected). What is the probability their child will develop the disease?
Hh × hh gives Hh and hh in equal proportions. P(Hh) = 1/2 = 50%, and Hh is affected since H is dominant.
A genetic test reveals a woman is a carrier of hemophilia (X^H X^h). Her partner is normal (X^H Y). What is the probability their daughter is a carrier?
Daughters receive X^H from father. From mother: 50% chance of X^H and 50% chance of X^h. So P(carrier X^H X^h) among daughters = 1/2.
Phenylketonuria (PKU) is autosomal recessive and affects 1 in 10,000 births. Using Hardy-Weinberg, what is the approximate carrier frequency?
q² = 1/10,000 → q = 1/100 = 0.01. p ≈ 0.99. Carrier frequency = 2pq ≈ 2(0.99)(0.01) ≈ 0.02 = 1 in 50.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Aa × Aa: P(Aa) = 2/4 = 1/2. Carriers (Aa) have the allele but don't show symptoms.
Hh × hh gives Hh and hh in equal proportions. P(Hh) = 1/2 = 50%, and Hh is affected since H is dominant.
Each child's genotype is determined independently by random allele segregation. Past offspring have no effect on future probabilities.
Important Concepts

    [ ] Girls are more likely
    Explanation: The father contributes either an X or Y chromosome with roughly equal probability, giving approximately 50% chance for each sex.

  Q7. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium applies to populations that are:
    [ ] Small and undergoing natural selection
    [ ] Experiencing rapid genetic drift
    [*] Large, randomly mating, with no mutation, migration, or natural selection
    [ ] Isolated on islands only
    Explanation: Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium requires a large population, random mating, no mutation, no migration, and no selection.

  Q8. In the Hardy-Weinberg equation p² + 2pq + q² = 1, the term 2pq represents:
    [*] The frequency of heterozygous individuals (carriers)
    [ ] The frequency of homozygous recessive individuals
    [ ] The frequency of homozygous dominant individuals
    [ ] The total population size
    Explanation: p² = homozygous dominant (AA), 2pq = heterozygous (Aa), q² = homozygous recessive (aa).

  Q9. Cystic fibrosis is autosomal recessive. Two carrier parents (Aa) have a child. What is the probability the child is a carrier but not affected?
    [*] 1/2
    [ ] 0
    [ ] 1/4
    [ ] 3/4
    Explanation: Aa × Aa: P(Aa) = 2/4 = 1/2. Carriers (Aa) have the allele but don't show symptoms.

  Q10. X-linked recessive traits (like color blindness) are more common in males because:
    [ ] Females cannot carry the allele
    [ ] The Y chromosome blocks dominant alleles
    [*] Males have only one X chromosome, so one recessive allele is sufficient for expression
    [ ] Males have two X chromosomes
    Explanation: Males are XY; a single recessive allele on the X chromosome is expressed since there's no second X to mask it.

  Q11. What is a Punnett square?
    [ ] A type of histogram for genetic data that displays allele frequencies in a population
    [ ] A statistical test for genetics that evaluates whether observed ratios match expected
    [ ] A square-shaped gene that determines the physical structure of traits in organisms
    [*] A grid used to predict the genotype and phenotype ratios of offspring from a genetic cross
    Explanation: A Punnett square lists one parent's alleles along the top and the other along the side, showing all possible offspring combinations.

  Q12. What does 'genotype' mean?
    [ ] The physical appearance of an organism
    [ ] The number of chromosomes
    [ ] The type of gene an organism prefers
    [*] The specific combination of alleles an organism carries for a trait (e.g., AA, Aa, or aa)
    Explanation: Genotype is the genetic makeup—the actual alleles present at a specific locus.

  Q13. What does 'phenotype' mean?
    [ ] The genetic makeup of an organism
    [*] The observable physical characteristics of an organism, determined by its genotype and environment
    [ ] The number of alleles an organism has
    [ ] A type of phone used in genetics labs
    Explanation: Phenotype is what you can observe (e.g., brown eyes). Genotypes AA and Aa may produce the same dominant phenotype.

  Q14. What does 'heterozygous' mean?
    [ ] Having more than two alleles
    [ ] Having no alleles for a gene
    [*] Having two different alleles for a gene (e.g., Aa)
    [ ] Having two identical alleles (e.g., AA)
    Explanation: Heterozygous means carrying one dominant and one recessive allele at a genetic locus.

  Q15. What does 'homozygous' mean?
    [*] Having two identical alleles for a gene (e.g., AA or aa)
    [ ] Being a carrier of a recessive trait
    [ ] Having two different alleles
    [ ] Having only one allele
    Explanation: Homozygous means both alleles are the same—either both dominant (AA) or both recessive (aa).

  Q16. What does 'allele' mean?
    [ ] A protein produced by a gene that performs a specific function in the cell
    [*] One of two or more alternative forms of a gene at a specific location on a chromosome
    [ ] An entire genome, which is the complete set of all genetic material
    [ ] A complete chromosome carrying many genes from one end to the other
    Explanation: Alleles are different versions of the same gene. For example, brown-eye allele (B) and blue-eye allele (b).

  Q17. What does 'carrier' mean in genetics?
    [ ] A person who physically carries another person
    [ ] An individual who shows the recessive trait
    [ ] An individual with the dominant phenotype who is homozygous
    [*] An individual who is heterozygous for a recessive trait and can pass it on without showing symptoms
    Explanation: Carriers (Aa for a recessive disorder) appear unaffected but can pass the recessive allele to offspring.

  Q18. What is 'incomplete dominance'?
    [ ] When no allele is expressed
    [ ] When one allele completely masks the other
    [*] A pattern where the heterozygous phenotype is a blend between the two homozygous phenotypes
    [ ] When both alleles are equally expressed without blending
    Explanation: In incomplete dominance, Aa produces an intermediate phenotype (e.g., red × white = pink flowers).

  Q19. What does 'codominance' mean?
    [ ] A blending of two traits resulting in an intermediate phenotype between both parents
    [ ] Neither allele is expressed in the heterozygous individual, producing no visible trait
    [*] A pattern where both alleles are fully and equally expressed in the heterozygous phenotype
    [ ] One allele dominates the other completely, masking the recessive allele expression
    Explanation: In codominance, both alleles are visibly expressed (e.g., AB blood type shows both A and B antigens).

  Q20. What is the Hardy-Weinberg principle?
    [ ] A method for drawing Punnett squares to predict genotype ratios in genetic crosses
    [ ] A law requiring genetic testing for all populations to monitor public health outcomes
    [*] A mathematical model stating that allele and genotype frequencies remain constant in a population in the absence of evolutionary forces
    [ ] A formula for calculating mutation rates in a population across successive generations
    Explanation: The Hardy-Weinberg principle (p² + 2pq + q² = 1) provides the expected genotype frequencies when no evolution is occurring.

  Q21. Both parents are carriers for sickle cell trait (Aa). They want to know the probability that their next child will have sickle cell disease (aa). What should the genetic counselor tell them?
    [ ] 100%, because both parents carry the recessive allele and all offspring will be affected
    [*] 25%, since Aa × Aa produces 1/4 aa offspring
    [ ] 50%, because half of the offspring inherit the recessive allele from each parent
    [ ] 0% because neither parent is affected, so they cannot pass on the recessive condition
    Explanation: The Punnett square for Aa × Aa: AA(25%), Aa(50%), aa(25%). Each child has a 25% chance of being affected.

  Q22. A carrier mother (X^A X^a) and a normal father (X^A Y) have a son. What is the probability the son is color-blind (X^a Y)?
    [ ] 100%
    [*] 50%
    [ ] 0%
    [ ] 25%
    Explanation: Sons get Y from father and either X^A or X^a (equally likely) from mother. P(X^a Y) = 1/2 = 50%.

  Q23. In a population where q² = 0.04 (4% homozygous recessive), what is the frequency of carriers (2pq) under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium?
    [ ] 4%
    [*] 32%
    [ ] 20%
    [ ] 64%
    Explanation: q² = 0.04, so q = 0.20 and p = 0.80. Carrier frequency = 2pq = 2(0.80)(0.20) = 0.32 = 32%.

  Q24. Two parents with blood types A (genotype AO) and B (genotype BO) have a child. What are the possible blood types of the child?
    [ ] Only O, because the parental genotypes can only produce homozygous recessive offspring
    [*] A, B, AB, or O (all four blood types are possible)
    [ ] Only AB, because one parent contributes A and the other contributes B exclusively
    [ ] Only A or B, because the offspring must inherit one allele from each parent only
    Explanation: AO × BO produces AB, AO, BO, OO. So blood types A, B, AB, and O are all possible.

  Q25. A couple has 4 children. Each child independently has a 3/4 probability of having brown eyes (dominant) and 1/4 of having blue eyes (recessive). What is the probability all 4 have brown eyes?
    [ ] 1/256, which represents the probability of all four children having the recessive trait
    [ ] 3/4, which is the probability that a single offspring shows the dominant phenotype
    [ ] 1/4, which is the probability for one child only, not for three out of four children
    [*] 81/256 ≈ 0.316
    Explanation: P(all 4 brown) = (3/4)⁴ = 81/256 ≈ 0.316.

  Q26. Huntington's disease is autosomal dominant (H). A father is Hh (affected) and the mother is hh (unaffected). What is the probability their child will develop the disease?
    [ ] 25%
    [ ] 100%
    [*] 50%
    [ ] 75%
    Explanation: Hh × hh gives Hh and hh in equal proportions. P(Hh) = 1/2 = 50%, and Hh is affected since H is dominant.

  Q27. In a dihybrid cross (AaBb × AaBb), the expected phenotypic ratio of offspring showing both dominant traits : one dominant one recessive : etc. is:
    [*] 9:3:3:1
    [ ] 1:1:1:1
    [ ] 1:2:1
    [ ] 3:1
    Explanation: A dihybrid cross produces the classic 9:3:3:1 ratio (9 both dominant, 3 first dominant, 3 second dominant, 1 both recessive).

  Q28. A genetic test reveals a woman is a carrier of hemophilia (X^H X^h). Her partner is normal (X^H Y). What is the probability their daughter is a carrier?
    [ ] 25%
    [ ] 100%
    [*] 50%
    [ ] 0%
    Explanation: Daughters receive X^H from father. From mother: 50% chance of X^H and 50% chance of X^h. So P(carrier X^H X^h) among daughters = 1/2.

  Q29. Phenylketonuria (PKU) is autosomal recessive and affects 1 in 10,000 births. Using Hardy-Weinberg, what is the approximate carrier frequency?
    [ ] About 1 in 100 (1%)
    [ ] About 1 in 5 (20%)
    [*] About 1 in 50 (2%)
    [ ] About 1 in 10,000
    Explanation: q² = 1/10,000 → q = 1/100 = 0.01. p ≈ 0.99. Carrier frequency = 2pq ≈ 2(0.99)(0.01) ≈ 0.02 = 1 in 50.

  Q30. A genetic counselor explains to a couple (both carriers Aa for a recessive disorder) that having one affected child does NOT change the probability for their next child. This is because:
    [ ] Genetics follows the Gambler's Fallacy
    [*] Each conception is an independent event; the probability remains 1/4 for each child regardless of previous outcomes
    [ ] The probability increases to 1/2 after an affected child
    [ ] Previous children's genotypes influence future children
    Explanation: Each child's genotype is determined independently by random allele segregation. Past offspring have no effect on future probabilities.
