--- Lesson 4.1: Basic Probability Rules (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Basic Probability Rules
Basic Probability Rules
Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring, expressed as a number from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).
  • Sample space (S): The set of all possible outcomes.  • Event (E): A subset of the sample space.  • P(E) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of outcomes (for equally likely outcomes).  • Complement rule: P(E') = 1 − P(E). The probability of an event NOT happening.  • Addition rule (mutually exclusive): P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) when A and B cannot both occur.  • General addition rule: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B).  • Multiplication rule (independent): P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) when events are independent.
Probabilities can be expressed as fractions, decimals, or percentages. The sum of probabilities of all outcomes in a sample space is 1.

--- Lesson 4.2: Counting Principles (permutations, combinations) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Counting Principles (permutations, combinations)
Counting Principles
Counting principles determine the number of ways to arrange or select items, which is essential for calculating probabilities.
  • Fundamental Counting Principle: If event A can occur in m ways and event B in n ways, then A followed by B can occur in m × n ways.  • Permutation: An ordered arrangement of objects. The number of permutations of n objects taken r at a time: P(n, r) = n! / (n − r)!  • Combination: A selection where order does NOT matter. C(n, r) = n! / [r!(n − r)!]  • Factorial: n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × … × 1. By definition, 0! = 1.
Key distinction: Use permutations when order matters (e.g., rankings, codes), combinations when order doesn't matter (e.g., committees, groups).

--- Lesson 4.3: Independent & Dependent Events (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Independent & Dependent Events
Independent & Dependent Events
Understanding whether events are independent or dependent is critical for calculating probabilities correctly.
  • Independent events: The occurrence of one event does NOT affect the probability of the other. Example: Flipping a coin twice — the outcome of the first flip doesn't affect the second.  • Dependent events: The occurrence of one event DOES affect the probability of the other. Example: Drawing two cards from a deck without replacement — the first draw changes the remaining cards.
Test for independence: Events A and B are independent if and only if P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B), or equivalently P(A|B) = P(A).

Multiplication rule:
  • Independent: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)  • Dependent: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
'With replacement' typically makes events independent; 'without replacement' makes them dependent.

--- Lesson 4.4: Conditional Probability (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conditional Probability
Conditional Probability
Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred.

Formula: P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B), where P(B) > 0.

Read 'P(A|B)' as 'the probability of A given B.'
  • The '|' symbol means 'given that' — it restricts the sample space to only the outcomes where B occurred.  • P(A|B) ≠ P(B|A) in general — the order matters.  • P(A|B) = P(A) only if A and B are independent.
Bayes' Theorem: P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B). This allows you to reverse conditional probabilities.

Example: In a group of 100 students, 30 play sports and 20 of those also play music. P(music | sports) = 20/30 = 2/3.

--- Lesson 4.5: Probability Trees (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability Trees
Probability Trees
A probability tree (tree diagram) is a visual tool for organizing and computing probabilities of sequential events.
  • Each branch represents a possible outcome, labeled with its probability.  • Branches from the same node must have probabilities that sum to 1.  • To find P(a specific sequence): multiply the probabilities along the path.  • To find P(an event that can occur via multiple paths): add the probabilities of all paths leading to that event.
Probability trees are especially helpful for:
  • Multi-stage experiments (e.g., drawing cards, flipping coins, medical testing).  • Problems involving conditional probability (branches after the first split may have updated probabilities).  • Visualizing all possible outcomes systematically.

  Q2. To find the probability of a specific path in a tree:

--- Lesson 4.6: Venn Diagrams (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Venn Diagrams
Venn Diagrams in Probability
A Venn diagram uses overlapping circles within a rectangle to display relationships between events.
  • The rectangle represents the entire sample space (S).  • Each circle represents an event.  • The overlap (intersection) represents outcomes in both events: A ∩ B.  • The union (everything in either circle) represents A ∪ B.
Using Venn diagrams to find probabilities:
  • P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B) → corresponds to the total area of both circles.  • P(A only) = P(A) − P(A and B) → the part of A's circle not in the overlap.  • P(neither A nor B) = 1 − P(A or B) → the area outside both circles.
For mutually exclusive events, the circles do not overlap (intersection is empty).

--- Lesson 4.7: Applications in Games of Chance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Games of Chance
Applications in Games of Chance
Probability theory originated from analyzing games of chance. Understanding these applications reinforces fundamental concepts.
  • Dice: A fair six-sided die has P(any face) = 1/6. Two dice → 36 equally likely outcomes. P(sum = 7) = 6/36 = 1/6.  • Cards: A standard deck has 52 cards (4 suits × 13 ranks). P(ace) = 4/52 = 1/13.  • Coins: P(heads) = 1/2 for a fair coin. n flips → 2ⁿ total outcomes.  • Lottery: Lotteries involve combinations — e.g., choosing 6 numbers from 49 → C(49,6) = 13,983,816 combinations. P(winning) ≈ 1 in 14 million.  • Expected value: The long-run average outcome. E(X) = Σ[x · P(x)]. Casino games always have a negative expected value for the player (house edge).  • Gambler's fallacy: The mistaken belief that past random outcomes affect future ones (e.g., 'the coin landed heads 5 times, so tails is due').

--- Lesson 4.8: Probability in Genetics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability in Genetics
Probability in Genetics
Genetics was one of the earliest applications of probability. Gregor Mendel's work in the 1860s used probability principles to explain inheritance patterns.
  • Punnett Square: A grid used to predict genotype ratios of offspring. It is essentially a probability table based on the multiplication rule.  • Genotype: The genetic composition (e.g., Bb = heterozygous). Phenotype: The observable trait (e.g., brown eyes).  • Dominant allele (B): Expressed in both BB and Bb genotypes. Recessive allele (b): Only expressed in bb genotype.  • Monohybrid cross (Bb × Bb): Probability of BB = 1/4, Bb = 1/2, bb = 1/4. Phenotype ratio: 3 dominant : 1 recessive.  • Independent assortment: Genes on different chromosomes are inherited independently — allowing use of the multiplication rule.  • Hardy-Weinberg principle: In a large population with no evolutionary forces, allele frequencies remain constant: p² + 2pq + q² = 1.
