--- Lesson 2.1: Frequency Tables (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Frequency Tables
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a frequency table?
A frequency table organizes data by listing each distinct value or category alongside its count (frequency) in the dataset.
What is a 'class interval' in a grouped frequency table?
A class interval defines the lower and upper boundaries of a bin, grouping all data values that fall within that range.
What is an 'ungrouped frequency table'?
An ungrouped frequency table lists every distinct value separately with its frequency, without combining values into intervals.
A teacher records quiz scores for 30 students: five scored 70, eight scored 80, twelve scored 90, and five scored 100. What is the relative frequency of students who scored 90?
Relative frequency = 12/30 = 0.40, which is 40%. Twelve out of thirty students scored 90.
In a survey of 500 people about their favorite fruit, the frequency table shows: Apples 150, Bananas 120, Oranges 100, Grapes 80, Other 50. What is the cumulative frequency up to and including Oranges (listed in order)?
Cumulative frequency up to Oranges = 150 + 120 + 100 = 370.
A school surveys 200 students about study hours per week and builds a frequency table: 0-4 hrs (30), 5-9 hrs (55), 10-14 hrs (70), 15-19 hrs (35), 20-24 hrs (10). What is the class midpoint for the 10-14 hour class?
Class midpoint = (10 + 14) / 2 = 12 hours.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which type of frequency table would best organize the ages of 500 survey respondents (ages 18-85)?
With a wide range of ages and many data points, grouping ages into intervals makes the table more readable and informative.
Example: A teacher records quiz scores for 30 students: five scored 70, eight scored 80, twelve scored 90, and five scored 100. What is the relative frequency of students who scored 90?
Relative frequency = 12/30 = 0.40, which is 40%. Twelve out of thirty students scored 90.
Example: A hospital emergency room records patient wait times. The grouped frequency table shows: 0-14 min: 20 patients, 15-29 min: 45 patients, 30-44 min: 30 patients, 45-59 min: 5 patients. What percentage of patients waited less than 30 minutes?
Patients waiting less than 30 minutes: 20 + 45 = 65 out of 100 total patients, which is 65%.
Example: A researcher collects data on the ages of 60 participants. The frequency table with 5-year intervals shows: 20-24 (8), 25-29 (15), 30-34 (20), 35-39 (12), 40-44 (5). She wants to quickly determine what percentage of participants are under 35. Using cumulative relative frequency, the answer is:
Participants under 35 = 8 + 15 + 20 = 43 out of 60. Cumulative relative frequency = 43/60 = 0.717, or about 72%.
Example: A manager looks at a frequency table of employee salaries and notices that the relative frequencies are: Under $40K: 0.15, $40K-$59K: 0.35, $60K-$79K: 0.30, $80K-$99K: 0.12, $100K+: 0.08. If the company has 400 employees, how many earn between $40K and $59K?
Frequency = relative frequency x total = 0.35 x 400 = 140 employees.
Example: A city planner examines a frequency table of daily traffic counts at an intersection: 0-99 cars (2 days), 100-199 (8 days), 200-299 (12 days), 300-399 (6 days), 400-499 (2 days). Over the 30-day period, what percentage of days had fewer than 300 cars?
Days with fewer than 300 cars: 2 + 8 + 12 = 22 days. Percentage = 22/30 = 0.733, or about 73%.

--- Lesson 2.2: Histograms & Bar Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Histograms & Bar Graphs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a histogram?
A histogram uses touching bars to show how quantitative data is distributed across bins (intervals), revealing the shape and spread of the data.
What is a bar graph?
A bar graph uses separated bars to display the frequency, count, or other measure for each distinct category of qualitative data.
What is a 'Pareto chart'?
A Pareto chart is a special bar graph that arranges categories from most frequent to least frequent, often including a cumulative line.
Real-World Applications
Example: A teacher records test scores for 40 students: most score between 70-89, a few score in the 50-69 range, and a handful score 90-100. The histogram of these scores would most likely be:
With most scores in 70-89, a few lower scores, and some high scores, the distribution peaks at 70-89 with a slight left tail, making it left-skewed.
Example: A survey of 500 people asks their favorite pizza topping. Results: Pepperoni (180), Cheese (120), Mushroom (80), Sausage (70), Other (50). The most appropriate graph to display this data is:
Pizza toppings are categorical data, and bar graphs are the appropriate choice for displaying frequencies of categories.
Example: A company creates a bar graph showing quarterly sales. The bars show: Q1 = $2M, Q2 = $2.1M, Q3 = $2.05M, Q4 = $2.15M. However, the y-axis starts at $1.9M instead of $0. This graph would:
Starting the y-axis at $1.9M instead of $0 magnifies relatively small differences, making the sales changes look more dramatic than they actually are.
Example: An analyst has data on the ages of 1,000 marathon runners. The histogram shows a peak around ages 30-35, with the distribution tapering off gradually toward older ages but dropping sharply below age 20. This distribution is:
Since the distribution peaks at 30-35 and has a longer tail toward older ages (higher values), it is right-skewed.
Example: A city collects data on commute times. The histogram shows two distinct peaks: one around 15 minutes and another around 45 minutes. This distribution is best described as:
Two distinct peaks indicate a bimodal distribution, possibly representing two groups: those who live close to work and those who live farther away.
Example: A real estate agent creates a histogram of home prices in a neighborhood. Most homes are priced between $200,000 and $400,000, but there are a few mansions priced over $1,000,000. The distribution shape would be:
A few very expensive homes create a long right tail, making the distribution right-skewed (positively skewed).
Example: Two classes take the same exam. Class A's histogram shows a bell-shaped curve centered at 75. Class B's histogram shows a left-skewed distribution with most scores near 90. Which statement is correct?
Class B's left-skewed distribution with a peak near 90 indicates most students scored high, with a few low scores creating the left tail.
Important Concepts
A frequency histogram where each bar represents exactly one value (e.g., number of siblings: 0, 1, 2, 3) is used for:
When each bar represents a single countable value, the histogram displays discrete quantitative data.
What does 'skewed right' mean for a distribution
A right-skewed (positively skewed) distribution has most data concentrated on the left with a long tail stretching toward the higher values on the right.
What does 'skewed left' mean for a distribution
A left-skewed (negatively skewed) distribution has most data concentrated on the right with a long tail stretching toward the lower values on the left.
What does 'bimodal distribution' mean
A bimodal distribution has two noticeable peaks, suggesting the data may come from two different groups or processes.
What does 'relative frequency histogram' mean
A relative frequency histogram has bars whose heights represent the proportion (or percentage) of the total data in each bin.

Overview

Key Definitions
What is a stem-and-leaf plot?
A stem-and-leaf plot separates each data value into a stem (leading digits) and a leaf (last digit), displaying all individual values while showing the distribution shape.
What is a 'back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot'?
A back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot places the stems in the center with one dataset's leaves on the left and the other on the right for easy comparison.
What is the purpose of a 'key' or 'legend' on a stem-and-leaf plot?
The key provides an example showing how to combine a stem and leaf to reconstruct the original data value.
What is the main advantage of a stem-and-leaf plot over a frequency table?
A stem-and-leaf plot simultaneously shows the distribution shape (like a histogram) and retains all original data values (unlike a grouped frequency table).

--- Lesson 2.4: Boxplots & Quartiles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Boxplots & Quartiles
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a boxplot (box-and-whisker plot)?
A boxplot visually represents the five-number summary (min, Q1, median, Q3, max), shows the spread through the box and whiskers, and identifies potential outliers.
What is a quartile?
Quartiles are the three values (Q1, Q2, Q3) that divide a sorted dataset into four approximately equal groups.
What is the interquartile range (IQR)?
The IQR measures the spread of the central half of the data, calculated by subtracting Q1 from Q3.
What is an outlier in the context of boxplots?
In boxplots, an outlier is a value that lies beyond the fences, which are set at Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and Q3 + 1.5(IQR).
A dataset of 12 values is: 3, 5, 7, 8, 10, 12, 14, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25. What is Q1?
For 12 values, Q1 is the median of the lower half (3,5,7,8,10,12). The median of these 6 values is (7+8)/2 = 7.5.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Outliers are data points that fall more than 1.5 times the IQR below Q1 or above Q3.
In boxplots, an outlier is a value that lies beyond the fences, which are set at Q1 - 1.5(IQR) and Q3 + 1.5(IQR).
The IQR is resistant because it is based on Q1 and Q3, which are not influenced by extreme values at the tails of the distribution.
Q1 = 20, Q3 = 55, IQR = 35. Upper fence = 55 + 1.5(35) = 107.5. Since 100 < 107.5, it is actually within the fence. However, 100 is the most extreme and the value most likely flagged in context.
Lower fence = Q1 - 1.5(IQR) = 20 - 1.5(20) = 20 - 30 = -10. Any value below -10 would be a lower outlier.
Outliers represent properties with prices far from the bulk of the data, warranting investigation but not automatic removal.
Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5(IQR) = 8.3 + 1.5(1.1) = 8.3 + 1.65 = 9.95. Since 12.0 > 9.95, it is classified as an outlier.
Real-World Applications

--- Lesson 2.5: Scatterplots & Correlation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scatterplots & Correlation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a scatterplot?
A scatterplot displays each observation as a point defined by its x and y values, revealing the pattern of association between two quantitative variables.
Data shows that cities with more ice cream shops also tend to have more crime. A student concludes ice cream shops cause crime. What is the flaw?
City population is a lurking variable that drives both ice cream shop count and crime rates; correlation does not imply causation.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
A single outlier can dramatically change the correlation coefficient, either inflating or deflating it.
Real-World Applications
Example: A researcher plots study hours (x) vs. exam scores (y) for 30 students. The scatterplot shows an upward trend with points moderately spread around an imaginary line. The correlation is most likely:
An upward trend indicates positive correlation, and moderate spread suggests a moderate r value around 0.65.
Example: A scatterplot of advertising spending vs. sales revenue shows a clear upward curve that levels off at higher spending. The correlation r = 0.60. Why might r understate the relationship?
Since r measures only linear relationships, a curved pattern will produce a moderate r even when the actual association is strong.
Example: A manager creates a scatterplot of employee experience (years) vs. salary. Two clusters appear: one for entry-level roles (low experience, low salary) and one for senior roles (high experience, high salary). The overall r = 0.88. However, within each cluster, r is about 0.20. This situation illustrates:
When distinct groups exist, the between-group difference can create a strong overall correlation even when the within-group relationship is weak.

--- Lesson 2.6: Pie Charts & Circle Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Pie Charts & Circle Graphs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a pie chart?
A pie chart is a circular graph where each slice represents a category's proportion of the total, making it easy to see how a whole is divided.
What is a 'donut chart'?
A donut chart is a variation of a pie chart with a blank center, sometimes used to show a total or label in the middle.
What is a 'legend' on a pie chart?
A legend maps each color or pattern to its corresponding category name, helping readers identify what each slice represents.
A survey of 400 students asks their favorite school subject: Math (100), Science (80), English (90), History (70), Art (60). What is the central angle for the Math slice?
Math proportion = 100/400 = 0.25. Central angle = 0.25 x 360 = 90 degrees.
Real-World Applications
Example: A disadvantage of pie charts compared to bar graphs is that pie charts:
Humans find it harder to compare angles and areas in pie charts than bar heights, making precise comparisons more difficult.

--- Lesson 2.7: Choosing Appropriate Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Choosing Appropriate Graphs
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'time series plot'?
A time series plot uses a line graph with time on the x-axis and the measured variable on the y-axis to show trends over time.
What is a 'stacked bar graph'?
A stacked bar graph divides each bar into colored segments representing sub-categories, showing both total and composition.
What is a 'dot plot'?
A dot plot uses dots stacked above a number line, with one dot per observation, showing both individual values and the distribution.
What is a 'segmented bar graph' (100% stacked bar)?
A 100% stacked bar graph makes all bars the same height and shows the percentage breakdown within each bar, facilitating proportion comparisons.
What is a 'pictograph'?
A pictograph uses repeated symbols (like small icons) where each symbol represents a certain number of items, making data visually intuitive.
Real-World Applications

Side-by-side boxplots allow easy comparison of center, spread, and outliers across multiple groups on a single scale.

--- Lesson 2.8: Case Studies in Data Visualization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Data Visualization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a polar area chart?
A polar area chart uses wedge-shaped sectors whose area (not just radius) represents data values. Nightingale's Rose Diagram is the most famous example.
What is the data-ink ratio?
The data-ink ratio, introduced by Tufte, measures the share of ink in a graphic devoted to data rather than non-data elements. A higher ratio indicates a cleaner graphic.
What is a choropleth map?
A choropleth map shades geographic regions (states, countries) according to a data variable, such as population density or election results.
What is meant by 'context' in data visualization?
Context helps the audience understand what the data represent and how to interpret them correctly.
What is an interactive dashboard?
Interactive dashboards are modern data visualization tools that allow users to manipulate views, apply filters, and explore underlying data dynamically.
What is meant by 'data storytelling'?
Data storytelling combines data, visuals, and narrative to convey a message, highlight trends, and engage the audience.
What is a heat map?
A heat map uses color gradients to represent data intensity, making it easy to spot patterns, clusters, and outliers across a matrix or map.
A public health department creates a choropleth map showing COVID-19 cases by county using raw case counts rather than per-capita rates. A large urban county with 2 million people and 10,000 cases appears bright red, while a rural county with 5,000 people and 800 cases appears pale. What is the primary issue?
The rural county actually has a 16% case rate versus the urban county's 0.5% rate. Using per-capita rates would more accurately represent relative impact.
Real-World Applications
Example: Cherry-picking a favorable time window in a line chart is an example of:
Showing only a favorable time period while hiding the broader trend is a misleading visualization practice.
Example: A hospital administrator creates a bar chart comparing patient wait times across five departments. She notices the y-axis starts at 25 minutes instead of 0, making the emergency department's 30-minute average look dramatically higher than the 28-minute average of the outpatient clinic. What should she do to present the data honestly?
Starting at 0 or adding a clear axis break notation prevents exaggerating small differences, which is essential for honest data communication.
Example: A marketing team builds a 3D pie chart showing quarterly sales. The quarter closest to the viewer (Q4) appears visually larger than Q2, even though both represent 25% of sales. An intern suggests switching to a flat 2D pie chart or a bar chart. Why is the intern correct?
3D perspective causes foreshortening, making slices closer to the viewer look disproportionately large. A flat chart or bar chart avoids this distortion.

The actual increase is (72-60)/60 = 20%, not 100%. Furthermore, doubling both dimensions of a 2D icon quadruples the visual area, creating a lie factor well above 1.

--- Lesson 2.8: Case Studies in Data Visualization (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Florence Nightingale used data visualization to show that:
    [*] Preventable disease killed more soldiers than combat
    [ ] The war lasted only one year
    [ ] Hospitals were unnecessary
    [ ] Battle wounds were the main cause of death
    Explanation: Her Rose Diagram revealed that most military deaths were from preventable disease, driving medical reforms.

  Q2. John Snow's cholera map is famous for:
    [ ] Proving germ theory conclusively
    [*] Identifying a contaminated water pump as the source of a cholera outbreak
    [ ] Showing Napoleon's army size
    [ ] Displaying national budget data
    Explanation: Snow plotted cholera deaths on a map, revealing the cluster around the Broad Street pump.

  Q3. Minard's map of Napoleon's Russian campaign is renowned because it:
    [ ] Is a standard pie chart showing the distribution of army casualties by category
    [ ] Uses only one variable, which is the total number of soldiers in the army over time
    [ ] Was the first bar chart ever created, establishing the conventions for all future bar charts
    [*] Integrates multiple variables (army size, location, direction, temperature) in one graphic
    Explanation: Minard's graphic combined six types of data into a single compelling image, often called the best statistical graphic ever drawn.

  Q4. A truncated y-axis can:
    [ ] Only reduce visual differences
    [ ] Never mislead anyone
    [ ] Always improve the accuracy of a graph
    [*] Exaggerate small differences between data values
    Explanation: Not starting the y-axis at zero can make small differences look much larger, potentially misleading the audience.

  Q5. Good data visualization should:
    [ ] Always use 3D effects to attract attention
    [ ] Hide the data source to simplify the graphic
    [ ] Use as many colors and decorations as possible
    [*] Choose the right graph, label clearly, avoid distortion, and provide context
    Explanation: Effective visualizations are clear, honest, well-labeled, and contextual.

  Q6. John Snow's map is a landmark contribution in which field?
    [ ] Economics
    [*] Epidemiology
    [ ] Astronomy
    [ ] Physics
    Explanation: Snow's work mapping cholera deaths helped found modern epidemiology.

  Q7. Nightingale's data visualization ultimately changed:
    [ ] Tax legislation
    [ ] Fashion design standards
    [*] Military medical and sanitation policy
    [ ] Cooking regulations
    Explanation: Her data-driven arguments led to sanitation reforms that saved thousands of soldiers' lives.

  Q8. A key principle of ethical data visualization is:
    [ ] Hiding unfavorable results from the audience
    [*] Presenting data honestly without distortion
    [ ] Using only pie charts for all data
    [ ] Making data look as dramatic as possible
    Explanation: Ethical visualization avoids manipulation and presents data accurately so the audience can draw valid conclusions.

  Q9. Cherry-picking a favorable time window in a line chart is an example of:
    [ ] Standard data labeling
    [ ] Good data practice
    [*] Visualization misuse
    [ ] Proper statistical analysis
    Explanation: Showing only a favorable time period while hiding the broader trend is a misleading visualization practice.

  Q10. Edward Tufte is best known for advocating:
    [ ] Heavy use of 3D effects and chart junk
    [ ] Replacing all graphs with tables
    [ ] Using only hand-drawn illustrations
    [*] Clarity, integrity, and minimalism in data graphics
    Explanation: Tufte's principle 'Above all else, show the data' emphasizes clarity and integrity in visualization.

  Q11. What is a polar area chart?
    [ ] Another name for a scatterplot
    [ ] A map of the Arctic and Antarctic regions
    [ ] A standard bar chart rotated 90 degrees
    [*] A circular chart where wedge areas represent data magnitudes, famously used in Nightingale's Rose Diagram
    Explanation: A polar area chart uses wedge-shaped sectors whose area (not just radius) represents data values. Nightingale's Rose Diagram is the most famous example.

  Q12. What does 'chart junk' mean in data visualization?
    [ ] Charts that contain too much data
    [ ] Deleted or discarded graphs
    [*] Unnecessary decorative elements that do not convey data and can distract the viewer
    [ ] Charts made from recycled paper
    Explanation: Chart junk is a term coined by Tufte referring to unnecessary visual elements (gridlines, decorations, 3D effects) that clutter a graph without adding information.

  Q13. What is the data-ink ratio?
    [ ] The cost of ink per printed page
    [ ] The ratio of colored ink to black ink
    [ ] The number of data points per chart
    [*] The proportion of ink used on a page that actually presents data, as opposed to decoration
    Explanation: The data-ink ratio, introduced by Tufte, measures the share of ink in a graphic devoted to data rather than non-data elements. A higher ratio indicates a cleaner graphic.

  Q14. What is a choropleth map?
    [ ] A type of bar chart showing geographic distances
    [ ] A musical score with geographic data
    [ ] A scatter plot overlaid on a world map
    [*] A map that uses shading or color gradients to represent data values across geographic areas
    Explanation: A choropleth map shades geographic regions (states, countries) according to a data variable, such as population density or election results.

  Q15. What does 'infographic' mean?
    [ ] A raw data table with no visuals that presents information as plain rows and columns
    [ ] A spoken presentation of data delivered verbally to an audience without visual aids
    [*] A visual representation that combines images, charts, and text to present information clearly and quickly
    [ ] A photograph used in a research paper to provide visual documentation of the study site
    Explanation: An infographic is a visual tool that blends graphics, charts, and concise text to communicate data or concepts at a glance.

  Q16. What is meant by 'context' in data visualization?
    [ ] Only the colors chosen for each element within the chart's visual design palette
    [ ] Only the title of the graph that appears at the top of the visualization display
    [ ] The font used in the chart, including the typeface, weight, and size of all text
    [*] Background information such as the data source, time period, units, and relevant circumstances
    Explanation: Context helps the audience understand what the data represent and how to interpret them correctly.

  Q17. What is an interactive dashboard?
    [ ] A single pie chart on a website that displays one category breakdown in a static format
    [*] A digital display that lets users filter, drill down, and explore data visualizations in real time
    [ ] A spreadsheet with no formatting that organizes raw data into rows and columns only
    [ ] A printed poster of static charts designed for display at conferences or presentations
    Explanation: Interactive dashboards are modern data visualization tools that allow users to manipulate views, apply filters, and explore underlying data dynamically.

  Q18. What is meant by 'data storytelling'?
    [ ] Reading data aloud without any visuals
    [ ] Making up fictional data to tell a story
    [*] Using visualizations, narrative, and data together to communicate insights and persuade an audience
    [ ] Removing all text from a chart
    Explanation: Data storytelling combines data, visuals, and narrative to convey a message, highlight trends, and engage the audience.

  Q19. What is a heat map?
    [ ] A line graph that plots daily high temperatures over time to show seasonal weather trends
    [ ] A bar chart that uses warm colors like red, orange, and yellow for visual appeal in reports
    [*] A visualization that uses color intensity to represent the magnitude of values in a matrix or geographic area
    [ ] A map that shows only temperature data recorded from weather stations across a geographic region
    Explanation: A heat map uses color gradients to represent data intensity, making it easy to spot patterns, clusters, and outliers across a matrix or map.

  Q20. What does 'lie factor' refer to in data visualization?
    [ ] The number of incorrect labels on a chart
    [ ] The number of colors used incorrectly
    [*] The ratio of the visual effect size to the actual data effect size, measuring how much a graphic distorts the truth
    [ ] The percentage of people who misread a chart
    Explanation: Tufte defined the lie factor as (size of effect shown in graphic) / (size of effect in data). A lie factor of 1 means no distortion; values far from 1 indicate misleading graphics.

  Q21. A hospital administrator creates a bar chart comparing patient wait times across five departments. She notices the y-axis starts at 25 minutes instead of 0, making the emergency department's 30-minute average look dramatically higher than the 28-minute average of the outpatient clinic. What should she do to present the data honestly?
    [*] Start the y-axis at 0 or clearly mark the axis break so viewers understand the true scale
    [ ] Keep the truncated axis because it highlights real differences
    [ ] Switch to a pie chart to avoid the issue
    [ ] Remove the y-axis labels entirely
    Explanation: Starting at 0 or adding a clear axis break notation prevents exaggerating small differences, which is essential for honest data communication.

  Q22. A news network shows a line graph of unemployment rates from January to June, during which the rate dropped from 5.2% to 4.8%. However, in the full year the rate rose from 4.5% in July of the prior year to 5.2% in January before falling. Which visualization practice is the network guilty of?
    [ ] Proper time-series analysis
    [ ] Overloading the chart with variables
    [*] Cherry-picking a favorable time window
    [ ] Using a truncated y-axis
    Explanation: By showing only the six months of decline and omitting the earlier rise, the network cherry-picks a time window that gives a misleadingly positive impression.

  Q23. A public health department creates a choropleth map showing COVID-19 cases by county using raw case counts rather than per-capita rates. A large urban county with 2 million people and 10,000 cases appears bright red, while a rural county with 5,000 people and 800 cases appears pale. What is the primary issue?
    [ ] The colors are too similar
    [ ] Choropleth maps should never be used for health data
    [*] Using raw counts instead of per-capita rates makes larger populations appear worse regardless of actual severity
    [ ] The map should only show state-level data
    Explanation: The rural county actually has a 16% case rate versus the urban county's 0.5% rate. Using per-capita rates would more accurately represent relative impact.

  Q24. A marketing team builds a 3D pie chart showing quarterly sales. The quarter closest to the viewer (Q4) appears visually larger than Q2, even though both represent 25% of sales. An intern suggests switching to a flat 2D pie chart or a bar chart. Why is the intern correct?
    [*] 3D perspective distorts the perceived area of slices, making some look bigger or smaller than they actually are
    [ ] 2D charts always have fewer colors
    [ ] 3D charts are always illegal in business reports
    [ ] Pie charts cannot show quarterly data
    Explanation: 3D perspective causes foreshortening, making slices closer to the viewer look disproportionately large. A flat chart or bar chart avoids this distortion.

  Q25. A researcher studying commute times creates a histogram for 500 employees. She uses 50 bins for data ranging from 5 to 65 minutes. The histogram looks extremely jagged and hard to interpret. A colleague suggests using 10-12 bins. Why would fewer bins improve the visualization?
    [*] Too many bins create noise that obscures the overall distribution shape; moderate bins reveal meaningful patterns
    [ ] More bins always produce better graphs because they show the maximum amount of detail possible
    [ ] Fewer bins lose all the data detail and make the visualization completely uninformative
    [ ] The number of bins never matters because the overall shape of the distribution stays the same
    Explanation: With 50 bins over a 60-minute range, each bin spans only about 1.2 minutes, creating jagged noise. Using 10-12 bins reveals the true shape of the distribution.

  Q26. A school district publishes an infographic claiming test scores 'doubled' by showing a cartoon pencil that is twice as tall and twice as wide for the current year compared to last year. If scores rose from 60 to 72, what TWO problems exist?
    [*] Scores increased by 20% (not doubled), and the pencil's area is 4 times larger, greatly exaggerating the change
    [ ] Only the width should have changed, not the height
    [ ] The infographic is fine because the pencil is visually appealing
    [ ] The pencil should have been three times as tall
    Explanation: The actual increase is (72-60)/60 = 20%, not 100%. Furthermore, doubling both dimensions of a 2D icon quadruples the visual area, creating a lie factor well above 1.

  Q27. A city council reviews a line chart of traffic accidents over 10 years. The chart has no title, no axis labels, and no source citation. A data analyst says the chart lacks context. Which of the following best describes why context matters here?
    [ ] Context is optional for internal reports
    [*] Without labels, title, and source, viewers cannot determine what is measured, the time frame, or whether the data are reliable
    [ ] Axis labels are cosmetic and do not affect interpretation
    [ ] The chart only needs color to be understood
    Explanation: Titles, axis labels, units, and source citations are essential context. Without them, the audience cannot properly interpret or trust the data.

  Q28. A pharmaceutical company presents clinical trial results using a dual y-axis chart. The left axis shows percentage of patients improved (0-100%) and the right axis shows side effect count (0-500). A critic argues this is misleading. Why?
    [ ] Dual y-axes are always wrong and should never be used under any circumstances in data visualization
    [ ] Only one variable should ever be plotted on a chart, regardless of the research question being explored
    [ ] Clinical trials cannot be visualized with charts because the data is too complex for graphical display
    [*] Dual y-axes with different scales can make unrelated trends appear correlated or hide important differences by manipulating the scale alignment
    Explanation: Dual y-axes allow the creator to manipulate which scale aligns with which, potentially making a small effect look large or implying a spurious relationship between the two measures.

  Q29. An environmental group creates an interactive dashboard showing deforestation data. Users can filter by country, year, and forest type. One user filters to show only 2020-2023 for Brazil and concludes deforestation is declining globally. What principle of data visualization has this user violated?
    [ ] Using interactive dashboards is always unreliable because the filters can distort any analysis or conclusion
    [*] The user generalized from a filtered subset to a global conclusion, ignoring that one country in a limited time frame does not represent the whole
    [ ] Only static charts should be used for environmental data because interactive tools introduce too much complexity
    [ ] Dashboards should not allow filtering because it gives users the ability to misinterpret the underlying data
    Explanation: While interactive dashboards are powerful, users must avoid over-generalizing from narrow filters. A single country over a short period cannot represent global trends.

  Q30. A nonprofit creates a visualization showing donation amounts using bubble sizes on a map. A $100,000 donation bubble has a radius 10 times that of a $10,000 donation bubble. Since circle area = pi*r^2, the large bubble's area is actually 100 times bigger, not 10 times. What type of distortion is this?
    [ ] This only matters in 3D charts because two-dimensional visualizations are immune to area-scaling distortions
    [*] An area-scaling error where the visual area grows quadratically while the data grows linearly, creating a large lie factor
    [ ] This is correct because the radius represents the data value directly and area is irrelevant to perception
    [ ] Bubble charts should never be used in data visualization because they always introduce visual distortion
    Explanation: When scaling bubbles, the area (not the radius) should be proportional to the data value. Scaling the radius linearly causes the area to grow quadratically, inflating the lie factor to 10.
