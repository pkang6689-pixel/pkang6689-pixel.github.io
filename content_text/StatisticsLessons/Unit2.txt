--- Lesson 2.1: Frequency Tables (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Frequency Tables
Frequency Tables
A frequency table organizes data by listing each value (or group of values) and the number of times it occurs.
  • Frequency (f): The count of observations for a particular value or class.  • Relative frequency: The proportion of the total that falls in each class: f / n.  • Cumulative frequency: The running total of frequencies up to and including a given class.  • Class (bin): A range of values grouped together (e.g., 60–69, 70–79).  • Class width: The difference between the upper and lower boundaries of a class.
Frequency tables are foundational — they provide the data behind histograms and other charts. Choosing appropriate class widths is important: too few classes hide patterns; too many classes produce noise.

--- Lesson 2.2: Histograms & Bar Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Histograms & Bar Graphs
Histograms & Bar Graphs
Bar graphs and histograms are two of the most common data visualizations, but they serve different purposes.
  • Bar graph: Used for categorical (qualitative) data. Bars are separated by gaps. The height of each bar represents the frequency or relative frequency of each category.  • Histogram: Used for quantitative (numerical) data. Bars are adjacent (no gaps) because they represent continuous intervals. The height shows frequency, and the width represents the class interval.
Key features to examine in a histogram:
  • Shape: Symmetric, skewed left (tail on left), skewed right (tail on right), uniform, or bimodal.  • Center: Where the 'middle' of the data lies.  • Spread: How wide the distribution is.  • Outliers: Unusual values far from the rest of the data.

--- Lesson 2.3: Stem-and-Leaf Plots (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Stem-and-Leaf Plots
Stem-and-Leaf Plots
A stem-and-leaf plot (stemplot) is a way to display quantitative data while preserving the individual data values. It combines features of a table and a histogram.
  • Stem: The leading digit(s) of each data value (e.g., the tens digit for two-digit numbers).  • Leaf: The trailing digit(s) (e.g., the ones digit).  • Example: For data values 23, 25, 31, 37, 38, the stems are 2 and 3; the leaves for stem 2 are 3, 5; for stem 3 are 1, 7, 8.
Advantages: Shows the shape of the distribution like a histogram, but retains the exact data values. Useful for small to moderate datasets.

A back-to-back stem-and-leaf plot compares two datasets using a shared stem, with leaves extending in opposite directions.

Limitations: Not practical for very large datasets or data with many decimal places.

--- Lesson 2.4: Boxplots & Quartiles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Boxplots & Quartiles
Boxplots & Quartiles
A boxplot (box-and-whisker plot) displays the five-number summary of a dataset: minimum, Q1, median, Q3, and maximum.
  • Quartile 1 (Q1): The 25th percentile — 25% of data fall below this value.  • Median (Q2): The 50th percentile — the middle value of the ordered data.  • Quartile 3 (Q3): The 75th percentile — 75% of data fall below this value.  • Interquartile Range (IQR): Q3 − Q1, measuring the spread of the middle 50% of data.  • Outliers: Values below Q1 − 1.5×IQR or above Q3 + 1.5×IQR are flagged as outliers.
The box spans from Q1 to Q3, with a line at the median. Whiskers extend to the smallest and largest non-outlier values. Outliers are plotted as individual points.

Boxplots are excellent for comparing distributions across groups and for identifying skewness and outliers at a glance.

--- Lesson 2.5: Scatterplots & Correlation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scatterplots & Correlation
Scatterplots & Correlation
A scatterplot displays the relationship between two quantitative variables. Each point represents one observation with coordinates (x, y).
  • Explanatory variable (x): The independent variable, plotted on the horizontal axis.  • Response variable (y): The dependent variable, plotted on the vertical axis.  • When examining a scatterplot, describe the direction (positive or negative), form (linear or nonlinear), and strength (strong, moderate, weak) of the relationship.
The correlation coefficient (r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.
  • r ranges from −1 to +1.  • r = +1: perfect positive linear relationship.  • r = −1: perfect negative linear relationship.  • r = 0: no linear relationship.  • r is unitless and not affected by changes in units or which variable is x or y.
Caution: Correlation does not imply causation. Also, r only measures linear relationships — a strong curved pattern may have r near 0.

--- Lesson 2.6: Pie Charts & Circle Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Pie Charts & Circle Graphs
Pie Charts & Circle Graphs
A pie chart (circle graph) displays categorical data as slices of a circle, where each slice's size represents the proportion of the whole.
  • The entire circle represents 100% of the data.  • Each slice's central angle = (relative frequency) × 360°.  • Pie charts are best for showing parts of a whole when there are few categories (typically 2–6).
When to use pie charts:
  • Showing proportions or percentages of a total.  • Comparing a few categories to the whole.
When NOT to use pie charts:
  • When there are many categories (too many small slices).  • When comparing exact values between categories (bar charts are better).  • When displaying data that do not sum to a meaningful whole.
3D pie charts should generally be avoided because the perspective distorts slice sizes.

--- Lesson 2.7: Choosing Appropriate Graphs (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Choosing Appropriate Graphs
Choosing Appropriate Graphs
Selecting the right graph for your data is crucial for effective communication. The choice depends on the type of data and the purpose of the display.

Categorical data:
  • Bar chart: Compare frequencies or values across categories.  • Pie chart: Show proportions of a whole (best with few categories).
Quantitative data – one variable:
  • Histogram: Show the shape of a distribution.  • Stemplot: Show distribution while preserving individual values (small datasets).  • Boxplot: Summarize distribution and compare groups; highlight outliers.  • Dotplot: Show individual values for small datasets.
Quantitative data – two variables:
  • Scatterplot: Show the relationship between two quantitative variables.  • Line graph: Show trends over time (time series).
Ask yourself: What am I trying to show — distribution, comparison, relationship, or trend?

--- Lesson 2.8: Case Studies in Data Visualization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Data Visualization
Case Studies in Data Visualization
Effective data visualization tells a clear story with data. History provides powerful examples — as well as cautionary tales.
  • Florence Nightingale's Rose Diagram (1858): Used a polar area chart to demonstrate that most British soldiers in the Crimean War died from preventable disease rather than battle wounds. Her visualization changed military medical policy.  • John Snow's Cholera Map (1854): Plotted cholera deaths on a London street map, revealing a cluster around the Broad Street water pump. This visualization helped identify contaminated water as the source and is a landmark in epidemiology.  • Charles Minard's Map (1869): Depicted Napoleon's 1812 Russian campaign, combining army size, geographic location, direction of movement, and temperature in a single graphic. Considered one of the greatest statistical graphics ever produced.  • Modern misuse: Truncated y-axes in news graphics, misleading scale choices, and cherry-picked timeframes continue to be common problems.
Good visualization principles: choose the right graph type, label axes clearly, avoid distortion, provide context, and let the data speak.
