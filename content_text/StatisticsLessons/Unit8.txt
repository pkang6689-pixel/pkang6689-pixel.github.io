--- Lesson 8.1: Scatterplots & Correlation Coefficient (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scatterplots & Correlation Coefficient
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the correlation coefficient?
The correlation coefficient r quantifies how tightly data cluster around a straight line and in which direction.
What is a scatterplot?
A scatterplot uses the coordinate plane to display paired quantitative observations.
What is meant by the 'direction' of a scatterplot?
Direction describes whether the association between the two variables is positive (upward trend) or negative (downward trend).
What is meant by the 'strength' of a correlation?
Strength is determined by the absolute value of r; values closer to 1 indicate a tighter linear pattern.
What is the explanatory variable in a scatterplot?
By convention the explanatory (independent) variable is placed on the x-axis.
A researcher records daily ice cream sales and daily drowning incidents over a summer. She finds r = 0.87. What is the best interpretation?
Temperature is a confounding variable that increases both ice cream sales and swimming activity, illustrating that correlation does not imply causation.
A data analyst notices that a dataset of car age (years) versus resale value ($) has r = −0.91. One car is a rare vintage model worth far more than predicted. If that car is removed, what is most likely to happen to |r|?
An outlier that deviates from the overall linear trend pulls r toward 0; removing it typically strengthens the measured linear relationship.
A pharmaceutical company tests a new drug and reports r = 0.45 between dosage and symptom improvement. A newspaper headline claims 'Drug causes improvement.' What is wrong with this claim?
Without a properly controlled experiment, confounders such as patient health or placebo effects could produce the observed correlation.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Outliers can pull r toward 0 or inflate it, depending on their position.

--- Lesson 8.2: Least Squares Regression Line (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Least Squares Regression Line
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the least squares regression line?
The LSRL is the line that makes the total squared prediction error as small as possible.
What is extrapolation in regression?
Extrapolation extends the model beyond the data range, where the linear pattern may no longer hold.
What is the formula for the slope of the LSRL?
The slope equals the correlation times the ratio of the standard deviation of y to the standard deviation of x.
What is meant by 'predicted value' (ŷ)?
ŷ is the output of the regression equation for a specific x value, representing the expected average y.
A real estate agent fits a regression: ŷ = 50,000 + 120x, where x is square footage and ŷ is predicted sale price in dollars. A 2,000 sq ft house recently sold for $310,000. What is the residual?
ŷ = 50,000 + 120(2,000) = $290,000. Residual = 310,000 − 290,000 = $20,000, meaning the house sold for $20,000 more than predicted.
A car manufacturer models fuel efficiency: ŷ = 45 − 0.005x, where x = vehicle weight (lbs) and ŷ = miles per gallon. For a 3,000-lb car, ŷ = 45 − 0.005(3000) = 30 mpg. The actual fuel efficiency is 28 mpg. What is the residual and what does it mean?
Residual = y − ŷ = 28 − 30 = −2. The negative residual means the actual mpg was 2 less than the model predicted.
A school district collects data on class size (x, number of students) and average standardized test score (y). The LSRL is ŷ = 95 − 0.4x with r² = 0.36. An administrator considers reducing class size from 30 to 20 students. According to the model, what is the predicted change in average test score?
The slope is −0.4, so each additional student is associated with a 0.4-point decrease. Reducing by 10 students predicts a 0.4 × 10 = 4-point increase.
Real-World Applications
Example: A real estate agent fits a regression: ŷ = 50,000 + 120x, where x is square footage and ŷ is predicted sale price in dollars. A 2,000 sq ft house recently sold for $310,000. What is the residual?
ŷ = 50,000 + 120(2,000) = $290,000. Residual = 310,000 − 290,000 = $20,000, meaning the house sold for $20,000 more than predicted.
Example: A teacher has the regression ŷ = 40 + 6x, where x = hours studied and ŷ = predicted test score. She wants to predict the score for a student who studied 12 hours, but students in the dataset studied between 1 and 8 hours. What concern applies?
Predicting at x = 12 when data only goes up to x = 8 is extrapolation; the relationship may level off or change beyond 8 hours.
Example: A school district collects data on class size (x, number of students) and average standardized test score (y). The LSRL is ŷ = 95 − 0.4x with r² = 0.36. An administrator considers reducing class size from 30 to 20 students. According to the model, what is the predicted change in average test score?
The slope is −0.4, so each additional student is associated with a 0.4-point decrease. Reducing by 10 students predicts a 0.4 × 10 = 4-point increase.

--- Lesson 8.4: Multiple Regression (introductory) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Multiple Regression (introductory)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is multiple regression?
Multiple regression extends simple regression by allowing more than one predictor to explain variation in a single response.
What is multicollinearity?
Multicollinearity means predictors share redundant information, making their individual coefficients unstable.
What is adjusted R²?
Adjusted R² accounts for the number of predictors, only increasing when a new variable provides a genuine improvement.
What is a dummy variable?
Dummy variables code categories numerically (e.g., male = 0, female = 1) so they can enter a regression equation.
What is stepwise regression?
Stepwise methods automate variable selection, though they have limitations such as dependence on the order of variable entry.
A hospital uses multiple regression to predict length of stay (y, days) from patient age (x₁), severity score (x₂), and number of procedures (x₃): ŷ = 1.5 + 0.03x₁ + 0.8x₂ + 1.2x₃. For a 60-year-old patient with severity score 5 and 2 procedures, what is the predicted stay?
ŷ = 1.5 + 0.03(60) + 0.8(5) + 1.2(2) = 1.5 + 1.8 + 4.0 + 2.4 = 9.7 days.
A researcher predicting college GPA includes both SAT Math score and SAT Total score as predictors. The VIF for SAT Math is 18.5. What is the problem and what should she consider?
VIF > 10 signals severe multicollinearity. Since SAT Total includes SAT Math, these predictors are redundant and one should be removed.
A health study uses height (x₁, inches) and weight (x₂, lbs) to predict blood pressure. These predictors are correlated (r = 0.75). What is the practical consequence for the model?
When predictors are correlated, their shared information makes individual coefficients harder to estimate precisely.
An agricultural researcher models crop yield using rainfall (x₁), fertilizer (x₂), and sunlight hours (x₃). The overall F-test has p-value < 0.001, but the individual p-values for x₁ and x₃ are both above 0.05. What is the best interpretation?
A significant F-test with individually insignificant predictors often points to multicollinearity — the predictors together explain variation, but their shared information prevents any single one from standing out.
Real-World Applications
Example: Multiple regression uses:
Multiple regression includes multiple explanatory variables to predict a single response.
Example: Multiple regression can include categorical predictors by using:
Categorical variables are encoded as indicator (dummy) variables so they can be included in the regression equation.
Example: What is multiple regression?
Multiple regression extends simple regression by allowing more than one predictor to explain variation in a single response.
Example: What does the F-test assess in multiple regression?
The F-test compares the model with predictors to a model with no predictors (intercept only) to determine overall significance.
Example: A hospital uses multiple regression to predict length of stay (y, days) from patient age (x₁), severity score (x₂), and number of procedures (x₃): ŷ = 1.5 + 0.03x₁ + 0.8x₂ + 1.2x₃. For a 60-year-old patient with severity score 5 and 2 procedures, what is the predicted stay?

Example: A real estate model predicts house price (y, in $1,000s) using square footage (x₁), number of bedrooms (x₂), and a dummy variable for pool (x₃ = 1 if pool, 0 if not): ŷ = 50 + 0.1x₁ + 10x₂ + 25x₃. How much more is a house with a pool predicted to sell for, all else equal?
The coefficient on the pool dummy variable is 25, meaning a pool adds a predicted $25,000 to the house price, holding square footage and bedrooms constant.

--- Lesson 8.5: Nonlinear Regression Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nonlinear Regression Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a logarithmic model?
Logarithmic models capture situations where increases in x produce smaller and smaller increases in y.
What is a logistic model?
Logistic growth has an upper limit (carrying capacity), creating an S-curve as growth slows near that limit.
What is the coefficient of determination (r²) used for when comparing models?
r² helps compare models, but residual plots must also be checked to ensure the model's assumptions are met.
What is overfitting in the context of nonlinear models?
Adding excessive complexity (e.g., high-degree polynomials) captures sample-specific noise rather than the real trend.
What is the role of the residual plot after fitting a nonlinear model?
Regardless of model type, residuals should be patternless if the model is appropriate.

--- Lesson 8.6: Applications in Prediction Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Prediction Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a prediction interval?
Prediction intervals are wider than confidence intervals because they must cover not just where the average falls but where an individual observation might land.
What is feature engineering?
Feature engineering creates derived variables that may capture patterns the original variables alone do not.
What is the difference between a confidence interval for the mean response and a prediction interval?
The prediction interval is wider because it adds individual variation on top of the uncertainty about the mean.
What is the training-testing split in predictive modeling?
Holding out a test set prevents overfitting and gives a realistic measure of how the model performs on new data.
What is the difference between machine learning regression and classical regression?
Both share mathematical foundations, but ML focuses on prediction while classical statistics also prioritizes understanding relationships.
What is 'customer churn prediction'?
Churn prediction helps businesses retain customers by identifying at-risk individuals before they leave.
A health clinic builds a model predicting blood pressure from BMI, age, and smoking status. An engineer suggests also adding shoe size and favorite color as predictors. What is likely to happen?
Adding irrelevant predictors inflates R² through noise but hurts adjusted R² and test-set performance.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does the Framingham Heart Study risk score illustrate about prediction models?
The Framingham score is a classic example of multiple regression applied to medicine, combining several risk factors into a single prediction.
Example: A hospital builds a regression model to predict patient readmission within 30 days using age, diagnosis severity, and length of stay. The model has r² = 0.42 on the training data and r² = 0.38 on the test data. What does the small difference in r² values suggest?
A small drop from training to testing r² indicates the model captures real patterns rather than noise, even though moderate r² is common in healthcare.
Example: A weather service uses a regression model with 12 variables (temperature, humidity, wind speed, etc.) to predict rainfall. After 5-fold cross-validation, the average prediction error is 3.2 mm. If the model were only tested on its training data, the error would be 1.8 mm. Why is the cross-validation error higher?
Training error is optimistically low because the model has already seen those data; cross-validation provides a realistic estimate of out-of-sample performance.
Example: A sports analytics team predicts player performance using age, games played, and training hours. For a 25-year-old with 80 games and 500 training hours, the predicted score is 78 with a 95% prediction interval of (62, 94) and a 95% confidence interval for the mean of (74, 82). A scout asks 'Will this specific player score at least 70?' Which interval should be used?
For predicting an individual outcome, the prediction interval is appropriate because it captures individual variation beyond just the mean.
Example: A retail company builds a sales forecast model in 2020. By 2023, online shopping has grown significantly and consumer behavior has shifted. The model's predictions are increasingly inaccurate. What should the company do?
When the underlying process changes (structural change), models must be refitted with recent data to remain accurate.
Example: An insurance company uses regression to predict annual claim costs from driver age, vehicle type, and driving record. They test three models: Model A (2 variables, r² = 0.45 on test), Model B (5 variables, r² = 0.52 on test), Model C (20 variables, r² = 0.48 on test). Which model is likely best?
Model C's lower test r² compared to B suggests overfitting from too many variables. Model B balances complexity and predictive power.
Example: A marketing team performs 10-fold cross-validation on a customer spending model. The model has r² = 0.65 on training and r² = 0.62 on average across the 10 test folds. The manager asks, 'Can we use this model for next quarter's budget planning?'
Stable cross-validation results indicate reliable generalization; the model can inform planning while acknowledging the inherent uncertainty.
Example: A health clinic builds a model predicting blood pressure from BMI, age, and smoking status. An engineer suggests also adding shoe size and favorite color as predictors. What is likely to happen?
Adding irrelevant predictors inflates R² through noise but hurts adjusted R² and test-set performance.
Example: A university admissions office uses a regression model to predict first-year GPA from SAT score, high school GPA, and extracurricular hours. They notice that after a new feature — parent income — is added, prediction improves significantly. However, using income raises ethical concerns about fairness. What should they consider?
Responsible prediction modeling considers both accuracy and fairness; including sensitive variables may perpetuate systemic inequalities.

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Economics & Finance
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the risk-free rate in CAPM?
The risk-free rate represents the baseline return an investor can earn without taking on market risk.
What is the Phillips Curve?
The Phillips Curve describes the tradeoff: lower unemployment tends to coincide with higher inflation.
What is 'tail risk' in financial models?
Financial returns often have 'fat tails' — extreme losses occur more frequently than a normal distribution would predict.
What is a hedonic pricing model?
Hedonic models decompose a product's price into the implicit prices of its characteristics.
What is Value at Risk (VaR)?
VaR answers: 'What is the worst loss I can expect with 95% (or 99%) confidence over this time horizon?'
A financial analyst regresses a tech stock's monthly returns against the S&P 500 returns and finds β = 1.5. If the S&P 500 drops 8% next month, what is the predicted change in the stock's return?
β = 1.5 means the stock moves 1.5 times the market. A −8% market move predicts 1.5 × (−8%) = −12% for the stock.

Price = 80,000 + 150(1,500) + 20,000(3) + 35,000(1) − 5,000(10) = 80,000 + 225,000 + 60,000 + 35,000 − 50,000 = $350,000.
