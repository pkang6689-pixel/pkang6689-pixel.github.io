--- Lesson 8.2: Least Squares Regression Line (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Least Squares Regression Line
Least Squares Regression Line (LSRL)
The least squares regression line (LSRL) is the line that minimizes the sum of the squared vertical distances (residuals) between the data points and the line.

Equation: Е· = a + bx, where:
  вҖў b = r(sy/sx) вҖ” the slope.  вҖў a = Иі вҲ’ bxМ„ вҖ” the y-intercept.  вҖў The line always passes through the point (xМ„, Иі).
Interpreting slope (b): For each one-unit increase in x, Е· changes by b units on average.

Interpreting intercept (a): The predicted y-value when x = 0 (may not be meaningful in context).

rВІ (coefficient of determination): The proportion of variation in y explained by the linear model. rВІ = 0.75 means 75% of the variability in y is explained by x.

Extrapolation: Predicting y for x values far outside the data range вҖ” unreliable and should be avoided.

--- Lesson 8.4: Multiple Regression (introductory) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Multiple Regression (introductory)

--- Lesson 8.5: Nonlinear Regression Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nonlinear Regression Models
Nonlinear Regression Models
When the relationship between x and y is not linear, nonlinear models may fit better.
  вҖў Quadratic model: Е· = a + bx + cxВІ. Used for U-shaped or inverted-U patterns.  вҖў Exponential model: Е· = abx. Used for growth/decay patterns. Used when growth slows over time.  вҖў Transformations: Taking log, square root, or reciprocal of x or y to straighten a curved relationship so linear regression can be applied.
Choosing a model: Examine the scatterplot, try transformations, check residual plots, and compare rВІ values of different models.

--- Lesson 8.6: Applications in Prediction Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Prediction Models
Applications in Prediction Models
Regression models are widely used for prediction in many fields.
  вҖў Weather forecasting: Multiple regression with variables like pressure, temperature, humidity to predict rainfall or temperature.  вҖў Sports analytics: Sabermetrics uses regression to predict player performance and team wins (e.g., MLB's Moneyball approach).  вҖў Medical prediction: Risk models predict disease likelihood based on patient characteristics (e.g., Framingham cardiovascular risk score).  вҖў Business: Sales forecasting, customer churn prediction, pricing models.  вҖў Machine learning: Linear and nonlinear regression are foundational algorithms in predictive modeling and AI.
Cross-validation: Testing the model on data not used to build it, ensuring it generalizes well. Common approach: split data into training and testing sets.

Prediction intervals are wider than confidence intervals because they account for both the regression uncertainty and individual variation.

--- Lesson 8.7: Case Studies in Economics & Finance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Economics & Finance
Case Studies in Economics & Finance
Statistics and regression analysis are central to economics and finance.
  вҖў Supply and demand: Regression models estimate how price changes affect quantity demanded (elasticity).  вҖў Stock market: The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) uses regression: E(Rбөў) = RвӮ‘ + ОІбөў(RвӮҳ вҲ’ RвӮ‘), where ОІ is the stock's sensitivity to market movements.  вҖў Inflation and unemployment: The Phillips Curve models the inverse relationship вҖ” as unemployment decreases, inflation tends to increase.  вҖў GDP prediction: Multiple regression models forecast GDP using investment, consumption, government spending, and net exports.  вҖў Risk management: Value at Risk (VaR) uses normal distributions and confidence levels to estimate potential portfolio losses.  вҖў Consumer behavior: Regression on demographic and economic variables predicts spending patterns.
Economic data often violates regression assumptions (autocorrelation, non-stationarity), requiring specialized techniques like time series analysis.
