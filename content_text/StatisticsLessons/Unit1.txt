--- Lesson 1.1: What is Statistics? (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: What is Statistics?
What is Statistics?
Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting data. It provides tools to make sense of large amounts of information and to draw conclusions about the world.
  • Descriptive statistics summarize and organize data (e.g., mean, median, charts).  • Inferential statistics use sample data to make predictions or generalizations about a larger population.  • Data are facts or observations collected for analysis.
Statistics is used in virtually every field: medicine, business, government, sports, science, and more. Understanding statistics helps you critically evaluate claims and make better decisions.

Key terminology: A variable is any characteristic that can be measured or counted. An observation is a single data point. A dataset is a collection of observations.

--- Lesson 1.2: Populations vs. Samples (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Populations vs. Samples
Populations vs. Samples
In statistics, a population is the entire group of individuals or items that we want to study. A sample is a subset of the population selected for analysis.
  • Population: All members of a defined group (e.g., all high-school students in the U.S.).  • Sample: A smaller group drawn from the population (e.g., 500 randomly selected high-school students).  • Parameter: A numerical value describing a characteristic of a population (e.g., the true average height of all students).  • Statistic: A numerical value describing a characteristic of a sample (e.g., the average height of the 500 selected students).
Since studying an entire population is usually impractical, we use samples and then apply inferential statistics to draw conclusions about the population. The quality of our conclusions depends on how well the sample represents the population.

Census – a study that collects data from every member of the population. Censuses are expensive and time-consuming, which is why sampling is preferred in most studies.

--- Lesson 1.3: Experimental Design & Bias (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Experimental Design & Bias
Experimental Design & Bias
Good statistical studies require careful experimental design to produce reliable results. A poorly designed study can introduce bias, leading to conclusions that are misleading or wrong.
  • Observational study: Researchers observe subjects without manipulating any variables. Example: tracking diet and health outcomes.  • Experiment: Researchers deliberately impose a treatment on subjects and measure the response. Example: testing a new drug vs. a placebo.  • Bias: A systematic error that causes results to deviate from the truth. Common types include selection bias, response bias, and measurement bias.  • Control group: The group that does not receive the treatment, used for comparison.  • Placebo: An inactive treatment given to the control group to account for psychological effects.  • Blinding: Subjects (single-blind) or both subjects and researchers (double-blind) do not know who is in the treatment or control group, reducing bias.  • Confounding variable: An outside variable that affects both the explanatory and response variables, making it hard to determine cause and effect.
The gold standard in experimental design is the randomized controlled double-blind experiment, which minimizes bias and confounding.

--- Lesson 1.4: Types of Data (qualitative vs. quantitative) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Data (qualitative vs. quantitative)
Types of Data
Data can be classified into two broad categories based on the nature of the information they represent.
  • Qualitative (categorical) data describe qualities or categories that cannot be measured numerically. Examples: eye color, gender, favorite food, zip code.  • Quantitative (numerical) data represent measurable quantities expressed as numbers. Examples: height, weight, temperature, income.
Quantitative data are further divided into:
  • Discrete data: Values that can be counted and are typically whole numbers (e.g., number of students in a class, number of cars in a parking lot).  • Continuous data: Values that can take any number within a range, often involving measurements (e.g., height = 5.7 feet, temperature = 72.3°F).
Correctly classifying data is essential because different types require different statistical methods and visualizations. For instance, bar charts suit categorical data, while histograms suit quantitative data.

--- Lesson 1.5: Levels of Measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Levels of Measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio)
Levels of Measurement
Data can be classified into four levels of measurement, each with increasing mathematical properties.
  • Nominal: Data are labels or names with no inherent order. Examples: eye color, gender, country. You can only check equality (same or different).  • Ordinal: Data have a meaningful order, but the differences between values are not consistent or meaningful. Examples: letter grades (A, B, C), rankings (1st, 2nd, 3rd), satisfaction ratings (low, medium, high).  • Interval: Data have a meaningful order and consistent intervals between values, but there is no true zero point. Examples: temperature in °C or °F (0°C ≠ absence of temperature), calendar years.  • Ratio: Data have a meaningful order, consistent intervals, and a true zero point (zero means the absence of the quantity). Examples: height, weight, age, income, distance.
The level of measurement determines which statistical operations are appropriate. You can compute a mean for interval and ratio data but not meaningfully for nominal or ordinal data.

--- Lesson 1.6: Random Sampling & Randomization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Random Sampling & Randomization
Random Sampling & Randomization
Random sampling is a method of selecting participants for a study so that every member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. This is the foundation of unbiased statistical inference.

Types of random sampling methods:
  • Simple random sample (SRS): Every possible sample of size n has an equal chance of being chosen. Like drawing names from a hat.  • Systematic sampling: Selecting every k-th individual from a list after a random starting point.  • Stratified sampling: Dividing the population into groups (strata) and randomly sampling from each stratum.  • Cluster sampling: Dividing the population into clusters, randomly selecting some clusters, and including all members of those clusters.
Randomization in experiments refers to randomly assigning subjects to treatment and control groups. This helps ensure the groups are similar in all respects except the treatment, reducing the impact of confounding variables.

Random sampling gives us a representative sample; randomization in experiments allows us to establish cause and effect.

--- Lesson 1.7: Case Studies in Statistical Misuse (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Statistical Misuse
Case Studies in Statistical Misuse
Statistics can be a powerful tool, but they can also be misused — intentionally or accidentally — to mislead. Understanding common pitfalls helps you become a critical consumer of data.
  • Cherry-picking data: Selecting only data that supports a desired conclusion while ignoring contradictory evidence.  • Misleading graphs: Manipulating axis scales, using truncated axes, or choosing inappropriate graph types to distort the visual impression of data.  • Correlation vs. causation: Assuming that because two variables are correlated, one must cause the other. Example: ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer, but ice cream doesn't cause drowning — hot weather drives both.  • Small or biased samples: Drawing sweeping conclusions from tiny or unrepresentative samples.  • Loaded questions: Wording survey questions to encourage a particular response.  • Simpson's Paradox: A trend that appears in several groups of data reverses when the groups are combined.
Always ask: Who collected the data? How was the sample selected? What is the sample size? Are the graphs scaled honestly? Could there be confounding variables?
