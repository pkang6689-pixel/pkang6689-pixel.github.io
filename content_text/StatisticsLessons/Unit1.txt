--- Lesson 1.1: What is Statistics? (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: What is Statistics?
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the main purpose of statistics?
Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting data to inform decisions.
What is descriptive statistics?
Descriptive statistics is the branch focused on organizing, summarizing, and presenting the main features of a dataset.
What is a dataset?
A dataset is a structured collection of data points or observations gathered for the purpose of statistical analysis.
What is a variable in the context of statistics?
A variable is any measurable or countable characteristic that can vary from one observation to another, such as age or income.
What is the meaning of the term 'population' in statistics?
In statistics, a population is the complete set of all individuals, items, or observations that are of interest in a particular study.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a dataset?
A dataset is a collection of data points or observations, like a table of many students' scores.

Calculating the class average is summarizing data, which is descriptive statistics.
Example: Which is NOT a goal of statistics?
Statistics helps analyze and interpret data but cannot guarantee outcomes with absolute certainty.

Computing the average wait time summarizes the collected data, which is a descriptive statistics technique.

Computing and reporting the average number of goals is analyzing and describing the collected data using descriptive statistics.

--- Lesson 1.2: Populations vs. Samples (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Populations vs. Samples
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a census?
A census is a complete count or study that gathers information from every individual in the population of interest.
What is a point estimate?
A point estimate is a single value from a sample, such as the sample mean, used as the best guess for the corresponding population parameter.
What is the difference between a parameter and a statistic?
Parameters are numerical summaries of populations, while statistics are numerical summaries of samples drawn from those populations.
What is the meaning of 'sample size' (denoted n)?
Sample size (n) is the count of individual observations or data points included in a particular sample.
Real-World Applications
Example: The average GPA of ALL students at a university is an example of:
Because it describes the entire population (all students), it is a parameter.
Example: A study examines the heights of every student in a school of 800. This is best described as a:
Since data is collected from every member of the school population (all 800 students), it is a census.
Example: A quality control manager inspects 100 items from a production run of 50,000 and finds that 3% are defective. The 3% defect rate is a:
Since the 3% was computed from a sample of 100 items (not the full 50,000), it is a statistic that estimates the population defect rate.
Example: A university wants to know the average student loan debt of its 25,000 graduates. It surveys 400 graduates and calculates x̄ = $32,500. The university then estimates that the average for all graduates is about $32,500. In this scenario, $32,500 is:
The $32,500 sample mean is a point estimate because it uses sample data to approximate the unknown population mean.
Example: A city health department wants to estimate the percentage of residents who have received a flu vaccine. They randomly call 500 households. If 60% of those called report vaccination, but people without phones are excluded, this could lead to:
Excluding people without phones means some residents have zero probability of selection, causing under-coverage bias and a potentially unrepresentative sample.
Example: Two researchers study the same school district. Researcher A surveys 50 students and finds an average math score of 78. Researcher B surveys a different group of 50 students and finds an average of 82. The difference between these two results is best explained by:
Sampling variability means that different random samples from the same population will produce slightly different statistics, which is a normal and expected phenomenon.

--- Lesson 1.3: Experimental Design & Bias (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Experimental Design & Bias
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a confounding variable?
A confounding variable influences both the explanatory and response variables, creating a misleading association that makes it difficult to determine true cause and effect.
What is a placebo?
A placebo is an inactive treatment, such as a sugar pill, given to control group subjects so they cannot tell whether they received the real treatment.
What is the placebo effect?
The placebo effect occurs when subjects experience real changes in symptoms simply because they believe they are being treated.
What is an explanatory variable?
The explanatory (independent) variable is the factor the researcher manipulates or identifies as possibly causing changes in the response variable.
What is a response variable?
The response (dependent) variable is the outcome being measured in a study to determine whether the treatment had an effect.
What is a lurking variable?
A lurking variable is not measured or accounted for in the study but can influence the results and create misleading associations.
A teacher assigns the front row to use a new study method and the back row to use the old method, then compares test scores. What is the main flaw?
Without random assignment, students in the front row may differ systematically from those in the back, making seating position a confounding variable.
Real-World Applications
Example: Researchers find that ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer. The most likely confounding variable is:
Hot weather is a confounding variable that drives both ice cream purchases and swimming (and thus drowning), creating a spurious correlation.
Example: A researcher studying a new drug knows which patients received it and subtly treats them differently during evaluations. This is an example of:
When the researcher's knowledge of group assignments affects their behavior toward subjects, it introduces observer bias.
Example: A teacher assigns the front row to use a new study method and the back row to use the old method, then compares test scores. What is the main flaw?
Without random assignment, students in the front row may differ systematically from those in the back, making seating position a confounding variable.
Example: A school wants to test whether free breakfast improves attendance. They randomly select 5 of their 10 elementary schools to offer free breakfast; the other 5 serve as controls. After one semester, they compare average attendance rates. Which key principle of experimental design does this scenario demonstrate?
Randomly assigning schools to treatment (free breakfast) and control groups allows the school district to compare outcomes and draw causal conclusions about breakfast's effect on attendance.

--- Lesson 1.4: Types of Data (qualitative vs. quantitative) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Data (qualitative vs. quantitative)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is qualitative data?
Qualitative (categorical) data describes qualities or categories, such as gender, color, or brand, rather than numerical amounts.
What is quantitative data?
Quantitative data represents numerical values that can be counted or measured, such as height, weight, or number of items.
What is a histogram used for?
A histogram groups quantitative data into bins (intervals) and uses adjacent bars to display the frequency distribution.
What is the key difference between discrete and continuous data?
Discrete values are countable with gaps (e.g., 0, 1, 2), while continuous values can be any number within an interval (e.g., 3.14159).
What is a bar chart best used for?
Bar charts use separated bars to show the frequency or proportion of different categories, making them ideal for categorical data.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of quantitative data?
Height is a measurable numerical value, making it quantitative data.
Example: Eye color is an example of:
Eye color describes a category, not a numerical measurement, so it is qualitative.
Example: Which of the following is NOT an example of qualitative data?
Number of pets is a count, making it a quantitative (discrete) value rather than qualitative.
Example: A researcher studying phone usage records each participant's phone brand (Apple, Samsung, Google) and daily screen time in hours (3.2, 5.1, 4.7). The appropriate graph for the phone brand data would be a _____ and for screen time a _____.
Phone brand is categorical, best shown with a bar chart, while screen time is continuous quantitative data, best shown with a histogram.
Example: A city collects zip code data from residents (e.g., 90210, 10001, 30301). A new analyst calculates the 'average zip code' as 43,504. Why is this meaningless?
Although zip codes are written as numbers, they are qualitative labels for geographic areas, so computing an average has no meaningful interpretation.
Example: A school cafeteria tracks both the number of meals served each day (150, 203, 178) and the type of entree offered (pizza, tacos, salad). A statistician wants to find the average number of meals per day and the most popular entree. Which analysis is correct?
Meal counts are discrete quantitative data suitable for averaging, while entree types are categorical data best summarized by the mode (most frequent category).

--- Lesson 1.5: Levels of Measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Levels of Measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is nominal data?
Nominal data uses names or labels to classify observations into categories that have no natural ranking or order.
What is ordinal data?
Ordinal data has a meaningful order or ranking, but the intervals between consecutive values are not necessarily equal.
What is interval data?
Interval data has ordered values with equal, measurable gaps between them, but lacks a true zero, so ratios are not meaningful.
What is ratio data?
Ratio data possesses all measurement properties, including a true zero point, enabling statements like 'twice as much' to be meaningful.
Real-World Applications
Example: Temperature measured in Fahrenheit is an example of:
Fahrenheit temperature has equal intervals but no true zero (0 degrees F does not mean no temperature).
Example: A hospital records the pain level of patients using a scale of 1 (no pain) to 10 (worst pain). A doctor wants to compute the average pain score for the unit. Which concern applies?
Pain scales are ordinal because the difference between pain levels 2 and 3 may not equal the difference between 7 and 8, even though averages are commonly used in practice.

Overview

What is the best defense against statistical misuse?

What is Simpson's Paradox?

    [*] Potential bias from the funding source and whether negative results were suppressed
    [ ] Nothing — the results are always reliable
    [ ] That the study must have been a census
    Explanation: Industry-funded studies may be subject to funding bias, and negative or inconclusive results may not be published.

  Q24. A news headline states 'Chocolate prevents heart disease' based on one small observational study of 50 people. What are the problems with this headline?
    [ ] Headlines are always perfectly accurate
    [*] The sample is small, the study is observational (cannot prove causation), and headlines oversimplify findings
    [ ] There are no problems; one study is enough
    [ ] Chocolate is unhealthy, so the study must be wrong
    Explanation: A small observational study cannot establish causation, and the headline overgeneralizes a preliminary finding.

  Q25. A survey asks: 'Don't you agree that taxes are too high?' This question is problematic because:
    [ ] It is a form of random sampling
    [ ] Taxes are never too high
    [ ] It is a well-designed neutral question
    [*] It is a leading question that pushes respondents toward agreement
    Explanation: The phrasing 'Don't you agree...' is a leading question that biases respondents toward the affirmative answer.

  Q26. A study concludes that countries with more TVs per capita have higher life expectancy. A confounding variable might be:
    [ ] Life expectancy causes TV sales to increase
    [*] Wealthier countries can afford both more TVs and better healthcare
    [ ] TVs directly improve health
    [ ] There is no confounding variable in this scenario
    Explanation: National wealth is a confounding variable that drives both TV ownership and access to quality healthcare.

  Q27. An advertisement reports 'our product is 50% more effective!' Without stating what it is compared to or what 'effective' means, this claim is:
    [ ] Conclusive evidence of superiority
    [ ] Always true because 50% is a large number
    [ ] A result from a randomized controlled trial
    [*] A potentially misleading claim that lacks context and a clear baseline
    Explanation: Without a clear benchmark or definition of 'effective,' the '50% more effective' claim is vague and potentially meaningless.

  Q28. A study with only 15 participants claims a dietary supplement cures insomnia. The study has no control group and no blinding. A reader should be concerned because:
    [ ] Dietary supplements always work
    [ ] The study is well-designed since it has participants
    [ ] 15 is always a sufficient sample size
    [*] The tiny sample, lack of control group, and absence of blinding make the results unreliable
    Explanation: Without a control group, any improvement could be a placebo effect; without blinding, bias may influence results; and 15 participants is too few for reliable conclusions.

  Q29. Hospital A has a better survival rate for each type of surgery, but Hospital B has a better overall survival rate. This is an example of Simpson's Paradox. The most likely explanation is:
    [ ] This paradox is mathematically impossible and cannot actually occur in real data analysis
    [ ] Statistics are fundamentally unreliable and should not be used for comparing hospital outcomes
    [*] Hospital B handles a larger proportion of easier surgeries, inflating its overall rate despite being worse for each individual type
    [ ] The data must contain calculation errors because it is not possible for subgroup and aggregate trends to differ
    Explanation: Simpson's Paradox occurs here because the mix of surgery types differs between hospitals, and Hospital B's overall rate is boosted by performing more of the easier surgeries.

  Q30. A social media post claims: 'City X has the highest crime rate in the state — it's the most dangerous place to live!' A statistically literate person would ask all of the following EXCEPT:
    [ ] Is the data source reliable and recent?
    [ ] What types of crimes are included in the statistic?
    [ ] How was 'crime rate' calculated (per capita or total crimes)?
    [*] Does the post have enough likes and shares to be true?
    Explanation: The number of likes or shares has no bearing on the accuracy of a statistical claim. A critical thinker should examine methodology, definitions, and data sources.
