--- Lesson 10.1: Review of Key Formulas (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Review of Key Formulas
Review of Key Formulas
A comprehensive review of the essential formulas used throughout AP Statistics.
Descriptive Statistics  • Mean: x̄ = Σxᵢ/n  • Standard deviation: s = √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²/(n−1)]  • z-score: z = (x − μ)/σ  • IQR = Q₃ − Q₁; Outlier fences: Q₁ − 1.5·IQR, Q₃ + 1.5·IQRProbability  • P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)  • P(A|B) = P(A and B)/P(B)  • Binomial: P(X=k) = C(n,k)pk(1−p)n−k, μ = np, σ = √(np(1−p))Inference  • Confidence interval: statistic ± (critical value)(SE)  • SE of mean: s/√n; SE of proportion: √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)  • t-statistic: t = (x̄ − μ₀)/(s/√n)  • z-statistic for proportion: z = (p̂ − p₀)/√(p₀(1−p₀)/n)  • Chi-square: χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E  • Regression slope: b = r(sᵧ/sₓ); r² = proportion of variation explained

--- Lesson 10.1: Review of Key Formulas (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The formula for population z-score is:
    [ ] z = (x − s)/n
    [*] z = (x − μ)/σ
    [ ] z = μ/σ
    [ ] z = σ/x
    Explanation: z-score standardizes a value relative to the mean and standard deviation.

  Q2. Sample standard deviation uses n − 1 because:
    [ ] It's simpler
    [*] It corrects bias in estimating the population standard deviation (Bessel's correction)
    [ ] n − 1 is always larger
    [ ] It's a convention with no reason
    Explanation: Bessel's correction gives an unbiased estimator of population variance.

  Q3. The addition rule P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B) prevents:
    [ ] Underestimating
    [*] Double-counting the overlap
    [ ] Ignoring P(B)
    [ ] Negative probabilities
    Explanation: Subtracting the intersection corrects for events counted twice.

  Q4. The SE of a sample mean is:
    [ ] s × n
    [ ] s × √n
    [*] s/√n
    [ ] σ/n
    Explanation: SE = s/√n estimates how much x̄ varies from sample to sample.

  Q5. The SE of a sample proportion p̂ is:
    [ ] p̂/n
    [ ] p̂ × n
    [*] √(p̂(1−p̂)/n)
    [ ] p̂/√n
    Explanation: The standard error of a proportion involves p̂(1−p̂) divided by n.

  Q6. A confidence interval has the form:
    [ ] Statistic × SE
    [*] Statistic ± (critical value)(SE)
    [ ] SE ± critical value
    [ ] Parameter ± statistic
    Explanation: CI = point estimate ± margin of error.

  Q7. The t-statistic for a one-sample mean test is:
    [*] t = (x̄ − μ₀)/(s/√n)
    [ ] t = μ₀/s
    [ ] t = n/s
    [ ] t = x̄ × s
    Explanation: t measures how far x̄ is from μ₀ in standard-error units.

  Q8. For binomial, μ = np gives:
    [ ] The variance
    [ ] The standard deviation
    [*] The expected number of successes
    [ ] The probability
    Explanation: The mean of a binomial is the number of trials times the probability of success.

  Q9. For binomial, σ = √(np(1−p)) gives:
    [ ] The mean
    [*] The standard deviation
    [ ] The variance
    [ ] The range
    Explanation: σ measures the typical spread of the number of successes.

  Q10. The regression slope b = r(sᵧ/sₓ) depends on:
    [ ] Only r
    [ ] Only the standard deviations
    [*] Both the correlation r and the ratio of standard deviations
    [ ] Only the means
    Explanation: b combines the strength of association (r) with the relative scales (sᵧ/sₓ).

  Q11. r² represents:
    [ ] The correlation
    [*] The proportion of variation in y explained by the linear relationship with x
    [ ] The slope squared
    [ ] The residual sum
    Explanation: r² = coefficient of determination.

  Q12. The chi-square statistic sums:
    [ ] Observed frequencies
    [ ] Expected frequencies
    [*] Squared differences between observed and expected, divided by expected
    [ ] Raw data
    Explanation: χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E.

  Q13. P(A|B) = P(A and B)/P(B) is the formula for:
    [ ] Independence
    [ ] The addition rule
    [*] Conditional probability
    [ ] Bayes' theorem
    Explanation: Conditional probability gives the probability of A given that B has occurred.

  Q14. The IQR is calculated as:
    [ ] Q₂ − Q₁
    [*] Q₃ − Q₁
    [ ] Max − Min
    [ ] Mean − Median
    Explanation: IQR spans the middle 50% of the data.

  Q15. A value is a suspected outlier if it falls:
    [ ] Within the IQR
    [ ] On the median
    [*] Below Q₁ − 1.5·IQR or above Q₃ + 1.5·IQR
    [ ] Within 1 SD of the mean

  Q16. The z-statistic for testing a proportion is:
    [ ] z = (p̂ − p₀)/p₀

    [ ] z = p̂/p₀

  Q17. Degrees of freedom for a one-sample t-test:
    [ ] n
    [*] n − 1
    [ ] n + 1
    [ ] 2n

  Q18. The critical value z* = 1.96 corresponds to:
    [ ] 90% confidence
    [*] 95% confidence
    [ ] 99% confidence
    [ ] 80% confidence
    Explanation: For a 95% CI, the two-tailed z* is 1.96.

  Q19. C(n,k) in the binomial formula counts:
    [ ] Probabilities
    [*] The number of ways to choose k successes from n trials
    [ ] The mean
    [ ] The variance
    Explanation: C(n,k) = n!/(k!(n−k)!) is the combination formula.

  Q20. Knowing these formulas matters because:
    [ ] They are arbitrary
    [ ] Memorization is the goal
    [*] They are the tools used to analyze data, make inferences, and solve AP problems
    [ ] They only appear on tests
    Explanation: Formulas connect concepts to calculations in every part of statistics.

--- Lesson 10.2: AP-Style Practice Problems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP-Style Practice Problems
AP-Style Practice Problems
Success on the AP Statistics exam requires applying concepts to multi-step problems under timed conditions.
Problem-Solving Strategy  • Read carefully — identify whether the problem involves inference, probability, regression, or experimental design.  • State hypotheses clearly — for inference questions, always write H₀ and Hₐ in context.  • Check conditions — randomness, normality, independence. State them explicitly.  • Show your work — name the procedure, show the formula, plug in values, compute.  • Conclude in context — always relate your answer back to the real-world situation described.Common Free-Response Topics  • Designing an experiment or survey with proper randomization.  • Performing and interpreting a hypothesis test (one/two-sample, proportions, χ²).  • Constructing and interpreting confidence intervals.  • Analyzing residual plots and regression output.  • Probability calculations including conditional probability and expected value.

--- Lesson 10.3: Interpreting Graphical Data in AP Exams (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Interpreting Graphical Data in AP Exams
Interpreting Graphical Data in AP Exams
The AP exam frequently presents data in graphical form. You must be able to read, interpret, and draw conclusions from graphs.
  • Histograms: Identify shape (symmetric, skewed, bimodal), center, spread, and outliers. Don't confuse frequency with value.  • Boxplots: Read the five-number summary (min, Q₁, median, Q₃, max). Compare distributions across groups. Identify outliers (dots beyond whiskers).  • Scatterplots: Describe direction, form, strength, and unusual points. Use the regression line and r/r² if given.  • Residual plots: Random scatter = good fit; patterns = model problems.  • Normal probability plots (Q-Q plots): Points roughly on a straight line = approximately normal.  • Dotplots and stemplots: Good for small datasets. Show individual values and distributional shape.  • Bar charts and pie charts: For categorical data. Compare relative frequencies, not just heights.

Real-World Applications
Statistics is applied across virtually every field. This lesson surveys major applications.

--- Lesson 10.5: Capstone Project: Statistical Analysis of a Dataset (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Capstone Project: Statistical Analysis of a Dataset
Capstone Project: Statistical Analysis of a Dataset
A capstone project integrates all statistical skills by analyzing a real dataset from start to finish.
Project Steps  • Choose a question: What are you investigating? State a clear research question.  • Collect or obtain data: Use a reputable source (government databases, academic datasets, surveys with proper sampling).  • Explore the data: Compute descriptive statistics (mean, SD, median, IQR). Create graphs (histograms, boxplots, scatterplots).  • State hypotheses: Based on your question, formulate H₀ and Hₐ.  • Check conditions: Verify independence, normality, and sample size requirements.  • Perform analysis: Run the appropriate test (t-test, χ², regression, ANOVA) or construct confidence intervals.  • Interpret results: State conclusions in context. Discuss limitations and potential confounding variables.  • Present findings: Create a clear report or presentation summarizing your work.
