--- Lesson 4.1: Invertible Matrices (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Invertible Matrices
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If A is invertible, what is A * A^(-1)?
By definition, A * A^(-1) = A^(-1) * A = I, the identity matrix.
What is (A^(-1))^(-1)?
The inverse of A^(-1) is A itself, since A^(-1) * A = I.
For a diagonal matrix D = diag(2, 5, -3), what is D^(-1)?
The inverse of a diagonal matrix inverts each diagonal entry: D^(-1) = diag(1/2, 1/5, -1/3).
What is the 'Invertible Matrix Theorem'?
The Invertible Matrix Theorem lists many equivalent statements: A is invertible iff det(A)!=0 iff rank(A)=n iff columns are linearly independent iff Ax=0 has only x=0 iff A row reduces to I, etc.
What is the relationship between invertibility and linear independence of columns?
An nxn matrix is invertible iff its n columns are linearly independent, which is equivalent to the columns spanning all of R^n.
A control engineer has system matrix A. She needs to solve Ax = b for multiple different b vectors. She computes A^(-1) once. What is the advantage?
Once A^(-1) is computed, solving for any b is just x = A^(-1)b, which is an O(n^2) matrix-vector multiplication rather than O(n^3) for each Gaussian elimination.
A computer graphics programmer uses the matrix A = [[cos t, -sin t], [sin t, cos t]]. What is A^(-1)?
Rotation matrices are orthogonal: A^T A = I, so A^(-1) = A^T. This corresponds to rotation by angle -t.
Real-World Applications
Example: A control engineer has system matrix A. She needs to solve Ax = b for multiple different b vectors. She computes A^(-1) once. What is the advantage?
Once A^(-1) is computed, solving for any b is just x = A^(-1)b, which is an O(n^2) matrix-vector multiplication rather than O(n^3) for each Gaussian elimination.
Example: A supply chain manager models transitions with matrix P. She computes P^10 to predict long-term behavior. If P is invertible, is P^10 invertible?
If det(P) != 0, then det(P^10) = (det P)^10 != 0, so P^10 is invertible.

--- Lesson 4.2: Finding Inverses (row reduction method) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Finding Inverses (row reduction method)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
After row reducing [A | I] to [I | B], what is B?
When the left side becomes I, the right side is the inverse: B = A^(-1).
Find the inverse of [[1,2],[3,7]] using row reduction. What is A^(-1)?
det=7-6=1. A^(-1) = [[7,-2],[-3,1]]. Verify: [[1,2],[3,7]]*[[7,-2],[-3,1]] = [[1,0],[0,1]].
Row reduce [[2,4|1,0],[1,3|0,1]]. After R1<->R2 and elimination, what is A^(-1)?
Swap rows: [[1,3|0,1],[2,4|1,0]]. R2-2R1: [[1,3|0,1],[0,-2|1,-2]]. R2/-2: [[1,3|0,1],[0,1|-1/2,1]]. R1-3R2: [[1,0|3/2,-2],[0,1|-1/2,1]]. A^(-1)=[[3/2,-2],[-1/2,1]].
What is the inverse of the 2x2 identity matrix?
I*I = I, so I^(-1) = I.
If row reduction of [A|I] yields [[1,0,0|2,-1,0],[0,1,0|-3,2,1],[0,0,1|1,0,-1]], what is A^(-1)?
The right side after complete reduction gives A^(-1) = [[2,-1,0],[-3,2,1],[1,0,-1]].
What is the computational cost of finding an inverse via row reduction for an nxn matrix?
Row reducing [A|I] to [I|A^(-1)] requires roughly 2n^3/3 + n^3 = O(n^3) operations, similar to solving n systems.
What is a 'row reduction algorithm' for finding inverses?
The algorithm applies forward elimination and back elimination (Gauss-Jordan) to [A|I], transforming the left side to I and the right side to A^(-1).
What is the 'Gauss-Jordan method' for inverses?
Gauss-Jordan applies forward elimination (zeros below pivots) and backward elimination (zeros above pivots) plus pivot scaling to transform A into I.
What is 'partial pivoting' used for in inverse computation?
Partial pivoting selects the largest absolute value in the pivot column, reducing numerical errors during row reduction.
What is the relationship between row reducing A to I and expressing I as a product of elementary matrices times A?
Row reduction of A to I means E_k...E_1*A = I, so A^(-1) = E_k...E_1. The same operations applied to I give A^(-1).
An electrical engineer row-reduces [[1,-1|1,0],[2,3|0,1]]. After R2-2R1: [[1,-1|1,0],[0,5|-2,1]]. After R2/5 and R1+R2: what is A^(-1)?
R2/5: [[1,-1|1,0],[0,1|-2/5,1/5]]. R1+R2: [[1,0|3/5,1/5],[0,1|-2/5,1/5]]. A^(-1)=[[3/5,1/5],[-2/5,1/5]] = [[0.6,0.2],[-0.4,0.2]].
A game developer needs to invert a 3x3 rotation matrix R. She knows R is orthogonal. What is the fastest way?
For orthogonal matrices, R^(-1) = R^T. Simply transposing is O(n^2), much faster than row reduction.
A climate researcher row-reduces [A|I] for a 5x5 matrix and successfully obtains [I|B]. She wants to verify B = A^(-1). What is the most reliable check?
The definitive verification is A*B = I (and optionally B*A = I). This directly confirms B is the inverse.

--- Lesson 4.3: Adjoint Method (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Adjoint Method
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is adj(I) for the nxn identity matrix?
Since the cofactor of each entry of I gives back I's structure, adj(I) = I.
If det(A) = 3 and adj(A) = [[6,-3],[0,3]], what is A^(-1)?
A^(-1) = adj(A)/det(A) = (1/3)*[[6,-3],[0,3]] = [[2,-1],[0,1]].
What is the computational complexity of the adjoint method for an nxn inverse?
Computing n^2 cofactors, each requiring an (n-1)x(n-1) determinant, gives O(n^2 * n^2) = O(n^4) using cofactor expansion, or O(n^2 * (n-1)^3) using elimination for each.
If A = [[2,1],[5,3]], what is adj(A)?
For 2x2: swap diagonal (2,3)->(3,2), negate off-diagonal (1,5)->(-1,-5). adj(A) = [[3,-1],[-5,2]].
For a singular matrix (det=0), what is adj(A)?
A*adj(A) = det(A)*I = 0*I = 0. The adjugate itself may have nonzero entries, but the product with A is always zero.
What is the 'adjugate' of a matrix?
Adjugate is the modern mathematical term for the classical adjoint, to avoid confusion with the Hermitian adjoint.
What is det(adj(A)) for an nxn matrix A?
det(adj(A)) = det(A)^(n-1). This follows from A*adj(A) = det(A)*I and taking determinants of both sides.
What is the adjugate of a diagonal matrix diag(a,b,c)?
For a diagonal matrix, the cofactors give products of the other diagonal entries: adj = diag(bc, ac, ab).
What is the use of the adjugate in differential equations?
In systems of ODEs dx/dt = Ax, the adjugate of (sI-A) appears in the Laplace transform solution and transfer functions.
A cryptographer needs the inverse of [[3,5],[2,7]] modulo 26. det = 11, and 11^(-1) mod 26 = 19. Using adj(A) = [[7,-5],[-2,3]], what is A^(-1) mod 26?
A^(-1) = 19*[[7,-5],[-2,3]] mod 26 = [[133,-95],[-38,57]] mod 26 = [[3,9],[14,5]].
A control engineer has a 2x2 transfer function matrix. She uses the adjugate to find the inverse symbolically: if A = [[s+1, 2],[3, s+4]], what is adj(A)?
For 2x2: swap diagonal entries and negate off-diagonal: adj = [[s+4, -2],[-3, s+1]].
A mathematician proves that for any nxn matrix A, A*adj(A) = det(A)*I. If A is 3x3 with det(A)=5, what is A*adj(A)?
A*adj(A) = det(A)*I = 5*I = diag(5,5,5).
A chemical engineer uses the formula adj(A)/det(A) for A = [[4,7],[2,6]]. det = 24-14 = 10. adj = [[6,-7],[-2,4]]. What is A^(-1)?
A^(-1) = (1/10)*[[6,-7],[-2,4]] = [[0.6,-0.7],[-0.2,0.4]].

--- Lesson 4.4: Singular vs. Non-Singular Matrices (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Singular vs. Non-Singular Matrices
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If matrix A is non-singular, what is the rank of an n x n matrix A?
A non-singular n x n matrix has full rank n, meaning all its rows and columns are linearly independent.
What is a singular matrix?
A singular matrix is a square matrix with determinant zero, which means no inverse exists for it.
What is a non-singular matrix?
A non-singular (invertible) matrix is a square matrix whose determinant is nonzero, guaranteeing the existence of a unique inverse.
What is the rank of a matrix?
Rank measures the dimension of the column space (or row space), counting the maximum number of linearly independent rows or columns.
What is linear dependence among rows of a matrix?
Rows are linearly dependent when at least one row can be written as a combination of the others, reducing the effective rank of the matrix.
What is the null space of a matrix?
The null space (kernel) consists of all vectors x such that Ax = 0. For a singular matrix this space is nontrivial.
What is full rank for a square matrix?
An n x n matrix has full rank when its rank equals n, meaning all rows and columns are linearly independent.
What is a rank-deficient matrix?
A rank-deficient matrix has rank smaller than its theoretical maximum, indicating linear dependence among its rows or columns.
What is the determinant test for invertibility?
The determinant test states that a square matrix is invertible (non-singular) if and only if its determinant is not equal to zero.
What is the column space of a matrix?
The column space is the span of all column vectors and represents every vector b for which Ax = b has a solution.
A game developer uses a 3x3 matrix to transform objects. She notices that after transformation, all objects appear flattened into a line. What is the rank of her matrix?
Objects collapsing into a line means the image of the transformation is one-dimensional, so the matrix has rank 1 and is singular.
Real-World Applications
Example: An economics student multiplies two non-singular matrices A and B to form C = AB. Her professor asks: is C singular or non-singular?
The product of two non-singular matrices is non-singular because det(AB) = det(A)*det(B), and the product of two nonzero numbers is nonzero.

--- Lesson 4.5: Applications of Inverse Matrices (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications of Inverse Matrices
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If A is a 2x2 matrix with A^(-1) = [[3, -1], [0, 2]], what is A^(-1) times b = [5, 4]?
Computing A^(-1)b: first entry = 3(5) + (-1)(4) = 11, second entry = 0(5) + 2(4) = 8, giving [11, 8].
What is one advantage of using A^(-1) to solve multiple systems Ax = b1, Ax = b2, etc.?
Once A^(-1) is computed, each new system only requires a matrix-vector multiplication, which is much faster than re-solving from scratch.
For a 2x2 matrix A = [[a, b], [c, d]], what is the formula for A^(-1)?
The inverse of a 2x2 matrix swaps the diagonal entries, negates the off-diagonal entries, and divides by the determinant ad - bc.
If A^(-1) = B, what is the value of AB?
By definition of the matrix inverse, A times A^(-1) equals the identity matrix I.
What is an inverse matrix?
The inverse of a square matrix A is a unique matrix B (denoted A^(-1)) satisfying AB = BA = I.
What is the Leontief input-output model?
The Leontief model describes how industries consume each other's outputs and uses (I - C)^(-1) to find total production needed for a given demand.
What is a coordinate transformation in the context of inverse matrices?
Coordinate transformations use invertible matrices to convert between reference frames, and the inverse matrix reverses the transformation.
What is the adjugate (classical adjoint) of a matrix?
The adjugate is the transpose of the matrix of cofactors, and it appears in the classical formula for computing the inverse of a matrix.
What is a change-of-basis matrix?
A change-of-basis matrix P converts vectors expressed in one basis to their representation in another, and P^(-1) reverses the conversion.
What is the consumption matrix in a Leontief model?
The consumption matrix C has entries c_ij representing how much of industry j's output is consumed by industry i per unit of production.
What is Cramer's Rule?
Cramer's Rule solves Ax = b by computing x_i = det(A_i)/det(A), where A_i replaces the i-th column of A with b.
What is meant by solving a linear system via matrix inversion?
Solving via inversion means computing the inverse of the coefficient matrix and multiplying it by b to directly obtain x.
What is the relationship between (AB)^(-1) and the inverses of A and B?
The inverse of a product reverses the order: (AB)^(-1) = B^(-1)A^(-1). This is sometimes called the 'socks-shoes' property.
What is a matrix equation?
A matrix equation writes a system of linear equations compactly as AX = B, where A is the coefficient matrix and X is the unknown matrix or vector.
A physics student uses a rotation matrix R to rotate a vector by 30 degrees. To undo the rotation, she needs R^(-1). For a rotation matrix, what is R^(-1)?
Rotation matrices are orthogonal, so R^(-1) = R^T. The transpose rotates by the negative angle, undoing the original rotation.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is one advantage of using A^(-1) to solve multiple systems Ax = b1, Ax = b2, etc.?
Once A^(-1) is computed, each new system only requires a matrix-vector multiplication, which is much faster than re-solving from scratch.
Example: A manufacturer has three plants, and the production system is modeled as Ax = b for five different monthly demand vectors b1 through b5. The manager computes A^(-1) once. How many full matrix inversions are needed for all five months?
Only one inversion is needed because A^(-1) is computed once and then multiplied by each demand vector b_i to get each month's production plan.
Example: A cryptographer encodes a message as b = Ax and decodes it using x = A^(-1)b. If an attacker intercepts b but does not know A, what protects the message?
The encryption key is matrix A; without knowing A or A^(-1), the attacker cannot compute x = A^(-1)b to decode the original message.
Example: A supply-chain manager solves for optimal shipping quantities using x = A^(-1)b. After a factory closes, one row and column of A change. What must the manager do?
When the coefficient matrix A changes, its inverse must be recomputed because the old inverse no longer corresponds to the updated system.

--- Lesson 4.6: Using Inverses in Cryptography (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Using Inverses in Cryptography
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the multiplicative inverse of 3 modulo 26?
Since 3 * 9 = 27, and 27 mod 26 = 1, the multiplicative inverse of 3 modulo 26 is 9.
What is the Hill cipher?
The Hill cipher is a polygraphic substitution cipher that encrypts groups of letters simultaneously using matrix multiplication modulo 26.
What is a modular multiplicative inverse?
The modular multiplicative inverse of a modulo n is the integer b satisfying a*b mod n = 1, and it exists only when gcd(a, n) = 1.
What is a known-plaintext attack on a Hill cipher?
In a known-plaintext attack, the attacker uses matched plaintext-ciphertext pairs to set up equations and solve for the key matrix using its inverse.
What is a polygraphic cipher?
A polygraphic cipher encrypts multiple letters as a single unit, which the Hill cipher achieves by processing vectors of letters through matrix multiplication.
What is the key space of a Hill cipher?
The key space consists of all n x n matrices with integer entries modulo 26 whose determinant is coprime to 26, ensuring invertibility.
What is symmetric-key encryption?
In symmetric-key encryption, both parties share the same secret key. The Hill cipher is symmetric because the decryption key is derived directly from the encryption key.
What is frequency analysis in the context of cryptanalysis?
Frequency analysis exploits the fact that certain letters or n-grams appear more often in natural language, potentially revealing patterns in the ciphertext.
What is a block cipher?
A block cipher processes plaintext in fixed-size blocks. The Hill cipher is a block cipher where the block size equals the dimension of the key matrix.
What is the Extended Euclidean Algorithm used for in Hill cipher cryptography?
The Extended Euclidean Algorithm finds integers x, y such that ax + ny = gcd(a, n), which gives the modular inverse of the determinant when gcd = 1.
What is diffusion in cryptography?
Diffusion means each plaintext letter influences multiple ciphertext letters. The Hill cipher achieves diffusion because each ciphertext letter depends on all letters in the block.
A university professor demonstrates the Hill cipher in class using K = [[6, 1], [1, 4]]. A student points out that det(K) = 23 and asks if the key works. What is the correct response?
Since gcd(23, 26) = 1, the determinant has a modular inverse mod 26, making the key matrix invertible and valid for the Hill cipher.
A cybersecurity student is told that a Hill cipher key has det(K) = 13 (mod 26). She tries to find the modular inverse of 13 mod 26. What is the result?
Since 13 divides 26, gcd(13, 26) = 13 and not 1, so no modular inverse exists and the key is invalid for encryption.

An adjacency matrix is a square matrix representation of a graph, with a 1 (or weight) at position (i,j) if vertices i and j are connected.

A stochastic matrix has non-negative entries with rows (or columns) summing to 1, modeling transition probabilities in Markov chains and PageRank.

A convolution kernel is a small matrix (e.g., 3x3) applied across an image via element-wise multiplication and summation to produce filtered output.

A Markov chain transitions between states based on fixed probabilities, and the state after n steps is found by multiplying the transition matrix n times.

Dimensionality reduction uses techniques like PCA or SVD to project data into fewer dimensions while retaining the most informative features.
What is a projection matrix in computer graphics?

LSA uses SVD to reduce the term-document matrix to a lower-dimensional representation, revealing latent semantic structure in the text data.

--- Lesson 4.8: Applications in Economics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Economics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If the external demand vector changes from d to d', and the consumption matrix stays the same, what is the new production vector?
The Leontief inverse (I - C)^(-1) only needs to be computed once; any new demand d' is simply multiplied by it to get the new production vector.
For the Leontief model with C = [[0.1, 0.3], [0.2, 0.1]], what is the matrix (I - C)?
I - C subtracts C from the identity: [[1-0.1, 0-0.3], [0-0.2, 1-0.1]] = [[0.9, -0.3], [-0.2, 0.9]].
What is an input-output model in economics?
An input-output model describes inter-industry flows of goods and services using a matrix, showing how each sector's production feeds into others and final demand.
What is the consumption matrix in the Leontief model?
The consumption matrix C has entries showing inter-industry requirements: c_ij is the units of good i consumed per unit of good j produced.
What is an equilibrium price vector?
The equilibrium price vector satisfies all supply-demand equations simultaneously, meaning no market has excess supply or demand.
What is the Leontief inverse?
The Leontief inverse (I - C)^(-1) converts external demand into total production, accounting for all rounds of inter-industry consumption.
What is a closed Leontief model?
In a closed model, there is no external demand (d = 0), so (I - C)x = 0, and solutions represent the relative proportions of production.
What is a productive economy in the context of the Leontief model?
A productive economy has a consumption matrix where (I - C)^(-1) exists and is non-negative, ensuring that positive demand yields positive production.
What is an exchange model in economics?
An exchange model uses a stochastic matrix to describe how each participant distributes income among others, and equilibrium prices balance all exchanges.
What is a multiplier effect in input-output analysis?
The multiplier effect arises because increased demand in one sector triggers additional production in supplying sectors, amplifying the original demand change.
What is the output multiplier for a given sector?
The output multiplier for sector j is the sum of column j of (I - C)^(-1), showing total economy-wide production generated per unit of final demand in sector j.
What is the Hawkins-Simon condition?
The Hawkins-Simon condition states that all leading principal minors of (I - C) must be positive, ensuring the economy can meet any non-negative demand.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is a multiplier effect in input-output analysis?
The multiplier effect arises because increased demand in one sector triggers additional production in supplying sectors, amplifying the original demand change.
Example: What is the output multiplier for a given sector?
The output multiplier for sector j is the sum of column j of (I - C)^(-1), showing total economy-wide production generated per unit of final demand in sector j.
Example: An economic consulting firm compares two industries' multiplier effects. Industry A has an output multiplier of 2.1 and Industry B has 1.4. A government wants to maximize economic stimulus. Which industry should receive the investment?
A higher output multiplier means more total economic activity per dollar of final demand, making Industry A the better target for stimulus investment.
Example: A regional planning agency uses a five-sector Leontief model. After computing (I - C)^(-1), the agency wants to know the total economic impact of building a new hospital (which increases health sector demand by $50 million). How is this computed?
The impact is found by multiplying the Leontief inverse by the incremental demand vector, which has $50M in the health sector position and zeros elsewhere.
Example: An economics student examines a consumption matrix C and finds that one column sums to 1.1. She concludes the economy is not productive. Why is she correct?
A column sum greater than 1 means that sector requires more than one unit of total input to produce one unit of output, making the economy unsustainable.
