--- Lesson 9.1: Complex Vector Spaces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Complex Vector Spaces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is (2 + 3i)(1 - i)?
(2+3i)(1-i) = 2 - 2i + 3i - 3i^2 = 2 + i + 3 = 5 + i.
What is the modulus (absolute value) of z = 3 + 4i?
|z| = sqrt(3^2 + 4^2) = sqrt(9 + 16) = sqrt(25) = 5.
In C^2, the standard inner product of u = (1, i) and v = (i, 1) is  = conj(u1)*v1 + conj(u2)*v2. What is ?
= conj(1)*i + conj(i)*1 = 1*i + (-i)*1 = i - i = 0. The vectors u and v are orthogonal in C^2.
The dimension of C^n as a complex vector space is n. What is its dimension as a real vector space?
Each complex dimension has two real degrees of freedom (real and imaginary parts). So C^n viewed as a real vector space has dimension 2n.
What is a 'complex vector space'?
A complex vector space is a vector space over the field C of complex numbers. The key distinction from a real vector space is that scalar multiplication is by complex numbers, not just real numbers.
What is a 'Hermitian matrix'?
A Hermitian matrix satisfies A = A* (its conjugate transpose). It is the complex generalization of a real symmetric matrix and always has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.
What is a 'skew-Hermitian' matrix?
A skew-Hermitian matrix satisfies A* = -A. Its eigenvalues are purely imaginary (or zero). It is the complex analog of a real skew-symmetric matrix.
What is 'Euler's formula' and how does it relate to complex vector spaces?
Euler's formula e^{i*theta} = cos(theta) + i*sin(theta) shows that complex exponentials represent rotations on the unit circle. It is fundamental for understanding eigenvalues of rotation matrices and oscillatory systems.
What is the 'spectral theorem for normal matrices'?
A normal matrix commutes with its conjugate transpose. The spectral theorem states that every normal matrix can be diagonalized by a unitary matrix: A = UDU*, where U is unitary and D is diagonal.
What is 'Schur's decomposition theorem'?
Schur's theorem guarantees that any square complex matrix can be unitarily triangularized: A = UTU* with U unitary and T upper triangular. The diagonal of T contains the eigenvalues of A.
What is a 'normal matrix'?
A normal matrix satisfies AA* = A*A. This class includes Hermitian (A* = A), skew-Hermitian (A* = -A), unitary (A*A = I), and diagonal matrices. All normal matrices are unitarily diagonalizable.
What is the 'polar form' of a complex number?
The polar form expresses a complex number using its modulus r = |z| and argument theta: z = r*e^{i*theta}. This representation makes multiplication easy (multiply moduli, add arguments) and reveals rotational structure.
An electrical engineer models an AC circuit with impedance matrix Z with complex entries. The impedance of a component is z = 3 + 4i ohms. What is the magnitude of this impedance?
|z| = sqrt(3^2 + 4^2) = sqrt(25) = 5 ohms. The modulus of the complex impedance gives the total impedance magnitude affecting current flow.
A quantum physicist works with the Hermitian matrix A = [[2, 1-i], [1+i, 3]]. She verifies it is Hermitian by checking A = A*. What is the (2,1) entry of A*?
A* is the conjugate transpose. The (2,1) entry of A* = conj(A(1,2)) = conj(1-i) = 1+i. Since this equals A(2,1) = 1+i, the matrix is indeed Hermitian.
A vibration engineer finds that a system's complex eigenvalues are -0.5 + 3i and -0.5 - 3i. The oscillation frequency is determined by the imaginary part. What is the frequency in radians per second?
For complex eigenvalues a +/- bi, the oscillation frequency is |b| = 3 rad/s, and the decay rate is |a| = 0.5 per second. The system oscillates at 3 rad/s while exponentially decaying.
A data scientist encounters a real 3x3 matrix with eigenvalues 2, 1+i, and 1-i. She verifies the characteristic polynomial has degree 3 with real coefficients. What is the product of all eigenvalues (which equals the determinant)?
det(A) = product of eigenvalues = 2 * (1+i) * (1-i) = 2 * (1 + 1) = 2 * 2 = 4. The conjugate pair product (1+i)(1-i) = 1 - i^2 = 2 is real, so the determinant is real as expected.
A telecommunications engineer uses the DFT (Discrete Fourier Transform) matrix F_4, a 4x4 complex matrix. The entries involve omega = e^{2*pi*i/4} = i. What is omega^2?
omega = e^{2*pi*i/4} = e^{i*pi/2} = i. So omega^2 = i^2 = -1.

The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra states that every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one complex root, implying it factors completely into n linear factors over C.

--- Lesson 9.2: Jordan Canonical Form (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Jordan Canonical Form
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Jordan canonical form used for?
The Jordan canonical form is the 'best possible' near-diagonal form for matrices that are not diagonalizable. It uses Jordan blocks with eigenvalues on the diagonal and 1s on the superdiagonal.
What is the Jordan form of the matrix A = [[5, 1], [0, 5]]?
A is already in Jordan canonical form -- it is a single 2x2 Jordan block with eigenvalue 5. The eigenvalue 5 has algebraic multiplicity 2 but geometric multiplicity 1.
For the Jordan block J = [[3, 1], [0, 3]], what is J^2?
J^2 = [[3,1],[0,3]]^2 = [[9, 3+3],[0, 9]] = [[9, 6],[0, 9]]. For a 2x2 Jordan block, J^n = [[lambda^n, n*lambda^{n-1}], [0, lambda^n]].
What is a 'Jordan block'?
A Jordan block J_k(lambda) is a k x k matrix with eigenvalue lambda on every diagonal entry and 1 on each superdiagonal entry. All other entries are 0. It is the building block of the Jordan canonical form.
What is a 'generalized eigenvector'?
A generalized eigenvector of rank k satisfies (A - lambda*I)^k v = 0 but (A - lambda*I)^{k-1} v != 0. Ordinary eigenvectors are generalized eigenvectors of rank 1. These form the columns of the change-of-basis matrix P.
What is a 'defective matrix'?
A defective matrix does not have a full set of linearly independent eigenvectors (geometric multiplicity < algebraic multiplicity for some eigenvalue). Its Jordan form contains at least one block of size >= 2.
What is the 'nilpotent part' of a Jordan block?
Any Jordan block J = lambda*I + N decomposes into a scalar part lambda*I and a nilpotent part N (with 1s on the superdiagonal). N^k = 0 where k is the block size, making it nilpotent.
What is the 'Jordan chain' for a generalized eigenvector?
A Jordan chain is a sequence where v_1 is an eigenvector and each subsequent v_j is a generalized eigenvector satisfying (A - lambda*I)v_j = v_{j-1}. The chain length equals the Jordan block size.
What is the 'minimal polynomial' of a matrix?
The minimal polynomial m(x) is the monic polynomial of lowest degree satisfying m(A) = 0. Its roots are exactly the distinct eigenvalues of A, and the power of each root equals the size of the largest Jordan block for that eigenvalue.
What is the 'index of nilpotency' of a nilpotent matrix N?
The index of nilpotency k is the smallest power for which N^k = 0. For the nilpotent part of a Jordan block of size m, the index is m. For a general nilpotent matrix, it equals the size of the largest Jordan block.
A physicist computes A^{100} for a 2x2 matrix with Jordan form J = [[2, 1], [0, 2]]. Using J^n = [[2^n, n*2^{n-1}], [0, 2^n]], what is the (1,2) entry of J^{100}?
For a 2x2 Jordan block with eigenvalue lambda, J^n has (1,2) entry = n * lambda^{n-1}. With lambda = 2 and n = 100: 100 * 2^{99}.
A differential equations student needs to compute e^{Jt} for the Jordan block J = [[3, 1], [0, 3]]. Using the formula for a 2x2 Jordan block, what is e^{Jt}?
For J = lambda*I + N, e^{Jt} = e^{lambda*t}(I + tN). With lambda = 3: e^{Jt} = e^{3t} * [[1, t], [0, 1]] = [[e^{3t}, t*e^{3t}], [0, e^{3t}]]. The t factor in the (1,2) entry comes from the non-diagonalizable part.
A numerical analyst encounters a matrix where the eigenvalue 5 has algebraic multiplicity 4 and she finds dim(ker(A-5I)) = 2, dim(ker(A-5I)^2) = 3, dim(ker(A-5I)^3) = 4. What is the Jordan structure for eigenvalue 5?
The jumps in kernel dimensions are: 2, 1, 1 (from k=1,2,3). The number of blocks of size >= j equals dim(ker(A-5I)^j) - dim(ker(A-5I)^{j-1}). Blocks >= 1: 2, blocks >= 2: 1, blocks >= 3: 1. So one block of size 3 and one of size 1.
A robotics engineer models a system with matrix A = [[0, 1, 0], [0, 0, 1], [0, 0, 0]]. This is a single Jordan block with eigenvalue 0 and size 3. What is A^3?
A is the nilpotent part of a 3x3 Jordan block with eigenvalue 0. A^2 = [[0,0,1],[0,0,0],[0,0,0]], and A^3 = 0 (the zero matrix). The nilpotency index equals the block size.
A mathematician studying matrix functions needs to compute f(A) where A has Jordan form J. If f(x) = x^2 + 1 and J = [[2, 1], [0, 2]], what is f(J)?
f(J) = J^2 + I. J^2 = [[4, 4], [0, 4]]. So f(J) = [[4,4],[0,4]] + [[1,0],[0,1]] = [[5, 4], [0, 5]]. Alternatively, for a 2x2 Jordan block: f(J) = [[f(lambda), f'(lambda)], [0, f(lambda)]] = [[5, 4], [0, 5]] since f'(2) = 2*2 = 4.
A stability analyst examines a 4x4 system where A has Jordan form diag(J_2(1), J_2(-1)) -- two 2x2 Jordan blocks with eigenvalues 1 and -1. For large n, what is the behavior of A^n?
For eigenvalue 1: J_2(1)^n = [[1, n],[0, 1]], which grows linearly in n. For eigenvalue -1: J_2(-1)^n = [[(-1)^n, n(-1)^{n-1}],[0, (-1)^n]], which oscillates with linearly growing amplitude. Overall, A^n grows like O(n).
Real-World Applications
Example: A 3x3 matrix has eigenvalue 2 with algebraic multiplicity 3 and geometric multiplicity 1. How many Jordan blocks does it have for eigenvalue 2?
The number of Jordan blocks for an eigenvalue equals its geometric multiplicity (dimension of eigenspace). With geometric multiplicity 1, there is one Jordan block. Since the algebraic multiplicity is 3, it must be a single 3x3 block.
Example: A 4x4 matrix has eigenvalue 1 with algebraic multiplicity 4 and geometric multiplicity 2. Which Jordan form structure is possible?
Geometric multiplicity 2 means 2 Jordan blocks. These must have sizes summing to 4 (the algebraic multiplicity). Options: 3+1 or 2+2. Both are valid Jordan structures; the answer 'Two 2x2 Jordan blocks' is one possibility. Note: 3+1 is also possible.
Example: What does 'algebraic multiplicity' of an eigenvalue mean?
The algebraic multiplicity is the multiplicity of lambda as a root of det(A - lambda*I) = 0. It determines the total size (sum of dimensions) of all Jordan blocks associated with that eigenvalue.
Example: What does 'geometric multiplicity' of an eigenvalue mean?
The geometric multiplicity is dim(ker(A - lambda*I)), the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for eigenvalue lambda. It equals the number of Jordan blocks for that eigenvalue and satisfies 1 <= geo. mult. <= alg. mult.
Example: A control engineer analyzes a system with state matrix A that has eigenvalue 0 with algebraic multiplicity 3 and geometric multiplicity 2. She needs to determine the Jordan form. Which structure does A have?
With geometric multiplicity 2, there are 2 Jordan blocks. Their sizes must sum to 3 (algebraic multiplicity). The only partition of 3 into 2 parts is 2 + 1, so there is one 2x2 and one 1x1 Jordan block.
Example: A numerical analyst encounters a matrix where the eigenvalue 5 has algebraic multiplicity 4 and she finds dim(ker(A-5I)) = 2, dim(ker(A-5I)^2) = 3, dim(ker(A-5I)^3) = 4. What is the Jordan structure for eigenvalue 5?
The jumps in kernel dimensions are: 2, 1, 1 (from k=1,2,3). The number of blocks of size >= j equals dim(ker(A-5I)^j) - dim(ker(A-5I)^{j-1}). Blocks >= 1: 2, blocks >= 2: 1, blocks >= 3: 1. So one block of size 3 and one of size 1.
Example: A stability analyst examines a 4x4 system where A has Jordan form diag(J_2(1), J_2(-1)) -- two 2x2 Jordan blocks with eigenvalues 1 and -1. For large n, what is the behavior of A^n?
For eigenvalue 1: J_2(1)^n = [[1, n],[0, 1]], which grows linearly in n. For eigenvalue -1: J_2(-1)^n = [[(-1)^n, n(-1)^{n-1}],[0, (-1)^n]], which oscillates with linearly growing amplitude. Overall, A^n grows like O(n).
Example: An applied mathematician solves a system of ODEs dx/dt = Ax where A has Jordan form [[2, 1, 0], [0, 2, 0], [0, 0, 3]]. The general solution for the first two components involves e^{2t} multiplied by:
The 2x2 Jordan block with eigenvalue 2 contributes solutions e^{2t} and t*e^{2t}. So the first component is (c1 + c2*t)e^{2t}. The polynomial factor's degree is one less than the block size.

    [ ] [[lambda, 0], [1, lambda]]
    Explanation: A Jordan block J_k(lambda) is a k x k upper triangular matrix with lambda on the diagonal and 1s on the superdiagonal. For k=2: [[lambda, 1], [0, lambda]].

  Q3. A 3x3 matrix has eigenvalue 2 with algebraic multiplicity 3 and geometric multiplicity 1. How many Jordan blocks does it have for eigenvalue 2?
    [*] 1 (a single 3x3 Jordan block)
    [ ] 0 (no Jordan blocks needed)
    [ ] 3 (three 1x1 blocks)
    [ ] 2 (one 2x2 and one 1x1 block)
    Explanation: The number of Jordan blocks for an eigenvalue equals its geometric multiplicity (dimension of eigenspace). With geometric multiplicity 1, there is one Jordan block. Since the algebraic multiplicity is 3, it must be a single 3x3 block.

  Q4. A matrix is diagonalizable if and only if its Jordan form consists of:
    [ ] At least one 2x2 Jordan block
    [*] All 1x1 Jordan blocks (i.e., a diagonal matrix)
    [ ] A single large Jordan block
    [ ] Only blocks with eigenvalue 0
    Explanation: When all Jordan blocks are 1x1 (no superdiagonal 1s), the Jordan form is a diagonal matrix, meaning the original matrix is diagonalizable.

  Q5. What is the Jordan form of the matrix A = [[5, 1], [0, 5]]?
    [*] [[5, 1], [0, 5]]
    [ ] [[5, 0], [1, 5]]
    [ ] [[1, 5], [5, 0]]
    [ ] [[5, 0], [0, 5]]
    Explanation: A is already in Jordan canonical form -- it is a single 2x2 Jordan block with eigenvalue 5. The eigenvalue 5 has algebraic multiplicity 2 but geometric multiplicity 1.

  Q6. For the Jordan block J = [[3, 1], [0, 3]], what is J^2?
    [*] [[9, 6], [0, 9]]
    [ ] [[9, 1], [0, 9]]
    [ ] [[6, 9], [0, 6]]
    [ ] [[9, 2], [0, 9]]
    Explanation: J^2 = [[3,1],[0,3]]^2 = [[9, 3+3],[0, 9]] = [[9, 6],[0, 9]]. For a 2x2 Jordan block, J^n = [[lambda^n, n*lambda^{n-1}], [0, lambda^n]].

  Q7. A 4x4 matrix has eigenvalue 1 with algebraic multiplicity 4 and geometric multiplicity 2. Which Jordan form structure is possible?
    [ ] One 4x4 Jordan block
    [ ] Four 1x1 Jordan blocks
    [ ] One 3x3 and one 1x1 Jordan block
    [*] Two 2x2 Jordan blocks
    Explanation: Geometric multiplicity 2 means 2 Jordan blocks. These must have sizes summing to 4 (the algebraic multiplicity). Options: 3+1 or 2+2. Both are valid Jordan structures; the answer 'Two 2x2 Jordan blocks' is one possibility. Note: 3+1 is also possible.

  Q8. The nilpotent part N of a Jordan block J = lambda*I + N satisfies N^k = 0 for which smallest k?

    [ ] N is never nilpotent
    [ ] k = the eigenvalue
    [ ] k = 2 always
    Explanation: For a k x k Jordan block, N has 1s on the superdiagonal and 0s elsewhere. N^k = 0 and N^{k-1} != 0. The nilpotency index equals the block size.

  Q9. Every square matrix over C is similar to its Jordan canonical form. This means there exists an invertible P such that:
    [ ] A = J + P
    [ ] A = J^P
    [*] A = PJP^(-1)
    [ ] A = PJ
    Explanation: The Jordan decomposition theorem states that A = PJP^(-1) where J is the Jordan canonical form and P is the matrix of generalized eigenvectors. This is the fundamental similarity transformation.

  Q10. The Jordan form of the 3x3 identity matrix I is:
    [*] The 3x3 identity matrix itself
    [ ] [[1,1,0],[0,1,1],[0,0,1]]
    [ ] The zero matrix
    [ ] A 3x3 Jordan block with eigenvalue 1
    Explanation: The identity matrix is already diagonal (three 1x1 Jordan blocks with eigenvalue 1). It is diagonalizable with eigenvalue 1 having geometric multiplicity 3, so no superdiagonal 1s appear.

  Q11. What is a 'Jordan block'?
    [ ] Any diagonal matrix, particularly regarding every following
    [*] A square matrix with a single eigenvalue on the diagonal and 1s on the superdiagonal
    [ ] A matrix with blocks of zeros when measured under controlled conditions
    [ ] A permutation matrix, particularly regarding eigenvalue as
    Explanation: A Jordan block J_k(lambda) is a k x k matrix with eigenvalue lambda on every diagonal entry and 1 on each superdiagonal entry. All other entries are 0. It is the building block of the Jordan canonical form.

  Q12. What is a 'generalized eigenvector'?

    [ ] An eigenvector with a complex eigenvalue
    [*] A nonzero vector v satisfying (A - lambda*I)^k v = 0 for some positive integer k
    [ ] A vector in the column space of A
    Explanation: A generalized eigenvector of rank k satisfies (A - lambda*I)^k v = 0 but (A - lambda*I)^{k-1} v != 0. Ordinary eigenvectors are generalized eigenvectors of rank 1. These form the columns of the change-of-basis matrix P.

    Explanation: The algebraic multiplicity is the multiplicity of lambda as a root of det(A - lambda*I) = 0. It determines the total size (sum of dimensions) of all Jordan blocks associated with that eigenvalue.

    [ ] The size of the largest Jordan block
    [ ] The number of eigenvectors with that eigenvalue
    Explanation: The geometric multiplicity is dim(ker(A - lambda*I)), the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for eigenvalue lambda. It equals the number of Jordan blocks for that eigenvalue and satisfies 1 <= geo. mult. <= alg. mult.

  Q15. What is a 'defective matrix'?
    [ ] A matrix with negative eigenvalues, particularly regarding some under the given assumptions
    [ ] A matrix with a zero determinant, particularly regarding least when applying fundamental principles
    [*] A matrix where geometric multiplicity < algebraic multiplicity for at least one eigenvalue, making it non-diagonalizable
    [ ] A matrix that produces numerical errors when evaluated systematically
    Explanation: A defective matrix does not have a full set of linearly independent eigenvectors (geometric multiplicity < algebraic multiplicity for some eigenvalue). Its Jordan form contains at least one block of size >= 2.

  Q16. What is the 'nilpotent part' of a Jordan block?
    [ ] A block with all entries equal to 1
    [ ] The diagonal part of the Jordan block
    [*] The matrix N with 1s on the superdiagonal and 0s elsewhere, where J = lambda*I + N
    [ ] The inverse of the Jordan block
    Explanation: Any Jordan block J = lambda*I + N decomposes into a scalar part lambda*I and a nilpotent part N (with 1s on the superdiagonal). N^k = 0 where k is the block size, making it nilpotent.

  Q17. What is the 'Jordan chain' for a generalized eigenvector?
    [ ] A cyclic group generated by Jordan blocks
    [ ] The set of all eigenvalues of A
    [*] A sequence v_1, v_2, ..., v_k where (A - lambda*I)v_j = v_{j-1} and (A - lambda*I)v_1 = 0
    [ ] The sequence of powers A, A^2, A^3, ...
    Explanation: A Jordan chain is a sequence where v_1 is an eigenvector and each subsequent v_j is a generalized eigenvector satisfying (A - lambda*I)v_j = v_{j-1}. The chain length equals the Jordan block size.

  Q18. What is the 'minimal polynomial' of a matrix?
    [*] The monic polynomial of smallest degree such that m(A) = 0
    [ ] The trace of the characteristic polynomial
    [ ] The characteristic polynomial divided by the determinant
    [ ] The polynomial with the smallest eigenvalue as its root
    Explanation: The minimal polynomial m(x) is the monic polynomial of lowest degree satisfying m(A) = 0. Its roots are exactly the distinct eigenvalues of A, and the power of each root equals the size of the largest Jordan block for that eigenvalue.

  Q19. What is the 'index of nilpotency' of a nilpotent matrix N?
    [ ] The rank of N, particularly regarding
    [ ] The trace of N, particularly regarding
    [ ] The determinant of N as determined by
    [*] The smallest positive integer k such that N^k = 0

--- Lesson 9.3: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between the singular values and the 2-norm (operator norm) of a matrix?
The operator 2-norm (spectral norm) of a matrix equals its largest singular value: ||A||_2 = sigma_1 = max(||Ax|| / ||x||). This represents the maximum stretching factor of A.
If a matrix A has singular values 5, 3, and 1, what is the Frobenius norm ||A||_F?
||A||_F = sqrt(sum of sigma_i^2) = sqrt(25 + 9 + 1) = sqrt(35). The Frobenius norm equals the square root of the sum of squared singular values.
What is a 'singular value' of a matrix?
Singular values are the non-negative square roots of the eigenvalues of A^T A. They measure the amount of stretching that A applies along each of its principal directions (right singular vectors).
What is the 'pseudoinverse' (Moore-Penrose inverse) A^+ of a matrix?
The pseudoinverse is computed via SVD: replace each nonzero sigma_i with 1/sigma_i in S, then A^+ = V S^+ U^T. It gives the minimum-norm least-squares solution for any linear system.
What is the 'truncated SVD'?
The truncated (or thin) SVD keeps only the k largest singular values and their corresponding left and right singular vectors: A_k = U_k S_k V_k^T. This is the optimal rank-k approximation.
What is the 'compact SVD' (also called thin SVD)?
The compact SVD uses only the r columns of U and V corresponding to nonzero singular values. For an m x n matrix of rank r, U_r is m x r, S_r is r x r, and V_r is n x r.
What is the 'nuclear norm' (trace norm) of a matrix?
The nuclear norm is the sum of singular values. It is the tightest convex relaxation of the rank function and is used in matrix completion and low-rank optimization problems.
What is the geometric interpretation of the SVD?
The SVD decomposes any linear transformation into three steps: rotate/reflect input (V^T), scale along the coordinate axes (S), then rotate/reflect output (U). A unit sphere maps to an ellipsoid whose semi-axes are the singular values.
What is 'randomized SVD'?
Randomized SVD uses random projections to find an approximate low-rank basis, then computes the SVD on the smaller projected matrix. It is much faster than full SVD for large matrices where only the top k singular values are needed.
A numerical analyst finds that a 4x4 matrix A has singular values 100, 50, 0.01, and 0.005. What is the condition number, and what does it imply?
kappa = sigma_max / sigma_min = 100 / 0.005 = 20,000. This large condition number means the system is ill-conditioned -- small perturbations in the data can cause large changes in the solution.
Real-World Applications
Example: A data scientist compresses a 1000x800 image matrix using rank-20 SVD approximation. How many values must be stored for the compressed representation?
Store 20 left singular vectors (1000 entries each = 20,000) + 20 right singular vectors (800 entries each = 16,000) + 20 singular values = 36,020. Compare to 800,000 for the full matrix -- about 22:1 compression.
Example: A computer vision researcher applies SVD to a 100x100 grayscale image. The image has singular values sigma_1 = 200, sigma_2 = 50, sigma_3 = 10, and all others less than 1. Using rank-3 approximation, what fraction of the Frobenius norm is captured?
||A_3||_F^2 / ||A||_F^2 = (200^2 + 50^2 + 10^2) / (200^2 + 50^2 + 10^2 + remaining) = 42,600 / (42,600 + small) ~ 99.99%. The top 3 singular values dominate the image information.
Example: A natural language processing engineer computes SVD of a word-context matrix to create word embeddings. She keeps the top 300 dimensions. The resulting word vectors capture semantic similarity because:
SVD extracts latent semantic dimensions from word co-occurrence patterns. Words appearing in similar contexts project similarly onto these dimensions, creating embeddings where cosine similarity correlates with semantic relatedness.

--- Lesson 9.4: Applications in Machine Learning (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Machine Learning
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In batch processing of neural networks, inputs are stacked as rows of a matrix X (batch x features). The forward pass computes Y = XW^T + b. If X is 32x784 and W is 256x784, what is the dimension of Y?
X is 32x784 and W^T is 784x256, so XW^T is 32x256. Each of the 32 samples gets mapped from 784 features to 256 hidden units.
What is 'gradient descent' from a linear algebra perspective?
Gradient descent uses the gradient vector (direction of steepest ascent) to iteratively update parameters: w_{t+1} = w_t - alpha * grad(L). The gradient is a vector in parameter space computed using matrix calculus.
What is the 'vanishing gradient problem' in terms of matrix multiplication?
In deep networks, the gradient flows backward through products of weight matrices. If the singular values of these matrices are < 1, the gradient magnitude shrinks exponentially with depth, preventing early layers from learning.
What is the 'kernel trick' in machine learning?
The kernel trick replaces explicit computation of phi(x)^T phi(y) in a high-dimensional feature space with a kernel function K(x,y) that gives the same result. This enables nonlinear methods using only inner products.
What is 'batch normalization' from a linear algebra standpoint?
Batch normalization subtracts the mean and divides by the standard deviation of each feature across the mini-batch. This is a per-feature affine transformation that stabilizes the distribution of layer inputs during training.
What is 'transfer learning' in terms of the weight matrices of a neural network?
Transfer learning uses weight matrices learned on a large dataset (like ImageNet) as starting parameters for a new task. The learned linear transformations capture useful features that transfer across related tasks.
What is 'spectral graph theory' and its role in machine learning?
Spectral graph theory analyzes graph properties through the eigenvalues/eigenvectors of associated matrices. In ML, it enables spectral clustering, graph partitioning, and graph neural network design.
What is 'Fisher's Linear Discriminant' (LDA)?
LDA finds the projection direction w that maximizes the ratio of between-class scatter to within-class scatter: max (w^T S_B w) / (w^T S_W w). This is a generalized eigenvalue problem: S_B w = lambda S_W w.
What is 'tensor decomposition' in machine learning?
Tensor decomposition (CP, Tucker, etc.) extends matrix factorization to 3D+ arrays. It is used for multi-way data analysis, knowledge graph completion, and compressing neural network weight tensors.
A computer vision engineer applies SVD-based compression to a convolutional layer with weight tensor of shape (256, 128, 3, 3). She reshapes it to a 256 x 1152 matrix and applies rank-32 SVD. What is the compression ratio?
Original: 256 * 1152 = 294,912 params. Rank-32 SVD stores U (256 x 32) + S (32) + V^T (32 x 1152) = 8,192 + 32 + 36,864 = 45,088. Ratio: 294,912 / 45,088 ~ 6.5:1.
A natural language processing researcher measures word similarity using cosine similarity of embedding vectors. If word A has embedding (1, 2, 2) and word B has embedding (2, 1, 2), what is their cosine similarity?
cos(theta) = (A . B) / (||A|| ||B||) = (2 + 2 + 4) / (sqrt(9) * sqrt(9)) = 8 / 9. The words are quite similar in the embedding space.
A graph neural network uses the adjacency matrix A of a social network with 1000 nodes. The message passing layer computes H' = sigma(AHW). If H is 1000x64 and W is 64x32, what is the dimension of H'?
A (1000x1000) * H (1000x64) = 1000x64, then * W (64x32) = 1000x32. After activation sigma, H' is 1000x32. Each node's 64-dim feature is aggregated with neighbors' features and projected to 32 dimensions.
A reinforcement learning researcher uses a neural network to approximate a Q-function. The weight update involves the outer product gradient dL/dW = delta * x^T where delta is the error vector and x is the input. What is the rank of this gradient matrix?
The outer product delta * x^T of two vectors always produces a rank-1 matrix (every row is a scalar multiple of x^T). In stochastic gradient descent, each sample contributes a rank-1 update to the weights.
A machine learning engineer implements self-attention in a Transformer. The attention output for a single head is Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QK^T / sqrt(d_k)) V. If Q, K, V are all 10x64, what is the dimension of QK^T?
Q (10x64) * K^T (64x10) = 10x10 attention score matrix. Each entry (i,j) represents how much token i attends to token j, computed as the dot product of their query and key vectors.

--- Lesson 9.5: Applications in Quantum Mechanics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Quantum Mechanics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
The density matrix rho for a pure state |psi> is rho = |psi> = (1/sqrt(2))(|0> + |1>), what is the (1,1) entry of rho?
|psi> = (1/sqrt(2), 1/sqrt(2))^T. rho = |psi>.
What is a 'quantum state' in the language of linear algebra?
A pure quantum state is a unit vector |psi> in a complex Hilbert space H with ||psi|| = 1. The Hilbert space structure (inner product, completeness) provides the mathematical framework for quantum probability.
What is the 'Hamiltonian' in quantum mechanics?
The Hamiltonian H is a Hermitian matrix (or operator) whose eigenvalues are the system's energy levels and whose eigenvectors are the energy eigenstates. It drives time evolution via U(t) = e^{-iHt/hbar}.
What is a 'tensor product' in quantum mechanics?
The tensor product H_A (x) H_B creates the composite Hilbert space for two quantum systems. If system A is in C^m and B is in C^n, the joint system lives in C^{mn}. This exponential growth enables quantum computational advantage.
What is 'quantum entanglement' mathematically?
An entangled state |psi> in H_A (x) H_B cannot be factored as |a> (x) |b>. Entanglement is detected by the Schmidt rank: if the state has more than one term in its Schmidt decomposition, it is entangled.
What is the 'Schmidt decomposition'?
The Schmidt decomposition is the SVD applied to the coefficient matrix of a bipartite quantum state. The Schmidt coefficients lambda_i quantify entanglement: a state is entangled iff it has more than one nonzero Schmidt coefficient.
What is 'quantum decoherence' in terms of density matrices?
Decoherence causes the off-diagonal elements (coherences) of the density matrix to decay, transforming a pure state rho = |psi>
What is the 'trace distance' between two quantum states?
The trace distance D = (1/2)||rho - sigma||_1 measures the maximum probability of distinguishing two quantum states. It uses the trace norm (sum of singular values) and ranges from 0 (identical) to 1 (orthogonal).
What is a 'quantum channel' mathematically?
A quantum channel E is a linear map on density matrices that is completely positive (preserves positivity even on extended systems) and trace-preserving (trace(E(rho)) = 1). It describes the most general physical evolution of a quantum state.
What is the 'fidelity' between two quantum states?
Fidelity measures how close two quantum states are. For pure states, F = ||^2. For mixed states, the general formula involves matrix square roots. F ranges from 0 (orthogonal) to 1 (identical).
A quantum computing researcher prepares a 2-qubit state |psi> = (1/sqrt(3))|00> + (1/sqrt(3))|01> + (1/sqrt(3))|10>. What is the reduced density matrix of the first qubit obtained by tracing out the second qubit?
rho_1 = trace_2(|psi> = |0>(1/sqrt(3)|0>+1/sqrt(3)|1>) + |1>(1/sqrt(3)|0>): rho_1,00 = 1/3+1/3=2/3, rho_1,11 = 1/3, rho_1,01 = 1/3*1 = 1/3, rho_1,10 = 1/3. So rho_1 = [[2/3, 1/3],[1/3, 1/3]].
A quantum simulation researcher models a spin chain with nearest-neighbor interactions. For 4 spins, the Hilbert space has dimension 2^4 = 16. The Hamiltonian H = J * sum(Z_i Z_{i+1}) is a 16x16 matrix. What is a key property that makes this Hamiltonian tractable?
Each Z_i Z_{i+1} term is diagonal in the computational basis, making H sparse (actually diagonal in this case). Even for non-diagonal interaction terms, the tensor product structure makes H very sparse, with only O(n*2^n) nonzero entries instead of 2^{2n}.
A quantum cryptography researcher studies the BB84 protocol. Alice sends qubits in either the Z-basis ({|0>, |1>}) or X-basis ({|+>, |->}). What is the inner product <0|+>?
|+> = (|0> + |1>)/sqrt(2). So <0|+> = <0|(|0> + |1>)/sqrt(2) = (1 + 0)/sqrt(2) = 1/sqrt(2). This means measuring a |+> state in the Z-basis gives |0> with probability |1/sqrt(2)|^2 = 1/2.
A quantum hardware engineer designs a controlled-Z gate (CZ), which applies a phase of -1 to |11> and leaves other states unchanged. What is the CZ matrix?
CZ|00> = |00>, CZ|01> = |01>, CZ|10> = |10>, CZ|11> = -|11>. In matrix form, CZ = diag(1, 1, 1, -1). It is a diagonal unitary gate that applies a pi phase shift only to the |11> state.

A pure quantum state is a unit vector |psi> in a complex Hilbert space H with ||psi|| = 1. The Hilbert space structure (inner product, completeness) provides the mathematical framework for quantum probability.

--- Lesson 9.6: Applications in Image Processing (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Image Processing
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'image kernel' (filter)?
An image kernel is a small matrix (typically 3x3 or 5x5) whose entries are weights. Different kernels achieve different effects: blurring, sharpening, edge detection, embossing, etc.
What is 'image sharpening' in terms of matrix operations?
Unsharp masking adds back the high-frequency content: A_sharp = (1+alpha)*A - alpha*A_blur. The Laplacian kernel [[0,-1,0],[-1,4,-1],[0,-1,0]] directly detects and enhances edges through convolution.
What is 'image segmentation' using spectral methods?
Spectral segmentation builds a graph where pixels are nodes and edge weights represent similarity. The eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian (especially the Fiedler vector) reveal natural partitions of the image.
What is 'image registration' from a linear algebra perspective?
Image registration finds a transformation matrix T that maps points in one image to corresponding points in another. The 2D affine transformation is a 2x3 matrix, and finding it involves solving a least-squares system.
What is 'homography' in computer vision?
A homography H is a 3x3 matrix (up to scale) that maps points between two views of a planar surface using homogeneous coordinates: x' ~ Hx. It encompasses rotations, translations, scaling, and perspective distortions.
What is 'total variation' denoising?
Total variation (TV) denoising minimizes ||grad(u)||_1 + lambda||u - f||^2 where f is the noisy image. The L1 gradient penalty preserves edges while removing noise, making it superior to simple smoothing.
What is 'morphological image processing' and how does it relate to matrix operations?
Morphological operations use min (erosion) and max (dilation) instead of weighted sums. While not strictly linear, they can be expressed using matrix-like operations with a structuring element acting like a kernel.
A graphics designer applies a color transformation matrix to convert an image from one color space to another. The transformation from RGB to YCbCr uses a 3x3 matrix. If the original pixel is (R, G, B) = (255, 0, 0) (pure red), the Y (luminance) component is approximately 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B. What is Y?
Y = 0.299(255) + 0.587(0) + 0.114(0) = 76.245 ~ 76.2. The luminance weights reflect human visual sensitivity (most sensitive to green, least to blue).
Real-World Applications
Example: A grayscale image of size m x n is naturally represented as:
A grayscale image is an m x n matrix where each entry represents the pixel intensity (typically 0-255 for 8-bit images). This matrix representation enables linear algebra operations for image processing.
Example: Image compression using SVD approximates an m x n image matrix A as A_k = U_k S_k V_k^T. If m = n = 500 and k = 20, how many values need to be stored?
Store U_k (500x20 = 10,000) + S_k (20 values) + V_k (500x20 = 10,000) = 20,020 values instead of 250,000. This gives about 12.5:1 compression.
Example: In image filtering, convolution with a 3x3 kernel can be expressed as multiplication by which type of matrix?
2D image convolution can be expressed as multiplication by a block-Toeplitz matrix with Toeplitz blocks. The kernel values appear shifted along diagonals, reflecting the translation-invariant nature of convolution.
Example: The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) of an image converts it from the spatial domain to the:
The 2D DFT decomposes an image into a sum of sinusoidal patterns of different frequencies, orientations, and phases. This frequency-domain representation is useful for filtering, compression, and analysis.
Example: In JPEG compression, images are divided into 8x8 blocks and transformed using the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT). The DCT matrix C for an 8x8 block is what type of matrix?
The DCT matrix C is orthogonal (C^T C = I), meaning the transform is invertible and norm-preserving. The DCT is applied as CXC^T for each 8x8 block X, transforming to a frequency representation.
Example: A color image (RGB) with resolution m x n is represented as:
An RGB color image is represented as three separate m x n matrices for the Red, Green, and Blue channels. SVD or other matrix operations can be applied to each channel independently or jointly.
Example: In image denoising using SVD, noisy pixel values primarily affect which singular values?

--- Lesson 9.7: Applications in Signal Processing (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Signal Processing
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Parseval's theorem states that the DFT preserves signal energy. If x = (1, 2, 3, 4), what is ||x||^2 = sum |x[n]|^2?
||x||^2 = 1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + 4^2 = 1 + 4 + 9 + 16 = 30. By Parseval's theorem, (1/N) * sum |X[k]|^2 = 30 as well, where X = DFT(x).
What is a 'frequency response' of a linear system?
The frequency response H(omega) is the DFT (or Fourier transform) of the impulse response h[n]. It shows how the system amplifies or attenuates each frequency component and what phase shift it introduces.
What is a 'filter bank' in signal processing?
A filter bank splits a signal into subbands. The analysis filters can be arranged as rows of a matrix, making the decomposition a matrix-vector product. Perfect reconstruction filter banks have an invertible analysis matrix.
What is 'aliasing' in terms of the DFT matrix?
Aliasing occurs when the sampling rate is below 2 times the highest frequency (Nyquist rate). Higher frequencies 'fold' into lower ones because different continuous frequencies map to the same discrete-time sequence.
What is 'matched filtering' in signal detection?
A matched filter maximizes SNR by correlating the received signal with a copy of the known signal template. This is equivalent to computing the inner product  where h is the time-reversed conjugate of the expected signal.
What is 'MIMO' (Multiple-Input Multiple-Output) in terms of linear algebra?
In MIMO, the channel matrix H maps the transmitted vector x to received vector y = Hx + n. The SVD of H reveals the channel's independent communication modes, and its singular values determine the capacity of each mode.
What is 'spectral analysis' of a signal?
Spectral analysis identifies which frequencies are present in a signal and their relative strengths. Methods include the DFT (periodogram) and eigenvalue-based methods like MUSIC and ESPRIT that use the signal's correlation matrix.
What is the 'MUSIC algorithm' (Multiple Signal Classification)?
MUSIC decomposes the correlation matrix R = E_s*Lambda*E_s^H + sigma^2*I into signal and noise eigenspaces. The signal frequencies are found where the steering vector is orthogonal to the noise subspace.
What is 'wavelet transform' in terms of linear algebra?
The discrete wavelet transform (DWT) represents a signal using shifted and scaled wavelets. It can be expressed as an orthogonal matrix multiplication, providing a multi-resolution analysis that captures both frequency and time information.
What is 'adaptive filtering' in signal processing?
Adaptive filters update their weight vector w in real-time: w[n+1] = w[n] + mu * e[n] * x[n] (LMS algorithm). This is a stochastic gradient descent on the MSE surface, where the autocorrelation matrix R determines convergence.
What is 'Independent Component Analysis' (ICA)?
ICA solves the 'cocktail party problem': given mixed signals x = As, find the unmixing matrix W = A^{-1} such that s = Wx gives the original independent sources. Unlike PCA (which decorrelates), ICA makes components statistically independent.
A telecommunications engineer designs a MIMO wireless system with 4 transmit and 4 receive antennas. The channel matrix H is 4x4 with singular values 3, 2, 1, 0.1. Using water-filling power allocation, she allocates more power to channels with larger singular values. What is the maximum theoretical data rate (proportional to)?
MIMO capacity is C = sum log2(1 + P_i * sigma_i^2 / N_0), where each singular value creates an independent sub-channel. Water-filling optimally distributes power by allocating more to stronger channels.
A radar engineer uses a matched filter to detect a target. The transmitted pulse is s = (1, 1, -1, -1). The matched filter h is the time-reversed conjugate. What is the peak output when the target is present and the signal is received without noise?
The matched filter output peaks at the signal energy: h^T s = sum s[n]*conj(s[n]) = sum |s[n]|^2 = 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 4. This is the maximum SNR achievable by any linear filter.
A digital audio engineer implements an 8-band equalizer. Each band is a bandpass filter, and the combined system is a parallel filter bank. In matrix form, the analysis is y = [H_1; H_2; ...; H_8] * x where each H_i is a row vector. What is the dimension of the output y if x has length N?
Each of the 8 bandpass filters produces N output samples (same length as input). Stacking all 8 outputs gives a total of 8N values. This is equivalent to multiplication by a block matrix where each block is an N x N convolution matrix.

The FIR filter y[n] = 0.5*x[n] + 0.5*x[n-1] corresponds to a lower-triangular Toeplitz matrix with constant diagonals: H has 0.5 on the main diagonal and 0.5 on the first subdiagonal.

--- Lesson 9.8: Applications in Statistics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Statistics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
The variance of a linear combination a^T X is Var(a^T X) = a^T Sigma a. For Sigma = [[4, 2], [2, 3]] and a = (1, -1), what is Var(a^T X)?
a^T Sigma a = [1,-1] [[4,2],[2,3]] [1,-1]^T = [1,-1] [4-2, 2-3]^T = [1,-1] [2, -1]^T = 2 + 1 = 3.
What is the 'covariance matrix' in statistics?
The covariance matrix Sigma has entry Sigma_ij = Cov(X_i, X_j) = E[(X_i - mu_i)(X_j - mu_j)]. Diagonal entries are variances, off-diagonal entries measure linear co-movement between variable pairs.
What is 'multivariate normal distribution'?
The multivariate normal N(mu, Sigma) is a joint distribution where any linear combination a^T X is univariate normal. It is completely determined by its mean vector mu and covariance matrix Sigma.
What is 'principal component regression' (PCR)?
PCR first applies PCA to X to get orthogonal principal components, then regresses y on the top k components. This eliminates multicollinearity (components are orthogonal) and removes noise in the low-variance directions.
What is the 'precision matrix' (inverse covariance)?
The precision matrix Omega = Sigma^{-1} directly encodes the conditional independence structure. If Omega_ij = 0, then X_i and X_j are conditionally independent given all other variables. This is central to graphical models.
What is 'Hotelling's T-squared test'?
Hotelling's T^2 tests whether a multivariate mean vector equals a hypothesized value. It uses the sample covariance matrix S to account for correlations, and follows an F-distribution under the null hypothesis.
What is 'MANOVA' (Multivariate Analysis of Variance)?
MANOVA generalizes ANOVA to multiple dependent variables. It compares mean vectors using the ratio of between-group to within-group scatter matrices (B and W). Test statistics involve eigenvalues of W^{-1}B.
What is the 'graphical lasso'?
The graphical lasso estimates the precision matrix Omega by maximizing the penalized log-likelihood: max [log det(Omega) - trace(S*Omega) - lambda * ||Omega||_1]. The L1 penalty encourages sparsity, revealing the conditional independence graph.
What is 'discriminant analysis' using linear algebra?
Linear discriminant analysis finds directions w that maximize the ratio w^T S_B w / w^T S_W w, where S_B is the between-class scatter matrix and S_W is the within-class scatter. This is a generalized eigenvalue problem.
A social scientist uses factor analysis on 20 survey questions and finds that 3 factors explain 80% of the variance. The loading matrix Lambda is 20x3. If the factor scores F = (1.5, -0.5, 0.8) and the first row of Lambda is (0.8, 0.1, 0.3), what is the model's prediction for variable 1?
X_1 = Lambda_1 . F = 0.8(1.5) + 0.1(-0.5) + 0.3(0.8) = 1.20 - 0.05 + 0.24 = 1.39. This is the dot product of the first row of the loading matrix with the factor score vector.
A marketing analyst uses canonical correlation analysis between 5 customer demographic variables (X) and 3 purchase behavior variables (Y). What is the maximum number of canonical correlations?
The number of canonical correlations equals min(p, q) = min(5, 3) = 3. Each canonical correlation is the maximum correlation between a linear combination of X and a linear combination of Y, subject to orthogonality constraints.

Real-World Applications
Example: In canonical correlation analysis (CCA), the goal is to find linear combinations of two sets of variables that are maximally correlated. This is solved as a generalized eigenvalue problem involving which matrices?
CCA solves Sigma_xy Sigma_yy^{-1} Sigma_yx a = rho^2 Sigma_xx a, a generalized eigenvalue problem. The canonical correlations are the square roots of these eigenvalues, and the vectors a, b give the optimal linear combinations.
Example: What does 'leverage' mean in regression, in terms of the hat matrix?
The leverage h_ii = e_i^T H e_i measures how much observation i influences its own predicted value. High leverage points (h_ii close to 1) are far from the center of the predictor space and can unduly affect the regression.
Example: What does 'shrinkage estimator' mean for covariance matrices?
When n < p (more variables than samples), the sample covariance is singular. Shrinkage estimators blend the sample covariance S with a well-conditioned target T (like alpha*I): Sigma_hat = alpha*S + (1-alpha)*T.

    Explanation: Hotelling's T^2 tests whether a multivariate mean vector equals a hypothesized value. It uses the sample covariance matrix S to account for correlations, and follows an F-distribution under the null hypothesis.

  Q17. What is 'MANOVA' (Multivariate Analysis of Variance)?
    [ ] A multivariate regression technique
    [*] A statistical test comparing mean vectors of multiple groups using matrices of between-group and within-group sums of squares
    [ ] An analysis of variance performed on each variable separately
    [ ] A method for computing the variance of a matrix
    Explanation: MANOVA generalizes ANOVA to multiple dependent variables. It compares mean vectors using the ratio of between-group to within-group scatter matrices (B and W). Test statistics involve eigenvalues of W^{-1}B.

  Q18. What is the 'graphical lasso'?
    [ ] A technique for drawing graphs of matrix entries
    [ ] A visualization tool for lasso regression
    [*] A method for estimating a sparse precision matrix by adding an L1 penalty on the off-diagonal entries of Omega
    [ ] A method for finding the shortest path in a graph
    Explanation: The graphical lasso estimates the precision matrix Omega by maximizing the penalized log-likelihood: max [log det(Omega) - trace(S*Omega) - lambda * ||Omega||_1]. The L1 penalty encourages sparsity, revealing the conditional independence graph.

  Q19. What does 'shrinkage estimator' mean for covariance matrices?
    [ ] Dividing all covariance entries by the sample size according to conventional analysis
    [ ] An estimator that reduces the size of the matrix, particularly regarding blend when measured under controlled conditions
    [ ] An estimator that removes variables with small variance under normal circumstances
    [*] A regularized estimate that shrinks the sample covariance toward a structured target (like the identity), improving conditioning: Sigma_hat = alpha*S + (1-alpha)*T
    Explanation: When n < p (more variables than samples), the sample covariance is singular. Shrinkage estimators blend the sample covariance S with a well-conditioned target T (like alpha*I): Sigma_hat = alpha*S + (1-alpha)*T.

  Q20. What is 'discriminant analysis' using linear algebra?
    [ ] A method for discriminating between good and bad data when considering all relevant factors
    [*] A classification method that finds linear combinations of variables that best separate classes, using within-class and between-class scatter matrices
    [ ] Analyzing which eigenvalues are positive vs negative in the context of this analysis
    [ ] A technique for removing outliers, particularly regarding using as observed in standard experiments
    Explanation: Linear discriminant analysis finds directions w that maximize the ratio w^T S_B w / w^T S_W w, where S_B is the between-class scatter matrix and S_W is the within-class scatter. This is a generalized eigenvalue problem.

  Q21. A medical researcher has measurements on 5 biomarkers for 200 patients. The sample covariance matrix S is 5x5. She wants to test if the population mean vector equals mu_0 = (10, 20, 30, 40, 50) using Hotelling's T^2. What distribution does the test statistic follow under H_0?
    [ ] A chi-squared distribution with 5 degrees of freedom
    [ ] A uniform distribution
    [*] An F-distribution (after transformation) with p and n-p degrees of freedom (5 and 195)
    [ ] A normal distribution
    Explanation: Under H_0, the transformed statistic [(n-p)/(p(n-1))] * T^2 follows an F(p, n-p) = F(5, 195) distribution. This accounts for the estimation uncertainty in both the mean and covariance.

  Q22. A financial analyst estimates the covariance matrix of 100 stock returns using 60 trading days of data. Why is the sample covariance matrix problematic?
    [*] With n = 60 < p = 100, the sample covariance is singular (rank at most 59) and cannot be inverted
    [ ] Stock returns don't have a covariance following the established framework
    [ ] It will be too small, particularly regarding data as predicted by standard models
    [ ] 60 days is too much data for 100 stocks when evaluated systematically
    Explanation: The sample covariance matrix with n observations and p variables has rank at most min(n-1, p) = 59. Since 59 < 100, the matrix is singular (rank-deficient) and its inverse doesn't exist. Shrinkage or factor models are needed.

  Q23. A social scientist uses factor analysis on 20 survey questions and finds that 3 factors explain 80% of the variance. The loading matrix Lambda is 20x3. If the factor scores F = (1.5, -0.5, 0.8) and the first row of Lambda is (0.8, 0.1, 0.3), what is the model's prediction for variable 1?
    [ ] 1.5 - 0.5 + 0.8 = 1.8
    [ ] 1.5 + 0.8 = 2.3
    [*] 0.8(1.5) + 0.1(-0.5) + 0.3(0.8) = 1.39
    [ ] 0.8 * 0.1 * 0.3 = 0.024
    Explanation: X_1 = Lambda_1 . F = 0.8(1.5) + 0.1(-0.5) + 0.3(0.8) = 1.20 - 0.05 + 0.24 = 1.39. This is the dot product of the first row of the loading matrix with the factor score vector.

  Q24. A data scientist computes the sample correlation matrix of 4 variables and finds eigenvalues 2.5, 1.0, 0.4, 0.1. How many principal components should she retain using the 'eigenvalue > 1' (Kaiser) criterion?
    [*] 2 (eigenvalues 2.5 and 1.0 are >= 1)
    [ ] 3 (the first three eigenvalues)
    [ ] 1 (only eigenvalue 2.5 exceeds 1)
    [ ] 4 (all eigenvalues)
    Explanation: The Kaiser criterion retains components with eigenvalues >= 1 (for a correlation matrix, where the average eigenvalue is 1). Eigenvalues 2.5 and 1.0 meet this criterion, so retain 2 components. They explain (2.5 + 1.0)/4 = 87.5% of variance.

  Q25. A geneticist uses MANOVA to test whether 3 genetic groups differ in their mean vectors across 10 gene expression measurements. The Wilks' Lambda statistic involves the determinant of which matrices?
    [ ] Lambda = det(X^T X), particularly regarding lambda as typically measured in practice
    [ ] Lambda = det(B) / det(W), particularly regarding matrices under normal circumstances
    [ ] Lambda = trace(W) / trace(B) within the typical range of values
    [*] Lambda = det(W) / det(W + B), where W is within-group and B is between-group scatter matrix
    Explanation: Wilks' Lambda = det(W)/det(W+B) measures how much of the total variation is within-group vs between-group. Small Lambda (close to 0) means groups differ significantly. It is related to the eigenvalues of W^{-1}B.

  Q26. A marketing analyst uses canonical correlation analysis between 5 customer demographic variables (X) and 3 purchase behavior variables (Y). What is the maximum number of canonical correlations?
    [ ] 5 (the maximum of the two set sizes)
    [ ] 15 (the product)
    [*] 3 (the minimum of the two set sizes)
    [ ] 8 (the sum)
    Explanation: The number of canonical correlations equals min(p, q) = min(5, 3) = 3. Each canonical correlation is the maximum correlation between a linear combination of X and a linear combination of Y, subject to orthogonality constraints.

  Q27. A bioinformatician analyzes gene expression with p = 20,000 genes and n = 50 samples. She estimates the precision matrix using the graphical lasso. The sparsity pattern of the estimated precision matrix reveals:
    [ ] The marginal correlation between all gene pairs as determined by standard methodology
    [*] The conditional independence structure -- a gene regulatory network where edges represent direct interactions
    [ ] Which samples are outliers, particularly regarding indicate under the given assumptions
    [ ] Which genes are most expressed, particularly regarding with when evaluated systematically
    Explanation: Nonzero entries in the precision matrix indicate conditional dependence (direct interaction). The graphical lasso encourages sparsity, revealing only the strongest direct gene-gene interactions and filtering out indirect correlations.

  Q28. A psychometrician applies confirmatory factor analysis to a 30-item test and hypothesizes 5 factors. She constrains certain entries of the loading matrix Lambda (30x5) to zero based on theory. Why is this different from exploratory factor analysis?
    [ ] They are the same method with different names
    [*] Confirmatory FA tests a specific hypothesized structure with constrained loadings, while exploratory FA estimates all loadings freely
    [ ] Confirmatory FA uses eigenvalues and exploratory FA does not
    [ ] Confirmatory FA only works with 2 factors
    Explanation: In confirmatory FA, the researcher specifies which items load on which factors (setting some Lambda entries to zero). This tests whether the hypothesized structure fits the observed covariance matrix, unlike exploratory FA which freely estimates all loadings.

  Q29. A quality control engineer monitors p = 8 process variables using multivariate control charts. She uses the T^2 statistic based on the Mahalanobis distance. If a new observation has T^2 = 25 and the 99% control limit is 20.1, what action should she take?
    [ ] Recalculate the covariance matrix
    [ ] Increase the number of monitored variables
    [ ] No action needed, the process is in control
    [*] Investigate the process, as T^2 = 25 exceeds the control limit of 20.1, indicating an out-of-control condition
    Explanation: Since T^2 = 25 > 20.1 (the 99% limit), the observation is statistically unusual at the 1% significance level. This signals that the multivariate process has shifted from its in-control state and needs investigation.

  Q30. A climate scientist models 50 years of monthly temperature data at 100 weather stations using a space-time covariance matrix. The matrix is 6000 x 6000 (100 stations x 60 months). What technique can make this tractable?
    [ ] Ignoring all off-diagonal entries, particularly regarding covariance under normal circumstances
    [ ] Using only 2 weather stations, particularly regarding climate following the established framework
    [*] Assuming separable space-time structure: Sigma = Sigma_space (x) Sigma_time, reducing to a 100x100 and 60x60 matrix
    [ ] Replacing the covariance with the identity as predicted by standard models
    Explanation: The separability assumption Sigma = Sigma_space (x) Sigma_time (Kronecker product) reduces estimation from 6000^2/2 ~ 18 million parameters to 100^2/2 + 60^2/2 ~ 6,800 parameters. The Kronecker structure also enables efficient computation.
