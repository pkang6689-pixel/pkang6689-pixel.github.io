--- Lesson 7.1: Characteristic Polynomial (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Characteristic Polynomial
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the characteristic polynomial of a 2x2 matrix A defined as?
The characteristic polynomial of a matrix A is defined as p(lambda) = det(A - lambda I), where I is the identity matrix of the same size.
What is the degree of the characteristic polynomial of a 3x3 matrix?
The characteristic polynomial of an n x n matrix is always a polynomial of degree n. For a 3x3 matrix, the degree is 3.
For a diagonal matrix D = diag(2, 5, -1), what is the characteristic polynomial?
For a diagonal matrix, the characteristic polynomial is the product of (d_i - lambda) for each diagonal entry d_i. So p(lambda) = (2 - lambda)(5 - lambda)(-1 - lambda).
If the characteristic polynomial of a 2x2 matrix is lambda^2 - 5lambda + 6, what is the trace of the matrix?
For a 2x2 matrix, the characteristic polynomial is lambda^2 - (trace)lambda + det. Comparing with lambda^2 - 5lambda + 6, the trace is 5.
What is the characteristic polynomial of the 2x2 identity matrix I?
For I = [[1,0],[0,1]], det(I - lambda I) = (1-lambda)^2 = (lambda-1)^2. The only eigenvalue is 1 with algebraic multiplicity 2.
What is the constant term of the characteristic polynomial of a matrix A?
Evaluating the characteristic polynomial at lambda = 0 gives det(A - 0*I) = det(A). Therefore, the constant term equals the determinant of A.
What is the characteristic polynomial of a matrix?
The characteristic polynomial is p(lambda) = det(A - lambda I). Its roots are precisely the eigenvalues of the matrix A.
What is the trace of a matrix in relation to its characteristic polynomial?
The trace of a matrix equals the sum of its eigenvalues, which are the roots of the characteristic polynomial. For an n x n matrix, the coefficient of lambda^(n-1) equals (-1) times the trace.
What is a 'monic polynomial' in the context of characteristic polynomials?
A monic polynomial has a leading coefficient of 1. Characteristic polynomials are often written in monic form as lambda^n - (trace)lambda^(n-1) + ... + (-1)^n det(A).
What is the minimal polynomial of a matrix?
The minimal polynomial is the monic polynomial of smallest degree such that p(A) = 0. It always divides the characteristic polynomial and shares the same roots.
What is the 'characteristic equation' of a matrix?
The characteristic equation is det(A - lambda I) = 0. Solving this equation yields the eigenvalues of the matrix A.
What is the 'spectrum' of a matrix?
The spectrum of a matrix is the set of all its eigenvalues. It consists of all values of lambda that are roots of the characteristic polynomial.
What is the spectral radius of a matrix?
The spectral radius rho(A) is the maximum absolute value of the eigenvalues: rho(A) = max(|lambda_i|). It plays a key role in convergence analysis of iterative methods.
A structural engineer models a two-story building's vibration modes using the stiffness matrix K = [[5, -2], [-2, 3]]. To find the natural frequencies, she needs the characteristic polynomial of K. What is it?
det(K - lambda I) = (5 - lambda)(3 - lambda) - (-2)(-2) = lambda^2 - 8lambda + 15 - 4 = lambda^2 - 8lambda + 11. The roots give the squares of the natural frequencies.
A population biologist models two competing species with the matrix A = [[1.2, -0.3], [0.1, 0.8]]. To analyze long-term behavior, she computes the characteristic polynomial. What is the sum of the eigenvalues?
The sum of the eigenvalues equals the trace of A. trace(A) = 1.2 + 0.8 = 2.0. This is the coefficient of lambda in the characteristic polynomial (with a sign change).
A data scientist computes the covariance matrix C = [[4, 2], [2, 3]] for a dataset. What is the product of the eigenvalues of C?
The product of the eigenvalues equals the determinant. det(C) = 4(3) - 2(2) = 12 - 4 = 8. This fact comes directly from the constant term of the characteristic polynomial.
A chemical engineer models a two-reactor system with the rate matrix A = [[-3, 1], [2, -4]]. She uses the Cayley-Hamilton theorem to express A^(-1) in terms of A and I. If the characteristic polynomial is lambda^2 + 7lambda + 10, what is A^(-1)?
By Cayley-Hamilton, A^2 + 7A + 10I = 0. Multiplying both sides by A^(-1): A + 7I + 10A^(-1) = 0, so A^(-1) = (-1/10)(A + 7I).
A quantum computing researcher works with the Pauli-Z matrix Z = [[1, 0], [0, -1]]. What is the characteristic polynomial of Z?
det(Z - lambda I) = (1-lambda)(-1-lambda) = -(1-lambda)(1+lambda) = lambda^2 - 1. The eigenvalues are 1 and -1, corresponding to spin-up and spin-down states.
A financial analyst models portfolio risk with the covariance matrix S = [[9, 3, 0], [3, 5, 1], [0, 1, 2]]. What is the sum of all eigenvalues of S?
The sum of eigenvalues equals the trace. trace(S) = 9 + 5 + 2 = 16. This represents the total variance across all three assets in the portfolio.
An electrical engineer designs a filter described by the transfer matrix H = [[2, -1], [1, 4]]. To determine the system's poles, she finds the characteristic polynomial. What is it?
det(H - lambda I) = (2-lambda)(4-lambda) - (-1)(1) = 8 - 6lambda + lambda^2 + 1 = lambda^2 - 6lambda + 9 = (lambda - 3)^2. The repeated eigenvalue 3 indicates a critically damped system.
A wildlife biologist uses a Leslie matrix L = [[0, 4], [0.5, 0]] to model a fish population. What is the dominant eigenvalue (largest in absolute value)?
det(L - lambda I) = (0 - lambda)(0 - lambda) - (4)(0.5) = lambda^2 - 2. So lambda = sqrt(2) and -sqrt(2). The dominant eigenvalue is sqrt(2) approximately 1.414, indicating population growth.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does the term 'algebraic multiplicity' mean for an eigenvalue?
The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue is the number of times it appears as a root of the characteristic polynomial, counting repeated roots.
Example: What does 'geometric multiplicity' of an eigenvalue refer to?
The geometric multiplicity is the dimension of the eigenspace ker(A - lambda I), i.e., the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for that eigenvalue. It is always at most equal to the algebraic multiplicity.

--- Lesson 7.2: Finding Eigenvalues (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Finding Eigenvalues
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A matrix has eigenvalues 2, -3, and 5. What is its determinant?
The determinant equals the product of all eigenvalues: 2 * (-3) * 5 = -30.
If lambda = 4 is an eigenvalue of A, what is an eigenvalue of 3A?
If Av = 4v, then (3A)v = 3(Av) = 3(4v) = 12v. Scaling a matrix by c multiplies all eigenvalues by c.
If lambda = 3 is an eigenvalue of A, what is the corresponding eigenvalue of A^2?
If Av = 3v, then A^2 v = A(Av) = A(3v) = 3(Av) = 9v. The eigenvalues of A^k are lambda^k.
If A is invertible with eigenvalue lambda = 2, what is the corresponding eigenvalue of A^(-1)?
If Av = 2v, then v = A^(-1)(2v) = 2A^(-1)v, so A^(-1)v = (1/2)v. The eigenvalues of A^(-1) are the reciprocals of those of A.
What is an eigenvalue of a matrix?
An eigenvalue lambda is a scalar for which Av = lambda v has a nonzero solution v. This means A acts on v by simply scaling it.
What is meant by a 'defective eigenvalue'?
An eigenvalue is defective when its geometric multiplicity (dimension of eigenspace) is strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity (multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial).
What is a 'dominant eigenvalue' of a matrix?
The dominant eigenvalue is the eigenvalue with the largest absolute value. It determines the long-term behavior of systems like A^k v as k grows large.
What is meant by a 'similar matrix' in the context of eigenvalues?
Matrices A and B are similar if B = P^(-1)AP for some invertible P. Similar matrices share the same characteristic polynomial and therefore the same eigenvalues.
What is meant by the 'power method' for finding eigenvalues?
The power method starts with a random vector and repeatedly multiplies by A, normalizing at each step. The sequence converges to the eigenvector of the dominant eigenvalue.
What is a 'characteristic root' of a matrix?
A characteristic root is simply another term for an eigenvalue. It refers to a root of the characteristic polynomial det(A - lambda I) = 0.
A Markov chain model for customer loyalty has transition matrix T = [[0.9, 0.2], [0.1, 0.8]]. One eigenvalue is always 1 for a stochastic matrix. What is the other eigenvalue?
The trace = 0.9 + 0.8 = 1.7 = sum of eigenvalues. Since one eigenvalue is 1, the other is 1.7 - 1 = 0.7. Since |0.7| < 1, the chain converges to its steady state.
A climate scientist models heat transfer between two ocean layers with A = [[-0.4, 0.2], [0.4, -0.2]]. One eigenvalue is 0. What is the other eigenvalue, and what does it indicate?
The trace = -0.4 + (-0.2) = -0.6. Since the eigenvalues sum to the trace and one is 0, the other is -0.6. The negative eigenvalue means the non-equilibrium component decays exponentially.
A web developer uses the adjacency matrix A = [[0, 1, 1], [1, 0, 1], [1, 1, 0]] for a 3-page website. What is the largest eigenvalue of A?
This is the adjacency matrix of the complete graph K3. The eigenvalues are 2 (once) and -1 (twice). The vector (1,1,1) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue 2, since each row sums to 2.
An economist models a two-sector economy with the input-output matrix A = [[0.3, 0.4], [0.2, 0.5]]. What is the dominant eigenvalue, and does the Hawkins-Simon condition hold (all eigenvalues of A less than 1)?
det(A - lambda I) = (0.3-lambda)(0.5-lambda) - 0.08 = lambda^2 - 0.8lambda + 0.07 = (lambda - 0.7)(lambda - 0.1). The dominant eigenvalue is 0.7 < 1, confirming the Hawkins-Simon condition holds.
Real-World Applications
Example: A web developer uses the adjacency matrix A = [[0, 1, 1], [1, 0, 1], [1, 1, 0]] for a 3-page website. What is the largest eigenvalue of A?
This is the adjacency matrix of the complete graph K3. The eigenvalues are 2 (once) and -1 (twice). The vector (1,1,1) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue 2, since each row sums to 2.

    [*] An eigenvalue whose geometric multiplicity is less than its algebraic multiplicity
    [ ] An eigenvalue that is negative
    [ ] An eigenvalue equal to zero
    Explanation: An eigenvalue is defective when its geometric multiplicity (dimension of eigenspace) is strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity (multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial).

  Q15. What does the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra guarantee about eigenvalues of an n x n matrix?
    [ ] All eigenvalues are real numbers
    [*] The characteristic polynomial has exactly n roots (counted with multiplicity) in the complex numbers
    [ ] There are exactly n distinct eigenvalues
    [ ] The matrix is always diagonalizable
    Explanation: By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, the degree-n characteristic polynomial has exactly n complex roots (with multiplicity). Every n x n matrix therefore has exactly n eigenvalues over the complex numbers.

  Q16. What is a 'dominant eigenvalue' of a matrix?
    [ ] The eigenvalue with the smallest absolute value
    [ ] The only real eigenvalue of a matrix
    [*] The eigenvalue with the largest absolute value
    [ ] The eigenvalue associated with the most eigenvectors
    Explanation: The dominant eigenvalue is the eigenvalue with the largest absolute value. It determines the long-term behavior of systems like A^k v as k grows large.

  Q17. What does it mean for a matrix to be 'positive definite' in terms of eigenvalues?
    [*] The matrix is symmetric and all eigenvalues are strictly positive
    [ ] The trace is positive
    [ ] All entries of the matrix are positive
    [ ] The determinant is positive
    Explanation: A symmetric matrix is positive definite if and only if all of its eigenvalues are strictly positive. This is equivalent to x^T A x > 0 for all nonzero vectors x.

  Q18. What is meant by a 'similar matrix' in the context of eigenvalues?
    [ ] Matrices that differ by a scalar multiple
    [*] Matrices related by B = P^(-1)AP that share the same eigenvalues
    [ ] Matrices that have the same dimension
    [ ] Matrices with the same entries in different order
    Explanation: Matrices A and B are similar if B = P^(-1)AP for some invertible P. Similar matrices share the same characteristic polynomial and therefore the same eigenvalues.

  Q19. What is meant by the 'power method' for finding eigenvalues?

    [ ] Raising each entry of the matrix to a power
    [*] An iterative algorithm that repeatedly multiplies a vector by A to converge to the dominant eigenvector
    [ ] A method that uses the trace and determinant to compute eigenvalues
    Explanation: The power method starts with a random vector and repeatedly multiplies by A, normalizing at each step. The sequence converges to the eigenvector of the dominant eigenvalue.

  Q20. What is a 'characteristic root' of a matrix?
    [ ] The square root of the determinant
    [*] Another name for an eigenvalue
    [ ] The principal square root of the matrix
    [ ] A root of the minimal polynomial only
    Explanation: A characteristic root is simply another term for an eigenvalue. It refers to a root of the characteristic polynomial det(A - lambda I) = 0.

  Q21. A structural engineer analyzes a bridge support modeled by the stiffness matrix K = [[6, -3], [-3, 6]]. What eigenvalues determine the natural vibration frequencies?
    [ ] -3 and 9
    [*] 3 and 9
    [ ] 6 and 6
    [ ] 0 and 12
    Explanation: det(K - lambda I) = (6-lambda)^2 - 9 = lambda^2 - 12lambda + 27 = (lambda - 3)(lambda - 9). The eigenvalues 3 and 9 give the squared natural frequencies.

  Q22. A Markov chain model for customer loyalty has transition matrix T = [[0.9, 0.2], [0.1, 0.8]]. One eigenvalue is always 1 for a stochastic matrix. What is the other eigenvalue?
    [*] 0.7
    [ ] 0.8
    [ ] 0.9
    [ ] 0.5
    Explanation: The trace = 0.9 + 0.8 = 1.7 = sum of eigenvalues. Since one eigenvalue is 1, the other is 1.7 - 1 = 0.7. Since |0.7| < 1, the chain converges to its steady state.

  Q23. A robotics engineer models a 2D arm with rotation matrix R(60 degrees) = [[0.5, -0.866], [0.866, 0.5]]. What are the eigenvalues?

    [ ] 1 and -1

    Explanation: The trace = -0.4 + (-0.2) = -0.6. Since the eigenvalues sum to the trace and one is 0, the other is -0.6. The negative eigenvalue means the non-equilibrium component decays exponentially.

--- Lesson 7.3: Finding Eigenvectors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Finding Eigenvectors
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For the identity matrix I, what is the eigenspace for lambda = 1?
I - 1*I = 0 (the zero matrix). The null space of the zero matrix is all of R^n, so every nonzero vector is an eigenvector of I with eigenvalue 1.
For A = [[1, 0, 0], [0, 2, 0], [0, 0, 3]], what is the eigenvector corresponding to lambda = 2?
For diagonal matrices, the eigenvector for eigenvalue d_i is the standard basis vector e_i. Since lambda = 2 is the second diagonal entry, the eigenvector is e2 = (0, 1, 0).
What is an eigenvector of a matrix?
An eigenvector is a nonzero vector v satisfying Av = lambda v. It is a direction that the matrix scales without changing direction (or reverses for negative lambda).
What is the eigenspace of a matrix for a given eigenvalue?
The eigenspace for eigenvalue lambda is the null space of (A - lambda I). It consists of all eigenvectors for that eigenvalue together with the zero vector, forming a subspace.
What is a 'generalized eigenvector' of a matrix A?
A generalized eigenvector satisfies (A - lambda I)^k v = 0 for some k >= 1. Regular eigenvectors correspond to k = 1. Generalized eigenvectors are needed when A is not diagonalizable.
What is the 'null space' of (A - lambda I) in the context of eigenvectors?
The null space of (A - lambda I) is exactly the eigenspace for eigenvalue lambda. It contains all vectors v with (A - lambda I)v = 0, i.e., Av = lambda v.
What is meant by 'left eigenvector' versus 'right eigenvector'?
A right eigenvector v satisfies Av = lambda v (column vector on the right of A). A left eigenvector w satisfies w^T A = lambda w^T (row vector on the left of A). Left eigenvectors are eigenvectors of A^T.
What is an 'invariant subspace' with respect to a matrix A?
An invariant subspace W under A satisfies A(W) subset of W. Every eigenspace is an invariant subspace, since if v is in the eigenspace, Av = lambda v is also in the eigenspace.
A Google-style PageRank algorithm uses the matrix M = [[0, 1/2], [1, 1/2]]. The dominant eigenvector (for lambda = 1) gives the page rankings. What is this eigenvector (unnormalized)?
M - I = [[-1, 1/2], [1, -1/2]]. Row reducing gives [[1, -1/2], [0, 0]], so x1 = x2/2. Taking x2 = 2 gives (1, 2). Page 2 has twice the rank of page 1.
An ecologist models a population with the Leslie matrix L = [[0, 6], [0.5, 0]]. The dominant eigenvalue is sqrt(3). What is the corresponding eigenvector (stable age distribution)?
L - sqrt(3)I = [[-sqrt(3), 6], [0.5, -sqrt(3)]]. From row 1: -sqrt(3)*x1 + 6*x2 = 0, so x1 = 6x2/sqrt(3) = 2sqrt(3)*x2. The eigenvector is (2sqrt(3), 1).
A mechanical engineer analyzes a coupled spring system with matrix K = [[3, -1, 0], [-1, 2, -1], [0, -1, 3]]. One eigenvalue is 3. What is an eigenvector for this eigenvalue?
K - 3I = [[0,-1,0],[-1,-1,-1],[0,-1,0]]. From rows 1 and 3: x2 = 0. From row 2: -x1-x3 = 0, so x1 = -x3. The eigenvector is (1, 0, -1), representing outer masses moving opposite while center stays still.
Real-World Applications
Example: A Google-style PageRank algorithm uses the matrix M = [[0, 1/2], [1, 1/2]]. The dominant eigenvector (for lambda = 1) gives the page rankings. What is this eigenvector (unnormalized)?
M - I = [[-1, 1/2], [1, -1/2]]. Row reducing gives [[1, -1/2], [0, 0]], so x1 = x2/2. Taking x2 = 2 gives (1, 2). Page 2 has twice the rank of page 1.
Example: An ecologist models a population with the Leslie matrix L = [[0, 6], [0.5, 0]]. The dominant eigenvalue is sqrt(3). What is the corresponding eigenvector (stable age distribution)?
L - sqrt(3)I = [[-sqrt(3), 6], [0.5, -sqrt(3)]]. From row 1: -sqrt(3)*x1 + 6*x2 = 0, so x1 = 6x2/sqrt(3) = 2sqrt(3)*x2. The eigenvector is (2sqrt(3), 1).

  Q1. To find eigenvectors for eigenvalue lambda, which system must be solved?
    [*] (A - lambda I)v = 0
    [ ] Av = 0

    [ ] (A + lambda I)v = 0
    Explanation: Eigenvectors satisfy Av = lambda v, which rearranges to (A - lambda I)v = 0. We solve this homogeneous system to find the eigenspace.

  Q2. Find an eigenvector of A = [[3, 1], [0, 2]] for eigenvalue lambda = 3.
    [*] (1, 0)
    [ ] (1, 1)
    [ ] (0, 1)
    [ ] (3, 1)
    Explanation: A - 3I = [[0, 1], [0, -1]]. Row reducing gives [[0, 1], [0, 0]], so x2 = 0 and x1 is free. The eigenvector is (1, 0).

  Q3. For A = [[2, 1], [1, 2]], find an eigenvector for lambda = 3.
    [ ] (2, 1)
    [*] (1, 1)
    [ ] (1, -1)
    [ ] (0, 1)
    Explanation: A - 3I = [[-1, 1], [1, -1]]. Row reducing gives [[-1, 1], [0, 0]], so x1 = x2. Taking x2 = 1 gives eigenvector (1, 1).

--- Lesson 7.4: Diagonalization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Diagonalization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If A = PDP^(-1), what is A^5?
A^5 = (PDP^(-1))^5 = PD(P^(-1)P)D(P^(-1)P)...(P^(-1)P)DP^(-1) = PD^5 P^(-1). The inner P^(-1)P pairs all cancel, leaving only the outermost P and P^(-1).
What is D^k for a diagonal matrix D = diag(2, 3)?
For a diagonal matrix, D^k = diag(d1^k, d2^k, ...). Each diagonal entry is raised to the k-th power independently.
Diagonalize A = [[4, 1], [0, 3]]. What is D?
The eigenvalues are 4 and 3 (diagonal entries of this upper triangular matrix). So D = diag(4, 3).
If A = PDP^(-1) with D = diag(1, -1), what is A^100?
A^100 = PD^100 P^(-1) = P diag(1^100, (-1)^100) P^(-1) = P diag(1, 1) P^(-1) = P I P^(-1) = I. Any diagonalizable involution raised to an even power is the identity.
If A is diagonalizable with eigenvalues 2 and 5, what is det(A)?
det(A) = det(PDP^(-1)) = det(D) = 2 * 5 = 10. The determinant is always the product of the eigenvalues.
What is the Spectral Theorem for real symmetric matrices?
The Spectral Theorem states that every real symmetric matrix has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors, so A = QDQ^T where Q is an orthogonal matrix.
What is a 'defective matrix' in the context of diagonalization?
A defective matrix has at least one eigenvalue whose geometric multiplicity is less than its algebraic multiplicity, resulting in fewer than n independent eigenvectors.
What is meant by 'matrix similarity'?
Matrices A and B are similar if B = P^(-1)AP for some invertible P. Similar matrices share the same eigenvalues, trace, determinant, and characteristic polynomial.
What is the role of the matrix P in the diagonalization A = PDP^(-1)?
In A = PDP^(-1), P is the modal matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors of A. The i-th column of P is the eigenvector corresponding to the i-th diagonal entry of D.
What is a 'matrix exponential' and how does diagonalization help compute it?
The matrix exponential e^A = I + A + A^2/2! + ... If A = PDP^(-1), then e^A = P e^D P^(-1) where e^D = diag(e^(d1), e^(d2), ...), making computation straightforward.
What is a 'diagonal matrix' in linear algebra?
A diagonal matrix has nonzero entries only on the main diagonal (d_ij = 0 when i != j). It is the simplest type of matrix for computation.
A population biologist models a species with growth matrix A = [[0.5, 0.6], [0.4, 0.9]]. To predict the population after 20 generations efficiently, she diagonalizes A. If eigenvalues are 0.2 and 1.2, what is the dominant term of A^20?
A^20 = P diag(0.2^20, 1.2^20) P^(-1). Since 0.2^20 is essentially 0 and 1.2^20 is approximately 38.34, the long-term behavior is dominated by the eigenvalue 1.2 and its eigenvector.
A financial engineer uses diagonalization to compute the matrix exponential e^(At) for portfolio risk modeling with A = [[-1, 2], [0, -3]]. What is e^(0*A)?
e^(0) = e^0 = I for any matrix. This is the initial condition: at time t = 0, the state transition matrix is the identity.
A climate modeler diagonalizes the heat transfer matrix A = [[0.6, 0.3], [0.4, 0.7]] to predict long-term temperature distribution. The eigenvalues are 1 and 0.3. What is the steady-state temperature ratio between the two regions?
The steady state is the eigenvector for lambda=1. A - I = [[-0.4, 0.3], [0.4, -0.3]], giving 0.4x1 = 0.3x2, so x1/x2 = 3/4. The steady-state distribution is (3, 4) normalized to (3/7, 4/7).
Real-World Applications
Example: An image processing engineer needs to apply a sharpening filter A repeatedly. She diagonalizes A and finds eigenvalues 1.5 and 0.8. After 10 applications, which eigenvalue dominates?
1.5^10 approximately equals 57.67 while 0.8^10 approximately equals 0.107. The component along the eigenvector for 1.5 grows exponentially, dominating after multiple applications.

--- Lesson 7.5: Applications in Differential Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Differential Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If a 2x2 system has eigenvalues 2 and -1, what is the behavior near the origin?
One positive eigenvalue (2) and one negative eigenvalue (-1) produce a saddle point. Solutions grow along one eigenvector direction and decay along the other.
For the system x' = Ax with purely imaginary eigenvalues +/- 3i, what is the behavior?
Purely imaginary eigenvalues (a = 0) produce a center with closed orbits. Solutions oscillate with period 2*pi/3 and constant amplitude.
What is the matrix exponential e^(At) used for in differential equations?
The solution to the linear ODE system x' = Ax with x(0) = x0 is x(t) = e^(At) x0. The matrix exponential serves as the fundamental matrix of the system.
What is a 'phase portrait' in the context of linear ODE systems?
A phase portrait shows the trajectories of solutions in the x1-x2 plane (state space). The shape of trajectories depends on the eigenvalues: nodes, saddles, spirals, or centers.
What is the 'fundamental matrix' of a linear system x' = Ax?
The fundamental matrix Phi(t) has columns that are n linearly independent solutions. The general solution is x(t) = Phi(t) c for a constant vector c. The matrix exponential e^(At) is a fundamental matrix.
What is a 'node' in the classification of equilibrium points?
A node has two real eigenvalues of the same sign. If both are negative, it is a stable node (sink); if both are positive, it is an unstable node (source).
What is the 'half-life' of a decaying mode in a linear system?
For a decaying exponential e^(lambda t) with lambda < 0, the half-life is t_half = ln(2)/|lambda|. This is the time for the mode amplitude to decrease by 50%.
What is a 'saddle point' in the phase plane?
A saddle point has one positive and one negative eigenvalue. Trajectories approach along the eigenvector of the negative eigenvalue and depart along that of the positive eigenvalue.
What is meant by 'variation of parameters' for a nonhomogeneous system x' = Ax + g(t)?
Variation of parameters writes x_p(t) = Phi(t) integral of Phi^(-1)(s) g(s) ds, using the fundamental matrix Phi(t) to find a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous system.
What is the 'state-space representation' of a differential equation?
State-space form converts a single nth-order ODE into a system of n first-order ODEs by introducing state variables x1 = y, x2 = y', etc., giving x' = Ax + Bu.
What is meant by the 'time constant' of a linear system mode?
The time constant tau = 1/|lambda| characterizes how quickly a mode decays (or grows). After time tau, the mode has decayed to e^(-1) or about 36.8% of its initial value.
A mechanical engineer models a damped spring-mass system with the equation mx'' + cx' + kx = 0 where m=1, c=5, k=6. Using eigenvalues, what is the response type?
The characteristic equation lambda^2 + 5lambda + 6 = (lambda+2)(lambda+3) = 0 gives eigenvalues -2 and -3. Two distinct negative reals = overdamped. The discriminant c^2 - 4mk = 25 - 24 = 1 > 0.
A control engineer designs a feedback system with x' = [[-2, 1], [-1, -2]]x. The eigenvalues are -2 +/- i. What is the frequency of oscillation?
For eigenvalues a +/- bi = -2 +/- i, the oscillation frequency is b = 1 rad/s. The real part a = -2 determines the decay rate. The solution oscillates at frequency 1 while decaying with time constant 1/2.
A robotics engineer linearizes a nonlinear system around an equilibrium and gets x' = [[0, 1], [-9, -6]]x. The eigenvalues are -3 (repeated). What is the nature of this equilibrium?
lambda = -3 repeated. A - (-3)I = [[3,1],[-9,-3]] has rank 1, so geometric multiplicity is 1 (defective). This gives a degenerate stable node: the system is critically damped with solutions decaying as (c1 + c2 t)e^(-3t).
Important Concepts
A control engineer designs a feedback system with x' = [[-2, 1], [-1, -2]]x. The eigenvalues are -2 +/- i. What is the frequency of oscillation
For eigenvalues a +/- bi = -2 +/- i, the oscillation frequency is b = 1 rad/s. The real part a = -2 determines the decay rate. The solution oscillates at frequency 1 while decaying with time constant 1/2.

    Explanation: The fundamental matrix Phi(t) has columns that are n linearly independent solutions. The general solution is x(t) = Phi(t) c for a constant vector c. The matrix exponential e^(At) is a fundamental matrix.

    [ ] Setting all initial conditions to zero

    Explanation: Substituting x = Py (where P contains eigenvectors) transforms x' = Ax into y' = Dy, where D is diagonal. Each variable y_i satisfies y_i' = lambda_i y_i independently.

    Explanation: A node has two real eigenvalues of the same sign. If both are negative, it is a stable node (sink); if both are positive, it is an unstable node (source).

    [ ] An equilibrium where solutions form closed loops
    [ ] An equilibrium where both eigenvalues are negative
    [ ] An equilibrium where both eigenvalues are complex
    Explanation: A saddle point has one positive and one negative eigenvalue. Trajectories approach along the eigenvector of the negative eigenvalue and depart along that of the positive eigenvalue.

--- Lesson 7.6: Applications in Physics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Physics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A quantum system is in state |psi> = (3/5)|+> + (4/5)|->. If |+> and |-> are eigenstates of an operator with eigenvalues +1 and -1, what is the expectation value?
The expectation value is sum of (probability)(eigenvalue): (3/5)^2(+1) + (4/5)^2(-1) = 9/25 - 16/25 = -7/25.
What is a 'normal mode' in the physics of oscillating systems?
A normal mode is a pattern of oscillation in which all parts of the system move sinusoidally at the same frequency. Each normal mode corresponds to an eigenvector of the system matrix.
What is the 'spectral decomposition' of a Hermitian operator in quantum mechanics?
The spectral decomposition writes A = sum of lambda_i |v_i>
What is the physical meaning of a 'degenerate eigenvalue' in quantum mechanics?
A degenerate eigenvalue means multiple linearly independent eigenstates share the same energy. This degeneracy often arises from symmetry in the system.
What is a 'unitary matrix' and why is it important in quantum mechanics?
A unitary matrix satisfies U^dagger U = I. In quantum mechanics, time evolution is unitary, meaning it preserves inner products and hence probabilities.
What is the 'strain tensor' in continuum mechanics?
The strain tensor is a symmetric matrix describing deformation. Its eigenvalues are the principal strains (maximum and minimum stretching), and eigenvectors are the principal strain directions.
What is an 'Hermitian matrix' and why must physical observables be Hermitian?
An Hermitian matrix satisfies A = A^dagger. Physical observables must be Hermitian because measurement outcomes must be real numbers, and Hermitian matrices are guaranteed to have real eigenvalues.
What is 'perturbation theory' in quantum mechanics as it relates to eigenvalues?

The probability of measuring eigenvalue +1 is |<0|psi>|^2 = |1/sqrt(2)|^2 = 1/2. The state has equal amplitudes in both eigenstates, giving 50-50 measurement probabilities.
An astrophysicist models the moment of inertia tensor of an asteroid as I = [[4, 0, 0], [0, 6, 2], [0, 2, 3]] kg*m^2. What is the smallest principal moment of inertia?
One eigenvalue is 4 (from the 1,1 block). For the 2x2 block [[6,2],[2,3]]: trace=9, det=14, eigenvalues = (9 +/- sqrt(81-56))/2 = (9 +/- 5)/2 = 7 and 2. The smallest principal moment is 2 kg*m^2.

    Explanation: A degenerate eigenvalue means multiple linearly independent eigenstates share the same energy. This degeneracy often arises from symmetry in the system.

    [ ] The momentum operator

    [ ] A matrix with all entries equal to 1; it represents superposition

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In a Markov chain with transition matrix P, what is the steady-state vector?
The steady-state vector pi satisfies P*pi = pi, making it the eigenvector for eigenvalue 1. It represents the long-run probability distribution.
What is the algebraic connectivity (Fiedler value) of a graph?
The algebraic connectivity (Fiedler value) is the second smallest eigenvalue of the graph Laplacian. It measures how well connected the graph is; a larger value means more connected.
What is 'spectral graph theory'?
Spectral graph theory analyzes graph properties using the spectrum (eigenvalues) of matrices like the adjacency matrix or Laplacian. It connects linear algebra to combinatorial properties.
What is the 'graph Laplacian' matrix?
The Laplacian L = D - A is a positive semidefinite matrix where D is diagonal with vertex degrees. It encodes the graph structure and its eigenvalues reveal connectivity.
What is the 'damping factor' in Google's PageRank and how does it affect eigenvalues?
The damping factor d blends the link matrix with a uniform matrix: M' = dM + (1-d)(1/n)ee^T. This guarantees all secondary eigenvalues have |lambda| <= d < 1, ensuring unique convergence.
What is 'convergence rate' of an iterative method in terms of eigenvalues?
The error after k iterations decays approximately as rho(B)^k, where rho(B) is the spectral radius of the iteration matrix. Smaller rho means faster convergence.
What is 'eigenface' in computer vision?
Eigenfaces are eigenvectors of the covariance matrix computed from a set of face images. They form a basis for face space, allowing efficient face recognition by projection.
What is the relationship between the adjacency matrix eigenvalues and graph properties?
The spectral radius of the adjacency matrix provides bounds on the chromatic number, independence number, and clique number. The spectrum encodes much structural information about the graph.
What is 'spectral embedding' in dimensionality reduction?
Spectral embedding maps data points to coordinates given by eigenvectors of a similarity or Laplacian matrix. Methods like Laplacian Eigenmaps and spectral clustering use this approach.
What is 'matrix factorization' in recommender systems?
Matrix factorization approximates the sparse rating matrix R as a product of low-rank matrices (R ~ UV^T), where U represents user factors and V represents item factors. This is closely related to eigendecomposition.

For a column-stochastic matrix, the dominant eigenvalue is always 1. The corresponding eigenvector gives the steady-state PageRank distribution.

--- Lesson 7.8: Applications in Economics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications in Economics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A two-sector economy has input-output matrix A = [[0.2, 0.3], [0.4, 0.1]]. What is the largest eigenvalue of A?
det(A - lambda I) = (0.2-lambda)(0.1-lambda) - 0.12 = lambda^2 - 0.3lambda - 0.1. Using the quadratic formula: lambda = (0.3 +/- sqrt(0.09 + 0.4))/2 = (0.3 +/- 0.7)/2. So lambda = 0.5 and -0.2.
What is the Leontief input-output model?
The Leontief model describes how sectors of an economy depend on each other. Production x must satisfy x = Ax + d, solved as x = (I-A)^(-1)d when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.
What is the 'Hawkins-Simon condition' in economics?
The Hawkins-Simon condition ensures the economy is productive: each sector can produce more than it consumes. Mathematically, the spectral radius of A must be less than 1.
What is a 'stochastic matrix' in economic modeling?
A stochastic matrix has nonnegative entries with columns (or rows) summing to 1. It models probabilistic transitions such as consumer brand switching or economic state transitions.
What is 'dynamic input-output analysis' in economics?
Dynamic input-output analysis extends the Leontief model to include capital accumulation: x(t+1) = Ax(t) + Bx(t). Eigenvalues determine growth rates and stability of the economic trajectory.
What is the 'von Neumann growth model' in economics?
The von Neumann model finds the maximum rate at which all sectors can grow simultaneously. This balanced growth rate equals the dominant eigenvalue, and the eigenvector gives the balanced growth proportions.
What is 'convergence' in economic growth theory related to eigenvalues?
In the Solow model, the speed of convergence to steady state is -(1-alpha)*delta, where this is the eigenvalue of the linearized system. A more negative eigenvalue means faster convergence.
What is the 'multiplier effect' in the Leontief model related to eigenvalues?
The Leontief inverse (I-A)^(-1) = I + A + A^2 + ... captures all rounds of inter-industry demand. This geometric series converges precisely when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.
What is 'factor analysis' in econometrics?
Factor analysis uses eigendecomposition of the correlation matrix to identify latent factors (e.g., market risk, interest rate risk) that explain the correlations among observed economic variables.
What is a 'steady-state distribution' in an economic Markov model?
The steady-state distribution pi is the eigenvector for eigenvalue 1 of the transition matrix, normalized so entries sum to 1. It represents the long-run market shares or state probabilities.
A financial analyst has a portfolio covariance matrix C = [[0.04, 0.02], [0.02, 0.09]]. The eigenvalues are approximately 0.1 and 0.03. What is the maximum portfolio variance achievable?
The maximum variance of a unit-weight portfolio w^T C w is the largest eigenvalue (0.1), achieved when w is the corresponding eigenvector. This is the direction of maximum risk.
A real estate economist models housing market dynamics with x(t+1) = [[0.7, 0.1], [0.3, 0.9]]x(t), where x1 is urban and x2 is suburban population. What is the long-run urban-to-suburban ratio?
This is a column-stochastic matrix (columns sum to 1), so eigenvalue 1 exists. A - I = [[-0.3, 0.1], [0.3, -0.1]]. From row 1: 0.3*x1 = 0.1*x2, so x1/x2 = 1/3. The long-run ratio is 1:3, meaning the suburban population is three times the urban population.
Real-World Applications
Example: A Leslie population model for age-structured economic labor force has matrix L with dominant eigenvalue 1.03. What does this predict?
The dominant eigenvalue determines long-run growth: lambda > 1 means growth, lambda < 1 means decline. With lambda = 1.03, the population grows at 3% per period, approaching the stable age distribution given by the eigenvector.
Example: In a simple Solow growth model linearized around the steady state, the eigenvalue determines the convergence speed. If lambda = 0.95, what percentage of the gap to steady state closes each period?
If the deviation from steady state decays as 0.95^t, then each period the gap shrinks by (1 - 0.95) = 5%. The economy closes 5% of its distance to steady state each period.
Example: What is the 'multiplier effect' in the Leontief model related to eigenvalues?
The Leontief inverse (I-A)^(-1) = I + A + A^2 + ... captures all rounds of inter-industry demand. This geometric series converges precisely when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.
Example: A development economist computes the Leontief inverse for a developing country and finds the largest entry is 3.2. What does this 'multiplier' mean?
The column sum of the Leontief inverse for sector j gives the total output multiplier: how much total output across all sectors increases for a $1 increase in final demand for sector j.

    Explanation: A stochastic matrix has nonnegative entries with columns (or rows) summing to 1. It models probabilistic transitions such as consumer brand switching or economic state transitions.

  Q14. What does the 'Perron-Frobenius theorem' guarantee for economic models?
    [ ] That government intervention is necessary when evaluated systematically
    [ ] That all economic growth rates are positive when applying fundamental principles
    [*] That nonnegative matrices (like input-output matrices) have a unique dominant real eigenvalue with a nonnegative eigenvector
    [ ] That market equilibrium always exists, particularly regarding guarantee following conventional understanding
    Explanation: Perron-Frobenius guarantees a dominant nonneg eigenvalue with a nonneg eigenvector for nonneg matrices. In economics, this ensures meaningful production levels and growth rates.

  Q15. What is 'dynamic input-output analysis' in economics?
    [ ] Measuring GDP in real time
    [ ] Adjusting the input-output table for inflation
    [ ] Computing static equilibrium with time-varying prices
    [*] Studying how inter-industry relationships evolve over time using eigenvalue-driven dynamics
    Explanation: Dynamic input-output analysis extends the Leontief model to include capital accumulation: x(t+1) = Ax(t) + Bx(t). Eigenvalues determine growth rates and stability of the economic trajectory.

  Q16. What is the 'von Neumann growth model' in economics?
    [ ] A model of random economic fluctuations
    [*] A model where the maximum balanced growth rate equals the dominant eigenvalue of a production matrix
    [ ] A game theory model of market competition
    [ ] A model of individual savings decisions
    Explanation: The von Neumann model finds the maximum rate at which all sectors can grow simultaneously. This balanced growth rate equals the dominant eigenvalue, and the eigenvector gives the balanced growth proportions.

  Q17. What is 'convergence' in economic growth theory related to eigenvalues?
    [ ] Rich countries always grow faster than poor countries
    [ ] All countries eventually reach the same GDP
    [*] The rate at which an economy approaches its steady state, determined by the eigenvalue of the linearized system
    [ ] Economic predictions become more accurate over time
    Explanation: In the Solow model, the speed of convergence to steady state is -(1-alpha)*delta, where this is the eigenvalue of the linearized system. A more negative eigenvalue means faster convergence.

  Q18. What is the 'multiplier effect' in the Leontief model related to eigenvalues?
    [ ] The ratio of output to input for each sector in most standard conditions
    [*] The Leontief inverse (I-A)^(-1) = I + A + A^2 + ..., whose convergence depends on eigenvalues of A being less than 1
    [ ] The government spending multiplier, particularly regarding spectral as observed in standard experiments
    [ ] The product of all eigenvalues, particularly regarding leontief when measured under controlled conditions
    Explanation: The Leontief inverse (I-A)^(-1) = I + A + A^2 + ... captures all rounds of inter-industry demand. This geometric series converges precisely when the spectral radius of A is less than 1.

  Q19. What is 'factor analysis' in econometrics?
    [ ] Factoring large numbers for cryptographic applications
    [ ] Analyzing the factors of production (land, labor, capital)
    [ ] Decomposing GDP into its components
    [*] Using eigenvalue decomposition to identify latent factors driving observed economic variables
    Explanation: Factor analysis uses eigendecomposition of the correlation matrix to identify latent factors (e.g., market risk, interest rate risk) that explain the correlations among observed economic variables.

  Q20. What is a 'steady-state distribution' in an economic Markov model?
    [ ] Any probability distribution
    [ ] The distribution that maximizes entropy
    [*] The eigenvector of the transition matrix corresponding to eigenvalue 1, normalized to sum to 1
    [ ] The average of all rows of the transition matrix
    Explanation: The steady-state distribution pi is the eigenvector for eigenvalue 1 of the transition matrix, normalized so entries sum to 1. It represents the long-run market shares or state probabilities.

  Q21. An economist models a 3-sector economy with input-output matrix A = [[0.1, 0.2, 0.1], [0.2, 0.1, 0.3], [0.1, 0.3, 0.1]]. The column sums are 0.4, 0.6, and 0.5. Is the Hawkins-Simon condition likely satisfied?
    [ ] No, because column sums exceed 0.5
    [ ] No, because the matrix is not diagonal
    [ ] Cannot determine without computing eigenvalues exactly
    [*] Yes, because all column sums are less than 1, suggesting the spectral radius is less than 1
    Explanation: The spectral radius is bounded by the maximum column sum (0.6 < 1). Since max column sum < 1, the spectral radius < 1, and the Hawkins-Simon condition is satisfied.

  Q22. A financial analyst has a portfolio covariance matrix C = [[0.04, 0.02], [0.02, 0.09]]. The eigenvalues are approximately 0.1 and 0.03. What is the maximum portfolio variance achievable?
    [ ] 0.09 (the largest diagonal entry)
    [ ] 0.04 (the smallest diagonal entry)
    [*] 0.1 (the largest eigenvalue)
    [ ] 0.13
    Explanation: The maximum variance of a unit-weight portfolio w^T C w is the largest eigenvalue (0.1), achieved when w is the corresponding eigenvector. This is the direction of maximum risk.

  Q23. A labor economist models worker mobility between 3 sectors with transition matrix P. After computing eigenvalues 1, 0.7, and 0.3, she wants to know how many periods until the distribution is within 1% of steady state. Which eigenvalue determines this?
    [ ] The eigenvalue 0.3
    [ ] The product 0.7 * 0.3 = 0.21
    [*] The eigenvalue 0.7 (second largest in absolute value)
    [ ] The eigenvalue 1
    Explanation: Convergence rate is determined by the second-largest eigenvalue. For 0.7^k < 0.01: k > log(0.01)/log(0.7) = 12.9, so about 13 periods. The eigenvalue 0.3 mode decays much faster.

  Q24. A trade economist applies the Leontief model to find that (I - A)^(-1) has entry (2,1) = 0.45. What does this mean economically?
    [*] A $1 increase in final demand for sector 1's product requires $0.45 additional output from sector 2 (directly and indirectly)
    [ ] Sector 2 produces 45% of sector 1's output
    [ ] The price of sector 2's good is 45% of sector 1's price
    [ ] 45% of sector 2's output goes to sector 1
    Explanation: Entry (i,j) of the Leontief inverse gives the total output of sector i needed (directly and through all rounds of indirect demand) per unit of final demand for sector j's product.

  Q25. A macroeconomist linearizes a dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) model and finds eigenvalues 0.97, 0.85, and 1.03. What does this imply about the model's dynamics?
    [*] The model has a saddle-path structure with one unstable and two stable roots
    [ ] The model is stable; all variables converge
    [ ] The model is completely unstable
    [ ] The model has no meaningful solution
    Explanation: One eigenvalue > 1 (1.03) and two < 1 (0.97, 0.85) create a saddle-path structure. The Blanchard-Kahn conditions require the number of unstable roots to equal the number of forward-looking variables for a unique solution.

  Q26. An international trade analyst models 3 countries' trade flows with a transition matrix. The second eigenvalue is 0.4 + 0.3i (in absolute value approximately 0.5). What does the complex eigenvalue indicate?
    [*] Trade patterns show dampened cyclical behavior as they converge to equilibrium
    [ ] Trade is impossible between these countries
    [ ] Trade grows without bound
    [ ] The model is incorrectly specified
    Explanation: Complex eigenvalues in a Markov transition matrix produce oscillatory convergence to steady state. The imaginary part creates cyclical patterns while the modulus < 1 ensures they dampen over time.

  Q27. A real estate economist models housing market dynamics with x(t+1) = [[0.7, 0.1], [0.3, 0.9]]x(t), where x1 is urban and x2 is suburban population. What is the long-run urban-to-suburban ratio?
    [ ] 1:1
    [*] 1:3
    [ ] 3:1
    [ ] 7:9
    Explanation: This is a column-stochastic matrix (columns sum to 1), so eigenvalue 1 exists. A - I = [[-0.3, 0.1], [0.3, -0.1]]. From row 1: 0.3*x1 = 0.1*x2, so x1/x2 = 1/3. The long-run ratio is 1:3, meaning the suburban population is three times the urban population.

  Q28. A development economist computes the Leontief inverse for a developing country and finds the largest entry is 3.2. What does this 'multiplier' mean?
    [ ] Imports are 3.2 times exports, particularly regarding across following conventional understanding
    [ ] GDP is 3.2 times the population as determined by standard methodology
    [*] A $1 increase in final demand in one sector generates $3.20 total output across all sectors combined
    [ ] The economy grows at 320% per year as observed in standard experiments
    Explanation: The column sum of the Leontief inverse for sector j gives the total output multiplier: how much total output across all sectors increases for a $1 increase in final demand for sector j.

  Q29. A central banker models inflation dynamics across 2 sectors. The system matrix has eigenvalues 0.98 and 0.6. After a supply shock, how many quarters until the inflation impact is halved?
    [ ] About 2 quarters (from the eigenvalue 0.6)
    [*] About 34 quarters (from the eigenvalue 0.98)
    [ ] About 50 quarters
    [ ] About 10 quarters
    Explanation: The dominant (slowest-decaying) eigenvalue 0.98 determines long-run dynamics. Half-life: 0.98^k = 0.5 gives k = ln(0.5)/ln(0.98) = -0.693/(-0.0202) = 34.3 quarters. Inflation is highly persistent.

  Q30. A behavioral economist models consumer habit formation with x(t+1) = 0.7*x(t) + 0.3*s(t), where s is a stimulus. The eigenvalue 0.7 represents the 'habit persistence parameter.' If a marketing campaign (stimulus) stops, how quickly do old habits return?
    [ ] Never; the effect is permanent
    [*] With a half-life of about 2 periods (0.7^2 = 0.49)
    [ ] After exactly 0.7 periods
    [ ] Immediately (within 1 period)
    Explanation: After the stimulus stops (s=0), the habit evolves as x(t) = 0.7^t * x(0). Half-life: 0.7^k = 0.5 gives k = ln(0.5)/ln(0.7) = 1.94 periods. The effect halves roughly every 2 periods.
