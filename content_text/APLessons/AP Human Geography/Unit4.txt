--- Lesson 4.1: Territorial Organization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Territorial Organization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a state in political geography?
In political geography, a state is a sovereign political entity with a permanent population, defined territory, effective government, and capacity to enter relations with other states, as defined by the Montevideo Convention (1933).
What is sovereignty?
Sovereignty is the principle that a state has supreme authority over its territory and domestic affairs, free from external interference. It is the foundational concept of the modern international system established by the Peace of Westphalia (1648).
What is a federal state?
A federal state divides power between national and subnational governments. The United States, Germany, Canada, India, and Brazil are federal states where provinces or states have their own legislatures and significant autonomy.
What is a unitary state?
In a unitary state, the central government holds supreme authority and any local or regional governments derive their power from the center. France, Japan, and the United Kingdom are examples of unitary states.
What is a territory in political geography?
Territories are areas under a state's control that lack full sovereignty or statehood. Examples include Puerto Rico (U.S. territory), French Polynesia (French territory), and the British Virgin Islands.
What is the concept of territorial integrity?
Territorial integrity is the international norm that states should not have their borders violated or territory seized by other states. It is a fundamental principle of the UN Charter and international law.
What is devolution?
Devolution transfers power from national to subnational levels. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have devolved governments within the UK, and Spain has granted significant autonomy to regions like Catalonia and the Basque Country.
What is a capital city?
A capital city is the seat of government. Some countries have purpose-built capitals (Brasilia, Canberra, Abuja) separate from their largest city, while others have capitals that are also their primate cities (London, Paris, Tokyo).
What is a 'buffer state'?
A buffer state is positioned between rival powers, reducing the chance of direct conflict. Historical examples include Thailand between British and French colonial empires, and Mongolia between Russia and China.
What is a 'landlocked state'?
Landlocked states lack ocean coastlines, which historically limited trade access. Examples include Bolivia, Switzerland, Mongolia, and many African states. Being landlocked is generally considered an economic disadvantage.
What is a 'prorupted state'?
A prorupted state has a compact core with an elongated extension. Thailand has a proruption extending southward, and the Democratic Republic of Congo has a proruption reaching the Atlantic coast. Proruptions often exist for strategic access.
What is an 'elongated state'?
An elongated state is significantly longer than it is wide. Chile, stretching 4,300 km north to south but averaging only 177 km wide, is the classic example. Elongation creates challenges for infrastructure and governance.
Real-World Applications
Example: Brasilia was built in the 1960s as a new capital in Brazil's interior, replacing coastal Rio de Janeiro. The goal was to promote development of the interior, create a neutral capital away from regional rivalries, and symbolize a modern Brazil. What does the creation of purpose-built capitals reveal?
Purpose-built capitals (Brasilia, Canberra, Abuja, Naypyidaw) demonstrate that capital location is a strategic political decision. Moving a capital can promote territorial integration, reduce the dominance of one region, and signal a new national direction.
Example: Switzerland is a federal state with 26 cantons, four official languages, and a tradition of direct democracy. Despite its diversity, it has avoided the ethnic conflicts that plague many multilingual states. What factors explain Switzerland's successful territorial organization?
Switzerland succeeds through radical decentralization: cantons have extensive autonomy in language, education, and culture, while shared national institutions, military service, and economic prosperity create centripetal forces. Its success model is often cited but rarely replicated.
