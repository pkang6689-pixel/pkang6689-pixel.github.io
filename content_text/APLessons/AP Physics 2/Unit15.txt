--- Lesson 15.1: Photoelectric Effect (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Photoelectric Effect
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the photoelectric effect?
The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons (photoelectrons) from a material's surface when electromagnetic radiation of sufficient frequency (energy) strikes it. It was explained by Einstein using the photon concept.
What is a photon?
A photon is the quantum of the electromagnetic field - a discrete packet of energy E = hf that also carries momentum p = h/lambda. It has zero rest mass and always travels at the speed of light.
What is the work function of a metal?
The work function (phi) is the minimum photon energy needed to liberate an electron from a metal's surface. It is a characteristic property of each metal, typically measured in electron volts (eV).
What is the threshold frequency?
The threshold frequency f0 = phi/h is the minimum light frequency that can cause electron emission. Below this frequency, photons lack sufficient energy to overcome the work function.
What is the stopping potential?
The stopping potential V0 is the reverse voltage that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons: eV0 = KE_max = hf - phi. It depends on frequency but not intensity.
What is an electron volt (eV)?
An electron volt (eV) is a convenient energy unit in atomic/particle physics: 1 eV = 1.6 * 10^-19 J, the energy gained by one electron charge accelerated through 1 volt.
What is the photoelectric equation (Einstein's equation)?
Einstein's photoelectric equation KE_max = hf - phi states that the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons equals the photon energy (hf) minus the minimum energy to free the electron (work function phi).
What is a photoelectron?
A photoelectron is an electron that has been emitted from a material through the photoelectric effect. Its kinetic energy depends on the photon's energy and the material's work function.
What is the relationship between photon energy and wavelength?
Since E = hf and f = c/lambda, photon energy E = hc/lambda. Shorter wavelengths (blue/UV) have higher energy; longer wavelengths (red/IR) have lower energy.
What is the significance of the photoelectric effect in the history of physics?
The photoelectric effect was pivotal in establishing quantum physics. Einstein's 1905 explanation using photons (light quanta) earned him the 1921 Nobel Prize and showed that electromagnetic energy is quantized.
Light of wavelength 400 nm strikes a metal surface with a work function of 2.0 eV. What is the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons?
E = hc/lambda = (6.63e-34 * 3e8)/(400e-9) = 4.97e-19 J = 3.1 eV. KE_max = E - phi = 3.1 - 2.0 = 1.1 eV.
A metal has a work function of 4.5 eV. What is the maximum wavelength of light that can eject electrons?
At threshold: hf0 = phi, so lambda_max = hc/phi = (6.63e-34 * 3e8)/(4.5 * 1.6e-19) = 1.989e-25/7.2e-19 = 2.76e-7 m = 276 nm.
In a photoelectric experiment, the stopping potential is 1.5 V when light of frequency 8 * 10^14 Hz is used. What is the work function of the metal?
KE_max = eV0 = 1.5 eV. E_photon = hf = (6.63e-34)(8e14) = 5.304e-19 J = 3.31 eV. phi = hf - KE_max = 3.31 - 1.5 = 1.81 eV.
A solar cell uses the photoelectric effect with a material having a work function of 1.1 eV. What is the longest wavelength of light that can generate current?
lambda_max = hc/phi = (6.63e-34 * 3e8)/(1.1 * 1.6e-19) = 1.989e-25/1.76e-19 = 1.13e-6 m = 1130 nm (near infrared).
Cesium has a work function of 2.1 eV. If 450 nm light hits a cesium surface, what is the maximum speed of the emitted electrons? (m_e = 9.11 * 10^-31 kg)
E_photon = hc/lambda = (6.63e-34*3e8)/(450e-9) = 4.42e-19 J = 2.76 eV. KE_max = 2.76 - 2.1 = 0.66 eV = 1.06e-19 J. v = sqrt(2*KE/m) = sqrt(2*1.06e-19/9.11e-31) = sqrt(2.33e11) = 4.8*10^5 m/s ≈ 4.6*10^5 m/s.
A photomultiplier tube detects individual photons with wavelength 500 nm. What is the energy of each detected photon in joules?
E = hc/lambda = (6.63*10^-34)(3*10^8)/(500*10^-9) = 1.989*10^-25/5*10^-7 = 3.98 * 10^-19 J ≈ 2.48 eV.

--- Lesson 15.2: Wave-Particle Duality (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Wave-Particle Duality
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is wave-particle duality?
Wave-particle duality is a central concept of quantum mechanics: all quantum objects (photons, electrons, atoms) display both wave and particle behavior. Which aspect is observed depends on the experimental setup.
What is the de Broglie wavelength?
The de Broglie wavelength lambda = h/p = h/(mv) assigns a wave character to all moving matter. De Broglie's 1924 hypothesis was confirmed by electron diffraction experiments in 1927.
What is the Heisenberg uncertainty principle?
The uncertainty principle is a fundamental limit of nature (not instrument limitations): the more precisely one knows a particle's position, the less precisely one can know its momentum, and vice versa.
What is Compton scattering?
Compton scattering occurs when a photon collides with a free electron. The photon transfers some energy and momentum to the electron, emerging with a longer wavelength: delta_lambda = (h/mc)(1 - cos theta).
What is a matter wave?
A matter wave (de Broglie wave) is the wave-like behavior of a particle. Its wavelength lambda = h/p determines how the particle diffracts and interferes. The wave function psi describes the probability of finding the particle.
What is a wave function?
The wave function psi(x,t) is a complex-valued function in quantum mechanics. |psi|^2 gives the probability density of finding the particle at position x at time t. It is obtained by solving the Schrodinger equation.
What is the Davisson-Germer experiment?
Davisson and Germer (1927) fired electrons at a nickel crystal and observed diffraction peaks at angles predicted by de Broglie's wavelength formula, providing the first direct evidence for matter waves.
What is the complementarity principle?
Bohr's complementarity principle states that wave and particle aspects of quantum objects are complementary: a single experiment reveals one aspect or the other, never both simultaneously. Both are necessary for complete understanding.
What is the Compton wavelength shift?
The Compton shift delta_lambda = (h/(m_e*c))(1 - cos theta) describes how much a photon's wavelength increases when it scatters off an electron at angle theta. h/(m_e*c) = 0.00243 nm is the Compton wavelength of the electron.
What is quantum tunneling?
Quantum tunneling occurs when a particle's wave function extends through a potential barrier, giving it a nonzero probability of appearing on the other side. It is crucial in nuclear fusion, semiconductor devices, and scanning tunneling microscopes.
An electron is accelerated from rest through a 150 V potential difference. What is its de Broglie wavelength?
lambda = h/sqrt(2meV) = 6.63e-34/sqrt(2*9.11e-31*1.6e-19*150) = 6.63e-34/sqrt(4.37e-47) = 6.63e-34/6.61e-24 = 1.0e-10 m = 0.1 nm.
A neutron (mass 1.67 * 10^-27 kg) has a de Broglie wavelength of 0.14 nm. What is its kinetic energy?
p = h/lambda = 6.63e-34/(0.14e-9) = 4.74e-24 kg*m/s. KE = p^2/(2m) = (4.74e-24)^2/(2*1.67e-27) = 2.25e-47/3.34e-27 = 6.73e-21 J = 0.042 eV.
In Compton scattering, an X-ray photon of wavelength 0.07 nm scatters off a free electron at 90 degrees. What is the wavelength of the scattered photon?
delta_lambda = (h/mc)(1-cos(90)) = 0.00243 nm * (1-0) = 0.00243 nm. lambda' = 0.07 + 0.00243 = 0.0724 nm.
A 70 kg person walks at 1.5 m/s. What is their de Broglie wavelength?
lambda = h/(mv) = 6.63e-34/(70*1.5) = 6.63e-34/105 = 6.3 * 10^-36 m. This is incredibly small - far too small to observe, which is why macroscopic objects don't exhibit wave behavior.
An electron is confined to a region of width 0.1 nm (approximately the size of an atom). What is the minimum uncertainty in its velocity?
delta_p >= h-bar/(2*delta_x) = (1.055e-34)/(2*0.1e-9) = 5.28e-25 kg*m/s. delta_v = delta_p/m = 5.28e-25/9.11e-31 = 5.8*10^5 m/s.
An electron microscope uses electrons accelerated through 50,000 V. What is the de Broglie wavelength of these electrons, and how does it compare to visible light?
lambda = h/sqrt(2meV) = 6.63e-34/sqrt(2*9.11e-31*1.6e-19*50000) = 6.63e-34/sqrt(1.46e-44) = 6.63e-34/1.21e-22 = 5.5e-12 m = 0.0055 nm. This is ~100,000 times shorter than visible light, enabling much higher resolution.
In a scanning tunneling microscope, electrons tunnel across a gap of 1 nm. If the barrier height is 4 eV, what is the approximate transmission probability factor for the exponential decay?
The decay constant kappa = sqrt(2mU)/h-bar = sqrt(2*9.11e-31*4*1.6e-19)/(1.055e-34) = sqrt(1.166e-48)/1.055e-34 = 1.08e-24/1.055e-34 = 1.024e10 /m. For d = 1 nm: 2*kappa*d = 2*1.024e10*1e-9 = 20.5. T ∝ e^-20.5 ≈ 1.2*10^-9.

--- Lesson 15.3: Atomic Models and Energy Levels (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Atomic Models and Energy Levels
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Bohr model of the atom?
The Bohr model (1913) postulates that electrons orbit the nucleus in discrete energy levels. Transitions between levels produce or absorb photons. While superseded by quantum mechanics, it correctly predicts hydrogen's spectrum.
What is an energy level in an atom?
An energy level is one of the discrete, quantized energy values that an electron in an atom can possess. The electron can only exist at these specific energies, not in between.
What is an emission spectrum?
An emission spectrum is the characteristic pattern of spectral lines emitted by excited atoms. Each line corresponds to a specific electronic transition, and the pattern is unique to each element.
What is the principal quantum number?
The principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3, ... determines the main energy level and approximate size of the electron orbital. For hydrogen, E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV.
What is the Lyman series?
The Lyman series consists of transitions from n = 2, 3, 4, ... to n = 1 (ground state). These produce UV photons because the energy differences to the ground state are large.
What is an excited state?
An excited state is any quantum state with higher energy than the ground state. Atoms reach excited states by absorbing photons or through collisions. They typically return to the ground state quickly by emitting photons.
What is the Rydberg formula?
The Rydberg formula: 1/lambda = R_H(1/n_f^2 - 1/n_i^2), where R_H = 1.097*10^7 m^-1 is the Rydberg constant. It accurately predicts all hydrogen spectral line wavelengths.
What is spectroscopy?
Spectroscopy analyzes the electromagnetic spectrum emitted or absorbed by matter. It is used to identify elements (each has a unique spectral fingerprint), study atomic structure, and determine compositions of distant objects.
What is the Pauli exclusion principle?
The Pauli exclusion principle states that no two fermions (including electrons) in an atom can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously. This limits each orbital to two electrons with opposite spins.
A hydrogen atom transitions from n = 3 to n = 1. What is the energy of the emitted photon?
E_3 = -13.6/9 = -1.51 eV. E_1 = -13.6 eV. E_photon = E_3 - E_1 = -1.51 - (-13.6) = 12.09 eV.
What is the wavelength of the photon emitted during the n = 3 to n = 2 transition in hydrogen (the first Balmer line)?
E_photon = -13.6(1/4 - 1/9) = -13.6(5/36) = 1.89 eV = 3.02e-19 J. lambda = hc/E = (6.63e-34*3e8)/3.02e-19 = 6.58e-7 m = 656 nm (red).
The radius of the nth Bohr orbit in hydrogen is given by r_n = n^2 * a_0, where a_0 = 0.0529 nm. What is the radius of the n = 3 orbit?
r_3 = 3^2 * 0.0529 = 9 * 0.0529 = 0.476 nm.
A helium-neon laser produces light at 632.8 nm. What is the energy difference between the two energy levels involved in this transition?
E = hc/lambda = (6.63e-34*3e8)/(632.8e-9) = 3.14e-19 J = 1.96 eV.

    [*] 10

    [ ] 13.6 eV

--- Lesson 15.4: Nuclear Physics and Radioactivity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nuclear Physics and Radioactivity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is radioactivity?
Radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration of unstable atomic nuclei, emitting alpha particles, beta particles, gamma rays, or other radiation. The process transforms the nucleus into a different, more stable configuration.
What is half-life?
Half-life (t_1/2) is the time for exactly half the atoms in a radioactive sample to decay. It is constant for each isotope and ranges from fractions of a second to billions of years.
What is alpha decay?
In alpha decay, a heavy nucleus ejects an alpha particle (He-4: 2p + 2n). Example: U-238 -> Th-234 + He-4. The parent atom loses 2 from its atomic number and 4 from its mass number.
What is beta decay?
Beta decay involves the weak nuclear force: in beta-minus, a neutron becomes a proton + electron + antineutrino; in beta-plus, a proton becomes a neutron + positron + neutrino. The mass number stays constant.
What is nuclear binding energy?
Nuclear binding energy is the energy needed to disassemble a nucleus into individual protons and neutrons. It equals the mass defect (difference between separate nucleon masses and actual nuclear mass) times c^2.
What is mass defect?
Mass defect (delta_m) is the 'missing mass' of a nucleus: the combined mass of separated nucleons minus the actual nuclear mass. This mass is converted to binding energy: E_b = delta_m * c^2.
What is nuclear fission?
Nuclear fission occurs when a heavy nucleus (like U-235 or Pu-239) splits into two lighter nuclei after absorbing a neutron. The process releases ~200 MeV per event and additional neutrons that can sustain a chain reaction.
What is nuclear fusion?
Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei (like hydrogen isotopes) into heavier ones (like helium), releasing energy. It requires extremely high temperatures (~10^7 K) to overcome electrostatic repulsion, as in stellar cores.
What is the decay constant?
The decay constant (lambda) is the probability per unit time of a nucleus decaying: N(t) = N0*e^(-lambda*t). It relates to half-life: lambda = ln(2)/t_1/2 = 0.693/t_1/2.
What is the strong nuclear force?
The strong nuclear force (strong interaction) is the strongest of the four fundamental forces. It binds quarks together and, in its residual form, holds protons and neutrons in the nucleus. It only acts over ~10^-15 m.
Uranium-238 undergoes alpha decay. What is the daughter nucleus?
U-238 -> Th-234 + He-4. Alpha decay reduces the atomic number by 2 (92 -> 90 = Thorium) and mass number by 4 (238 -> 234).
A PET scan uses Fluorine-18 (half-life = 110 min). A dose of 10 mCi is prepared at 8:00 AM but the scan doesn't start until 11:40 AM. What is the activity at the time of the scan?
Time elapsed = 3 hours 40 min = 220 min. Number of half-lives = 220/110 = 2. Activity = 10 * (1/2)^2 = 10/4 = 2.5 mCi.

    [ ] A gamma ray

    [ ] An alpha particle
    [*] An electron and an antineutrino
    Explanation: In beta-minus decay, a neutron converts to a proton, emitting an electron (beta particle) and an antineutrino. The atomic number increases by 1 while the mass number stays the same.

  Q4. Gamma radiation is:
    [ ] A stream of neutrons
    [ ] A stream of electrons
    [*] High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons)
    [ ] A stream of helium nuclei

    [ ] All atoms to decay

  Q10. Nuclear fusion is the process that powers:
    [ ] Radioactive decay
    [*] The Sun and other stars
