--- Lesson 1.1: Fluids and Pressure (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fluids and Pressure
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI unit of pressure?
The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), which equals one newton per square meter (N/m²).
A fluid with a density of 1000 kg/m³ fills a container to a depth of 5 m. What is the gauge pressure at the bottom? (g = 10 m/s²)
Gauge pressure is P = ρgh = 1000 × 10 × 5 = 50,000 Pa. Gauge pressure does not include atmospheric pressure.
What is the absolute pressure at the bottom of a 10 m deep freshwater lake? (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s², P_atm = 100,000 Pa)
Absolute pressure = P_atm + ρgh = 100,000 + (1000)(10)(10) = 100,000 + 100,000 = 200,000 Pa.
What is 'gauge pressure'?
Gauge pressure is the pressure measured relative to atmospheric pressure: P_gauge = P_absolute − P_atm. A tire pressure gauge, for example, reads gauge pressure.
What is 'hydrostatic pressure'?
Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid at rest due to the weight of the fluid above. It is given by P = ρgh and increases linearly with depth.
What is meant by 'density' in the context of fluids?
Density (ρ) is defined as mass per unit volume (ρ = m/V). For water at standard conditions, ρ ≈ 1000 kg/m³. Density is distinct from weight density, which is weight per unit volume.
What is a 'manometer'?
A manometer is a U-shaped tube partially filled with a liquid (often mercury or water) used to measure the pressure difference between two points, typically between a gas and the atmosphere.
What is meant by an 'incompressible fluid'?
An incompressible fluid is one whose density does not change appreciably with pressure. Liquids like water are nearly incompressible, while gases are highly compressible.
A submarine descends to a depth of 200 m in seawater (ρ = 1025 kg/m³). What is the approximate total pressure on the hull? (g = 9.8 m/s², P_atm = 101,325 Pa)
P = P_atm + ρgh = 101,325 + (1025)(9.8)(200) = 101,325 + 2,009,000 ≈ 2,110,325 Pa ≈ 2,110,000 Pa, which is roughly 21 atmospheres.
A dam holds back a freshwater reservoir 30 m deep. An engineer needs to know the pressure at the base of the dam to design the structure. What is the gauge pressure at the base? (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 9.8 m/s²)
Gauge pressure at the base: P = ρgh = 1000 × 9.8 × 30 = 294,000 Pa. This is about 2.9 atm, which is the pressure the dam structure must withstand.
A city's water tower stores water at a height of 40 m above the ground-floor faucets. What is the water pressure available at those faucets, ignoring friction? (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 9.8 m/s²)
P = ρgh = 1000 × 9.8 × 40 = 392,000 Pa ≈ 3.87 atm. Water towers use gravitational potential energy to provide consistent water pressure to buildings.
An aircraft cabin is pressurized to 75,000 Pa while flying at an altitude where the outside atmospheric pressure is 25,000 Pa. A passenger notices their sealed bag of chips has puffed up. What is the pressure difference causing this expansion?
The chips were sealed at ground-level pressure (~101,325 Pa). Inside the cabin at 75,000 Pa, the bag's internal pressure exceeds the cabin pressure by about 101,325 − 75,000 ≈ 26,325 Pa. However, the pressure difference between the cabin and outside is 75,000 − 25,000 = 50,000 Pa, which is what drives structural stress on the fuselage.
A researcher designs an experiment using a U-tube manometer filled with mercury (ρ = 13,600 kg/m³). One side is open to the atmosphere and the other is connected to a gas supply. The mercury level on the gas side is 15 cm lower than on the open side. What is the gauge pressure of the gas? (g = 9.8 m/s²)
If the mercury is lower on the gas side, the gas pressure exceeds atmospheric. P_gauge = ρgh = 13,600 × 9.8 × 0.15 ≈ 19,992 Pa ≈ 20,000 Pa.
A swimming pool has a flat bottom with dimensions 10 m × 25 m and is filled to a uniform depth of 2 m with water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). What is the total force exerted by the water on the pool floor due to gauge pressure alone? (g = 10 m/s²)
Gauge pressure at bottom: P = ρgh = 1000 × 10 × 2 = 20,000 Pa. Floor area: A = 10 × 25 = 250 m². Total force: F = PA = 20,000 × 250 = 5,000,000 N (5 MN).
Real-World Applications
Example: A nurse connects an IV bag to a patient's arm. The bag is positioned 1.2 m above the insertion point, and the IV fluid has a density of 1020 kg/m³. What gauge pressure does the fluid exert at the needle tip? (g = 9.8 m/s²)
P = ρgh = 1020 × 9.8 × 1.2 ≈ 11,995 Pa ≈ 12,000 Pa. The height of the IV bag above the patient determines the driving pressure for fluid delivery.

  Q2. How does pressure change as you go deeper in a static fluid?
    [ ] It increases exponentially with depth
    [ ] It remains constant at all depths
    [ ] It decreases linearly with depth
    [*] It increases linearly with depth
    Explanation: Hydrostatic pressure increases linearly with depth according to P = P₀ + ρgh, where ρ is fluid density, g is gravitational acceleration, and h is depth.

  Q3. Standard atmospheric pressure at sea level is approximately:
    [ ] 1,013,250 Pa
    [ ] 50,000 Pa
    [ ] 10,325 Pa
    [*] 101,325 Pa
    Explanation: Standard atmospheric pressure is approximately 101,325 Pa (1 atm), which equals 760 mmHg or 14.7 psi.

  Q4. A fluid with a density of 1000 kg/m³ fills a container to a depth of 5 m. What is the gauge pressure at the bottom? (g = 10 m/s²)
    [ ] 100,000 Pa
    [*] 50,000 Pa
    [ ] 500,000 Pa
    [ ] 5,000 Pa
    Explanation: Gauge pressure is P = ρgh = 1000 × 10 × 5 = 50,000 Pa. Gauge pressure does not include atmospheric pressure.

  Q5. Which property distinguishes a fluid from a solid?
    [ ] Fluids have greater mass than solids
    [ ] Fluids always have lower density than solids
    [*] Fluids flow and cannot sustain a shear stress at rest
    [ ] Fluids cannot exert forces on surfaces
    Explanation: A fluid is a substance that flows and cannot maintain a shear stress when at rest. Both liquids and gases are fluids because they deform continuously under applied shear stress.

  Q6. Pressure at a point in a static fluid acts in which direction(s)?
    [ ] Only perpendicular to the container walls
    [*] Equally in all directions
    [ ] Only upward as a reaction force
    [ ] Only downward due to gravity
    Explanation: Pascal's principle states that pressure at a point in a static fluid acts equally in all directions. This is a fundamental property of fluids at rest.

  Q7. If the area of a piston doubles while the applied force stays the same, the pressure exerted by the piston:
    [ ] Stays the same
    [ ] Doubles
    [*] Is halved
    [ ] Quadruples
    Explanation: Since P = F/A, doubling A while keeping F constant results in half the original pressure.

  Q8. A mercury barometer reads 760 mm at sea level. What does this measurement represent?
    [ ] The gauge pressure of the atmosphere
    [ ] The density of mercury
    [*] The absolute atmospheric pressure
    [ ] The vacuum pressure inside the tube
    Explanation: A mercury barometer directly measures absolute atmospheric pressure. A reading of 760 mmHg corresponds to 1 atm or 101,325 Pa.

  Q9. Two containers of different shapes are filled with the same fluid to the same height. How do the pressures at the bottom compare?
    [ ] The wider container has greater pressure
    [*] The pressures are equal
    [ ] It depends on the total volume of fluid
    [ ] The narrower container has greater pressure
    Explanation: Hydrostatic pressure depends only on fluid density, gravitational acceleration, and depth (P = ρgh), not on the shape or total volume of the container. This is known as the hydrostatic paradox.

  Q10. What is the absolute pressure at the bottom of a 10 m deep freshwater lake? (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s², P_atm = 100,000 Pa)
    [ ] 100,000 Pa
    [ ] 300,000 Pa
    [*] 200,000 Pa
    [ ] 10,000 Pa
    Explanation: Absolute pressure = P_atm + ρgh = 100,000 + (1000)(10)(10) = 100,000 + 100,000 = 200,000 Pa.

  Q11. What is 'gauge pressure'?
    [*] The pressure measured relative to atmospheric pressure
    [ ] The pressure at the surface of a fluid
    [ ] The pressure exerted only by a gas
    [ ] The total pressure including atmospheric pressure
    Explanation: Gauge pressure is the pressure measured relative to atmospheric pressure: P_gauge = P_absolute − P_atm. A tire pressure gauge, for example, reads gauge pressure.

  Q12. What does 'absolute pressure' mean?
    [*] The total pressure including atmospheric and gauge contributions
    [ ] Pressure exerted by a solid on a fluid
    [ ] Pressure that cannot change over time
    [ ] Pressure measured only in a vacuum
    Explanation: Absolute pressure is the total pressure at a point, equal to atmospheric pressure plus gauge pressure: P_abs = P_atm + P_gauge. It is always positive and equals zero only in a perfect vacuum.

  Q13. What is 'hydrostatic pressure'?
    [ ] Pressure caused by fluid motion
    [ ] Pressure that only exists in ideal fluids
    [ ] Pressure applied externally to a fluid
    [*] Pressure exerted by a fluid at rest due to gravity
    Explanation: Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid at rest due to the weight of the fluid above. It is given by P = ρgh and increases linearly with depth.

  Q14. What does 'Pascal's principle' state?
    [ ] Fluids always flow from high to low pressure
    [ ] Fluid pressure depends on the shape of the container
    [*] A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid
    [ ] Pressure in a fluid decreases with depth
    Explanation: Pascal's principle states that a pressure change applied to an enclosed, incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished throughout the fluid and to the walls of its container.

  Q15. What is meant by 'density' in the context of fluids?
    [ ] The weight of a fluid per unit volume
    [*] The mass of a fluid per unit volume
    [ ] The force exerted by a fluid per unit area
    [ ] The resistance of a fluid to flow
    Explanation: Density (ρ) is defined as mass per unit volume (ρ = m/V). For water at standard conditions, ρ ≈ 1000 kg/m³. Density is distinct from weight density, which is weight per unit volume.

  Q16. What does 'specific gravity' mean?
    [ ] The gravitational force on a specific object
    [ ] The acceleration due to gravity at a specific location
    [ ] The weight of a fluid divided by its volume
    [*] The ratio of a substance's density to the density of water
    Explanation: Specific gravity is a dimensionless ratio comparing the density of a substance to the density of a reference substance (usually water at 4°C). A specific gravity of 2.0 means the substance is twice as dense as water.

  Q17. What is a 'manometer'?
    [ ] A sealed tube used to measure temperature
    [ ] A device that measures fluid viscosity
    [ ] A device that measures fluid flow rate
    [*] A U-shaped tube device used to measure pressure differences
    Explanation: A manometer is a U-shaped tube partially filled with a liquid (often mercury or water) used to measure the pressure difference between two points, typically between a gas and the atmosphere.

  Q18. What does 'viscosity' refer to in fluid mechanics?
    [ ] The surface tension of a fluid
    [ ] The buoyant force exerted by a fluid
    [ ] The ability of a fluid to compress under pressure
    [*] The internal friction or resistance of a fluid to flow
    Explanation: Viscosity is a measure of a fluid's internal resistance to flow. High-viscosity fluids like honey flow slowly, while low-viscosity fluids like water flow easily.

  Q19. What is meant by an 'incompressible fluid'?
    [ ] A fluid that cannot support shear stress
    [ ] A fluid that cannot be heated
    [ ] A fluid with zero viscosity
    [*] A fluid whose density remains essentially constant regardless of pressure changes
    Explanation: An incompressible fluid is one whose density does not change appreciably with pressure. Liquids like water are nearly incompressible, while gases are highly compressible.

  Q20. What does 'barometer' mean?
    [ ] An instrument that measures wind speed
    [*] An instrument that measures atmospheric pressure
    [ ] An instrument that measures temperature
    [ ] An instrument that measures fluid flow velocity
    Explanation: A barometer is an instrument used to measure atmospheric pressure. The mercury barometer, invented by Torricelli, uses the height of a mercury column to indicate pressure.

  Q21. A nurse connects an IV bag to a patient's arm. The bag is positioned 1.2 m above the insertion point, and the IV fluid has a density of 1020 kg/m³. What gauge pressure does the fluid exert at the needle tip? (g = 9.8 m/s²)

--- Lesson 1.2: Waves (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Waves
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A wave has a frequency of 500 Hz and a wavelength of 0.68 m. What is the wave speed?
Wave speed v = fλ = 500 × 0.68 = 340 m/s. This is the fundamental wave equation relating speed, frequency, and wavelength.
What is the period of a wave with a frequency of 250 Hz?
Period T = 1/f = 1/250 = 0.004 s. Period and frequency are reciprocals of each other.
What is 'amplitude' of a wave?
Amplitude is the maximum displacement from the equilibrium (rest) position. It determines the energy of the wave: energy is proportional to the square of the amplitude.
What is the 'principle of superposition'?
The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap in the same region, the net displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual wave displacements.
What is a 'standing wave'?
A standing wave results from the superposition of two identical waves traveling in opposite directions. It features nodes (zero displacement) and antinodes (maximum displacement) that appear stationary.
What is 'diffraction'?
Diffraction is the bending and spreading of waves when they encounter an obstacle or pass through an opening. It is most noticeable when the opening size is comparable to the wavelength.
What is the 'Doppler effect'?
The Doppler effect is the perceived change in frequency when a wave source and observer are in relative motion. Approaching produces a higher observed frequency; receding produces a lower one.
What is a 'longitudinal wave'?
In a longitudinal wave, particles oscillate back and forth parallel to the wave's direction of travel. Sound waves in air are longitudinal, producing alternating compressions and rarefactions.
A guitar string is plucked and vibrates at its fundamental frequency of 330 Hz. If the string is 0.65 m long and fixed at both ends, what is the speed of the wave on the string?
For the fundamental mode of a string fixed at both ends, λ = 2L = 2 × 0.65 = 1.30 m. Speed v = fλ = 330 × 1.30 = 429 m/s.
A physics student creates standing waves on a 2.0 m long string fixed at both ends. She observes 4 antinodes. What is the wavelength of the standing wave?
With 4 antinodes on a string fixed at both ends, there are 4 half-wavelengths: L = 4(λ/2), so λ = 2L/4 = 2(2.0)/4 = 1.0 m. This is the 4th harmonic.
A microwave oven operates at a frequency of 2.45 GHz. If microwaves travel at the speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s), what is the wavelength of the microwaves?
λ = v/f = 3 × 10⁸ / 2.45 × 10⁹ ≈ 0.122 m ≈ 12.2 cm. This wavelength explains why microwave ovens can have hot and cold spots separated by about 6 cm (half wavelength).
A boat is anchored in a lake and bobs up and down as waves pass. The boat completes 12 full oscillations in 1 minute, and the distance between successive crests is 8 m. What is the speed of the water waves?
Frequency f = 12/60 = 0.2 Hz. Wavelength λ = 8 m. Wave speed v = fλ = 0.2 × 8 = 1.6 m/s.
Important Concepts
A wave has a frequency of 500 Hz and a wavelength of 0.68 m. What is the wave speed
Wave speed v = fλ = 500 × 0.68 = 340 m/s. This is the fundamental wave equation relating speed, frequency, and wavelength.
What happens to the frequency of a wave when it passes from one medium to another
When a wave crosses a boundary between media, its frequency stays constant. The speed and wavelength change, but frequency is determined by the source and does not change.
What is the period of a wave with a frequency of 250 Hz
Period T = 1/f = 1/250 = 0.004 s. Period and frequency are reciprocals of each other.
If you double the frequency of a wave while the wave speed remains constant, the wavelength will:
Since v = fλ and v is constant, doubling f means λ must be halved. Frequency and wavelength are inversely proportional when speed is fixed.
A guitar string is plucked and vibrates at its fundamental frequency of 330 Hz. If the string is 0.65 m long and fixed at both ends, what is the speed of the wave on the string
For the fundamental mode of a string fixed at both ends, λ = 2L = 2 × 0.65 = 1.30 m. Speed v = fλ = 330 × 1.30 = 429 m/s.
An ambulance approaches you at 30 m/s with its siren emitting a frequency of 700 Hz. If the speed of sound is 340 m/s, what frequency do you hear
Using the Doppler equation for an approaching source: f_obs = f_s × v/(v − v_s) = 700 × 340/(340 − 30) = 700 × 340/310 ≈ 768 Hz.
A microwave oven operates at a frequency of 2.45 GHz. If microwaves travel at the speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s), what is the wavelength of the microwaves
λ = v/f = 3 × 10⁸ / 2.45 × 10⁹ ≈ 0.122 m ≈ 12.2 cm. This wavelength explains why microwave ovens can have hot and cold spots separated by about 6 cm (half wavelength).
Two speakers placed 3 m apart emit identical sound waves at the same frequency and in phase. A listener walks along a line parallel to the speakers and notices alternating loud and quiet spots. Which phenomenon explains this observation
The alternating loud (constructive interference) and quiet (destructive interference) spots result from two-source interference. The path length difference from the two speakers determines whether waves add or cancel at each location.

--- Lesson 1.2: Waves (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What type of wave requires a medium to travel through?
    [ ] Radio wave
    [ ] Electromagnetic wave
    [ ] Light wave
    [*] Mechanical wave
    Explanation: Mechanical waves (such as sound, water, and seismic waves) require a material medium to propagate. Electromagnetic waves can travel through a vacuum.

    [ ] They do not move at all
    [ ] Parallel to the wave direction
    [*] Perpendicular to the wave direction
    [ ] In circular paths
    Explanation: In a transverse wave, the medium's particles oscillate perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave propagates, such as waves on a string.

  Q3. A wave has a frequency of 500 Hz and a wavelength of 0.68 m. What is the wave speed?
    [ ] 170 m/s
    [ ] 0.00136 m/s
    [ ] 735 m/s
    [*] 340 m/s
    Explanation: Wave speed v = fλ = 500 × 0.68 = 340 m/s. This is the fundamental wave equation relating speed, frequency, and wavelength.

  Q4. What happens to the frequency of a wave when it passes from one medium to another?
    [ ] It doubles
    [*] It remains the same
    [ ] It increases
    [ ] It decreases
    Explanation: When a wave crosses a boundary between media, its frequency stays constant. The speed and wavelength change, but frequency is determined by the source and does not change.

  Q5. What is the period of a wave with a frequency of 250 Hz?
    [ ] 0.25 s
    [ ] 0.04 s
    [*] 0.004 s
    [ ] 250 s
    Explanation: Period T = 1/f = 1/250 = 0.004 s. Period and frequency are reciprocals of each other.

  Q6. When two wave pulses of equal amplitude on the same side meet from opposite directions, they produce:
    [ ] A standing wave permanently
    [ ] Destructive interference with zero amplitude
    [*] Constructive interference with doubled amplitude
    [ ] No change in amplitude
    Explanation: When two pulses on the same side overlap, they undergo constructive interference. By the principle of superposition, the amplitudes add, producing a combined amplitude twice the original.

    [ ] It reflects with doubled amplitude
    [ ] It reflects with no change
    [ ] It is completely absorbed
    [*] It reflects inverted (phase shift of 180°)
    Explanation: When a wave pulse reflects off a fixed boundary, it inverts (undergoes a 180° phase change). At a free end, the pulse reflects without inversion.

  Q8. The speed of a wave on a string depends on:
    [ ] Frequency and amplitude
    [ ] Only the wavelength
    [ ] Only the amplitude
    [*] Tension and linear mass density of the string
    Explanation: The speed of a wave on a string is v = sqrt(T/μ), where T is the tension and μ is the linear mass density. Greater tension or lower mass density increases wave speed.

  Q9. If you double the frequency of a wave while the wave speed remains constant, the wavelength will:
    [ ] Double
    [*] Be halved
    [ ] Quadruple
    [ ] Stay the same
    Explanation: Since v = fλ and v is constant, doubling f means λ must be halved. Frequency and wavelength are inversely proportional when speed is fixed.

  Q10. In a longitudinal wave, regions where particles are pushed close together are called:
    [ ] Crests
    [ ] Troughs
    [*] Compressions
    [ ] Nodes
    Explanation: In longitudinal waves, compressions are regions of high density where particles are pushed together. Rarefactions are regions where particles are spread apart.

  Q11. What is 'amplitude' of a wave?
    [ ] The number of cycles per second
    [ ] The distance between two consecutive crests
    [*] The maximum displacement of a particle from its equilibrium position
    [ ] The speed at which the wave travels
    Explanation: Amplitude is the maximum displacement from the equilibrium (rest) position. It determines the energy of the wave: energy is proportional to the square of the amplitude.

  Q12. What does 'wavelength' mean?
    [ ] The number of waves passing a point per second
    [ ] The time for one complete oscillation
    [*] The distance between two consecutive points in phase on a wave
    [ ] The maximum height of a wave
    Explanation: Wavelength (λ) is the spatial distance between two consecutive points that are in phase (e.g., crest to crest or compression to compression). It is measured in meters.

  Q13. What is the 'principle of superposition'?
    [ ] Waves speed up when they overlap
    [ ] Only one wave can exist in a medium at a time
    [ ] Waves always cancel each other when they meet
    [*] When two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement equals the sum of individual displacements
    Explanation: The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap in the same region, the net displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual wave displacements.

    [ ] A wave that does not carry energy under most circumstances involving displacement
    [ ] A wave that travels in only one direction without accounting for additional influences
    [*] A pattern formed by the interference of two waves traveling in opposite directions with the same frequency and amplitude
    [ ] A wave with zero amplitude everywhere in most scenarios involving displacement factors
    Explanation: A standing wave results from the superposition of two identical waves traveling in opposite directions. It features nodes (zero displacement) and antinodes (maximum displacement) that appear stationary.

    [ ] A point of maximum displacement
    [*] A point of zero displacement that remains stationary
    [ ] The midpoint of a wave
    [ ] A point where the wave speed is maximum
    Explanation: A node is a point in a standing wave where destructive interference causes the displacement to always be zero. Nodes are equally spaced half a wavelength apart.

  Q16. What is 'diffraction'?
    [ ] The splitting of light into colors
    [ ] The bouncing of waves off a barrier
    [*] The bending and spreading of waves as they pass through an opening or around an obstacle
    [ ] The change in frequency due to relative motion
    Explanation: Diffraction is the bending and spreading of waves when they encounter an obstacle or pass through an opening. It is most noticeable when the opening size is comparable to the wavelength.

  Q17. What does 'refraction' refer to in wave behavior?
    [ ] The absorption of a wave by a medium
    [ ] The reflection of a wave at a boundary
    [*] The change in direction of a wave as it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed
    [ ] The splitting of a wave into two separate waves
    Explanation: Refraction is the bending of a wave's path when it enters a new medium at an angle, caused by a change in wave speed. The frequency stays constant while speed and wavelength change.

  Q18. What is the 'Doppler effect'?
    [ ] The bending of waves around obstacles
    [ ] The increase in wave amplitude due to resonance
    [ ] The loss of wave energy over distance
    [*] The change in observed frequency of a wave due to relative motion between the source and observer
    Explanation: The Doppler effect is the perceived change in frequency when a wave source and observer are in relative motion. Approaching produces a higher observed frequency; receding produces a lower one.

  Q19. What does 'resonance' mean?
    [*] The large-amplitude oscillation that occurs when a system is driven at its natural frequency
    [ ] The complete absorption of wave energy
    [ ] The gradual decrease in amplitude over time
    [ ] The cancellation of two waves of different frequencies
    Explanation: Resonance occurs when a periodic driving force matches the natural frequency of a system, causing energy to build up and producing large-amplitude oscillations.

  Q20. What is a 'longitudinal wave'?
    [ ] A wave that can only travel through solids
    [*] A wave in which particles oscillate parallel to the direction of propagation
    [ ] A wave with no measurable wavelength
    [ ] A wave in which particles oscillate perpendicular to the direction of propagation
    Explanation: In a longitudinal wave, particles oscillate back and forth parallel to the wave's direction of travel. Sound waves in air are longitudinal, producing alternating compressions and rarefactions.

--- Lesson 1.3: Sound (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sound
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A sound wave has a frequency of 440 Hz in air (v = 340 m/s). What is its wavelength?
λ = v/f = 340/440 ≈ 0.773 m ≈ 0.77 m. The note A4 (440 Hz) has a wavelength of about 77 cm in air.
When two tuning forks of slightly different frequencies are sounded together, what is heard?
When two tones of slightly different frequencies interfere, they produce beats — periodic fluctuations in loudness. The beat frequency equals the absolute difference of the two frequencies.
A closed pipe (closed at one end, open at the other) of length 0.5 m produces its fundamental frequency. If the speed of sound is 340 m/s, what is the fundamental frequency?
For a closed pipe, the fundamental wavelength is λ = 4L = 4 × 0.5 = 2.0 m. So f = v/λ = 340/2.0 = 170 Hz.
What is 'intensity' of a sound wave?
Sound intensity is the power (energy per second) transmitted through a unit area perpendicular to the wave's direction. It is measured in watts per square meter (W/m²).
What is the 'beat frequency'?
The beat frequency is |f₁ − f₂|, the absolute value of the difference between two simultaneously sounding frequencies. It represents the rate at which the loudness fluctuates.
What is 'ultrasound'?
Ultrasound refers to sound waves with frequencies above 20 kHz, beyond the range of human hearing. It is widely used in medical imaging, sonar, and industrial testing.
What is a 'harmonic' in the context of sound and vibration?
A harmonic is any frequency that is a whole-number multiple of the fundamental frequency. The 1st harmonic is the fundamental, the 2nd harmonic is twice the fundamental, and so on.
What is 'infrasound'?
Infrasound consists of sound waves below 20 Hz, which are too low-pitched for human ears to detect. Elephants and some natural phenomena (earthquakes, volcanoes) produce infrasound.
What is 'Mach number'?
Mach number is the ratio of an object's speed to the local speed of sound: M = v_object/v_sound. Mach 1 is the speed of sound, Mach 2 is twice the speed of sound, and so on.
A pipe organ in a church has an open pipe that is 3.4 m long. What is the fundamental frequency of this pipe if the speed of sound in the church is 340 m/s?
For an open pipe, f₁ = v/(2L) = 340/(2 × 3.4) = 340/6.8 = 50 Hz. This low fundamental frequency produces a deep bass tone.
A guitar tuner sounds a 440 Hz tuning fork alongside a guitar string. The player hears 3 beats per second. If the guitar string's frequency is known to be slightly sharp, what is the string's frequency?
Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂| = 3 Hz. Since the string is sharp (higher than the tuning fork), f_string = 440 + 3 = 443 Hz.
A doctor uses an ultrasound machine at 5 MHz to image a fetus. If the speed of sound in tissue is approximately 1540 m/s, what is the wavelength of the ultrasound in the tissue?
λ = v/f = 1540 / (5 × 10⁶) = 3.08 × 10⁻⁴ m = 0.308 mm. This short wavelength allows high-resolution imaging of small structures.
A student blows across the top of a test tube that is 17 cm long and closed at the bottom. What is the fundamental frequency of the sound produced? (v_sound = 340 m/s)
A test tube acts as a closed pipe. f₁ = v/(4L) = 340/(4 × 0.17) = 340/0.68 = 500 Hz.
A rock band performs at 110 dB near the stage. If the threshold of hearing corresponds to 0 dB (I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m²), what is the intensity of the sound at 110 dB?
β = 10 log(I/I₀), so 110 = 10 log(I/10⁻¹²). Therefore log(I/10⁻¹²) = 11, giving I = 10¹¹ × 10⁻¹² = 10⁻¹ W/m² = 0.1 W/m².
Two identical fire alarms are placed in a room. One alarm alone produces a sound level of 80 dB at a certain point. If both alarms sound simultaneously from the same location, what is the approximate sound level at that point?
Doubling the intensity adds about 3 dB: 10 log(2I/I₀) = 10 log(I/I₀) + 10 log(2) ≈ 80 + 3 = 83 dB. Decibels are logarithmic, so doubling intensity does not double the dB level.
Real-World Applications

λ = v/f = 1540 / (5 × 10⁶) = 3.08 × 10⁻⁴ m = 0.308 mm. This short wavelength allows high-resolution imaging of small structures.

  Key Concepts: Ideal Gas Behavior
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the value of the universal gas constant R in SI units?
The universal gas constant R = 8.314 J/(mol·K). The value 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K is Boltzmann's constant, and 6.022 × 10²³ is Avogadro's number.
A gas occupies 4 L at 300 K. If the gas is heated to 600 K at constant pressure, what is the new volume?
By Charles's law (V/T = constant at fixed P and n), V₂ = V₁ × T₂/T₁ = 4 × 600/300 = 8 L. Volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature.
What is the 'ideal gas law'?
The ideal gas law PV = nRT relates pressure (P), volume (V), number of moles (n), the gas constant (R = 8.314 J/(mol·K)), and absolute temperature (T in kelvins).
What is 'Boltzmann's constant'?
Boltzmann's constant k_B = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K bridges macroscopic and microscopic physics. It relates the average kinetic energy of a single molecule to temperature: KE = (3/2)k_BT.
What is meant by 'root-mean-square (rms) speed' of gas molecules?
The rms speed is v_rms = sqrt(3k_BT/m) = sqrt(3RT/M). It is a measure of the typical molecular speed and is slightly higher than both the average and most probable speeds.
What is an 'isothermal process'?
An isothermal process occurs at constant temperature. For an ideal gas undergoing an isothermal process, PV = constant (Boyle's law).
What is 'Dalton's law of partial pressures'?
Dalton's law states P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... where each partial pressure is the pressure that gas would exert if it alone occupied the entire volume at the same temperature.
What is an 'adiabatic process'?
An adiabatic process is one in which no heat (Q) is transferred between the system and its surroundings (Q = 0). Rapid compressions and expansions are approximately adiabatic.
A car tire contains air at a gauge pressure of 220 kPa and a temperature of 20°C (293 K). After a long drive, the temperature rises to 50°C (323 K). Assuming the tire volume is constant, what is the new gauge pressure? (P_atm = 101 kPa)
Absolute initial pressure = 220 + 101 = 321 kPa. At constant V: P₂ = P₁(T₂/T₁) = 321 × (323/293) ≈ 354 kPa. New gauge pressure = 354 − 101 ≈ 253 kPa.
A weather balloon is filled with helium to a volume of 1.0 m³ at sea level (P = 101 kPa, T = 300 K). As it rises, the external pressure drops to 30 kPa and the temperature falls to 220 K. What is the balloon's approximate volume at altitude?
Using the combined gas law: V₂ = V₁ × (P₁/P₂) × (T₂/T₁) = 1.0 × (101/30) × (220/300) = 1.0 × 3.37 × 0.733 ≈ 2.47 m³ ≈ 2.5 m³.
A sealed, rigid container holds 2 moles of an ideal gas at 400 K and 3 atm. If 3 additional moles of the same gas are pumped in at the same temperature, what is the new pressure?
At constant T and V, P is proportional to n. New total moles = 2 + 3 = 5. P₂ = P₁ × (n₂/n₁) = 3 × (5/2) = 7.5 atm.
In a laboratory, nitrogen gas (N₂, M = 28 g/mol) is at 300 K. What is the approximate rms speed of nitrogen molecules? (R = 8.314 J/(mol·K))
v_rms = sqrt(3RT/M) = sqrt(3 × 8.314 × 300 / 0.028) = sqrt(267,879) ≈ 517 m/s.
A hot air balloon works by heating the air inside the envelope to 100°C (373 K) while the outside air is at 20°C (293 K). If the balloon has a volume of 2800 m³ at the higher temperature, what is the mass of air displaced by the balloon compared to the mass of hot air inside? (Air density at 20°C ≈ 1.2 kg/m³)
At constant pressure, density is inversely proportional to temperature: ρ_hot/ρ_cold = T_cold/T_hot = 293/373 ≈ 0.785. The hot air is about 21.5% less dense, providing buoyant lift.
A chemist mixes 2 atm of oxygen and 3 atm of nitrogen in a rigid, sealed container at the same temperature. According to Dalton's law, what is the total pressure in the container?
By Dalton's law, P_total = P_O₂ + P_N₂ = 2 + 3 = 5 atm. Each gas contributes its partial pressure independently.
A syringe with no leaks contains 60 mL of gas at atmospheric pressure (100 kPa). A nurse pushes the plunger, reducing the volume to 20 mL at constant temperature. What is the gas pressure inside the syringe?
By Boyle's law: P₂ = P₁ × V₁/V₂ = 100 × 60/20 = 300 kPa. Compressing the gas to one-third its volume triples the pressure.
Real-World Applications
Example: The average translational kinetic energy of an ideal gas molecule depends on:
The average translational kinetic energy per molecule is KE = (3/2)k_BT, which depends only on absolute temperature T, not on the type of molecule, pressure, or volume.

--- Lesson 1.5: Entropy and Second Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Entropy and Second Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A heat engine operates between a hot reservoir at 600 K and a cold reservoir at 300 K. What is the maximum possible (Carnot) efficiency?
Carnot efficiency = 1 − T_cold/T_hot = 1 − 300/600 = 0.50 = 50%. No real engine can exceed this efficiency between these temperatures.
What is 'entropy'?
Entropy quantifies the disorder or randomness of a system. Thermodynamically, the change in entropy is ΔS = Q_rev/T. Systems tend to evolve toward states of higher entropy.
What is a 'Carnot engine'?
A Carnot engine is an idealized, reversible heat engine that achieves the maximum theoretical efficiency between two temperature reservoirs: η = 1 − T_cold/T_hot.
What is 'thermal equilibrium'?
Thermal equilibrium is the state where two bodies in thermal contact have the same temperature and there is no net transfer of heat between them.
What is a 'heat engine'?
A heat engine absorbs heat Q_H from a hot reservoir, converts part of it to useful work W, and rejects the remainder Q_C to a cold reservoir. Its efficiency is η = W/Q_H.
What is the 'Kelvin-Planck statement' of the second law?
The Kelvin-Planck statement says no cyclical process can extract heat from a single reservoir and convert it entirely to work. Some heat must always be rejected to a colder reservoir.
What is the 'Clausius statement' of the second law?
The Clausius statement says no process is possible whose sole result is the transfer of heat from a cooler body to a hotter body. Refrigerators accomplish this only by requiring work input.
A coal-fired power plant operates with steam at 550°C (823 K) and exhausts waste heat to a river at 25°C (298 K). What is the maximum theoretical efficiency of this plant?
Carnot efficiency = 1 − T_C/T_H = 1 − 298/823 ≈ 0.638 ≈ 64%. Real power plants achieve 30-40% due to irreversible losses.
A car engine operates between combustion gases at 1500 K and an exhaust temperature of 500 K. If the engine absorbs 4000 J of heat per cycle, what is the maximum work it can produce per cycle?
Carnot efficiency = 1 − 500/1500 = 2/3 ≈ 66.7%. Maximum work = 0.667 × 4000 ≈ 2667 J.
A homeowner uses a heat pump to warm a house at 22°C (295 K) when the outdoor temperature is 0°C (273 K). What is the maximum coefficient of performance (COP) of this heat pump?
For a heat pump, COP_HP = T_H/(T_H − T_C) = 295/(295 − 273) = 295/22 ≈ 13.4. This means for every joule of work, up to 13.4 joules of heat are delivered.
A geothermal power plant uses underground steam at 200°C (473 K) and rejects heat to the atmosphere at 30°C (303 K). If 1 MW of heat is extracted per second, what is the maximum electrical power output?
Carnot efficiency = 1 − 303/473 ≈ 0.359 = 35.9%. Maximum power = 0.359 × 1000 kW = 359 kW.
An engineer proposes building a heat engine between the warm surface water of a tropical ocean (30°C = 303 K) and cold deep water (5°C = 278 K). What is the maximum possible efficiency of such an engine?
Carnot efficiency = 1 − T_C/T_H = 1 − 278/303 ≈ 0.083 = 8.3%. The small temperature difference between reservoirs severely limits efficiency, a key challenge for ocean thermal energy conversion.

    [ ] Entropy measures the pressure of a system
    [ ] Entropy measures the temperature of a system
    Explanation: Statistically, entropy S = k_B ln(W), where W is the number of microstates. Systems naturally evolve toward macrostates with the largest number of microstates (highest probability).

  Q11. What is 'entropy'?
    [*] A measure of the disorder or number of possible microscopic configurations of a system
    [ ] A measure of the total heat in a system
    [ ] The work done by a system
    [ ] The internal energy of a gas
    Explanation: Entropy quantifies the disorder or randomness of a system. Thermodynamically, the change in entropy is ΔS = Q_rev/T. Systems tend to evolve toward states of higher entropy.

  Q12. What does the 'second law of thermodynamics' state?
    [ ] Internal energy is always conserved in any process
    [ ] At absolute zero, entropy equals zero
    [ ] Energy can be created but not destroyed
    [*] The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease over time
    Explanation: The second law states that the total entropy of an isolated system cannot decrease. Natural processes are irreversible and always increase the total entropy of the universe.

  Q13. What is a 'Carnot engine'?
    [ ] An engine that only uses gas as fuel within the broader context of engine analysis
    [ ] An engine that operates at 100% efficiency without accounting for additional influences
    [*] A theoretical engine operating in a reversible cycle between two temperatures, achieving the maximum possible efficiency
    [ ] An engine that violates the second law, which is a common assumption in the field
    Explanation: A Carnot engine is an idealized, reversible heat engine that achieves the maximum theoretical efficiency between two temperature reservoirs: η = 1 − T_cold/T_hot.

  Q14. What does 'irreversible process' mean in thermodynamics?
    [ ] A process where no energy is transferred
    [*] A process that cannot be reversed without leaving a net change in the entropy of the universe
    [ ] A process that cannot occur naturally
    [ ] A process that violates conservation of energy
    Explanation: An irreversible process increases the total entropy of the universe and cannot be undone without increasing entropy elsewhere. Nearly all real processes are irreversible.

  Q15. What is 'thermal equilibrium'?
    [ ] When two objects have the same mass
    [ ] When all motion in a system has stopped
    [ ] When pressure equals atmospheric pressure
    [*] When two objects in contact reach the same temperature and no net heat flows between them
    Explanation: Thermal equilibrium is the state where two bodies in thermal contact have the same temperature and there is no net transfer of heat between them.

  Q16. What is a 'heat engine'?
    [ ] A device that creates thermal energy from nothing
    [ ] A device that converts work entirely into heat
    [ ] Any device that produces heat
    [*] A device that converts heat from a hot reservoir partially into work while rejecting some heat to a cold reservoir
    Explanation: A heat engine absorbs heat Q_H from a hot reservoir, converts part of it to useful work W, and rejects the remainder Q_C to a cold reservoir. Its efficiency is η = W/Q_H.

  Q17. What does 'Carnot efficiency' represent?
    [ ] The ratio of heat rejected to heat absorbed
    [ ] The efficiency at which entropy remains constant
    [*] The maximum theoretical efficiency of a heat engine operating between two temperatures
    [ ] The actual efficiency of any real engine
    Explanation: Carnot efficiency η = 1 − T_C/T_H is the upper bound on efficiency for any engine operating between hot temperature T_H and cold temperature T_C (in kelvins). No real engine can exceed it.

  Q18. What is the 'Kelvin-Planck statement' of the second law?
    [ ] Entropy of a system is always zero at absolute zero
    [*] It is impossible to construct a cyclical heat engine that converts heat completely into work with no other effect
    [ ] Heat can flow from cold to hot without any work input
    [ ] Energy is always conserved in thermal processes
    Explanation: The Kelvin-Planck statement says no cyclical process can extract heat from a single reservoir and convert it entirely to work. Some heat must always be rejected to a colder reservoir.

  Q19. What is the 'Clausius statement' of the second law?
    [ ] All real processes are reversible
    [ ] Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold
    [ ] Entropy is always conserved
    [*] It is impossible for heat to flow from a cold body to a hot body without external work being done
    Explanation: The Clausius statement says no process is possible whose sole result is the transfer of heat from a cooler body to a hotter body. Refrigerators accomplish this only by requiring work input.

  Q20. What does 'microstate' mean in statistical thermodynamics?
    [ ] A very small thermodynamic system, based on conventional assumptions about microstate
    [ ] A single gas molecule, particularly when microstate considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    [*] A specific arrangement of all individual particles' positions and momenta consistent with the macroscopic properties of a system
    [ ] The smallest unit of temperature regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    Explanation: A microstate is one specific way all the particles in a system can be arranged (positions and velocities) that is consistent with the observed macroscopic state. Entropy increases with the number of accessible microstates.

  Q21. A coal-fired power plant operates with steam at 550°C (823 K) and exhausts waste heat to a river at 25°C (298 K). What is the maximum theoretical efficiency of this plant?
    [ ] About 50%
    [*] About 64%
    [ ] About 36%
    [ ] About 95%
    Explanation: Carnot efficiency = 1 − T_C/T_H = 1 − 298/823 ≈ 0.638 ≈ 64%. Real power plants achieve 30-40% due to irreversible losses.

  Q22. A student drops a ball from 2 meters. It bounces several times, each time reaching a lower height, until it stops. Where did the ball's initial potential energy go, and how does entropy change?
    [ ] Energy was stored in the ball as elastic potential energy; entropy stayed constant
    [ ] Energy was radiated as light; entropy decreased
    [ ] Energy was destroyed; entropy decreased
    [*] Energy was converted to thermal energy of the ball and floor; entropy increased
    Explanation: With each bounce, kinetic energy is converted to thermal energy (heat) through inelastic deformation. The energy spreads among many molecules, increasing the number of microstates and thus entropy.

  Q23. A car engine operates between combustion gases at 1500 K and an exhaust temperature of 500 K. If the engine absorbs 4000 J of heat per cycle, what is the maximum work it can produce per cycle?
    [*] About 2667 J
    [ ] About 4000 J
    [ ] About 3000 J
    [ ] About 1333 J
    Explanation: Carnot efficiency = 1 − 500/1500 = 2/3 ≈ 66.7%. Maximum work = 0.667 × 4000 ≈ 2667 J.

  Q24. A homeowner uses a heat pump to warm a house at 22°C (295 K) when the outdoor temperature is 0°C (273 K). What is the maximum coefficient of performance (COP) of this heat pump?
    [ ] About 6.7
    [*] About 13.4
    [ ] About 1.08
    [ ] About 0.93
    Explanation: For a heat pump, COP_HP = T_H/(T_H − T_C) = 295/(295 − 273) = 295/22 ≈ 13.4. This means for every joule of work, up to 13.4 joules of heat are delivered.

  Q25. A cup of hot coffee (90°C) is left in a room at 20°C. Over time, the coffee cools to room temperature. Which statement about entropy is correct?
    [ ] Both the coffee and room lose entropy
    [ ] The total entropy of the system remains unchanged
    [*] The entropy of the coffee decreases, but the total entropy of the system (coffee + room) increases
    [ ] The entropy of the coffee increases, and the entropy of the room decreases by the same amount
    Explanation: As the coffee cools, it loses entropy (ΔS = Q/T at high T is small per joule lost). The room gains entropy (ΔS = Q/T at lower T is larger per joule gained). The net effect is an increase in total entropy.

  Q26. A perpetual motion machine of the second kind claims to continuously convert ocean thermal energy entirely into electricity with no waste heat. Why is this impossible?
    [ ] Because electricity cannot be generated from heat
    [ ] Because the first law prevents it
    [*] Because it violates the Kelvin-Planck statement of the second law
    [ ] Because oceans do not contain enough energy
    Explanation: A device that completely converts heat from a single reservoir to work with no other effect violates the Kelvin-Planck statement. Some heat must be rejected to a cooler reservoir.

  Q27. A geothermal power plant uses underground steam at 200°C (473 K) and rejects heat to the atmosphere at 30°C (303 K). If 1 MW of heat is extracted per second, what is the maximum electrical power output?
    [*] About 359 kW
    [ ] About 170 kW
    [ ] About 1000 kW
    [ ] About 640 kW
    Explanation: Carnot efficiency = 1 − 303/473 ≈ 0.359 = 35.9%. Maximum power = 0.359 × 1000 kW = 359 kW.

  Q28. A deck of cards is initially arranged in perfect order (all suits sorted, ascending). The deck is shuffled repeatedly. In terms of entropy, this process illustrates that:
    [*] Entropy increases because the number of possible disordered arrangements vastly exceeds the number of ordered ones
    [ ] Entropy only applies to gases, not cards
    [ ] Entropy decreases because the deck becomes more random
    [ ] Entropy stays the same because no heat is involved
    Explanation: The ordered arrangement is a single microstate among approximately 8 × 10⁶⁷ possible arrangements. Shuffling overwhelmingly leads to disordered states because there are vastly more of them — a statistical illustration of entropy increase.

  Q29. An engineer proposes building a heat engine between the warm surface water of a tropical ocean (30°C = 303 K) and cold deep water (5°C = 278 K). What is the maximum possible efficiency of such an engine?
    [*] About 8.3%
    [ ] About 25%
    [ ] About 50%
    [ ] About 83%
    Explanation: Carnot efficiency = 1 − T_C/T_H = 1 − 278/303 ≈ 0.083 = 8.3%. The small temperature difference between reservoirs severely limits efficiency, a key challenge for ocean thermal energy conversion.

  Q30. A biological cell maintains internal order (low entropy) by consuming food and releasing waste heat. Does this violate the second law of thermodynamics?
    [*] No, because the cell is not an isolated system and the entropy of its surroundings increases by more than the cell's entropy decreases
    [ ] Yes, because living systems do not obey thermodynamic laws
    [ ] Yes, because entropy must always increase locally
    [ ] No, because biological processes do not involve entropy
    Explanation: Living cells decrease their local entropy by using energy from food, but they release heat and waste to the environment, increasing the surroundings' entropy by a greater amount. The total entropy of the universe still increases.

  1. Q: Define: Thermodynamics
     A: The study of heat, energy, and the relationships between thermal, mechanical, and other forms of energy

  2. Q: Define: Entropy
     A: A measure of disorder in a system; the second law states that entropy in an isolated system always increases

  3. Q: Define: Electric Field
     A: A region of space around a charged object where another charged object experiences a force

  4. Q: Define: Magnetic Field
     A: A region of space around a magnet or moving charge where magnetic forces are exerted

  5. Q: Define: Electromagnetic Wave
     A: A wave that consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation

  6. Q: Define: Capacitor
     A: A device that stores electrical energy by accumulating electric charge on two conducting plates

  7. Q: Define: Resistance
     A: The opposition to the flow of electric current through a material, measured in ohms

  8. Q: Define: Interference
     A: The phenomenon where two or more waves overlap and combine to create a resultant wave

  9. Q: Which of the following is the primary way water returns to the atmosphere?
     A: Evaporation from oceans and transpiration from plants

  10. Q: Which human activity most directly affects the water cycle?
     A: Urbanization reduces infiltration and increases runoff
