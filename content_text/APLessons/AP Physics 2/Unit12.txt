--- Lesson 12.1: Current, Resistance, and Ohm's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Current, Resistance, and Ohm's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'electric current'?
Current measures charge flow rate. 1 ampere = 1 coulomb per second passing a cross-section of the conductor.
What is 'Ohm's law'?
Ohm's law V = IR applies to materials with constant resistance. The slope of V vs I graph is R.
What is 'electrical resistance'?
Resistance R = V/I arises from collisions between charge carriers and the lattice. R = rho*L/A for a uniform wire.
What is 'resistivity'?
Resistivity rho depends on material and temperature. Copper: 1.7e-8 ohm*m. Glass: ~10^12 ohm*m.
What is 'drift velocity'?
v_d = I/(nAe), where n is carrier density. Drift velocity is small (~mm/s) but the field signal propagates near c.
What is 'Joule heating'?
Joule heating (I^2R losses) occurs in all resistive elements. It is the principle behind electric heaters, toasters, and incandescent bulbs.
What is 'electromotive force' (EMF)?
Despite its name, EMF is not a force but a voltage. EMF = V_terminal + I*r_internal for a battery.
What is an 'ohmic material'?
Ohmic materials (metals at constant temperature) have linear V-I graphs. Non-ohmic materials (diodes, LEDs) have nonlinear behavior.
What is 'conductivity'?
Conductivity sigma = 1/rho. Copper has high conductivity (~5.9e7 S/m). Units: siemens per meter (S/m).
What is 'internal resistance'?
V_terminal = EMF - I*r, where r is internal resistance. Larger current means more voltage drop across the internal resistance.
A 100 W light bulb operates at 120 V. What is its resistance?
P = V^2/R. R = V^2/P = 14400/100 = 144 ohms.
A copper wire (rho = 1.7 x 10^-8 ohm*m) is 100 m long and has diameter 2 mm. What is its resistance?
A = pi*r^2 = pi*(0.001)^2 = 3.14e-6 m^2. R = rho*L/A = 1.7e-8 * 100 / 3.14e-6 = 0.54 ohms.
A nichrome wire has resistance 10 ohms at 20 C. If the temperature coefficient is 0.0004/C, what is its resistance at 520 C?
R = R_0(1 + alpha*Delta T) = 10(1 + 0.0004*500) = 10(1.2) = 12 ohms.
A car battery (12 V, internal resistance 0.04 ohm) must deliver 200 A to the starter motor. What is the terminal voltage?
V = EMF - Ir = 12 - 200(0.04) = 12 - 8 = 4 V. The large current causes a significant voltage drop.
A household circuit breaker trips at 15 A on a 120 V line. What is the maximum power that can be drawn?
P_max = V*I_max = 120*15 = 1800 W. Drawing more than 1800 W trips the breaker.
Real-World Applications
Example: If the resistance of a wire doubles while voltage stays constant, the current:
I = V/R. If R doubles with V constant, I = V/(2R) = half the original current.
Example: A car battery (12 V, internal resistance 0.04 ohm) must deliver 200 A to the starter motor. What is the terminal voltage?
V = EMF - Ir = 12 - 200(0.04) = 12 - 8 = 4 V. The large current causes a significant voltage drop.

--- Lesson 12.2: Series and Parallel Circuits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Series and Parallel Circuits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'series circuit'?
In series, components share the same current. The total voltage is divided among the components according to their resistances.
What is a 'parallel circuit'?
Parallel components share the same voltage. Total current splits among branches, with more current through lower resistance.
What is a 'voltage divider'?
Voltage dividers are widely used to create reference voltages and to reduce voltage levels in circuits.
What is 'equivalent resistance'?
Equivalent resistance simplifies circuit analysis. It draws the same current from a source as the original combination.
What is a 'current divider'?
In parallel, current divides inversely with resistance. More current flows through the path of least resistance.
What is a 'short circuit'?
A short circuit bypasses the intended load with near-zero resistance, causing very large current. Fuses and breakers protect against this.
What is an 'open circuit'?
An open circuit has a break: I = 0. The full source voltage appears across the break.
What is a 'Wheatstone bridge'?
When balanced (R1/R2 = R3/R4), no current flows through the galvanometer. Used for precision resistance measurement.
What is 'power distribution' in a circuit?
Total power = sum of individual powers. In series: larger R gets more power. In parallel: smaller R gets more power.
What is a 'combination circuit'?
Most real circuits combine series and parallel elements. Analysis involves simplifying step by step.
Three resistors (4 ohm, 6 ohm, 12 ohm) are connected in parallel across a 12 V battery. What is the total current from the battery?
1/R = 1/4 + 1/6 + 1/12 = 3/12 + 2/12 + 1/12 = 6/12 = 1/2. R = 2 ohms. I = 12/2 = 6 A.
A 10 ohm and a 15 ohm resistor are in series, and this combination is in parallel with a 10 ohm resistor across a 30 V source. What is the total current?
Series: 10+15=25 ohms. Parallel with 10: R_eq = 25*10/(25+10) = 250/35 = 7.14 ohms. I = 30/7.14 = 4.2 A.
In a series circuit with a 9 V battery and resistors of 100 ohm and 200 ohm, what is the voltage across the 200 ohm resistor?
V = 9 * 200/(100+200) = 9 * 2/3 = 6 V (voltage divider).
Five identical 10 ohm resistors are connected: two in series forming a 20 ohm branch, and three in parallel (each 10 ohm) forming another branch. These two branches are in parallel. What is the total resistance?
Three in parallel: R = 10/3 = 3.33 ohms. Two in series: 20 ohms. Parallel combination: R = 20*3.33/(20+3.33) = 66.6/23.33 = 2.86 ohms.
A string of 20 identical holiday lights is connected in series across 120 V. What is the voltage across each bulb?
In series, voltage divides equally for identical resistances: V_each = 120/20 = 6 V.
A 2 ohm, 3 ohm, and 6 ohm resistor are in parallel. A 4 ohm resistor is in series with this parallel combination. What is the total resistance?
Parallel: 1/R = 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/6 = 6/6 = 1. R_parallel = 1 ohm. Total = 1 + 4 = 5 ohms.

Parallel branches connect at the same two nodes, so they share the same voltage.
Example: A voltage divider uses:
V_out = V_in * R2/(R1+R2). The output voltage is a fraction determined by the resistance ratio.

Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In a closed loop with a 12 V battery and two resistors (4 ohm and 2 ohm in series), what is the current?
By Kirchhoff's loop rule: 12 V - I(4) - I(2) = 0, so 12 = 6I, giving I = 2 A.
What is Kirchhoff's junction rule (current law)?
Kirchhoff's junction rule states that at any junction (node) in a circuit, the sum of currents flowing in equals the sum of currents flowing out, reflecting conservation of charge.
What is Kirchhoff's loop rule (voltage law)?
Kirchhoff's loop rule states that the algebraic sum of all EMFs and voltage drops (IR) around any closed loop in a circuit must equal zero, based on conservation of energy.
What is a junction (node) in circuit analysis?
A junction (or node) is a point in a circuit where three or more conductors meet, allowing current to split into different branches or combine from different branches.
What is a branch in circuit analysis?
A branch is a path connecting two junctions that contains one or more circuit elements (resistors, batteries, etc.) in series. Each branch carries a single current value.
What is a loop in the context of Kirchhoff's analysis?
A loop is any closed path through a circuit that begins and ends at the same node. The loop rule is applied to these closed paths to generate equations relating voltages.
What is the sign convention for traversing a resistor against the direction of current in Kirchhoff's loop analysis?
When traversing a resistor against the assumed current direction, the potential increases by +IR. This is because you are moving from lower to higher potential across the resistor.
What is the relationship between the number of independent loop equations and the number of branches and junctions?
The number of independent loop equations needed is B - N + 1, where B is the number of branches and N is the number of junctions. This ensures a complete system of equations.
What is an EMF source in the context of Kirchhoff's laws?
An EMF (electromotive force) source is a device like a battery or generator that provides energy to charges, maintaining a potential difference to drive current through the circuit.
What is internal resistance in the context of a real battery analyzed with Kirchhoff's laws?
Internal resistance is the resistance within a real battery. It causes a voltage drop inside the battery, making the terminal voltage (V = EMF - Ir) less than the EMF when current flows.
What is a multi-loop circuit?
A multi-loop circuit contains multiple independent closed paths for current flow. These circuits require Kirchhoff's laws to solve because simple series/parallel reduction is insufficient.

--- Lesson 12.4: RC Circuits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: RC Circuits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the initial current when a 10 V battery is connected to an uncharged capacitor through a 1000-ohm resistor?
Initially, the uncharged capacitor acts like a short circuit (no voltage across it), so the initial current is I = V/R = 10/1000 = 0.01 A = 10 mA.
An RC circuit has R = 2 k-ohm and C = 5 microfarads. What is the time constant?
tau = RC = 2000 ohm * 5 * 10^-6 F = 0.01 s = 10 ms.
In a fully charged RC circuit, what is the current flowing through the circuit?
When the capacitor is fully charged, the voltage across it equals the battery voltage, so no potential difference drives current through the resistor, and the current is zero.
What is an RC circuit?
An RC circuit is an electric circuit composed of a resistor (R) and a capacitor (C) connected together, typically with a voltage source. It exhibits characteristic exponential charging and discharging behavior.
What is the time constant of an RC circuit?
The time constant tau = RC is a characteristic parameter of an RC circuit that determines how quickly the capacitor charges or discharges. It has units of seconds.
What is exponential decay in the context of RC circuits?
Exponential decay describes how quantities like charge, voltage, or current decrease in a discharging RC circuit. The rate of decrease is proportional to the current value, producing the characteristic e^(-t/RC) curve.
What is the half-life of an RC circuit?
The half-life of an RC circuit is the time for a discharging capacitor to lose half its charge (or voltage): t_half = RC * ln(2) = 0.693 * RC.
What is a charging curve in an RC circuit?
A charging curve shows how the charge (or voltage) on a capacitor increases exponentially toward its maximum value, following Q(t) = Q_max(1 - e^(-t/RC)). It rises steeply at first and then gradually levels off.
What is the discharging equation for voltage across a capacitor in an RC circuit?
During discharge, the voltage across a capacitor decreases exponentially: V(t) = V0 * e^(-t/RC), where V0 is the initial voltage and RC is the time constant.
What is the role of the resistor in an RC charging circuit?
The resistor in an RC circuit limits the current flowing to the capacitor, thereby controlling the rate of charging. A larger resistance means slower charging (larger time constant).
What is meant by the transient response of an RC circuit?
The transient response refers to the time-varying behavior of an RC circuit as it transitions from one state to another (e.g., from uncharged to charged). This transient period lasts approximately 5 time constants.
What is the energy stored in a charged capacitor in an RC circuit?
The energy stored in a capacitor is E = (1/2)CV^2 = (1/2)Q^2/C = (1/2)QV. This energy is stored in the electric field between the capacitor plates.
What is a DC steady state in an RC circuit?
DC steady state is the condition reached after sufficient time (~5 tau) where all transient behavior has died out. The capacitor acts as an open circuit (no current flows) and is either fully charged or fully discharged.
A defibrillator uses an RC circuit with C = 32 microfarads charged to 5000 V. It discharges through a patient with effective resistance of 50 ohm. What is the time constant of the discharge?
tau = RC = 50 ohm * 32 * 10^-6 F = 1.6 * 10^-3 s = 1.6 ms. This rapid discharge delivers energy quickly to the heart.
An RC circuit has R = 5 k-ohm and C = 2 microfarads. The capacitor is initially charged to 20 V and then discharged. What is the voltage across the capacitor at t = 10 ms?
tau = RC = 5000 * 2 * 10^-6 = 0.01 s = 10 ms. At t = tau: V = 20 * e^(-1) = 20 * 0.368 = 7.36 V.
A student connects a 12 V battery, a 500-ohm resistor, and a 200-microfarad capacitor in series. What is the maximum charge stored on the capacitor?
Maximum charge Q_max = CV = 200 * 10^-6 F * 12 V = 2.4 * 10^-3 C = 2.4 mC. The resistance only affects how fast it charges, not the maximum charge.
A capacitor in an RC circuit is charged to 100 V and begins discharging through a 20 k-ohm resistor. After 0.2 s, the voltage is 36.8 V. What is the capacitance?
36.8 V = 100 * e^(-0.2/RC). Since 36.8/100 = 0.368 = e^(-1), we know t = tau, so RC = 0.2 s. Therefore C = 0.2/20000 = 10 * 10^-6 F = 10 microfarads.
Real-World Applications
Example: During discharging of a capacitor, the voltage across the capacitor:
During discharge, the capacitor voltage decreases exponentially: V(t) = V0 * e^(-t/RC). It starts at V0 and asymptotically approaches zero.
Example: What is the discharging equation for voltage across a capacitor in an RC circuit?
During discharge, the voltage across a capacitor decreases exponentially: V(t) = V0 * e^(-t/RC), where V0 is the initial voltage and RC is the time constant.
Example: An RC circuit has R = 5 k-ohm and C = 2 microfarads. The capacitor is initially charged to 20 V and then discharged. What is the voltage across the capacitor at t = 10 ms?
tau = RC = 5000 * 2 * 10^-6 = 0.01 s = 10 ms. At t = tau: V = 20 * e^(-1) = 20 * 0.368 = 7.36 V.
Example: A capacitor in an RC circuit is charged to 100 V and begins discharging through a 20 k-ohm resistor. After 0.2 s, the voltage is 36.8 V. What is the capacitance?
36.8 V = 100 * e^(-0.2/RC). Since 36.8/100 = 0.368 = e^(-1), we know t = tau, so RC = 0.2 s. Therefore C = 0.2/20000 = 10 * 10^-6 F = 10 microfarads.
Example: A heart pacemaker uses an RC circuit to time its pulses. If the capacitor must reach 75% of the supply voltage before triggering, and tau = 1.2 s, at what time does the trigger occur?
V/V0 = 0.75 = 1 - e^(-t/tau). So e^(-t/tau) = 0.25, giving -t/tau = ln(0.25) = -1.386. Therefore t = 1.386 * 1.2 = 1.66 s.

  Q1. In an RC circuit, what does the time constant (tau) represent?
    [*] The time for the capacitor to reach about 63% of its maximum charge
    [ ] The time for the capacitor to fully charge
    [ ] The period of oscillation
    [ ] The time for all current to stop flowing
    Explanation: The time constant tau = RC is the time it takes for the capacitor to charge to approximately 63.2% (1 - 1/e) of its maximum charge during charging.

  Q2. The time constant of an RC circuit is calculated as:
    [ ] tau = C/R
    [ ] tau = R + C
    [ ] tau = R/C
    [*] tau = RC
    Explanation: The time constant for an RC circuit is tau = RC, where R is resistance in ohms and C is capacitance in farads. The result is in seconds.

  Q3. During the charging of a capacitor in an RC circuit, the current:
    [ ] Increases exponentially
    [ ] Remains constant
    [*] Decreases exponentially
    [ ] Oscillates sinusoidally
    Explanation: As a capacitor charges, the current decreases exponentially according to I = (V/R)e^(-t/RC). The current starts at maximum (V/R) and approaches zero.

  Q4. After approximately how many time constants is a capacitor considered fully charged (over 99%)?
    [ ] 2 time constants
    [ ] 3 time constants
    [ ] 1 time constant
    [*] 5 time constants
    Explanation: After 5 time constants (5 tau), the capacitor reaches approximately 99.3% of its maximum charge, which is practically considered fully charged.

  Q5. What is the initial current when a 10 V battery is connected to an uncharged capacitor through a 1000-ohm resistor?
    [ ] 10 A
    [ ] 0.1 A
    [*] 0.01 A
    [ ] 0 A
    Explanation: Initially, the uncharged capacitor acts like a short circuit (no voltage across it), so the initial current is I = V/R = 10/1000 = 0.01 A = 10 mA.

  Q6. During discharging of a capacitor, the voltage across the capacitor:
    [*] Decreases exponentially
    [ ] Drops immediately to zero
    [ ] Remains constant
    [ ] Increases linearly
    Explanation: During discharge, the capacitor voltage decreases exponentially: V(t) = V0 * e^(-t/RC). It starts at V0 and asymptotically approaches zero.

  Q7. An RC circuit has R = 2 k-ohm and C = 5 microfarads. What is the time constant?
    [ ] 100 ms
    [ ] 0.4 ms
    [ ] 10 s
    [*] 10 ms
    Explanation: tau = RC = 2000 ohm * 5 * 10^-6 F = 0.01 s = 10 ms.

  Q8. In a fully charged RC circuit, what is the current flowing through the circuit?
    [ ] Maximum (V/R)
    [ ] Half of maximum
    [ ] Depends on the capacitance
    [*] Zero
    Explanation: When the capacitor is fully charged, the voltage across it equals the battery voltage, so no potential difference drives current through the resistor, and the current is zero.

  Q9. Increasing the resistance in an RC charging circuit will:
    [ ] Not affect the charging time
    [ ] Decrease the time constant and charge faster
    [ ] Change the maximum charge on the capacitor
    [*] Increase the time constant and charge slower
    Explanation: Since tau = RC, increasing R increases the time constant, which means the capacitor takes longer to charge. The maximum charge (Q = CV) remains unchanged.

  Q10. The charge on a capacitor during charging in an RC circuit follows which equation?
    [ ] Q(t) = Q_max * e^(-t/RC)
    [ ] Q(t) = Q_max * t/RC
    [*] Q(t) = Q_max * (1 - e^(-t/RC))
    [ ] Q(t) = Q_max * sin(t/RC)
    Explanation: During charging, the charge builds up exponentially toward Q_max: Q(t) = Q_max(1 - e^(-t/RC)), where Q_max = CV.

  Q11. What is an RC circuit?
    [ ] A circuit with only resistors
    [*] A circuit containing a resistor and a capacitor, often with a voltage source
    [ ] A circuit with only capacitors
    [ ] A circuit with resistors and inductors
    Explanation: An RC circuit is an electric circuit composed of a resistor (R) and a capacitor (C) connected together, typically with a voltage source. It exhibits characteristic exponential charging and discharging behavior.

  Q12. What is the time constant of an RC circuit?
    [ ] The total time for the circuit to reach equilibrium, as conventional circuit theory would suggest
    [ ] The ratio of voltage to current in most scenarios involving circuit factors
    [*] The product of resistance and capacitance (tau = RC) that characterizes the rate of exponential charging or discharging
    [ ] The frequency of current oscillation, at least according to standard theory
    Explanation: The time constant tau = RC is a characteristic parameter of an RC circuit that determines how quickly the capacitor charges or discharges. It has units of seconds.

  Q13. What is exponential decay in the context of RC circuits?
    [ ] A sudden drop to zero under most circumstances involving current
    [ ] A linear decrease in voltage over time, even when conditions shift significantly
    [*] A process where a quantity decreases at a rate proportional to its current value, following e^(-t/RC)
    [ ] An oscillating decrease when considering all relevant current factors
    Explanation: Exponential decay describes how quantities like charge, voltage, or current decrease in a discharging RC circuit. The rate of decrease is proportional to the current value, producing the characteristic e^(-t/RC) curve.

  Q14. What is the half-life of an RC circuit?
    [ ] Half of the time constant
    [*] The time for the charge to reach 50% of maximum, equal to RC * ln(2)
    [ ] The time for current to reach half its value during charging
    [ ] The time for all charge to dissipate
    Explanation: The half-life of an RC circuit is the time for a discharging capacitor to lose half its charge (or voltage): t_half = RC * ln(2) = 0.693 * RC.

  Q15. What is a charging curve in an RC circuit?
    [ ] A parabolic curve of current vs. time, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [*] A graph showing the exponential rise of charge or voltage on the capacitor as it approaches its maximum value
    [ ] A straight line showing constant charge increase according to widely held views on this topic
    [ ] A sinusoidal waveform without accounting for additional influences
    Explanation: A charging curve shows how the charge (or voltage) on a capacitor increases exponentially toward its maximum value, following Q(t) = Q_max(1 - e^(-t/RC)). It rises steeply at first and then gradually levels off.

  Q16. What is the discharging equation for voltage across a capacitor in an RC circuit?
    [ ] V(t) = V0 * t/RC
    [ ] V(t) = V0 / (1 + t/RC)
    [*] V(t) = V0 * e^(-t/RC)
    [ ] V(t) = V0(1 - e^(-t/RC))
    Explanation: During discharge, the voltage across a capacitor decreases exponentially: V(t) = V0 * e^(-t/RC), where V0 is the initial voltage and RC is the time constant.

  Q17. What is the role of the resistor in an RC charging circuit?
    [ ] It stores energy in its electric field
    [ ] It converts AC to DC
    [*] It limits the current flow and controls the rate at which the capacitor charges
    [ ] It increases the maximum charge on the capacitor
    Explanation: The resistor in an RC circuit limits the current flowing to the capacitor, thereby controlling the rate of charging. A larger resistance means slower charging (larger time constant).

  Q18. What is meant by the transient response of an RC circuit?
    [ ] The steady-state behavior after the capacitor is fully charged
    [ ] The oscillating behavior of the circuit
    [ ] The behavior when both R and C are zero
    [*] The temporary, time-dependent behavior of voltage and current as the circuit transitions between states
    Explanation: The transient response refers to the time-varying behavior of an RC circuit as it transitions from one state to another (e.g., from uncharged to charged). This transient period lasts approximately 5 time constants.

  Q19. What is the energy stored in a charged capacitor in an RC circuit?
    [*] E = (1/2)CV^2
    [ ] E = CV
    [ ] E = (1/2)RV^2
    [ ] E = QV
    Explanation: The energy stored in a capacitor is E = (1/2)CV^2 = (1/2)Q^2/C = (1/2)QV. This energy is stored in the electric field between the capacitor plates.

  Q20. What is a DC steady state in an RC circuit?
    [ ] The initial moment when current starts flowing
    [ ] When the resistor reaches maximum temperature
    [*] The condition reached after many time constants where current stops and the capacitor is fully charged or discharged
    [ ] When the circuit oscillates at a constant frequency
    Explanation: DC steady state is the condition reached after sufficient time (~5 tau) where all transient behavior has died out. The capacitor acts as an open circuit (no current flows) and is either fully charged or fully discharged.

  Q21. A defibrillator uses an RC circuit with C = 32 microfarads charged to 5000 V. It discharges through a patient with effective resistance of 50 ohm. What is the time constant of the discharge?
    [ ] 16 ms
    [ ] 160 ms
    [ ] 0.16 ms
    [*] 1.6 ms
    Explanation: tau = RC = 50 ohm * 32 * 10^-6 F = 1.6 * 10^-3 s = 1.6 ms. This rapid discharge delivers energy quickly to the heart.

  Q22. A camera flash uses an RC circuit with R = 10 k-ohm and C = 100 microfarads charged to 300 V. How long does it take the capacitor to charge to approximately 190 V (about 63%)?
    [ ] 0.01 s
    [ ] 0.1 s
    [ ] 10 s
    [*] 1.0 s
    Explanation: The capacitor reaches 63% of its maximum voltage in one time constant: tau = RC = 10,000 * 100 * 10^-6 = 1.0 s. So 63% of 300 V ≈ 189 V is reached at t = 1.0 s.

  Q23. An RC circuit has R = 5 k-ohm and C = 2 microfarads. The capacitor is initially charged to 20 V and then discharged. What is the voltage across the capacitor at t = 10 ms?
    [ ] 0 V
    [ ] 20 V
    [*] 7.36 V
    [ ] 12.64 V
    Explanation: tau = RC = 5000 * 2 * 10^-6 = 0.01 s = 10 ms. At t = tau: V = 20 * e^(-1) = 20 * 0.368 = 7.36 V.

  Q24. A student connects a 12 V battery, a 500-ohm resistor, and a 200-microfarad capacitor in series. What is the maximum charge stored on the capacitor?
    [*] 2.4 mC
    [ ] 0.024 C
    [ ] 6.0 mC
    [ ] 0.6 mC
    Explanation: Maximum charge Q_max = CV = 200 * 10^-6 F * 12 V = 2.4 * 10^-3 C = 2.4 mC. The resistance only affects how fast it charges, not the maximum charge.

  Q25. An engineer designs a timing circuit using an RC combination. If the desired time constant is 0.5 s and the available capacitor is 10 microfarads, what resistance is needed?
    [*] 50 k-ohm
    [ ] 5 k-ohm
    [ ] 500 k-ohm
    [ ] 500 ohm
    Explanation: tau = RC, so R = tau/C = 0.5 / (10 * 10^-6) = 50,000 ohm = 50 k-ohm.

  Q26. A capacitor in an RC circuit is charged to 100 V and begins discharging through a 20 k-ohm resistor. After 0.2 s, the voltage is 36.8 V. What is the capacitance?
    [ ] 5 microfarads
    [*] 10 microfarads
    [ ] 20 microfarads
    [ ] 1 microfarad
    Explanation: 36.8 V = 100 * e^(-0.2/RC). Since 36.8/100 = 0.368 = e^(-1), we know t = tau, so RC = 0.2 s. Therefore C = 0.2/20000 = 10 * 10^-6 F = 10 microfarads.

  Q27. In a lab experiment, a student charges a 47-microfarad capacitor through a 100 k-ohm resistor with a 9 V battery. How long must the student wait for the capacitor to be practically fully charged (99%)?
    [ ] 9.4 s
    [*] 23.5 s
    [ ] 47 s
    [ ] 4.7 s
    Explanation: tau = RC = 100,000 * 47 * 10^-6 = 4.7 s. Full charge (99%) takes approximately 5 tau = 5 * 4.7 = 23.5 s.

  Q28. A heart pacemaker uses an RC circuit to time its pulses. If the capacitor must reach 75% of the supply voltage before triggering, and tau = 1.2 s, at what time does the trigger occur?
    [ ] 1.2 s
    [ ] 2.4 s
    [*] 1.66 s
    [ ] 0.83 s
    Explanation: V/V0 = 0.75 = 1 - e^(-t/tau). So e^(-t/tau) = 0.25, giving -t/tau = ln(0.25) = -1.386. Therefore t = 1.386 * 1.2 = 1.66 s.

  Q29. Two RC circuits have the same capacitor (C = 50 microfarads) but different resistors: Circuit A has R = 1 k-ohm and Circuit B has R = 10 k-ohm. Both charge from the same battery. After one time constant of Circuit A, what fraction of maximum charge has Circuit B reached?
    [*] 9.5%
    [ ] 36.8%
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 63.2%
    Explanation: tau_A = 1000 * 50e-6 = 0.05 s. tau_B = 10000 * 50e-6 = 0.5 s. At t = 0.05 s, Circuit B has charged to 1 - e^(-0.05/0.5) = 1 - e^(-0.1) = 1 - 0.905 = 0.095 = 9.5%.

  Q30. A sensor circuit uses an RC network where the capacitor charges to 8 V (from a 12 V supply) and triggers an alarm. If R = 220 k-ohm and C = 4.7 microfarads, how long after the switch closes does the alarm trigger?
    [ ] 0.52 s
    [*] 1.14 s
    [ ] 2.06 s
    [ ] 1.03 s
    Explanation: tau = RC = 220000 * 4.7e-6 = 1.034 s. At trigger: 8 = 12(1 - e^(-t/1.034)), so e^(-t/1.034) = 1/3, giving t = 1.034 * ln(3) = 1.034 * 1.099 = 1.14 s.
