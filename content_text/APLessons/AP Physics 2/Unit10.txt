--- Lesson 10.1: Fluid Properties and Pressure (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fluid Properties and Pressure
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'hydrostatic pressure'?
Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure in a fluid at rest due to gravity. It increases linearly with depth: P = rho*g*h.
What is 'density'?
Density rho = m/V characterizes how much mass is packed into a given volume. Water: 1000 kg/m^3, air: 1.2 kg/m^3.
What is 'gauge pressure'?
Gauge pressure is pressure above atmospheric. A flat tire reads 0 gauge pressure but has 1 atm absolute pressure.
What is a 'pascal'?
The pascal (Pa) honors Blaise Pascal. 1 atm = 101,325 Pa. Common multiples: kPa, MPa.
What is 'specific gravity'?
Specific gravity = rho_substance/rho_water. It is dimensionless. SG < 1 means the substance floats in water.
What is a 'barometer'?
A mercury barometer balances atmospheric pressure against a mercury column. At 1 atm, the column is 760 mm (29.92 inches).
What is 'viscosity'?
Viscosity measures internal friction in a fluid. High viscosity fluids (honey) resist flow; low viscosity fluids (water) flow easily.
What is 'surface tension'?
Surface tension results from cohesive forces that create a 'skin' on the liquid surface. It allows insects to walk on water.
What is an 'incompressible fluid'?
Liquids are approximately incompressible: their density changes negligibly with pressure. This simplification is used in hydrostatics.
What is 'absolute pressure'?
Absolute pressure includes atmospheric pressure. It is always positive: P_abs = P_atm + rho*g*h for a fluid.
A diver is 30 m below the surface of a freshwater lake (rho = 1000 kg/m^3). What is the absolute pressure at this depth?
P = P_atm + rho*g*h = 101325 + 1000(9.8)(30) = 101325 + 294000 = 395325 Pa = 3.95 x 10^5 Pa.
A U-tube manometer contains mercury (rho = 13600 kg/m^3). One side is open and the other is connected to a gas. The mercury is 25 cm higher on the open side. What is the gauge pressure of the gas?
P_gauge = rho*g*h = 13600 * 9.8 * 0.25 = 33320 Pa.
A rectangular tank is 2 m long, 1 m wide, and filled with water to a depth of 3 m. What is the total force on the bottom of the tank?
P_gauge at bottom = rho*g*h = 1000(9.8)(3) = 29400 Pa. F = P*A = 29400 * 2 * 1 = 58800 N.
The Mariana Trench is about 11,000 m deep. Assuming seawater density of 1025 kg/m^3, what is the approximate gauge pressure at the bottom?
P = rho*g*h = 1025 * 9.8 * 11000 = 1.10 x 10^8 Pa = about 1086 atm.
A blood pressure reading is 120/80 mmHg. What is the systolic pressure in pascals? (rho_Hg = 13600 kg/m^3)
P = rho*g*h = 13600 * 9.8 * 0.12 = 15,994 Pa. About 16 kPa gauge pressure.
A water tower raises water 40 m above ground level. What is the water pressure at ground level? (Ignore atmospheric pressure.)
P = rho*g*h = 1000 * 9.8 * 40 = 392,000 Pa = 392 kPa. This is the gauge pressure available at ground level.
A sealed container has oil (rho = 800 kg/m^3) floating on water (rho = 1000 kg/m^3). The oil layer is 0.5 m thick and the water layer is 1 m thick. What is the gauge pressure at the very bottom?
P = rho_oil*g*h_oil + rho_water*g*h_water = 800(9.8)(0.5) + 1000(9.8)(1.0) = 3920 + 9800 = 13720 Pa.
Real-World Applications
Example: A hydraulic garage lift has a large piston of area 0.5 m^2 and a small piston of area 0.01 m^2. If a mechanic pushes with 200 N on the small piston, what force is exerted on the car?
F2 = F1*(A2/A1) = 200*(0.5/0.01) = 200*50 = 10,000 N. The hydraulic system multiplies force by the area ratio.

--- Lesson 10.2: Pascal's Principle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Pascal's Principle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A hydraulic jack multiplies force. What is NOT multiplied?
Energy is conserved: W1 = W2, so F1*d1 = F2*d2. The hydraulic jack multiplies force but not energy. It trades distance for force.
What is 'Pascal's principle'?
Pascal's principle describes how pressure changes propagate in enclosed fluids. It is the basis for all hydraulic systems.
What is a 'hydraulic system'?
Hydraulic systems use fluid under pressure to transmit and multiply forces. Examples include car brakes, lifts, and heavy machinery.
What is 'mechanical advantage' in a hydraulic system?
Mechanical advantage = F_out/F_in = A_large/A_small. A hydraulic lift with MA = 50 converts 100 N input into 5000 N output.
What is a 'hydraulic press'?
A hydraulic press has a small input piston and a large output piston. The force multiplication ratio equals the area ratio of the pistons.
What is 'hydraulic fluid'?
Hydraulic fluid must be nearly incompressible, have appropriate viscosity, resist corrosion, and maintain properties over a range of temperatures.
What is 'pressure multiplication'?
Pressure is the same on both pistons. Force is multiplied because F = P*A, and the larger piston has more area.
What is a 'piston'?
Pistons convert fluid pressure into mechanical force (or vice versa). They must seal tightly to prevent fluid leakage.
What is 'work-energy conservation' in hydraulic systems?
Energy conservation means F1*d1 = F2*d2. If force is multiplied by 10, the large piston moves only 1/10 the distance.
What is an 'enclosed fluid'?
Pascal's principle specifically applies to enclosed fluids. The fluid must be confined so that pressure changes affect the entire volume.

--- Lesson 10.3: Buoyancy and Archimedes' Principle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Buoyancy and Archimedes' Principle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'Archimedes' principle'?
Archimedes' principle: F_b = rho_fluid * g * V_displaced. It explains floating, sinking, and apparent weight in fluids.
What is 'buoyant force'?
The buoyant force is the net upward force from fluid pressure acting on all surfaces of the submerged object.
What is 'apparent weight'?
Apparent weight = W - F_b = mg - rho_fluid*V*g. Objects feel lighter in water because buoyancy partially supports them.
What is 'neutral buoyancy'?
At neutral buoyancy, the object remains suspended at any depth. Submarines and scuba divers adjust their buoyancy to achieve this.
What is 'displaced volume'?
Displaced volume equals the submerged portion of the object. For a fully submerged object, it equals the object's total volume.
What is 'specific gravity' in the context of buoyancy?
SG = rho_object/rho_water. If SG < 1, the object floats. The fraction submerged equals the SG when floating in water.
What is a 'hydrometer'?
A hydrometer floats higher in denser liquids. The scale reads specific gravity directly from how deep the instrument sinks.
What is 'draft' of a ship?
A ship's draft increases as cargo is loaded (more weight requires more displaced water). Draft determines which harbors a ship can enter.
What is 'ballast'?
Submarines use ballast tanks (flooding with water to sink, emptying to rise). Ships use ballast water for stability when cargo is unloaded.
What is the 'metacenter' in naval architecture?
If the metacenter is above the center of gravity, the ship is stable. If below, the ship will capsize.
A 5 kg iron block (density 7800 kg/m^3) is submerged in water. What is its apparent weight?
V = m/rho = 5/7800 = 6.41e-4 m^3. F_b = 1000*9.8*6.41e-4 = 6.28 N. Apparent weight = 49.0 - 6.28 = 42.7 N.
A hot air balloon has a volume of 2800 m^3. If the outside air density is 1.2 kg/m^3 and the hot air inside has density 0.9 kg/m^3, what is the maximum payload (including balloon mass)?
Net lift = (rho_outside - rho_inside)*V*g = (1.2-0.9)*2800*9.8 = 0.3*2800*9.8 = 8232 N = 840 kg.
A 10 cm x 10 cm x 10 cm cube of wood (density 800 kg/m^3) floats in water. A mass is placed on top, pushing the cube until it is fully submerged. What is the mass?
V = 0.001 m^3. Wood mass = 800*0.001 = 0.8 kg. Max buoyancy = 1000*0.001*9.8 = 9.8 N. Extra mass = (1000-800)*0.001 = 0.2 kg.
A 2 kg object hangs from a spring scale. When lowered into oil (density 800 kg/m^3), the scale reads 17.6 N. What is the object's density?
F_b = 19.6 - 17.6 = 2.0 N. V = F_b/(rho_oil*g) = 2.0/(800*9.8) = 2.55e-4 m^3. rho = m/V = 2/2.55e-4 = 7843 kg/m^3. Approximately 6530 kg/m^3.
Real-World Applications
Example: An iceberg (density 917 kg/m^3) floats in seawater (density 1025 kg/m^3). What percentage of the iceberg is above water?
Fraction submerged = 917/1025 = 0.895. Above water = 1 - 0.895 = 0.105 = 10.5%. The tip of the iceberg is truly a small fraction.

--- Lesson 10.4: Fluid Dynamics and Bernoulli's Equation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fluid Dynamics and Bernoulli's Equation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'Bernoulli's equation'?
Bernoulli's equation expresses conservation of energy per unit volume in fluid flow. The three terms represent pressure energy, kinetic energy, and gravitational potential energy.
What is the 'continuity equation'?
The continuity equation ensures mass conservation: the volume flow rate A*v is constant throughout a pipe for incompressible flow.
What is 'laminar flow'?
Laminar flow has smooth parallel streamlines. It occurs at low velocities and high viscosities (Reynolds number < ~2300 in pipes).
What is 'turbulent flow'?
Turbulent flow has random eddies and vortices. It increases energy dissipation and mixing. It occurs at Reynolds number > ~4000.
What is 'volume flow rate'?
Volume flow rate Q = A*v. It is constant along a pipe for incompressible flow and has units of m^3/s or L/min.
What is a 'streamline'?
Streamlines show flow direction. In steady flow, fluid particles follow streamlines. They never cross each other.
What is the 'Venturi effect'?
The Venturi effect: as fluid enters a constriction, it speeds up (continuity) and pressure drops (Bernoulli). Used in carburetors, aspirators, etc.
What is 'Torricelli's theorem'?
Torricelli's theorem applies Bernoulli's equation to a tank with a hole: the exit speed equals the free-fall speed from height h.
What is the 'Reynolds number'?
Reynolds number Re = rho*v*D/mu compares inertial to viscous forces. Low Re = laminar, high Re = turbulent.
What is 'dynamic pressure'?
Dynamic pressure q = (1/2)*rho*v^2 represents the kinetic energy density. Bernoulli: P_static + q + rho*g*h = constant.
Water flows through a pipe that narrows from 6 cm diameter to 3 cm diameter. If the speed in the wide section is 2 m/s, what is the speed in the narrow section?
v2 = v1*(A1/A2) = v1*(d1/d2)^2 = 2*(6/3)^2 = 2*4 = 8 m/s.
A water tank has a small hole 5 m below the water surface. What is the speed of the water exiting the hole?
v = sqrt(2gh) = sqrt(2*9.8*5) = sqrt(98) = 9.9 m/s (Torricelli's theorem).
In a horizontal pipe, water flows at 3 m/s with pressure 200 kPa. If the pipe narrows and speed increases to 6 m/s, what is the new pressure?
P2 = P1 + (1/2)rho(v1^2 - v2^2) = 200000 + 500(9-36) = 200000 - 13500 = 186500 Pa = 186.5 kPa.
A Pitot tube on an airplane measures a stagnation pressure of 105 kPa and static pressure of 100 kPa. If air density is 1.2 kg/m^3, what is the airspeed?
v = sqrt(2*Delta P/rho) = sqrt(2*5000/1.2) = sqrt(8333) = 91.3 m/s.
A fire hose has a 10 cm diameter and narrows to a 3 cm nozzle. If the water flow rate is 0.03 m^3/s, what is the exit speed from the nozzle?
A_nozzle = pi*(0.015)^2 = 7.07e-4 m^2. v = Q/A = 0.03/7.07e-4 = 42.4 m/s.
Water rises through a building's plumbing from the ground floor (v = 2 m/s, P = 300 kPa) to the 3rd floor 10 m above. If the pipe diameter is the same, what is the pressure on the 3rd floor?
Same diameter means same velocity. P2 = P1 - rho*g*h = 300000 - 1000*9.8*10 = 300000 - 98000 = 202000 Pa = 202 kPa.
A siphon transfers water from a tank over a wall 1 m above the water surface. The siphon outlet is 3 m below the water surface. What is the flow speed at the outlet?
v = sqrt(2g*h) where h = 3 m (height difference between water surface and outlet). v = sqrt(2*9.8*3) = sqrt(58.8) = 7.67 m/s.
An aorta has a cross-sectional area of 3 cm^2 and blood flows at 30 cm/s. The total cross-sectional area of the capillaries is 3000 cm^2. What is the blood speed in the capillaries?
v2 = v1*(A1/A2) = 30*(3/3000) = 0.03 cm/s. The enormous total capillary area dramatically slows blood flow for efficient exchange.
A Venturi flowmeter has a pipe diameter of 10 cm and a throat diameter of 5 cm. If the pressure difference is 20 kPa, what is the flow rate of water?
Using Bernoulli + continuity: v1 = sqrt(2*Delta P/(rho*(A1/A2)^2 -1)) = sqrt(2*20000/(1000*(4^2-1))) = sqrt(40000/15000) = sqrt(2.667) = 1.63 m/s. Q = A1*v1 = pi*(0.05)^2*1.63 = 0.0128 m^3/s.
An airplane wing has area 30 m^2. Air flows at 70 m/s over the top and 60 m/s under the bottom. If air density is 1.2 kg/m^3, what is the lift force?
Delta P = (1/2)*rho*(v_bottom^2 - v_top^2) -- wait: lower pressure on top. Delta P = (1/2)(1.2)(70^2 - 60^2) = 0.6*(4900-3600) = 0.6*1300 = 780 Pa. F = Delta P * A = 780*30 = 23400 N.

    [ ] Viscosity and flow rate in most scenarios involving Bernoulli factors
    Explanation: Bernoulli's equation: P + (1/2)rho*v^2 + rho*g*h = constant along a streamline. It is an energy conservation statement for fluids.

  Q3. According to Bernoulli's principle, when the speed of a fluid increases:
    [ ] The pressure remains unchanged
    [ ] The pressure also increases
    [ ] The density increases
    [*] The pressure decreases
    Explanation: In Bernoulli's equation, if v increases at constant height, P must decrease to keep the sum constant. This explains lift on airplane wings.

  Q4. Torricelli's theorem gives the speed of fluid exiting a hole at depth h below the surface:
    [ ] v = 2gh
    [ ] v = rho*g*h
    [*] v = sqrt(2gh)
    [ ] v = sqrt(gh)
    Explanation: Torricelli's theorem: v = sqrt(2gh). The exit speed is the same as if the fluid had fallen freely through height h.

  Q5. A Venturi meter works by measuring:
    [ ] Temperature change in a flowing fluid when considering all relevant pressure
    [ ] The viscosity of the fluid, as conventional pressure theory would suggest
    [*] The pressure drop as fluid flows through a constriction, which relates to flow velocity
    [ ] The weight of the fluid, even when conditions shift significantly
    Explanation: In a Venturi tube, the constriction speeds up the fluid and lowers the pressure (Bernoulli's principle). The pressure difference indicates flow rate.

  Q6. An ideal fluid in Bernoulli's equation is assumed to be:
    [ ] At constant temperature
    [ ] Compressible, viscous, and turbulent
    [*] Incompressible, non-viscous, and undergoing steady (laminar) flow
    [ ] Only applicable to gases
    Explanation: Bernoulli's equation assumes incompressible, non-viscous, steady, irrotational flow along a streamline.

  Q7. Volume flow rate (Q) is defined as:
    [ ] Q = A/v when considering all
    [*] Q = A*v (cross-sectional area times velocity)
    [ ] Q = P*A regardless of prevailing
    [ ] Q = rho*v under most circumstances
    Explanation: Q = A*v has units of m^3/s. It represents the volume of fluid passing a point per unit time.

  Q8. When water flows through a garden hose and you partially cover the end with your thumb:
    [ ] The flow rate increases according to widely held views on this topic
    [*] The water speeds up because the area decreases while flow rate stays constant
    [ ] The pressure at the exit increases within the broader context of exit
    [ ] The water slows down within the broader context of exit analysis
    Explanation: By the continuity equation, reducing the exit area increases the exit velocity. The water shoots farther.

  Q9. Laminar flow differs from turbulent flow in that:
    [ ] Laminar flow occurs only in gases within the broader context of flow analysis
    [ ] There is no difference, as conventional flow theory would suggest
    [*] Laminar flow has smooth, parallel streamlines while turbulent flow has chaotic, irregular motion
    [ ] Laminar flow is always faster, as conventional flow theory would suggest
    Explanation: Laminar flow has orderly layers (low Reynolds number). Turbulent flow has eddies and mixing (high Reynolds number).

  Q10. Airplane wings generate lift primarily because:
    [ ] The air pushes harder on the bottom
    [ ] The engine pushes the plane upward
    [ ] Gravity is weaker above the wing
    [*] The wing shape and angle cause faster airflow over the top, creating lower pressure above than below
    Explanation: Bernoulli's principle: faster air over the curved top creates lower pressure than slower air below. The pressure difference produces lift.

  Q11. What is 'Bernoulli's equation'?
    [ ] An equation for turbulent flow only
    [*] An energy conservation equation for ideal fluid flow: P + (1/2)rho*v^2 + rho*g*h = constant along a streamline
    [ ] An equation for fluid viscosity
    [ ] An equation relating temperature and pressure
    Explanation: Bernoulli's equation expresses conservation of energy per unit volume in fluid flow. The three terms represent pressure energy, kinetic energy, and gravitational potential energy.

  Q12. What is the 'continuity equation'?
    [ ] An equation for calculating Reynolds number
    [ ] An equation about fluid temperature
    [*] A conservation of mass statement for fluid flow: A1*v1 = A2*v2 for incompressible fluids in a pipe
    [ ] An equation about pressure loss in pipes
    Explanation: The continuity equation ensures mass conservation: the volume flow rate A*v is constant throughout a pipe for incompressible flow.

  Q13. What is 'laminar flow'?
    [ ] High-speed chaotic flow, since standard models of flow behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    [ ] Flow driven by gravity only in most scenarios involving flow factors
    [*] Smooth, orderly fluid motion in which adjacent layers flow in parallel without mixing, occurring at low Reynolds numbers
    [ ] Flow in a circular pipe only under most circumstances involving flow
    Explanation: Laminar flow has smooth parallel streamlines. It occurs at low velocities and high viscosities (Reynolds number < ~2300 in pipes).

  Q14. What is 'turbulent flow'?
    [*] Chaotic, irregular fluid motion with eddies and mixing, occurring at high Reynolds numbers
    [ ] Flow in a straight line only according to widely held views on this topic
    [ ] Very slow fluid motion, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] Flow without any viscosity in most scenarios involving flow factors
    Explanation: Turbulent flow has random eddies and vortices. It increases energy dissipation and mixing. It occurs at Reynolds number > ~4000.

  Q15. What is 'volume flow rate'?
    [ ] The mass of fluid flowing per second, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [*] The volume of fluid passing through a cross-section per unit time, Q = A*v, measured in m^3/s
    [ ] The pressure times velocity in most scenarios involving flow factors
    [ ] The speed of the fluid, at least according to standard theory
    Explanation: Volume flow rate Q = A*v. It is constant along a pipe for incompressible flow and has units of m^3/s or L/min.

  Q16. What is a 'streamline'?
    [ ] A line of constant pressure, particularly in the context of flow
    [ ] The edge of a fluid jet, which is consistent with how flow mechanisms are understood to operate in theory
    [ ] A straight pipe for fluid flow, as conventional flow theory would suggest
    [*] A line tangent to the velocity vector of the fluid at every point, showing the path a fluid element follows in steady flow
    Explanation: Streamlines show flow direction. In steady flow, fluid particles follow streamlines. They never cross each other.

  Q17. What is the 'Venturi effect'?
    [ ] The increase in pressure when a fluid speeds up
    [ ] The creation of turbulence in narrow pipes
    [ ] The increase in flow rate through a constriction
    [*] The reduction in fluid pressure when it flows through a constricted section of pipe, as described by Bernoulli's equation
    Explanation: The Venturi effect: as fluid enters a constriction, it speeds up (continuity) and pressure drops (Bernoulli). Used in carburetors, aspirators, etc.

  Q18. What is 'Torricelli's theorem'?
    [*] A special case of Bernoulli's equation stating that the speed of fluid flowing from an opening at depth h is v = sqrt(2gh)
    [ ] A theorem about atmospheric pressure in most scenarios involving speed factors
    [ ] A theorem about fluid viscosity under most circumstances involving speed
    [ ] A theorem about turbulent flow, particularly in the context of speed
    Explanation: Torricelli's theorem applies Bernoulli's equation to a tank with a hole: the exit speed equals the free-fall speed from height h.

  Q19. What is the 'Reynolds number'?
    [ ] A number measuring fluid density, even when conditions shift significantly
    [*] A dimensionless number (Re = rho*v*D/mu) that predicts whether flow will be laminar or turbulent
    [ ] A measure of flow rate, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] The ratio of pressure to velocity regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    Explanation: Reynolds number Re = rho*v*D/mu compares inertial to viscous forces. Low Re = laminar, high Re = turbulent.

  Q20. What is 'dynamic pressure'?
    [*] The kinetic energy per unit volume of a flowing fluid: (1/2)*rho*v^2
    [ ] The pressure at the bottom of a container
    [ ] The pressure in a static fluid
    [ ] The change in pressure over time
    Explanation: Dynamic pressure q = (1/2)*rho*v^2 represents the kinetic energy density. Bernoulli: P_static + q + rho*g*h = constant.

  Q21. Water flows through a pipe that narrows from 6 cm diameter to 3 cm diameter. If the speed in the wide section is 2 m/s, what is the speed in the narrow section?
    [ ] 16 m/s
    [*] 8 m/s
    [ ] 4 m/s
    [ ] 1 m/s
    Explanation: v2 = v1*(A1/A2) = v1*(d1/d2)^2 = 2*(6/3)^2 = 2*4 = 8 m/s.

  Q22. A water tank has a small hole 5 m below the water surface. What is the speed of the water exiting the hole?
    [*] About 9.9 m/s
    [ ] About 14 m/s
    [ ] About 4.95 m/s
    [ ] About 49 m/s
    Explanation: v = sqrt(2gh) = sqrt(2*9.8*5) = sqrt(98) = 9.9 m/s (Torricelli's theorem).

  Q23. In a horizontal pipe, water flows at 3 m/s with pressure 200 kPa. If the pipe narrows and speed increases to 6 m/s, what is the new pressure?
    [ ] About 100 kPa
    [ ] About 213.5 kPa
    [ ] About 200 kPa
    [*] About 186.5 kPa
    Explanation: P2 = P1 + (1/2)rho(v1^2 - v2^2) = 200000 + 500(9-36) = 200000 - 13500 = 186500 Pa = 186.5 kPa.

  Q24. A Pitot tube on an airplane measures a stagnation pressure of 105 kPa and static pressure of 100 kPa. If air density is 1.2 kg/m^3, what is the airspeed?
    [ ] About 64 m/s
    [ ] About 129 m/s
    [*] About 91 m/s
    [ ] About 45 m/s
    Explanation: v = sqrt(2*Delta P/rho) = sqrt(2*5000/1.2) = sqrt(8333) = 91.3 m/s.

  Q25. A fire hose has a 10 cm diameter and narrows to a 3 cm nozzle. If the water flow rate is 0.03 m^3/s, what is the exit speed from the nozzle?
    [ ] About 3.8 m/s
    [ ] About 127 m/s
    [ ] About 12.7 m/s
    [*] About 42.4 m/s
    Explanation: A_nozzle = pi*(0.015)^2 = 7.07e-4 m^2. v = Q/A = 0.03/7.07e-4 = 42.4 m/s.

  Q26. Water rises through a building's plumbing from the ground floor (v = 2 m/s, P = 300 kPa) to the 3rd floor 10 m above. If the pipe diameter is the same, what is the pressure on the 3rd floor?
    [ ] About 398 kPa
    [ ] About 100 kPa
    [*] About 202 kPa
    [ ] About 300 kPa
    Explanation: Same diameter means same velocity. P2 = P1 - rho*g*h = 300000 - 1000*9.8*10 = 300000 - 98000 = 202000 Pa = 202 kPa.

  Q27. A siphon transfers water from a tank over a wall 1 m above the water surface. The siphon outlet is 3 m below the water surface. What is the flow speed at the outlet?
    [*] About 7.7 m/s
    [ ] About 2.0 m/s
    [ ] About 4.4 m/s
    [ ] About 6.3 m/s
    Explanation: v = sqrt(2g*h) where h = 3 m (height difference between water surface and outlet). v = sqrt(2*9.8*3) = sqrt(58.8) = 7.67 m/s.

  Q28. An aorta has a cross-sectional area of 3 cm^2 and blood flows at 30 cm/s. The total cross-sectional area of the capillaries is 3000 cm^2. What is the blood speed in the capillaries?
    [*] 0.03 cm/s
    [ ] 30,000 cm/s
    [ ] 0.3 cm/s
    [ ] 3 cm/s
    Explanation: v2 = v1*(A1/A2) = 30*(3/3000) = 0.03 cm/s. The enormous total capillary area dramatically slows blood flow for efficient exchange.

  Q29. A Venturi flowmeter has a pipe diameter of 10 cm and a throat diameter of 5 cm. If the pressure difference is 20 kPa, what is the flow rate of water?
    [ ] About 0.13 m^3/s
    [*] About 0.013 m^3/s
    [ ] About 0.0013 m^3/s
    [ ] About 1.3 m^3/s
    Explanation: Using Bernoulli + continuity: v1 = sqrt(2*Delta P/(rho*(A1/A2)^2 -1)) = sqrt(2*20000/(1000*(4^2-1))) = sqrt(40000/15000) = sqrt(2.667) = 1.63 m/s. Q = A1*v1 = pi*(0.05)^2*1.63 = 0.0128 m^3/s.

  Q30. An airplane wing has area 30 m^2. Air flows at 70 m/s over the top and 60 m/s under the bottom. If air density is 1.2 kg/m^3, what is the lift force?
    [ ] About 234,000 N
    [ ] About 46,800 N
    [*] About 23,400 N
    [ ] About 2340 N
    Explanation: Delta P = (1/2)*rho*(v_bottom^2 - v_top^2) -- wait: lower pressure on top. Delta P = (1/2)(1.2)(70^2 - 60^2) = 0.6*(4900-3600) = 0.6*1300 = 780 Pa. F = Delta P * A = 780*30 = 23400 N.
