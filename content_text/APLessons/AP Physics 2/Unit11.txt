--- Lesson 11.1: Electric Charge and Coulomb's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Charge and Coulomb's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'electric charge'?
Electric charge is a fundamental, conserved, quantized property. Protons carry +e, electrons carry -e.
What is 'Coulomb's law'?
Coulomb's law gives the force between stationary charges. It is analogous to Newton's law of gravitation but can be attractive or repulsive.
What is a 'conductor'?
Conductors (metals like copper, silver) have free electrons in the conduction band that move easily under applied electric fields.
What is an 'insulator'?
Insulators (glass, rubber, plastic) have tightly bound electrons. Charge stays where placed and does not flow freely.
What is 'quantization of charge'?
All observable charges are integer multiples of e. You cannot have 1.5e of charge. Quarks have fractional charges but are never observed in isolation.
What is 'charging by induction'?
Induction: a charged rod polarizes a neutral conductor; grounding removes one charge type; removing the ground and then the rod leaves the object permanently charged.
What is 'polarization' of a material?
Polarization shifts charges without changing net charge. This is why a charged balloon sticks to a neutral wall.
What is the 'triboelectric effect'?
The triboelectric effect: rubbing transfers electrons between materials. Which material gains or loses electrons depends on the triboelectric series.
What is the 'principle of superposition' for electric forces?
Superposition means each charge exerts its force independently. The net force requires vector addition of all individual forces.
What is an 'electroscope'?
An electroscope has thin metal leaves that spread apart when charged, since like charges repel. Greater charge = greater deflection.
Two charges of +3 microC and -5 microC are separated by 20 cm. What is the magnitude of the Coulomb force between them?
F = k*|q1*q2|/r^2 = (8.99e9)(3e-6)(5e-6)/(0.2)^2 = (8.99e9)(15e-12)/0.04 = 0.1349/0.04 = 3.37 N.
Three charges are in a line: +2 microC at x=0, -3 microC at x=0.3 m, and +4 microC at x=0.6 m. What is the net force on the middle charge?
F from +2 on -3: k(2e-6)(3e-6)/(0.3)^2 = 0.6 N (attractive, toward left). F from +4 on -3: k(3e-6)(4e-6)/(0.3)^2 = 1.2 N (attractive, toward right). Net = 1.2 - 0.6 = 0.6 N right. Recalculating: F_left = 8.99e9*6e-12/0.09 = 0.599 N. F_right = 8.99e9*12e-12/0.09 = 1.199 N. Net = 0.6 N right. Approximately 0.8 N accounting for the actual values.
A hydrogen atom has a proton and an electron separated by about 5.3 x 10^-11 m. What is the Coulomb force between them?
F = k*e^2/r^2 = (8.99e9)(1.6e-19)^2/(5.3e-11)^2 = (8.99e9)(2.56e-38)/(2.81e-21) = 8.2e-8 N.
Two charged spheres hang from strings of equal length from the same point. They repel and make angles of 15 degrees each with the vertical. If each has mass 0.01 kg and the separation is 10 cm, what is the charge on each?
At equilibrium: F_e = mg*tan(15) = 0.01*9.8*0.268 = 0.0263 N. F_e = kq^2/r^2. q = r*sqrt(F_e/k) = 0.1*sqrt(0.0263/8.99e9) = 0.1*1.71e-6 = 1.71e-7 C = 17 nC.
A Van de Graaff generator has a 30 cm diameter sphere charged to 500 kV. What is the charge on the sphere? (Hint: V = kQ/R for a sphere)
Q = V*R/k = 500000*0.15/(8.99e9) = 75000/8.99e9 = 8.34e-6 C = 8.3 microC.
A lightning bolt transfers about 5 C of charge between a cloud and the ground in 0.001 seconds. What is the average current?
I = Q/t = 5/0.001 = 5000 A. Lightning involves enormous but brief currents.
Two point charges +Q are fixed at opposite corners of a square of side L. What is the force on a third charge +Q placed at another corner?
One charge at distance L gives F1 = kQ^2/L^2 along the side. The other at distance L*sqrt(2) gives F2 = kQ^2/(2L^2) along the diagonal. Vector sum points along the bisector with magnitude kQ^2/L^2*sqrt(1 + 1/4 + sqrt(2)/2).
Real-World Applications
Example: A lightning bolt transfers about 5 C of charge between a cloud and the ground in 0.001 seconds. What is the average current?
I = Q/t = 5/0.001 = 5000 A. Lightning involves enormous but brief currents.

--- Lesson 11.2: Electric Fields and Field Lines (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Fields and Field Lines
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'electric field'?
The electric field E exists at every point in space around charges. A test charge q experiences force F = qE.
What is 'electric flux'?
Electric flux Phi = integral of E dot dA measures how much field 'flows through' a surface. Units: N*m^2/C.
What is 'Gauss's law'?
Gauss's law: Phi_E = Q_enc/epsilon_0. It is one of Maxwell's equations and is equivalent to Coulomb's law.
What is an 'electric dipole'?
A dipole has moment p = qd. Far from the dipole, the field falls off as 1/r^3, faster than for a single charge.
What is 'electrostatic shielding'?
A Faraday cage redistributes surface charges to cancel external fields inside. The interior field is zero regardless of external fields.
What is 'surface charge density'?
Surface charge density sigma = Q/A determines the field near a surface: E = sigma/epsilon_0 for an infinite plane.
What is a 'test charge'?
A test charge q_0 is infinitesimally small so it measures the field E = F/q_0 without altering the charge distribution.
What is 'permittivity of free space' (epsilon_0)?
epsilon_0 = 8.85 x 10^-12 F/m. It appears in k = 1/(4*pi*epsilon_0) and determines field strength in vacuum.
What is 'electric field superposition'?
E_total = E_1 + E_2 + ... (vector addition). Each charge contributes independently to the field at any point.
A proton enters a uniform electric field of 500 N/C. What is its acceleration?
F = qE = (1.6e-19)(500) = 8e-17 N. a = F/m = 8e-17/1.67e-27 = 4.79e10 m/s^2.
Two parallel plates are separated by 2 cm and have a potential difference of 100 V. What is the electric field between them?
E = V/d = 100/0.02 = 5000 V/m. The field is uniform between large parallel plates.
A charged sphere of radius 10 cm has total charge +50 nC. What is the electric field at 30 cm from the center?
E = kQ/r^2 = (8.99e9)(50e-9)/(0.3)^2 = 449.5/0.09 = 4994 N/C.
An inkjet printer deflects charged drops using parallel plates with E = 10^5 N/C over 1 cm. If drops have mass 10^-10 kg and charge 10^-13 C, what is the vertical deflection?
a = qE/m = (1e-13)(1e5)/(1e-10) = 100 m/s^2. Time to cross 1 cm at 10 m/s: t = 0.01/10 = 0.001 s. y = (1/2)at^2 = 0.5(100)(1e-6) = 5e-5 m = 0.05 mm.
A non-conducting sphere of radius R has total charge Q uniformly distributed. Using Gauss's law, what is the field at r < R (inside)?
Inside: Q_enc = Q(r^3/R^3). By Gauss: E(4*pi*r^2) = Q(r^3/R^3)/epsilon_0. E = kQr/R^3, increasing linearly with r.
A long straight wire has linear charge density lambda = 2 x 10^-7 C/m. What is the electric field 5 cm from the wire?
E = lambda/(2*pi*epsilon_0*r) = 2*k*lambda/r = 2(8.99e9)(2e-7)/0.05 = 3.596e3/0.05 = 71920 N/C.
Two charges +4 microC and -4 microC are separated by 10 cm. What is the electric field at the midpoint?
Both fields point toward the negative charge at the midpoint. E = 2*kQ/r^2 = 2*(8.99e9)(4e-6)/(0.05)^2 = 2*14384000 = 2.88e7 N/C.
A uniform electric field of 3000 N/C points to the right. What is the electric flux through a 0.5 m^2 surface perpendicular to the field?
Phi = E*A*cos(0) = 3000*0.5*1 = 1500 N*m^2/C.
Real-World Applications
Example: A thundercloud has a charge distribution that creates a field of 10,000 N/C at the ground (breakdown field for air is about 3 x 10^6 V/m). If the cloud base is 1 km above, what voltage difference exists?
V = E*d = 10000*1000 = 10^7 V = 10 MV. Actual lightning requires local fields near breakdown strength.

--- Lesson 11.3: Electric Potential and Voltage (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Potential and Voltage
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'electric potential'?
Electric potential V = U/q is a scalar. It simplifies calculations since potentials add algebraically, not as vectors.
What is 'voltage' (potential difference)?
Voltage is what drives current. A 12V battery does 12 J of work per coulomb of charge it moves from - to + terminal.
What is an 'equipotential surface'?
No work is done moving charge along an equipotential. For a point charge, equipotentials are concentric spheres.
What is 'electric potential energy'?
Electric PE is stored in charge configurations. It converts to KE as charges move, similar to gravitational PE.
What is an 'electron volt'?
1 eV = 1.6 x 10^-19 J. It is convenient for atomic and particle physics where energies are very small in joules.
What is 'grounding'?
Grounding allows charge to flow to or from Earth until the object reaches Earth's potential (defined as zero).
What is a 'potential gradient'?
The potential gradient is how rapidly voltage changes with distance. Steeper gradients mean stronger fields.
What is 'electrostatic potential energy of a system'?
U_system = sum of k*qi*qj/rij for all pairs. It equals the total work done against electric forces to assemble the charges.
What is a 'volt per meter'?
V/m = N/C. Both are valid units for electric field. V/m emphasizes the potential-distance relationship.
What is 'electric potential due to continuous charge distributions'?
For continuous distributions, V = integral of k*dq/r. Since V is scalar, no vector addition is needed, simplifying calculations.
An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of 500 V. What is its final speed?
KE = eV = (1.6e-19)(500) = 8e-17 J. v = sqrt(2KE/m) = sqrt(2*8e-17/9.11e-31) = sqrt(1.76e14) = 1.33e7 m/s.
What is the electric potential at the center of a square (side 1 m) with charges +1 microC at each corner?
Distance from center to corner = sqrt(2)/2 = 0.707 m. V = 4*kQ/r = 4*(8.99e9)(1e-6)/0.707 = 50860 V = 50.9 kV.
Two point charges +3 microC and -3 microC are 40 cm apart. What is the potential at the midpoint?
V = kQ1/r1 + kQ2/r2 = k(3e-6)/0.2 + k(-3e-6)/0.2 = 0 V. Potentials from equal and opposite charges cancel at the midpoint.
A proton and an alpha particle (charge +2e, mass 4m_p) are both accelerated from rest through 1000 V. What is the ratio of their speeds?
v = sqrt(2qV/m). v_p = sqrt(2eV/m_p). v_alpha = sqrt(2*2eV/(4m_p)) = sqrt(eV/m_p). Ratio = sqrt(2eV/m_p)/sqrt(eV/m_p) = sqrt(2).
What is the total electric potential energy of three charges: +2 microC, +3 microC, and -1 microC at the vertices of an equilateral triangle with side 0.5 m?
U = k[q1q2/r12 + q1q3/r13 + q2q3/r23] = k/0.5*[(2)(3) + (2)(-1) + (3)(-1)] * 10^-12 = 8.99e9/0.5 * [6-2-3]e-12 = 17.98e9 * 1e-12 = 0.018*4 = 0.072 J.
A Van de Graaff generator produces a potential of 2 MV on its dome. If a spark occurs when E exceeds 3 x 10^6 V/m, what is the minimum dome radius?
E = V/R for a sphere. R = V/E = 2e6/3e6 = 0.667 m.
Two large parallel plates separated by 1 mm have a potential difference of 500 V. An oil drop with charge 3.2 x 10^-19 C and mass 5 x 10^-15 kg is between them. What is the net force on the drop?
E = 500/0.001 = 500000 V/m. F_E = qE = 3.2e-19 * 5e5 = 1.6e-13 N up. F_g = mg = 5e-15*9.8 = 4.9e-14 N down. Net = 1.6e-13 - 4.9e-14 = 1.1e-13 N up.
The potential at a distance of 1 m from a charged sphere is 9000 V. What is the potential at 3 m from the center?
V = kQ/r is proportional to 1/r. At 3 times the distance, V = 9000/3 = 3000 V.

--- Lesson 11.4: Capacitance and Dielectrics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Capacitance and Dielectrics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'capacitance'?
Capacitance measures charge storage ability. A 1 F capacitor stores 1 C per volt of potential difference.
What is a 'capacitor'?
Capacitors store energy electrostatically. Common types include parallel plate, cylindrical, and spherical capacitors.
What is a 'dielectric'?
Dielectrics (glass, paper, ceramic) increase capacitance by factor kappa and increase the breakdown voltage of the capacitor.
What is the 'dielectric constant' (kappa)?
Kappa (or relative permittivity) ranges from 1 (vacuum) to ~80 (water). C_with_dielectric = kappa * C_vacuum.
What is 'dielectric breakdown'?
Dielectric breakdown occurs when E exceeds the material's dielectric strength. For air, this is about 3 x 10^6 V/m (sparking).
What is 'energy density of an electric field'?
Energy density u = (1/2)*epsilon_0*E^2 shows that energy is stored in the field itself, not just on the plates.
What is a 'parallel plate capacitor'?
The parallel plate capacitor is the simplest geometry. The uniform field between plates makes it ideal for analysis.
What is 'dielectric polarization'?
Molecular dipoles align (or are induced) by the external field. This creates bound surface charges that reduce the net field inside.
What is 'equivalent capacitance'?
Equivalent capacitance simplifies analysis: parallel adds directly (C_eq = C1+C2), series adds reciprocally (1/C_eq = 1/C1+1/C2).
What is a 'supercapacitor'?
Supercapacitors use electrochemical double layers to achieve enormous capacitance (1-5000 F) for rapid charge/discharge applications.
Three capacitors of 2 microF, 3 microF, and 6 microF are connected in series. What is the equivalent capacitance?
1/C = 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/6 = 3/6 + 2/6 + 1/6 = 6/6 = 1. C = 1 microF.
A parallel plate capacitor has plates of area 0.1 m^2 separated by 1 mm. What is its capacitance?
C = epsilon_0*A/d = (8.85e-12)(0.1)/(0.001) = 8.85e-10 F = 885 pF.
A 4 microF capacitor charged to 100 V is connected to an uncharged 6 microF capacitor. What is the final voltage across both?
Q_initial = 4e-6*100 = 4e-4 C. Q is conserved. V_final = Q/(C1+C2) = 4e-4/10e-6 = 40 V.
A camera flash uses a 100 microF capacitor charged to 300 V. If the flash lasts 1 ms, what is the average power?
U = (1/2)CV^2 = 0.5*100e-6*90000 = 4.5 J. P = U/t = 4.5/0.001 = 4500 W.
A 5 microF and a 20 microF capacitor are connected in parallel across a 12 V battery. What is the total charge stored?
C_total = 5 + 20 = 25 microF. Q = CV = 25e-6 * 12 = 300 microC.
A parallel plate capacitor with air (C = 50 pF) has plates separated by 2 mm. What is the maximum voltage before dielectric breakdown of air (E_max = 3 x 10^6 V/m)?
V_max = E_max * d = 3e6 * 0.002 = 6000 V. Above this, the air ionizes and sparking occurs.

  Q1. Capacitance is defined as:
    [ ] C = QV according to widely held views
    [ ] C = Q^2/V in most scenarios involving
    [ ] C = V/Q, despite what intuition might
    [*] C = Q/V, the ratio of stored charge to voltage
    Explanation: Capacitance C = Q/V, measured in farads (F). 1 F = 1 C/V. Typical capacitors range from pF to mF.

  Q2. The capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor is:
    [*] C = epsilon_0 * A / d
    [ ] C = epsilon_0 / (A * d)
    [ ] C = A * d / epsilon_0
    [ ] C = epsilon_0 * d / A
    Explanation: C = epsilon_0 * A / d. Larger area A increases capacitance; larger separation d decreases it.

  Q3. The energy stored in a charged capacitor is:
    [ ] U = (1/2)Q^2V in most
    [ ] U = CV^2 according to widely
    [*] U = (1/2)CV^2 = (1/2)QV = Q^2/(2C)
    [ ] U = QV when considering all
    Explanation: The factor of 1/2 arises because voltage increases as charge accumulates: U = (1/2)CV^2 = (1/2)QV = Q^2/(2C).

  Q4. When a dielectric is inserted between capacitor plates:
    [ ] Capacitance stays the same
    [*] Capacitance increases by the dielectric constant factor kappa
    [ ] The charge disappears
    [ ] Capacitance decreases
    Explanation: C = kappa * epsilon_0 * A / d. The dielectric constant kappa > 1 always increases capacitance.

  Q5. Capacitors in parallel combine as:
    [*] C_total = C1 + C2
    [ ] 1/C_total = 1/C1 + 1/C2
    [ ] C_total = C1 - C2
    [ ] C_total = C1 * C2
    Explanation: Parallel capacitors share voltage, so charges add: C_total = C1 + C2 + C3 + ...

  Q6. Capacitors in series combine as:
    [ ] C_total = C1 * C2
    [ ] C_total = (C1 + C2)/2
    [*] 1/C_total = 1/C1 + 1/C2
    [ ] C_total = C1 + C2
    Explanation: Series capacitors share charge, so voltages add: 1/C_total = 1/C1 + 1/C2. The total is always less than the smallest individual capacitor.

  Q7. The farad (F) is a unit of:
    [ ] Charge
    [ ] Voltage
    [ ] Energy
    [*] Capacitance
    Explanation: 1 farad = 1 coulomb per volt. One farad is very large; most practical capacitors are measured in microfarads or picofarads.

  Q8. A dielectric material between capacitor plates reduces the electric field because:
    [ ] It increases the plate separation
    [ ] It conducts charge between the plates
    [*] Polarized molecules create an opposing field that partially cancels the applied field
    [ ] It absorbs the charges
    Explanation: The dielectric polarizes, creating bound surface charges that oppose the free charges on the plates, reducing the net field by factor kappa.

  Q9. The energy density (energy per unit volume) stored in an electric field is:
    [ ] u = epsilon_0 * E
    [*] u = (1/2) * epsilon_0 * E^2
    [ ] u = epsilon_0 * E^2
    [ ] u = (1/2) * epsilon_0 / E^2
    Explanation: u = (1/2)*epsilon_0*E^2 gives the energy per unit volume stored in any electric field.

  Q10. When a charged capacitor is disconnected from the battery and the plates are pulled apart:
    [*] Charge stays the same, voltage increases, and capacitance decreases
    [ ] Charge increases, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] Voltage stays the same, particularly in the context of apart
    [ ] Energy decreases in most scenarios involving apart factors
    Explanation: Isolated: Q constant. C = epsilon_0*A/d decreases. V = Q/C increases. U = Q^2/(2C) increases (work was done pulling plates apart).

  Q11. What is 'capacitance'?
    [ ] The amount of charge a device stores
    [ ] The resistance to charge flow
    [*] The ability of a device to store charge per unit voltage: C = Q/V, measured in farads
    [ ] The voltage across a device
    Explanation: Capacitance measures charge storage ability. A 1 F capacitor stores 1 C per volt of potential difference.

  Q12. What is a 'capacitor'?
    [ ] A device that generates voltage, particularly in the context of capacitors
    [ ] A device that stores energy as heat, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [*] A device consisting of two conductors separated by an insulator that stores energy in an electric field between the conductors
    [ ] A type of resistor, which represents a common but incomplete view of how these capacitors dynamics operate
    Explanation: Capacitors store energy electrostatically. Common types include parallel plate, cylindrical, and spherical capacitors.

  Q13. What is a 'dielectric'?
    [ ] A type of conductor when the interaction between increase and breakdown factors is taken into full account
    [ ] A material that blocks all electric fields, especially during periods of significant change
    [*] An insulating material placed between capacitor plates that increases capacitance by reducing the effective electric field through polarization
    [ ] A semiconductor, since the relevant increase factors typically produce this outcome in practice
    Explanation: Dielectrics (glass, paper, ceramic) increase capacitance by factor kappa and increase the breakdown voltage of the capacitor.

  Q14. What is the 'dielectric constant' (kappa)?
    [ ] The charge stored in a dielectric, which is a common assumption in the field
    [*] A dimensionless number (always >= 1) that indicates how much a dielectric material increases the capacitance relative to vacuum
    [ ] The resistance of a dielectric, particularly in the context of kappa
    [ ] A property of conductors, as the dynamics involved tend to push conditions in this direction over time
    Explanation: Kappa (or relative permittivity) ranges from 1 (vacuum) to ~80 (water). C_with_dielectric = kappa * C_vacuum.

  Q15. What is 'dielectric breakdown'?
    [ ] When a dielectric becomes magnetized because the underlying forces at work tend to produce exactly this type of outcome
    [*] The failure of an insulating material to resist electric field, causing it to become conducting when the field exceeds its dielectric strength
    [ ] When a capacitor loses its charge, which is consistent with how dielectric mechanisms are understood to operate in theory
    [ ] When a dielectric melts, although this interpretation oversimplifies the actual dielectric mechanisms at work
    Explanation: Dielectric breakdown occurs when E exceeds the material's dielectric strength. For air, this is about 3 x 10^6 V/m (sparking).

  Q16. What is 'energy density of an electric field'?
    [ ] The voltage per unit length
    [*] The energy stored per unit volume in an electric field: u = (1/2)*epsilon_0*E^2
    [ ] The total energy stored in a capacitor
    [ ] The charge per unit volume
    Explanation: Energy density u = (1/2)*epsilon_0*E^2 shows that energy is stored in the field itself, not just on the plates.

  Q17. What is a 'parallel plate capacitor'?
    [ ] Two plates connected in parallel to a battery
    [*] A capacitor consisting of two flat, parallel conducting plates separated by a distance d, with capacitance C = epsilon_0*A/d
    [ ] A capacitor with infinite capacitance
    [ ] A capacitor that can only be used horizontally
    Explanation: The parallel plate capacitor is the simplest geometry. The uniform field between plates makes it ideal for analysis.

  Q18. What is 'dielectric polarization'?
    [*] The alignment or displacement of bound charges within a dielectric material in response to an external electric field
    [ ] The magnetization of a dielectric, based on conventional assumptions about field
    [ ] The conduction of charge through a dielectric under most circumstances involving field
    [ ] The destruction of a dielectric in most scenarios involving field factors
    Explanation: Molecular dipoles align (or are induced) by the external field. This creates bound surface charges that reduce the net field inside.

  Q19. What is 'equivalent capacitance'?
    [ ] The capacitance of a single plate
    [ ] The largest capacitor in a circuit
    [ ] The average capacitance of a circuit
    [*] A single capacitance value that would have the same effect as a combination of capacitors in a circuit
    Explanation: Equivalent capacitance simplifies analysis: parallel adds directly (C_eq = C1+C2), series adds reciprocally (1/C_eq = 1/C1+1/C2).

  Q20. What is a 'supercapacitor'?

    [ ] A theoretical capacitor, which represents a common but incomplete view of how these achieve dynamics operate
