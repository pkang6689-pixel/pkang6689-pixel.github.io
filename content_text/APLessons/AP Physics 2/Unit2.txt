--- Lesson 2.1: Electricity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electricity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI unit of electric charge?
The SI unit of electric charge is the coulomb (C). One coulomb equals the charge of approximately 6.24 × 10¹⁸ elementary charges (protons or electrons).
Two point charges of +2 μC and −2 μC are separated by 0.1 m. What is the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them? (k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²)
F = kq₁q₂/r² = (9 × 10⁹)(2 × 10⁻⁶)(2 × 10⁻⁶)/(0.1)² = (9 × 10⁹)(4 × 10⁻¹²)/0.01 = 0.036/0.01 = 3.6 N.
What is 'Coulomb's law'?
Coulomb's law: F = kq₁q₂/r² describes the electrostatic force between two point charges. It is analogous in form to Newton's law of gravitation but much stronger.
What is 'electrostatic induction'?
Electrostatic induction is the process by which a charged object causes a redistribution of charges on a nearby conductor without touching it, creating regions of positive and negative charge.
What is an 'insulator'?
An insulator (like glass, rubber, or plastic) is a material in which electrons are tightly bound to atoms and do not move freely, strongly resisting the flow of electric charge.
What is a 'semiconductor'?
Semiconductors (like silicon and germanium) have conductivity between conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be modified by temperature, light, or adding impurities (doping), making them essential for electronics.
What is 'electric flux'?
Electric flux (Φ_E) is the total electric field passing through a surface: Φ_E = E·A·cos(θ). Gauss's law relates the net flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge.
A student shuffles across a carpet and accumulates −0.5 μC of charge. She then touches a metal doorknob, producing a spark. If the spark transfers all the charge in 1 millisecond, what is the average current during the spark?
I = Q/t = 0.5 × 10⁻⁶ C / 1 × 10⁻³ s = 5 × 10⁻⁴ A = 0.5 mA. Though the current is small, the high voltage makes the spark noticeable.
Three charges are arranged in a line: +4 μC at x = 0, −2 μC at x = 3 m, and +1 μC at x = 5 m. What is the direction of the net force on the −2 μC charge?
The +4 μC charge at x=0 attracts the −2 μC charge to the left. The +1 μC charge at x=5 attracts the −2 μC charge to the right. Since +4 μC is larger and closer (3 m vs 2 m), the net force is toward the left.

Photocopiers (xerography) rely on electrostatic attraction. Toner with charge opposite to the drum's remaining charge is attracted to and sticks to charged areas, reproducing the image.
Example: During a thunderstorm, the bottom of a cloud has a large negative charge. This induces a positive charge on the ground below. When the electric field becomes strong enough, lightning strikes. If the cloud base is 1000 m above the ground and the breakdown field of air is about 3 × 10⁶ V/m, what approximate voltage between cloud and ground triggers lightning?
V = E × d = 3 × 10⁶ V/m × 1000 m = 3 × 10⁹ V. In practice, stepped leaders reduce the effective gap, so actual breakdown occurs at somewhat lower voltages.
Example: A student shuffles across a carpet and accumulates −0.5 μC of charge. She then touches a metal doorknob, producing a spark. If the spark transfers all the charge in 1 millisecond, what is the average current during the spark?
I = Q/t = 0.5 × 10⁻⁶ C / 1 × 10⁻³ s = 5 × 10⁻⁴ A = 0.5 mA. Though the current is small, the high voltage makes the spark noticeable.
Example: An inkjet printer uses electric fields to steer tiny charged ink droplets onto paper. If a droplet with charge 1.5 × 10⁻¹³ C passes between two plates separated by 2 mm with a voltage of 1000 V between them, what electric force acts on the droplet?
E = V/d = 1000/0.002 = 500,000 V/m. F = qE = 1.5 × 10⁻¹³ × 500,000 = 7.5 × 10⁻⁸ N. This force steers the droplet to the correct position on the page.

  Q1. What is the SI unit of electric charge?
    [ ] Volt (V)
    [ ] Ohm (Ω)
    [ ] Ampere (A)
    [*] Coulomb (C)
    Explanation: The SI unit of electric charge is the coulomb (C). One coulomb equals the charge of approximately 6.24 × 10¹⁸ elementary charges (protons or electrons).

  Q2. The elementary charge (charge on a single proton) is approximately:
    [*] 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
    [ ] 6.02 × 10²³ C
    [ ] 1.6 × 10⁻¹² C
    [ ] 9.0 × 10⁹ C
    Explanation: The elementary charge e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. Protons carry +e and electrons carry −e. All observable charges are integer multiples of this fundamental unit.

  Q3. Two point charges of +2 μC and −2 μC are separated by 0.1 m. What is the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them? (k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²)
    [ ] 36 N
    [*] 3.6 N
    [ ] 0.36 N
    [ ] 0.036 N
    Explanation: F = kq₁q₂/r² = (9 × 10⁹)(2 × 10⁻⁶)(2 × 10⁻⁶)/(0.1)² = (9 × 10⁹)(4 × 10⁻¹²)/0.01 = 0.036/0.01 = 3.6 N.

  Q4. If the distance between two charged objects is tripled, the electrostatic force between them:
    [*] Becomes one-ninth as strong
    [ ] Triples
    [ ] Becomes one-third as strong
    [ ] Becomes nine times as strong
    Explanation: Coulomb's law: F ∝ 1/r². Tripling r gives F_new = F/(3²) = F/9. The force follows an inverse-square law.

  Q5. A positively charged rod is brought near an uncharged metal sphere without touching it. The side of the sphere nearest the rod becomes:
    [ ] Both positively and negatively charged simultaneously
    [ ] Positively charged
    [*] Negatively charged
    [ ] Unchanged
    Explanation: By electrostatic induction, the positive rod attracts free electrons in the metal to the near side, making it negative, while the far side becomes positive. The sphere's net charge remains zero.

  Q6. Electric charge is conserved, meaning:
    [ ] Charge can be destroyed but not created
    [ ] Only positive charges are conserved
    [*] The total electric charge in an isolated system remains constant
    [ ] Charge can be created if enough energy is available
    Explanation: Conservation of charge is a fundamental law: electric charge cannot be created or destroyed. Charge can be transferred between objects, but the total charge of an isolated system remains constant.

  Q7. Two identical conducting spheres carry charges of +6 μC and −2 μC. They are brought into contact and then separated. What charge does each carry afterward?
    [ ] 0 μC each
    [ ] +3 μC each
    [*] +2 μC each
    [ ] +4 μC each
    Explanation: Total charge = +6 + (−2) = +4 μC. When identical conductors touch, charge distributes equally: each gets +4/2 = +2 μC.

  Q8. Materials in which electrons can move freely are called:
    [ ] Superconductors only
    [ ] Insulators
    [ ] Semiconductors
    [*] Conductors
    Explanation: Conductors (like metals) have free electrons that move easily through the material, allowing charge to flow. Insulators restrict electron movement.

  Q9. Like charges (both positive or both negative) will:
    [*] Repel each other
    [ ] Have no interaction
    [ ] Cancel each other out
    [ ] Attract each other
    Explanation: Like charges repel and opposite charges attract. This is a fundamental property of electric charge described by Coulomb's law.

  Q10. The Coulomb constant k in Coulomb's law has a value of approximately:
    [*] 9.0 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
    [ ] 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
    [ ] 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/(N·m²)
    [ ] 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ N·m²/C²
    Explanation: Coulomb's constant k = 9.0 × 10⁹ N·m²/C². The value 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ is the gravitational constant G, and 8.85 × 10⁻¹² is the permittivity of free space ε₀.

  Q11. What is 'Coulomb's law'?
    [*] The law stating that the electrostatic force between two point charges is proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of their separation
    [ ] The law relating current, voltage, and resistance, since the prevailing theory about Coulomb would support this particular interpretation
    [ ] The law relating electric field to charge density due to the well-established relationship between Coulomb trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] The law governing magnetic forces on wires given that prevailing Coulomb conditions tend to reinforce this particular trajectory
    Explanation: Coulomb's law: F = kq₁q₂/r² describes the electrostatic force between two point charges. It is analogous in form to Newton's law of gravitation but much stronger.

  Q12. What does 'electric field' mean?
    [ ] The force between two magnets in most scenarios involving field factors
    [ ] The flow of electrons through a wire according to widely held views on this topic
    [*] A region of space around a charged object where another charge would experience an electric force
    [ ] The energy stored in a capacitor according to widely held views on this topic
    Explanation: An electric field E exists around any charged object. It represents the force per unit positive test charge: E = F/q. Field lines point away from positive charges and toward negative charges.

  Q13. What is 'electrostatic induction'?
    [ ] The flow of charge through a conductor
    [ ] The creation of current by a changing magnetic field
    [*] The redistribution of charges on an object caused by a nearby charged object without direct contact
    [ ] Charging an object by rubbing it with another material
    Explanation: Electrostatic induction is the process by which a charged object causes a redistribution of charges on a nearby conductor without touching it, creating regions of positive and negative charge.

  Q14. What is an 'insulator'?
    [ ] A material that only conducts at high temperatures
    [ ] A material that stores magnetic energy
    [ ] A material that conducts electricity well
    [*] A material that strongly resists the flow of electric charge
    Explanation: An insulator (like glass, rubber, or plastic) is a material in which electrons are tightly bound to atoms and do not move freely, strongly resisting the flow of electric charge.

  Q15. What does 'grounding' mean in electricity?
    [ ] Placing a charged object on the ground physically
    [ ] Removing all charge from an object by heating it
    [ ] Surrounding an object with an insulating material
    [*] Connecting a charged object to the Earth to allow excess charge to flow to or from the ground, neutralizing the object
    Explanation: Grounding connects a charged object to Earth via a conductor. The Earth acts as a virtually infinite charge reservoir, absorbing or supplying electrons to neutralize the object.

  Q16. What does 'quantization of charge' mean?
    [*] Electric charge exists only in integer multiples of the elementary charge e
    [ ] Charge is always positive
    [ ] Charge can take any continuous value
    [ ] Charge depends on the mass of the object
    Explanation: Charge quantization means all observed charges are integer multiples of e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. You cannot have a charge of 0.5e; charge comes in discrete packets.

  Q17. What is a 'semiconductor'?
    [*] A material with electrical conductivity between that of a conductor and an insulator, whose conductivity can be controlled
    [ ] A material that is half conductor and half magnet
    [ ] A material that conducts electricity only when dissolved in water
    [ ] A perfect insulator at all temperatures
    Explanation: Semiconductors (like silicon and germanium) have conductivity between conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be modified by temperature, light, or adding impurities (doping), making them essential for electronics.

  Q18. What does 'charging by contact' mean?
    [ ] Charging an object by placing it near another charged object
    [*] Transferring charge from one object to another by physically touching them together
    [ ] Generating charge using a battery
    [ ] Creating charge through a chemical reaction
    Explanation: Charging by contact (conduction) involves physically touching a charged object to an uncharged one. Charge flows from one to the other until they reach the same potential, giving both the same sign of charge.

  Q19. What is 'electric flux'?
    [*] A measure of the number of electric field lines passing through a surface
    [ ] The rate of flow of electric current
    [ ] The voltage across a capacitor
    [ ] The energy stored in an electric field
    Explanation: Electric flux (Φ_E) is the total electric field passing through a surface: Φ_E = E·A·cos(θ). Gauss's law relates the net flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge.

  Q20. What does 'superposition principle' mean for electric forces?
    [ ] Electric forces only act between two charges at a time
    [ ] Electric forces from multiple charges cancel out completely
    [*] The net force on a charge is the vector sum of the individual forces from each other charge
    [ ] Only the closest charge contributes to the force
    Explanation: The superposition principle states that the net electric force on a charge equals the vector sum of the individual Coulomb forces from every other charge. Each pair interaction is independent.

  Q21. A Van de Graaff generator in a science museum accumulates charge on its metal dome. A child touches the dome and their hair stands on end. Which principle explains the hair standing up?
    [*] Like charges on individual hair strands repel each other
    [ ] Gravitational effects of the charge
    [ ] Static electricity removes the hair's mass
    [ ] Magnetic repulsion between hair strands
    Explanation: When the child touches the dome, charge transfers to their body and hair. Each hair strand acquires the same sign of charge. Like charges repel, causing the strands to push apart and stand on end.

  Q22. A photocopier works by first charging a drum uniformly, then projecting an image onto it that removes charge in lit areas. Toner particles (charged opposite to the drum) stick to the remaining charged areas. This process primarily uses:
    [ ] Magnetic attraction
    [ ] Chemical bonding
    [*] Electrostatic attraction between opposite charges
    [ ] Gravitational settling
    Explanation: Photocopiers (xerography) rely on electrostatic attraction. Toner with charge opposite to the drum's remaining charge is attracted to and sticks to charged areas, reproducing the image.

  Q23. During a thunderstorm, the bottom of a cloud has a large negative charge. This induces a positive charge on the ground below. When the electric field becomes strong enough, lightning strikes.   Q25. A laser printer charges a photoconductor drum to −600 V. A laser then discharges specific spots, and positively charged toner sticks to those discharged areas. If the drum has a capacitance of 10 nF, how much charge was initially on the drum?

  Q26. A student shuffles across a carpet and accumulates −0.5 μC of charge. She then touches a metal doorknob, producing a spark. If the spark transfers all the charge in 1 millisecond, what is the average current during the spark?
    [ ] 0.5 A

--- Lesson 2.2: Magnetism (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Magnetism
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the magnitude of the magnetic force on a proton (q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) moving at 3 × 10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a 0.5 T magnetic field?
F = qvB sin(90°) = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 3 × 10⁶ × 0.5 = 2.4 × 10⁻¹³ N.
What is a 'magnetic dipole'?
A magnetic dipole has both a north and south pole, like a bar magnet or a current-carrying loop. Magnetic monopoles (isolated north or south poles) have never been observed.
What is 'magnetic flux'?
Magnetic flux Φ_B = BA cos(θ) measures the total magnetic field passing through a surface, where θ is the angle between B and the surface normal. It is measured in webers (Wb).
What is the 'right-hand rule' for magnetic force?
For F = qv × B, point the right hand's fingers along v, curl them toward B, and the extended thumb gives the force direction for a positive charge. For negative charges, reverse the direction.
What is a 'solenoid'?
A solenoid is a helical coil of wire that, when current flows through it, produces a uniform magnetic field inside (B = μ₀nI). It behaves like a bar magnet with defined north and south poles.
What is 'paramagnetism'?
Paramagnetic materials (like aluminum, platinum) have atoms with unpaired electrons that partially align with an external field, producing a weak attraction. The effect disappears when the field is removed.
What is the 'Curie temperature'?
The Curie temperature is the critical temperature above which thermal agitation disrupts the alignment of magnetic domains, and a ferromagnet becomes paramagnetic. For iron, it is about 770°C.
An MRI machine uses a superconducting magnet with a field strength of 3 T. A hydrogen proton (q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, m = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg) precesses in this field. What is the cyclotron frequency of the proton?
Cyclotron frequency f = qB/(2πm) = (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 3)/(2π × 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷) = 4.8 × 10⁻¹⁹/(1.05 × 10⁻²⁶) ≈ 4.57 × 10⁷ Hz. The actual proton Larmor frequency in a 3T MRI is about 128 MHz (using the precise gyromagnetic ratio).
An electromagnet in a junkyard lifts cars using a solenoid with 500 turns per meter carrying 200 A. What is the magnetic field inside? (μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A)
B = μ₀nI = 4π × 10⁻⁷ × 500 × 200 = 4π × 10⁻² ≈ 0.126 T. A ferromagnetic core would multiply this dramatically.
A particle accelerator uses a magnetic field of 1.5 T to bend protons (m = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) traveling at 2 × 10⁷ m/s into a circular path. What is the radius of the proton's path?
r = mv/(qB) = (1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ × 2 × 10⁷)/(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 1.5) = 3.34 × 10⁻²⁰/(2.4 × 10⁻¹⁹) ≈ 0.139 m ≈ 0.14 m.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student holds a strong permanent magnet near a CRT (cathode ray tube) television and notices the image distorts with swirling colors. This occurs because:
In a CRT, electrons are steered by electromagnetic fields to hit specific phosphor dots. An external magnet deflects the beam (F = qv × B), causing electrons to hit wrong phosphors and distorting the colors.
Important Concepts
An MRI machine uses a superconducting magnet with a field strength of 3 T. A hydrogen proton (q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, m = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg) precesses in this field. What is the cyclotron frequency of the proton
Cyclotron frequency f = qB/(2πm) = (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 3)/(2π × 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷) = 4.8 × 10⁻¹⁹/(1.05 × 10⁻²⁶) ≈ 4.57 × 10⁷ Hz. The actual proton Larmor frequency in a 3T MRI is about 128 MHz (using the precise gyromagnetic ratio).

--- Lesson 2.2: Magnetism (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What creates a magnetic field?
    [*] Moving electric charges or electric currents
    [ ] Thermal energy
    [ ] Gravitational forces
    [ ] Stationary electric charges
    Explanation: Magnetic fields are produced by moving electric charges (currents). Even permanent magnets derive their magnetism from the motion of electrons within their atoms.

  Q2. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the:
    [*] Tesla
    [ ] Volt
    [ ] Coulomb
    [ ] Ampere
    Explanation: The SI unit of magnetic field (B) is the tesla (T). 1 T = 1 kg/(A·s²). Earth's magnetic field at the surface is roughly 5 × 10⁻⁵ T.

  Q3. Magnetic field lines outside a bar magnet go from:
    [ ] South pole to north pole
    [ ] Positive to negative
    [*] North pole to south pole
    [ ] They form straight lines from the center outward
    Explanation: By convention, magnetic field lines emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole outside the magnet. Inside the magnet, they go from south to north, forming continuous closed loops.

  Q4. If you break a bar magnet in half, you get:
    [ ] One magnetic piece and one non-magnetic piece
    [ ] One north pole piece and one south pole piece
    [*] Two smaller magnets, each with its own north and south pole
    [ ] Two unmagnetized pieces
    Explanation: Magnetic monopoles do not exist. Breaking a magnet always produces two complete magnets, each with a north and south pole. This is true no matter how many times you subdivide.

  Q5. The magnetic force on a moving charged particle is always:
    [ ] Parallel to the velocity
    [ ] In the direction of the electric field
    [ ] Parallel to the magnetic field
    [*] Perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field
    Explanation: The magnetic force F = qv × B is always perpendicular to both v and B. Because it is perpendicular to velocity, it does no work and only changes the direction of motion.

  Q6. A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field experiences a force. What happens if the current is parallel to the magnetic field?
    [*] Zero force
    [ ] Maximum force
    [ ] Oscillating force
    [ ] Force along the wire
    Explanation: F = IL × B = ILB sin(θ). When current is parallel to B, θ = 0° and sin(0°) = 0, so the force is zero. Maximum force occurs when current is perpendicular to B.

  Q7. The magnetic field at the center of a long solenoid depends on:
    [*] The current and the number of turns per unit length
    [ ] Only the radius of the solenoid
    [ ] Only the total number of turns
    [ ] The material of the wire only
    Explanation: For a long solenoid, B = μ₀nI, where n is the number of turns per unit length and I is the current. The field is uniform inside and essentially zero outside.

  Q8. A charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field follows what path?
    [ ] A parabola
    [*] A circle
    [ ] A straight line
    [ ] A spiral of decreasing radius
    Explanation: Since the magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity and constant in magnitude, it provides centripetal acceleration, causing the charge to move in a circle: r = mv/(qB).

  Q9. The right-hand rule for a straight current-carrying wire tells you that the magnetic field lines around the wire form:
    [ ] Radial lines pointing outward
    [*] Concentric circles around the wire
    [ ] Straight lines parallel to the wire
    [ ] Spirals along the wire
    Explanation: The magnetic field around a long straight current-carrying wire forms concentric circles. Point the right thumb in the direction of current, and the fingers curl in the direction of B.

  Q10. What is the magnitude of the magnetic force on a proton (q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) moving at 3 × 10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a 0.5 T magnetic field?
    [*] 2.4 × 10⁻¹³ N
    [ ] 4.8 × 10⁻¹³ N
    [ ] 2.4 × 10⁻¹⁰ N
    [ ] 7.5 × 10⁻¹⁴ N
    Explanation: F = qvB sin(90°) = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 3 × 10⁶ × 0.5 = 2.4 × 10⁻¹³ N.

  Q11. What is a 'magnetic dipole'?
    [ ] A point charge at rest, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] A single moving charge, despite what intuition might initially
    [*] An object with a north and south pole, such as a bar magnet or current loop
    [ ] An isolated magnetic north pole, especially during periods
    Explanation: A magnetic dipole has both a north and south pole, like a bar magnet or a current-carrying loop. Magnetic monopoles (isolated north or south poles) have never been observed.

  Q12. What does 'ferromagnetism' mean?
    [ ] A weak repulsion from magnetic fields exhibited by all materials
    [*] The strong magnetic behavior in materials like iron, where atomic magnetic moments align cooperatively to produce large net magnetization
    [ ] Magnetism generated solely by electric current
    [ ] The magnetism of superconductors
    Explanation: Ferromagnetism occurs in materials (iron, nickel, cobalt) where magnetic domains—regions of aligned atomic magnetic moments—can align to produce strong magnetization.

--- Lesson 2.3: Electric Potential (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Potential
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'electric potential'?
Electric potential V is the potential energy per unit charge: V = U/q. It is a scalar field that describes the energy landscape for charges, measured in volts (J/C).
What is an 'equipotential surface'?
An equipotential surface connects all points at the same potential. No work is required to move a charge along an equipotential surface. These surfaces are always perpendicular to electric field lines.
What is an 'electron-volt' (eV)?
An electron-volt is a unit of energy: 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. It is the kinetic energy gained by a particle with charge e when accelerated through 1 V.
What is a 'volt' defined as?
One volt = one joule per coulomb (1 V = 1 J/C). It represents the potential energy per unit charge.
What is 'electric potential gradient'?
The electric potential gradient is how rapidly V changes with position. The electric field is the negative gradient of potential: E = −dV/dr. Steeper gradients mean stronger fields.
A defibrillator charges a capacitor to 5000 V and delivers approximately 200 J to a patient's chest. If the capacitor stores energy U = (1/2)CV², what is the approximate capacitance?
C = 2U/V² = 2(200)/(5000)² = 400/25,000,000 = 1.6 × 10⁻⁵ F = 16 μF.
A Van de Graaff generator has a spherical dome of radius 0.15 m charged to a potential of 300,000 V. What is the charge on the dome? (k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²)
For a sphere, V = kQ/R, so Q = VR/k = 300,000 × 0.15 / (9 × 10⁹) = 45,000/(9 × 10⁹) = 5 × 10⁻⁶ C = 5 μC.
A charged parallel-plate capacitor has a uniform electric field of 10,000 V/m between its plates, which are 2 cm apart. What is the potential difference between the plates?
ΔV = Ed = 10,000 × 0.02 = 200 V.
An alpha particle (charge +2e, mass 6.64 × 10⁻²⁷ kg) is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of 1000 V. What is its final speed?
KE = qΔV = 2(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)(1000) = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁶ J. v = sqrt(2KE/m) = sqrt(2 × 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁶ / 6.64 × 10⁻²⁷) = sqrt(9.64 × 10¹⁰) ≈ 3.1 × 10⁵ m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: The SI unit of electric potential (voltage) is the:
Electric potential is measured in volts (V). One volt equals one joule per coulomb: 1 V = 1 J/C.
Example: What does 'potential difference' (voltage) mean?
Potential difference (voltage) ΔV = V_B − V_A is the work done per unit charge to move a test charge from A to B: ΔV = W/q. It drives current in circuits.
Example: A bird sits safely on a high-voltage power line carrying 100,000 V. Why doesn't the bird get shocked?
Both of the bird's feet are on the same wire at essentially the same potential. With no potential difference across its body, no current flows through it. If it touched two wires at different potentials, it would be electrocuted.

  Key Concepts: Capacitors and Dielectrics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI unit of capacitance?
Capacitance is measured in farads (F). 1 F = 1 C/V. One farad is very large; most practical capacitors are measured in microfarads (μF) or picofarads (pF).
A 4 μF and a 6 μF capacitor are connected in series. What is the equivalent capacitance?
1/C = 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12. C = 12/5 = 2.4 μF.
What is a 'capacitor'?
A capacitor stores energy in the electric field between two conducting plates separated by an insulating material. It stores charge Q proportional to voltage: Q = CV.
What is a 'dielectric'?
A dielectric is a non-conducting material (glass, paper, ceramic) inserted between capacitor plates. It becomes polarized in the electric field, reducing the effective field and increasing capacitance by factor κ.
What is 'polarization' of a dielectric?
In an applied electric field, molecular dipoles in the dielectric align (or charge distribution shifts), creating a net internal field that opposes the applied field. This reduces the effective field and increases capacitance.
What is 'energy density' in the context of a capacitor?
Energy density u = (1/2)ε₀E² gives the energy stored per unit volume in an electric field. This concept applies to any region of space with an electric field, not just capacitors.
What is a 'supercapacitor'?
Supercapacitors (ultracapacitors) use high surface area electrodes and very thin effective dielectric layers to achieve capacitances of farads, bridging the gap between batteries and conventional capacitors.
Three 6 μF capacitors are connected in parallel across a 12 V battery. What is the total charge stored?
C_total = 6 + 6 + 6 = 18 μF. Q = CV = 18 × 10⁻⁶ × 12 = 216 × 10⁻⁶ C = 216 μC.
A researcher studying lightning builds a parallel-plate capacitor model. The plates are 10 m × 10 m and separated by 1000 m of air (κ = 1). What is the capacitance of this model? (ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m)
C = ε₀A/d = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² × 100/1000 = 8.85 × 10⁻¹³ F ≈ 0.885 pF. This tiny capacitance shows why enormous voltages build up in thunderclouds.
A ceramic capacitor used in a computer circuit has a capacitance of 100 pF. A dielectric with κ = 2000 is used with a plate separation of 0.01 mm. What is the approximate plate area? (ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m)
A = Cd/(κε₀) = (100 × 10⁻¹²)(10⁻⁵)/(2000 × 8.85 × 10⁻¹²) = 10⁻¹⁵/(1.77 × 10⁻⁸) ≈ 5.65 × 10⁻⁸ m². High-κ ceramics allow tiny capacitors.
Real-World Applications

For an isolated capacitor (Q fixed), doubling d halves C (since C = ε₀A/d). Since Q = CV, V = Q/C doubles.
Example: A defibrillator stores 360 J of energy in a capacitor. If the capacitance is 32 μF, what voltage must the capacitor be charged to?
V = sqrt(2U/C) = sqrt(2 × 360 / (32 × 10⁻⁶)) = sqrt(720/3.2 × 10⁻⁵) = sqrt(2.25 × 10⁷) ≈ 4,743 V.
Example: A student wants to store 1 J of energy using a 10 μF capacitor. What voltage does she need to charge it to?
V = sqrt(2U/C) = sqrt(2 × 1/(10 × 10⁻⁶)) = sqrt(200,000) ≈ 447 V.

--- Lesson 2.5: Electric Circuits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electric Circuits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Three 6 Ω resistors are connected in series. What is the total resistance?
In series, R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ = 6 + 6 + 6 = 18 Ω.
Two 10 Ω resistors are connected in parallel. What is the equivalent resistance?
For two equal resistors in parallel: R = R₁/2 = 10/2 = 5 Ω. In general: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂.
What is the power dissipated by a 100 Ω resistor with 2 A flowing through it?
P = I²R = (2)²(100) = 400 W. Equivalently, V = IR = 200 V, so P = IV = 2 × 200 = 400 W.
What is 'Ohm's law'?
Ohm's law: V = IR states that the voltage across an ohmic conductor is proportional to the current through it. R is the constant of proportionality (resistance).
What is 'Kirchhoff's junction rule'?
Kirchhoff's junction (current) rule is based on conservation of charge: the total current entering a node must equal the total current leaving it.
What is 'Kirchhoff's loop rule'?
Kirchhoff's loop (voltage) rule is based on conservation of energy: the sum of EMFs and voltage drops around any closed loop is zero.
What is 'electric power'?
Electric power P = IV = I²R = V²/R is the rate of energy transfer or conversion in a circuit. It is measured in watts (W).
What is a 'voltmeter' and how is it connected?
A voltmeter measures the potential difference across a component and must be connected in parallel. It has very high internal resistance so it draws negligible current.
What is an 'ammeter' and how is it connected?
An ammeter measures the current flowing through a component and must be connected in series. It has very low internal resistance so it does not significantly affect the circuit.
A household circuit breaker is rated at 15 A on a 120 V line. What is the maximum power that can be drawn from this circuit before the breaker trips?
P = IV = 15 × 120 = 1,800 W. Exceeding this wattage draws more than 15 A and trips the breaker.
A battery with EMF 9 V and internal resistance 0.5 Ω is connected to an external 4 Ω resistor. What is the current in the circuit and the terminal voltage of the battery?
I = ε/(R + r) = 9/(4 + 0.5) = 9/4.5 = 2 A. V_terminal = ε − Ir = 9 − 2(0.5) = 8 V.
An electrician measures the resistance of a copper wire that is 100 m long with a cross-sectional area of 1 mm² = 10⁻⁶ m². If copper's resistivity is 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m, what is the resistance?
R = ρL/A = (1.7 × 10⁻⁸)(100)/(10⁻⁶) = 1.7 × 10⁻⁶/10⁻⁶ = 1.7 Ω.
A laboratory power supply delivers 5 V to a circuit containing a 10 Ω and 20 Ω resistor in series. What is the voltage across the 20 Ω resistor?
Using the voltage divider: V₂₀ = V × R₂₀/(R₁₀ + R₂₀) = 5 × 20/30 = 100/30 ≈ 3.33 V.
A toaster rated at 1200 W operates on a 120 V circuit. What is the resistance of its heating element, and how much current does it draw?
R = V²/P = (120)²/1200 = 14400/1200 = 12 Ω. I = P/V = 1200/120 = 10 A.
Real-World Applications
Example: In a parallel circuit, the voltage across each branch is:
In parallel, all branches connect to the same two nodes, so the voltage across each branch is identical. The current divides inversely with resistance.
Example: A battery with EMF 9 V and internal resistance 0.5 Ω is connected to an external 4 Ω resistor. What is the current in the circuit and the terminal voltage of the battery?
I = ε/(R + r) = 9/(4 + 0.5) = 9/4.5 = 2 A. V_terminal = ε − Ir = 9 − 2(0.5) = 8 V.
Example: A laboratory power supply delivers 5 V to a circuit containing a 10 Ω and 20 Ω resistor in series. What is the voltage across the 20 Ω resistor?
Using the voltage divider: V₂₀ = V × R₂₀/(R₁₀ + R₂₀) = 5 × 20/30 = 100/30 ≈ 3.33 V.

  Q18. What does 'short circuit' mean?
    [ ] A circuit that is physically short in length
    [*] A low-resistance path that allows excessive current to flow, bypassing the intended circuit
    [ ] A circuit that has been turned off
    [ ] A circuit with very high resistance
    Explanation: A short circuit is an unintended low-resistance path that diverts most of the current. It causes very high current flow, which can overheat wires and cause fires. Fuses and circuit breakers protect against this.

  Q19. What is a 'voltmeter' and how is it connected?
    [ ] A device that measures power; connected at the source
    [*] A device that measures voltage (potential difference); connected in parallel across the component
    [ ] A device that measures current; connected in series
    [ ] A device that measures resistance; connected in series
    Explanation: A voltmeter measures the potential difference across a component and must be connected in parallel. It has very high internal resistance so it draws negligible current.

  Q20. What is an 'ammeter' and how is it connected?
    [*] A device that measures current; connected in series
    [ ] A device that measures resistance; connected in parallel
    [ ] A device that measures voltage; connected in parallel
    [ ] A device that measures charge; connected at the battery
    Explanation: An ammeter measures the current flowing through a component and must be connected in series. It has very low internal resistance so it does not significantly affect the circuit.

  Q21. A household circuit breaker is rated at 15 A on a 120 V line. What is the maximum power that can be drawn from this circuit before the breaker trips?
    [*] 1,800 W
    [ ] 180 W
    [ ] 8 W
    [ ] 1,500 W
    Explanation: P = IV = 15 × 120 = 1,800 W. Exceeding this wattage draws more than 15 A and trips the breaker.

  Q22. A string of old-fashioned Christmas lights has 50 bulbs in series, each rated at 2.4 V. If one bulb burns out (open circuit), what happens?
    [*] All bulbs go dark because the circuit is broken
    [ ] Only that bulb goes dark
    [ ] Half the bulbs go dark
    [ ] The remaining bulbs get brighter
    Explanation: In a series circuit, current flows through all bulbs sequentially. If one bulb burns out (creates an open gap), the circuit is broken and no current flows to any bulb.

  Q23. An electric heater has a resistance of 12 Ω and operates on a 240 V supply. How much current does it draw, and what power does it consume?
    [ ] 5 A, 1200 W
    [ ] 20 A, 2400 W
    [*] 20 A, 4800 W
    [ ] 10 A, 2400 W
    Explanation: I = V/R = 240/12 = 20 A. P = IV = 20 × 240 = 4800 W = 4.8 kW.

  Q24. A battery with EMF 9 V and internal resistance 0.5 Ω is connected to an external 4 Ω resistor. What is the current in the circuit and the terminal voltage of the battery?
    [ ] I = 1 A, V_terminal = 4 V
    [ ] I = 18 A, V_terminal = 0 V
    [*] I = 2 A, V_terminal = 8 V
    [ ] I = 2.25 A, V_terminal = 9 V
    Explanation: I = ε/(R + r) = 9/(4 + 0.5) = 9/4.5 = 2 A. V_terminal = ε − Ir = 9 − 2(0.5) = 8 V.

  Q25. An electrician measures the resistance of a copper wire that is 100 m long with a cross-sectional area of 1 mm² = 10⁻⁶ m². If copper's resistivity is 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m, what is the resistance?
    [*] 1.7 Ω
    [ ] 0.017 Ω
    [ ] 0.17 Ω
    [ ] 17 Ω
    Explanation: R = ρL/A = (1.7 × 10⁻⁸)(100)/(10⁻⁶) = 1.7 × 10⁻⁶/10⁻⁶ = 1.7 Ω.

  Q26. A student builds a circuit with a 6 V battery and two resistors: 3 Ω and 6 Ω in parallel. What total current does the battery supply?
    [ ] 2 A
    [ ] 9 A
    [ ] 1 A
    [*] 3 A
    Explanation: Parallel resistance: 1/R = 1/3 + 1/6 = 3/6 = 1/2, so R = 2 Ω. Total current: I = V/R = 6/2 = 3 A.

  Q27. An electric car's motor draws 200 A from a 400 V battery pack while accelerating. What power does the motor consume?
    [ ] 80 W within
    [*] 80,000 W (80 kW)
    [ ] 800 W, even
    [ ] 2 W,
    Explanation: P = IV = 200 × 400 = 80,000 W = 80 kW. This is roughly 107 horsepower.

  Q28. A homeowner's electricity bill shows they used 500 kWh in one month. If electricity costs $0.12/kWh, how much is the bill?
    [ ] $6,000
    [*] $60
    [ ] $6
    [ ] $600
    Explanation: Cost = 500 kWh × $0.12/kWh = $60. A kilowatt-hour is a unit of energy (1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J).

  Q29. A laboratory power supply delivers 5 V to a circuit containing a 10 Ω and 20 Ω resistor in series. What is the voltage across the 20 Ω resistor?
    [ ] 5 V
    [ ] About 1.67 V
    [*] About 3.33 V
    [ ] 2.5 V
    Explanation: Using the voltage divider: V₂₀ = V × R₂₀/(R₁₀ + R₂₀) = 5 × 20/30 = 100/30 ≈ 3.33 V.

  Q30. A toaster rated at 1200 W operates on a 120 V circuit. What is the resistance of its heating element, and how much current does it draw?
    [ ] R = 0.1 Ω, I = 1200 A
    [ ] R = 10 Ω, I = 12 A
    [*] R = 12 Ω, I = 10 A
    [ ] R = 120 Ω, I = 1 A
    Explanation: R = V²/P = (120)²/1200 = 14400/1200 = 12 Ω. I = P/V = 1200/120 = 10 A.

--- Lesson 2.5: Electric Circuits (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Thermodynamics
     A: The study of heat, energy, and the relationships between thermal, mechanical, and other forms of energy

  2. Q: Define: Entropy
     A: A measure of disorder in a system; the second law states that entropy in an isolated system always increases

  3. Q: Define: Electric Field
     A: A region of space around a charged object where another charged object experiences a force

  4. Q: Define: Magnetic Field
     A: A region of space around a magnet or moving charge where magnetic forces are exerted

  5. Q: Define: Electromagnetic Wave
     A: A wave that consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation

  6. Q: Define: Capacitor
     A: A device that stores electrical energy by accumulating electric charge on two conducting plates

  7. Q: Define: Resistance
     A: The opposition to the flow of electric current through a material, measured in ohms

  8. Q: Define: Interference
     A: The phenomenon where two or more waves overlap and combine to create a resultant wave

  9. Q: Which of the following is the primary way water returns to the atmosphere?
     A: Evaporation from oceans and transpiration from plants

  10. Q: Which human activity most directly affects the water cycle?
     A: Urbanization reduces infiltration and increases runoff
