--- Lesson 3.1: Magnetic Fields and Forces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Magnetic Fields and Forces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the force per unit length between two parallel wires carrying currents I1 and I2 separated by distance d?
The force per length between parallel wires is F/L = mu_0*I1*I2/(2*pi*d). Same-direction currents attract; opposite-direction currents repel.
What is a 'magnetic field'?
A magnetic field is a vector field that exerts forces on moving electric charges and magnetic dipoles. It is produced by currents and changing electric fields.
What is the 'Lorentz force'?
The Lorentz force F = q(E + v x B) is the total force on a charged particle due to both electric and magnetic fields.
What is Ampere's law?
Ampere's law: integral of B dot dl = mu_0 * I_enclosed relates the magnetic field circulation to the enclosed current.
What is the 'Biot-Savart law'?
The Biot-Savart law gives the contribution to B from an infinitesimal current element. It is the magnetic analog of Coulomb's law.
What is a 'magnetic monopole'?
A magnetic monopole would be an isolated north or south pole. Despite extensive searches, no monopoles have been observed.
What is 'magnetic flux density'?
Magnetic flux density B (in teslas) is the magnetic field vector. Flux = B*A*cos(theta).
A mass spectrometer injects ions with charge +e and mass 3.82 x 10^-25 kg at 10^5 m/s into a 0.5 T field. What is the radius of their circular path?
r = mv/(qB) = (3.82e-25)(1e5)/((1.6e-19)(0.5)) = 3.82e-20/8e-20 = 0.478 m.
An electric motor coil has 100 turns, each 0.1 m x 0.05 m, in a 0.2 T field carrying 5 A. What is the maximum torque?
Torque_max = NIAB = 100 * 5 * (0.005) * 0.2 = 0.5 N*m.
Two parallel wires 0.1 m apart carry 10 A in the same direction. What is the force per meter?
F/L = mu_0*I1*I2/(2*pi*d) = (4pi*1e-7)(100)/(2pi*0.1) = 2e-4 N/m. Same direction = attractive.
Earth's magnetic field is about 5 x 10^-5 T. A cosmic ray proton enters at 3 x 10^7 m/s perpendicular to the field. What is the radius of its path?
r = mv/(qB) = (1.67e-27)(3e7)/((1.6e-19)(5e-5)) = 5.01e-20/8e-24 = 6260 m = 6.3 km.
A cyclotron accelerates protons in a 1.5 T field. What is the cyclotron frequency?
f = qB/(2*pi*m) = (1.6e-19)(1.5)/(2pi*1.67e-27) = 2.4e-19/1.05e-26 = 2.29e7 Hz = 22.9 MHz.

This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.

  Key Concepts: Electromagnetic Induction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A coil with 200 turns experiences a flux change of 0.05 Wb in 0.1 s. What is the induced EMF?
EMF = N * delta_Phi/delta_t = 200 * 0.05/0.1 = 100 V.
What is 'electromagnetic induction'?
Electromagnetic induction is the generation of an EMF when the magnetic flux through a circuit changes, discovered by Faraday and Henry.
What is 'Faraday's law of induction'?
Faraday's law: EMF = -N*d(Phi)/dt. The minus sign (Lenz's law) indicates the induced EMF opposes the flux change.
What is an 'eddy current'?
Eddy currents are loops of current induced in conductors by changing B fields. They cause heating and are used in induction cooktops and braking systems.
What is 'self-inductance'?
Self-inductance L measures a coil's tendency to oppose changes in its own current. The induced back-EMF is EMF = -L*dI/dt.
What is 'back-EMF'?
Back-EMF is the voltage induced in an inductor that opposes the current change. In motors, it opposes the applied voltage.
What is 'motional EMF'?
Motional EMF = BvL is generated when a conductor of length L moves at speed v perpendicular to field B.

--- Lesson 3.5: Alternating Current (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Alternating Current
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'alternating current' (AC)?
AC is electric current that periodically reverses direction. Most power grids use sinusoidal AC.
What is 'impedance'?
Impedance Z is the AC equivalent of resistance. It includes resistance R and net reactance (X_L - X_C).
What is 'resonance' in an AC circuit?
At resonance, inductive and capacitive reactances cancel, impedance is minimum (= R), and current is maximum.
What is a 'transformer'?
A transformer uses mutual induction between primary and secondary coils to step voltage up or down while conserving power.
What is 'capacitive reactance'?
Capacitive reactance X_C = 1/(2*pi*f*C) decreases with frequency. At high frequencies, capacitors pass current easily.
What is 'inductive reactance'?
Inductive reactance X_L = 2*pi*f*L increases with frequency. At high frequencies, inductors strongly oppose current changes.
A 120 V, 60 Hz source is connected to a 100 ohm resistor. What is the rms current and average power?
I_rms = V_rms/R = 120/100 = 1.2 A. P = I^2*R = 1.44*100 = 144 W.
A Tesla coil is a resonant transformer that produces very high voltages. If the primary has 10 turns at 120 V and the secondary has 1000 turns, what is the secondary voltage?
V_s = V_p * N_s/N_p = 120 * 1000/10 = 12,000 V.
Real-World Applications
Example: The peak voltage of 120 V rms AC is approximately:
V_peak = V_rms * sqrt(2) = 120 * 1.414 = 169.7 V, approximately 170 V.

In a pure resistor, current and voltage rise and fall together: they are in phase.
Example: Long-distance power transmission uses high voltage (e.g., 500 kV) rather than the 120 V used in homes because:
P = IV, so for the same power, higher voltage means lower current. Power loss = I^2*R, so lower current dramatically reduces transmission losses.
