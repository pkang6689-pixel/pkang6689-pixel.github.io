--- Lesson 9.1: Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'thermal equilibrium'?
Thermal equilibrium is the condition where objects in contact reach the same temperature and net heat exchange ceases.
What is 'absolute zero'?
Absolute zero (0 K) is the theoretical lower limit of temperature. At this point, particles have minimal possible energy.
What is the 'Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics'?
The Zeroth Law establishes transitivity of thermal equilibrium and provides the fundamental basis for temperature measurement.
What is 'Boltzmann's constant'?
Boltzmann's constant k = 1.38 x 10^-23 J/K connects microscopic molecular energy to macroscopic temperature.
What is 'thermal energy'?
Thermal energy is the total kinetic and potential energy of the molecules due to their random motion and intermolecular forces.
What is a 'thermometer'?
A thermometer exploits the Zeroth Law by reaching thermal equilibrium with the measured object and displaying the shared temperature.
What is the 'Kelvin scale'?
The Kelvin scale is an absolute scale where 0 K is absolute zero. One kelvin equals one degree Celsius in size.
What is 'thermal contact'?
Thermal contact means heat can flow between systems. This does not require physical contact since radiation can transfer heat across a vacuum.
What is 'internal energy'?
Internal energy U includes all microscopic kinetic and potential energies. It does not include bulk kinetic or potential energy of the system.
What is 'thermal expansion'?
Thermal expansion occurs because increased temperature causes greater molecular vibration, increasing average separation between atoms.
A 500 g aluminum block at 200 C is placed in 1 kg of water at 20 C in an insulated container. Using c_Al = 900 J/(kg*C) and c_water = 4186 J/(kg*C), what is the equilibrium temperature?
Heat lost = heat gained: 0.5(900)(200-T) = 1(4186)(T-20). Solving: 90000 - 450T = 4186T - 83720. 173720 = 4636T. T = 37.5 C. Approximately 35.7 C accounting for rounding.
A researcher measures the rms speed of nitrogen molecules (M = 28 g/mol) at 300 K. Using v_rms = sqrt(3RT/M), what is the approximate rms speed?
v_rms = sqrt(3 * 8.314 * 300 / 0.028) = sqrt(267214) = 517 m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: The average translational kinetic energy of an ideal gas molecule is given by:
Each molecule has average translational KE = (3/2)kT, where k is Boltzmann's constant and T is in Kelvin.
Example: An ideal gas in a sealed container has a temperature of 300 K. If the temperature is doubled to 600 K, the average molecular kinetic energy:
KE_avg = (3/2)kT. Since KE is directly proportional to T, doubling T doubles the average kinetic energy.

--- Lesson 9.2: Heat Transfer Methods (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heat Transfer Methods
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'thermal conductivity'?
Thermal conductivity k measures how well a material conducts heat. Higher k means faster heat conduction.
What is 'convection'?
Convection transfers heat through fluid motion. Natural convection uses buoyancy; forced convection uses external means like fans.
What is 'thermal radiation'?
All objects emit electromagnetic radiation due to thermal energy. The spectrum and intensity depend on temperature.
What is 'emissivity'?
Emissivity e ranges from 0 (perfect reflector) to 1 (perfect black body). It describes how efficiently a surface radiates thermal energy.
What is 'Newton's law of cooling'?
Newton's law of cooling: dT/dt = -h(T - T_env). The rate of temperature change is proportional to the temperature difference.
What is an 'R-value' in insulation?
R-value = L/k (thickness divided by thermal conductivity). Higher R-value means better insulation and less heat transfer.
What is 'forced convection'?
Forced convection uses external devices to move fluid, greatly enhancing heat transfer rates compared to natural convection.
What is 'Wien's displacement law'?
Wien's law: lambda_max * T = 2.898 x 10^-3 m*K. Hotter objects peak at shorter wavelengths (bluer light).
What is 'thermal resistance'?
Thermal resistance R_th = L/(kA) opposes heat flow. Like electrical resistance in series, thermal resistances add for layered materials.
What is a 'heat sink'?
Heat sinks use high-conductivity materials (like aluminum) with fins to increase surface area, enhancing heat dissipation by conduction and convection.
A window pane is 0.5 cm thick glass (k = 0.8 W/(m*K)) with area 2 m^2. If the inside is 20 C and outside is -10 C, what is the rate of heat loss?
P = kA(Delta T)/L = 0.8 * 2 * 30 / 0.005 = 9600 W.
A spherical black body at 600 K has radius 0.1 m. What is its radiated power? (sigma = 5.67 x 10^-8 W/(m^2*K^4))
A = 4*pi*r^2 = 4*pi*(0.01) = 0.1257 m^2. P = sigma*A*T^4 = 5.67e-8 * 0.1257 * (600)^4 = 5.67e-8 * 0.1257 * 1.296e11 = 924 W.
A double-pane window has two 4 mm glass panes (k = 0.8 W/(m*K)) separated by a 1 cm air gap (k = 0.025 W/(m*K)). For 1 m^2 area and 30 C temperature difference, what is the approximate heat flow rate?
Total R = R_glass1 + R_air + R_glass2 = 0.004/0.8 + 0.01/0.025 + 0.004/0.8 = 0.005 + 0.4 + 0.005 = 0.41 m^2*K/W. P = A*Delta T/R = 1*30/0.41 = 73 W.
The Sun's surface temperature is about 5800 K. Using Wien's law (b = 2.898 x 10^-3 m*K), what is the peak emission wavelength?
lambda_max = b/T = 2.898e-3/5800 = 5.0e-7 m = 500 nm, in the visible green-yellow range.

  Q26. A coffee cup at 90 C cools to 70 C in 10 minutes in a 20 C room. Using Newton's law of cooling, approximately how long to cool from 70 C to 50 C?

  Key Concepts: Specific Heat and Calorimetry
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'specific heat capacity'?
Specific heat capacity c appears in Q = mc(Delta T). Materials with high c resist temperature changes; those with low c change temperature easily.
What is 'calorimetry'?
Calorimetry measures heat transfer during processes. It uses the principle that in an isolated system, total heat exchange is zero.
What is 'latent heat'?
Latent heat (Q = mL) is 'hidden' heat that changes the phase of a substance without changing its temperature.
What is 'latent heat of fusion'?

What is 'latent heat of vaporization'?

What is a 'calorimeter'?
A calorimeter is an insulated container that minimizes heat exchange with the environment, enabling accurate measurement of heat transfers.
What is 'heat capacity'?
Heat capacity C = mc is the product of mass and specific heat. It represents the heat needed to raise the entire object by 1 K.
What is 'phase transition'?
Phase transitions (melting, boiling, sublimation, etc.) occur at specific temperatures where energy goes into changing intermolecular bonding rather than temperature.
What is 'thermal equilibrium' in the context of calorimetry?
In calorimetry, thermal equilibrium is the final state. The equilibrium temperature is found by setting Q_lost + Q_gained = 0.
What is 'molar heat capacity'?
Molar heat capacity C_m = Mc where M is molar mass. For ideal monatomic gases, C_v = (3/2)R and C_p = (5/2)R.

--- Lesson 9.4: Laws of Thermodynamics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Laws of Thermodynamics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'First Law of Thermodynamics'?
The First Law is conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems: Delta U = Q - W.
What is 'entropy'?
Entropy (S) quantifies disorder. Delta S = Q/T for reversible processes. The Second Law requires Delta S >= 0 for isolated systems.
What is a 'Carnot engine'?
The Carnot engine is an ideal, reversible engine with maximum efficiency e = 1 - T_C/T_H. No real engine can exceed its efficiency.
What is an 'isothermal process'?
Isothermal (iso = same, thermal = temperature) processes maintain constant T. For ideal gases, PV = constant on isotherms.
What is an 'adiabatic process'?
In adiabatic processes, Q = 0. Work changes internal energy directly. Adiabatic curves are steeper than isotherms on PV diagrams.
What is a 'heat engine'?
A heat engine absorbs Q_H from a hot reservoir, converts some to work W, and exhausts Q_C to a cold reservoir. Efficiency = W/Q_H.
What is 'internal energy'?
Internal energy U includes all microscopic energies. For ideal gas: U = (f/2)nRT where f is degrees of freedom. Delta U = nCv*Delta T.
What is a 'reversible process'?
A reversible process is quasi-static with no friction or dissipation. It produces zero entropy change and represents the theoretical ideal.
What is 'PV work'?
PV work W = integral of P dV represents the area under the curve on a PV diagram. Expansion (Delta V > 0) means positive work done by the gas.
What is the 'Kelvin-Planck statement' of the Second Law?
The Kelvin-Planck statement says no heat engine operating in a cycle can convert 100% of heat to work. Some heat must be exhausted.
A Carnot engine operates between a hot reservoir at 500 K and a cold reservoir at 300 K. What is its efficiency?
e = 1 - T_C/T_H = 1 - 300/500 = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4 = 40%.
An ideal monatomic gas (Cv = 3R/2) undergoes adiabatic compression from 2 L at 300 K to 1 L. What is the final temperature? (gamma = 5/3)
TV^(gamma-1) = constant. T1*V1^(2/3) = T2*V2^(2/3). 300*(2)^(2/3) = T2*(1)^(2/3). T2 = 300*2^(2/3) = 300*1.587 = 476 K.
A diesel engine compresses air adiabatically from 300 K with a compression ratio of 20:1. If gamma = 1.4, what is the final temperature?
T2 = T1*(V1/V2)^(gamma-1) = 300*(20)^0.4 = 300*3.314 = 994 K. Hot enough to ignite diesel fuel without a spark plug.

    [ ] All heat engines have the same efficiency without accounting for additional influences
    Explanation: The First Law is conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems: Delta U = Q - W.

  Q12. What is 'entropy'?
    [*] A thermodynamic quantity measuring the amount of disorder or the number of possible microscopic arrangements in a system, with units J/K
    [ ] A type of energy, particularly when Delta considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    [ ] The heat capacity of a system, since standard models of Delta behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    [ ] The work done in a cycle due to the well-established relationship between Delta trends and broader outcomes
    Explanation: Entropy (S) quantifies disorder. Delta S = Q/T for reversible processes. The Second Law requires Delta S >= 0 for isolated systems.

  Q13. What is a 'Carnot engine'?
    [ ] An engine that converts 100% of heat to work in most scenarios involving engine factors
    [*] A theoretical heat engine operating on a reversible cycle that achieves the maximum possible efficiency between two temperature reservoirs
    [ ] A real engine used in cars, particularly when engine considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    [ ] A steam engine invented by Carnot, since standard models of engine behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    Explanation: The Carnot engine is an ideal, reversible engine with maximum efficiency e = 1 - T_C/T_H. No real engine can exceed its efficiency.

  Q14. What is an 'isothermal process'?
    [ ] A process at constant pressure, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] A process with no heat exchange under most circumstances involving constant
    [*] A thermodynamic process that occurs at constant temperature, where the system remains in thermal equilibrium with a reservoir
    [ ] A process at constant volume when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    Explanation: Isothermal (iso = same, thermal = temperature) processes maintain constant T. For ideal gases, PV = constant on isotherms.

  Q15. What is an 'adiabatic process'?
    [ ] A process at constant pressure, which is a common assumption in the field
    [*] A thermodynamic process in which no heat is exchanged between the system and its surroundings (Q = 0)
    [ ] A reversible process only in most scenarios involving adiabatic factors
    [ ] A process at constant temperature, based on conventional assumptions about adiabatic
    Explanation: In adiabatic processes, Q = 0. Work changes internal energy directly. Adiabatic curves are steeper than isotherms on PV diagrams.

  Q16. What is a 'heat engine'?
    [ ] A refrigerator, since the prevailing theory about engine would support this particular interpretation
    [ ] A device that only absorbs heat without accounting for additional influences
    [*] A device that converts thermal energy into mechanical work by transferring heat from a hot reservoir to a cold reservoir
    [ ] Any device that produces heat according to widely held views on this topic
    Explanation: A heat engine absorbs Q_H from a hot reservoir, converts some to work W, and exhausts Q_C to a cold reservoir. Efficiency = W/Q_H.

  Q17. What is 'internal energy'?
    [ ] The work done on the system considering that the key variables involved tend to move in this direction together
    [ ] The heat absorbed by the system, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] The pressure times volume, since the relevant Delta factors typically produce this outcome in practice
    [*] The total microscopic kinetic and potential energy of all molecules in a system, which for an ideal gas depends only on temperature
    Explanation: Internal energy U includes all microscopic energies. For ideal gas: U = (f/2)nRT where f is degrees of freedom. Delta U = nCv*Delta T.

  Q18. What is a 'reversible process'?
    [ ] Any cyclic process considering that the key variables involved tend to move in this direction together
    [*] An idealized process that can be reversed without any net change in the system and surroundings, occurring through a series of equilibrium states
    [ ] A process that returns to its starting point in most scenarios involving process factors
    [ ] A process that can only go in one direction, especially during periods of significant change
    Explanation: A reversible process is quasi-static with no friction or dissipation. It produces zero entropy change and represents the theoretical ideal.

  Q19. What is 'PV work'?
    [ ] The product of pressure and velocity according to widely held views on this topic
    [ ] The energy stored in a piston when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [ ] The potential energy of a gas when considering all relevant work factors
    [*] The work done by or on a gas during expansion or compression, calculated as the integral of P dV
    Explanation: PV work W = integral of P dV represents the area under the curve on a PV diagram. Expansion (Delta V > 0) means positive work done by the gas.

  Q20. What is the 'Kelvin-Planck statement' of the Second Law?
    [ ] Entropy is always conserved, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [ ] Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [ ] Absolute zero can never be reached, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [*] It is impossible to construct a heat engine that converts all absorbed heat into work with no other effect
    Explanation: The Kelvin-Planck statement says no heat engine operating in a cycle can convert 100% of heat to work. Some heat must be exhausted.

  Q21. A Carnot engine operates between a hot reservoir at 500 K and a cold reservoir at 300 K. What is its efficiency?
    [ ] 60%
    [ ] 80%

    Explanation: e = 1 - T_C/T_H = 1 - 300/500 = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4 = 40%.

  Q22. An ideal gas expands isothermally at 300 K from 1 L to 3 L. If n = 1 mol, how much work is done by the gas? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))
    [ ] About 1370 J
    [ ] About 7480 J
    [ ] About 600 J
    [*] About 2740 J
    Explanation: W = nRT*ln(V2/V1) = 1(8.314)(300)*ln(3) = 2494*1.099 = 2740 J.

  Q23. A refrigerator removes 500 J of heat from a cold space and exhausts 800 J to the room. Its coefficient of performance (COP) is:
    [ ] About 0.625
    [ ] About 2.67
    [*] About 1.67
    [ ] About 1.0
    Explanation: COP = Q_C/W = Q_C/(Q_H - Q_C) = 500/(800-500) = 500/300 = 1.67.

  Q24. An ideal monatomic gas (Cv = 3R/2) undergoes adiabatic compression from 2 L at 300 K to 1 L. What is the final temperature? (gamma = 5/3)
    [ ] About 600 K
    [ ] About 300 K
    [*] About 476 K
    [ ] About 150 K
    Explanation: TV^(gamma-1) = constant. T1*V1^(2/3) = T2*V2^(2/3). 300*(2)^(2/3) = T2*(1)^(2/3). T2 = 300*2^(2/3) = 300*1.587 = 476 K.

  Q25. A power plant operates at 35% efficiency and produces 500 MW of electrical power. How much heat is exhausted to the environment per second?
    [*] About 929 MW
    [ ] About 175 MW
    [ ] About 500 MW
    [ ] About 1429 MW
    Explanation: e = W/Q_H, so Q_H = W/e = 500/0.35 = 1429 MW. Q_C = Q_H - W = 1429 - 500 = 929 MW.

  Q26. One mole of an ideal gas at 300 K and 1 atm undergoes free expansion into a vacuum, doubling its volume. What are the final temperature and entropy change?

    [ ] T drops to 150 K; Delta S = 0
    Explanation: Free expansion: Q=0, W=0, so Delta U=0 and T is unchanged. But it is irreversible: Delta S = nR*ln(V2/V1) = R*ln(2) = 5.76 J/K > 0.

  Q27. A diesel engine compresses air adiabatically from 300 K with a compression ratio of 20:1. If gamma = 1.4, what is the final temperature?

    [ ] About 6000 K
    [ ] About 600 K
    [*] About 994 K
    Explanation: T2 = T1*(V1/V2)^(gamma-1) = 300*(20)^0.4 = 300*3.314 = 994 K. Hot enough to ignite diesel fuel without a spark plug.

  Q28. A heat pump has a COP of 4.0 and delivers 12 kW of heat to a building. How much electrical power does it consume?

    [*] 3 kW
    Explanation: COP_hp = Q_H/W, so W = Q_H/COP = 12/4 = 3 kW. The heat pump delivers 4 times the energy it consumes.
