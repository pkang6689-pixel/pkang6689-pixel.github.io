--- Lesson 5.1: Momentum and Impulse (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Momentum and Impulse
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A 3 kg object moves at 4 m/s. What is its momentum?
p = mv = 3 × 4 = 12 kg·m/s.
A constant force of 20 N acts on an object for 3 seconds. What is the impulse delivered?
J = FΔt = 20 × 3 = 60 N·s.
A 2 kg ball moving at 5 m/s is brought to rest. What is the magnitude of the change in momentum?
|Δp| = |mv_f - mv_i| = |0 - 2(5)| = 10 kg·m/s.
A 0.5 kg ball bounces off a wall. It hits at 6 m/s and rebounds at 4 m/s. What is the magnitude of the impulse on the ball?
Taking the initial direction as negative: Δp = m(v_f - v_i) = 0.5(4 - (-6)) = 0.5(10) = 5 N·s.
Newton's second law in its general form states F = dp/dt. If p(t) = 3t² + 2t (in kg·m/s), what is the force at t = 2 s?
F = dp/dt = 6t + 2. At t = 2: F = 12 + 2 = 14 N.
A force F(t) = 4t N acts on a 2 kg object initially at rest from t = 0 to t = 3 s. What is the final speed?
J = ∫₀³ 4t dt = 2t²|₀³ = 18 N·s = Δp = mv. v = 18/2 = 9 m/s.
A 1500 kg car changes velocity from 10 m/s to 25 m/s. What is the impulse on the car?
J = Δp = mΔv = 1500(25 - 10) = 1500(15) = 22,500 N·s.
A 0.15 kg baseball is pitched at 40 m/s and hit back at 50 m/s. What is the magnitude of the impulse on the ball?
|J| = m|v_f - v_i| = 0.15|50 - (-40)| = 0.15(90) = 13.5 N·s.
Two objects have the same kinetic energy. Object A has mass 2 kg and Object B has mass 8 kg. What is the ratio of their momenta p_A/p_B?
KE = p²/(2m), so p = √(2mKE). p_A/p_B = √(m_A/m_B) = √(2/8) = √(1/4) = 1/2.
What is momentum?
Momentum is a vector quantity equal to the product of mass and velocity: p = mv. Its SI unit is kg·m/s.
What is impulse?
Impulse is the integral of force over time: J = ∫F dt. By the impulse-momentum theorem, J = Δp.
What is the general form of Newton's second law in terms of momentum?
F_net = dp/dt is the general form of Newton's second law. When mass is constant, it reduces to F = ma.
What is the difference between momentum and kinetic energy?
Momentum is a vector (p = mv) while KE is a scalar (½mv²). Momentum is proportional to v; KE is proportional to v².
What is the SI unit of impulse?
Impulse is measured in N·s, which is dimensionally equivalent to kg·m/s (the same units as momentum).
What is the relationship between impulse and the area under a F-t curve for a variable force?
For any force (constant or variable), impulse J = ∫F dt, which graphically is the area under the F-t curve.
A soccer player kicks a 0.45 kg ball initially at rest. The player's foot is in contact with the ball for 0.02 seconds, and the ball leaves at 25 m/s. What is the average force exerted by the foot?
J = Δp = 0.45(25) = 11.25 N·s. F_avg = J/Δt = 11.25/0.02 = 562.5 N.
A 70 kg stunt performer jumps from a 5 m ledge. In one take, they land on concrete (stop time 0.01 s). In another, they land on a foam pad (stop time 0.5 s). What is the ratio of the average force on concrete to foam? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
Both landings have the same Δp (same momentum change). F_avg = Δp/Δt. The ratio = Δt_foam/Δt_concrete = 0.5/0.01 = 50. The concrete force is 50 times larger.
A 1000 kg rocket expels 5 kg of exhaust gas per second at a velocity of 3000 m/s relative to the rocket. What is the thrust force on the rocket?
Thrust = v_exhaust × (dm/dt) = 3000 × 5 = 15,000 N.
During a car crash test, a 1200 kg car traveling at 15 m/s hits a wall and stops. The crumple zone deforms over 0.3 seconds. What is the average force on the car during the crash?
J = Δp = 1200(15) = 18,000 N·s. F_avg = 18,000/0.3 = 60,000 N.
A 0.06 kg tennis ball hits a racket at 20 m/s and rebounds at 30 m/s. The contact time is 0.005 s. A force sensor records the force as approximately triangular in shape. What is the peak force?
J = m(v_f - v_i) = 0.06(30 - (-20)) = 0.06(50) = 3 N·s. For a triangular force pulse, J = ½ × F_peak × Δt. 3 = ½ × F_peak × 0.005. F_peak = 1200 N.
A 2000 kg spacecraft in deep space fires its thrusters, producing a force that varies as F(t) = 500 + 100t (in newtons) for 10 seconds. What is the final speed of the spacecraft (initially at rest)?
J = ∫₀¹⁰ (500 + 100t) dt = [500t + 50t²]₀¹⁰ = 5000 + 5000 = 10,000 N·s. v = J/m = 10,000/2000 = 5 m/s.
A 75 kg skydiver reaches terminal velocity of 55 m/s and then opens a parachute, decelerating to 5 m/s in 4 seconds. What is the average net force during the deceleration?
Taking downward as positive: Δp = 75(5 - 55) = 75(-50) = -3750 kg·m/s. F_avg = -3750/4 = -937.5 N. The negative sign means the net force is upward: 937.5 N upward.
Real-World Applications

Example: A 70 kg stunt performer jumps from a 5 m ledge. In one take, they land on concrete (stop time 0.01 s). In another, they land on a foam pad (stop time 0.5 s). What is the ratio of the average force on concrete to foam? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
Both landings have the same Δp (same momentum change). F_avg = Δp/Δt. The ratio = Δt_foam/Δt_concrete = 0.5/0.01 = 50. The concrete force is 50 times larger.
Example: During a car crash test, a 1200 kg car traveling at 15 m/s hits a wall and stops. The crumple zone deforms over 0.3 seconds. What is the average force on the car during the crash?
J = Δp = 1200(15) = 18,000 N·s. F_avg = 18,000/0.3 = 60,000 N.
Example: An airbag deploys in a car crash. A 5 kg head traveling at 12 m/s is decelerated to rest over 0.15 s by the airbag. Without the airbag, the head would stop in 0.003 s against the dashboard. By what factor does the airbag reduce the average force?
Same Δp in both cases. F_ratio = Δt_airbag/Δt_dashboard = 0.15/0.003 = 50. The airbag reduces the force by a factor of 50.

Taking downward as positive: Δp = 75(5 - 55) = 75(-50) = -3750 kg·m/s. F_avg = -3750/4 = -937.5 N. The negative sign means the net force is upward: 937.5 N upward.

     A: The branch of mechanics that describes the motion of objects without considering the forces that cause the motion

  2. Q: Define: Force
     A: A push or pull that can change an object's velocity, measured in newtons

  3. Q: Define: Acceleration
     A: The rate of change of velocity with respect to time

  4. Q: Define: Momentum
     A: The product of an object's mass and velocity, representing its resistance to motion changes

  5. Q: Define: Work
     A: The transfer of energy that occurs when a force acts on an object over a distance

  6. Q: Define: Power
     A: The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred, measured in watts

  7. Q: Define: Torque
     A: The rotational equivalent of force; a measure of how much a force causes an object to rotate about an axis

  8. Q: Define: Angular Momentum
     A: The product of an object's moment of inertia and its angular velocity

     A: Evaporation from oceans and transpiration from plants

     A: Urbanization reduces infiltration and increases runoff

--- Lesson 5.2: Conservation of Momentum (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conservation of Momentum
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A 5 kg cart moving at 6 m/s collides with and sticks to a 10 kg cart at rest. What is the velocity of the combined carts?
p_i = 5(6) + 10(0) = 30 kg·m/s. After: 15v = 30. v = 2 m/s.
A 2 kg object moving at 8 m/s east collides with a 2 kg object moving at 4 m/s west. They stick together. What is the final velocity?
p_i = 2(8) + 2(-4) = 16 - 8 = 8 kg·m/s east. After: 4v = 8. v = 2 m/s east.
A 1 kg gun fires a 0.01 kg bullet at 500 m/s. What is the recoil speed of the gun?
Initial momentum = 0. After: 0.01(500) + 1(v) = 0. v = -5 m/s. Recoil speed = 5 m/s.
A 4 kg block at rest on a frictionless surface is hit by a 1 kg block moving at 10 m/s. The 1 kg block bounces back at 2 m/s. What is the speed of the 4 kg block?
1(10) = 1(-2) + 4v. 10 = -2 + 4v. 4v = 12. v = 3 m/s.
In a head-on collision, a 1000 kg car moving at 20 m/s hits a 2000 kg truck moving at 10 m/s in the opposite direction. They lock together. What is the final velocity?
1000(20) + 2000(-10) = 3000v. 20000 - 20000 = 0. v = 0 m/s.
A firecracker at rest explodes into three pieces of equal mass (each 0.1 kg). Two pieces fly off at 5 m/s at right angles to each other. What is the speed of the third piece?
Total p = 0. The two pieces have momenta 0.5 kg·m/s in x and 0.5 kg·m/s in y. The third must have p = -0.5x̂ - 0.5ŷ, magnitude = 0.5√2. Speed = 0.5√2/0.1 = 5√2 ≈ 7.07 m/s.
What is the law of conservation of momentum?
Conservation of momentum states that the total momentum of a system is constant when the net external force is zero.
What is an isolated system in the context of momentum conservation?
An isolated system has no net external force acting on it. Internal forces can redistribute momentum, but the total is conserved.
What is recoil in physics?
Recoil is the backward motion resulting from ejecting mass in one direction. By conservation of momentum, the remaining object moves in the opposite direction.
What is an internal force?
Internal forces act between objects within the system. By Newton's third law, they come in equal and opposite pairs, so they cannot change the system's total momentum.
What is an external force in the context of momentum?
An external force comes from outside the system. It can change the total momentum of the system: F_ext = dp_total/dt.
What is the total momentum of a system?
Total system momentum is the vector sum of all individual momenta: p_total = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ + ... = Σm_i v_i.
What is an explosion in momentum terms?
In an explosion, internal forces push parts apart. Total momentum is conserved (often zero before the explosion), but kinetic energy increases (from stored chemical energy).
What is the condition for applying conservation of momentum?
Momentum is conserved when the net external force is zero. Even with friction between colliding objects (internal force), if the system is isolated, total momentum is conserved.
A 90 kg hockey player skating at 6 m/s collides with an 80 kg player skating at 4 m/s in the same direction. They slide together after the collision. What is their combined speed?
90(6) + 80(4) = 170v. 540 + 320 = 860 = 170v. v = 860/170 ≈ 5.06 m/s.
A 2000 kg car traveling north at 15 m/s collides with a 1500 kg car traveling east at 20 m/s at an intersection. The cars lock together. What is the speed of the wreckage?
p_N = 2000(15) = 30,000. p_E = 1500(20) = 30,000. p_total = √(30000² + 30000²) = 30000√2. v = 30000√2/3500 ≈ 42426/3500 ≈ 12.1 m/s.
A cannon (mass 500 kg) mounted on a wheeled carriage fires a 5 kg ball at 100 m/s. What is the recoil speed of the cannon?
0 = 5(100) + 500v. v = -500/500 = -1 m/s. Recoil speed = 1 m/s.
A railroad car (mass 10,000 kg) moving at 3 m/s hits and couples with two identical stationary cars. What is the final speed of the three-car train?
10000(3) = 30000v. v = 30000/30000 = 1 m/s.
A 50 kg person standing on a stationary 150 kg boat on still water walks at 2 m/s relative to the boat toward shore. What is the boat's velocity relative to the water?
Let boat velocity = -v (away from shore). Person's velocity relative to water = 2 - v. Momentum: 0 = 50(2 - v) + 150(-v). 0 = 100 - 50v - 150v. 200v = 100. v = 0.5 m/s away from shore.
A firework (mass 3 kg) moving upward at 20 m/s explodes into two pieces. A 1 kg piece continues straight up at 50 m/s. What is the velocity of the 2 kg piece?
3(20) = 1(50) + 2v. 60 = 50 + 2v. v = 5 m/s upward.
Real-World Applications
Example: A 2000 kg car traveling north at 15 m/s collides with a 1500 kg car traveling east at 20 m/s at an intersection. The cars lock together. What is the speed of the wreckage?
p_N = 2000(15) = 30,000. p_E = 1500(20) = 30,000. p_total = √(30000² + 30000²) = 30000√2. v = 30000√2/3500 ≈ 42426/3500 ≈ 12.1 m/s.
Example: A cannon (mass 500 kg) mounted on a wheeled carriage fires a 5 kg ball at 100 m/s. What is the recoil speed of the cannon?
0 = 5(100) + 500v. v = -500/500 = -1 m/s. Recoil speed = 1 m/s.

    Explanation: p_i = 2(8) + 2(-4) = 16 - 8 = 8 kg·m/s east. After: 4v = 8. v = 2 m/s east.

  Q3. A 60 kg ice skater standing still catches a 0.5 kg ball thrown at 10 m/s. How fast does the skater move afterward?
    [ ] 5 m/s
    [ ] 0.5 m/s
    [ ] 10 m/s
    [*] 0.083 m/s
    Explanation: p_i = 0.5(10) = 5 kg·m/s. After: 60.5v = 5. v = 5/60.5 ≈ 0.083 m/s.

  Q4. A 1 kg gun fires a 0.01 kg bullet at 500 m/s. What is the recoil speed of the gun?
    [ ] 500 m/s
    [ ] 50 m/s
    [ ] 0.5 m/s
    [*] 5 m/s
    Explanation: Initial momentum = 0. After: 0.01(500) + 1(v) = 0. v = -5 m/s. Recoil speed = 5 m/s.

  Q5. Two objects (3 kg and 6 kg) are initially at rest. An internal explosion pushes them apart. The 3 kg piece moves at 4 m/s. How fast does the 6 kg piece move?
    [ ] 4 m/s
    [ ] 8 m/s
    [ ] 1 m/s
    [*] 2 m/s
    Explanation: Total p = 0. After: 3(4) + 6v = 0. v = -12/6 = -2 m/s. Speed = 2 m/s (opposite direction).

  Q6. A 4 kg block at rest on a frictionless surface is hit by a 1 kg block moving at 10 m/s. The 1 kg block bounces back at 2 m/s. What is the speed of the 4 kg block?
    [ ] 2 m/s
    [ ] 8 m/s
    [*] 3 m/s
    [ ] 12 m/s
    Explanation: 1(10) = 1(-2) + 4v. 10 = -2 + 4v. 4v = 12. v = 3 m/s.

  Q7. A 100 kg astronaut floating in space throws a 2 kg tool at 5 m/s. How fast does the astronaut drift in the opposite direction?
    [ ] 1 m/s
    [ ] 5 m/s
    [ ] 0.01 m/s
    [*] 0.1 m/s
    Explanation: Initial p = 0. 2(5) + 100v = 0. v = -10/100 = -0.1 m/s. Speed = 0.1 m/s.

  Q8. In a head-on collision, a 1000 kg car moving at 20 m/s hits a 2000 kg truck moving at 10 m/s in the opposite direction. They lock together. What is the final velocity?
    [*] 0 m/s
    [ ] 3.33 m/s in the car's direction
    [ ] 6.67 m/s in the car's direction
    [ ] 10 m/s in the truck's direction
    Explanation: 1000(20) + 2000(-10) = 3000v. 20000 - 20000 = 0. v = 0 m/s.

  Q9. A 0.02 kg bullet embeds in a 2 kg block on a frictionless surface. The block moves at 1 m/s after impact. What was the bullet's speed?
    [*] 101 m/s
    [ ] 50 m/s
    [ ] 200 m/s
    [ ] 100 m/s
    Explanation: 0.02v = (0.02 + 2)(1) = 2.02. v = 2.02/0.02 = 101 m/s.

  Q10. A firecracker at rest explodes into three pieces of equal mass (each 0.1 kg). Two pieces fly off at 5 m/s at right angles to each other. What is the speed of the third piece?
    [ ] 5 m/s
    [ ] 10 m/s
    [ ] 2.5 m/s
    [*] 5√2 ≈ 7.07 m/s
    Explanation: Total p = 0. The two pieces have momenta 0.5 kg·m/s in x and 0.5 kg·m/s in y. The third must have p = -0.5x̂ - 0.5ŷ, magnitude = 0.5√2. Speed = 0.5√2/0.1 = 5√2 ≈ 7.07 m/s.

  Q11. What is the law of conservation of momentum?
    [*] The total momentum of a system remains constant when no net external force acts on it
    [ ] Momentum is always conserved in every situation
    [ ] The momentum of each object in a system is always constant
    [ ] Momentum can be created by internal forces
    Explanation: Conservation of momentum states that the total momentum of a system is constant when the net external force is zero.

  Q12. What is an isolated system in the context of momentum conservation?
    [ ] A system with no internal forces
    [ ] A system with only one object
    [*] A system with no net external force, so its total momentum is conserved
    [ ] A system where no collisions occur
    Explanation: An isolated system has no net external force acting on it. Internal forces can redistribute momentum, but the total is conserved.

  Q13. What is recoil in physics?
    [ ] The force of friction during a collision
    [ ] The forward motion of a projectile after being fired
    [ ] The energy stored in a spring after compression
    [*] The backward motion of an object after it launches or ejects another object, as required by conservation of momentum
    Explanation: Recoil is the backward motion resulting from ejecting mass in one direction. By conservation of momentum, the remaining object moves in the opposite direction.

  Q14. What is an internal force?
    [ ] A force from outside the system
    [ ] The force of gravity on an object
    [*] A force between objects within the system that does not change the total momentum of the system
    [ ] A force that always does positive work
    Explanation: Internal forces act between objects within the system. By Newton's third law, they come in equal and opposite pairs, so they cannot change the system's total momentum.

  Q15. What is an external force in the context of momentum?
    [ ] A force that is always friction
    [ ] A force between two objects within the system
    [ ] A force that acts only during collisions
    [*] A force exerted on the system by an agent outside the system, which can change the system's total momentum
    Explanation: An external force comes from outside the system. It can change the total momentum of the system: F_ext = dp_total/dt.

  Q16. What does Newton's third law say about internal forces and momentum?
    [ ] Newton's third law only applies to external forces
    [*] Internal forces come in equal and opposite pairs, so they redistribute but do not change total system momentum
    [ ] Internal forces always cancel each other's work
    [ ] Internal forces can create momentum from nothing
    Explanation: Newton's third law: F_AB = -F_BA. Internal forces always come in pairs that sum to zero, so they cannot change the total momentum of the system.

  Q17. What is the total momentum of a system?
    [ ] The scalar sum of all speeds times masses
    [*] The vector sum of the momenta of all objects in the system: p_total = Σm_i v_i
    [ ] The momentum of the largest object in the system
    [ ] The average momentum per object
    Explanation: Total system momentum is the vector sum of all individual momenta: p_total = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ + ... = Σm_i v_i.

  Q18. What is an explosion in momentum terms?
    [ ] An event that creates new momentum from energy
    [ ] An event where kinetic energy is conserved
    [*] An event where internal forces push parts of a system apart, but total momentum is conserved
    [ ] An event where momentum is destroyed by the blast
    Explanation: In an explosion, internal forces push parts apart. Total momentum is conserved (often zero before the explosion), but kinetic energy increases (from stored chemical energy).

  Q19. What is the condition for applying conservation of momentum?
    [ ] Both objects must have the same mass
    [ ] The collision must be perfectly elastic
    [*] The net external force on the system must be zero (or negligibly small compared to internal forces)
    [ ] No friction can be present anywhere
    Explanation: Momentum is conserved when the net external force is zero. Even with friction between colliding objects (internal force), if the system is isolated, total momentum is conserved.

  Q20. What does conservation of momentum in two dimensions mean?
    [*] Momentum is conserved independently in each direction: p_x is conserved and p_y is conserved separately
    [ ] Momentum is only conserved in one direction at a time
    [ ] The x-component of momentum must equal the y-component
    [ ] Total momentum magnitude is conserved but direction can change
    Explanation: Momentum is a vector, so conservation applies independently in each direction: Σp_x(before) = Σp_x(after), and Σp_y(before) = Σp_y(after).

  Q21. A 90 kg hockey player skating at 6 m/s collides with an 80 kg player skating at 4 m/s in the same direction. They slide together after the collision. What is their combined speed?
    [ ] 4.5 m/s
    [*] 5.06 m/s
    [ ] 10 m/s
    [ ] 5 m/s
    Explanation: 90(6) + 80(4) = 170v. 540 + 320 = 860 = 170v. v = 860/170 ≈ 5.06 m/s.

  Q22. A ballistic pendulum consists of a 5 kg block hanging from a string. A 0.05 kg bullet hits the block and embeds in it, causing the block to swing up 0.2 m. What was the bullet's speed? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
    [*] 202 m/s
    [ ] 404 m/s
    [ ] 50 m/s
    [ ] 101 m/s
    Explanation: After embedding, v_block = √(2gh) = √(2(10)(0.2)) = 2 m/s. Momentum: 0.05(v) = 5.05(2). v = 10.1/0.05 = 202 m/s.

  Q23. Two ice boats on a frictionless frozen lake are connected by a compressed spring. Boat A (mass 200 kg) and Boat B (mass 100 kg) are released. If Boat A moves at 3 m/s, how fast does Boat B move?
    [ ] 3 m/s
    [ ] 9 m/s
    [*] 6 m/s
    [ ] 1.5 m/s
    Explanation: Initial p = 0. After: 200(3) + 100v = 0. v = -600/100 = -6 m/s. Speed = 6 m/s (opposite direction).

  Q24. A 2000 kg car traveling north at 15 m/s collides with a 1500 kg car traveling east at 20 m/s at an intersection. The cars lock together. What is the speed of the wreckage?
    [ ] 8.6 m/s
    [*] 12.1 m/s
    [ ] 35 m/s
    [ ] 17.5 m/s
    Explanation: p_N = 2000(15) = 30,000. p_E = 1500(20) = 30,000. p_total = √(30000² + 30000²) = 30000√2. v = 30000√2/3500 ≈ 42426/3500 ≈ 12.1 m/s.

  Q25. A cannon (mass 500 kg) mounted on a wheeled carriage fires a 5 kg ball at 100 m/s. What is the recoil speed of the cannon?
    [ ] 2 m/s
    [ ] 10 m/s
    [ ] 0.5 m/s
    [*] 1 m/s
    Explanation: 0 = 5(100) + 500v. v = -500/500 = -1 m/s. Recoil speed = 1 m/s.

  Q26. A pool ball moving at 3 m/s strikes a stationary ball of equal mass. The first ball deflects 30° from its original direction. If the collision is elastic, at what angle does the second ball move?
    [ ] 90°
    [*] 60°
    [ ] 45°
    [ ] 30°
    Explanation: In an elastic collision between equal masses, the two objects always move at 90° to each other after collision. If one goes 30° from original, the other goes 90° - 30° = 60° from original.

  Q27. A railroad car (mass 10,000 kg) moving at 3 m/s hits and couples with two identical stationary cars. What is the final speed of the three-car train?
    [*] 1 m/s
    [ ] 0.5 m/s
    [ ] 1.5 m/s
    [ ] 3 m/s
    Explanation: 10000(3) = 30000v. v = 30000/30000 = 1 m/s.

  Q28. A 50 kg person standing on a stationary 150 kg boat on still water walks at 2 m/s relative to the boat toward shore. What is the boat's velocity relative to the water?
    [*] 0.5 m/s away from shore
    [ ] 2 m/s away from shore
    [ ] 0.67 m/s away from shore
    [ ] 1 m/s away from shore
    Explanation: Let boat velocity = -v (away from shore). Person's velocity relative to water = 2 - v. Momentum: 0 = 50(2 - v) + 150(-v). 0 = 100 - 50v - 150v. 200v = 100. v = 0.5 m/s away from shore.

  Q29. A neutron (mass m) traveling at speed v₀ hits a stationary carbon-12 nucleus (mass 12m) head-on and bounces back elastically. What fraction of its original kinetic energy does the neutron retain?
    [ ] (12/13)² ≈ 85.2%
    [*] (11/13)² ≈ 71.6%
    [ ] 25%
    [ ] 50%
    Explanation: For elastic head-on collision, v₁' = v₀(m₁-m₂)/(m₁+m₂) = v₀(m-12m)/13m = -11v₀/13. KE ratio = (v₁'/v₀)² = (11/13)² = 121/169 ≈ 71.6%.

    [*] 5 m/s upward
    [ ] 10 m/s upward

--- Lesson 5.3: Collisions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Collisions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In a perfectly inelastic collision, a 4 kg cart moving at 6 m/s hits a 2 kg cart at rest. What is the final speed?
4(6) = 6v. v = 24/6 = 4 m/s.
A 3 kg ball at 8 m/s hits a 1 kg ball at rest elastically head-on. What is the velocity of the 1 kg ball after collision?
v₂' = 2m₁v₁/(m₁+m₂) = 2(3)(8)/(3+1) = 48/4 = 12 m/s.
A ball bouncing off a floor reaches 81% of its drop height. What is the coefficient of restitution?
e = √(h_f/h_i) = √0.81 = 0.9.
A 1 kg ball moving at 10 m/s collides with a 3 kg ball at rest. After collision, the 1 kg ball moves at -2 m/s (bounces back). What is the speed of the 3 kg ball?
1(10) = 1(-2) + 3v. 10 + 2 = 3v. v = 12/3 = 4 m/s.
What is an elastic collision?
An elastic collision conserves both momentum and kinetic energy. No energy is converted to heat, sound, or deformation.
What is a perfectly inelastic collision?
In a perfectly inelastic collision, objects stick together. This loses the maximum kinetic energy (consistent with momentum conservation) but does not lose all KE unless p_total = 0.
What is an inelastic collision?
An inelastic collision conserves momentum but not kinetic energy. Some KE is converted to heat, sound, or deformation.
What is the coefficient of restitution?
The coefficient of restitution e measures how bouncy a collision is. e = 1 for elastic, e = 0 for perfectly inelastic.
What is a head-on (one-dimensional) collision?
A head-on collision is one-dimensional: the velocities before and after collision are all along the same line.
What is a glancing (two-dimensional) collision?
A glancing collision occurs when objects scatter at angles to the original direction. Momentum must be conserved in both x and y independently.
What is a superelastic collision?
A superelastic (or explosive) collision is one where KE after > KE before, because internal energy (chemical, spring, etc.) is converted to kinetic energy.
What is a ballistic pendulum?
A ballistic pendulum uses conservation of momentum (during collision) and conservation of energy (during swing) to determine a projectile's speed.
What is true about momentum in all types of collisions?
Momentum is conserved in ALL collision types when no net external force acts, regardless of whether KE is conserved.
Two bumper cars at an amusement park collide head-on. Car A (mass 200 kg including rider) moves at 4 m/s, Car B (mass 250 kg including rider) moves at 3 m/s in the opposite direction. They bounce apart and Car A rebounds at 2 m/s. What is Car B's final speed?
Taking Car A's initial direction as +: p_i = 200(4) + 250(-3) = 800 - 750 = 50. After: 200(-2) + 250v = 50. -400 + 250v = 50. v = 450/250 = 1.8 m/s. This is in A's original direction, meaning B reversed. Actually: B initially moved in - direction at 3 m/s. v_B = +1.8 m/s means B now moves in + direction. So B moves opposite its original direction at 1.8 m/s. Wait let me recheck. Car A rebounds means it reverses. A was moving +4, now moves -2. B was moving -3. p_i = 800-750 = 50. p_f = 200(-2) + 250(v_B) = -400 + 250v_B = 50. v_B = 450/250 = 1.8. Since B was initially going in the - direction and now v_B = +1.8, yes B has reversed direction. Speed = 1.8 m/s opposite original.
A curling stone (mass 20 kg) moving at 3 m/s strikes a stationary stone of equal mass. The collision is elastic. The first stone deflects at 30° from its original path. What is the speed of the first stone after collision?
For elastic collision between equal masses with one initially at rest, v₁' = v₀cos θ₁ = 3cos30° ≈ 2.6 m/s.
In a demolition derby, a 1500 kg car traveling east at 10 m/s collides with a 1000 kg car traveling north at 15 m/s. They lock together. What is the angle of the combined wreckage relative to east?
p_E = 1500(10) = 15,000. p_N = 1000(15) = 15,000. θ = arctan(p_N/p_E) = arctan(1) = 45°.
A spacecraft (mass 1000 kg) moving at 100 m/s docks with a space station (mass 49,000 kg) at rest. What is the final speed of the combined system? How much KE is lost?
v = 1000(100)/50000 = 2 m/s. KE_i = ½(1000)(10000) = 5,000,000 J. KE_f = ½(50000)(4) = 100,000 J. Lost = 4,900,000 J.
A 50 kg ice skater throws a 0.4 kg puck at 20 m/s. The skater then catches an identical puck thrown back at 20 m/s. What is the skater's final speed?
Step 1 (throw): 0 = 50v₁ + 0.4(20). v₁ = -8/50 = -0.16 m/s. Step 2 (catch puck coming at +20 m/s): 50(-0.16) + 0.4(20) = 50.4v₂. -8 + 8 = 50.4v₂. v₂ = 0 m/s. Hmm, that gives zero. But the puck thrown back is in the opposite direction from the first. Let me reconsider: after throwing, skater moves at -0.16 m/s. The second puck comes from the same direction as the first throw at 20 m/s (so in + direction). 50(-0.16) + 0.4(20) = 50.4v. 0 = 50.4v. v = 0. Real-World Applications
Example: In a demolition derby, a 1500 kg car traveling east at 10 m/s collides with a 1000 kg car traveling north at 15 m/s. They lock together. What is the angle of the combined wreckage relative to east?
p_E = 1500(10) = 15,000. p_N = 1000(15) = 15,000. θ = arctan(p_N/p_E) = arctan(1) = 45°.
Example: A forensic analyst examines a car accident. A 1200 kg car rear-ends a 800 kg car stopped at a light. The cars lock together and skid 10 m before stopping (μ_k = 0.5). What was the moving car's speed? (Use g = 10 m/s²)
After collision: friction stops combined mass. v_after² = 2μgd = 2(0.5)(10)(10) = 100. v_after = 10 m/s. Momentum: 1200v = 2000(10). v = 20000/1200 = 16.67 m/s.

--- Lesson 5.4: Center of Mass (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Center of Mass
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A 4 kg mass at x = 2 m and a 6 kg mass at x = 7 m. What is x_cm?
x_cm = (4×2 + 6×7)/(4+6) = (8+42)/10 = 50/10 = 5 m.
A 2 kg object moves at 6 m/s right and a 4 kg object moves at 3 m/s left. What is the velocity of the center of mass?
v_cm = (2(6) + 4(-3))/(2+4) = (12-12)/6 = 0 m/s.
A 10 kg object at the origin and a 5 kg object at (6, 0) m. What is x_cm?
x_cm = (10×0 + 5×6)/15 = 30/15 = 2 m.
Three equal masses are at (0,0), (4,0), and (0,3) m. What is the center of mass?
x_cm = (0+4+0)/3 = 4/3. y_cm = (0+0+3)/3 = 1. Center of mass = (4/3, 1) m.
The total momentum of a system is 30 kg·m/s and total mass is 10 kg. What is the velocity of the center of mass?
v_cm = p_total/M_total = 30/10 = 3 m/s.
A linear mass density is given by λ(x) = 2x (kg/m) for a rod from x = 0 to x = 3 m. What is the total mass?
M = ∫₀³ 2x dx = x²|₀³ = 9 kg.
For the rod in the previous question (λ = 2x from 0 to 3), what is x_cm?
x_cm = (1/M)∫₀³ x(2x) dx = (1/9)∫₀³ 2x² dx = (1/9)(2x³/3)|₀³ = (1/9)(18) = 2 m.
What is the center of mass of a system?
The center of mass is the mass-weighted average position: r_cm = Σm_i r_i / M_total. It is the point where the system balances.
What is the velocity of the center of mass?
v_cm = (Σm_i v_i)/M_total = p_total/M_total.
What is the center of mass formula for a continuous mass distribution?
For a continuous distribution, x_cm = (1/M)∫x dm. The integral sums up x dm over the entire mass, where dm = λ dx for a rod, σ dA for a surface, etc.
What is linear mass density?
Linear mass density λ = dm/dx has units kg/m. For a small segment dx, the mass is dm = λ dx.
What is the significance of the center of mass in collisions?
In any collision with no net external force, v_cm is constant because total momentum is conserved. Individual objects change velocities, but v_cm does not.
What is the center of mass of a uniform symmetric object?
For a uniform (constant density) object with a line or point of symmetry, the center of mass lies at the geometric center.
What is the relationship between total momentum and center of mass motion?
p_total = Σm_i v_i = M v_cm. This means total momentum is equivalent to the entire mass moving at the center of mass velocity.
A uniform T-shaped piece of sheet metal consists of a horizontal bar (mass 2 kg, centered at y = 3 m) and a vertical bar (mass 1 kg, centered at y = 1 m). What is y_cm?
y_cm = (2(3) + 1(1))/(2+1) = (6+1)/3 = 7/3 ≈ 2.33 m.
A 500 kg satellite in orbit ejects a 50 kg module at 10 m/s relative to the original satellite. What is the change in the satellite's velocity?
In the satellite's initial frame: 0 = 50(10) + 450v_sat. v_sat = -500/450 ≈ -1.11 m/s. The satellite gains about 1.1 m/s in the opposite direction.
A mobile sculpture consists of a 2 kg ball at (0, 0), a 1 kg ball at (3, 4) m, and a 3 kg ball at (6, 0) m. What is the center of mass?
x_cm = (2(0) + 1(3) + 3(6))/6 = (0+3+18)/6 = 21/6 = 3.5 m. y_cm = (2(0) + 1(4) + 3(0))/6 = 4/6 = 0.67 m.
