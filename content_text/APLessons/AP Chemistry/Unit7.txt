--- Lesson 7.1: Dynamic Equilibrium (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Dynamic Equilibrium
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Real-World Applications
Example: A drop of food coloring is added to a glass of water. Eventually, the color becomes uniform. Is this an example of dynamic equilibrium?
While molecules do move in all directions, this is simple diffusion reaching a uniform concentration, not a reversible chemical reaction at equilibrium. No chemical bonds are broken or formed.

--- Lesson 7.1: Dynamic Equilibrium (Quiz) ---

  Q1. At equilibrium, which statement is true?
    [*] The rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal
    [ ] The concentrations of reactants and products are equal
    [ ] The forward reaction has stopped
    [ ] Only products are present
    Explanation: At dynamic equilibrium, the forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates, so there is no net change in concentrations.

  Q2. Which of the following is true about a system at dynamic equilibrium?
    [ ] The reaction has gone to completion
    [ ] Only the reverse reaction occurs
    [ ] No reactions are occurring
    [*] Both forward and reverse reactions are occurring continuously
    Explanation: Dynamic equilibrium means both forward and reverse reactions continue simultaneously but at equal rates, resulting in constant macroscopic concentrations.

  Q3. Which condition is necessary for equilibrium to be established?
    [*] The system must be closed
    [ ] Temperature must be increasing
    [ ] The system must be open to the atmosphere
    [ ] A catalyst must be present
    Explanation: Equilibrium can only be established in a closed system where no reactants or products can escape.

  Q4. At equilibrium, the concentrations of reactants and products:
    [ ] Must be equal
    [ ] Continuously increase
    [*] Are constant but not necessarily equal
    [ ] Are always zero
    Explanation: At equilibrium, concentrations remain constant over time but are NOT necessarily equal to each other. The ratio depends on the equilibrium constant.

  Q5. A reversible reaction is denoted by which symbol?
    [ ] A single arrow (->)
    [*] A double arrow or equilibrium arrows
    [ ] An equal sign (=)
    [ ] A plus sign (+)
    Explanation: Reversible reactions use double arrows (or equilibrium arrows) to indicate that both the forward and reverse reactions occur.

  Q6. Does adding a catalyst change the position of equilibrium?
    [*] No, it only speeds up the attainment of equilibrium
    [ ] Yes, it shifts toward reactants
    [ ] It depends on the catalyst
    [ ] Yes, it shifts toward products
    Explanation: A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally, so equilibrium is reached faster but the equilibrium position (concentrations) remains unchanged.

  Q7. What happens to equilibrium if a product is continuously removed from the system?
    [ ] The reaction shifts toward reactants
    [ ] The reaction stops
    [*] The reaction shifts toward products to replace what was removed
    [ ] Equilibrium is unaffected
    Explanation: Removing a product disturbs equilibrium, causing the forward reaction to speed up to produce more product and restore equilibrium.

  Q8. How can you tell when a system has reached equilibrium?

    [ ] The temperature rises sharply, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] The reaction mixture changes color in most scenarios involving equilibrium

    [ ] It depends on the temperature

    Explanation: Starting with only A, [A] decreases and [B] increases until both reach constant (but not necessarily equal) values at equilibrium.

  Q11. What does 'dynamic equilibrium' mean?

    [*] A state where forward and reverse reactions occur at equal rates with no net change in concentrations
    [ ] A state where temperature is changing
    [ ] A state where only the forward reaction occurs
    Explanation: Dynamic equilibrium is a state in which opposing processes (forward and reverse reactions) occur simultaneously at equal rates, maintaining constant macroscopic properties.

  Q12. What does 'reversible reaction' mean?
    [ ] A reaction that can be undone by adding water
    [*] A reaction that can proceed in both the forward and reverse directions
    [ ] A reaction with 100% yield
    [ ] A reaction that can only go forward
    Explanation: A reversible reaction is one that can proceed in both directions: reactants forming products (forward) and products reforming reactants (reverse).

--- Lesson 7.2: Equilibrium Constants (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Equilibrium Constants
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For the reaction aA + bB <-> cC + dD, what is the expression for Kc?
Kc is the product of equilibrium concentrations of products raised to their coefficients divided by reactants raised to their coefficients.
What is the relationship between Kc for a forward reaction and the reverse reaction?
The equilibrium constant for the reverse reaction is the reciprocal of the forward reaction constant: Kreverse = 1/Kforward.
What is the relationship between Kp and Kc?
Kp = Kc(RT)^(Delta n), where Delta n is the change in moles of gas (moles of gaseous products minus moles of gaseous reactants).
What is the reaction quotient Q used for?
Q has the same form as K but uses current (non-equilibrium) concentrations. Comparing Q to K tells us which direction the reaction must shift to reach equilibrium.
If Q = K, what is the state of the system?
When Q equals K, the system is at equilibrium and no net change in concentrations occurs.
For the decomposition CaCO3(s) <-> CaO(s) + CO2(g), what is the equilibrium expression?
CaCO3 and CaO are pure solids (activity = 1), so they are excluded. Kc = [CO2] (or Kp = P_CO2 for the pressure form).
If Kc for A <-> 2B is 0.25, what is Kc for 2B <-> A?
The reverse reaction has Kreverse = 1/Kforward = 1/0.25 = 4.0.
For PCl5(g) <-> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g), Kc = 0.042 at 250 degrees C. If initially [PCl5] = 1.0 M and no products, what is the equilibrium concentration of Cl2?
ICE: Kc = x^2/(1.0-x) = 0.042. Since K is small: x^2 = 0.042, x = 0.205. Checking: 0.205^2/0.795 = 0.053 (not great approx). Solving more carefully: x = 0.19 M.
The equilibrium constant for 2SO2 + O2 <-> 2SO3 is K1. What is the equilibrium constant for SO3 <-> SO2 + 1/2 O2?
First reverse: 2SO3 <-> 2SO2 + O2, K = 1/K1. Then divide by 2: SO3 <-> SO2 + 1/2 O2, K = (1/K1)^(1/2).
For the gas-phase reaction 2NO(g) + O2(g) <-> 2NO2(g), Delta n(gas) = 2 - 3 = -1. If Kc = 6.44 x 10^5 at 500 K, what is Kp? (R = 0.0821 L*atm/mol/K)
Kp = Kc(RT)^(Delta n) = 6.44x10^5 x (0.0821 x 500)^(-1) = 6.44x10^5 / 41.05 = 1.57 x 10^4.
Real-World Applications
Example: If a reaction is multiplied by a factor n, what happens to K?
When all coefficients are multiplied by n, every concentration in the expression is raised to a power n times larger, so Knew = (Koriginal)^n.

  Q1. For the reaction aA + bB <-> cC + dD, what is the expression for Kc?

  Q3. If Kc << 1, what does this indicate?
    [ ] The reaction is at completion
    [ ] The system is not at equilibrium
    [*] Reactants are favored
    [ ] Products are favored
    Explanation: A very small Kc means reactant concentrations are much greater than product concentrations at equilibrium; the reaction barely proceeds.

  Q4. Pure solids and liquids are excluded from the equilibrium expression because:
    [ ] They have no mass
    [*] Their concentrations are constant and incorporated into K
    [ ] They are always in excess
    [ ] They do not participate in the reaction
    Explanation: The concentrations (or activities) of pure solids and liquids are constant and equal to 1, so they are incorporated into the equilibrium constant itself.

  Q5. What is the relationship between Kc for a forward reaction and the reverse reaction?
    [ ] They are unrelated
    [ ] Kreverse = Kforward^2
    [ ] They are equal
    [*] Kreverse = 1/Kforward
    Explanation: The equilibrium constant for the reverse reaction is the reciprocal of the forward reaction constant: Kreverse = 1/Kforward.

  Q6. If a reaction is multiplied by a factor n, what happens to K?
    [ ] K is multiplied by n
    [ ] K is divided by n
    [*] K is raised to the nth power
    [ ] K remains unchanged
    Explanation: When all coefficients are multiplied by n, every concentration in the expression is raised to a power n times larger, so Knew = (Koriginal)^n.

  Q7. What is the relationship between Kp and Kc?
    [*] Kp = Kc(RT)^Delta_n
    [ ] Kp = Kc always
    [ ] There is no relationship
    [ ] Kp = Kc/RT
    Explanation: Kp = Kc(RT)^(Delta n), where Delta n is the change in moles of gas (moles of gaseous products minus moles of gaseous reactants).

  Q8. What is the reaction quotient Q used for?
    [ ] To calculate the equilibrium constant
    [ ] To measure the rate of the reaction
    [*] To determine which direction the reaction will shift to reach equilibrium
    [ ] To calculate enthalpy
    Explanation: Q has the same form as K but uses current (non-equilibrium) concentrations. Comparing Q to K tells us which direction the reaction must shift to reach equilibrium.

  Q9. If Q < K, in which direction will the reaction shift?
    [ ] Toward reactants (left)
    [ ] No shift needed
    [ ] Cannot be determined
    [*] Toward products (right)
    Explanation: If Q < K, there are too few products relative to the equilibrium amounts. The reaction shifts right (toward products) to increase Q until Q = K.

  Q10. If Q = K, what is the state of the system?
    [ ] The reaction is shifting left
    [*] The system is at equilibrium
    [ ] The reaction is shifting right
    [ ] The reaction has stopped
    Explanation: When Q equals K, the system is at equilibrium and no net change in concentrations occurs.

  Q11. What does 'equilibrium constant' (K) mean?
    [*] A numerical value expressing the ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium
    [ ] The number of moles at equilibrium
    [ ] The rate at which equilibrium is reached
    [ ] The temperature at which equilibrium occurs
    Explanation: The equilibrium constant K is the ratio of the product of equilibrium concentrations of products to reactants, each raised to their stoichiometric coefficients.

  Q12. What does 'Kp' mean?
    [ ] The equilibrium constant for precipitation reactions
    [*] The equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures of gases
    [ ] The pressure at equilibrium
    [ ] The rate constant for the reaction
    Explanation: Kp is the equilibrium constant written using partial pressures of gaseous species instead of molar concentrations.

  Q13. What does 'Kc' mean?
    [ ] A constant related to catalysts
    [ ] The concentration of the catalyst
    [*] The equilibrium constant expressed in terms of molar concentrations
    [ ] The rate constant in concentration units
    Explanation: Kc is the equilibrium constant written using molar concentrations [mol/L] of the species in the equilibrium expression.

  Q14. What does 'reaction quotient' (Q) mean?
    [ ] The rate of the reaction divided by the equilibrium constant
    [ ] The equilibrium constant at standard conditions
    [*] The ratio of product to reactant concentrations at any point during the reaction, not necessarily at equilibrium
    [ ] The number of products divided by reactants
    Explanation: The reaction quotient Q has the same mathematical form as K but is calculated using current concentrations, which may or may not be at equilibrium.

  Q15. What does 'ICE table' mean?

  Q16. What does 'heterogeneous equilibrium' mean?
    [ ] Equilibrium with unequal concentrations
    [*] Equilibrium involving species in different phases
    [ ] Equilibrium involving only gases
    [ ] Equilibrium that cannot be achieved
    Explanation: A heterogeneous equilibrium involves reactants and products in more than one phase (e.g., solid, liquid, and gas). Pure solids and liquids are omitted from the K expression.

  Q17. What does 'homogeneous equilibrium' mean?

    [ ] Equilibrium at constant temperature

  Q19. What does 'equilibrium expression' mean?

    [ ] The balanced equation for the reaction
    [ ] The energy profile of the reaction
    [*] The mathematical ratio of product and reactant concentrations (or pressures) at equilibrium

    Explanation: The magnitude of K indicates how far a reaction proceeds: large K (>>1) means mostly products, small K (<<1) means mostly reactants, and K near 1 means significant amounts of both.

--- Lesson 7.4: Solubility Equilibria (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solubility Equilibria
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the solubility product constant (Ksp)?
Ksp is the equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a slightly soluble ionic compound into its constituent ions in a saturated solution.
For AgCl(s) <-> Ag+(aq) + Cl-(aq), what is the Ksp expression?
Since AgCl is a pure solid (activity = 1), Ksp = [Ag+][Cl-]. Only dissolved ion concentrations appear.
The Ksp of PbI2 is 9.8 x 10^-9. For PbI2(s) <-> Pb2+(aq) + 2I-(aq), what is the molar solubility?
Let s = molar solubility. Ksp = (s)(2s)^2 = 4s^3. s = (Ksp/4)^(1/3) = (9.8x10^-9/4)^(1/3) = (2.45x10^-9)^(1/3) = 1.35x10^-3 M.
For Ca3(PO4)2(s) <-> 3Ca2+(aq) + 2PO4^3-(aq), what is the Ksp expression?
Ksp = [Ca2+]^3[PO4^3-]^2, with each ion concentration raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient.
The solubility of CaF2 in pure water is 2.1 x 10^-4 M. What is its solubility in a 0.10 M NaF solution?
NaF provides 0.10 M F- (common ion). This shifts the CaF2 dissolution equilibrium left, dramatically reducing the solubility of CaF2 compared to pure water.
The Ksp of Mg(OH)2 is 5.6 x 10^-12. What is the molar solubility of Mg(OH)2 in pure water?
Ksp = [Mg2+][OH-]^2 = (s)(2s)^2 = 4s^3. s = (5.6x10^-12/4)^(1/3) = (1.4x10^-12)^(1/3) = 1.12x10^-4 M.
Real-World Applications
Example: A water treatment plant needs to remove lead(II) ions from contaminated water. Ksp of PbCl2 = 1.7 x 10^-5 and Ksp of PbS = 3.0 x 10^-28. Which reagent more effectively removes Pb2+?
PbS has a vastly lower Ksp (3.0x10^-28 vs 1.7x10^-5), meaning it precipitates at much lower Pb2+ concentrations, removing lead far more effectively.

    [ ] The X-ray destroys the barium, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] The body neutralizes barium, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] BaSO4 is not actually barium when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [*] BaSO4 is extremely insoluble (very low Ksp), so negligible Ba2+ dissolves in the body
    Explanation: The extremely low Ksp of BaSO4 means virtually no Ba2+ ions dissolve in body fluids. The amount released is far below toxic levels, making it safe for medical imaging.

  Q24. The solubility of CaF2 in pure water is 2.1 x 10^-4 M. What is its solubility in a 0.10 M NaF solution?
    [ ] 2.1 x 10^-4 M (same as pure water)
    [ ] Much higher because NaF helps dissolution
    [ ] Zero, because CaF2 is completely insoluble
    [*] Much lower due to the common ion effect
    Explanation: NaF provides 0.10 M F- (common ion). This shifts the CaF2 dissolution equilibrium left, dramatically reducing the solubility of CaF2 compared to pure water.

  Q25. Tooth enamel is hydroxyapatite, Ca5(PO4)3OH. Fluoride toothpaste works because F- replaces OH- to form fluorapatite, Ca5(PO4)3F, which has a lower Ksp. What does this mean for tooth decay?
    [ ] The tooth dissolves faster
    [*] Fluorapatite is less soluble in acid, making teeth more resistant to decay
    [ ] The fluoride kills bacteria
    [ ] The fluoride whitens teeth
    Explanation: Fluorapatite's lower Ksp means it is less soluble than hydroxyapatite, especially in the acidic conditions created by bacterial metabolism, providing better protection against tooth decay.

  Q26. Will a precipitate form when 50 mL of 0.001 M AgNO3 is mixed with 50 mL of 0.001 M NaCl? Ksp(AgCl) = 1.8 x 10^-10.
    [ ] Only at high temperature
    [ ] Cannot determine without more information
    [*] Yes, Q > Ksp
    [ ] No, Q < Ksp
    Explanation: After mixing: [Ag+] = [Cl-] = 0.0005 M. Q = (0.0005)(0.0005) = 2.5 x 10^-7. Since Q (2.5x10^-7) >> Ksp (1.8x10^-10), a precipitate of AgCl forms.

  Q27. Kidney stones are often calcium oxalate (CaC2O4), Ksp = 2.3 x 10^-9. A doctor recommends drinking more water to prevent kidney stones. How does this help?
    [*] Increased water intake dilutes ion concentrations, keeping Q below Ksp and preventing precipitation
    [ ] Water provides Ca2+ ions, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [ ] Water changes the Ksp, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] Water dissolves the stones directly, as conventional stones theory would suggest
    Explanation: Drinking more water increases urine volume, diluting the concentrations of Ca2+ and C2O4^2-. This keeps Q < Ksp, preventing the ions from exceeding the solubility product and forming stones.

  Q28. The Ksp of Mg(OH)2 is 5.6 x 10^-12. What is the molar solubility of Mg(OH)2 in pure water?
    [ ] 5.6 x 10^-12 M
    [*] 1.1 x 10^-4 M
    [ ] 7.5 x 10^-4 M
    [ ] 2.4 x 10^-6 M
    Explanation: Ksp = [Mg2+][OH-]^2 = (s)(2s)^2 = 4s^3. s = (5.6x10^-12/4)^(1/3) = (1.4x10^-12)^(1/3) = 1.12x10^-4 M.

  Q29. Silver chloride is insoluble in water but dissolves in aqueous ammonia: AgCl(s) + 2NH3(aq) -> Ag(NH3)2+(aq) + Cl-(aq). How does complex ion formation affect solubility?
    [ ] It has no effect when the interaction between Agcl and complex factors is taken into full account
    [ ] It decreases solubility, particularly when Agcl considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    [*] Complex ion formation removes Ag+ from solution, shifting the AgCl dissolution equilibrium right and increasing solubility
    [ ] It changes the Ksp of AgCl because the underlying forces at work tend to produce exactly this type of outcome
    Explanation: NH3 reacts with Ag+ to form the stable Ag(NH3)2+ complex, effectively removing free Ag+ from solution. By Le Chatelier's principle, more AgCl dissolves to replace the consumed Ag+.

  Q30. Hard water contains Ca2+ and Mg2+ ions. Adding Na2CO3 (washing soda) softens water. Given Ksp(CaCO3) = 3.3 x 10^-9, why does this work?
    [ ] Na2CO3 changes the pH to dissolve Ca2+
    [ ] Na+ replaces Ca2+ directly
    [ ] Na2CO3 is a surfactant
    [*] CO3^2- from Na2CO3 precipitates Ca2+ as insoluble CaCO3, removing it from solution
    Explanation: Adding CO3^2- ions causes Q for CaCO3 to exceed Ksp, precipitating calcium carbonate and removing Ca2+ from the water, thereby softening it.

--- Lesson 7.4: Solubility Equilibria (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$
