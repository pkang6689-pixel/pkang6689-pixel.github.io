--- Lesson 9.1: Gibbs Free Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gibbs Free Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
At equilibrium, what is the value of delta-G?
At equilibrium, delta-G = 0. The system has reached its lowest free energy state for the given conditions, and there is no net driving force for the reaction in either direction.
What is the standard free energy change (delta-G) calculated from standard free energies of formation?
The standard free energy change is calculated as delta-G = Sum of [n x delta-Gf(products)] - Sum of [n x delta-Gf(reactants)], where n is the stoichiometric coefficient. This is analogous to Hess's law for enthalpy.
An industrial chemist calculates delta-G-standard for the synthesis of ammonia: N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g), delta-H = -92.4 kJ and delta-S = -198.3 J/K at 298 K. What is delta-G-standard?
delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) = -92,400 J - (298 K)(-198.3 J/K) = -92,400 + 59,093 = -33,307 J = -33.3 kJ. The reaction is spontaneous at 298 K, but the negative entropy means it becomes nonspontaneous at higher temperatures.
A researcher finds that delta-G-standard = -5.7 kJ/mol for a reaction at 298 K. What is the equilibrium constant K? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))
Using delta-G-standard = -RT ln K: -5700 = -(8.314)(298) ln K. ln K = 5700/2478 = 2.30. K = e^2.30 = 10.0. A moderately negative delta-G corresponds to K = 10, meaning products are moderately favored.
A biologist studying cellular metabolism knows that the hydrolysis of ATP has delta-G-standard = -30.5 kJ/mol. She wants to couple it with a reaction that has delta-G-standard = +18.0 kJ/mol. What is the overall delta-G-standard?
When reactions are coupled, their delta-G values are additive: delta-G(overall) = -30.5 + 18.0 = -12.5 kJ/mol. The overall reaction is spontaneous because the ATP hydrolysis provides more than enough free energy to drive the endergonic reaction.
A student calculates delta-G for a reaction at 298 K and finds delta-G-standard = +10.0 kJ/mol. If the actual concentrations give Q = 0.001, what is delta-G? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))
delta-G = delta-G-standard + RT ln Q = 10,000 + (8.314)(298) ln(0.001) = 10,000 + (2478)(-6.908) = 10,000 - 17,114 = -7,114 J = -7.1 kJ/mol. Even though delta-G-standard is positive, the very low Q (mostly reactants present) makes the reaction spontaneous in the forward direction.
An engineer is evaluating the combustion of methane: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(l). Using standard free energies of formation: delta-Gf(CO2) = -394.4 kJ/mol, delta-Gf(H2O) = -237.1 kJ/mol, delta-Gf(CH4) = -50.7 kJ/mol, delta-Gf(O2) = 0. What is delta-G-standard?
delta-G = [(-394.4) + 2(-237.1)] - [(-50.7) + 2(0)] = [-394.4 - 474.2] - [-50.7] = -868.6 + 50.7 = -817.9 kJ. The large negative value confirms that methane combustion is highly spontaneous under standard conditions.
A pharmaceutical researcher needs to know whether the dissolution of a drug (delta-H = +25 kJ/mol, delta-S = +100 J/(mol*K)) is spontaneous at body temperature (37 degrees C = 310 K). What is delta-G?
delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) = 25,000 - (310)(100) = 25,000 - 31,000 = -6,000 J = -6.0 kJ/mol. The dissolution is spontaneous at body temperature because the entropy increase at 310 K is large enough to overcome the endothermic enthalpy.
A student measures K = 2.5 x 10^-3 for a reaction at 298 K. What is delta-G-standard? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))
delta-G-standard = -RT ln K = -(8.314)(298) ln(2.5 x 10^-3) = -(2478)(-5.991) = +14,846 J = +14.8 kJ/mol. Since K < 1, delta-G-standard is positive, indicating that reactants are favored at equilibrium under standard conditions.
A materials scientist is studying the rusting of iron: 4Fe(s) + 3O2(g) -> 2Fe2O3(s). Given delta-G-standard = -1484 kJ at 298 K, she wants to know why iron still requires time to rust despite the highly negative delta-G. What is the best explanation?
Thermodynamics (delta-G) tells us whether a reaction CAN occur, while kinetics tells us how FAST it occurs. Rusting has a very negative delta-G (thermodynamically very favorable) but a significant activation energy barrier, which makes it a slow process at room temperature. This distinction between thermodynamics and kinetics is fundamental.

--- Lesson 9.1: Gibbs Free Energy (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The Gibbs free energy equation is:
    [ ] G = H + TS
    [ ] G = H / TS
    [ ] G = H x TS
    [*] G = H - TS
    Explanation: The Gibbs free energy is defined as G = H - TS, where H is enthalpy, T is absolute temperature in Kelvin, and S is entropy. The change form is delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S).

  Q2. A reaction is spontaneous when delta-G is:
    [ ] Positive
    [ ] Zero
    [*] Negative
    [ ] Equal to delta-H
    Explanation: A reaction is spontaneous (thermodynamically favorable) when delta-G < 0. A negative delta-G means the process releases free energy and can proceed without external input.

  Q3. At equilibrium, what is the value of delta-G?
    [*] Equal to zero
    [ ] Greater than zero
    [ ] Equal to delta-H
    [ ] Less than zero
    Explanation: At equilibrium, delta-G = 0. The system has reached its lowest free energy state for the given conditions, and there is no net driving force for the reaction in either direction.

  Q4. For a reaction with delta-H < 0 and delta-S > 0, the reaction is spontaneous at:
    [*] All temperatures
    [ ] No temperatures
    [ ] Only low temperatures
    [ ] Only high temperatures
    Explanation: When delta-H is negative and delta-S is positive, delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) is always negative because both terms contribute to making delta-G negative. The reaction is spontaneous at all temperatures.

  Q5. The standard Gibbs free energy of formation (delta-Gf) for an element in its standard state is:
    [ ] Always negative
    [ ] Always positive
    [*] Zero by definition
    [ ] Depends on the element
    Explanation: By convention, the standard Gibbs free energy of formation for any element in its most stable form at standard conditions (1 bar, usually 298 K) is defined as zero. This serves as the reference point for calculating delta-Gf of compounds.

  Q6. Which relationship connects delta-G to the equilibrium constant K?
    [ ] delta-G = RT / K
    [ ] delta-G = RT ln K
    [ ] delta-G = K / RT
    [*] delta-G = -RT ln K
    Explanation: The standard Gibbs free energy change is related to the equilibrium constant by delta-G-standard = -RT ln K, where R = 8.314 J/(mol*K) and T is the temperature in Kelvin. When K > 1, delta-G is negative (spontaneous); when K < 1, delta-G is positive.

  Q7. For a reaction with delta-H > 0 and delta-S > 0, at what condition is the reaction spontaneous?
    [ ] Never spontaneous
    [*] At high temperatures where T(delta-S) > delta-H
    [ ] At all temperatures
    [ ] At low temperatures
    Explanation: When delta-H > 0 and delta-S > 0, delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) becomes negative only when the T(delta-S) term exceeds delta-H. This occurs at high temperatures, where the entropy contribution overcomes the endothermic enthalpy.

  Q8. What is the standard free energy change (delta-G) calculated from standard free energies of formation?
    [ ] Sum of delta-Gf(products) + Sum of delta-Gf(reactants)
    [*] Sum of delta-Gf(products) - Sum of delta-Gf(reactants)
    [ ] Sum of delta-Gf(products) x Sum of delta-Gf(reactants)
    [ ] Sum of delta-Gf(reactants) - Sum of delta-Gf(products)
    Explanation: The standard free energy change is calculated as delta-G = Sum of [n x delta-Gf(products)] - Sum of [n x delta-Gf(reactants)], where n is the stoichiometric coefficient. This is analogous to Hess's law for enthalpy.

  Q9. If K >> 1 for a reaction, what can be said about delta-G?
    [ ] delta-G is approximately zero
    [*] delta-G is very negative
    [ ] delta-G is very positive
    [ ] delta-G cannot be determined
    Explanation: Since delta-G = -RT ln K, when K >> 1, ln K is a large positive number, making delta-G a large negative number. A very negative delta-G means the reaction strongly favors product formation.

  Q10. The temperature at which a reaction changes from nonspontaneous to spontaneous can be found by setting delta-G equal to:
    [ ] One
    [ ] delta-H
    [*] Zero
    [ ] delta-S
    Explanation: Setting delta-G = 0 gives delta-H - T(delta-S) = 0, so T = delta-H / delta-S. This crossover temperature is where the reaction transitions between spontaneous and nonspontaneous.

  Q11. What does the term 'Gibbs free energy' mean?
    [ ] The total energy content of a system without accounting for additional influences
    [ ] The energy required to break all bonds in a molecule in most scenarios involving energy factors
    [ ] The kinetic energy of molecules in a reaction, as conventional energy theory would suggest
    [*] A thermodynamic quantity that combines enthalpy and entropy to predict whether a process is spontaneous at constant temperature and pressure
    Explanation: Gibbs free energy (G) is a thermodynamic state function defined as G = H - TS. The change in Gibbs free energy (delta-G) determines whether a reaction is spontaneous at constant temperature and pressure. It represents the maximum amount of non-expansion work a system can perform.

  Q12. What does the term 'thermodynamically favorable' mean?
    [ ] A process that produces heat in most scenarios involving favorable factors
    [ ] A process that requires a catalyst, based on conventional assumptions about
    [*] A process that has a negative delta-G and can occur without continuous external energy input
    [ ] A process that occurs rapidly, as conventional favorable theory would suggest
    Explanation: A thermodynamically favorable process has delta-G < 0, meaning it can occur spontaneously. However, this does not mean it occurs quickly; thermodynamics tells us if a reaction CAN occur, while kinetics tells us how fast it occurs.

  Q13. What does 'standard conditions' mean when referring to delta-G-standard?
    [ ] Any conditions where the reaction is spontaneous
    [ ] Conditions at 0 degrees Celsius and 1 atm
    [ ] Conditions at equilibrium
    [*] All reactants and products at 1 M or 1 bar, typically at 298 K
    Explanation: Standard conditions for thermodynamic calculations mean all solutes at 1 M concentration, all gases at 1 bar pressure, and all pure solids and liquids in their standard states. The temperature is usually 298 K (25 degrees C) unless otherwise stated.

  Q14. What does the term 'nonspontaneous' mean for a chemical process?
    [*] The process has delta-G > 0 and requires continuous energy input to proceed
    [ ] The process occurs slowly
    [ ] The process is impossible
    [ ] The process does not produce any products
    Explanation: A nonspontaneous process has delta-G > 0, meaning it requires a continuous input of energy to proceed. It is not impossible; it simply will not occur on its own. The reverse of a nonspontaneous process is spontaneous.

  Q15. What does 'coupling reactions' mean in the context of Gibbs free energy?
    [ ] Mixing two solutions together
    [*] Combining a nonspontaneous reaction with a spontaneous one so the overall delta-G is negative
    [ ] Running two reactions at the same temperature
    [ ] Using a catalyst to speed up a reaction
    Explanation: Coupling reactions means pairing a thermodynamically unfavorable (positive delta-G) reaction with a highly favorable (negative delta-G) reaction so that the sum of their delta-G values is negative. Biological systems commonly couple ATP hydrolysis (delta-G = -30.5 kJ/mol) with endergonic processes.

  Q16. What does 'delta-G-standard' (standard free energy change) mean as distinct from delta-G?
    [*] Delta-G-standard is the free energy change under standard conditions; delta-G is the free energy change under actual reaction conditions
    [ ] They mean the same thing, which is consistent with how delta mechanisms are understood to operate in theory
    [ ] Delta-G-standard applies only to elements, not compounds without accounting for additional influences
    [ ] Delta-G-standard is always larger than delta-G in most scenarios involving delta factors
    Explanation: Delta-G-standard is the free energy change when all species are in their standard states (1 M, 1 bar). Delta-G is the free energy change under actual conditions, related by delta-G = delta-G-standard + RT ln Q, where Q is the reaction quotient.

  Q17. What does the term 'free energy of formation' mean?
    [ ] The energy released when a compound dissolves
    [ ] The energy needed to break a compound into its elements
    [*] The change in Gibbs free energy when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states
    [ ] The total energy stored in a compound
    Explanation: The standard free energy of formation (delta-Gf) is the change in Gibbs free energy when one mole of a compound is formed from its constituent elements, each in their standard states. It is a reference value used to calculate delta-G for reactions.

  Q18. What does the term 'reaction quotient' (Q) mean in the delta-G equation?
    [ ] The same as the equilibrium constant K
    [*] The ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at any point during a reaction, raised to their stoichiometric powers
    [ ] The number of moles of products divided by moles of reactants
    [ ] The rate of the forward reaction divided by the rate of the reverse
    Explanation: The reaction quotient Q has the same mathematical form as the equilibrium constant K, but uses the actual concentrations at any given moment, not just at equilibrium. In the equation delta-G = delta-G-standard + RT ln Q, Q determines how far the system is from equilibrium.

  Q19. What does the term 'exergonic' mean?
    [ ] A reaction that occurs at high temperature
    [ ] A reaction that requires free energy
    [*] A reaction that releases free energy (delta-G < 0)
    [ ] A reaction that releases heat
    Explanation: Exergonic means the reaction releases free energy (delta-G < 0). This is distinct from exothermic (releases heat, delta-H < 0). An exergonic reaction is spontaneous, while an endergonic reaction (delta-G > 0) is nonspontaneous.

  Q20. What does the term 'endergonic' mean?
    [ ] A reaction that occurs at all temperatures
    [ ] A reaction that releases free energy
    [ ] A reaction that absorbs heat
    [*] A reaction that absorbs free energy (delta-G > 0)
    Explanation: Endergonic means the reaction requires an input of free energy (delta-G > 0). Such reactions are nonspontaneous and will not proceed without a continuous supply of energy. In biological systems, endergonic reactions are often driven by coupling with ATP hydrolysis.

  Q21. A chemist determines that a reaction has delta-H = -120 kJ/mol and delta-S = -200 J/(mol*K). At what temperature does this reaction become nonspontaneous?
    [ ] The reaction is always spontaneous
    [ ] 1200 K
    [*] 600 K
    [ ] 300 K
    Explanation: Setting delta-G = 0: 0 = delta-H - T(delta-S). T = delta-H/delta-S = -120,000 J / (-200 J/K) = 600 K. Below 600 K, delta-G < 0 (spontaneous). Above 600 K, the large negative entropy term dominates, making delta-G > 0 (nonspontaneous).

  Q22. An industrial chemist calculates delta-G-standard for the synthesis of ammonia: N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g), delta-H = -92.4 kJ and delta-S = -198.3 J/K at 298 K. What is delta-G-standard?
    [ ] -92.4 kJ
    [*] -33.3 kJ
    [ ] -151.5 kJ
    [ ] +33.3 kJ
    Explanation: delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) = -92,400 J - (298 K)(-198.3 J/K) = -92,400 + 59,093 = -33,307 J = -33.3 kJ. The reaction is spontaneous at 298 K, but the negative entropy means it becomes nonspontaneous at higher temperatures.

  Q23. A researcher finds that delta-G-standard = -5.7 kJ/mol for a reaction at 298 K. What is the equilibrium constant K? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))
    [ ] 1.0
    [ ] 0.10
    [*] 10
    [ ] 100
    Explanation: Using delta-G-standard = -RT ln K: -5700 = -(8.314)(298) ln K. ln K = 5700/2478 = 2.30. K = e^2.30 = 10.0. A moderately negative delta-G corresponds to K = 10, meaning products are moderately favored.

  Q24. A biologist studying cellular metabolism knows that the hydrolysis of ATP has delta-G-standard = -30.5 kJ/mol. She wants to couple it with a reaction that has delta-G-standard = +18.0 kJ/mol. What is the overall delta-G-standard?
    [*] -12.5 kJ/mol
    [ ] +12.5 kJ/mol
    [ ] +48.5 kJ/mol
    [ ] -48.5 kJ/mol
    Explanation: When reactions are coupled, their delta-G values are additive: delta-G(overall) = -30.5 + 18.0 = -12.5 kJ/mol. The overall reaction is spontaneous because the ATP hydrolysis provides more than enough free energy to drive the endergonic reaction.

  Q25. A geochemist studying the decomposition of limestone (CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2) finds delta-H = +178.3 kJ and delta-S = +160.5 J/K. Above what temperature is this reaction spontaneous?
    [ ] 298 K (25 degrees C)
    [*] 1111 K (838 degrees C)
    [ ] 500 K (227 degrees C)
    [ ] 835 K (562 degrees C)
    Explanation: T = delta-H/delta-S = 178,300 J / 160.5 J/K = 1111 K (838 degrees C). Above 1111 K, the T(delta-S) term exceeds delta-H, making delta-G negative. This is why lime kilns must operate at very high temperatures to decompose limestone.

  Q26. A student calculates delta-G for a reaction at 298 K and finds delta-G-standard = +10.0 kJ/mol. If the actual concentrations give Q = 0.001, what is delta-G? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))
    [ ] +27.1 kJ/mol
    [ ] 0 kJ/mol
    [ ] +10.0 kJ/mol
    [*] -7.1 kJ/mol
    Explanation: delta-G = delta-G-standard + RT ln Q = 10,000 + (8.314)(298) ln(0.001) = 10,000 + (2478)(-6.908) = 10,000 - 17,114 = -7,114 J = -7.1 kJ/mol. Even though delta-G-standard is positive, the very low Q (mostly reactants present) makes the reaction spontaneous in the forward direction.

  Q27. An engineer is evaluating the combustion of methane: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(l). Using standard free energies of formation: delta-Gf(CO2) = -394.4 kJ/mol, delta-Gf(H2O) = -237.1 kJ/mol, delta-Gf(CH4) = -50.7 kJ/mol, delta-Gf(O2) = 0. What is delta-G-standard?

  Q28. A pharmaceutical researcher needs to know whether the dissolution of a drug (delta-H = +25 kJ/mol, delta-S = +100 J/(mol*K)) is spontaneous at body temperature (37 degrees C = 310 K). What is delta-G?
    [ ] +25.0 kJ/mol

    Explanation: delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) = 25,000 - (310)(100) = 25,000 - 31,000 = -6,000 J = -6.0 kJ/mol. The dissolution is spontaneous at body temperature because the entropy increase at 310 K is large enough to overcome the endothermic enthalpy.

  Q29. A student measures K = 2.5 x 10^-3 for a reaction at 298 K. What is delta-G-standard? (R = 8.314 J/(mol*K))

--- Lesson 9.1: Gibbs Free Energy (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$

--- Lesson 9.2: Spontaneity and Entropy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Spontaneity and Entropy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A student calculates delta-S for the reaction CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g) using standard entropies: S(CaCO3) = 92.9, S(CaO) = 39.7, S(CO2) = 213.7 J/(mol*K). What is delta-S?
delta-S = Sum S(products) - Sum S(reactants) = [39.7 + 213.7] - [92.9] = 253.4 - 92.9 = +160.5 J/(mol*K). The positive value makes sense because a gas is produced from a solid, greatly increasing the number of microstates.
A physicist studying entropy considers what happens when a hot metal block at 500 K is placed in cold water at 300 K. The block transfers 1000 J of heat to the water. What is the entropy change of the universe?
delta-S(metal) = -1000/500 = -2.00 J/K. delta-S(water) = +1000/300 = +3.33 J/K. delta-S(universe) = -2.00 + 3.33 = +1.33 J/K. The positive total confirms this is a spontaneous process, as expected for heat flow from hot to cold.

--- Lesson 9.2: Spontaneity and Entropy (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Entropy is a measure of:
    [ ] The temperature of a system
    [*] The disorder or number of possible microstates of a system
    [ ] The pressure of a system
    [ ] The heat content of a system
    Explanation: Entropy (S) is a thermodynamic quantity that measures the randomness or disorder in a system. More precisely, it measures the number of possible microstates (arrangements) available to the system. Higher entropy means more disorder and more possible microstates.

  Q2. According to the second law of thermodynamics, the total entropy of the universe:
    [ ] Remains constant
    [*] Always increases for spontaneous processes
    [ ] Can either increase or decrease
    [ ] Always decreases
    Explanation: The second law of thermodynamics states that for any spontaneous process, the total entropy of the universe (system + surroundings) increases. For reversible processes at equilibrium, the total entropy remains constant.

  Q3. Which process results in an increase in entropy?
    [ ] A gas condensing to a liquid
    [ ] Water freezing to ice
    [ ] Crystallization from a solution
    [*] A solid dissolving in water
    Explanation: When a solid dissolves in water, the highly ordered crystal lattice breaks apart and the ions or molecules become dispersed throughout the solution, significantly increasing the number of possible arrangements and thus entropy.

  Q4. The third law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of a perfect crystal at 0 K is:
    [ ] Undefined
    [*] Zero
    [ ] Negative
    [ ] Infinite
    Explanation: The third law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of a perfect crystal at absolute zero (0 K) is exactly zero. At this temperature, there is only one possible microstate (perfectly ordered arrangement), so S = k ln(1) = 0.

  Q5. For a phase change from liquid to gas at the boiling point, delta-S is:
    [ ] Positive but very small
    [ ] Zero
    [*] Positive and large
    [ ] Negative
    Explanation: Vaporization (liquid to gas) involves a large increase in entropy because gas molecules have much more freedom of motion and occupy a much larger volume than liquid molecules. The entropy change is typically large (e.g., about 88 J/(mol*K) for many liquids, known as Trouton's rule).

  Q6. Which has the highest molar entropy at 298 K?
    [*] Methane (CH4, gas)
    [ ] Diamond (C, solid)
    [ ] Iron (Fe, solid)
    [ ] Water (H2O, liquid)
    Explanation: Gases have much higher molar entropies than liquids, which in turn have higher entropies than solids. Among these options, methane gas has the highest entropy because gas molecules have translational, rotational, and vibrational freedom that solids and liquids lack.

  Q7. For the reaction 2H2(g) + O2(g) -> 2H2O(g), predict the sign of delta-S:
    [ ] Zero, because only gases are involved
    [ ] Positive, because gases are produced
    [ ] Cannot be determined without more data
    [*] Negative, because the number of gas moles decreases from 3 to 2
    Explanation: The reaction goes from 3 moles of gas (2H2 + 1O2) to 2 moles of gas (2H2O). A decrease in the total number of gas moles means fewer particles in the gas phase and decreased entropy, so delta-S is negative.

  Q8. The Boltzmann equation for entropy is S = k ln W. What does W represent?
    [ ] The work done by the system
    [ ] The wavelength of light emitted
    [ ] The weight of the system
    [*] The number of microstates
    Explanation: In Boltzmann's equation S = k ln W, W is the number of microstates (possible molecular arrangements) and k is Boltzmann's constant (1.38 x 10^-23 J/K). More microstates means higher entropy.

  Q9. Standard molar entropy values are always:
    [ ] Negative
    [ ] Equal to delta-Hf / T
    [ ] Zero for elements
    [*] Positive (greater than zero)
    Explanation: Unlike standard enthalpies of formation, standard molar entropies are always positive for all substances at temperatures above 0 K. This is because any substance above absolute zero has some molecular motion and thus some disorder.

  Q10. Entropy increases when:
    [ ] Gases combine to form a solid
    [ ] A gas is compressed to a smaller volume
    [*] A complex molecule breaks into simpler ones
    [ ] A solution crystallizes
    Explanation: Entropy increases when a complex molecule breaks into simpler ones because this increases the total number of particles and the number of possible arrangements. More particles with more degrees of freedom means more microstates and higher entropy.

  Q11. What does the term 'entropy' mean?
    [ ] The heat transferred at constant pressure
    [ ] The speed at which a reaction occurs
    [ ] The total energy stored in a system
    [*] A thermodynamic property that measures the degree of disorder or randomness in a system
    Explanation: Entropy (S) is a thermodynamic state function that quantifies the degree of disorder or randomness in a system. At the molecular level, it reflects the number of ways energy can be distributed among the particles (microstates). Its units are J/(mol*K).

  Q12. What does 'spontaneous process' mean in thermodynamics?
    [ ] A process that occurs rapidly
    [ ] A process that releases heat
    [*] A process that occurs on its own without continuous external intervention, once initiated
    [ ] A process that reaches equilibrium instantly
    Explanation: A spontaneous process is one that has a natural tendency to occur without continuous external energy input. It does NOT necessarily mean the process is fast; diamond converting to graphite is spontaneous but takes geological time. Spontaneity is determined by delta-G, not rate.

  Q13. What does the term 'microstate' mean?
    [*] A specific arrangement of particles and distribution of energy in a system
    [ ] The state of a system at absolute zero
    [ ] The smallest possible state of matter
    [ ] A state where all particles are at rest
    Explanation: A microstate is one particular arrangement of all the particles in a system with a specific distribution of energy. A system with many possible microstates has high entropy because there are many ways the particles can be arranged while maintaining the same macroscopic properties.

  Q14. What does the 'second law of thermodynamics' mean?
    [ ] Heat always flows from cold to hot objects
    [ ] Energy cannot be created or destroyed
    [ ] Entropy is always zero at absolute zero
    [*] The total entropy of the universe increases for any spontaneous process
    Explanation: The second law states that the entropy of the universe (system + surroundings) always increases for spontaneous processes: delta-S(universe) = delta-S(system) + delta-S(surroundings) > 0. This law gives directionality to natural processes.

  Q15. What does 'Trouton's rule' mean?
    [*] The entropy of vaporization for many liquids is approximately 88 J/(mol*K) at their normal boiling points
    [ ] The total entropy of a closed system never changes
    [ ] The entropy of fusion is always positive
    [ ] All gases have the same molar entropy at STP
    Explanation: Trouton's rule states that for many non-hydrogen-bonded liquids, the standard entropy of vaporization at the normal boiling point is approximately 88 J/(mol*K). Liquids with strong intermolecular forces (like water) have higher values because more energy is needed to overcome those forces.

  Q16. What does 'positional entropy' mean?
    [ ] The entropy of a substance at a specific position in the periodic table
    [*] The entropy associated with the number of positions or locations available to particles in a system
    [ ] The entropy change when a substance changes position from reactant to product
    [ ] The entropy of a GPS tracking system
    Explanation: Positional entropy (also called configurational entropy) refers to the entropy associated with the spatial distribution of particles. A gas has higher positional entropy than a liquid because gas molecules can occupy a much larger volume, giving them more possible positions.

  Q17. What does the term 'reversible process' mean in thermodynamics?
    [*] An idealized process where the system is always in equilibrium and can be reversed with an infinitesimal change, with no increase in total entropy
    [ ] A process that occurs in the reverse direction when considering all relevant process factors
    [ ] A process that happens twice due to the well-established relationship between process trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] Any process that can be undone when the interaction between process and reversible factors is taken into full account
    Explanation: A reversible process is an idealized process that proceeds through a series of equilibrium states and can be exactly reversed by an infinitesimal change in conditions. For reversible processes, delta-S(universe) = 0. Real processes are irreversible with delta-S(universe) > 0.

  Q18. What does 'delta-S-surroundings' mean?
    [ ] The entropy change of the system multiplied by -1
    [ ] The entropy change that equals zero for all processes
    [*] The entropy change of the surroundings, calculated as -delta-H / T for a process at constant temperature and pressure
    [ ] The total entropy of everything outside the laboratory
    Explanation: The entropy change of the surroundings is calculated as delta-S(surr) = -delta-H(sys) / T. An exothermic reaction (negative delta-H) increases the entropy of the surroundings (positive delta-S-surr), while an endothermic reaction decreases it.

  Q19. What does 'absolute entropy' mean?
    [ ] The entropy of a substance in absolute vacuum
    [*] The entropy value determined relative to the reference point of zero entropy at 0 K for a perfect crystal
    [ ] The maximum possible entropy of any substance
    [ ] The entropy without any sign (positive or negative)
    Explanation: Absolute entropy (also called third-law entropy) is the entropy of a substance measured from the reference point established by the third law of thermodynamics: S = 0 at 0 K for a perfect crystal. Unlike enthalpy, we can determine absolute values of entropy.

  Q20. What does the term 'residual entropy' mean?
    [ ] The small amount of entropy in a vacuum
    [ ] The difference between system and surroundings entropy
    [ ] The entropy left over after a reaction is complete
    [*] Entropy that remains in a crystal at 0 K due to molecular disorder that persists even at absolute zero
    Explanation: Residual entropy is the entropy that remains at absolute zero for substances that do not form a perfect crystal. For example, CO molecules can orient as CO or OC in the crystal, creating disorder even at 0 K. Ice also has residual entropy due to hydrogen bond arrangements.

  Q21. A chemist observes that the dissolution of ammonium nitrate in water is endothermic (delta-H = +25.7 kJ/mol) yet spontaneous at 25 degrees C. What must be true about delta-S for this process?
    [ ] Delta-S is irrelevant to spontaneity
    [ ] Delta-S must be negative
    [*] Delta-S must be positive and large enough that T(delta-S) > delta-H
    [ ] Delta-S must be zero
    Explanation: For a spontaneous endothermic process, delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) < 0. Since delta-H is positive, T(delta-S) must be greater than delta-H to make delta-G negative. This requires delta-S to be sufficiently large and positive: delta-S > 25,700/298 = 86.2 J/(mol*K).

  Q22. An environmental scientist compares CO2(g) (S = 213.7 J/(mol*K)) with CaCO3(s) (S = 92.9 J/(mol*K)). Why does the gas have much higher entropy?
    [ ] Because CO2 is lighter than CaCO3, as conventional Caco theory would suggest
    [ ] Because CO2 has more atoms per molecule, based on conventional assumptions about Caco
    [*] Because gas molecules have much greater translational freedom and occupy more volume than molecules in a solid lattice
    [ ] Because solids always have zero entropy regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    Explanation: Gas-phase CO2 has much higher entropy than solid CaCO3 primarily because gas molecules have extensive translational freedom, moving randomly throughout their container. Solid CaCO3 has ions locked in a crystal lattice with limited motion. The phase of matter is the dominant factor in determining entropy.

  Q23. A student calculates delta-S for the reaction CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g) using standard entropies: S(CaCO3) = 92.9, S(CaO) = 39.7, S(CO2) = 213.7 J/(mol*K). What is delta-S?
    [ ] -160.5 J/(mol*K)
    [ ] +81.0 J/(mol*K)
    [ ] +346.3 J/(mol*K)
    [*] +160.5 J/(mol*K)
    Explanation: delta-S = Sum S(products) - Sum S(reactants) = [39.7 + 213.7] - [92.9] = 253.4 - 92.9 = +160.5 J/(mol*K). The positive value makes sense because a gas is produced from a solid, greatly increasing the number of microstates.

  Q24. A physicist studying entropy considers what happens when a hot metal block at 500 K is placed in cold water at 300 K. The block transfers 1000 J of heat to the water. What is the entropy change of the universe?
    [*] +1.33 J/K
    [ ] +5.33 J/K
    [ ] -1.33 J/K
    [ ] 0 J/K
    Explanation: delta-S(metal) = -1000/500 = -2.00 J/K. delta-S(water) = +1000/300 = +3.33 J/K. delta-S(universe) = -2.00 + 3.33 = +1.33 J/K. The positive total confirms this is a spontaneous process, as expected for heat flow from hot to cold.

  Q25. A biochemist studies the folding of a protein, which has delta-H = -40 kJ/mol and delta-S = -100 J/(mol*K). At what temperature does protein folding become nonspontaneous?
    [ ] It is always spontaneous
    [ ] 200 K
    [ ] 600 K
    [*] 400 K
    Explanation: T = delta-H / delta-S = -40,000 / -100 = 400 K. Below 400 K, the exothermic enthalpy drives spontaneity (delta-G < 0). Above 400 K, the unfavorable entropy term dominates, making delta-G > 0. This explains why proteins denature (unfold) at high temperatures.

  Q26. A student is given four reactions and asked which has the most positive delta-S. Which reaction has the greatest entropy increase?
    [*] 2KClO3(s) -> 2KCl(s) + 3O2(g)
    [ ] N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g)
    [ ] NaCl(s) -> Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)
    [ ] H2O(l) -> H2O(s)
    Explanation: 2KClO3(s) -> 2KCl(s) + 3O2(g) has the greatest entropy increase because 3 moles of gas are produced from a solid. Gas formation is the strongest driver of entropy increase. Freezing water (negative delta-S) and ammonia synthesis (decrease in gas moles) have negative delta-S. Dissolving NaCl has positive delta-S but less than producing gas.

  Q27. A chemical engineer determines that an industrial reaction has delta-H = +50 kJ/mol and delta-S = -80 J/(mol*K). What can she conclude about the spontaneity of this reaction?
    [*] Nonspontaneous at all temperatures
    [ ] Spontaneous only at low temperatures
    [ ] Spontaneous at all temperatures
    [ ] Spontaneous only at high temperatures
    Explanation: When delta-H > 0 (endothermic) and delta-S < 0 (entropy decreases), delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) is always positive because both terms contribute positively to delta-G. The enthalpy term is positive and the -T(delta-S) term is also positive (since delta-S is negative). The reaction is nonspontaneous at all temperatures.

  Q28. An atmospheric scientist considers the entropy of the reaction: N2O4(g) -> 2NO2(g). Given S(N2O4) = 304.3 J/(mol*K) and S(NO2) = 240.1 J/(mol*K), calculate delta-S and explain the result.
    [ ] +64.2 J/(mol*K)
    [ ] -64.2 J/(mol*K)
    [*] +175.9 J/(mol*K)
    [ ] -175.9 J/(mol*K)
    Explanation: delta-S = 2(240.1) - 304.3 = 480.2 - 304.3 = +175.9 J/(mol*K). The positive value is expected because one molecule splits into two gas molecules, doubling the number of independent particles and greatly increasing the number of available microstates.

  Q29. A food scientist knows that liquid water freezes spontaneously below 0 degrees C despite having delta-S(system) < 0. How is this consistent with the second law of thermodynamics?
    [ ] The entropy of the system actually increases during freezing
    [*] The exothermic nature of freezing increases the entropy of the surroundings enough to make delta-S(universe) > 0
    [ ] Freezing is not actually spontaneous below 0 degrees C
    [ ] The second law does not apply to phase changes
    Explanation: Freezing is exothermic (delta-H < 0), so heat is released to the surroundings. This increases delta-S(surroundings) = -delta-H/T. Below 0 degrees C, the positive delta-S(surroundings) exceeds the negative delta-S(system), making delta-S(universe) > 0, satisfying the second law.

  Q30. A researcher studies the dissolution of NaCl in water. The process has delta-H = +3.9 kJ/mol. At 25 degrees C, the process is spontaneous. What minimum value must delta-S have?
    [*] Greater than 13.1 J/(mol*K)
    [ ] It can be any negative value
    [ ] Greater than 130 J/(mol*K)
    [ ] Greater than 0 J/(mol*K)
    Explanation: For spontaneity: delta-G = delta-H - T(delta-S) < 0. So T(delta-S) > delta-H: 298(delta-S) > 3900 J. delta-S > 3900/298 = 13.1 J/(mol*K). The actual delta-S for NaCl dissolution is about +43 J/(mol*K), well above this minimum, confirming spontaneity.

--- Lesson 9.3: Electrochemistry and Redox (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electrochemistry and Redox
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the oxidation state of Mn in KMnO4?
In KMnO4: K = +1, O = -2 (four oxygens = -8). The sum must be 0: +1 + Mn + (-8) = 0, so Mn = +7. Manganese in permanganate has its highest common oxidation state.
What is the oxidation state of S in H2SO4?
In H2SO4: H = +1 (two = +2), O = -2 (four = -8). Sum = 0: +2 + S + (-8) = 0, so S = +6. Sulfur in sulfuric acid is in its highest common oxidation state.
What is the oxidation state of Cr in Cr2O7^2-?
In Cr2O7^2-: O = -2 (seven = -14). The total charge is -2: 2(Cr) + (-14) = -2, so 2(Cr) = +12, Cr = +6. Chromium in dichromate is a strong oxidizing agent in its +6 state.
A student is told that a silver coin will not dissolve in HCl(aq) but will dissolve in HNO3(aq). Given that Ag is below H2 in the activity series, what is the best explanation?
Since Ag is below H2 in the activity series, H+ alone cannot oxidize Ag. In HCl, the only oxidizing agent is H+. However, in HNO3, the nitrate ion (NO3-) is a powerful oxidizing agent that can oxidize silver: 3Ag + 4H+ + NO3- -> 3Ag+ + NO + 2H2O. The oxidizing power of NO3- drives the reaction.
Real-World Applications
Example: A reducing agent is a substance that:
A reducing agent reduces another substance by donating electrons to it. In the process, the reducing agent itself is oxidized (loses electrons). The stronger the reducing agent, the more easily it gives up its electrons.
Example: In the reaction Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s), which species is the oxidizing agent?
Cu2+ is reduced (gains electrons to become Cu), so Cu2+ is the oxidizing agent. It oxidizes Zn by accepting the electrons that Zn loses. Zn is oxidized (loses electrons) and serves as the reducing agent.
Example: What does the term 'oxidizing agent' mean?
An oxidizing agent (oxidant) causes the oxidation of another substance by accepting electrons from it. In the process, the oxidizing agent itself is reduced. Common oxidizing agents include MnO4-, Cr2O7^2-, O2, and halogens.
Example: What does the term 'reducing agent' mean?
A reducing agent (reductant) causes the reduction of another substance by donating electrons to it. The reducing agent itself is oxidized in the process. Common reducing agents include active metals (Na, Mg, Zn), H2, and C (carbon).

--- Lesson 9.3: Electrochemistry and Redox (Quiz) ---

  Q1. In a redox reaction, the substance that is oxidized:
    [ ] Gains electrons
    [ ] Gains protons
    [ ] Loses neutrons
    [*] Loses electrons
    Explanation: Oxidation is the loss of electrons. The mnemonic OIL RIG helps: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). The substance that is oxidized donates electrons to the substance being reduced.

  Q2. What is the oxidation state of Mn in KMnO4?
    [ ] +5
    [ ] +2
    [*] +7
    [ ] +3
    Explanation: In KMnO4: K = +1, O = -2 (four oxygens = -8). The sum must be 0: +1 + Mn + (-8) = 0, so Mn = +7. Manganese in permanganate has its highest common oxidation state.

  Q3. A reducing agent is a substance that:
    [*] Gets oxidized and donates electrons to another substance
    [ ] Gets reduced and gains electrons
    [ ] Decreases the temperature of the reaction
    [ ] Accepts protons from another substance
    Explanation: A reducing agent reduces another substance by donating electrons to it. In the process, the reducing agent itself is oxidized (loses electrons). The stronger the reducing agent, the more easily it gives up its electrons.

  Q4. The oxidation state of any free (uncombined) element is:
    [ ] +1
    [ ] -1
    [ ] Equal to its group number
    [*] 0
    Explanation: Any element in its free, uncombined state (e.g., Fe, O2, Na, Cl2) has an oxidation state of 0. Oxidation states only change from zero when atoms form bonds with other atoms.

  Q5. In the reaction Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s), which species is the oxidizing agent?
    [ ] Zn(s)
    [*] Cu2+(aq)
    [ ] Cu(s)
    [ ] Zn2+(aq)
    Explanation: Cu2+ is reduced (gains electrons to become Cu), so Cu2+ is the oxidizing agent. It oxidizes Zn by accepting the electrons that Zn loses. Zn is oxidized (loses electrons) and serves as the reducing agent.

  Q6. What is the oxidation state of S in H2SO4?
    [ ] +4
    [ ] -2
    [ ] +2
    [*] +6
    Explanation: In H2SO4: H = +1 (two = +2), O = -2 (four = -8). Sum = 0: +2 + S + (-8) = 0, so S = +6. Sulfur in sulfuric acid is in its highest common oxidation state.

  Q7. In a balanced half-reaction, what must be conserved?
    [*] Both mass and charge
    [ ] Only charge
    [ ] Neither mass nor charge
    [ ] Only mass
    Explanation: In a balanced half-reaction, both mass (equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides) and charge (equal total charge on both sides) must be conserved. Electrons are added to one side to balance charge.

  Q8. Which element undergoes disproportionation in the reaction: 2H2O2 -> 2H2O + O2?
    [ ] Both hydrogen and oxygen
    [ ] Neither; this is not a redox reaction
    [*] Oxygen
    [ ] Hydrogen
    Explanation: Oxygen undergoes disproportionation. In H2O2, oxygen is -1. In the products, it becomes -2 in H2O (reduction) and 0 in O2 (oxidation). The same element is both oxidized and reduced, which is the definition of disproportionation.

  Q9. In acidic solution, which species are used to balance oxygen atoms in half-reactions?
    [*] H2O molecules
    [ ] O2- ions
    [ ] OH- ions
    [ ] O2 molecules
    Explanation: In acidic solution, oxygen atoms are balanced by adding H2O molecules. Then hydrogen atoms are balanced by adding H+ ions. Finally, charge is balanced with electrons. In basic solution, OH- and H2O are used instead.

  Q10. What is the oxidation state of Cr in Cr2O7^2-?
    [ ] +3
    [ ] +7
    [*] +6
    [ ] +12
    Explanation: In Cr2O7^2-: O = -2 (seven = -14). The total charge is -2: 2(Cr) + (-14) = -2, so 2(Cr) = +12, Cr = +6. Chromium in dichromate is a strong oxidizing agent in its +6 state.

  Q11. What does the term 'oxidation' mean?
    [*] The loss of electrons or an increase in oxidation state
    [ ] The decrease in oxidation state
    [ ] The addition of oxygen to a molecule only
    [ ] The gain of electrons
    Explanation: Oxidation is defined as the loss of electrons, which results in an increase in oxidation state. While historically it referred to gaining oxygen, the modern definition focuses on electron transfer. For example, when Fe goes from Fe to Fe2+, it is oxidized by losing 2 electrons.

  Q12. What does the term 'reduction' mean?
    [ ] Making a substance smaller
    [ ] The loss of electrons
    [*] The gain of electrons or a decrease in oxidation state
    [ ] The removal of oxygen from a compound
    Explanation: Reduction is the gain of electrons, resulting in a decrease in oxidation state. The term comes from 'reducing' the charge. For example, Cu2+ + 2e- -> Cu is a reduction, as copper's oxidation state decreases from +2 to 0.

  Q13. What does the term 'oxidation state' (or 'oxidation number') mean?
    [ ] The actual charge on an atom in an ionic compound
    [*] A hypothetical charge assigned to an atom based on a set of rules, assuming all bonds are ionic
    [ ] The number of bonds an atom forms
    [ ] The number of electrons in an atom's outer shell
    Explanation: The oxidation state is a hypothetical charge assigned to an atom using a set of rules (e.g., O is usually -2, H is usually +1, free elements are 0). It represents what the charge would be if all bonds were purely ionic. It is useful for tracking electron transfer in redox reactions.

  Q14. What does 'half-reaction' mean?
    [ ] Half of a chemical equation
    [*] Either the oxidation or reduction portion of a redox reaction, written separately to show electron transfer
    [ ] A reaction that only goes halfway to completion
    [ ] A reaction involving half a mole of substance
    Explanation: A half-reaction shows either the oxidation or the reduction part of a redox reaction separately, explicitly showing the electrons gained or lost. For example: Zn -> Zn2+ + 2e- (oxidation half) and Cu2+ + 2e- -> Cu (reduction half).

  Q15. What does the term 'oxidizing agent' mean?
    [ ] A substance that adds oxygen to another compound
    [*] A substance that causes another to be oxidized by accepting electrons; it itself is reduced
    [ ] A substance that is oxidized and loses electrons
    [ ] A catalyst for redox reactions
    Explanation: An oxidizing agent (oxidant) causes the oxidation of another substance by accepting electrons from it. In the process, the oxidizing agent itself is reduced. Common oxidizing agents include MnO4-, Cr2O7^2-, O2, and halogens.

  Q16. What does the term 'reducing agent' mean?
    [ ] A substance that is reduced and gains electrons
    [*] A substance that causes another to be reduced by donating electrons; it itself is oxidized
    [ ] A substance that slows down a reaction
    [ ] A substance that removes oxygen from a compound
    Explanation: A reducing agent (reductant) causes the reduction of another substance by donating electrons to it. The reducing agent itself is oxidized in the process. Common reducing agents include active metals (Na, Mg, Zn), H2, and C (carbon).

  Q17. What does the term 'disproportionation' mean?
    [ ] A reaction where no electron transfer occurs
    [*] A reaction where the same element is both oxidized and reduced
    [ ] A reaction where two different elements exchange electrons
    [ ] A reaction that produces unequal amounts of products
    Explanation: Disproportionation is a redox reaction in which the same element in a single compound is simultaneously oxidized and reduced, producing two different oxidation states. For example, in 2Cu+ -> Cu + Cu2+, copper(I) is both oxidized to Cu2+ and reduced to Cu.

  Q18. What does the term 'standard reduction potential' mean?
    [ ] The rate at which reduction occurs
    [ ] The energy required to reduce one mole of a substance
    [*] The tendency of a species to be reduced, measured in volts relative to the standard hydrogen electrode
    [ ] The temperature at which reduction becomes spontaneous
    Explanation: The standard reduction potential (E-standard) is a measure of the tendency of a chemical species to gain electrons (be reduced), measured in volts under standard conditions and referenced to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), which is assigned E = 0.00 V.

  Q19. What does the term 'activity series' mean?
    [ ] A list of elements ordered by atomic number
    [*] A list of metals ranked in order of their reactivity or tendency to be oxidized
    [ ] A ranking of elements by their melting points
    [ ] A list of the most common oxidation states for each element
    Explanation: The activity series (reactivity series) ranks metals by their tendency to be oxidized (lose electrons). Metals higher in the series are more reactive and can displace metals lower in the series from solutions. For example, Zn is above Cu, so zinc can displace copper from CuSO4 solution.

  Q20. What does the term 'redox reaction' mean?
    [ ] A reaction that can only occur in solution
    [ ] Any reaction that produces a color change
    [*] A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between species, where one is oxidized and another is reduced
    [ ] A reaction involving the transfer of protons
    Explanation: A redox (reduction-oxidation) reaction involves the transfer of electrons from one species to another. Oxidation (electron loss) and reduction (electron gain) always occur simultaneously because electrons cannot exist alone in solution.

  Q21. A forensic chemist uses the reaction of dichromate with ethanol to detect drunk driving: Cr2O7^2- + C2H5OH -> Cr3+ + CH3COOH. In this reaction, what happens to chromium?
    [*] Chromium is reduced from +6 to +3
    [ ] Chromium's oxidation state does not change
    [ ] Chromium is oxidized from +6 to +3
    [ ] Chromium is oxidized from +3 to +6
    Explanation: Cr goes from +6 in Cr2O7^2- to +3 in Cr3+, a decrease in oxidation state. A decrease in oxidation state means the species gained electrons, so chromium is reduced. Dichromate acts as the oxidizing agent, oxidizing ethanol to acetic acid.

  Q22. A metallurgist places a strip of iron into a solution of CuSO4. He observes copper depositing on the iron. Using the activity series (Fe is above Cu), explain what happened in terms of electron transfer.
    [*] Fe donated electrons to Cu2+, causing Fe to dissolve as Fe2+ and Cu2+ to deposit as Cu metal
    [ ] No electron transfer occurred; this is an acid-base reaction
    [ ] Cu2+ donated electrons to Fe
    [ ] Both metals were reduced simultaneously
    Explanation: Since Fe is above Cu in the activity series, iron is a stronger reducing agent than copper. Fe donates 2 electrons to Cu2+: Fe -> Fe2+ + 2e- (oxidation) and Cu2+ + 2e- -> Cu (reduction). The iron dissolves while copper metal deposits on the surface.

  Q23. A student needs to balance the half-reaction MnO4- -> Mn2+ in acidic solution. How many electrons are transferred?
    [ ] 2 electrons
    [ ] 7 electrons
    [*] 5 electrons
    [ ] 3 electrons
    Explanation: Mn goes from +7 in MnO4- to +2 in Mn2+, a change of 5 units. The balanced half-reaction is: MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn2+ + 4H2O. Five electrons are gained by Mn, consistent with a reduction from +7 to +2.

  Q24. An environmental engineer analyzes the oxidation of iron(II) to iron(III) in acidic wastewater: Fe2+ -> Fe3+ + e-. If 0.10 mol of Fe2+ is fully oxidized, how many moles of electrons are transferred?
    [ ] 0.20 mol
    [ ] 0.05 mol
    [ ] 0.30 mol
    [*] 0.10 mol
    Explanation: The half-reaction Fe2+ -> Fe3+ + e- shows that each Fe2+ loses 1 electron. Therefore 0.10 mol Fe2+ produces 0.10 mol electrons. These electrons must be accepted by an oxidizing agent (such as dissolved oxygen or dichromate) in the reduction half-reaction.

  Q25. A chemist determines the oxidation states in the decomposition of KClO3: 2KClO3(s) -> 2KCl(s) + 3O2(g). Which statement is correct?
    [ ] Only Cl changes oxidation state
    [ ] This is not a redox reaction
    [ ] Cl is oxidized and O is reduced
    [*] Cl is reduced from +5 to -1, and O is oxidized from -2 to 0
    Explanation: In KClO3: K = +1, O = -2, so Cl = +5. In KCl: K = +1, Cl = -1. In O2: O = 0. Chlorine is reduced (+5 to -1, gains 6 electrons) and oxygen is oxidized (-2 to 0, loses 2 electrons per atom). This is a redox reaction where chlorate acts as the oxidizing agent for the oxygen within the same compound.

  Q26. A student is told that a silver coin will not dissolve in HCl(aq) but will dissolve in HNO3(aq). Given that Ag is below H2 in the activity series, what is the best explanation?
    [ ] Silver reacts with nitrogen but not chlorine
    [ ] HNO3 produces more H+ ions than HCl
    [ ] HNO3 is a stronger acid than HCl
    [*] The nitrate ion (NO3-) is a strong oxidizing agent that can oxidize Ag, while Cl- cannot
    Explanation: Since Ag is below H2 in the activity series, H+ alone cannot oxidize Ag. In HCl, the only oxidizing agent is H+. However, in HNO3, the nitrate ion (NO3-) is a powerful oxidizing agent that can oxidize silver: 3Ag + 4H+ + NO3- -> 3Ag+ + NO + 2H2O. The oxidizing power of NO3- drives the reaction.

  Q27. A laboratory technician needs to balance the following reaction in basic solution: MnO4- + C2O4^2- -> MnO2 + CO3^2-. How many electrons are transferred per MnO4-?
    [ ] 7 electrons
    [*] 3 electrons
    [ ] 2 electrons
    [ ] 5 electrons
    Explanation: Mn goes from +7 in MnO4- to +4 in MnO2, gaining 3 electrons per Mn atom. The reduction half-reaction is: MnO4- + 2H2O + 3e- -> MnO2 + 4OH-. Meanwhile, carbon is oxidized from +3 in C2O4^2- to +4 in CO3^2-.

  Q29. A water treatment plant uses chlorine to disinfect water: Cl2 + H2O -> HClO + HCl. Identify the type of redox reaction occurring.

    [*] Disproportionation - Cl2 (0) is both oxidized to +1 in HClO and reduced to -1 in HCl
    [ ] Single displacement reaction under most circumstances involving chlorine
    Explanation: Cl2 has oxidation state 0. In HClO, Cl is +1 (oxidized). In HCl, Cl is -1 (reduced). The same element (chlorine) is both oxidized and reduced, making this a disproportionation reaction. This is common for elements in intermediate oxidation states.

  Q30. A biochemist studies the electron transport chain, which involves the sequential reduction and oxidation of molecules. In the reaction: NADH -> NAD+ + H+ + 2e-, what role does NADH play?

    [ ] Oxidizing agent

--- Lesson 9.4: Electrochemical Cells and Batteries (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electrochemical Cells and Batteries
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the function of the salt bridge in a galvanic cell?
The salt bridge allows ions to migrate between the two half-cells to maintain electrical neutrality. Without it, charge buildup would quickly stop the cell from functioning. Anions migrate toward the anode and cations toward the cathode through the salt bridge.
A student builds a galvanic cell with Zn/Zn2+ and Cu/Cu2+ half-cells. Given E-standard(Cu2+/Cu) = +0.34 V and E-standard(Zn2+/Zn) = -0.76 V, what is E-standard-cell?
Cu2+ has the higher reduction potential, so it is reduced (cathode). Zn has the lower, so it is oxidized (anode). E-cell = E-cathode - E-anode = (+0.34) - (-0.76) = +1.10 V. This is the classic Daniell cell, one of the first practical galvanic cells.
A chemist calculates delta-G for a cell with E-standard = +0.46 V and n = 2 electrons. What is delta-G-standard? (F = 96,485 C/mol)
delta-G = -nFE = -(2)(96,485)(0.46) = -88,766 J = -88.8 kJ. The negative delta-G confirms the reaction is spontaneous, consistent with the positive cell potential.
A concentration cell consists of two Cu/Cu2+ half-cells where one has [Cu2+] = 1.0 M and the other has [Cu2+] = 0.010 M. What is the cell potential at 298 K?
For a concentration cell, E-standard = 0 (same electrodes). Cu2+ is reduced at the concentrated side (cathode) and oxidized at the dilute side (anode). E = 0 - (0.0592/2)log(0.010/1.0) = -(0.0296)(-2) = +0.059 V. The cell runs until concentrations equalize.
A student measures the cell potential of a Zn/Cu cell and finds E = 1.04 V instead of the expected 1.10 V. Using the Nernst equation with n = 2, what is the approximate value of Q?
E = E-standard - (0.0592/n)log Q. 1.04 = 1.10 - (0.0592/2)log Q. 0.0296 log Q = 0.06. log Q = 2.03. Q is approximately 10^2 = 100. This means [Zn2+]/[Cu2+] is approximately 100, indicating much more product than reactant.
A researcher determines that a galvanic cell reaction has E-standard = +0.80 V and n = 1. What is the equilibrium constant K at 298 K?
At equilibrium, E = 0. Using E-standard = (0.0592/n)log K: 0.80 = (0.0592/1)log K. log K = 0.80/0.0592 = 13.51. K = 10^13.51 = 3.2 x 10^13, approximately 3.8 x 10^13. The large K confirms the reaction strongly favors products.

  Q1. In a galvanic (voltaic) cell, oxidation occurs at which electrode?
    [ ] The cathode
    [ ] The salt bridge
    [ ] Both electrodes equally
    [*] The anode
    Explanation: In any electrochemical cell (galvanic or electrolytic), oxidation always occurs at the anode. The mnemonic 'An Ox' (Anode = Oxidation) helps remember this. In a galvanic cell, the anode is the negative electrode.

  Q2. What is the function of the salt bridge in a galvanic cell?
    [ ] To prevent the reaction from occurring
    [ ] To allow electron flow between the half-cells
    [ ] To increase the voltage of the cell
    [*] To maintain electrical neutrality by allowing ion migration between the half-cells
    Explanation: The salt bridge allows ions to migrate between the two half-cells to maintain electrical neutrality. Without it, charge buildup would quickly stop the cell from functioning. Anions migrate toward the anode and cations toward the cathode through the salt bridge.

  Q3. The standard cell potential (E-cell) is calculated as:
    [ ] E-cathode x E-anode
    [ ] E-cathode + E-anode
    [*] E-cathode - E-anode
    [ ] E-anode - E-cathode
    Explanation: The standard cell potential is E-cell = E-cathode (reduction) - E-anode (reduction). Both values are standard reduction potentials from the table. The species with the higher reduction potential is the cathode (gets reduced), and the lower one is the anode (gets oxidized).

  Q4. A positive cell potential (E-cell > 0) indicates that:
    [ ] The reaction is nonspontaneous
    [ ] The reaction is at equilibrium
    [*] The reaction is spontaneous
    [ ] No reaction will occur
    Explanation: A positive E-cell means the reaction is spontaneous (delta-G < 0). The relationship is delta-G = -nFE-cell, so when E-cell > 0, delta-G < 0. Galvanic cells always have positive cell potentials.

  Q5. The standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) has an assigned potential of:
    [ ] +1.00 V
    [ ] +0.50 V
    [*] 0.00 V
    [ ] -1.00 V
    Explanation: The standard hydrogen electrode (2H+ + 2e- -> H2) is defined as having E-standard = 0.00 V by convention. All other standard reduction potentials are measured relative to this reference electrode.

  Q6. In an electrolytic cell, electrical energy is used to:
    [ ] Generate electricity from a spontaneous reaction
    [ ] Create a salt bridge
    [*] Drive a nonspontaneous reaction
    [ ] Increase the temperature of the solution
    Explanation: An electrolytic cell uses electrical energy from an external source to drive a nonspontaneous reaction (E-cell < 0, delta-G > 0). This is the opposite of a galvanic cell, which uses a spontaneous reaction to generate electrical energy.

  Q7. In a galvanic cell, electrons flow through the external circuit from:
    [*] Anode to cathode
    [ ] Cathode to anode
    [ ] Through the salt bridge
    [ ] In both directions simultaneously
    Explanation: Electrons flow from the anode (where oxidation produces electrons) through the external wire to the cathode (where reduction consumes electrons). This electron flow is what provides the electrical current that can do work.

  Q8. The relationship between cell potential and Gibbs free energy is:
    [ ] delta-G = -nE/F
    [*] delta-G = -nFE
    [ ] delta-G = E/nF
    [ ] delta-G = nFE
    Explanation: delta-G = -nFE, where n is the number of moles of electrons transferred, F is Faraday's constant (96,485 C/mol), and E is the cell potential. This equation directly connects electrochemistry to thermodynamics.

  Q9. Faraday's constant (F) represents:
    [*] The charge carried by one mole of electrons (96,485 C/mol)
    [ ] The number of electrons in one mole
    [ ] The voltage of a standard cell
    [ ] The charge of one electron
    Explanation: Faraday's constant F = 96,485 C/mol is the total electric charge carried by one mole of electrons. It equals Avogadro's number times the charge of one electron: (6.022 x 10^23)(1.602 x 10^-19 C) = 96,485 C/mol.

  Q10. The Nernst equation allows calculation of cell potential under:
    [*] Non-standard conditions
    [ ] Only at equilibrium
    [ ] Standard conditions only
    [ ] Only at high temperatures
    Explanation: The Nernst equation, E = E-standard - (RT/nF) ln Q, allows calculation of the cell potential under any conditions, not just standard ones. It shows how concentration changes affect the cell voltage.

  Q11. What does the term 'galvanic cell' (or 'voltaic cell') mean?
    [ ] A cell that stores thermal energy
    [*] An electrochemical cell that uses a spontaneous redox reaction to generate electrical energy
    [ ] A cell that produces light
    [ ] A cell that uses electricity to drive a nonspontaneous reaction
    Explanation: A galvanic (voltaic) cell converts chemical energy from a spontaneous redox reaction into electrical energy. The two half-reactions are separated into half-cells connected by a salt bridge, and the electron flow through an external circuit can do useful work.

  Q12. What does the term 'electrolytic cell' mean?
    [ ] A cell containing only electrolyte solution
    [ ] A cell that dissolves metals in acid
    [*] An electrochemical cell that uses an external power source to drive a nonspontaneous redox reaction
    [ ] A cell that generates electricity from chemical reactions
    Explanation: An electrolytic cell uses electrical energy from an external source (like a battery or power supply) to force a nonspontaneous reaction to occur. Examples include electroplating, electrolysis of water, and aluminum refining.

  Q13. What does the term 'anode' mean?
    [ ] The electrode where reduction occurs
    [*] The electrode where oxidation occurs
    [ ] The electrode made of platinum
    [ ] The positive electrode in all types of cells
    Explanation: The anode is the electrode where oxidation occurs in any electrochemical cell. In a galvanic cell, the anode is negative (electrons flow away from it). In an electrolytic cell, the anode is positive (connected to the positive terminal of the power source).

  Q14. What does the term 'cathode' mean?
    [ ] The electrode that dissolves
    [*] The electrode where reduction occurs
    [ ] The negative electrode in all types of cells
    [ ] The electrode where oxidation occurs
    Explanation: The cathode is the electrode where reduction occurs. In a galvanic cell, the cathode is positive (electrons flow toward it). In an electrolytic cell, the cathode is negative (connected to the negative terminal of the power source). Cations migrate toward the cathode.

  Q15. What does the term 'cell potential' (or EMF) mean?
    [ ] The total amount of charge stored in a cell
    [*] The difference in electrical potential between the cathode and anode that drives electron flow
    [ ] The current flowing through the cell
    [ ] The resistance of the electrolyte
    Explanation: Cell potential (electromotive force, EMF) is the voltage difference between the two electrodes that drives electron flow through the external circuit. It is measured in volts (V) and depends on the identity of the electrode materials and the concentrations of species.

  Q16. What does the 'Nernst equation' describe?
    [*] How cell potential varies with concentration, pressure, and temperature under non-standard conditions
    [ ] The flow of current in a wire, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] How temperature affects reaction rate within the broader context of cell analysis
    [ ] The relationship between pH and pKa, as conventional cell theory would suggest
    Explanation: The Nernst equation, E = E-standard - (RT/nF)ln(Q), relates the cell potential to the standard cell potential and the reaction quotient Q. At 298 K, it simplifies to E = E-standard - (0.0592/n)log(Q). It shows that cell potential decreases as products accumulate.

  Q17. What does the term 'electrolysis' mean?
    [ ] The spontaneous generation of electricity
    [ ] The dissolution of an electrolyte in water
    [*] The use of electrical energy to decompose a compound or drive a nonspontaneous chemical reaction
    [ ] The flow of ions through a membrane
    Explanation: Electrolysis is the process of using direct electric current to drive a thermodynamically nonspontaneous chemical reaction. Common examples include the electrolysis of water into H2 and O2, electroplating metals, and the Hall-Heroult process for producing aluminum.

  Q18. What does 'electroplating' mean?
    [ ] Coating metal with plastic
    [*] Using electrolysis to deposit a thin layer of metal onto an object
    [ ] Using a galvanic cell to corrode metal
    [ ] Measuring the voltage of an electrochemical cell
    Explanation: Electroplating uses electrolysis to coat an object with a thin layer of metal. The object to be plated serves as the cathode, and the plating metal is in solution (or serves as the anode). Common applications include chrome plating, gold plating, and zinc galvanizing.

  Q19. What does the term 'half-cell' mean?
    [ ] The part of the cell where the salt bridge is located
    [ ] A cell that produces half the expected voltage
    [ ] A battery at 50% charge
    [*] One compartment of an electrochemical cell containing an electrode and electrolyte where one half-reaction occurs
    Explanation: A half-cell is one compartment of an electrochemical cell, containing an electrode immersed in an electrolyte solution. Each half-cell is the site of one half-reaction (either oxidation or reduction). The two half-cells are connected by a salt bridge and an external circuit.

  Q20. What does the term 'standard cell potential' mean?
    [ ] The maximum possible voltage of any cell
    [ ] The potential measured at equilibrium
    [*] The cell potential measured when all species are at standard conditions (1 M, 1 bar, 298 K)
    [ ] The average cell potential of all known cells
    Explanation: The standard cell potential (E-standard-cell) is the voltage of an electrochemical cell when all reactants and products are in their standard states: solutes at 1 M, gases at 1 bar, and solids in their pure form, typically at 298 K. It is calculated from standard reduction potentials.

  Q21. A student builds a galvanic cell with Zn/Zn2+ and Cu/Cu2+ half-cells. Given E-standard(Cu2+/Cu) = +0.34 V and E-standard(Zn2+/Zn) = -0.76 V, what is E-standard-cell?

  Q23. A Zn/Cu galvanic cell has E-standard = +1.10 V. If [Zn2+] is increased to 2.0 M while [Cu2+] remains at 1.0 M, how does the cell potential change? (Use the Nernst equation.)

    [ ] Becomes negative

Four cells in series: 4 x 3.8 = 15.2 V. Three parallel strings: voltage stays 15.2 V, but current capacity triples (each parallel string adds its capacity). Series connections add voltage; parallel connections add current capacity. This is how battery packs for electric vehicles and laptops are designed.

    [*] Both oxygen and water
    [ ] Only oxygen
    [ ] Only water
    [ ] Only acidic conditions
    Explanation: Iron corrosion (rusting) requires both oxygen and water (moisture). Iron is oxidized at anodic sites: Fe -> Fe2+ + 2e-, while oxygen is reduced at cathodic sites: O2 + 2H2O + 4e- -> 4OH-. The Fe2+ and OH- combine to eventually form Fe2O3 (rust).

  Q6. The voltage of a single cell in a lead-acid battery is approximately:
    [*] 2.0 V
    [ ] 1.5 V
    [ ] 3.7 V
    [ ] 12.0 V
    Explanation: Each cell in a lead-acid battery produces approximately 2.0 V. A standard 12 V car battery consists of six cells connected in series (6 x 2.0 = 12 V). The overall reaction is Pb + PbO2 + 2H2SO4 -> 2PbSO4 + 2H2O.

  Q7. Galvanizing is a corrosion prevention method that involves:
    [ ] Alloying iron with gold
    [ ] Coating iron with a layer of copper
    [ ] Painting iron with acid-resistant paint
    [*] Coating iron with a layer of zinc
    Explanation: Galvanizing involves coating iron or steel with zinc. Zinc is more reactive (more easily oxidized) than iron, so even if the coating is scratched, the zinc corrodes preferentially (acts as a sacrificial anode), protecting the underlying iron.

  Q8. A fuel cell differs from a battery because it:
    [ ] Does not involve redox reactions
    [*] Continuously converts fuel supplied from outside into electricity
    [ ] Can only operate at high temperatures
    [ ] Stores reactants internally
    Explanation: A fuel cell continuously converts chemical energy of an externally supplied fuel (such as hydrogen) and an oxidant (such as oxygen) into electricity. Unlike batteries, fuel cells do not store energy internally; they keep producing electricity as long as fuel is supplied.

  Q9. In a lithium-ion battery, lithium ions move from the anode to the cathode during:
    [ ] Charging
    [ ] Neither; Li+ ions do not move
    [ ] Both charging and discharging
    [*] Discharging
    Explanation: During discharging, lithium ions move from the anode (typically graphite) through the electrolyte to the cathode (typically a lithium metal oxide). During charging, the reverse occurs: Li+ ions move from the cathode back to the anode.

  Q10. Cathodic protection prevents corrosion by:
    [ ] Removing all oxygen from the environment
    [ ] Coating the metal with an insulating layer
    [ ] Heating the metal to drive off moisture
    [*] Attaching a more reactive metal that corrodes instead (sacrificial anode)
    Explanation: Cathodic protection uses a sacrificial anode (a more reactive metal like zinc or magnesium) that is preferentially oxidized instead of the protected metal. The protected metal becomes the cathode, where only reduction occurs, preventing its corrosion.

  Q11. What does the term 'primary cell' mean?
    [*] An electrochemical cell whose reaction is irreversible, making it non-rechargeable
    [ ] A cell that provides primary power to a grid
    [ ] The first cell in a series of cells
    [ ] A cell with the highest voltage
    Explanation: A primary cell is a non-rechargeable electrochemical cell. The chemical reactions that generate electricity are irreversible, meaning the original reactants cannot be regenerated by applying an external current. Once the reactants are depleted, the cell must be discarded.

  Q12. What does the term 'secondary cell' mean?
    [ ] A backup cell in case the primary fails
    [ ] A cell with lower voltage than a primary cell
    [*] A rechargeable electrochemical cell whose reaction can be reversed by applying an external current
    [ ] A cell that uses a secondary electrolyte
    Explanation: A secondary cell (rechargeable battery) uses reversible chemical reactions. Applying external electrical energy forces the reactions to run in reverse, regenerating the original reactants. This allows the cell to be charged and discharged many times.

  Q13. What does the term 'corrosion' mean?
    [ ] The melting of metal at high temperatures
    [ ] The dissolution of metal in acid
    [*] The gradual destruction of a metal by electrochemical oxidation reactions with substances in the environment
    [ ] The physical wearing away of metal by friction
    Explanation: Corrosion is the degradation of a metal through electrochemical oxidation reactions with environmental agents, primarily oxygen and water. It is an electrochemical process with distinct anodic (oxidation) and cathodic (reduction) sites on the metal surface.

  Q14. What does the term 'sacrificial anode' mean?
    [ ] The anode in a primary cell
    [ ] An anode that has been coated with a protective layer
    [ ] An anode that dissolves during electrolysis
    [*] An anode that is designed to be corroded (oxidized) preferentially to protect another metal
    Explanation: A sacrificial anode is a more reactive (more easily oxidized) metal intentionally attached to a less reactive metal to protect it from corrosion. The sacrificial anode corrodes instead, being consumed over time. Examples include zinc blocks on ship hulls and magnesium rods in water heaters.

  Q15. What does the term 'fuel cell' mean?
    [ ] A generator that converts heat directly to electricity
    [ ] A cell that stores fuel for later combustion
    [*] An electrochemical cell that continuously generates electricity from externally supplied fuel and oxidant
    [ ] A battery powered by gasoline
    Explanation: A fuel cell is an electrochemical device that converts chemical energy directly into electrical energy through redox reactions, using externally supplied fuel and oxidant. The most common type is the hydrogen fuel cell: 2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O, producing electricity, water, and heat.

  Q16. What does 'galvanizing' mean?
    [*] Coating iron or steel with zinc to protect against corrosion
    [ ] Heating metal to improve its conductivity
    [ ] Shocking metal with electricity to harden it
    [ ] Combining two metals to form an alloy
    Explanation: Galvanizing is the process of coating iron or steel with zinc, usually by hot-dipping or electroplating. The zinc layer protects the iron both as a physical barrier and through cathodic protection (zinc is oxidized preferentially since it is more reactive than iron).

  Q17. What does 'passivation' mean in corrosion chemistry?
    [ ] The removal of all oxide from a metal surface
    [ ] The process of making a metal more reactive
    [ ] The complete dissolution of a metal in acid
    [*] The formation of a thin, protective oxide layer on a metal surface that prevents further corrosion
    Explanation: Passivation occurs when a metal forms a thin, stable oxide layer on its surface that acts as a barrier, preventing further oxidation. Aluminum, chromium, and stainless steel are examples of metals that passivate effectively, which is why they resist corrosion despite being reactive.

  Q18. What does 'energy density' mean when comparing batteries?
    [ ] The number of cells in the battery
    [ ] How quickly the battery can deliver energy
    [ ] The voltage output of the battery
    [*] The amount of energy stored per unit mass or volume of the battery
    Explanation: Energy density is the amount of energy stored per unit mass (Wh/kg, gravimetric) or per unit volume (Wh/L, volumetric). Lithium-ion batteries have high energy density (~250 Wh/kg), which is why they are preferred for portable electronics and electric vehicles.

  Q19. What does the term 'dry cell' mean?
    [ ] A battery that contains no electrolyte
    [ ] A fuel cell that uses dry hydrogen gas
    [*] A battery that uses a paste electrolyte rather than a liquid, making it portable and leak-resistant
    [ ] A battery that must be kept in a dry environment
    Explanation: A dry cell uses a paste or gel electrolyte instead of a liquid, making it portable and less prone to leaking. The common zinc-carbon and alkaline batteries are dry cells. The term distinguishes them from wet cells like lead-acid batteries that use liquid electrolyte.

  Q20. What does the term 'cell in series' mean for batteries?
    [ ] Cells that can only be used one after another
    [*] Multiple cells connected positive-to-negative to increase total voltage
    [ ] Multiple cells connected side-by-side to increase current
    [ ] Cells that are manufactured in sequential order
    Explanation: When cells are connected in series (positive terminal of one to negative terminal of the next), their voltages add together while the current capacity remains the same as a single cell. A 12 V car battery has six 2 V cells in series.

  Q21. A homeowner notices that the water heater's magnesium anode rod has corroded significantly after 5 years, but the steel tank is intact. This is an example of:
    [ ] Passivation of the magnesium, even when conditions shift significantly
    [*] Galvanic corrosion where magnesium protects steel by being oxidized preferentially
    [ ] Electrolysis of the water, based on conventional assumptions about
    [ ] A manufacturing defect without accounting for additional influences
    Explanation: This is cathodic protection using a sacrificial anode. Magnesium is more reactive than iron/steel (E-standard for Mg2+/Mg = -2.37 V vs Fe2+/Fe = -0.44 V), so it is preferentially oxidized, protecting the steel tank. The magnesium rod needs periodic replacement as it corrodes.

  Q22. A student is comparing a nickel-cadmium (NiCd) battery to a lithium-ion (Li-ion) battery for a portable device. The Li-ion battery has a nominal voltage of 3.7 V per cell and energy density of 250 Wh/kg. The NiCd has 1.2 V per cell and 45 Wh/kg. Why are Li-ion batteries preferred for smartphones?
    [ ] NiCd batteries are cheaper, based on conventional assumptions about battery
    [*] Li-ion batteries have higher energy density and voltage per cell, allowing lighter, more compact designs
    [ ] NiCd batteries cannot be recharged, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] Li-ion batteries last forever under most circumstances involving battery
    Explanation: Li-ion batteries have about 5 times the energy density of NiCd (250 vs 45 Wh/kg) and 3 times the voltage per cell (3.7 vs 1.2 V). This means a Li-ion battery can store more energy in less space and weight, which is critical for portable electronic devices.

  Q23. A marine engineer needs to protect an iron ship hull from corrosion in seawater. She attaches zinc blocks to the hull. Using standard reduction potentials (Zn2+/Zn = -0.76 V, Fe2+/Fe = -0.44 V), explain why this works.
    [ ] Zinc absorbs oxygen from the water in most scenarios involving zinc factors
    [*] Zinc has a more negative reduction potential, so it is oxidized preferentially, making the iron the cathode where only reduction occurs
    [ ] Zinc blocks the water from reaching the iron under most circumstances involving zinc
    [ ] Zinc neutralizes the salt in seawater when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    Explanation: Zinc has a more negative standard reduction potential (-0.76 V) than iron (-0.44 V), meaning zinc is more easily oxidized. When both are in contact in seawater (electrolyte), zinc becomes the anode and corrodes, while iron becomes the cathode where reduction occurs (O2 + 2H2O + 4e- -> 4OH-). The iron is protected as long as zinc remains.

  Q24. A chemistry teacher demonstrates a lemon battery using a copper penny and a zinc nail inserted into a lemon. The cell produces about 0.9 V. If three lemon cells are connected in series, what is the total voltage?
    [*] 2.7 V
    [ ] 0.3 V
    [ ] 0.9 V
    [ ] 8.1 V
    Explanation: When cells are connected in series, their voltages add: 0.9 + 0.9 + 0.9 = 2.7 V. The citric acid in the lemon acts as the electrolyte. Zinc is oxidized at the anode (more negative E-standard) and H+ is reduced at the copper cathode.

  Q25. A car battery is rated at 60 Ah (ampere-hours). If the car's starter motor draws 200 A, how long can the battery deliver this current before being completely discharged?
    [ ] 3.3 hours
    [ ] 12 hours
    [ ] 1.0 hour
    [*] 0.3 hours (18 minutes)
    Explanation: Time = capacity / current = 60 Ah / 200 A = 0.3 hours = 18 minutes. In practice, the actual time would be less because high discharge rates reduce the effective capacity. The Ah rating tells you how many amperes the battery can deliver for one hour.

  Q26. A researcher studying hydrogen fuel cells notes that the overall reaction is 2H2(g) + O2(g) -> 2H2O(l) with E-cell = +1.23 V. What makes fuel cells more efficient than combustion engines?
    [*] Fuel cells convert chemical energy directly to electricity without the thermodynamic limitations of heat engines
    [ ] Fuel cells can use any fuel, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Fuel cells use cheaper fuel, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] Fuel cells produce more heat, based on conventional assumptions about cells
    Explanation: Fuel cells convert chemical energy directly to electrical energy without intermediate heat conversion. Combustion engines are limited by the Carnot efficiency (dependent on temperature differences), typically achieving 25-40% efficiency. Fuel cells can theoretically achieve 80-90% efficiency, though practical values are 40-60%.

  Q27. A bridge inspector notices that iron bolts connecting copper plates have corroded badly, while the copper remains pristine. Using electrochemistry, explain this observation.
    [ ] The copper prevented oxygen from reaching the iron
    [*] Iron is more easily oxidized than copper (lower reduction potential), so it acts as the anode and corrodes preferentially in the galvanic couple
    [ ] Iron reacted with the copper to form an alloy
    [ ] Copper is a better conductor, so it does not corrode
    Explanation: When iron (E = -0.44 V) and copper (E = +0.34 V) are in contact in the presence of moisture, they form a galvanic couple. Iron, with its more negative reduction potential, becomes the anode and corrodes, while copper becomes the cathode and is protected. This is galvanic corrosion and is why dissimilar metals in contact should be avoided.

  Q28. A manufacturing plant uses a lead-acid battery to power a forklift. During charging, the reaction PbSO4 -> Pb and PbSO4 -> PbO2 is driven in reverse. What happens to the H2SO4 concentration during charging?
    [*] It increases
    [ ] It remains the same
    [ ] It fluctuates randomly
    [ ] It decreases
    Explanation: During discharging, H2SO4 is consumed (Pb + PbO2 + 2H2SO4 -> 2PbSO4 + 2H2O). During charging, this reaction is reversed, regenerating H2SO4 from PbSO4 and H2O. Therefore, the acid concentration increases during charging. Mechanics check battery charge by measuring the specific gravity (density) of the acid.

  Q29. An environmental engineer is evaluating corrosion rates of steel pipes in different soils. In which environment would corrosion be fastest?
    [*] Wet, salty clay soil with high conductivity
    [ ] Pure dry nitrogen atmosphere
    [ ] Dry sandy soil with low conductivity
    [ ] Frozen soil with no moisture
    Explanation: Corrosion requires moisture (electrolyte), oxygen, and ionic conductivity. Wet, salty clay soil provides all three: water as the medium, dissolved salts (ions) for electrolyte conductivity, and access to oxygen. Higher salt concentration increases conductivity, accelerating the electrochemical corrosion process.

  Q30. A battery engineer designs a new lithium-ion cell with E-cell = 3.8 V. She connects 4 cells in series and 3 such series strings in parallel. What is the total voltage and how does the current capacity compare to a single cell?
    [*] 15.2 V, triple the current capacity of one cell
    [ ] 3.8 V, 12 times the current capacity
    [ ] 15.2 V, same current capacity as one cell
    [ ] 45.6 V, same current capacity as one cell
    Explanation: Four cells in series: 4 x 3.8 = 15.2 V. Three parallel strings: voltage stays 15.2 V, but current capacity triples (each parallel string adds its capacity). Series connections add voltage; parallel connections add current capacity. This is how battery packs for electric vehicles and laptops are designed.

--- Lesson 9.5: Batteries and Corrosion (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$
