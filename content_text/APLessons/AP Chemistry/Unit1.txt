--- Lesson 1.1: Atomic Structure and Subatomic Particles (Summary) ---

  Understanding Atomic Structure and Subatomic Particles
Atoms are the fundamental units of matter, composed of a nucleus (protons and neutrons) surrounded by electron clouds. Protons are positively charged and found only in the nucleus; the number of protons defines an element (atomic number). Neutrons are neutral and found in the nucleus. Electrons are negatively charged and occupy electron clouds around the nucleus. The nucleus contains most of the atom's mass; electrons occupy most of the volume.

  Key Concepts and Vocabulary
Key terminology: atomic number (number of protons, defines element identity); mass number (protons + neutrons); isotopes (atoms of same element with different neutron numbers, creating different mass numbers); ions (atoms with unequal proton and electron numbers, giving them net charge); cations (positive ions, fewer electrons than protons); anions (negative ions, more electrons than protons). Isotopes of an element have identical chemical properties (same number of electrons) but different physical properties.

  Real-World Applications and Examples
Real-world applications: the periodic table lists elements by atomic number. Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 are isotopes—C-14 is radioactive and used in carbon dating. Isotopes are used in medicine as radioisotopes for imaging and treatment. Uranium has isotopes of different masses; U-235 is fissile (undergoes fission) while U-238 is not, making isotope separation crucial for nuclear energy. Ion charges affect how atoms bond and interact in compounds.

  Deeper Understanding
Atomic behavior depends on electron number (chemical properties), not neutron number. This is why isotopes have identical chemistry. Atoms are neutral when electron number equals proton number. Understanding atomic structure reveals why the periodic table is organized by atomic number—elements with same number of electrons in outer shells have similar chemical properties. Subatomic particle composition determines both identity (proton number) and mass.

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

     A: 31

--- Lesson 1.5: Molecular Geometry (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the primary focus of Molecular Geometry?
     A: Understanding Molecular Geometry concepts

  10. Q: How would you apply Molecular Geometry to solve problems?
     A: By using the principles of Molecular Geometry
