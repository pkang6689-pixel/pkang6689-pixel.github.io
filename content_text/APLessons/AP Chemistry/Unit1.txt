--- Lesson 1.1: Atomic Structure and Subatomic Particles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Atomic Structure and Subatomic Particles
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the approximate mass of a proton in atomic mass units (amu)?
A proton has a mass of approximately 1 amu (1.673 x 10^-24 g). Neutrons also have a mass of about 1 amu, while electrons are roughly 1/1836 amu.
What is the mass number (A) of an atom with 17 protons and 18 neutrons?
Mass number (A) is the sum of protons and neutrons in the nucleus. A = 17 + 18 = 35. This atom is chlorine-35.
What is the atomic number (Z)?
The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus. It uniquely identifies each element and determines its position on the periodic table.
What is the mass number (A) of an atom?
The mass number (A) equals the sum of protons (Z) and neutrons (N) in the nucleus, expressed as A = Z + N.
What is an ion?
An ion is an atom that has gained or lost electrons. Cations are positively charged (lost electrons), and anions are negatively charged (gained electrons).
What is a cation?
A cation is a positively charged ion formed when an atom loses one or more electrons. Metals commonly form cations (e.g., Na+ loses one electron).
What is an anion?
An anion is a negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains one or more electrons. Nonmetals commonly form anions (e.g., Cl- gains one electron).
What is the 'electron cloud'?
The electron cloud is the region of space around the nucleus where there is a high probability of finding electrons. It represents the volume that electrons occupy in their orbitals.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'average atomic mass' mean?
Average atomic mass is the weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes, where each isotope's mass is multiplied by its fractional abundance.
Example: In a semiconductor fabrication plant, silicon wafers must be extremely pure. Engineers use mass spectrometry to verify that the silicon contains the correct isotopic composition: Si-28 (92.2%), Si-29 (4.7%), and Si-30 (3.1%). If a sample shows an unusually high proportion of Si-30, what would happen to the measured average atomic mass?
Since average atomic mass is a weighted average, increasing the proportion of the heavier isotope (Si-30) shifts the average upward. More Si-30 means more mass contributing to the average.

--- Lesson 1.2: Electron Configuration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Electron Configuration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the maximum number of electrons that can occupy the second energy level (n = 2)?
The maximum number of electrons in an energy level is given by 2n². For n = 2: 2(2²) = 8 electrons, distributed among the 2s and 2p subshells.
What is the electron configuration of a neutral sodium atom (Z = 11)?
Sodium has 11 electrons. Following the aufbau principle, they fill in order: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹, placing the last electron in the 3s orbital.
What is the electron configuration of oxygen (Z = 8)?
Oxygen has 8 electrons. They fill as: 1s² (2), 2s² (4), 2p⁴ (8). The 2p subshell has 4 of its maximum 6 electrons.
What is the noble gas shorthand electron configuration for iron (Z = 26)?
Iron has 26 electrons. The nearest preceding noble gas is argon (18 electrons). The remaining 8 electrons fill as 3d⁶ 4s², giving [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s².
What is the aufbau principle?
The aufbau principle (German for 'building up') states that electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, starting from 1s and following the diagonal rule.
What is Hund's rule?
Hund's rule states that for degenerate orbitals (same energy), electrons occupy each orbital singly with parallel spins before any orbital receives a second electron.
What is a 'ground state' electron configuration?
The ground state is the lowest energy electron configuration for an atom, where all electrons occupy the lowest available energy orbitals following the aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion, and Hund's rule.
What is an 'excited state' in electron configuration?
An excited state occurs when an electron absorbs energy and jumps to a higher energy orbital than it would occupy in the ground state. This is a temporary, unstable arrangement.
What is meant by 'degenerate orbitals'?
Degenerate orbitals are orbitals that have the same energy. For example, the three 2p orbitals (2px, 2py, 2pz) are degenerate because they all have the same energy.
A chemistry teacher demonstrates flame tests for her class. She holds different metal salts in a Bunsen burner flame and asks students to explain why copper produces a green flame while lithium produces a red flame. What is the best explanation?
Each element has unique electron energy levels. When excited electrons return to lower energy states, they emit photons with element-specific wavelengths, producing characteristic flame colors.
A student is asked to predict the electron configuration of a Cr³⁺ ion. Neutral chromium has the anomalous configuration [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹. When forming Cr³⁺, which electrons are removed first, and what is the resulting configuration?
When transition metals form cations, 4s electrons are removed before 3d electrons. For Cr³⁺, the single 4s electron is lost first, then two 3d electrons, giving [Ar] 3d³.
A student in a lab is determining the electron configuration of a mystery element. She knows it forms a 2+ ion with the configuration [Ar] 3d⁸. She also knows that when transition metals ionize, 4s electrons are lost before 3d electrons. What is the mystery element?
The 2+ ion has the configuration [Ar] 3d⁸ (18 + 8 = 26 electrons). The neutral atom had 2 more electrons (lost from 4s), giving 28 total electrons: nickel (Z = 28) with configuration [Ar] 3d⁸ 4s².

--- Lesson 1.3: Periodic Table Trends (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Periodic Table Trends
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the trend for electron affinity across a period from left to right?
Across a period, electron affinity generally becomes more negative (more energy released when gaining an electron) because the increasing nuclear charge attracts additional electrons more strongly.
What is ionization energy?
Ionization energy (IE) is the energy required to remove the most loosely held electron from a gaseous atom in its ground state. Higher IE means it is harder to remove an electron.
What is 'electron affinity'?
Electron affinity is the energy change when a neutral gaseous atom gains an electron. A large negative electron affinity means the atom releases energy and strongly attracts additional electrons.
What is 'atomic radius'?
Atomic radius is generally defined as half the distance between the nuclei of two identical bonded atoms. It reflects the size of the electron cloud surrounding the nucleus.
What is 'ionic radius'?
Ionic radius is the effective radius of an ion. Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (fewer electrons, same nuclear charge), while anions are larger (more electrons, same nuclear charge).
What is 'successive ionization energy'?
Successive ionization energies are the energies needed to remove electrons one at a time. Each successive IE is higher because the remaining electrons experience greater effective nuclear charge.
A chemist compares the atomic radii of O²⁻, F⁻, Na⁺, and Mg²⁺. All four species are isoelectronic with 10 electrons. She needs to rank them from largest to smallest. What is the correct order?
For isoelectronic species, the one with fewer protons has the largest radius. O²⁻ (8 protons) > F⁻ (9) > Na⁺ (11) > Mg²⁺ (12). More protons pull the same number of electrons closer.
A student notices that boron (Z = 5) has a lower first ionization energy than beryllium (Z = 4), which seems to violate the general left-to-right trend. What is the best explanation for this anomaly?
Beryllium has a filled 2s² subshell (extra stability), while boron's highest electron is in a 2p orbital, which is higher in energy and more easily removed. This causes the IE anomaly.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Fluorine's small atomic radius and high effective nuclear charge (7 protons pulling on 2 shells of electrons) give it the highest electronegativity and a strong tendency to gain an electron.

--- Lesson 1.4: Bonding (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bonding
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the bond order of the nitrogen-nitrogen bond in N₂?
N₂ contains a triple bond (one sigma and two pi bonds). Each nitrogen needs 3 more electrons to complete its octet, so they share 3 pairs of electrons, giving a bond order of 3.
What is an ionic bond?
An ionic bond results from the complete transfer of electrons from a metal to a nonmetal, creating oppositely charged ions (cation and anion) that are held together by electrostatic attraction.
What is a 'Lewis structure'?
A Lewis structure shows valence electrons as dots and bonding pairs as lines between atoms. It helps predict molecular geometry, bond order, and the distribution of electrons in a molecule.
What is 'resonance' in the context of Lewis structures?
Resonance occurs when multiple valid Lewis structures exist for a molecule, differing only in the placement of electrons (not atoms). The true structure is a weighted average (hybrid) of all resonance forms.
What is a 'sigma bond'?
A sigma bond is formed by head-on overlap of orbitals along the bond axis. It is the first bond formed between two atoms and is present in all single, double, and triple bonds.
What is a 'pi bond'?
A pi bond results from the sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals. Pi bonds are found in double bonds (1 sigma + 1 pi) and triple bonds (1 sigma + 2 pi), and they restrict rotation.
What is a 'lattice energy'?
Lattice energy is the energy released when gaseous cations and anions combine to form one mole of an ionic solid. Higher lattice energy indicates a more stable ionic compound.
A chemist synthesizes ozone (O₃) and needs to describe its bonding. She draws two resonance structures where the double bond alternates between the two terminal oxygens. If both resonance structures are equally valid, what is the actual O-O bond order in ozone?
Ozone has two equivalent resonance structures, each with one single and one double bond. The actual structure is a resonance hybrid with a bond order of (2+1)/2 = 1.5 for each O-O bond.

    [ ] N₂
    Explanation: HCl has a polar covalent bond because chlorine is more electronegative than hydrogen, creating an unequal sharing of electrons. Cl₂ and N₂ are nonpolar covalent, and Na₂O is ionic.

  Q6. In a Lewis structure, how many total valence electrons does CO₂ have?
    [*] 16
    [ ] 18
    [ ] 12
    [ ] 22
    Explanation: Carbon has 4 valence electrons and each oxygen has 6. Total = 4 + 6 + 6 = 16 valence electrons, which are distributed as two double bonds and four lone pairs in the Lewis structure.

  Q7. Which bond is the shortest and strongest: C-C, C=C, or C≡C?
    [ ] C-C (single bond)
    [ ] C=C (double bond)
    [ ] They are all the same length and strength
    [*] C≡C (triple bond)
    Explanation: As bond order increases, the bond becomes shorter and stronger. A triple bond has more electron density between the nuclei than a double or single bond, pulling the atoms closer together.

  Q8. What does the octet rule state?
    [ ] Atoms always form 8 bonds
    [ ] Every atom must have exactly 8 electrons total
    [*] Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons
    [ ] Only noble gases can have 8 electrons
    Explanation: The octet rule states that atoms tend to form bonds to achieve 8 electrons in their valence shell, mimicking the electron configuration of the nearest noble gas. Hydrogen is an exception, needing only 2.

  Q9. Which molecule is an exception to the octet rule by having an incomplete octet?
    [*] BF₃
    [ ] H₂O
    [ ] NH₃
    [ ] CO₂
    Explanation: In BF₃, boron has only 6 electrons in its valence shell (3 bonding pairs, no lone pairs). Boron is electron-deficient and is a common example of an incomplete octet.

  Q10. What type of bond is formed when one atom provides both electrons in a shared pair?
    [ ] Metallic bond
    [ ] Ionic bond
    [*] Coordinate covalent (dative) bond
    [ ] Nonpolar covalent bond
    Explanation: A coordinate covalent (dative) bond forms when one atom donates both electrons in a bonding pair. For example, in NH₄⁺, the nitrogen donates a lone pair to bond with the additional H⁺.

  Q11. What is an ionic bond?
    [ ] A bond formed by sharing electrons equally between atoms
    [ ] A weak attraction between polar molecules
    [ ] A bond formed by a sea of delocalized electrons
    [*] A bond formed by the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions after electron transfer
    Explanation: An ionic bond results from the complete transfer of electrons from a metal to a nonmetal, creating oppositely charged ions (cation and anion) that are held together by electrostatic attraction.

  Q12. What does 'bond polarity' mean?
    [ ] The equal distribution of electrons in a bond between identical atoms
    [ ] The number of bonds an atom can form
    [ ] The transfer of electrons from one atom to another
    [*] The unequal sharing of electrons in a covalent bond due to a difference in electronegativity between the bonded atoms
    Explanation: Bond polarity arises when two atoms in a covalent bond have different electronegativities. The more electronegative atom pulls electron density toward itself, creating partial charges (delta+ and delta-).

  Q13. What is a 'Lewis structure'?
    [ ] A diagram showing the nucleus and all electron shells
    [ ] A representation of only metallic bonds
    [*] A diagram that uses dots and lines to represent valence electrons and bonds in a molecule
    [ ] A diagram showing only the arrangement of atoms in 3D space
    Explanation: A Lewis structure shows valence electrons as dots and bonding pairs as lines between atoms. It helps predict molecular geometry, bond order, and the distribution of electrons in a molecule.

  Q14. What does 'formal charge' mean?
    [*] The charge assigned to an atom in a Lewis structure assuming electrons are shared equally, calculated as valence electrons minus (lone pair electrons + half of bonding electrons)
    [ ] The electronegativity of the atom, as the dynamics involved tend to push conditions in this direction over time
    [ ] The total number of bonds an atom forms due to the well-established relationship between electrons trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] The actual charge on an ion in an ionic compound, although this interpretation oversimplifies the actual electrons mechanisms at work
    Explanation: Formal charge = valence electrons - (lone pair electrons + 1/2 bonding electrons). It helps determine the most stable Lewis structure; the best structure minimizes formal charges on all atoms.

  Q15. What is 'resonance' in the context of Lewis structures?
    [ ] The oscillation of bond length over time within the broader context of resonance analysis
    [ ] The vibration of atoms in a molecule, which is consistent with how resonance mechanisms are understood to operate in theory
    [ ] The alternation between ionic and covalent bonding, even when conditions shift significantly
    [*] The phenomenon where two or more valid Lewis structures can be drawn for a molecule, with the actual structure being a hybrid of all resonance forms
    Explanation: Resonance occurs when multiple valid Lewis structures exist for a molecule, differing only in the placement of electrons (not atoms). The true structure is a weighted average (hybrid) of all resonance forms.

  Q16. What is a 'sigma bond'?
    [ ] A bond formed by the sideways overlap of p orbitals
    [*] A bond formed by the head-on (end-to-end) overlap of orbitals along the internuclear axis
    [ ] A bond that exists only in double and triple bonds
    [ ] A bond found only in metallic substances
    Explanation: A sigma bond is formed by head-on overlap of orbitals along the bond axis. It is the first bond formed between two atoms and is present in all single, double, and triple bonds.

  Q17. What is a 'pi bond'?
    [ ] The only type of bond in a single bond
    [ ] A bond formed by end-to-end overlap of s orbitals
    [ ] A bond formed between a metal and nonmetal
    [*] A bond formed by the sideways (lateral) overlap of parallel p orbitals above and below the internuclear axis
    Explanation: A pi bond results from the sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals. Pi bonds are found in double bonds (1 sigma + 1 pi) and triple bonds (1 sigma + 2 pi), and they restrict rotation.

  Q18. What does 'bond energy' refer to?
    [ ] The electronegativity difference between bonded atoms
    [ ] The energy released when a bond is formed
    [ ] The kinetic energy of bonded atoms
    [*] The energy required to break one mole of a particular bond in the gas phase
    Explanation: Bond energy (or bond dissociation energy) is the amount of energy required to break one mole of a specific bond in gaseous molecules. Higher bond energy indicates a stronger bond.

  Q19. What is a 'lattice energy'?
    [ ] The energy released when a covalent compound dissolves in water
    [ ] The energy required to melt a metallic solid
    [ ] The energy stored in the bonds of a crystal
    [*] The energy released when gaseous ions come together to form one mole of a solid ionic compound
    Explanation: Lattice energy is the energy released when gaseous cations and anions combine to form one mole of an ionic solid. Higher lattice energy indicates a more stable ionic compound.

  Q20. What does 'bond length' mean?
    [ ] The radius of the bonding orbital
    [*] The distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms at their equilibrium position
    [ ] The distance between two molecules in a solid
    [ ] The total length of all bonds in a molecule
    Explanation: Bond length is the average distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms at equilibrium. It depends on atomic radii and bond order; higher bond order leads to shorter bond length.

  Q21. A jeweler is comparing diamond and graphite, both made entirely of carbon. Diamond is the hardest natural substance while graphite is soft enough to use as pencil lead. Both contain covalent bonds. What difference in bonding structure accounts for these dramatically different properties?
    [*] Diamond has a 3D network of sigma bonds (sp³) with each carbon bonded to 4 others, while graphite has layered sheets (sp²) with weak forces between layers
    [ ] Graphite has stronger bonds than diamond but in fewer directions
    [ ] Diamond has ionic bonds while graphite has covalent bonds
    [ ] Diamond and graphite have the same bonding but different isotopes of carbon
    Explanation: In diamond, each carbon is sp³ hybridized and bonded to 4 neighbors in a rigid 3D tetrahedral network. In graphite, sp² carbons form flat sheets with weak London dispersion forces between layers, allowing them to slide.

  Q22. A food scientist is studying why table salt (NaCl) dissolves in water but not in oil. She considers the bonding in NaCl and the properties of both solvents. What explains NaCl's solubility in water but not in oil?
    [ ] NaCl has covalent bonds that are broken by water molecules

    [ ] NaCl forms metallic bonds with water but not oil
    [ ] Oil is too dense for NaCl to dissolve in
    Explanation: NaCl is an ionic compound. Polar water molecules can surround and stabilize individual Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions through ion-dipole forces, overcoming the lattice energy. Nonpolar oil cannot provide these stabilizing interactions.

  Q23. An engineer designing a new ceramic material needs to maximize strength and melting point. She compares MgO and NaCl, both ionic compounds. MgO has a melting point of 2852°C while NaCl melts at 801°C. What bonding difference explains MgO's much higher melting point?
    [ ] MgO has covalent bonds while NaCl has ionic bonds within the broader context of higher analysis

    [ ] MgO has more atoms per formula unit than NaCl when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    Explanation: MgO has Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ ions (higher charges) and smaller ionic radii than Na⁺ and Cl⁻. By Coulomb's law, the lattice energy increases with higher charge and smaller distance, making MgO much harder to melt.

  Q24. A chemist synthesizes ozone (O₃) and needs to describe its bonding. She draws two resonance structures where the double bond alternates between the two terminal oxygens. If both resonance structures are equally valid, what is the actual O-O bond order in ozone?
    [ ] 1 (single bond)
    [ ] 3 (triple bond)
    [ ] 2 (double bond)
    [*] 1.5 (between single and double)
    Explanation: Ozone has two equivalent resonance structures, each with one single and one double bond. The actual structure is a resonance hybrid with a bond order of (2+1)/2 = 1.5 for each O-O bond.

  Q25. A biomedical researcher is studying the oxygen-carrying protein hemoglobin. The iron(II) ion at its center forms a coordinate covalent bond with O₂. In this bond, oxygen donates a lone pair to the iron. Why is this classified as a coordinate covalent bond rather than a regular covalent bond?

--- Lesson 1.5: Molecular Geometry (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Molecular Geometry
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the molecular geometry of methane (CH₄)?
CH₄ has 4 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs around carbon, giving it a tetrahedral electron geometry and tetrahedral molecular geometry with bond angles of approximately 109.5°.
What is the bond angle in a trigonal planar molecule?
In a trigonal planar arrangement, three groups of electrons spread out evenly in a plane, creating bond angles of 120° (e.g., BF₃).
What is the molecular shape of water (H₂O)?
Water has 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs around oxygen. The electron geometry is tetrahedral, but the molecular geometry (considering only atoms) is bent with a bond angle of about 104.5°.
What is the molecular geometry of ammonia (NH₃)?
NH₃ has 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair around nitrogen. The electron geometry is tetrahedral, but the molecular geometry is trigonal pyramidal with bond angles of about 107°.
What is the shape of XeF₂?
XeF₂ has 2 bonding pairs and 3 lone pairs (5 electron domains, trigonal bipyramidal electron geometry). The two fluorines occupy axial positions, giving a linear molecular geometry (180°).
What is the approximate H-N-H bond angle in ammonia (NH₃)?
Ammonia has tetrahedral electron geometry (109.5° ideal), but the lone pair compresses the bond angle to approximately 107° because lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs.
What is VSEPR theory?
VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) theory predicts molecular shapes by arranging electron domains around a central atom to minimize electrostatic repulsion between them.
What is 'hybridization'?
Hybridization is the mixing of atomic orbitals (s, p, d) on the same atom to produce new hybrid orbitals (sp, sp², sp³, etc.) that have the same energy and appropriate geometry for bonding.
What is 'molecular polarity'?
Molecular polarity depends on both bond polarity and molecular geometry. A molecule is polar if its bond dipoles do not cancel out, resulting in a net dipole moment and uneven charge distribution.
What is 'electron geometry' versus 'molecular geometry'?
Electron geometry describes the arrangement of all electron domains (bonding + lone pairs) around the central atom. Molecular geometry describes only the arrangement of the atoms, ignoring lone pairs.
What is 'bond angle compression'?
Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs because they are closer to the nucleus. This extra repulsion compresses the bond angles below ideal values (e.g., 107° in NH₃ instead of 109.5°).

    [ ] Fluorine atoms repel each other into a square arrangement, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [ ] XeF₄ has sp³ hybridization like CH₄, since standard models of pairs behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    Explanation: XeF₄ has 4 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs (6 electron domains = octahedral electron geometry). The 2 lone pairs occupy positions 180° apart (trans) to minimize repulsion, leaving the 4 F atoms in a square planar arrangement.

  Q26. A climate scientist is analyzing the greenhouse gas sulfur hexafluoride (SF₆), which has a global warming potential 23,500 times that of CO₂. Despite having very polar S-F bonds, SF₆ is a nonpolar molecule. How does its geometry explain this paradox?

  Q28. A food chemist is studying the flavor molecule vanillin. She determines that the carbon bonded to the oxygen in the aldehyde group (C=O) has a trigonal planar geometry. What hybridization must this carbon have, and why?

  Q29. A toxicologist compares the structures of cis-platin (a cancer drug) and trans-platin (inactive form). Both have the formula PtCl₂(NH₃)₂ with square planar geometry around platinum.   Q30. A polymer chemist is working with polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE, Teflon). Each carbon in the polymer chain is bonded to two fluorines and two other carbons. --- Lesson 1.5: Molecular Geometry (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the primary focus of Molecular Geometry?
     A: Understanding Molecular Geometry concepts

  10. Q: How would you apply Molecular Geometry to solve problems?
     A: By using the principles of Molecular Geometry
