--- Lesson 4.1: Types of Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Types of Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'synthesis reaction'?
Synthesis (combination) reactions join simpler substances into more complex products. Examples: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl, N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃. Only one product is formed.
What is a 'single displacement reaction'?
In single displacement, a free element replaces a less active element in a compound. The activity series predicts which reactions occur. Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (iron is more active than copper).
What is 'reduction'?
Reduction is the gain of electrons (GER: Gain of Electrons is Reduction). The oxidation state decreases. The substance being reduced is the oxidizing agent because it accepts electrons from another species.
What is an 'oxidizing agent'?
The oxidizing agent accepts electrons from another species (is itself reduced), thereby causing the oxidation of that species. Common oxidizing agents include O₂, MnO₄⁻, Cr₂O₇²⁻, and halogens.
What is a 'reducing agent'?
The reducing agent donates electrons to another species (is itself oxidized), thereby causing the reduction of that species. Common reducing agents include active metals (Na, Mg, Zn), H₂, and C.
A student drops a piece of copper wire into silver nitrate solution and observes silver crystals growing on the wire while the solution turns blue. Write the net ionic equation and identify what is oxidized and what is reduced.
Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ (oxidation, turns solution blue). 2Ag⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → 2Ag(s) (reduction, silver crystals form). Copper is the reducing agent; silver ion is the oxidizing agent. NO₃⁻ is a spectator ion.
A water quality analyst tests a lake water sample by adding AgNO₃ solution. A white precipitate forms, which darkens in light. She suspects chloride contamination. What is the precipitate, and how does this test work?
Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s). Silver chloride is insoluble (solubility rules: Ag⁺ halides are insoluble except AgF). The white precipitate darkens due to photodecomposition. This is the classic test for chloride ions.
A metallurgist extracts aluminum from bauxite ore (Al₂O₃) using the Hall-Heroult process, an electrolytic reduction. The overall reaction is: 2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂. What is oxidized and what is reduced in this process?
Al goes from +3 in Al₂O₃ to 0 in Al metal (reduction, gains 3e⁻). O goes from -2 in Al₂O₃ to 0 in O₂ (oxidation, loses 2e⁻). This process requires enormous electrical energy, making aluminum production energy-intensive.
Real-World Applications
Example: When zinc metal is placed in a copper sulfate solution, zinc dissolves and copper metal is deposited. This is an example of:
Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s). Zinc is more active than copper, so it displaces copper from the solution. This is a single displacement (activity series) redox reaction.
Example: 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂ is an example of what type of reaction?
One compound (KClO₃) breaks down into two simpler substances (KCl and O₂). This is a decomposition reaction, often requiring heat, light, or electricity to initiate.
Example: What is an 'oxidizing agent'?
The oxidizing agent accepts electrons from another species (is itself reduced), thereby causing the oxidation of that species. Common oxidizing agents include O₂, MnO₄⁻, Cr₂O₇²⁻, and halogens.
Example: What is a 'reducing agent'?
The reducing agent donates electrons to another species (is itself oxidized), thereby causing the reduction of that species. Common reducing agents include active metals (Na, Mg, Zn), H₂, and C.
Example: A pool supply store sells calcium hypochlorite (Ca(ClO)₂) tablets for disinfection. When dissolved, hypochlorite ion acts as an oxidizing agent: ClO⁻ + 2e⁻ + H₂O → Cl⁻ + 2OH⁻. It kills bacteria by oxidizing their cell membranes. What happens to the chlorine's oxidation state?
In ClO⁻, Cl has an oxidation state of +1 (O is -2). In Cl⁻, Cl is -1. Chlorine gains 2 electrons (+1 → -1), so it is reduced. As an oxidizing agent, ClO⁻ takes electrons from bacteria cell components.

--- Lesson 4.1: Types of Reactions (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which type of reaction involves two compounds exchanging ions to form two new compounds?
    [ ] Decomposition, at least
    [ ] Synthesis under most
    [ ] Combustion without
    [*] Double displacement (metathesis)
    Explanation: In double displacement (metathesis) reactions, the cations and anions of two ionic compounds switch partners: AB + CD → AD + CB. These often produce a precipitate, gas, or water.

  Q2. In a combustion reaction of a hydrocarbon, the products are always:
    [ ] CO₂ and H₂
    [ ] C and H₂O
    [ ] CO and H₂
    [*] CO₂ and H₂O
    Explanation: Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon (CₓHᵧ) in excess oxygen always produces carbon dioxide and water: CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. Incomplete combustion can produce CO or soot (C).

  Q3. When zinc metal is placed in a copper sulfate solution, zinc dissolves and copper metal is deposited. This is an example of:
    [ ] Synthesis reaction
    [*] Single displacement (replacement) reaction
    [ ] Acid-base reaction
    [ ] Decomposition reaction
    Explanation: Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s). Zinc is more active than copper, so it displaces copper from the solution. This is a single displacement (activity series) redox reaction.

  Q4. Which of the following is a precipitation reaction?
    [*] AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
    [ ] HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
    [ ] 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
    [ ] CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
    Explanation: When AgNO₃ and NaCl solutions are mixed, insoluble AgCl precipitates out of solution. This is a double displacement reaction that forms a precipitate (indicated by ↓).

  Q5. In a redox reaction, the substance that is oxidized:
    [*] Loses electrons and its oxidation state increases
    [ ] Gains electrons and its oxidation state decreases
    [ ] Always gains oxygen atoms
    [ ] Neither gains nor loses electrons
    Explanation: Oxidation is the loss of electrons (OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss). When a substance loses electrons, its oxidation state becomes more positive. The oxidized substance acts as the reducing agent.

  Q6. Which of the following is NOT one of the driving forces for a reaction to go to completion?
    [*] Formation of a colored solution
    [ ] Formation of a gas
    [ ] Formation of a precipitate
    [ ] Formation of a weak electrolyte (water)
    Explanation: Reactions go to completion when products are removed from solution: precipitate formation, gas evolution, or formation of a weak electrolyte (like water in neutralization). Color change alone doesn't drive completion.

  Q7. 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂ is an example of what type of reaction?
    [ ] Double displacement
    [*] Decomposition
    [ ] Synthesis
    [ ] Single displacement
    Explanation: One compound (KClO₃) breaks down into two simpler substances (KCl and O₂). This is a decomposition reaction, often requiring heat, light, or electricity to initiate.

  Q8. In a net ionic equation, spectator ions are:
    [ ] Ions that catalyze the reaction
    [ ] Ions that form the precipitate
    [ ] Ions that change oxidation state
    [*] Ions that are present in solution but do not participate in the reaction
    Explanation: Spectator ions appear unchanged on both sides of the equation. They are present in solution but don't participate in the net reaction. The net ionic equation removes spectator ions to show only the species that actually react.

  Q9. Which combination of solutions would produce a precipitate? (Use solubility rules)
    [ ] KBr + NaNO₃
    [ ] NaCl + KNO₃
    [*] Pb(NO₃)₂ + KI
    [ ] NaOH + HCl
    Explanation: Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂↓ + 2KNO₃. Lead(II) iodide (PbI₂) is insoluble (exception to the general solubility of iodides). The bright yellow PbI₂ precipitate forms immediately.

  Q10. In the activity series, which metal can displace hydrogen from an acid?
    [ ] Platinum (Pt)
    [*] Zinc (Zn)
    [ ] Silver (Ag)
    [ ] Gold (Au)
    Explanation: Metals above hydrogen in the activity series (like Zn, Fe, Mg) can displace H₂ from acids: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂. Metals below hydrogen (Au, Ag, Pt) cannot react with ordinary acids.

  Q11. What is a 'synthesis reaction'?
    [*] A reaction where two or more reactants combine to form a single, more complex product: A + B → AB
    [ ] A reaction between an acid and a base
    [ ] A reaction where one element replaces another in a compound
    [ ] A reaction where a compound breaks into simpler substances
    Explanation: Synthesis (combination) reactions join simpler substances into more complex products. Examples: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl, N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃. Only one product is formed.

  Q12. What does 'decomposition reaction' mean?
    [*] A reaction in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances: AB → A + B
    [ ] A reaction combining two elements
    [ ] A reaction producing a gas only
    [ ] A reaction between two ionic compounds
    Explanation: Decomposition reactions break one compound into simpler substances. They often require energy input (heat, light, electricity). Examples: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolysis), CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (heating).

  Q13. What is a 'single displacement reaction'?
    [ ] A reaction between an acid and a metal oxide
    [ ] A reaction where two compounds switch partners
    [ ] A reaction where a compound breaks into elements
    [*] A reaction where a more reactive element replaces a less reactive element in a compound: A + BC → AC + B
    Explanation: In single displacement, a free element replaces a less active element in a compound. The activity series predicts which reactions occur. Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (iron is more active than copper).

  Q14. What does 'oxidation' mean?
    [ ] The addition of oxygen to a compound only
    [*] The loss of electrons by a species, resulting in an increase in oxidation state
    [ ] The decrease in oxidation number
    [ ] The gain of electrons by a species
    Explanation: Oxidation is the loss of electrons (LEO: Loss of Electrons is Oxidation). The oxidation state of the species increases. The substance being oxidized is the reducing agent because it donates electrons to another species.

  Q15. What is 'reduction'?
    [*] The gain of electrons by a species, resulting in a decrease in oxidation state
    [ ] The loss of electrons by a species
    [ ] The increase in oxidation number
    [ ] The removal of oxygen from a compound
    Explanation: Reduction is the gain of electrons (GER: Gain of Electrons is Reduction). The oxidation state decreases. The substance being reduced is the oxidizing agent because it accepts electrons from another species.

  Q16. What does 'net ionic equation' mean?
    [ ] An equation written without coefficients
    [ ] An equation balanced in terms of charge only
    [ ] An equation showing all ions present in solution
    [*] An equation showing only the species that actually participate in the reaction, with spectator ions removed
    Explanation: The net ionic equation removes spectator ions to show only the species undergoing chemical change. For AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃, the net ionic equation is Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s).

  Q17. What is an 'oxidizing agent'?
    [ ] A substance that provides oxygen
    [*] A substance that gains electrons and is itself reduced, causing another substance to be oxidized
    [ ] A catalyst that speeds up oxidation reactions
    [ ] A substance that loses electrons
    Explanation: The oxidizing agent accepts electrons from another species (is itself reduced), thereby causing the oxidation of that species. Common oxidizing agents include O₂, MnO₄⁻, Cr₂O₇²⁻, and halogens.

  Q18. What is a 'reducing agent'?
    [ ] A catalyst for reduction reactions regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    [ ] A substance that removes oxygen, as conventional reducing theory would suggest
    [ ] A substance that gains electrons without accounting for additional influences
    [*] A substance that donates electrons and is itself oxidized, causing another substance to be reduced
    Explanation: The reducing agent donates electrons to another species (is itself oxidized), thereby causing the reduction of that species. Common reducing agents include active metals (Na, Mg, Zn), H₂, and C.

  Q19. What does 'activity series' refer to?
    [ ] A list of elements ordered by atomic number
    [ ] A list of reaction rates for different elements
    [ ] A periodic table arrangement based on reactivity
    [*] A ranking of metals (and hydrogen) by their relative ease of oxidation, used to predict whether a displacement reaction will occur
    Explanation: The activity series ranks metals by their tendency to lose electrons. A metal higher in the series can displace a lower metal from its compound. Li > K > Ca > Na > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Ag > Au.

  Q20. What does 'solubility rules' refer to?
    [ ] Rules about temperature effects on solubility
    [ ] Laws governing gas solubility only
    [ ] Rules about how fast substances dissolve
    [*] General guidelines that predict whether an ionic compound will dissolve in water or form a precipitate
    Explanation: Solubility rules are empirical guidelines: most Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺, and NO₃⁻ compounds are soluble. Most Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, and Hg₂²⁺ halides are insoluble. These rules predict precipitation in double displacement reactions.

  Q21. A homeowner mixes bleach (NaClO) with ammonia (NH₃) while cleaning, producing toxic chloramine gas (NH₂Cl). The reaction is: NaClO + NH₃ → NH₂Cl + NaOH. What type of reaction is this, and why is it dangerous?
    [*] A redox reaction where Cl in bleach is reduced and N in ammonia is oxidized, producing toxic volatile chloramine gas that causes respiratory damage
    [ ] A decomposition reaction of bleach, as most introductory frameworks would predict when analyzing Naclo dynamics
    [ ] A synthesis reaction producing a harmless compound, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Acid-base neutralization; it produces a salt, which is a common assumption in the field
    Explanation: In NaClO, Cl is +1; in NH₂Cl, Cl is -1 (reduced). The N goes from -3 to -1 (oxidized). Chloramine is a toxic, volatile compound that irritates the respiratory system. This is why bleach and ammonia must never be mixed.

  Q22. A student drops a piece of copper wire into silver nitrate solution and observes silver crystals growing on the wire while the solution turns blue. Write the net ionic equation and identify what is oxidized and what is reduced.
    [*] Cu is oxidized (loses electrons) and Ag⁺ is reduced (gains electrons); Cu(s) + 2Ag⁺(aq) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2Ag(s)
    [ ] Nitrogen is reduced and copper is oxidized
    [ ] Neither is oxidized or reduced; this is a precipitation reaction
    [ ] Cu is reduced and Ag⁺ is oxidized; Cu²⁺ + 2Ag → Cu + 2Ag⁺
    Explanation: Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ (oxidation, turns solution blue). 2Ag⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → 2Ag(s) (reduction, silver crystals form). Copper is the reducing agent; silver ion is the oxidizing agent. NO₃⁻ is a spectator ion.

  Q23. A water quality analyst tests a lake water sample by adding AgNO₃ solution. A white precipitate forms, which darkens in light. She suspects chloride contamination. What is the precipitate, and how does this test work?
    [ ] AgO, formed by reaction with dissolved oxygen when considering all relevant chloride factors
    [ ] AgNO₃, which comes out of solution, which is consistent with how chloride mechanisms are understood to operate in theory
    [ ] NaCl, which precipitates when Ag⁺ is added under most circumstances involving chloride
    [*] AgCl, formed because silver chloride is insoluble; the precipitation of white AgCl that darkens in light confirms the presence of Cl⁻ ions
    Explanation: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s). Silver chloride is insoluble (solubility rules: Ag⁺ halides are insoluble except AgF). The white precipitate darkens due to photodecomposition. This is the classic test for chloride ions.

  Q24. A sculptor creates a bronze statue that develops a green patina over years of outdoor exposure. The green coating is copper(II) carbonate hydroxide (Cu₂(OH)₂CO₃). What type of reaction produced the patina, and what are the reactants?
    [ ] An acid-base reaction with acid rain only when considering all relevant patina factors
    [ ] A decomposition of the bronze alloy within the broader context of patina analysis
    [*] A complex oxidation reaction where copper reacts with oxygen, water, and CO₂ from the atmosphere: 2Cu + H₂O + CO₂ + O₂ → Cu₂(OH)₂CO₃
    [ ] A single displacement reaction with atmospheric nitrogen, especially during periods of significant change
    Explanation: The patina forms through oxidation of copper by atmospheric O₂, combined with reaction with H₂O and CO₂. Copper is oxidized from 0 to +2. The patina actually protects the underlying metal from further corrosion.

  Q25. A chemistry student accidentally spills sodium hydroxide on the lab bench. Her teacher instructs her to neutralize it with dilute acetic acid (vinegar) rather than strong HCl. Why is vinegar preferred over HCl for this cleanup?
    [ ] Vinegar produces a precipitate that absorbs the NaOH according to widely held views on this topic
    [ ] Vinegar is a stronger acid and neutralizes faster regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    [*] Vinegar is a weak acid that neutralizes gradually without risk of overshooting to a dangerously acidic pH, while HCl could create a strongly acidic spill
    [ ] HCl cannot neutralize NaOH given that prevailing acid conditions tend to reinforce this particular trajectory
    Explanation: Using a strong acid risks overshooting neutralization, creating a new acid hazard. Weak acetic acid reacts gradually: CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O. The buffering effect of the weak acid prevents dangerous pH swings.

  Q26. A metallurgist extracts aluminum from bauxite ore (Al₂O₃) using the Hall-Heroult process, an electrolytic reduction. The overall reaction is: 2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂. What is oxidized and what is reduced in this process?
    [ ] Neither species changes oxidation state
    [ ] Both aluminum and oxygen are reduced
    [*] Aluminum is reduced (Al³⁺ gains electrons to become Al⁰) and oxygen is oxidized (O²⁻ loses electrons to become O₂)
    [ ] Oxygen is reduced and aluminum is oxidized
    Explanation: Al goes from +3 in Al₂O₃ to 0 in Al metal (reduction, gains 3e⁻). O goes from -2 in Al₂O₃ to 0 in O₂ (oxidation, loses 2e⁻). This process requires enormous electrical energy, making aluminum production energy-intensive.

  Q27. A beekeeper uses a smoker to calm bees. The smoke is produced by incomplete combustion of burlap or pine needles. If complete combustion of pine resin (approximate formula C₁₀H₁₆O) produces CO₂ and H₂O, what products form during incomplete combustion?
    [ ] Only CO₂ and H₂O because conventional analysis of combustion suggests this would be the expected result
    [ ] Only CO and H₂ based on the conventional understanding of how combustion processes interact in practice
    [ ] Pure carbon and water only, which represents a common but incomplete view of how these combustion dynamics operate
    [*] CO, C (soot), H₂O, and various partially oxidized organic compounds, because insufficient O₂ prevents complete oxidation of all carbon to CO₂
    Explanation: Incomplete combustion occurs with limited oxygen supply. Not all carbon is fully oxidized to CO₂; some forms CO (toxic), C soot (particulates), and partially oxidized organic compounds. This is also why car exhaust contains CO.

  Q28. A pool supply store sells calcium hypochlorite (Ca(ClO)₂) tablets for disinfection. When dissolved, hypochlorite ion acts as an oxidizing agent: ClO⁻ + 2e⁻ + H₂O → Cl⁻ + 2OH⁻. It kills bacteria by oxidizing their cell membranes. What happens to the chlorine's oxidation state?
    [ ] Chlorine is oxidized from +1 to +7
    [*] Chlorine is reduced from +1 in ClO⁻ to -1 in Cl⁻, gaining 2 electrons
    [ ] Chlorine is reduced from 0 to -1
    [ ] Chlorine's oxidation state does not change
    Explanation: In ClO⁻, Cl has an oxidation state of +1 (O is -2). In Cl⁻, Cl is -1. Chlorine gains 2 electrons (+1 → -1), so it is reduced. As an oxidizing agent, ClO⁻ takes electrons from bacteria cell components.

  Q29. An emergency responder encounters a chemical spill where solid sodium metal has been exposed to water. A violent reaction occurs with flames and the release of a gas. What type of reaction is this, and what gas is produced?
    [ ] Decomposition; oxygen gas is released, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Combustion; CO₂ is produced, although this interpretation oversimplifies the actual reaction mechanisms at work
    [*] Single displacement; sodium displaces hydrogen from water: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂, and the heat generated ignites the H₂ gas
    [ ] Double displacement; NaCl is formed, especially during periods of significant change
    Explanation: Sodium, being very high in the activity series, vigorously displaces hydrogen from water. The reaction is highly exothermic: 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g). The heat ignites the hydrogen gas, causing flames.

  Q30. An art conservator restores a painting with darkened white lead paint (originally PbCO₃, now converted to black PbS by atmospheric H₂S over centuries). She treats the darkened paint with hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂). The paint returns to white. What reaction restores the white color?
    [ ] H₂O₂ dissolves the PbS, revealing the original PbCO₃ underneath
    [ ] H₂O₂ reacts with the oil paint medium, not the lead compound
    [ ] H₂O₂ decomposes PbS into Pb and S, both of which are white
    [*] H₂O₂ oxidizes black PbS to white PbSO₄: PbS + 4H₂O₂ → PbSO₄ + 4H₂O, converting the black sulfide to white sulfate
    Explanation: H₂O₂ is an oxidizing agent that converts black PbS (sulfide, S²⁻) to white PbSO₄ (sulfate, S⁶⁺). The sulfur is oxidized from -2 to +6. This clever redox reaction restores the painting without removing the original paint.

--- Lesson 4.1: Types of Reactions (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$

--- Lesson 4.2: Balancing Equations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Balancing Equations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the coefficient for O2 when the equation Fe + O2 -> Fe2O3 is balanced?
The balanced equation is 4Fe + 3O2 -> 2Fe2O3. The coefficient for O2 is 3, giving 6 oxygen atoms on each side.
What is the sum of all coefficients when CH4 + O2 -> CO2 + H2O is balanced?
The balanced equation is CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O. The sum of coefficients is 1 + 2 + 1 + 2 = 6.
What is the balanced coefficient for N2 in the equation N2 + H2 -> NH3?
The balanced equation is N2 + 3H2 -> 2NH3. The coefficient of N2 is 1 (2 nitrogen atoms on each side).
What is a chemical equation?
A chemical equation is a symbolic representation that uses chemical formulas and symbols to show the reactants and products of a chemical reaction.
What is a skeleton equation?
A skeleton equation is an unbalanced chemical equation that shows only the correct chemical formulas of the reactants and products without the proper coefficients.
A propane grill burns C3H8 in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water. What is the correctly balanced equation for this combustion?
C3H8 + 5O2 -> 3CO2 + 4H2O has 3 C, 8 H, and 10 O on each side. Combustion of hydrocarbons always produces CO2 and H2O.
A fireworks manufacturer uses the reaction KClO3 -> KCl + O2 to generate oxygen gas. What is the coefficient of KClO3 when the equation is balanced?
The balanced equation is 2KClO3 -> 2KCl + 3O2. The coefficient of KClO3 is 2, yielding 6 oxygen atoms on each side.
An environmental scientist studies the combustion of ethanol in a car engine: C2H5OH + O2 -> CO2 + H2O. What is the coefficient for O2 when balanced?
The balanced equation is C2H5OH + 3O2 -> 2CO2 + 3H2O. Counting oxygen: the left has 1 (from ethanol) + 6 (from 3O2) = 7 O, and the right has 4 (from 2CO2) + 3 (from 3H2O) = 7 O.
A teacher shows a demonstration where calcium carbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid: CaCO3 + HCl -> CaCl2 + H2O + CO2. What is the sum of all coefficients in the balanced equation?
The balanced equation is CaCO3 + 2HCl -> CaCl2 + H2O + CO2. The sum of all coefficients is 1 + 2 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 6.

--- Lesson 4.2: Balancing Equations (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What must be conserved when balancing a chemical equation?
    [*] The number of atoms of each element
    [ ] The number of molecules on each side
    [ ] The physical states of all substances
    [ ] The total volume of reactants and products
    Explanation: The law of conservation of mass requires that the number of atoms of each element be equal on both sides of a balanced chemical equation.

  Q2. What are the numbers placed in front of chemical formulas to balance an equation called?
    [ ] Subscripts
    [*] Coefficients
    [ ] Superscripts
    [ ] Exponents
    Explanation: Coefficients are the whole numbers placed before chemical formulas in an equation to balance the number of atoms on each side.

  Q3. Which of the following is a correctly balanced equation?
    [ ] H2 + O2 -> 2H2O
    [ ] H2 + O2 -> H2O
    [ ] 2H2 + 2O2 -> 2H2O
    [*] 2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O
    Explanation: 2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O has 4 hydrogen atoms and 2 oxygen atoms on each side, making it correctly balanced.

  Q4. When balancing an equation, which of the following should you NEVER change?
    [ ] The order of reactants
    [*] The subscripts in the chemical formulas
    [ ] The coefficients
    [ ] The physical states
    Explanation: Changing subscripts would change the identity of the substance. Only coefficients may be adjusted when balancing chemical equations.

  Q5. What is the coefficient for O2 when the equation Fe + O2 -> Fe2O3 is balanced?
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 4
    [ ] 6
    [*] 3
    Explanation: The balanced equation is 4Fe + 3O2 -> 2Fe2O3. The coefficient for O2 is 3, giving 6 oxygen atoms on each side.

  Q6. Which law is the fundamental basis for balancing chemical equations?
    [*] Law of conservation of mass
    [ ] Law of definite proportions
    [ ] Law of multiple proportions
    [ ] Avogadro's law
    Explanation: The law of conservation of mass states that matter cannot be created or destroyed, which is why atoms must be balanced on both sides of an equation.

  Q7. What is the sum of all coefficients when CH4 + O2 -> CO2 + H2O is balanced?
    [*] 6
    [ ] 5
    [ ] 7
    [ ] 8
    Explanation: The balanced equation is CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O. The sum of coefficients is 1 + 2 + 1 + 2 = 6.

  Q8. In a balanced equation, the arrow separating reactants and products represents what?
    [ ] Is greater than
    [ ] Equilibrium
    [ ] An equal sign
    [*] Yields or produces
    Explanation: The single arrow in a chemical equation means 'yields' or 'produces,' indicating the direction in which the reaction proceeds.

  Q9. What is the balanced coefficient for N2 in the equation N2 + H2 -> NH3?
    [*] 1
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 4
    Explanation: The balanced equation is N2 + 3H2 -> 2NH3. The coefficient of N2 is 1 (2 nitrogen atoms on each side).

  Q10. Which element is typically balanced last when balancing combustion reactions?
    [ ] Hydrogen
    [ ] Carbon
    [ ] Nitrogen
    [*] Oxygen
    Explanation: Oxygen appears in multiple products (CO2 and H2O) in combustion reactions, so it is balanced last after carbon and hydrogen are balanced.

  Q11. What is a chemical equation?
    [*] A symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using formulas and symbols
    [ ] A diagram showing molecular shapes
    [ ] A mathematical formula for calculating reaction rates
    [ ] A table listing the properties of elements
    Explanation: A chemical equation is a symbolic representation that uses chemical formulas and symbols to show the reactants and products of a chemical reaction.

  Q12. What does the term 'coefficient' mean in the context of a chemical equation?
    [ ] The charge on an ion in the equation
    [ ] A small number written below and to the right of an element symbol
    [ ] The energy released during the reaction

--- Lesson 4.3: Stoichiometry and Limiting Reagents (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Stoichiometry and Limiting Reagents
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the mole ratio of N2 to NH3 in the balanced equation N2 + 3H2 -> 2NH3?
From the balanced equation, the coefficient of N2 is 1 and NH3 is 2, giving a mole ratio of 1:2.
What is stoichiometry?
Stoichiometry is the branch of chemistry that deals with the quantitative relationships between the amounts of reactants consumed and products formed in a chemical reaction.
A pharmaceutical company reacts 50.0 g of salicylic acid (C7H6O3, MW = 138.1) with excess acetic anhydride to make aspirin (C9H8O4, MW = 180.2). What is the theoretical yield of aspirin?
Moles of salicylic acid = 50.0/138.1 = 0.362 mol. The 1:1 ratio gives 0.362 mol aspirin. Mass = 0.362 x 180.2 = 65.2 g of aspirin.
A student has 3.0 moles of Fe and 4.0 moles of O2 for the reaction 4Fe + 3O2 -> 2Fe2O3. What is the maximum number of moles of Fe2O3 that can be produced?
Fe: 3.0 mol / 4 = 0.75 equivalents. O2: 4.0 mol / 3 = 1.33 equivalents. Fe has fewer equivalents, so Fe is limiting. 3.0 mol Fe x (2 mol Fe2O3 / 4 mol Fe) = 1.5 mol Fe2O3.
Real-World Applications
Example: In the reaction 2Al + 3Cl2 -> 2AlCl3, if you have 2 moles of Al and 2 moles of Cl2, which is the limiting reagent?
2 moles of Al require 3 moles of Cl2, but only 2 moles of Cl2 are available. Cl2 is insufficient and is therefore the limiting reagent.
Example: What happens to the excess reagent when a reaction goes to completion?
The excess reagent is present in a greater amount than needed. When the limiting reagent is consumed, some excess reagent remains unreacted.
Example: What does 'limiting reagent' mean?
The limiting reagent is the reactant that is completely consumed first in a reaction, thereby determining (limiting) the maximum amount of product that can be formed.
Example: What does 'excess reagent' mean?
An excess reagent is the reactant that remains after the limiting reagent has been completely consumed; it is present in more than the stoichiometrically required amount.
Example: A baker needs to make cookies and has 5 eggs and 3 cups of flour. Each batch requires 2 eggs and 1 cup of flour. What limits the number of batches, and how does this relate to limiting reagents?
With 5 eggs at 2 per batch, you can make only 2 full batches (using 4 eggs, with 1 egg excess). Flour allows 3 batches but is irrelevant since eggs run out first -- just like a limiting reagent in chemistry.
Example: A lab technician mixes 10.0 g of zinc with 15.0 g of hydrochloric acid according to: Zn + 2HCl -> ZnCl2 + H2. Which is the limiting reagent? (Molar masses: Zn = 65.4 g/mol, HCl = 36.5 g/mol)
Moles of Zn = 10.0/65.4 = 0.153 mol; moles of HCl = 15.0/36.5 = 0.411 mol. Zn needs 2 x 0.153 = 0.306 mol HCl; we have 0.411 mol. Since there is more HCl than needed, Zn is limiting and is completely consumed first.
Example: A student reacts 20.0 g of NaOH (MW = 40) with 30.0 g of H2SO4 (MW = 98) in the reaction 2NaOH + H2SO4 -> Na2SO4 + 2H2O. How many grams of excess reagent remain?
Moles NaOH = 20.0/40 = 0.500 mol; moles H2SO4 = 30.0/98 = 0.306 mol. NaOH needs 0.500/2 = 0.250 mol H2SO4. Since 0.306 > 0.250, NaOH is limiting. Excess H2SO4 = 0.306 - 0.250 = 0.056 mol = 0.056 x 98 = 5.49 g.
Example: A water treatment plant uses chlorine gas to purify water. If 5.00 moles of Cl2 react with 5.00 moles of NaOH according to: Cl2 + 2NaOH -> NaCl + NaClO + H2O, how many moles of NaCl are produced and which reagent is in excess?
NaOH is limiting: 5.00 mol NaOH / 2 = 2.50 mol Cl2 needed. We have 5.00 mol Cl2, so Cl2 is in excess. 2.50 mol Cl2 reacts to produce 2.50 mol NaCl.

--- Lesson 4.4: Percent Yield (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Percent Yield
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for percent yield?
Percent yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) x 100%. It compares what was actually obtained to the maximum possible amount.
If the theoretical yield of a reaction is 50 g and the actual yield is 40 g, what is the percent yield?
Percent yield = (40/50) x 100% = 80%. This means 80% of the maximum possible product was obtained.
A student obtains 15 g of product from a reaction with a theoretical yield of 25 g. What is the percent yield?
Percent yield = (15/25) x 100% = 60%. The student obtained 60% of the theoretical maximum.
What is the actual yield if the theoretical yield is 80 g and the percent yield is 75%?
Actual yield = (percent yield / 100) x theoretical yield = (75/100) x 80 = 60 g.
A reaction has a theoretical yield of 100 g. If side reactions consume 10% of the reactant and 5 g of product is lost during filtration, what is the approximate actual yield?
If 10% is lost to side reactions, 90 g is produced. Losing 5 g in filtration leaves 85 g actual yield (85% percent yield).
A pharmaceutical company synthesizes 450 g of aspirin from a batch of salicylic acid. The theoretical yield was 520 g. The quality control team needs to determine the percent yield. What is it?
Percent yield = (450/520) x 100% = 86.5%. The company achieved 86.5% efficiency in their aspirin synthesis.
A student heats 10.0 g of KClO3 (MW = 122.6) to decompose it: 2KClO3 -> 2KCl + 3O2. The student collects 3.5 g of O2 (MW = 32). What is the percent yield?
Moles KClO3 = 10.0/122.6 = 0.0816 mol. Moles O2 = 0.0816 x (3/2) = 0.1224 mol. Theoretical yield = 0.1224 x 32 = 3.917 g. Percent yield = (3.5/3.917) x 100% = 89.4%.
A chemistry student wants to produce copper metal by reacting CuSO4 with zinc: CuSO4 + Zn -> ZnSO4 + Cu. Starting with 25.0 g of CuSO4 (MW = 159.6), the student obtains 8.5 g of Cu (MW = 63.5). What is the percent yield?
Moles CuSO4 = 25.0/159.6 = 0.1566 mol. The 1:1 ratio gives 0.1566 mol Cu. Theoretical yield = 0.1566 x 63.5 = 9.94 g. Percent yield = (8.5/9.94) x 100% = 85.5%.
A student performs the reaction: Mg + 2HCl -> MgCl2 + H2. Using 2.43 g of Mg (MW = 24.3), the student collects 0.18 g of H2 (MW = 2). What is the percent yield?
Moles Mg = 2.43/24.3 = 0.10 mol. The 1:1 ratio gives 0.10 mol H2. Theoretical yield = 0.10 x 2 = 0.20 g. Percent yield = (0.18/0.20) x 100% = 90%.
An industrial process has a percent yield of 60% for step 1 and 80% for step 2. If the theoretical yield of the final product is 100 g (assuming both steps were 100%), what is the approximate overall actual yield?
For multi-step syntheses, overall yield is the product of individual yields: 0.60 x 0.80 = 0.48, so 48% of 100 g = 48 g. This shows why multi-step reactions often have low overall yields.
Real-World Applications
Example: How does the theoretical yield relate to the limiting reagent?
The theoretical yield is calculated by assuming complete conversion of the limiting reagent to product, using the stoichiometric mole ratios from the balanced equation.
