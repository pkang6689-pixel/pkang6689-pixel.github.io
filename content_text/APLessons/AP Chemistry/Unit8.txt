--- Lesson 8.1: Acid-Base Definitions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acid-Base Definitions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the conjugate base of H2SO4?
When H2SO4 donates one proton, it becomes HSO4-, which is its conjugate base.
What is the conjugate acid of NH3?
When NH3 accepts a proton, it forms NH4+, which is the conjugate acid of NH3.

--- Lesson 8.1: Acid-Base Definitions (Quiz) ---

  Q1. According to the Arrhenius definition, an acid is a substance that:
    [ ] Donates a proton
    [*] Produces H+ ions in aqueous solution
    [ ] Accepts an electron pair
    [ ] Produces OH- ions in aqueous solution
    Explanation: Arrhenius defined acids as substances that produce H+ (or H3O+) ions when dissolved in water.

  Q2. According to Bronsted-Lowry theory, a base is:
    [ ] An OH- producer
    [ ] A proton donor
    [*] A proton acceptor
    [ ] An electron donor
    Explanation: The Bronsted-Lowry definition defines a base as a proton (H+) acceptor, which is broader than the Arrhenius definition.

  Q3. In the reaction NH3 + H2O <-> NH4+ + OH-, which species is the Bronsted-Lowry acid?
    [*] H2O
    [ ] OH-
    [ ] NH4+
    [ ] NH3
    Explanation: Water donates a proton to NH3, making water the Bronsted-Lowry acid in this reaction.

  Q4. A Lewis acid is defined as:
    [ ] A hydroxide producer
    [ ] A proton donor
    [ ] An electron pair donor
    [*] An electron pair acceptor
    Explanation: A Lewis acid accepts an electron pair from another species to form a coordinate bond. This is the broadest definition of an acid.

  Q5. What is the conjugate base of H2SO4?
    [ ] SO4^2-
    [ ] H3O+
    [*] HSO4-
    [ ] H2O
    Explanation: When H2SO4 donates one proton, it becomes HSO4-, which is its conjugate base.

  Q6. Which of the following is a strong acid?
    [ ] HF
    [ ] CH3COOH
    [ ] H2CO3
    [*] HCl
    Explanation: HCl is one of the seven common strong acids that completely ionize in water. CH3COOH (acetic acid) and HF are weak acids.

  Q7. Water can act as both an acid and a base. This property is called:
    [ ] Polyprotic, even when
    [ ] Neutral, even when
    [*] Amphoteric (or amphiprotic)
    [ ] Diprotic when
    Explanation: Water is amphoteric (amphiprotic) -- it can donate a proton (acting as an acid) or accept a proton (acting as a base) depending on the reaction partner.

  Q8. What is the conjugate acid of NH3?
    [ ] NH2-
    [*] NH4+
    [ ] NO3-
    [ ] N2H4
    Explanation: When NH3 accepts a proton, it forms NH4+, which is the conjugate acid of NH3.

    [ ] Al(OH)3
    [ ] CH3NH2
    [*] NaOH
    [ ] NH3
    Explanation: NaOH is a strong base that completely dissociates into Na+ and OH- in water. NH3 and CH3NH2 are weak bases.

  Q10. In a conjugate acid-base pair, the acid and base differ by:
    [ ] One hydroxide ion
    [ ] One electron
    [*] One proton (H+)
    [ ] One water molecule
    Explanation: A conjugate acid-base pair consists of two species that differ by exactly one proton. The acid has one more H+ than its conjugate base.

  Q11. What does 'Arrhenius acid' mean?
    [*] A substance that produces H+ (or H3O+) ions in aqueous solution
    [ ] A substance that accepts protons
    [ ] A substance that donates electron pairs

    Explanation: An Arrhenius acid is a substance that increases the concentration of H+ (hydronium, H3O+) ions when dissolved in water.

  Q12. What does 'Bronsted-Lowry acid' mean?
    [ ] A strong acid only
    [ ] A substance that only works in water
    [*] A proton (H+) donor in any solvent
    [ ] An electron pair acceptor
    Explanation: A Bronsted-Lowry acid is any species that can donate a proton (H+) to another species, regardless of solvent.

  Q13. What does 'Lewis base' mean?
    [*] An electron pair donor
    [ ] A proton acceptor
    [ ] An electron acceptor
    [ ] A hydroxide ion producer
    Explanation: A Lewis base is a species that donates an electron pair to form a coordinate (dative) bond with a Lewis acid.

  Q14. What does 'conjugate acid-base pair' mean?
    [ ] Any acid and base mixed together
    [*] Two species related by the transfer of a single proton
    [ ] A strong acid paired with a strong base
    [ ] An acid and its salt
    Explanation: A conjugate acid-base pair consists of two species that differ by exactly one proton: HA/A- or B/BH+.

  Q15. What does 'amphoteric' mean?
    [ ] A substance that is neither acid nor base
    [*] A substance that can act as both an acid and a base
    [ ] A very strong acid
    [ ] A substance that dissolves in both acids and bases
    Explanation: An amphoteric substance can act as either an acid or a base depending on the other reactant. Water, amino acids, and some metal hydroxides are amphoteric.

    [*] An acid that completely ionizes in water
    [ ] An acid that is dangerous
    [ ] An acid with pH = 0

  Q17. What does 'weak acid' mean?
    [ ] An acid with very low concentration
    [*] An acid that only partially ionizes in water
    [ ] An acid that is not dangerous
    [ ] An acid that does not react
    Explanation: A weak acid only partially ionizes in water, establishing an equilibrium between the undissociated acid and its ions.

  Q18. What does 'Ka' (acid dissociation constant) mean?
    [ ] The rate constant for acid reactions
    [ ] The concentration of acid in solution
    [*] The equilibrium constant for the ionization of a weak acid in water
    [ ] The pH of the acid

    [ ] The boiling point of the base

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

--- Lesson 8.2: pH and pOH (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: pH and pOH
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the pH of a 0.001 M HCl solution?
HCl is a strong acid: [H+] = 0.001 = 10^-3 M. pH = -log(10^-3) = 3.
What is the relationship between pH and pOH at 25 degrees C?
At 25 C, Kw = [H+][OH-] = 1.0 x 10^-14, so pH + pOH = pKw = 14.
A solution has a pH of 5. What is the [H+]?
[H+] = 10^(-pH) = 10^(-5) = 1 x 10^-5 M.
What is the pOH of a solution with [OH-] = 1 x 10^-4 M?
pOH = -log[OH-] = -log(10^-4) = 4. The pH would be 14 - 4 = 10.
What is the pH of a 0.10 M NaOH solution?
NaOH is a strong base: [OH-] = 0.10 M. pOH = 1. pH = 14 - 1 = 13.
What is Kw at 25 degrees C?
Kw = [H3O+][OH-] = 1.0 x 10^-14 at 25 degrees C. This is the ion product constant for water.
A solution has [H+] = 3.5 x 10^-4 M. What is its pH?
pH = -log(3.5 x 10^-4) = -log(3.5) - log(10^-4) = -0.544 + 4 = 3.46.
A student measures the pH of 0.10 M acetic acid (Ka = 1.8 x 10^-5) and gets pH = 2.87. What is the percent ionization?
[H+] = 10^-2.87 = 1.35 x 10^-3 M. Percent ionization = (1.35x10^-3 / 0.10) x 100 = 1.35%.
At 37 degrees C (body temperature), Kw = 2.4 x 10^-14, not 1.0 x 10^-14. What is the pH of neutral water at body temperature?
Neutral means [H+] = [OH-] = sqrt(Kw) = sqrt(2.4x10^-14) = 1.55x10^-7. pH = -log(1.55x10^-7) = 6.81. Neutral pH is not always 7!
A lab technician dilutes a pH 1 solution by a factor of 100. What is the new pH, assuming the acid is strong?
pH 1 means [H+] = 0.1 M. Diluting 100x: [H+] = 0.001 M = 10^-3. New pH = 3.
A student needs to determine whether a 0.10 M solution of NH4Cl is acidic, basic, or neutral. Given that NH4+ is the conjugate acid of the weak base NH3, what is the solution's nature?
NH4+ is the conjugate acid of weak base NH3. It hydrolyzes: NH4+ + H2O <-> NH3 + H3O+, producing excess H3O+ and making the solution acidic (pH < 7).
A 0.020 M Ba(OH)2 solution is prepared. What is the pH?
Ba(OH)2 is a strong base producing 2 OH- per formula unit: [OH-] = 2 x 0.020 = 0.040 M. pOH = -log(0.040) = 1.40. pH = 14 - 1.40 = 12.60.
Real-World Applications
Example: A swimming pool manager measures the pool water pH as 7.8. Is the water acidic, basic, or neutral, and what adjustment is needed to reach pH 7.4?
pH 7.8 is slightly basic. To lower pH to 7.4, an acid must be added. Pool managers commonly use muriatic acid (HCl) or sodium bisulfate.

--- Lesson 8.3: Titrations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Titrations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the purpose of a titration?
A titration is an analytical technique used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration (the standard solution).
What is the solution of known concentration called in a titration?
The standard solution, also called the titrant, is the solution of precisely known concentration that is added from a buret during a titration.
At the half-equivalence point of a weak acid titration, what is true?
At the half-equivalence point, exactly half the weak acid has been converted to its conjugate base, so [HA] = [A-]. Using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, pH = pKa + log(1) = pKa.
What is the initial pH region of a titration curve for a strong acid called?
The initial region of a titration curve represents the pH of the solution before any titrant has been added. For a strong acid, this is simply determined by the initial acid concentration.
A student titrates 25.0 mL of an unknown HCl solution with 0.100 M NaOH. The equivalence point is reached after adding 30.0 mL of NaOH. What is the molarity of the HCl?
Using MaVa = MbVb: Ma(25.0 mL) = (0.100 M)(30.0 mL). Ma = 3.00/25.0 = 0.120 M. The moles of HCl equal the moles of NaOH at the equivalence point since both are monoprotic/monobasic.
A chemist titrates 50.0 mL of 0.200 M acetic acid (Ka = 1.8 x 10^-5) with 0.200 M NaOH. What is the pH at the half-equivalence point?
At the half-equivalence point, [HA] = [A-], so pH = pKa = -log(1.8 x 10^-5) = 4.74. This is a key relationship that makes titration a useful method for determining Ka values of weak acids.
A student needs to determine the concentration of a sulfuric acid (H2SO4) solution. She titrates 20.0 mL of the acid with 0.150 M NaOH and reaches the equivalence point after adding 40.0 mL. What is the molarity of H2SO4?
H2SO4 is diprotic: H2SO4 + 2NaOH -> Na2SO4 + 2H2O. Moles NaOH = 0.150 x 0.0400 = 0.00600 mol. Since 2 mol NaOH react per mol H2SO4: moles H2SO4 = 0.00600/2 = 0.00300 mol. Molarity = 0.00300/0.0200 = 0.150 M.
A quality control analyst titrates 10.0 mL of vinegar (acetic acid solution) with 0.500 M NaOH. If 16.0 mL of NaOH is required to reach the equivalence point, what is the percent by mass of acetic acid in the vinegar? (Assume density of vinegar = 1.00 g/mL, molar mass of acetic acid = 60.05 g/mol)
Moles NaOH = 0.500 x 0.0160 = 0.00800 mol = moles acetic acid. Mass acetic acid = 0.00800 x 60.05 = 0.480 g. Mass vinegar = 10.0 mL x 1.00 g/mL = 10.0 g. Percent = (0.480/10.0) x 100 = 4.8%.
During a titration of 0.100 M NH3 (Kb = 1.8 x 10^-5) with 0.100 M HCl, a student collects pH data. At the half-equivalence point, what is the pH?
At the half-equivalence point of a weak base titration, [B] = [BH+], so pOH = pKb = -log(1.8 x 10^-5) = 4.74. Therefore pH = 14 - 4.74 = 9.26. This is equivalent to saying pH = pKa of the conjugate acid NH4+.
A student accidentally overshoots the equivalence point by adding 5.0 mL of excess 0.100 M NaOH to a total volume of 55.0 mL. What is the pH of the resulting solution?
Excess moles of NaOH = 0.100 x 0.0050 = 0.000500 mol. [OH-] = 0.000500/0.0550 = 0.00909 M. pOH = -log(0.00909) = 2.04. pH = 14 - 2.04 = 11.96. After the equivalence point, the pH is determined by the excess strong base.
Real-World Applications
Example: A researcher examines a titration curve and notices a very gradual slope near the equivalence point with no sharp inflection. What can she conclude?
A gradual slope near the equivalence point indicates that the acid being titrated is very weak (very small Ka). Strong acid-strong base titrations produce a very steep, sharp inflection at the equivalence point, while weaker acids produce more gradual transitions.

--- Lesson 8.4: Buffer Solutions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Buffer Solutions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A student prepares a buffer by mixing 100.0 mL of 0.200 M acetic acid with 100.0 mL of 0.150 M sodium acetate (pKa = 4.74). What is the pH of this buffer?
After mixing, moles HA = 0.200 x 0.100 = 0.0200 mol, moles A- = 0.150 x 0.100 = 0.0150 mol. pH = 4.74 + log(0.0150/0.0200) = 4.74 + log(0.75) = 4.74 + (-0.125) = 4.62. The pH is below 4.74 because there is more weak acid than conjugate base.
A researcher adds 0.010 mol of HCl to 1.00 L of a buffer containing 0.100 mol CH3COOH and 0.100 mol CH3COO- (pKa = 4.74). What is the new pH?
The HCl reacts with A-: 0.010 mol H+ converts 0.010 mol A- to HA. New moles: A- = 0.100 - 0.010 = 0.090 mol; HA = 0.100 + 0.010 = 0.110 mol. pH = 4.74 + log(0.090/0.110) = 4.74 + log(0.818) = 4.74 - 0.087 = 4.65. The pH changed by only 0.09 units, demonstrating the buffer's effectiveness.

--- Lesson 8.4: Buffer Solutions (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A buffer solution resists changes in pH when small amounts of which substances are added?
    [ ] Only acids
    [ ] Only bases
    [*] Both acids and bases
    [ ] Only neutral salts
    Explanation: A buffer solution resists pH changes when small amounts of either acid or base are added. It does this through the equilibrium between a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid).

  Q2. Which combination forms a buffer solution?
    [ ] HCl and NaOH
    [ ] HCl and NaCl
    [*] CH3COOH and CH3COONa
    [ ] NaOH and NaCl
    Explanation: A buffer consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid). Acetic acid (CH3COOH) and sodium acetate (CH3COONa) form a classic buffer pair. Strong acid/strong base combinations do not form buffers.

  Q3. The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation is pH = pKa + log([A-]/[HA]). When [A-] = [HA], the pH equals:
    [ ] 7.00
    [ ] 0
    [*] pKa
    [ ] pKb
    Explanation: When [A-] = [HA], the ratio [A-]/[HA] = 1, and log(1) = 0. So pH = pKa + 0 = pKa. This is why a buffer is most effective at a pH equal to the pKa of the weak acid.

  Q4. A buffer has the greatest capacity to resist pH changes when:
    [ ] The pH is far from the pKa
    [ ] The weak acid concentration is much greater than the conjugate base
    [ ] The solution is very dilute
    [*] The concentrations of the weak acid and conjugate base are both high and roughly equal
    Explanation: Buffer capacity is greatest when the concentrations of the weak acid and conjugate base are both high (providing more neutralizing capacity) and approximately equal (ensuring the buffer can handle both acid and base additions equally well).

  Q5. When a small amount of strong acid is added to a buffer solution containing HA and A-, what happens?
    [ ] The pH drops dramatically
    [*] The H+ ions react with A- to form HA, minimizing pH change
    [ ] The H+ ions react with HA to form H2A
    [ ] The A- concentration increases
    Explanation: When strong acid (H+) is added to a buffer, the conjugate base (A-) reacts with the H+ to form the weak acid (HA): A- + H+ -> HA. This consumes the added acid, preventing a large pH drop.

  Q6. A buffer is generally effective within what pH range relative to its pKa?
    [*] pKa +/- 1
    [ ] pKa +/- 3
    [ ] pKa +/- 0.5
    [ ] pKa +/- 2
    Explanation: A buffer is generally effective within +/- 1 pH unit of the pKa of the weak acid. Beyond this range, the ratio of [A-]/[HA] becomes too extreme (greater than 10:1 or less than 1:10) and the buffer can no longer resist pH changes effectively.

  Q7. Which of the following is NOT a buffer system?
    [ ] H2PO4- and HPO4^2-
    [ ] NH3 and NH4Cl
    [*] HCl and NaCl
    [ ] H2CO3 and NaHCO3
    Explanation: HCl is a strong acid, so HCl and NaCl do not form a buffer. A buffer requires a weak acid/conjugate base pair or a weak base/conjugate acid pair. All other options contain weak acid-conjugate base pairs.

  Q8. When a small amount of NaOH is added to a buffer of NH3/NH4+, what reaction occurs?

  Q10. If you need to prepare a buffer at pH 9.25, which weak acid/conjugate base pair would be most appropriate? (Ka values: acetic acid = 1.8 x 10^-5, NH4+ = 5.6 x 10^-10, HCN = 6.2 x 10^-10)

    Explanation: A buffer is most effective when pH = pKa. The NH4+/NH3 system has pKa = 9.25, which exactly matches the target pH. While HCN/CN- (pKa = 9.21) is close, NH4+/NH3 is the better choice since its pKa matches precisely.

  Q11. What does the term 'buffer capacity' mean?
    [ ] The pH at which the buffer operates
    [*] The amount of strong acid or base a buffer can absorb before its pH changes significantly
    [ ] The volume of the buffer solution
    [ ] The pKa of the weak acid in the buffer
    Explanation: Buffer capacity is a quantitative measure of how much strong acid or strong base can be added to a buffer solution before the pH changes by a significant amount (typically more than 1 unit). Higher concentrations of the buffer components increase buffer capacity.

  Q12. What does the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation describe?
    [*] The relationship between pH, pKa, and the ratio of conjugate base to weak acid concentrations
    [ ] The total concentration of all species in a buffer
    [ ] The relationship between temperature and pH
    [ ] The rate at which buffers neutralize acids
    Explanation: The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, pH = pKa + log([A-]/[HA]), relates the pH of a buffer solution to the pKa of the weak acid and the ratio of the concentrations of the conjugate base [A-] to the weak acid [HA].

  Q13. What does the term 'conjugate acid-base pair' mean?
    [ ] Two substances with the same Ka value
    [ ] Any acid and base that react together
    [*] Two substances that differ by one proton (H+)
    [ ] A strong acid paired with a strong base
    Explanation: A conjugate acid-base pair consists of two species that differ by exactly one proton (H+). For example, CH3COOH (acetic acid) and CH3COO- (acetate ion) are a conjugate pair; the acid has one more H+ than its conjugate base.

  Q14. What does 'pKa' mean?
    [ ] The concentration of H+ ions in a 1 M acid solution
    [ ] The pH at which an acid is completely dissociated
    [*] The negative base-10 logarithm of the acid dissociation constant Ka
    [ ] The percentage of acid molecules that ionize
    Explanation: pKa = -log(Ka), where Ka is the acid dissociation constant. A smaller pKa indicates a stronger acid (larger Ka, greater tendency to donate protons), while a larger pKa indicates a weaker acid.

  Q15. What does the term 'common ion effect' mean in the context of buffer solutions?
    [*] The shift in equilibrium that occurs when a salt containing an ion common to the weak electrolyte is added
    [ ] The increase in dissociation when more water is added
    [ ] The effect of temperature on equilibrium
    [ ] The precipitation that occurs when two ionic solutions are mixed
    Explanation: The common ion effect describes the suppression of ionization of a weak electrolyte when a strong electrolyte sharing a common ion is added. For example, adding sodium acetate (which provides CH3COO-) to acetic acid suppresses its ionization, a principle central to how buffers work.

  Q16. What does the term 'buffer range' mean?
    [ ] The concentration range of the weak acid component
    [ ] The total volume of buffer that can be prepared
    [*] The pH range over which a buffer effectively resists pH changes, typically pKa +/- 1
    [ ] The range of temperatures at which a buffer works
    Explanation: The buffer range is the pH interval over which the buffer effectively maintains pH stability. This is typically within +/- 1 pH unit of the pKa of the weak acid, corresponding to a [A-]/[HA] ratio between 0.1 and 10.

  Q17. What does the phrase 'the buffer has been overwhelmed' mean?
    [ ] The temperature has exceeded the buffer's effective range
    [*] So much acid or base has been added that one buffer component is essentially consumed, and the buffer can no longer resist pH changes
    [ ] The buffer solution has become too concentrated
    [ ] The buffer has been diluted to the point where its pH changes
    Explanation: A buffer is overwhelmed when enough strong acid or base is added to essentially consume one of its components (either the weak acid or its conjugate base). Once one component is depleted, the solution can no longer resist pH changes and behaves like a simple acid or base solution.

  Q18. What does 'physiological buffer' mean?
    [*] A buffer system that operates in living organisms to maintain pH within a narrow range necessary for life
    [ ] A buffer used only in medical laboratories
    [ ] A buffer that only works at room temperature
    [ ] A buffer made exclusively from organic compounds
    Explanation: A physiological buffer is a buffer system that functions within a living organism. The carbonic acid/bicarbonate system in blood, the phosphate buffer system in cells, and the protein buffer system are all examples that maintain the narrow pH ranges required for biological processes.

  Q19. What does the term 'alkalosis' mean in the context of blood buffering?
    [ ] A condition where blood pH falls below 7.35
    [ ] A condition where blood pH is exactly 7.40
    [ ] A condition where the blood buffer is overwhelmed by acid
    [*] A condition where blood pH rises above 7.45
    Explanation: Alkalosis is a medical condition in which blood pH rises above the normal range of 7.35-7.45. It can be caused by hyperventilation (respiratory alkalosis) or excess bicarbonate (metabolic alkalosis). The opposite condition, acidosis, occurs when blood pH drops below 7.35.

  Q20. What does the term 'acidosis' mean in the context of blood buffering?
    [*] A condition where blood pH drops below 7.35
    [ ] A condition where blood pH rises above 7.45
    [ ] A condition where lactic acid is produced during exercise
    [ ] A condition where the stomach produces too much acid
    Explanation: Acidosis is a condition in which the blood pH falls below the normal range of 7.35-7.45. It can result from excess CO2 buildup (respiratory acidosis) or accumulation of metabolic acids like lactic acid (metabolic acidosis). Both types can be life-threatening if severe.

  Q21. A biochemist needs to prepare a buffer at pH 5.00 using acetic acid (Ka = 1.8 x 10^-5, pKa = 4.74) and sodium acetate. What should the ratio of [CH3COO-]/[CH3COOH] be?
    [ ] 1.00
    [ ] 2.50
    [ ] 0.55
    [*] 1.82
    Explanation: Using Henderson-Hasselbalch: 5.00 = 4.74 + log([A-]/[HA]). log([A-]/[HA]) = 0.26. [A-]/[HA] = 10^0.26 = 1.82. The conjugate base must be at nearly twice the concentration of the weak acid to achieve pH 5.00.

  Q22. A student prepares a buffer by mixing 100.0 mL of 0.200 M acetic acid with 100.0 mL of 0.150 M sodium acetate (pKa = 4.74). What is the pH of this buffer?
    [ ] 4.92
    [ ] 4.74
    [ ] 5.00
    [*] 4.62
    Explanation: After mixing, moles HA = 0.200 x 0.100 = 0.0200 mol, moles A- = 0.150 x 0.100 = 0.0150 mol. pH = 4.74 + log(0.0150/0.0200) = 4.74 + log(0.75) = 4.74 + (-0.125) = 4.62. The pH is below 4.74 because there is more weak acid than conjugate base.

  Q23. A hospital pharmacist prepares a phosphate buffer (H2PO4-/HPO4^2-, pKa2 = 7.20) for an IV solution at pH 7.40. She has 0.100 M NaH2PO4 and 0.100 M Na2HPO4. What volume ratio of Na2HPO4 to NaH2PO4 should she use?
    [ ] 0.63 : 1.00
    [*] 1.58 : 1.00
    [ ] 2.00 : 1.00
    [ ] 1.00 : 1.00
    Explanation: Using Henderson-Hasselbalch: 7.40 = 7.20 + log([HPO4^2-]/[H2PO4-]). log(ratio) = 0.20, so ratio = 10^0.20 = 1.58. Since both stock solutions are the same concentration (0.100 M), the volume ratio of Na2HPO4 to NaH2PO4 is 1.58:1.00.

  Q24. A researcher adds 0.010 mol of HCl to 1.00 L of a buffer containing 0.100 mol CH3COOH and 0.100 mol CH3COO- (pKa = 4.74). What is the new pH?
    [*] 4.65
    [ ] 2.00
    [ ] 4.83
    [ ] 4.74
    Explanation: The HCl reacts with A-: 0.010 mol H+ converts 0.010 mol A- to HA. New moles: A- = 0.100 - 0.010 = 0.090 mol; HA = 0.100 + 0.010 = 0.110 mol. pH = 4.74 + log(0.090/0.110) = 4.74 + log(0.818) = 4.74 - 0.087 = 4.65. The pH changed by only 0.09 units, demonstrating the buffer's effectiveness.

  Q25. A student has two buffer solutions, both at pH 4.74. Buffer A contains 1.00 M acetic acid and 1.00 M sodium acetate. Buffer B contains 0.010 M acetic acid and 0.010 M sodium acetate. Which has greater buffer capacity?
    [ ] Neither; buffer capacity depends only on pKa
    [*] Buffer A, because it has higher concentrations of both buffer components
    [ ] Buffer B, because it is more dilute
    [ ] They have equal buffer capacity because they have the same pH
    Explanation: Buffer capacity depends on the total concentrations of the buffer components, not just the pH. Buffer A has 100 times more moles of both acetic acid and acetate ion per liter, so it can neutralize 100 times more strong acid or base before its pH changes significantly.

  Q26. A biologist is studying an enzyme that functions optimally at pH 7.4. During a reaction, the enzyme produces 0.005 mol of H+ ions in 500 mL of a buffer containing 0.050 mol NaH2PO4 and 0.050 mol Na2HPO4 (pKa = 7.20). Will the buffer maintain the enzyme near its optimal pH?
    [ ] No, the pH will drop below 6.0
    [ ] No, the buffer will be overwhelmed
    [*] Yes, the pH will change to approximately 7.02, still within a functional range
    [ ] Yes, the pH will not change at all
    Explanation: The 0.005 mol H+ reacts with HPO4^2-: new HPO4^2- = 0.050 - 0.005 = 0.045 mol; new H2PO4- = 0.050 + 0.005 = 0.055 mol. pH = 7.20 + log(0.045/0.055) = 7.20 + log(0.818) = 7.20 - 0.087 = 7.11. This is close enough to pH 7.4 to keep the enzyme somewhat functional, though not ideal. The buffer prevented a catastrophic pH drop.

  Q27. A chemistry student wants to prepare 500 mL of an NH3/NH4Cl buffer at pH 9.50 (pKa of NH4+ = 9.25). If the total buffer concentration ([NH3] + [NH4+]) is to be 0.200 M, how many moles of each component are needed?
    [ ] 0.036 mol NH3 and 0.064 mol NH4Cl
    [ ] 0.080 mol NH3 and 0.020 mol NH4Cl
    [ ] 0.050 mol NH3 and 0.050 mol NH4Cl
    [*] 0.064 mol NH3 and 0.036 mol NH4Cl
    Explanation: Using Henderson-Hasselbalch: 9.50 = 9.25 + log([NH3]/[NH4+]). log(ratio) = 0.25, ratio = 10^0.25 = 1.78. Total moles = 0.200 x 0.500 = 0.100 mol. If [NH3] = 1.78x and [NH4+] = x, then 2.78x = 0.100, x = 0.036 mol NH4+. NH3 = 0.100 - 0.036 = 0.064 mol. So 0.064 mol NH3 and 0.036 mol NH4Cl are needed.

  Q28. An environmental scientist is analyzing lake water and finds it naturally buffered at pH 6.4 by the carbonic acid/bicarbonate system (H2CO3/HCO3-, pKa1 = 6.35). Acid rain adds 0.001 mol H+ per liter. If the lake's total buffer concentration is 0.002 M, what happens?
    [ ] The lake becomes more alkaline
    [ ] The buffer easily handles the acid rain with minimal pH change
    [*] The buffer is overwhelmed because the acid added exceeds the buffer's capacity
    [ ] The pH rises above 7
    Explanation: The total buffer concentration is only 0.002 M, meaning HCO3- is at most about 0.001 M (roughly half the total). Adding 0.001 mol/L of H+ would consume all the available HCO3-, overwhelming the buffer. The lake's buffering capacity is too low to handle this acid load, and the pH would drop significantly.

  Q29. A chemical engineer needs to maintain the pH of a fermentation tank at 4.00. She chooses between a formic acid buffer (HCOOH/HCOO-, pKa = 3.75) and a benzoic acid buffer (C6H5COOH/C6H5COO-, pKa = 4.20). Which is the better choice and why?
    [ ] Either buffer works equally well
    [*] Benzoic acid, because its pKa (4.20) is closer to pH 4.00
    [ ] Neither buffer is appropriate for this pH
    [ ] Formic acid, because its pKa (3.75) is closer to pH 4.00
    Explanation: Benzoic acid (pKa = 4.20) is only 0.20 pH units away from the target pH of 4.00, while formic acid (pKa = 3.75) is 0.25 units away. Both are within the effective buffer range (pKa +/- 1), but benzoic acid is the better choice because its pKa is closer to the target pH, providing greater buffer capacity at that pH.

  Q30. A patient with severe diarrhea loses large amounts of bicarbonate (HCO3-). Given that the blood buffer system is H2CO3/HCO3- (pKa = 6.10) and normal blood pH is 7.40, how does this loss affect blood pH and the [HCO3-]/[H2CO3] ratio?
    [ ] Blood pH increases; the ratio increases
    [ ] Blood pH increases; the ratio decreases
    [*] Blood pH decreases; the [HCO3-]/[H2CO3] ratio decreases
    [ ] Blood pH stays the same; the body compensates immediately
    Explanation: Loss of HCO3- decreases the numerator in the [HCO3-]/[H2CO3] ratio, which reduces the log term in the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (pH = 6.10 + log([HCO3-]/[H2CO3])). A lower ratio means a lower pH, causing metabolic acidosis. The normal ratio is about 20:1; any decrease shifts blood pH downward.

--- Lesson 8.5: Polyprotic Acids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Polyprotic Acids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the first ionization product of carbonic acid (H2CO3)?
The first ionization of carbonic acid produces bicarbonate and a proton: H2CO3 -> HCO3- + H+. The second ionization then produces carbonate: HCO3- -> CO3^2- + H+.
A student dissolves 0.10 M H2CO3 in water (Ka1 = 4.3 x 10^-7, Ka2 = 4.7 x 10^-11). What is the approximate [H+] from the first ionization only?
For the first ionization: Ka1 = x^2/0.10 (assuming x << 0.10). x^2 = 4.3 x 10^-8, x = sqrt(4.3 x 10^-8) = 2.1 x 10^-4 M. This is the [H+] from the first ionization, which dominates the pH calculation.
A biochemist needs to determine the charge on the amino acid glycine (pKa1 = 2.34 for -COOH, pKa2 = 9.60 for -NH3+) at pH 7.4. What is the predominant form?
At pH 7.4, which is between pKa1 (2.34) and pKa2 (9.60), the carboxyl group is deprotonated (COO-) and the amino group is protonated (NH3+). This gives the zwitterion form H3N+CH2COO- with a net charge of zero. The isoelectric point is (2.34 + 9.60)/2 = 5.97.
A pharmaceutical company needs to calculate the pH of a 0.10 M NaHCO3 solution. Given that H2CO3 has Ka1 = 4.3 x 10^-7 and Ka2 = 4.7 x 10^-11, what is the approximate pH?
For an amphoteric species like NaHCO3, the pH is approximately (pKa1 + pKa2)/2 = (-log(4.3 x 10^-7) + -log(4.7 x 10^-11))/2 = (6.37 + 10.33)/2 = 16.70/2 = 8.35, approximately 8.3. This formula works when the concentration of the amphoteric species is much greater than Ka1 and Ka2.
A student is asked to calculate the isoelectric point of aspartic acid, which has pKa1 = 2.10 (-COOH on alpha carbon), pKa2 = 3.86 (-COOH on side chain), and pKa3 = 9.82 (-NH3+). What is the pI?
For aspartic acid (an acidic amino acid), the isoelectric point is the average of the two lowest pKa values (both carboxyl groups): pI = (pKa1 + pKa2)/2 = (2.10 + 3.86)/2 = 2.98. For acidic amino acids, pI uses the two acidic pKa values; for basic amino acids, pI uses the two highest pKa values.

--- Lesson 8.5: Polyprotic Acids (Quiz) ---

  Q1. How many ionizable hydrogen atoms does phosphoric acid (H3PO4) have?
    [ ] 4
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 2
    [*] 3
    Explanation: Phosphoric acid (H3PO4) is a triprotic acid with three ionizable hydrogen atoms. It can lose protons in three successive steps, producing H2PO4-, HPO4^2-, and PO4^3-.

  Q2. For a polyprotic acid, how do the successive Ka values compare?
    [ ] Ka1 < Ka2 < Ka3
    [ ] Ka1 = Ka2 = Ka3
    [*] Ka1 > Ka2 > Ka3
    [ ] The relationship varies depending on the acid
    Explanation: For polyprotic acids, Ka1 > Ka2 > Ka3 because it is progressively harder to remove a proton from an increasingly negatively charged species. Each successive ionization encounters greater electrostatic attraction.

  Q3. Which of the following is a diprotic acid?
    [*] H2SO4
    [ ] HNO3
    [ ] H3PO4
    [ ] HCl
    Explanation: Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) is a diprotic acid, meaning it has two ionizable hydrogen atoms. HCl and HNO3 are monoprotic (one H+), while H3PO4 is triprotic (three H+).

  Q4. What is the first ionization product of carbonic acid (H2CO3)?
    [ ] H2CO3^2-
    [*] HCO3- and H+
    [ ] CO2 and H2O
    [ ] CO3^2-
    Explanation: The first ionization of carbonic acid produces bicarbonate and a proton: H2CO3 -> HCO3- + H+. The second ionization then produces carbonate: HCO3- -> CO3^2- + H+.

  Q5. For H2SO4, the first ionization is strong (complete). What does this mean for Ka1?
    [ ] Ka1 equals Ka2
    [ ] Ka1 is exactly 1.0
    [ ] Ka1 is very small
    [*] Ka1 is very large (essentially infinity)
    Explanation: Since the first ionization of H2SO4 is complete (it is a strong acid in its first ionization), Ka1 is extremely large (effectively infinite). The second ionization (Ka2 = 1.2 x 10^-2) is much weaker but still relatively strong.

  Q6. How many equivalence points appear on the titration curve of a triprotic acid titrated with NaOH?
    [*] 3
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 4
    Explanation: A triprotic acid has three ionizable protons, so when titrated with a strong base, it shows three equivalence points, one for each proton removed. However, if two Ka values are very close, the equivalence points may merge and be difficult to distinguish.

  Q7. The intermediate species HCO3- (bicarbonate) can act as which of the following?
    [ ] Only an acid
    [*] Both an acid and a base (amphoteric)
    [ ] Only a base
    [ ] Neither an acid nor a base
    Explanation: Bicarbonate (HCO3-) is amphoteric. It can donate a proton to act as an acid (HCO3- -> CO3^2- + H+) or accept a proton to act as a base (HCO3- + H+ -> H2CO3). This dual behavior is characteristic of intermediate species of polyprotic acids.

  Q8. When calculating the pH of a polyprotic acid solution, which ionization step usually dominates?
    [*] The first ionization
    [ ] The last ionization
    [ ] All ionizations contribute equally
    [ ] The second ionization
    Explanation: The first ionization dominates the pH calculation because Ka1 is much larger than Ka2 (and Ka3). The H+ produced from the first ionization suppresses the second ionization through Le Chatelier's principle, making its contribution to [H+] negligible.

  Q9. For a diprotic acid H2A with Ka1 = 1.0 x 10^-3 and Ka2 = 1.0 x 10^-7, at what pH does [H2A] = [HA-]?
    [ ] 10.0
    [ ] 7.0
    [*] 3.0
    [ ] 5.0
    Explanation: When [H2A] = [HA-], the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation gives pH = pKa1 = -log(1.0 x 10^-3) = 3.0. Similarly, [HA-] = [A^2-] occurs at pH = pKa2 = 7.0.

  Q10. Which amino acid property makes them polyprotic?
    [ ] They contain multiple carbon atoms, even when conditions shift
    [ ] They form peptide bonds, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] They dissolve in water, which is a common assumption in the field
    [*] They contain both an ionizable amino group (-NH3+) and a carboxyl group (-COOH)
    Explanation: Amino acids are polyprotic because they contain at least two ionizable groups: an amino group (-NH3+, which can donate a proton) and a carboxyl group (-COOH, which can also donate a proton). Some amino acids have additional ionizable side chains, making them triprotic.

  Q11. What does the term 'polyprotic acid' mean?
    [ ] An acid that reacts with multiple bases simultaneously
    [ ] An acid that has multiple atoms in its formula
    [*] An acid that can donate more than one proton (H+) per molecule
    [ ] An acid that ionizes completely in water
    Explanation: A polyprotic acid is an acid that has more than one ionizable proton per molecule. Examples include diprotic acids (H2SO4, H2CO3) with two protons and triprotic acids (H3PO4) with three protons.

  Q12. What does the term 'amphoteric' mean when describing a substance?
    [ ] A substance that reacts violently with acids
    [ ] A substance that has a neutral pH
    [ ] A substance that is insoluble in water
    [*] A substance that can act as both an acid and a base
    Explanation: An amphoteric substance can act as either an acid or a base depending on the conditions. Intermediate species from polyprotic acids (like HCO3- and H2PO4-) are amphoteric because they can either donate or accept a proton.

  Q13. What does 'Ka2' mean for a diprotic acid?
    [*] The equilibrium constant for the second ionization step
    [ ] The overall equilibrium constant for both ionization steps
    [ ] The equilibrium constant at double the concentration
    [ ] Twice the value of Ka1
    Explanation: Ka2 is the acid dissociation constant for the second ionization step of a polyprotic acid. For example, for H2CO3: Ka2 refers to HCO3- -> H+ + CO3^2-. It is always smaller than Ka1 because removing a proton from a negatively charged species is more difficult.

  Q14. What does the term 'zwitterion' mean?
    [*] A molecule that carries both a positive and negative charge simultaneously
    [ ] A molecule that is always negatively charged
    [ ] A molecule that cannot ionize
    [ ] A molecule with no net charge
    Explanation: A zwitterion is a molecule that has both a positive and a negative charge at the same time, resulting in a net charge of zero. Amino acids at their isoelectric point exist as zwitterions, with the amino group protonated (-NH3+) and the carboxyl group deprotonated (-COO-).

  Q15. What does the term 'isoelectric point (pI)' mean?
    [ ] The pH where the acid is fully ionized
    [ ] The point where two acids have equal Ka values
    [ ] The temperature at which a compound melts
    [*] The pH at which a molecule carries no net electric charge
    Explanation: The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which a polyprotic molecule (such as an amino acid) carries no net electric charge. For a simple amino acid, pI = (pKa1 + pKa2)/2, where the two pKa values correspond to the ionizable groups.

  Q16. What does 'stepwise ionization' mean for a polyprotic acid?
    [ ] The acid loses all its protons at once in a single step
    [*] The acid loses its protons one at a time in sequential equilibrium steps
    [ ] The acid requires a catalyst to ionize
    [ ] The acid ionizes only at specific temperatures
    Explanation: Stepwise ionization means that a polyprotic acid loses its protons one at a time, with each step having its own equilibrium constant (Ka1, Ka2, Ka3). For example, H3PO4 first loses one H+ to form H2PO4-, then H2PO4- loses another H+ to form HPO4^2-, and so on.

  Q17. What does 'overall Ka' mean for a polyprotic acid?
    [*] The product of all stepwise Ka values (Ka1 x Ka2 x Ka3)
    [ ] The average of all Ka values
    [ ] The sum of Ka1 + Ka2 + Ka3
    [ ] The largest Ka value
    Explanation: The overall equilibrium constant for complete ionization of a polyprotic acid is the product of all stepwise Ka values: Ka(overall) = Ka1 x Ka2 x Ka3. This follows from the principle that when equilibrium expressions are added, their equilibrium constants are multiplied.

  Q18. What does the term 'predominant species' mean in a polyprotic acid system?
    [ ] The strongest acid in the system
    [*] The form of the acid or its ions that has the highest concentration at a given pH
    [ ] The species with the largest Ka value
    [ ] The species that reacts fastest
    Explanation: The predominant species is the form of the polyprotic acid system that exists in the highest concentration at a particular pH. For H3PO4: H3PO4 predominates at very low pH, H2PO4- at moderate pH, HPO4^2- at higher pH, and PO4^3- at very high pH.

  Q19. What does 'alpha fraction' mean in polyprotic acid chemistry?
    [ ] The first ionization step of the acid
    [ ] The ratio of Ka1 to Ka2
    [*] The fraction of total acid that exists in a particular ionization form at a given pH
    [ ] The percentage of acid that has not ionized
    Explanation: The alpha fraction represents the fraction of the total analytical concentration of the acid that exists in a particular protonation state at a given pH. For example, for H2CO3, alpha-0 is the fraction as H2CO3, alpha-1 is the fraction as HCO3-, and alpha-2 is the fraction as CO3^2-.

  Q20. What does the term 'triprotic acid' mean?
    [ ] An acid with three atoms
    [ ] An acid that ionizes in three seconds
    [ ] An acid that requires three moles of base to neutralize one mole
    [*] An acid that can donate three protons per molecule
    Explanation: A triprotic acid has three ionizable protons per molecule. Phosphoric acid (H3PO4) and citric acid (C6H8O7) are common examples. Each proton is removed in a separate ionization step with its own Ka value.

  Q21. A student dissolves 0.10 M H2CO3 in water (Ka1 = 4.3 x 10^-7, Ka2 = 4.7 x 10^-11). What is the approximate [H+] from the first ionization only?
    [ ] 4.7 x 10^-11 M
    [ ] 4.3 x 10^-7 M
    [ ] 6.5 x 10^-2 M
    [*] 2.1 x 10^-4 M
    Explanation: For the first ionization: Ka1 = x^2/0.10 (assuming x << 0.10). x^2 = 4.3 x 10^-8, x = sqrt(4.3 x 10^-8) = 2.1 x 10^-4 M. This is the [H+] from the first ionization, which dominates the pH calculation.

  Q22. A solution contains 0.10 M H3PO4 (Ka1 = 7.5 x 10^-3, Ka2 = 6.2 x 10^-8, Ka3 = 4.8 x 10^-13). At equilibrium, the concentration of PO4^3- is approximately equal to:
    [ ] Ka1 x Ka2 x Ka3
    [*] Ka3
    [ ] 0.10 M
    [ ] Ka1
    Explanation: For a triprotic acid, it can be shown that [A^3-] is approximately equal to Ka3 when Ka1 >> Ka2 >> Ka3. This is because [HA^2-] is approximately equal to Ka2, and when substituted into the Ka3 expression with [H+] approximately equal to [H2A-], the third ionization product concentration simplifies to approximately Ka3.

  Q23. A biochemist needs to determine the charge on the amino acid glycine (pKa1 = 2.34 for -COOH, pKa2 = 9.60 for -NH3+) at pH 7.4. What is the predominant form?
    [ ] H2NCH2COOH (net charge 0, uncharged)
    [ ] H2NCH2COO- (net charge -1)
    [ ] H3N+CH2COOH (net charge +1)
    [*] H3N+CH2COO- (zwitterion, net charge 0)
    Explanation: At pH 7.4, which is between pKa1 (2.34) and pKa2 (9.60), the carboxyl group is deprotonated (COO-) and the amino group is protonated (NH3+). This gives the zwitterion form H3N+CH2COO- with a net charge of zero. The isoelectric point is (2.34 + 9.60)/2 = 5.97.

  Q24. A water treatment engineer encounters a solution at pH 10.0 containing the phosphate system (pKa1 = 2.15, pKa2 = 7.20, pKa3 = 12.35). Which species is present in the highest concentration?
    [ ] H3PO4
    [ ] PO4^3-
    [*] HPO4^2-
    [ ] H2PO4-
    Explanation: At pH 10.0, the pH falls between pKa2 (7.20) and pKa3 (12.35). In this range, HPO4^2- is the predominant species. H2PO4- predominates between pKa1 and pKa2, and PO4^3- predominates above pKa3.

  Q25. A researcher titrates 50.0 mL of 0.100 M H2SO3 (Ka1 = 1.5 x 10^-2, Ka2 = 6.3 x 10^-8) with 0.100 M NaOH. How much NaOH is needed to reach the second equivalence point?
    [*] 100.0 mL
    [ ] 150.0 mL
    [ ] 50.0 mL
    [ ] 75.0 mL
    Explanation: H2SO3 is diprotic, so 2 moles of NaOH are needed per mole of H2SO3. Moles H2SO3 = 0.100 x 0.0500 = 0.00500 mol. Moles NaOH needed = 2 x 0.00500 = 0.0100 mol. Volume = 0.0100/0.100 = 0.100 L = 100.0 mL.

  Q26. A food scientist analyzes citric acid (H3C6H5O7, triprotic, Ka1 = 7.4 x 10^-4, Ka2 = 1.7 x 10^-5, Ka3 = 4.0 x 10^-7) in lemon juice. At pH 3.13 (= pKa1), what are the relative concentrations of H3Cit and H2Cit-?
    [ ] [H3Cit] >> [H2Cit-]
    [ ] [H3Cit] << [H2Cit-]
    [ ] Both are negligible compared to HCit^2-
    [*] [H3Cit] = [H2Cit-]
    Explanation: At pH = pKa1, the concentrations of the fully protonated form (H3Cit) and the first deprotonated form (H2Cit-) are exactly equal. This follows directly from the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: when pH = pKa, log([A-]/[HA]) = 0, so [A-] = [HA].

  Q27. A pharmaceutical company needs to calculate the pH of a 0.10 M NaHCO3 solution. Given that H2CO3 has Ka1 = 4.3 x 10^-7 and Ka2 = 4.7 x 10^-11, what is the approximate pH?
    [ ] 7.6
    [*] 8.3
    [ ] 11.0
    [ ] 10.3
    Explanation: For an amphoteric species like NaHCO3, the pH is approximately (pKa1 + pKa2)/2 = (-log(4.3 x 10^-7) + -log(4.7 x 10^-11))/2 = (6.37 + 10.33)/2 = 16.70/2 = 8.35, approximately 8.3. This formula works when the concentration of the amphoteric species is much greater than Ka1 and Ka2.

  Q28. An environmental chemist finds that a lake has a total carbonate alkalinity of 2.0 x 10^-3 M at pH 8.3. Using Ka1 = 4.3 x 10^-7 and Ka2 = 4.7 x 10^-11 for carbonic acid, which species dominates the alkalinity?
    [ ] CO3^2-
    [ ] H2CO3
    [*] HCO3-
    [ ] Equal amounts of all three
    Explanation: At pH 8.3, which falls between pKa1 (6.37) and pKa2 (10.33), bicarbonate (HCO3-) is the dominant species. At this pH, HCO3- accounts for over 99% of the total carbonate system. H2CO3 predominates below pH 6.37, and CO3^2- above pH 10.33.

  Q29. A clinical lab technician analyzes a blood sample and needs to understand how phosphate buffers work in cells. Given H3PO4 (pKa1 = 2.15, pKa2 = 7.20, pKa3 = 12.35), which phosphate buffer pair is most relevant at the intracellular pH of 7.2?
    [ ] H3PO4 / H2PO4-
    [*] H2PO4- / HPO4^2-
    [ ] HPO4^2- / PO4^3-
    [ ] H3PO4 / PO4^3-
    Explanation: At intracellular pH of 7.2, which equals pKa2 (7.20), the H2PO4-/HPO4^2- pair is the most effective buffer. A buffer is most effective when pH = pKa, and pKa2 = 7.20 is exactly at the intracellular pH, making this pair ideal for intracellular buffering.

  Q30. A student is asked to calculate the isoelectric point of aspartic acid, which has pKa1 = 2.10 (-COOH on alpha carbon), pKa2 = 3.86 (-COOH on side chain), and pKa3 = 9.82 (-NH3+). What is the pI?
    [ ] 6.86
    [ ] 3.86
    [ ] 5.96
    [*] 2.98
    Explanation: For aspartic acid (an acidic amino acid), the isoelectric point is the average of the two lowest pKa values (both carboxyl groups): pI = (pKa1 + pKa2)/2 = (2.10 + 3.86)/2 = 2.98. For acidic amino acids, pI uses the two acidic pKa values; for basic amino acids, pI uses the two highest pKa values.

--- Lesson 8.5: Polyprotic Acids (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$
