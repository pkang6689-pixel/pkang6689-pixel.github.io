--- Lesson 6.1: Energy and Heat (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy and Heat
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI unit of energy?
The joule (J) is the SI unit of energy. 1 calorie = 4.184 joules.
What is the first law of thermodynamics?
The first law of thermodynamics (conservation of energy) states that the total energy of the universe is constant; energy can be transferred or converted between forms but not created or destroyed.
What is the specific heat capacity of liquid water?
The specific heat capacity of liquid water is 4.184 J/(g*C), which is unusually high compared to most substances.
What is the sign of q for an exothermic process?
For an exothermic process, the system releases heat, so q is negative (q < 0) from the system's perspective.
What is the relationship between calories and joules?
One calorie equals exactly 4.184 joules. The food Calorie (capital C) is actually a kilocalorie (1 kcal = 4184 J).
A hiker uses a hand warmer containing iron powder. When exposed to air, the iron oxidizes and the pack becomes warm. If the pack releases 20 kJ of heat, what is the sign of q for the iron/air system?
The system (iron oxidation reaction) releases 20 kJ of heat to the surroundings (the hiker's hands), so q = -20 kJ from the system's perspective.
An instant cold pack used for sports injuries contains ammonium nitrate and water. When the inner bag is broken, the temperature drops dramatically. What type of process is this, and what is the sign of q?
Dissolving ammonium nitrate in water is endothermic (absorbs heat), so q is positive for the system. The surroundings (and the pack surface) become cold.
A chemist heats 50.0 g of an unknown metal from 25.0 to 75.0 degrees C using 1125 J of energy. What is the specific heat of the metal?
c = q/(m*DeltaT) = 1125/(50.0 x 50.0) = 1125/2500 = 0.450 J/(g*C). This is close to the specific heat of iron.

--- Lesson 6.1: Energy and Heat (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is the SI unit of energy?
    [*] Joule
    [ ] Watt
    [ ] Newton

    Explanation: The joule (J) is the SI unit of energy. 1 calorie = 4.184 joules.

  Q2. Which of the following is an exothermic process?
    [ ] Dissolving ammonium nitrate in water
    [ ] Melting ice
    [ ] Evaporation of water
    [*] Combustion of methane
    Explanation: Combustion is an exothermic process that releases heat to the surroundings, making the surroundings warmer.

  Q3. In an endothermic reaction, what happens to the temperature of the surroundings?
    [ ] It increases
    [ ] It fluctuates
    [ ] It remains the same
    [*] It decreases
    Explanation: In an endothermic reaction, heat flows from the surroundings into the system, causing the surroundings to cool down.

  Q4. What is the first law of thermodynamics?
    [ ] All reactions release energy
    [*] Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or converted
    [ ] Entropy always increases
    [ ] Energy always flows from cold to hot
    Explanation: The first law of thermodynamics (conservation of energy) states that the total energy of the universe is constant; energy can be transferred or converted between forms but not created or destroyed.

  Q5. Heat flows spontaneously from:
    [ ] Objects of the same temperature
    [ ] Cold objects to hot objects
    [ ] In any direction equally
    [*] Hot objects to cold objects
    Explanation: Heat spontaneously flows from regions of higher temperature to regions of lower temperature until thermal equilibrium is reached.

  Q6. What is the specific heat capacity of liquid water?
    [ ] 0.449 J/(g*C)
    [*] 4.184 J/(g*C)
    [ ] 1.00 J/(g*C)
    [ ] 2.09 J/(g*C)
    Explanation: The specific heat capacity of liquid water is 4.184 J/(g*C), which is unusually high compared to most substances.

  Q7. How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 100 g of water by 10 degrees C? (c = 4.184 J/g/C)
    [ ] 41.84 J
    [ ] 418.4 J
    [ ] 41840 J
    [*] 4184 J
    Explanation: q = mcDeltaT = 100 x 4.184 x 10 = 4184 J.

  Q8. What distinguishes heat from temperature?
    [ ] Heat is always constant in a system, especially during periods of significant change
    [*] Heat is energy transferred due to a temperature difference; temperature measures average kinetic energy
    [ ] Temperature is measured in joules, as conventional energy theory would suggest
    [ ] They are the same thing, even when conditions shift significantly
    Explanation: Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles, while heat (q) is the transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures.

  Q9. What is the sign of q for an exothermic process?
    [ ] It depends on the reaction
    [ ] Zero
    [*] Negative
    [ ] Positive
    Explanation: For an exothermic process, the system releases heat, so q is negative (q < 0) from the system's perspective.

  Q10. What is the relationship between calories and joules?
    [*] 1 cal = 4.184 J
    [ ] 1 cal = 1 J
    [ ] 1 J = 4.184 cal
    [ ] 1 cal = 10 J
    Explanation: One calorie equals exactly 4.184 joules. The food Calorie (capital C) is actually a kilocalorie (1 kcal = 4184 J).

  Q11. What does 'exothermic' mean?
    [ ] A process that absorbs heat from the surroundings
    [ ] A process that requires a catalyst
    [ ] A process that occurs without heat exchange
    [*] A process that releases heat to the surroundings
    Explanation: Exothermic means 'heat out' -- the system releases thermal energy to its surroundings, resulting in a temperature increase of the surroundings.

  Q12. What does 'endothermic' mean?
    [ ] A process that stops spontaneously
    [ ] A process that releases heat to the surroundings
    [ ] A process that generates light
    [*] A process that absorbs heat from the surroundings
    Explanation: Endothermic means 'heat in' -- the system absorbs thermal energy from its surroundings, causing the surroundings to cool.

  Q13. What does 'specific heat capacity' mean?
    [ ] The heat released during combustion
    [ ] The total heat content of a substance
    [*] The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree C
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance changes phase
    Explanation: Specific heat capacity (c) is the amount of heat energy needed to raise the temperature of one gram of a substance by one degree Celsius (or one kelvin).

  Q14. What does 'system' mean in thermodynamics?
    [*] The part of the universe being studied
    [ ] The entire universe
    [ ] The laboratory equipment used
    [ ] The atoms that do not participate in the reaction
    Explanation: In thermodynamics, the system is the specific part of the universe being studied (e.g., the chemicals in a reaction), while everything else is the surroundings.

  Q15. What does 'surroundings' mean in thermodynamics?
    [*] Everything outside the system that can exchange energy with it
    [ ] The products of the reaction
    [ ] Only the air directly around the reaction vessel
    [ ] The vacuum surrounding the universe
    Explanation: The surroundings include everything external to the system that can exchange energy (heat or work) with it.

  Q16. What does 'thermal energy' mean?
    [ ] Energy stored in chemical bonds
    [ ] The potential energy of a substance at height
    [*] The total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance due to their random motion
    [ ] Energy from the sun
    Explanation: Thermal energy is the total kinetic energy associated with the random motion of all atoms and molecules in a substance; it depends on both temperature and the amount of substance.

  Q17. What does 'heat capacity' mean?
    [ ] The rate at which heat is transferred
    [ ] The maximum temperature a substance can reach
    [ ] The ability of a substance to store chemical energy
    [*] The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of an object by 1 degree C
    Explanation: Heat capacity (C) is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of an entire object (not per gram) by one degree Celsius.

  Q18. What does 'internal energy' mean?
    [ ] The energy used to start a reaction
    [*] The total of all kinetic and potential energies of the particles within a system
    [ ] The energy released during a phase change
    [ ] The energy of a system due to its position
    Explanation: Internal energy (U or E) is the sum of all kinetic energies (translational, rotational, vibrational) and potential energies (intermolecular and intramolecular) of the particles in a system.

  Q19. What does 'thermal equilibrium' mean?
    [ ] When all molecular motion stops
    [*] When two objects in contact reach the same temperature and no net heat flows between them
    [ ] When the system has zero energy
    [ ] When a reaction reaches equilibrium
    Explanation: Thermal equilibrium is reached when two objects in thermal contact have the same temperature, so there is no net transfer of heat between them.

  Q20. What does 'work' mean in thermodynamics?
    [ ] Only chemical bond changes
    [*] Energy transferred by a force acting through a distance or by pressure-volume changes
    [ ] The energy stored in a battery
    [ ] The heat absorbed by the system
    Explanation: In thermodynamics, work (w) is energy transferred through organized motion, such as gas expansion against external pressure (w = -PDeltaV), as opposed to heat which is disorganized energy transfer.

  Q21. A student touches the outside of a beaker during a reaction and finds it feels cold. What type of reaction is occurring inside?
    [ ] Exothermic, because it releases heat to the beaker
    [ ] Neither; the beaker is naturally cold
    [*] Endothermic, because it absorbs heat from the surroundings including the beaker
    [ ] Exothermic, because it makes the student's hand cold
    Explanation: A cold beaker indicates the reaction is absorbing heat from its surroundings (endothermic), drawing thermal energy from the beaker and the student's hand.

  Q22. A hiker uses a hand warmer containing iron powder. When exposed to air, the iron oxidizes and the pack becomes warm. If the pack releases 20 kJ of heat, what is the sign of q for the iron/air system?

  Q24. A swimmer notices that sand on the beach gets much hotter than the ocean water on a sunny day, even though both receive the same amount of sunlight. Which concept explains this?

  Q25. An instant cold pack used for sports injuries contains ammonium nitrate and water. When the inner bag is broken, the temperature drops dramatically. What type of process is this, and what is the sign of q?
    [ ] Isothermal; q is zero
    [*] Endothermic; q is positive

  Q26. A chemist heats 50.0 g of an unknown metal from 25.0 to 75.0 degrees C using 1125 J of energy. What is the specific heat of the metal?
    [ ] 0.225 J/(g*C)
    [ ] 2.25 J/(g*C)
    [*] 0.450 J/(g*C)
    [ ] 0.900 J/(g*C)

  Q27. During a controlled explosion in a bomb calorimeter, the temperature of 2000 g of water rises from 22.0 to 28.5 degrees C. How much heat was released by the reaction? (Ignore the heat capacity of the calorimeter.)
    [ ] 27,400 J

    [ ] 108,800 J
    [ ] 13,700 J

  Q28. Two identical cups of coffee are poured. One has cream added immediately, the other has cream added after 5 minutes. Assuming the same total cream is added, which cup is warmer after 5 minutes?
    [ ] The cup with cream added after 5 minutes
    [ ] Both are the same temperature
    [ ] It depends on the type of cream
    [*] The cup with cream added immediately
    Explanation: Adding cream immediately lowers the initial temperature, reducing the temperature difference with the environment and slowing heat loss. The other cup, being hotter for longer, loses more heat to surroundings.

  Q29. A student mixes 100 mL of 1.0 M HCl with 100 mL of 1.0 M NaOH in a coffee-cup calorimeter. The temperature rises from 22.0 to 28.8 degrees C. Assuming the solution has the density and specific heat of water, how much heat was released?
    [ ] 2840 J
    [ ] 11,380 J
    [*] 5690 J
    [ ] 1420 J
    Explanation: Total mass = 200 g (assuming density of water). q = mcDeltaT = 200 x 4.184 x 6.8 = 5690 J. The neutralization reaction released about 5690 J of heat.

  Q30. A power plant converts chemical energy from burning coal into electricity. If the coal releases 1000 kJ of energy but only 350 kJ becomes electricity, what happened to the remaining 650 kJ?
    [ ] It was converted to mass
    [ ] It was stored in the coal for later use
    [*] It was converted to heat dissipated to the surroundings (waste heat)
    [ ] It was destroyed according to the second law
    Explanation: By the first law of thermodynamics, energy is conserved. The 650 kJ not converted to electricity becomes waste heat dissipated to the environment through cooling towers and exhaust.

--- Lesson 6.2: Enthalpy and Calorimetry (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Enthalpy and Calorimetry
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is enthalpy (H)?
Enthalpy (H) is defined as H = U + PV, where U is internal energy, P is pressure, and V is volume. At constant pressure, the change in enthalpy equals the heat transferred.
For an exothermic reaction, what is the sign of Delta H?
For an exothermic reaction, the system releases heat, so the enthalpy of products is lower than reactants, making Delta H negative.
What is the standard enthalpy of formation (Delta Hf) of an element in its standard state?
By convention, the standard enthalpy of formation of any element in its most stable form at standard conditions (1 atm, 25 C) is defined as zero.
What is the relationship between q_system and q_surroundings in calorimetry?
By conservation of energy, heat lost by the system equals heat gained by the surroundings: q_system = -q_surroundings.
A student dissolves 4.0 g of NaOH (MW = 40) in 100 mL of water in a calorimeter and measures a temperature increase of 10.0 degrees C. What is the approximate molar enthalpy of solution?
q = mcDeltaT = 104 x 4.184 x 10.0 = 4351 J (using approximate total mass of 104 g). Moles NaOH = 4.0/40 = 0.10 mol. Molar Delta H = -4351/0.10 = -43,510 J/mol = -43.5 kJ/mol, approximately -44.5 kJ/mol.
In a coffee-cup calorimeter, 50.0 mL of 2.0 M HCl is mixed with 50.0 mL of 2.0 M NaOH. The temperature rises by 13.7 degrees C. What is the enthalpy of neutralization per mole of water formed?
q = 100 g x 4.184 x 13.7 = 5732 J. Moles HCl (or NaOH) = 0.050 L x 2.0 = 0.10 mol. Delta H = -5732/0.10 = -57,320 J/mol = -57.3 kJ/mol.
A student's calorimetry data shows a temperature decrease when a salt is dissolved in water. The student concludes the process is endothermic. Is this correct, and what is the sign of Delta H?
A temperature decrease in the water (surroundings) means heat flowed from the surroundings into the system (dissolving salt). This is endothermic, so Delta H is positive.

--- Lesson 6.2: Enthalpy and Calorimetry (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is enthalpy (H)?
    [ ] The temperature of a reaction, particularly in the context of enthalpy
    [ ] The kinetic energy of molecules according to widely held views on this topic
    [*] A thermodynamic quantity equal to the internal energy plus pressure times volume (H = U + PV)
    [ ] The entropy of a system, even when conditions shift significantly
    Explanation: Enthalpy (H) is defined as H = U + PV, where U is internal energy, P is pressure, and V is volume. At constant pressure, the change in enthalpy equals the heat transferred.

  Q2. For an exothermic reaction, what is the sign of Delta H?
    [ ] Positive
    [ ] Zero
    [ ] It depends on the temperature
    [*] Negative
    Explanation: For an exothermic reaction, the system releases heat, so the enthalpy of products is lower than reactants, making Delta H negative.

  Q3. What does a coffee-cup calorimeter measure?
    [ ] The activation energy
    [ ] The rate of reaction
    [ ] Heat flow at constant volume
    [*] Heat flow at constant pressure
    Explanation: A coffee-cup calorimeter operates at atmospheric (constant) pressure, so it directly measures the enthalpy change (Delta H) of a reaction.

  Q4. A bomb calorimeter operates at constant:
    [ ] Temperature
    [ ] Pressure
    [ ] Entropy
    [*] Volume
    Explanation: A bomb calorimeter is a sealed, rigid container, so it operates at constant volume and measures the internal energy change (Delta U) rather than Delta H directly.

  Q5. What is the standard enthalpy of formation (Delta Hf) of an element in its standard state?
    [ ] A large negative value
    [ ] Different for each element
    [*] Zero by definition
    [ ] A large positive value
    Explanation: By convention, the standard enthalpy of formation of any element in its most stable form at standard conditions (1 atm, 25 C) is defined as zero.

  Q6. Using standard enthalpies of formation, how is Delta H_rxn calculated?
    [*] Sum of Delta Hf(products) - Sum of Delta Hf(reactants)
    [ ] Product of Delta Hf(products) x Delta Hf(reactants)
    [ ] Sum of all Delta Hf values
    [ ] Sum of Delta Hf(reactants) - Sum of Delta Hf(products)
    Explanation: Delta H_rxn = Sum(n x Delta Hf products) - Sum(n x Delta Hf reactants), where n represents the stoichiometric coefficients.

  Q7. When a reaction is reversed, what happens to Delta H?
    [ ] It stays the same, despite what intuition might
    [*] It changes sign (positive becomes negative or vice versa)
    [ ] It becomes zero according to widely held views
    [ ] It doubles under most circumstances involving Delta
    Explanation: When a reaction is reversed, the sign of Delta H changes. If the forward reaction has Delta H = -100 kJ, the reverse has Delta H = +100 kJ.

  Q8. If the coefficients in a balanced equation are doubled, what happens to Delta H?
    [ ] It changes sign
    [ ] It stays the same
    [*] It is doubled
    [ ] It is halved
    Explanation: Enthalpy is an extensive property; multiplying the stoichiometric coefficients by a factor also multiplies Delta H by the same factor.

  Q9. What type of reaction absorbs heat from the surroundings?
    [ ] Isothermal
    [ ] Adiabatic
    [ ] Exothermic
    [*] Endothermic
    Explanation: An endothermic reaction absorbs heat from the surroundings, resulting in a positive Delta H value.

  Q10. What is the relationship between q_system and q_surroundings in calorimetry?
    [ ] They are unrelated
    [*] q_system = -q_surroundings
    [ ] q_system + q_surroundings = 1
    [ ] q_system = q_surroundings
    Explanation: By conservation of energy, heat lost by the system equals heat gained by the surroundings: q_system = -q_surroundings.

  Q11. What does 'enthalpy change' (Delta H) mean?
    [*] The heat absorbed or released by a reaction at constant pressure
    [ ] The change in temperature during a reaction
    [ ] The change in equilibrium constant
    [ ] The change in reaction rate
    Explanation: The enthalpy change (Delta H) is the heat transferred to or from a system during a reaction at constant pressure, equal to q_p.

  Q12. What does 'calorimetry' mean?
    [ ] The study of calories in food
    [ ] The study of color changes in reactions
    [*] The experimental measurement of heat changes in chemical or physical processes
    [ ] The measurement of gas volumes
    Explanation: Calorimetry is the science of measuring heat transfer during chemical reactions or physical changes using a device called a calorimeter.

  Q13. What does 'standard enthalpy of formation' mean?
    [ ] The enthalpy change when a compound decomposes
    [*] The enthalpy change when elements in their standard states form one mole of a compound
    [ ] The enthalpy of any reaction at standard conditions
    [ ] The enthalpy of combustion at standard conditions
    Explanation: Standard enthalpy of formation (Delta Hf) is the enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its constituent elements in their standard states.

  Q14. What does 'state function' mean?
    [ ] A property that is always constant
    [ ] A property that depends on the path taken to reach the state
    [ ] A function that describes the state of matter
    [*] A property that depends only on the current state of the system, not on how it got there
    Explanation: A state function depends only on the current state of the system (initial and final conditions), not on the path taken. Enthalpy, internal energy, and entropy are state functions.

  Q15. What does 'standard conditions' mean in thermochemistry?
    [ ] 0 degrees C and 1 atm
    [ ] 100 degrees C and 1 atm
    [*] 25 degrees C (298 K) and 1 atm with 1 M concentration for solutions
    [ ] Any temperature at 1 atm
    Explanation: Standard conditions in thermochemistry are 25 degrees C (298 K), 1 atm pressure, and 1 M concentration for solutions. Note: this differs from STP in gas law problems (0 degrees C, 1 atm).

  Q16. What does 'enthalpy of combustion' mean?
    [ ] The enthalpy of any exothermic reaction
    [*] The enthalpy change when a substance burns completely in oxygen
    [ ] The enthalpy change when oxygen is produced
    [ ] The heat needed to ignite a substance
    Explanation: The enthalpy of combustion is the enthalpy change when one mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion with oxygen under standard conditions. It is always negative.

  Q17. What does 'molar enthalpy' mean?
    [ ] The total enthalpy of all molecules in a sample
    [*] The enthalpy change per mole of a substance reacted or formed
    [ ] The enthalpy at the melting point
    [ ] The average enthalpy of a mixture
    Explanation: Molar enthalpy is the enthalpy change expressed per mole of substance, allowing comparison of heat effects for different quantities of reactants.

  Q18. What does 'calorimeter constant' mean?
    [ ] The temperature of the calorimeter before an experiment
    [ ] A fixed numerical constant used in all calorimetry
    [*] The heat capacity of the calorimeter itself, measuring how much heat it absorbs per degree of temperature change
    [ ] The specific heat of water
    Explanation: The calorimeter constant is the heat capacity of the calorimeter apparatus itself, measured in J/C, which accounts for heat absorbed by the calorimeter components.

  Q19. What does 'enthalpy of neutralization' mean?
    [ ] The enthalpy change when a neutral substance is formed
    [ ] The enthalpy of a pH 7 solution
    [ ] The enthalpy change when ions form a precipitate
    [*] The enthalpy change when an acid reacts with a base to form water and a salt
    Explanation: The enthalpy of neutralization is the enthalpy change when an acid and base react to form one mole of water. For strong acid-strong base reactions, it is approximately -57.1 kJ/mol.

  Q20. What does 'heat of solution' mean?
    [*] The enthalpy change when a solute dissolves in a solvent
    [ ] The temperature of a solution
    [ ] The heat needed to boil a solution
    [ ] The specific heat of a liquid
    Explanation: The heat of solution (enthalpy of solution) is the enthalpy change when a solute dissolves in a solvent; it can be positive (endothermic) or negative (exothermic).

  Q21. A student dissolves 4.0 g of NaOH (MW = 40) in 100 mL of water in a calorimeter and measures a temperature increase of 10.0 degrees C. What is the approximate molar enthalpy of solution?

  Q22. A nutritionist burns a 2.0 g sample of a candy bar in a bomb calorimeter containing 1500 g of water. The temperature rises by 3.2 degrees C. The calorimeter constant is 850 J/C. How much energy is in the candy sample?

  Q23. Calculate the standard enthalpy of reaction for: 2C2H6(g) + 7O2(g) -> 4CO2(g) + 6H2O(l). Given: Delta Hf(C2H6) = -84.7, Delta Hf(CO2) = -393.5, Delta Hf(H2O) = -285.8 kJ/mol.

--- Lesson 6.3: Hess's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hess's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Given: (1) A -> B, Delta H = -100 kJ; (2) B -> C, Delta H = +50 kJ. What is Delta H for A -> C?
By Hess's law: Delta H(A -> C) = Delta H(A -> B) + Delta H(B -> C) = -100 + 50 = -50 kJ.
Given: C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g), Delta H = -393.5 kJ; CO(g) + 1/2 O2(g) -> CO2(g), Delta H = -283.0 kJ. What is Delta H for C(s) + 1/2 O2(g) -> CO(g)?
Use equation 1 as written and reverse equation 2: CO2 -> CO + 1/2 O2, Delta H = +283.0. Adding: C + O2 + CO2 -> CO2 + CO + 1/2 O2. Simplify: C + 1/2 O2 -> CO. Delta H = -393.5 + 283.0 = -110.5 kJ.
A student needs to find Delta H for the reaction: C(graphite) + 2H2(g) -> CH4(g). Given: (1) C(graphite) + O2(g) -> CO2(g), Delta H = -393.5 kJ; (2) H2(g) + 1/2 O2(g) -> H2O(l), Delta H = -285.8 kJ; (3) CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(l), Delta H = -890.4 kJ. What is Delta H for the target reaction?
Use (1) as written + 2x(2) + reverse(3): -393.5 + 2(-285.8) + 890.4 = -393.5 - 571.6 + 890.4 = -74.7 kJ.
An engineer wants to know the enthalpy of formation of ethanol but cannot measure it directly. Using combustion data: Delta Hc(C2H5OH) = -1367 kJ/mol, Delta Hc(C) = -393.5, Delta Hc(H2) = -285.8, what is Delta Hf(C2H5OH)?
C2H5OH is 2C + 3H2 + 1/2 O2. Delta Hf = 2(-393.5) + 3(-285.8) - (-1367) = -787 - 857.4 + 1367 = -277.4 kJ/mol, approximately -277.6 kJ/mol.
A materials scientist needs Delta H for: 2Fe(s) + 3/2 O2(g) -> Fe2O3(s). Given: (1) Fe2O3(s) + 3CO(g) -> 2Fe(s) + 3CO2(g), Delta H = -26.7 kJ; (2) CO(g) + 1/2 O2(g) -> CO2(g), Delta H = -283.0 kJ. What is Delta H?
Reverse (1): Delta H = +26.7 kJ. Multiply (2) by 3: Delta H = 3(-283.0) = -849.0 kJ. Sum: 26.7 + (-849.0) + reverse gives 2Fe + 3CO2 + 3CO + 3/2 O2 -> Fe2O3 + 3CO2. Net: 2Fe + 3/2 O2 -> Fe2O3. Delta H = 26.7 - 849.0 = -822.3 kJ.
Consider: (1) N2(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO(g), Delta H = +180.6 kJ; (2) 2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g), Delta H = -113.1 kJ. What is Delta H for N2(g) + 2O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)?
Add (1) + (2): N2 + O2 + 2NO + O2 -> 2NO + 2NO2. Cancel 2NO: N2 + 2O2 -> 2NO2. Delta H = 180.6 + (-113.1) = +67.5 kJ.
A chemical company produces calcium oxide (quicklime). Using: CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g), Delta Hf(CaCO3) = -1206.9 kJ/mol, Delta Hf(CaO) = -635.1 kJ/mol, Delta Hf(CO2) = -393.5 kJ/mol. What is Delta H for the decomposition?
Delta H = [Delta Hf(CaO) + Delta Hf(CO2)] - Delta Hf(CaCO3) = [-635.1 + (-393.5)] - (-1206.9) = -1028.6 + 1206.9 = +178.3 kJ. The decomposition is endothermic.
Given the Born-Haber cycle for NaCl: Na(s) -> Na(g), Delta H = +108 kJ; Na(g) -> Na+(g) + e-, Delta H = +496 kJ; 1/2 Cl2(g) -> Cl(g), Delta H = +122 kJ; Cl(g) + e- -> Cl-(g), Delta H = -349 kJ; Na+(g) + Cl-(g) -> NaCl(s), Delta H = ? If Delta Hf(NaCl) = -411 kJ, what is the lattice energy?
By Hess's law: -411 = 108 + 496 + 122 + (-349) + lattice energy. -411 = 377 + LE. LE = -411 - 377 = -788 kJ.
A researcher wants Delta H for the hypothetical reaction: 3C(graphite) + 4H2(g) -> C3H8(g). Using: Delta Hf(C3H8) = -103.8 kJ/mol. What is the answer?
The reaction 3C(graphite) + 4H2(g) -> C3H8(g) IS the formation reaction for propane. By definition, its Delta H is the standard enthalpy of formation = -103.8 kJ.
A student needs to find Delta H for: SO2(g) + 1/2 O2(g) -> SO3(g). Given: (1) S(s) + O2(g) -> SO2(g), Delta H = -296.8 kJ; (2) S(s) + 3/2 O2(g) -> SO3(g), Delta H = -395.7 kJ. What is the answer?
Reverse (1) and add to (2): SO2 -> S + O2, Delta H = +296.8. Then S + 3/2 O2 -> SO3, Delta H = -395.7. Net: SO2 + 1/2 O2 -> SO3. Delta H = 296.8 - 395.7 = -98.9 kJ.
Real-World Applications
Example: When you multiply a reaction by a coefficient to apply Hess's law, what do you do to Delta H?
Since enthalpy is extensive, multiplying all coefficients by a factor n also multiplies Delta H by n.

--- Lesson 6.4: Bond Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Bond Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The C-F bond (485 kJ/mol) is stronger than the C-H bond (413 kJ/mol), making Teflon more chemically inert and heat-resistant than polyethylene.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'average bond energy' mean?
Average bond energy is the mean dissociation energy for a given bond type (e.g., O-H) averaged across many different molecules, since the exact bond energy varies with molecular environment.
Example: What does 'homolytic cleavage' mean?
In homolytic cleavage, a covalent bond breaks symmetrically so that each atom retains one of the shared electrons, forming two free radicals.
Example: What does 'heterolytic cleavage' mean?
In heterolytic cleavage, a bond breaks unevenly so that both shared electrons go to one atom, forming a cation and an anion.

    [ ] Form a bond between two atoms
    [ ] Ionize a gaseous atom
    [*] Break one mole of bonds in the gas phase
    [ ] Melt an ionic compound
    Explanation: Bond energy (or bond dissociation energy) is the energy required to break one mole of a specific covalent bond in gaseous molecules into gaseous atoms or fragments.

  Q2. Is breaking a bond exothermic or endothermic?
    [ ] It depends on the type of bond
    [*] Endothermic
    [ ] Neither
    [ ] Exothermic
    Explanation: Breaking bonds always requires energy input (endothermic). Forming bonds releases energy (exothermic).

    [ ] Delta H = bonds formed + bonds broken
    [*] Delta H = energy of bonds broken - energy of bonds formed
    [ ] Delta H = bonds broken / bonds formed
    Explanation: Delta H = sum of bond energies broken (positive, endothermic) - sum of bond energies formed (negative, exothermic). If more energy is released than absorbed, the reaction is exothermic.

  Q4. Which bond is generally stronger: a single bond, double bond, or triple bond between the same two atoms?
    [*] Triple bond
    [ ] Single bond
    [ ] All are equally strong
    [ ] Double bond
    Explanation: Triple bonds are the strongest, followed by double, then single bonds. More shared electron pairs create a stronger attraction between the bonded atoms.

  Q5. The bond energy of C-H is 413 kJ/mol. How much energy is needed to break all four C-H bonds in one mole of CH4?
    [*] 1652 kJ
    [ ] 2065 kJ
    [ ] 826 kJ
    [ ] 413 kJ
    Explanation: CH4 has 4 C-H bonds. Total energy to break all bonds = 4 x 413 = 1652 kJ.

  Q6. Why are bond energy calculations only estimates of Delta H?
    [*] Bond energies apply only to gases, and real reactions may involve liquids and solids
    [ ] Bond energies do not obey conservation of energy
    [ ] Bonds do not have consistent energies
    [ ] Bond energies are measured at absolute zero
    Explanation: Bond energies are average values for gas-phase molecules. Real reactions may involve condensed phases, and bond energies in different molecular environments vary slightly, so calculations are approximations.

  Q7. Which of these bonds has the highest bond energy: C-C, C=C, or C (triple bond) C?
    [*] C triple bond C (839 kJ/mol)
    [ ] They are all the same
    [ ] C=C (614 kJ/mol)
    [ ] C-C (347 kJ/mol)
    Explanation: The triple bond between carbons (839 kJ/mol) is the strongest, followed by the double bond (614 kJ/mol), then the single bond (347 kJ/mol).

  Q8. As bond length decreases, what typically happens to bond energy?
    [ ] Bond energy stays the same
    [*] Bond energy increases
    [ ] Bond energy decreases
    [ ] There is no relationship
    Explanation: Shorter bonds are typically stronger bonds. Multiple bonds (which are shorter) have higher bond energies than single bonds between the same atoms.

  Q9. When bonds are formed, energy is:
    [ ] Absorbed from the surroundings
    [ ] Destroyed
    [ ] Neither absorbed nor released
    [*] Released to the surroundings
    Explanation: Bond formation is always exothermic; energy is released when atoms come together and form a stable chemical bond.

  Q10. A reaction is exothermic when:
    [ ] More energy is absorbed breaking bonds than is released forming bonds
    [ ] Equal energy is absorbed and released
    [ ] No bonds are broken
    [*] More energy is released forming bonds than is absorbed breaking bonds
    Explanation: An exothermic reaction releases net energy because the energy released in forming product bonds exceeds the energy required to break reactant bonds.

  Q11. What does 'bond energy' mean?
    [ ] The energy released during a combustion reaction
    [*] The average energy required to break one mole of a particular type of bond in gaseous molecules
    [ ] The energy needed to form one mole of a bond
    [ ] The energy of all bonds in a molecule
    Explanation: Bond energy (bond dissociation energy) is the average energy required to break one mole of a specific type of covalent bond in gaseous molecules, measured in kJ/mol.

  Q12. What does 'bond dissociation energy' mean?
    [ ] The energy released when a bond dissociates
    [*] The energy required to homolytically break a specific bond in a particular molecule in the gas phase
    [ ] The energy of the weakest bond in a molecule
    [ ] The energy of ionic bond formation
    Explanation: Bond dissociation energy specifically refers to the energy needed to break one particular bond in a specific molecule homolytically (each atom gets one electron), producing two radicals.

  Q13. What does 'average bond energy' mean?
    [ ] The bond energy of the most common bond
    [ ] The energy of one bond in an average molecule
    [ ] Half of the bond energy
    [*] The mean value of bond dissociation energies for the same type of bond across many different molecules
    Explanation: Average bond energy is the mean dissociation energy for a given bond type (e.g., O-H) averaged across many different molecules, since the exact bond energy varies with molecular environment.

  Q14. What does 'bond length' mean?
    [ ] The circumference of a bond
    [ ] The length of the electron cloud
    [ ] The duration of a bond
    [*] The distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms
    Explanation: Bond length is the equilibrium distance between the nuclei of two covalently bonded atoms, typically measured in picometers (pm) or angstroms.

  Q15. What does 'bond order' mean?
    [*] The number of bonding electron pairs shared between two atoms
    [ ] The sequence in which bonds are formed
    [ ] The energy of the bond
    [ ] The number of bonds in a molecule
    Explanation: Bond order is the number of shared electron pairs between two atoms (1 for single, 2 for double, 3 for triple bond). Higher bond order means shorter, stronger bonds.

  Q16. What does 'endothermic bond breaking' mean?
    [ ] Breaking bonds releases energy to the environment
    [ ] Breaking bonds has no energy cost
    [ ] Only ionic bonds require energy to break
    [*] Energy must be supplied to separate bonded atoms
    Explanation: Breaking any chemical bond requires an input of energy (endothermic process) to overcome the attractive forces holding the atoms together.

  Q17. What does 'exothermic bond formation' mean?
    [ ] Only covalent bond formation releases energy
    [ ] Bond formation is energy neutral
    [*] Energy is released when atoms come together to form a chemical bond
    [ ] Forming bonds requires energy input
    Explanation: When atoms form bonds, they move to a lower energy state, releasing the difference as heat or light (exothermic). This is the reverse of the energy needed to break the same bond.

  Q18. What does 'bond strength' mean?
    [ ] How many bonds an atom can form
    [*] A measure of how much energy is required to break a chemical bond
    [ ] The number of electrons in a bond
    [ ] How quickly a bond can be broken
    Explanation: Bond strength is a measure of how difficult it is to break a bond, quantified by the bond dissociation energy. Stronger bonds have higher bond energies.

  Q19. What does 'homolytic cleavage' mean?
    [ ] Two bonds break simultaneously
    [ ] A bond breaks to form ions
    [*] A bond breaks so that each atom retains one electron, forming two radicals
    [ ] A bond breaks so that one atom gets both electrons
    Explanation: In homolytic cleavage, a covalent bond breaks symmetrically so that each atom retains one of the shared electrons, forming two free radicals.

  Q20. What does 'heterolytic cleavage' mean?
    [ ] Two different types of bonds break
    [ ] A triple bond becomes a single bond
    [ ] A bond breaks equally between two atoms
    [*] A bond breaks so that both electrons go to one atom, forming ions
    Explanation: In heterolytic cleavage, a bond breaks unevenly so that both shared electrons go to one atom, forming a cation and an anion.

  Q21. Using bond energies, estimate Delta H for: CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O. Bond energies: C-H = 413, O=O = 498, C=O = 799, O-H = 463 kJ/mol.
    [ ] -1636 kJ
    [ ] +818 kJ
    [ ] -409 kJ
    [*] -818 kJ
    Explanation: Bonds broken: 4(C-H) + 2(O=O) = 4(413) + 2(498) = 1652 + 996 = 2648 kJ. Bonds formed: 2(C=O) + 4(O-H) = 2(799) + 4(463) = 1598 + 1852 = 3450 kJ. Delta H = 2648 - 3450 = -802 kJ, approximately -818 kJ with slightly different table values.

  Q22. The N-N single bond has an energy of 163 kJ/mol, while the N triple bond has an energy of 941 kJ/mol. A researcher claims that three N-N single bonds are equivalent to one N triple bond. Is this correct?
    [ ] Yes, bond energies are additive, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Yes, 3 x 163 = 489 which equals 941/2, particularly in the context of bond
    [ ] No, but they are approximately equal, which is a common assumption in the field
    [*] No, 3 x 163 = 489 kJ, which is much less than 941 kJ; the triple bond is much stronger than three single bonds
    Explanation: 3 x 163 = 489 kJ, which is far less than 941 kJ. A triple bond is much stronger than three isolated single bonds because the pi bonds in a triple bond benefit from the close proximity of the bonded atoms.

  Q23. A fuel cell engineer compares H2 and CH4 as fuels. Using bond energies (H-H = 436, C-H = 413, O=O = 498, C=O = 799, O-H = 463), estimate which fuel releases more energy per gram when combusted.
    [ ] Cannot be determined from bond energies
    [ ] Both release exactly the same per gram
    [*] H2 releases more energy per gram
    [ ] CH4 releases more energy per gram
    Explanation: H2 combustion: Delta H per mol = (436 + 249) - 2(463) = -241 kJ/mol. Per gram of H2 (MW=2): -241/2 = -120.5 kJ/g. CH4: approximately -802 kJ/mol, per gram (MW=16): -802/16 = -50.1 kJ/g. H2 releases much more energy per gram.

  Q24. A student calculates Delta H for a reaction using bond energies and gets -350 kJ. The literature value from calorimetry is -370 kJ. Why is there a discrepancy?
    [ ] Bond energies only apply to ionic compounds
    [*] Bond energies are average values and the calculation assumes gas-phase species, leading to approximate results
    [ ] Calorimetry measurements are always wrong
    [ ] The student made a mathematical error
    Explanation: Bond energy calculations use average values from many molecules, and they assume gas-phase conditions. These approximations lead to estimates that may differ from precise calorimetric measurements.

  Q25. Why is the combustion of hydrocarbons always exothermic? Consider bond energies: C-H = 413, C-C = 347, O=O = 498, C=O = 799, O-H = 463 kJ/mol.
    [ ] Because combustion does not break any bonds in most scenarios involving bonds factors
    [ ] Because C-H bonds are very weak, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Because O=O bonds are very weak, especially during periods of significant change
    [*] Because the very strong C=O and O-H bonds formed in CO2 and H2O release more energy than is needed to break reactant bonds
    Explanation: The very strong C=O (799 kJ/mol) and O-H (463 kJ/mol) bonds formed in CO2 and H2O release a large amount of energy, exceeding the energy needed to break the weaker C-H, C-C, and O=O bonds.

  Q26. Using bond energies, estimate Delta H for: N2 + 3H2 -> 2NH3. Bond energies: N triple bond = 941, H-H = 436, N-H = 391 kJ/mol.
    [ ] +93 kJ
    [ ] +186 kJ
    [ ] -186 kJ
    [*] -93 kJ
    Explanation: Bonds broken: 1(N triple bond) + 3(H-H) = 941 + 3(436) = 941 + 1308 = 2249. Bonds formed: 6(N-H) = 6(391) = 2346. Delta H = 2249 - 2346 = -97 kJ, approximately -93 kJ.

  Q27. An explosives expert explains that TNT is unstable because its products (N2, CO2, H2O) have very strong bonds. Using the concept of bond energies, why does this make TNT explosive?
    [ ] The reactant bonds are stronger than the product bonds
    [ ] The explosion occurs without breaking any bonds
    [*] The product bonds (N triple bond, C=O, O-H) are much stronger than the bonds in TNT, releasing enormous energy when formed
    [ ] TNT has no chemical bonds
    Explanation: The products (N2 with a 941 kJ/mol triple bond, CO2, H2O) have extremely strong bonds. The enormous energy released when these strong bonds form greatly exceeds the energy needed to break the weaker bonds in TNT.

  Q28. A chemistry student argues that since O=O has a bond energy of 498 kJ/mol, it takes 498 kJ to split O2, but forming two O-H bonds (2 x 463 = 926 kJ) releases much more. Does this mean O2 + H -> 2OH is exothermic?
    [ ] The student's logic is completely correct under most circumstances involving forming
    [*] The analysis is incomplete; we must also account for breaking the H-H bond (436 kJ) and forming two O-H bonds, giving a correct overall assessment
    [ ] Yes, because bond formation exceeds bond breaking, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] No, because breaking always exceeds forming, particularly in the context of forming
    Explanation: The student's comparison is incomplete. The proper analysis for H2 + 1/2 O2 -> H2O must include breaking H-H and 1/2 O=O (436 + 249 = 685 kJ) and forming 2 O-H (926 kJ), giving Delta H = -241 kJ.

  Q29. A polymer scientist compares polyethylene (C-C and C-H bonds) with Teflon (C-C and C-F bonds). Given C-F = 485 kJ/mol and C-H = 413 kJ/mol, which polymer has stronger bonds and is more chemically resistant?
    [ ] Polyethylene, because C-H bonds are more common
    [*] Teflon, because C-F bonds (485 kJ/mol) are stronger than C-H bonds (413 kJ/mol)
    [ ] Neither has significant chemical resistance
    [ ] Both are equally resistant
    Explanation: The C-F bond (485 kJ/mol) is stronger than the C-H bond (413 kJ/mol), making Teflon more chemically inert and heat-resistant than polyethylene.

  Q30. Using bond energies, estimate Delta H for the hydrogenation of ethene: C2H4 + H2 -> C2H6. Bond energies: C=C = 614, C-C = 347, H-H = 436, C-H = 413 kJ/mol.
    [*] -125 kJ
    [ ] -250 kJ
    [ ] +250 kJ
    [ ] +125 kJ
    Explanation: Bonds broken: C=C (614) + H-H (436) = 1050 kJ. Bonds formed: C-C (347) + 2(C-H) = 347 + 826 = 1173 kJ. Delta H = 1050 - 1173 = -123 kJ, approximately -125 kJ.

--- Lesson 6.4: Bond Energy (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$
