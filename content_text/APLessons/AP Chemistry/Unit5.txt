--- Lesson 5.1: Reaction Rates (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Reaction Rates
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the typical unit for measuring the rate of a chemical reaction in solution?
Reaction rates in solution are typically measured as the change in concentration per unit time, with units of mol/L/s or M/s.
For the reaction A -> B, if the concentration of A decreases from 0.80 M to 0.60 M in 10 seconds, what is the average rate?
Average rate = change in concentration / change in time = (0.80 - 0.60) / 10 = 0.20/10 = 0.020 M/s.
For the reaction 2A -> B, if the rate of disappearance of A is 0.10 M/s, what is the rate of appearance of B?
Since 2 mol A produces 1 mol B, the rate of appearance of B is half the rate of disappearance of A: 0.10/2 = 0.05 M/s.
What is a reaction rate?
Reaction rate is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time, measuring how fast a reaction proceeds.
Real-World Applications
Example: For the reaction A -> B, if the concentration of A decreases from 0.80 M to 0.60 M in 10 seconds, what is the average rate?
Average rate = change in concentration / change in time = (0.80 - 0.60) / 10 = 0.20/10 = 0.020 M/s.
Example: What does 'average rate' mean in the context of chemical kinetics?
The average rate is calculated over a finite time interval by dividing the total change in concentration by the elapsed time.
Example: A food processing company notices that fruit stored at 4 degrees C spoils much more slowly than fruit at 25 degrees C. Based on kinetics, approximately how many times slower might the spoilage reactions be at 4 degrees C?
A common rule of thumb is that reaction rates roughly double for every 10 degrees C increase. With a 21-degree difference, the rate would be approximately 2^2 to 2^2.1 = 4-8 times slower at the lower temperature.
Example: An enzyme in the human body speeds up the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide by a factor of 10^7. Without the enzyme, the reaction would take years. This enzyme is an example of what type of rate-affecting factor?
The enzyme catalase is a biological catalyst that dramatically lowers the activation energy for H2O2 decomposition, increasing the rate by a factor of about 10 million.

--- Lesson 5.2: Rate Laws and Order (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Rate Laws and Order
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the general form of a rate law for the reaction A + B -> Products?
The rate law has the form Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n, where k is the rate constant and m and n are the reaction orders determined experimentally.
What is the overall order of a reaction with rate law: Rate = k[A]^2[B]?
The overall reaction order is the sum of all individual orders: 2 + 1 = 3. This is a third-order reaction overall.
What is the half-life expression for a first-order reaction?
For a first-order reaction, the half-life t1/2 = ln(2)/k = 0.693/k. Notably, it is independent of the initial concentration.
A plot of ln[A] vs time gives a straight line. What is the order of the reaction?
A linear plot of ln[A] vs time indicates first-order kinetics, based on the integrated rate law: ln[A] = ln[A]0 - kt.
A plot of 1/[A] vs time gives a straight line. What is the order of the reaction?
A linear plot of 1/[A] vs time indicates second-order kinetics, based on the integrated rate law: 1/[A] = 1/[A]0 + kt.
What is a rate law?
A rate law is an equation that expresses the rate of a reaction as a function of the concentrations of reactants, each raised to a specific power (the order).
A researcher collects the following data: when [A] = 0.1 M, rate = 0.005 M/s; when [A] = 0.2 M, rate = 0.020 M/s. What is the order of the reaction with respect to A?
Doubling [A] from 0.1 to 0.2 M quadruples the rate (0.005 to 0.020). Since 2^n = 4, n = 2. The reaction is second-order with respect to A.
A pharmaceutical company studies drug decomposition. The plot of ln[drug] vs time is linear with a slope of -0.023 hr^-1. What is the half-life of the drug?
The linear ln[A] vs time plot confirms first-order kinetics with k = 0.023 hr^-1. Half-life = 0.693/k = 0.693/0.023 = 30.1 hours.
A student uses the method of initial rates with these results: Trial 1: [A]=0.10, [B]=0.10, rate=0.01; Trial 2: [A]=0.20, [B]=0.10, rate=0.02; Trial 3: [A]=0.10, [B]=0.20, rate=0.04. What is the rate law?
Comparing trials 1 and 2: doubling [A] doubles the rate, so order in A = 1. Comparing trials 1 and 3: doubling [B] quadruples the rate, so order in B = 2. Rate = k[A][B]^2.
An environmental scientist monitors the decomposition of a pesticide in soil. After plotting [pesticide] vs time, a straight line results. What is the order, and what does the slope represent?
A linear [A] vs time plot indicates zero-order kinetics. The integrated rate law is [A] = [A]0 - kt, so the slope is -k.
A chemical engineer studies a reaction where the rate law is Rate = k[A]^2. The initial concentration is 0.40 M and k = 0.50 M^-1s^-1. What is the concentration of A after 10 seconds?
For second-order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]0 + kt = 1/0.40 + (0.50)(10) = 2.5 + 5.0 = 7.5. Therefore [A] = 1/7.5 = 0.133 M.

    [ ] 4
    [ ] 2
    [*] 3

    Explanation: The overall reaction order is the sum of all individual orders: 2 + 1 = 3. This is a third-order reaction overall.

    [ ] Rate is proportional to concentration squared
    [ ] Rate decreases with concentration
    [ ] Rate increases with concentration
    [*] Rate is independent of concentration

    [ ] From the molar masses of reactants

    [ ] From the enthalpy of the reaction

    [ ] M/s
    [ ] 1/(M*s)

    Explanation: For a first-order reaction: Rate (M/s) = k[A] (M), so k = (M/s)/M = 1/s or s^-1.

  Q8. What is the half-life expression for a first-order reaction?

    [ ] t1/2 = [A]0 / 2k
    [ ] t1/2 = 1 / k[A]0
    [*] t1/2 = 0.693 / k
    Explanation: For a first-order reaction, the half-life t1/2 = ln(2)/k = 0.693/k. Notably, it is independent of the initial concentration.

  Q9. A plot of ln[A] vs time gives a straight line. What is the order of the reaction?
    [ ] Second-order
    [ ] Third-order
    [*] First-order
    [ ] Zero-order
    Explanation: A linear plot of ln[A] vs time indicates first-order kinetics, based on the integrated rate law: ln[A] = ln[A]0 - kt.

  Q10. A plot of 1/[A] vs time gives a straight line. What is the order of the reaction?
    [*] Second-order
    [ ] Third-order
    [ ] Zero-order
    [ ] First-order
    Explanation: A linear plot of 1/[A] vs time indicates second-order kinetics, based on the integrated rate law: 1/[A] = 1/[A]0 + kt.

  Q11. What is a rate law?
    [*] A mathematical expression relating reaction rate to the concentrations of reactants
    [ ] A rule for balancing chemical equations
    [ ] A law governing energy changes in reactions
    [ ] A law that states all reactions have the same rate
    Explanation: A rate law is an equation that expresses the rate of a reaction as a function of the concentrations of reactants, each raised to a specific power (the order).

  Q12. What does 'reaction order' mean?
    [ ] The sequence in which reactants are mixed
    [ ] The number of steps in a reaction mechanism
    [*] The exponent to which a reactant's concentration is raised in the rate law
    [ ] The stoichiometric coefficient of the reactant

  Q13. What does 'rate constant' (k) mean?
    [ ] A value that changes with concentration
    [ ] The equilibrium constant
    [*] A proportionality constant in the rate law that depends on temperature
    [ ] The initial rate of the reaction
    Explanation: The rate constant k is a proportionality constant in the rate law that is specific to a given reaction at a given temperature; it increases with temperature.

  Q14. What does 'method of initial rates' mean?
    [ ] A method for calculating equilibrium constants
    [ ] A technique for balancing equations
    [ ] A technique for measuring how fast a catalyst works
    [*] An experimental method that compares initial rates at different starting concentrations to determine reaction orders
    Explanation: The method of initial rates involves running a reaction multiple times with different initial concentrations and comparing the initial rates to determine the order with respect to each reactant.

  Q15. What does 'integrated rate law' mean?
    [*] A mathematical equation relating concentration to time for a given reaction order
    [ ] A rate law that has been balanced
    [ ] A rate law that includes a catalyst
    [ ] A law that integrates temperature and pressure effects
    Explanation: An integrated rate law is the mathematical result of integrating the differential rate law, expressing how concentration changes with time for a specific reaction order.

  Q16. What does 'half-life' mean in chemical kinetics?
    [ ] The time for the rate to double
    [ ] The time for all the reactant to be consumed
    [*] The time required for the concentration of a reactant to decrease to half its initial value
    [ ] Half the time of the total reaction
    Explanation: Half-life (t1/2) is the time it takes for the concentration of a reactant to decrease to exactly half of its initial value.

  Q17. What does 'zero-order reaction' mean?
    [ ] A reaction that does not occur
    [ ] A reaction with no products
    [*] A reaction whose rate is independent of the concentration of the reactant
    [ ] A reaction that requires no energy
    Explanation: A zero-order reaction has a rate that does not depend on reactant concentration; the rate remains constant until the reactant is completely consumed.

  Q18. What does 'overall reaction order' mean?
    [*] The sum of the exponents of all concentration terms in the rate law
    [ ] The coefficient of the product
    [ ] The number of reactants in the equation
    [ ] The order determined by the slowest step only
    Explanation: The overall reaction order is obtained by adding the individual orders (exponents) for each reactant in the rate law expression.

  Q19. What does the term 'pseudo-first-order' mean?
    [ ] A reaction with no rate law
    [ ] A reaction that only occurs in pseudo-solutions
    [ ] A reaction that is truly first-order
    [*] A reaction that appears first-order because one reactant is in large excess
    Explanation: A pseudo-first-order reaction is one that is second-order (or higher) overall, but appears first-order because one reactant is in such large excess that its concentration barely changes.

  Q20. What does 'differential rate law' mean?

    [ ] A rate law that only applies to gases
    [ ] A rate law that differs between trials
    [ ] A law that describes the difference in rates between two reactions
    Explanation: The differential rate law (rate = k[A]^m[B]^n) expresses the reaction rate as a function of instantaneous concentrations, as opposed to the integrated form that relates concentration to time.

--- Lesson 5.3: Collision Theory (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Collision Theory
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an effective collision?
An effective (or successful) collision is one where molecules collide with enough kinetic energy to overcome the activation energy barrier and in the correct orientation to form products.
What is the steric factor in collision theory?
The steric factor (p) accounts for the fraction of collisions that have the correct orientation. It is typically less than 1 and is multiplied into the collision theory rate equation.
What is collision theory?
Collision theory is a model explaining chemical reaction rates based on the idea that molecules must physically collide with adequate energy and correct orientation for a reaction to occur.
Important Concepts
How does increasing the concentration of reactants affect the frequency of molecular collisions
More molecules per unit volume means molecules are closer together and collide more often, increasing the collision frequency and the reaction rate.
What does 'collision frequency' mean
Collision frequency is the number of collisions between reactant molecules occurring per unit time in a given volume, and it depends on concentration and temperature.
An engineer compresses a gas mixture of reactants to half its original volume at constant temperature. By what factor does the collision frequency approximately change
Halving the volume doubles each reactant's concentration. For a bimolecular reaction, collision frequency is proportional to the product of concentrations, so it increases by 2 x 2 = 4 times.

--- Lesson 5.4: Activation Energy and Catalysts (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Activation Energy and Catalysts
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between a homogeneous and heterogeneous catalyst?
A homogeneous catalyst exists in the same phase (e.g., dissolved in the same solution) as the reactants, while a heterogeneous catalyst is in a different phase (e.g., a solid catalyst in a gas-phase reaction).
What is an enzyme?
Enzymes are biological catalysts (usually proteins) that dramatically speed up biochemical reactions by lowering activation energy, with high specificity for their substrates.
What is catalyst poisoning?
Catalyst poisoning occurs when a substance (the poison) irreversibly binds to the active sites of a catalyst, blocking reactant molecules and reducing or eliminating catalytic activity.
A student measures the rate constant for a reaction at two temperatures: k = 0.015 s^-1 at 300 K and k = 0.045 s^-1 at 320 K. Using the two-point Arrhenius equation, what is the approximate activation energy?
ln(k2/k1) = (Ea/R)(1/T1 - 1/T2). ln(0.045/0.015) = (Ea/8.314)(1/300 - 1/320). ln(3) = Ea/8.314 x (2.083 x 10^-4). 1.099 = Ea x 2.506 x 10^-5. Ea = 43,800 J/mol or about 44 kJ/mol.
A researcher compares the energy profile diagrams for a reaction with and without a catalyst. Both diagrams show the same reactant and product energy levels. What is different?
A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy (lower peak) but does not change the overall enthalpy change, so reactant and product energies remain the same.
A chemical plant uses a nickel catalyst for hydrogenation of vegetable oils. Over time, the catalyst becomes less effective due to sulfur-containing impurities in the feedstock. What is happening and how can it be addressed?
Sulfur compounds adsorb strongly onto the nickel active sites, blocking reactant access (catalyst poisoning). Removing sulfur from the feed or regenerating the catalyst restores activity.
Important Concepts
What does 'frequency factor' (A) mean in the Arrhenius equation
The frequency factor (pre-exponential factor) A accounts for the collision frequency and the steric (orientation) factor, representing how often molecules collide in the proper orientation.

    [ ] A substance that shifts equilibrium
    Explanation: An inhibitor is a substance that decreases the rate of a chemical reaction, often by blocking the active sites of a catalyst or by interfering with the reaction mechanism.

  Q20. What does 'adsorption' mean in heterogeneous catalysis?
    [ ] Molecules dissolving in the reaction solvent
    [ ] Molecules being repelled by the catalyst
    [*] Molecules binding to the surface of the catalyst
    [ ] Molecules being absorbed into the bulk of the catalyst
    Explanation: Adsorption is the process where reactant molecules bind to the surface of a heterogeneous catalyst, which weakens their bonds and facilitates the reaction.

  Q21. A car's catalytic converter contains platinum and palladium to convert toxic exhaust gases (CO, NOx) into harmless products (CO2, N2). At what point during a cold start is the converter least effective, and why?
    [ ] When the fuel is premium grade
    [*] When the converter is cold, because the catalyst needs sufficient temperature to be effective
    [ ] During idling, because there is not enough O2
    [ ] At high engine speeds, because there is too much exhaust
    Explanation: Catalytic converters require a minimum operating temperature (light-off temperature) to function. During cold starts, the catalyst has not yet reached this temperature and cannot effectively adsorb and convert the gases.

  Q22. A pharmaceutical company discovers that a reaction has Ea = 80 kJ/mol without a catalyst and Ea = 40 kJ/mol with a catalyst. By approximately what factor does the rate increase at 300 K? (R = 8.314 J/mol/K)
    [*] About 10^7 times faster
    [ ] About 100 times faster
    [ ] About 1000 times faster
    [ ] About 2 times faster
    Explanation: The ratio k_cat/k_uncat = e^((Ea_uncat - Ea_cat)/RT) = e^(40000/(8.314 x 300)) = e^(16.04) which is approximately 9 x 10^6, or roughly 10^7 times faster.

  Q23. Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) decomposes slowly at room temperature. Adding a small amount of MnO2 causes rapid bubbling. If the MnO2 is filtered out and weighed afterward, it has the same mass. What does this demonstrate?
    [ ] MnO2 provides extra oxygen for the reaction
    [ ] MnO2 absorbs the hydrogen peroxide
    [*] MnO2 acts as a catalyst, speeding up the reaction without being consumed
    [ ] MnO2 is a reactant that regenerates itself
    Explanation: The unchanged mass of MnO2 proves it was not consumed. It acted as a heterogeneous catalyst, lowering the activation energy for H2O2 decomposition into water and oxygen gas.

  Q24. A student measures the rate constant for a reaction at two temperatures: k = 0.015 s^-1 at 300 K and k = 0.045 s^-1 at 320 K. Using the two-point Arrhenius equation, what is the approximate activation energy?
    [ ] About 27 kJ/mol
    [ ] About 8.3 kJ/mol
    [*] About 44 kJ/mol
    [ ] About 100 kJ/mol
    Explanation: ln(k2/k1) = (Ea/R)(1/T1 - 1/T2). ln(0.045/0.015) = (Ea/8.314)(1/300 - 1/320). ln(3) = Ea/8.314 x (2.083 x 10^-4). 1.099 = Ea x 2.506 x 10^-5. Ea = 43,800 J/mol or about 44 kJ/mol.

  Q25. Industrial ammonia synthesis uses an iron catalyst at 400-500 degrees C. Without the catalyst, the reaction would require even higher temperatures. A student asks: why not just use a higher temperature instead of a catalyst?
    [*] Higher temperatures shift the equilibrium toward reactants (since the forward reaction is exothermic), reducing yield
    [ ] The catalyst is cheaper than heating
    [ ] Higher temperatures would decompose the iron
    [ ] Temperature has no effect on equilibrium
    Explanation: The Haber process is exothermic, so increasing temperature shifts equilibrium toward reactants by Le Chatelier's principle. A catalyst allows a reasonable rate at a moderate temperature without sacrificing too much equilibrium yield.

  Q26. A biologist studies an enzyme-catalyzed reaction. At low substrate concentration, increasing substrate speeds up the reaction. At high substrate concentration, the rate plateaus. Why does the rate stop increasing?
    [ ] The temperature decreases
    [ ] The activation energy increases
    [ ] The enzyme is destroyed at high concentrations
    [*] All enzyme active sites are occupied (saturated), so adding more substrate cannot increase the rate
    Explanation: At saturation, every enzyme molecule has substrate bound to its active site. Additional substrate must wait for an active site to become free, creating a rate ceiling known as Vmax.

  Q27. A food manufacturer uses enzymes to convert starch to glucose. The process works well at 37 degrees C but fails at 80 degrees C, even though higher temperatures normally speed up reactions. What explains this?
    [ ] The glucose breaks down at 80 degrees C
    [*] The enzyme denatures (loses its 3D structure) at 80 degrees C, destroying its catalytic activity
    [ ] The activation energy increases at 80 degrees C
    [ ] The starch becomes insoluble at 80 degrees C
    Explanation: Enzymes are proteins whose catalytic activity depends on their precise 3D shape. At 80 degrees C, the protein unfolds (denatures), destroying the active site and eliminating catalytic function.

  Q28. The ozone layer is depleted by chlorine radicals from CFCs. One Cl atom can destroy ~100,000 O3 molecules because Cl is regenerated: Cl + O3 -> ClO + O2, then ClO + O -> Cl + O2. What role does Cl play?
    [ ] Cl is an inhibitor
    [ ] Cl is a product of ozone decomposition
    [ ] Cl is a reactant that is consumed
    [*] Cl acts as a catalyst because it is regenerated after each cycle
    Explanation: Chlorine is regenerated at the end of the two-step mechanism, making it a catalyst for ozone destruction. It speeds up the overall reaction O3 + O -> 2O2 without being consumed.

  Q29. A researcher compares the energy profile diagrams for a reaction with and without a catalyst. Both diagrams show the same reactant and product energy levels. What is different?
    [*] The catalyzed pathway has a lower peak (lower activation energy) but the same overall energy change
    [ ] The products have lower energy in the catalyzed pathway
    [ ] The catalyzed pathway has a higher peak
    [ ] The reactants have higher energy in the catalyzed pathway
    Explanation: A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy (lower peak) but does not change the overall enthalpy change, so reactant and product energies remain the same.

  Q30. A chemical plant uses a nickel catalyst for hydrogenation of vegetable oils. Over time, the catalyst becomes less effective due to sulfur-containing impurities in the feedstock. What is happening and how can it be addressed?
    [ ] The sulfur increases the activation energy of the main reaction
    [ ] The catalyst is converting to NiS product; increase the temperature
    [*] Sulfur compounds poison the catalyst by binding to active sites; purifying the feedstock to remove sulfur can restore effectiveness
    [ ] The nickel is dissolving; use a larger amount
    Explanation: Sulfur compounds adsorb strongly onto the nickel active sites, blocking reactant access (catalyst poisoning). Removing sulfur from the feed or regenerating the catalyst restores activity.

--- Lesson 5.5: Reaction Mechanisms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Reaction Mechanisms
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a reaction mechanism?
A reaction mechanism describes the series of elementary steps by which reactants are converted to products, revealing the molecular-level pathway of the overall reaction.
What is an elementary step?
An elementary step is a single molecular event (collision or decomposition) in a mechanism; its rate law can be written directly from its stoichiometry.
What is the molecularity of an elementary step that involves two reactant molecules colliding?
A bimolecular step involves the simultaneous collision of two reactant molecules (or the same molecule twice).
What is an intermediate in a reaction mechanism?
An intermediate is a species that appears in the mechanism but not in the overall equation; it is formed in one elementary step and consumed in a later step.
The overall reaction 2NO + O2 -> 2NO2 has a proposed mechanism: Step 1 (fast, reversible): 2NO <-> N2O2; Step 2 (slow): N2O2 + O2 -> 2NO2. What is the rate law predicted by this mechanism?
The slow step gives Rate = k2[N2O2][O2]. From the fast pre-equilibrium: K = [N2O2]/[NO]^2, so [N2O2] = K[NO]^2. Substituting: Rate = k2K[NO]^2[O2] = k[NO]^2[O2].
A student proposes a mechanism for A + B -> C + D: Step 1: A -> X (slow); Step 2: X + B -> C + D (fast). What is the predicted rate law, and what is the intermediate?
The slow (rate-determining) step involves only A decomposing, so Rate = k[A]. X is the intermediate: it is formed in step 1 and consumed in step 2.
In the reaction 2NO2Cl -> 2NO2 + Cl2, the proposed mechanism is: Step 1 (slow): NO2Cl -> NO2 + Cl; Step 2 (fast): NO2Cl + Cl -> NO2 + Cl2. What is the predicted rate law?
The slow step is unimolecular: NO2Cl -> NO2 + Cl. The rate law is Rate = k[NO2Cl]. Cl is an intermediate consumed in the fast step 2.
In free radical halogenation of methane (CH4 + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + HCl), the chain propagation steps are: Cl* + CH4 -> CH3* + HCl, then CH3* + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + Cl*. What is Cl* in this mechanism?
Cl* is consumed in the first propagation step and regenerated in the second, functioning as both an intermediate and a catalyst within the propagation cycle of the chain reaction.

    [ ] The list of all possible products
    Explanation: A reaction mechanism describes the series of elementary steps by which reactants are converted to products, revealing the molecular-level pathway of the overall reaction.

  Q2. What is an elementary step?
    [ ] The slowest step of the mechanism
    [ ] Any balanced chemical equation
    [*] A single molecular event in a reaction mechanism
    [ ] The overall net equation
    Explanation: An elementary step is a single molecular event (collision or decomposition) in a mechanism; its rate law can be written directly from its stoichiometry.

  Q3. For an elementary step, the reaction orders are determined by:
    [ ] The overall balanced equation
    [ ] Experimental data only
    [ ] The temperature of the reaction
    [*] The stoichiometric coefficients of the reactants in that step
    Explanation: Unlike the overall reaction, for an elementary step the rate law can be written directly from the molecularity (stoichiometric coefficients) of the reactants in that step.

  Q4. What is the molecularity of an elementary step that involves two reactant molecules colliding?
    [ ] Unimolecular
    [ ] Termolecular
    [ ] Zero-molecular
    [*] Bimolecular
    Explanation: A bimolecular step involves the simultaneous collision of two reactant molecules (or the same molecule twice).

  Q5. Which step in a multi-step mechanism determines the overall rate of the reaction?
    [ ] The first step always
    [ ] The last step
    [*] The slowest step (rate-determining step)
    [ ] The fastest step
    Explanation: The rate-determining step is the slowest elementary step in the mechanism and acts as a bottleneck, controlling the overall rate of the reaction.

  Q6. What is an intermediate in a reaction mechanism?
    [ ] A catalyst added to speed up the reaction
    [*] A species produced in one step and consumed in a subsequent step
    [ ] The final product of the reaction
    [ ] An unreacted starting material
    Explanation: An intermediate is a species that appears in the mechanism but not in the overall equation; it is formed in one elementary step and consumed in a later step.

  Q7. When all elementary steps of a mechanism are added together, what should the result equal?
    [ ] The rate law
    [ ] The equilibrium expression
    [ ] The activation energy
    [*] The overall balanced equation
    Explanation: When all elementary steps are summed, intermediates cancel out and the result must match the overall balanced chemical equation.

  Q8. Why are termolecular reactions extremely rare?
    [ ] They violate the law of conservation of mass
    [ ] Termolecular steps are faster than bimolecular
    [ ] They always have zero activation energy
    [*] Three molecules colliding simultaneously with proper energy and orientation is extremely unlikely
    Explanation: The probability of three molecules simultaneously colliding with correct orientation and sufficient energy is vanishingly small, making termolecular elementary steps extremely rare.

  Q9. A proposed mechanism must be consistent with which experimental observation?
    [ ] The color of the reactants
    [ ] The percent yield of the reaction
    [*] The experimentally determined rate law
    [ ] The volume of the solution
    Explanation: A valid mechanism must predict a rate law that matches the experimentally observed rate law; this is a key criterion for evaluating proposed mechanisms.

  Q10. In a mechanism where the first step is slow and the second step is fast, the rate law is derived from:
    [ ] The overall equation
    [ ] The second step
    [ ] The sum of both steps
    [*] The first (slow) step
    Explanation: When the first step is rate-determining (slow), the overall rate law is derived from the rate law of this slow step, since it controls the overall rate.

  Q11. What does 'elementary reaction' mean?
    [ ] A simple reaction between common elements
    [ ] Any reaction involving only two elements
    [*] A reaction that occurs in a single step at the molecular level
    [ ] A reaction with first-order kinetics
    Explanation: An elementary reaction is one that occurs in a single molecular event (collision), with no intermediates. Its rate law can be written directly from its molecularity.

  Q12. What does 'molecularity' mean?
    [ ] The number of molecules in a sample
    [ ] The order of the overall reaction
    [ ] The molecular weight of the reactants
    [*] The number of reactant molecules (or atoms) participating in a single elementary step
    Explanation: Molecularity is the number of reactant particles involved in a single elementary step; it can be unimolecular (1), bimolecular (2), or rarely termolecular (3).

  Q13. What does 'rate-determining step' mean?
    [ ] The step with the lowest activation energy
    [ ] The step that produces the most product
    [*] The slowest elementary step in a mechanism that controls the overall rate
    [ ] The step involving the catalyst
    Explanation: The rate-determining step is the slowest step in a multi-step mechanism; like a bottleneck, it limits the overall speed at which reactants are converted to products.

  Q14. What does 'intermediate' mean in a reaction mechanism?
    [ ] A substance added to speed up the reaction
    [ ] The transition state of the reaction
    [*] A species formed in one step and consumed in a later step, not appearing in the overall equation
    [ ] A product that decomposes after formation
    Explanation: An intermediate is a transient species produced in one elementary step and consumed in a subsequent step; it cancels out when the steps are summed and does not appear in the net equation.

  Q15. What does 'unimolecular' mean?
    [*] An elementary step involving the rearrangement or decomposition of a single molecule
    [ ] A reaction involving only one type of molecule
    [ ] A first-order overall reaction
    [ ] A reaction with one product
    Explanation: A unimolecular elementary step involves a single reactant molecule undergoing a change (decomposition or isomerization) without colliding with another molecule.

  Q16. What does 'bimolecular' mean?
    [ ] A second-order overall reaction
    [ ] A two-step mechanism
    [*] An elementary step involving the collision of two reactant molecules
    [ ] A reaction with two products
    Explanation: A bimolecular elementary step involves two reactant molecules (or ions) colliding and reacting. The rate law for this step is first-order in each reactant.

  Q17. What does 'steady-state approximation' mean?
    [ ] The assumption that temperature stays constant
    [ ] The assumption that the reaction reaches equilibrium
    [*] The assumption that the concentration of an intermediate remains approximately constant during the reaction
    [ ] The assumption that all steps have the same rate
    Explanation: The steady-state approximation assumes that after an initial transient, the rate of formation of an intermediate equals its rate of consumption, so its concentration remains nearly constant.

  Q18. What does 'pre-equilibrium' mean in reaction mechanisms?
    [ ] When the slow step occurs before the fast step
    [ ] When products are converted back to reactants
    [ ] When the reaction has not yet started
    [*] When a fast, reversible initial step establishes equilibrium before a subsequent slow step
    Explanation: Pre-equilibrium occurs when an initial fast, reversible step reaches equilibrium quickly, and the slow (rate-determining) step follows, consuming the intermediate formed in the first step.

  Q19. What does 'chain reaction' mean?
    [ ] A reaction catalyzed by a metal chain
    [ ] A very slow multi-step reaction
    [ ] A reaction that occurs in a chain of beakers
    [*] A self-propagating sequence of reactions where products of one step become reactants in the next
    Explanation: A chain reaction involves initiation (creating reactive species), propagation (reactive intermediates generate more reactive intermediates), and termination steps.

  Q20. What does 'reaction coordinate' mean?
    [*] A variable representing the progress of a reaction from reactants to products
    [ ] The time elapsed since the reaction began
    [ ] The coordinates of atoms in a molecule
    [ ] The geographic location of the reaction
    Explanation: The reaction coordinate is an abstract variable that represents the progress of a reaction along the pathway from reactants through transition states to products on an energy diagram.

  Q21. The overall reaction 2NO + O2 -> 2NO2 has a proposed mechanism: Step 1 (fast, reversible): 2NO <-> N2O2; Step 2 (slow): N2O2 + O2 -> 2NO2. What is the rate law predicted by this mechanism?

  Q22. A student proposes a mechanism for A + B -> C + D: Step 1: A -> X (slow); Step 2: X + B -> C + D (fast). What is the predicted rate law, and what is the intermediate?

    [ ] Rate = k[B]; intermediate is C
    [*] Rate = k[A]; intermediate is X
    [ ] Rate = k[X][B]; intermediate is A
    Explanation: The slow (rate-determining) step involves only A decomposing, so Rate = k[A]. X is the intermediate: it is formed in step 1 and consumed in step 2.

  Q23. The decomposition of ozone follows: Step 1 (fast, reversible): O3 <-> O2 + O; Step 2 (slow): O + O3 -> 2O2. A scientist identifies the intermediate. Which species is the intermediate?

  Q24. A reaction has the experimentally determined rate law: Rate = k[A]^2. A student proposes a one-step mechanism: 2A -> products. Is this mechanism valid?

  Q25. A two-step mechanism is proposed: Step 1 (slow): A + B -> X; Step 2 (fast): X + C -> D. However, the experimental rate law is Rate = k[A][B][C]. Is this mechanism valid?

    Explanation: The higher peak corresponds to the higher activation energy and therefore the slower step. Step 1 is rate-determining, and the overall rate law is derived from the reactants in step 1.
