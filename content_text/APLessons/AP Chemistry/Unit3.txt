--- Lesson 3.1: States of Matter (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: States of Matter
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the name of the phase change from solid directly to gas?
Sublimation is the direct transition from solid to gas without passing through the liquid phase. Dry ice (solid CO₂) and mothballs (naphthalene) are common examples.
What is the 'kinetic molecular theory'?
The kinetic molecular theory (KMT) describes ideal gases: particles move randomly, collisions are elastic, particle volume is negligible compared to container volume, no IMFs, and KE is proportional to absolute temperature.
What is 'vapor pressure'?
Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by vapor molecules above a liquid when evaporation and condensation rates are equal (dynamic equilibrium). It increases with temperature and decreases with stronger IMFs.
What is 'sublimation'?
Sublimation occurs when a solid converts directly to gas. This happens at pressures below the triple point or when the solid's vapor pressure exceeds the external pressure. Examples include dry ice and freeze-drying.
What is the 'ideal gas law'?
PV = nRT combines Boyle's, Charles's, and Avogadro's laws into one equation. R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) or 8.314 J/(mol·K). It assumes no IMFs and negligible particle volume.
What is a 'Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution'?
The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shows that at any temperature, gas molecules have a range of speeds. Higher temperatures flatten and shift the curve to higher speeds, increasing the average and most probable speed.
A weather balloon is released at ground level with a volume of 1.0 m³ at 1.0 atm and 25°C. As it rises, the pressure drops to 0.10 atm and the temperature falls to -50°C. What is the approximate volume of the balloon at altitude?
Using combined gas law: V₂ = V₁(P₁/P₂)(T₂/T₁) = 1.0(1.0/0.10)(223/298) = 10 × 0.748 = 7.5 m³. The volume increases primarily due to lower pressure, partially offset by lower temperature.
A forensic scientist finds a sealed container of gas at a crime scene. She measures the gas density as 1.96 g/L at STP. Using the ideal gas law (d = PM/RT), what is the molar mass of the gas, and what might it be?
At STP (1 atm, 273 K): M = dRT/P = (1.96)(0.0821)(273)/(1.00) = 43.9 g/mol ≈ 44 g/mol. This matches CO₂ (44.01 g/mol) or propane C₃H₈ (44.10 g/mol).
A student collects hydrogen gas over water at 25°C and measures the total pressure as 755 mmHg. The vapor pressure of water at 25°C is 23.8 mmHg. What is the partial pressure of the dry hydrogen gas?
Using Dalton's law: P_H₂ = P_total - P_H₂O = 755 - 23.8 = 731.2 mmHg. When collecting gas over water, the water vapor contributes to the total pressure and must be subtracted.
An anesthesiologist administers a gas mixture of 70% N₂O and 30% O₂ at a total pressure of 1.0 atm. She needs to ensure the partial pressure of O₂ is sufficient for the patient. What is the partial pressure of O₂ in the mixture?
Partial pressure = mole fraction × total pressure. P_O₂ = 0.30 × 1.0 atm = 0.30 atm. This is higher than atmospheric O₂ (0.21 atm), providing adequate oxygenation during anesthesia.
Real-World Applications
Example: What happens to the average kinetic energy of gas molecules when temperature increases?
Average kinetic energy is directly proportional to absolute temperature (Kelvin): KE = 3/2 kT. Doubling the temperature doubles the average kinetic energy regardless of the gas type.

--- Lesson 3.1: States of Matter (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which state of matter has a definite volume but no definite shape?
    [ ] Gas
    [ ] Solid
    [*] Liquid
    [ ] Plasma
    Explanation: Liquids have a definite volume because molecules are close together, but no definite shape because they can flow and take the shape of their container.

  Q2. What happens to the average kinetic energy of gas molecules when temperature increases?
    [ ] It remains the same
    [ ] It decreases
    [*] It increases proportionally to absolute temperature
    [ ] It depends on the type of gas
    Explanation: Average kinetic energy is directly proportional to absolute temperature (Kelvin): KE = 3/2 kT. Doubling the temperature doubles the average kinetic energy regardless of the gas type.

  Q3. During a phase change (e.g., melting), what happens to the temperature of the substance?
    [*] It remains constant until the phase change is complete
    [ ] It fluctuates randomly according to widely held
    [ ] It decreases within the broader context of phase
    [ ] It increases rapidly regardless of prevailing
    Explanation: During a phase change, added energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces rather than increasing kinetic energy. Temperature remains constant until the entire sample has changed phase.

  Q4. According to the ideal gas law, PV = nRT. If pressure doubles while temperature and amount of gas remain constant, what happens to volume?
    [ ] Volume stays the same
    [ ] Volume doubles
    [*] Volume is halved
    [ ] Volume quadruples
    Explanation: By Boyle's law (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ at constant T and n), pressure and volume are inversely proportional. Doubling pressure halves the volume.

  Q5. What is the name of the phase change from solid directly to gas?
    [ ] Deposition
    [ ] Evaporation
    [ ] Condensation
    [*] Sublimation
    Explanation: Sublimation is the direct transition from solid to gas without passing through the liquid phase. Dry ice (solid CO₂) and mothballs (naphthalene) are common examples.

  Q6. At the critical point on a phase diagram:
    [ ] The substance can only exist as a solid
    [ ] All three phases coexist
    [ ] Temperature and pressure are both zero
    [*] The distinction between liquid and gas phases disappears
    Explanation: Above the critical point, the substance exists as a supercritical fluid with properties between liquid and gas. The density of the liquid and gas become equal, and the phase boundary vanishes.

  Q7. Which gas law states that at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to temperature (in Kelvin)?
    [*] Charles's law
    [ ] Boyle's law
    [ ] Gay-Lussac's law
    [ ] Dalton's law
    Explanation: Charles's law states V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ at constant pressure and amount of gas. As temperature increases, gas molecules move faster and push the volume outward.

  Q8. What does a heating curve plateau represent?
    [*] A phase change where energy overcomes intermolecular forces at constant temperature
    [ ] Cooling of the substance
    [ ] A temperature increase with no phase change
    [ ] A decrease in kinetic energy
    Explanation: Plateaus on a heating curve indicate phase changes. Energy is used to break intermolecular forces (potential energy increases) while temperature (kinetic energy) remains constant.

  Q9. On a phase diagram, the triple point represents:
    [*] The temperature and pressure where all three phases coexist in equilibrium
    [ ] The normal boiling point
    [ ] The point where gas becomes plasma
    [ ] The highest temperature at which a liquid can exist
    Explanation: The triple point is the unique temperature and pressure at which solid, liquid, and gas phases coexist in thermodynamic equilibrium. For water, it is 0.01°C and 611.73 Pa.

  Q10. Real gases deviate from ideal behavior most at:
    [ ] High temperature and low pressure
    [ ] Standard temperature and pressure
    [ ] Any temperature at sea level
    [*] Low temperature and high pressure
    Explanation: At low temperature, molecules move slowly and IMFs become significant. At high pressure, molecules are close together and their volume is no longer negligible. Both conditions violate ideal gas assumptions.

  Q11. What is the 'kinetic molecular theory'?
    [ ] A theory about chemical reaction rates considering that the key variables involved tend to move in this direction together
    [*] A model that explains gas behavior by assuming particles are in constant random motion, with elastic collisions and average KE proportional to temperature
    [ ] A theory about the arrangement of atoms in solids, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] A model describing only liquid behavior, although this interpretation oversimplifies the actual kinetic mechanisms at work
    Explanation: The kinetic molecular theory (KMT) describes ideal gases: particles move randomly, collisions are elastic, particle volume is negligible compared to container volume, no IMFs, and KE is proportional to absolute temperature.

  Q12. What does 'phase diagram' mean?
    [*] A graph showing the stable phases of a substance at different temperatures and pressures, with lines indicating phase boundaries
    [ ] A graph of concentration vs. time, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] A diagram showing the electron configuration of elements according to widely held views on this topic
    [ ] A graph of volume vs. moles of gas under most circumstances involving phase
    Explanation: A phase diagram maps the conditions (temperature and pressure) under which a substance exists as a solid, liquid, or gas. Phase boundary lines show conditions where two phases coexist.

  Q13. What is 'vapor pressure'?
    [ ] The atmospheric pressure at the boiling point
    [*] The pressure exerted by the vapor in equilibrium with its liquid (or solid) at a given temperature
    [ ] The pressure needed to compress a gas into a liquid
    [ ] The pressure of gas stored in a container
    Explanation: Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by vapor molecules above a liquid when evaporation and condensation rates are equal (dynamic equilibrium). It increases with temperature and decreases with stronger IMFs.

  Q14. What does 'boiling point' mean?
    [*] The temperature at which the vapor pressure of a liquid equals the external atmospheric pressure
    [ ] The temperature at which the first bubble appears
    [ ] A fixed temperature that never changes
    [ ] The temperature at which all liquid has evaporated
    Explanation: The boiling point is reached when the vapor pressure equals the external pressure. At 1 atm, this is the normal boiling point. At higher altitudes (lower pressure), the boiling point decreases.

  Q15. What is 'sublimation'?
    [ ] The breakdown of a compound into its elements
    [ ] The transition from liquid to gas
    [*] The direct transition from solid to gas without passing through the liquid phase
    [ ] The transition from gas to solid
    Explanation: Sublimation occurs when a solid converts directly to gas. This happens at pressures below the triple point or when the solid's vapor pressure exceeds the external pressure. Examples include dry ice and freeze-drying.

  Q16. What does 'Dalton's law of partial pressures' state?
    [ ] Equal volumes of gas at the same T and P contain equal numbers of molecules
    [ ] Pressure and volume are inversely proportional
    [*] The total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas
    [ ] Gas pressure is proportional to temperature
    Explanation: Dalton's law: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... Each gas in a mixture exerts pressure independently as if it were alone. The partial pressure of each gas depends on its mole fraction.

  Q17. What is the 'ideal gas law'?
    [*] PV = nRT, relating pressure, volume, moles, and temperature of an ideal gas through the gas constant R
    [ ] PV = constant at all conditions
    [ ] A law that applies only to liquids
    [ ] V = nT, relating only volume and temperature
    Explanation: PV = nRT combines Boyle's, Charles's, and Avogadro's laws into one equation. R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) or 8.314 J/(mol·K). It assumes no IMFs and negligible particle volume.

  Q18. What does 'critical point' refer to on a phase diagram?
    [ ] The lowest possible temperature
    [ ] The temperature and pressure below which a gas cannot be liquefied
    [ ] The point where all three phases meet
    [*] The temperature and pressure above which a distinct liquid phase cannot exist; the substance becomes a supercritical fluid
    Explanation: Above the critical temperature and pressure, a substance exists as a supercritical fluid with properties intermediate between liquid and gas. No amount of pressure can liquefy a gas above its critical temperature.

  Q19. What is a 'Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution'?
    [*] A graph showing the distribution of molecular speeds in a gas at a given temperature, with most molecules near the average speed and few at very high or low speeds
    [ ] The arrangement of atoms in a crystal considering that the key variables involved tend to move in this direction together
    [ ] The even distribution of gas molecules throughout a container, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] The distribution of electrons in energy levels because the underlying forces at work tend to produce exactly this type of outcome
    Explanation: The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shows that at any temperature, gas molecules have a range of speeds. Higher temperatures flatten and shift the curve to higher speeds, increasing the average and most probable speed.

  Q20. What does 'effusion' mean?
    [*] The escape of gas molecules through a tiny hole into a vacuum, with rate inversely proportional to the square root of molar mass (Graham's law)
    [ ] Gas molecules spreading out to fill a container, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] The condensation of gas into liquid due to the well-established relationship between effusion trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] The mixing of two gases at different pressures, as conventional effusion theory would suggest
    Explanation: Effusion is the passage of gas through a tiny opening. Graham's law states: rate₁/rate₂ = √(M₂/M₁). Lighter gases effuse faster; helium effuses about 2.7 times faster than nitrogen.

  Q21. A scuba diver is at a depth of 30 meters underwater where the pressure is about 4 atm. She breathes air from her tank and absorbs more nitrogen into her blood. If she ascends too quickly, nitrogen comes out of solution as bubbles (decompression sickness). Which gas law explains why more nitrogen dissolves at depth?
    [*] Henry's law - the solubility of a gas is directly proportional to its partial pressure; at 4 atm, more N₂ dissolves in blood
    [ ] Charles's law - higher temperature at depth increases solubility
    [ ] Avogadro's law - more nitrogen molecules exist at depth
    [ ] Boyle's law - decreased volume forces nitrogen into the blood
    Explanation: Henry's law states that gas solubility is proportional to partial pressure. At 4 atm, the partial pressure of N₂ is four times higher than at the surface, so four times as much dissolves in the blood.

  Q22. A weather balloon is released at ground level with a volume of 1.0 m³ at 1.0 atm and 25°C. As it rises, the pressure drops to 0.10 atm and the temperature falls to -50°C. What is the approximate volume of the balloon at altitude?
    [ ] 5.0 m³
    [*] 7.5 m³
    [ ] 15.0 m³
    [ ] 10.0 m³
    Explanation: Using combined gas law: V₂ = V₁(P₁/P₂)(T₂/T₁) = 1.0(1.0/0.10)(223/298) = 10 × 0.748 = 7.5 m³. The volume increases primarily due to lower pressure, partially offset by lower temperature.

  Q23. A forensic scientist finds a sealed container of gas at a crime scene. She measures the gas density as 1.96 g/L at STP. Using the ideal gas law (d = PM/RT), what is the molar mass of the gas, and what might it be?
    [*] M ≈ 44 g/mol, likely CO₂ or C₃H₈
    [ ] M ≈ 28 g/mol, likely N₂
    [ ] M ≈ 18 g/mol, likely H₂O
    [ ] M ≈ 32 g/mol, likely O₂
    Explanation: At STP (1 atm, 273 K): M = dRT/P = (1.96)(0.0821)(273)/(1.00) = 43.9 g/mol ≈ 44 g/mol. This matches CO₂ (44.01 g/mol) or propane C₃H₈ (44.10 g/mol).

  Q24. A freeze-drying facility preserves food by first freezing it, then reducing the pressure so the ice sublimates directly to water vapor without melting. Using the phase diagram of water, what pressure must the chamber be below for sublimation to occur?
    [ ] Below 1 atm, as conventional phase theory would suggest
    [ ] Any pressure below 100 atm, especially during periods
    [ ] Below 0.5 atm according to widely held views on this topic
    [*] Below the triple point pressure of water (611 Pa or about 0.006 atm)
    Explanation: On water's phase diagram, sublimation only occurs at pressures below the triple point (611 Pa ≈ 0.006 atm). Below this pressure, solid ice converts directly to vapor without passing through the liquid phase.

  Q25. An industrial chemist uses supercritical CO₂ as a solvent to extract caffeine from coffee beans (decaffeination). Above the critical point (31°C, 73 atm), CO₂ has properties of both a liquid and a gas. Why is supercritical CO₂ a better solvent than liquid CO₂?
    [*] Supercritical CO₂ has liquid-like density (good dissolving power) combined with gas-like diffusivity (penetrates solid materials easily), making it an excellent solvent
    [ ] Supercritical CO₂ is hotter and dissolves things faster, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Supercritical CO₂ is chemically different from regular CO₂, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] Liquid CO₂ cannot dissolve organic compounds, since standard models of supercritical behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    Explanation: Supercritical fluids combine the best of both phases: the density and solvating ability of a liquid with the low viscosity and high diffusivity of a gas. This allows supercritical CO₂ to penetrate the coffee beans and dissolve caffeine efficiently.

  Q26. A student collects hydrogen gas over water at 25°C and measures the total pressure as 755 mmHg. The vapor pressure of water at 25°C is 23.8 mmHg. What is the partial pressure of the dry hydrogen gas?
    [ ] 23.8 mmHg
    [ ] 755 mmHg
    [*] 731.2 mmHg
    [ ] 778.8 mmHg
    Explanation: Using Dalton's law: P_H₂ = P_total - P_H₂O = 755 - 23.8 = 731.2 mmHg. When collecting gas over water, the water vapor contributes to the total pressure and must be subtracted.

  Q27. A helium balloon gradually deflates over days, while a latex balloon filled with air lasts much longer. Both have tiny pores in the latex. Using Graham's law of effusion, why does helium escape faster?
    [ ] Helium is more reactive with the latex material
    [ ] Helium molecules are more energetic than air molecules
    [ ] Helium has stronger intermolecular forces that pull it through pores
    [*] Helium has a much lower molar mass (4 g/mol vs ~29 g/mol for air), so it effuses through the pores approximately 2.7 times faster according to Graham's law
    Explanation: Graham's law: rate_He/rate_air = √(M_air/M_He) = √(29/4) = 2.69. Lighter helium atoms move faster at the same temperature and escape through tiny latex pores about 2.7 times faster than air.

  Q28. A chemical plant stores propane (C₃H₈) in tanks at high pressure. The van der Waals equation accounts for real gas behavior: (P + an²/V²)(V - nb) = nRT. The 'a' constant for propane is larger than for methane. What does this indicate about propane's intermolecular forces?
    [ ] Propane has weaker intermolecular forces than methane
    [ ] Propane molecules are smaller than methane molecules
    [*] Propane has stronger intermolecular forces than methane, as the 'a' constant corrects for attractive forces between molecules
    [ ] The 'a' constant has no physical meaning
    Explanation: The van der Waals 'a' constant corrects for intermolecular attractions that reduce the measured pressure. A larger 'a' means stronger IMFs. Propane, being larger with more electrons, has stronger London dispersion forces than methane.

  Q29. An anesthesiologist administers a gas mixture of 70% N₂O and 30% O₂ at a total pressure of 1.0 atm. She needs to ensure the partial pressure of O₂ is sufficient for the patient. What is the partial pressure of O₂ in the mixture?
    [ ] 1.0 atm
    [ ] 0.21 atm
    [*] 0.30 atm
    [ ] 0.70 atm
    Explanation: Partial pressure = mole fraction × total pressure. P_O₂ = 0.30 × 1.0 atm = 0.30 atm. This is higher than atmospheric O₂ (0.21 atm), providing adequate oxygenation during anesthesia.

  Q30. A pressure cooker operates at about 2 atm. A home cook notices that food cooks much faster in the pressure cooker than in a regular pot. Using the concept of boiling point and phase diagrams, explain why.
    [ ] The higher pressure forces heat into the food more quickly
    [ ] The pressure squeezes the food, making it softer
    [ ] The enclosed space traps flavor molecules that speed cooking
    [*] At 2 atm, the boiling point of water increases to about 121°C, so the water and steam are hotter, cooking food faster
    Explanation: The boiling point of water increases with external pressure (as shown on the phase diagram). At 2 atm, water boils at ~121°C instead of 100°C. The higher temperature significantly accelerates chemical reactions involved in cooking.

--- Lesson 3.1: States of Matter (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$

--- Lesson 3.2: Solids and Liquids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solids and Liquids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Clausius-Clapeyron equation used for?
The Clausius-Clapeyron equation (ln(P₂/P₁) = -ΔH_vap/R × (1/T₂ - 1/T₁)) relates vapor pressure at different temperatures to the enthalpy of vaporization, allowing prediction of boiling points at different pressures.
What is the enthalpy of fusion (ΔH_fus)?
Enthalpy of fusion is the energy needed to melt one mole of a solid at its melting point. It is always less than ΔH_vap because melting only partially disrupts intermolecular forces while vaporization completely overcomes them.
What is a 'crystalline solid'?
Crystalline solids have a regular, repeating arrangement of particles forming a crystal lattice. They have sharp melting points and may exhibit characteristic geometric shapes. Examples include salt, quartz, and metals.
What is a 'covalent network solid'?
Covalent network solids have atoms bonded by covalent bonds in a continuous 3D network. There are no discrete molecules. Diamond (C), quartz (SiO₂), and silicon carbide (SiC) are examples with extremely high melting points.
What is a 'molecular solid'?
Molecular solids consist of individual molecules held in a lattice by IMFs. They typically have low melting points and poor conductivity. Examples include ice, dry ice, and sugar.
What is 'X-ray crystallography'?
X-ray crystallography uses the diffraction of X-rays by the regularly spaced atoms in a crystal to determine the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms. It revealed the structures of DNA, proteins, and countless crystals.
What is 'specific heat capacity'?
Specific heat capacity (c) is measured in J/(g·°C). Water has an unusually high specific heat (4.184 J/g·°C) due to hydrogen bonding, making it excellent for temperature regulation in biological and environmental systems.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a molecular solid?
Ice is a molecular solid composed of discrete H₂O molecules held together by hydrogen bonds. NaCl is ionic, diamond is covalent network, and iron is metallic.

--- Lesson 3.2: Solids and Liquids (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which type of solid has the highest melting point?
    [ ] Metallic solid
    [ ] Molecular solid
    [*] Covalent network solid
    [ ] Ionic solid
    Explanation: Covalent network solids (like diamond and SiO₂) have continuous networks of covalent bonds throughout the structure. Breaking these strong bonds requires enormous energy, giving them very high melting points.

  Q2. Which type of crystalline solid is typically brittle and conducts electricity when dissolved in water?
    [*] Ionic solid
    [ ] Covalent network solid
    [ ] Molecular solid
    [ ] Metallic solid
    Explanation: Ionic solids are hard and brittle (shifting layers puts like charges together, causing repulsion and fracture). When dissolved, the ions become mobile and conduct electricity.

  Q3. What determines the melting point of a molecular solid?
    [ ] The color of the substance
    [ ] The strength of covalent bonds within molecules
    [ ] The atomic number of the atoms
    [*] The strength of intermolecular forces between molecules
    Explanation: Melting a molecular solid requires overcoming intermolecular forces (LDF, dipole-dipole, H-bonds) between molecules, not the covalent bonds within them. Stronger IMFs mean higher melting points.

  Q4. An amorphous solid differs from a crystalline solid because:
    [ ] It contains only metallic bonds
    [ ] It has a higher melting point
    [*] It lacks a long-range, repeating ordered structure
    [ ] It is always transparent
    Explanation: Amorphous solids (glass, rubber, many plastics) lack the long-range ordered arrangement of atoms found in crystalline solids. They soften gradually over a temperature range rather than having a sharp melting point.

  Q5. Which of the following is an example of a molecular solid?
    [ ] Iron
    [ ] NaCl
    [*] Ice (H₂O)
    [ ] Diamond
    Explanation: Ice is a molecular solid composed of discrete H₂O molecules held together by hydrogen bonds. NaCl is ionic, diamond is covalent network, and iron is metallic.

  Q6. Why does ice float on liquid water?
    [ ] Liquid water has more dissolved gases making it denser
    [ ] Ice has stronger bonds than liquid water
    [*] The hydrogen bonding network in ice creates an open, hexagonal structure that is less dense than liquid water
    [ ] Ice contains air bubbles that make it lighter
    Explanation: In ice, each water molecule forms 4 hydrogen bonds in a rigid, open hexagonal lattice. This ordered structure has more empty space than liquid water, making ice about 9% less dense than liquid water.

  Q7. Which solid would you expect to be the poorest conductor of electricity?
    [ ] Molten NaCl
    [ ] Graphite
    [ ] Copper
    [*] Solid sugar (sucrose)
    Explanation: Sugar is a molecular solid held together by weak IMFs. It has no free electrons or mobile ions, making it an excellent insulator. Copper has delocalized electrons, graphite has delocalized pi electrons, and molten NaCl has mobile ions.

  Q8. What is the Clausius-Clapeyron equation used for?
    [ ] Determining crystal structure
    [ ] Calculating bond energy
    [*] Relating vapor pressure to temperature through the enthalpy of vaporization
    [ ] Calculating the density of solids
    Explanation: The Clausius-Clapeyron equation (ln(P₂/P₁) = -ΔH_vap/R × (1/T₂ - 1/T₁)) relates vapor pressure at different temperatures to the enthalpy of vaporization, allowing prediction of boiling points at different pressures.

  Q9. Which type of solid can conduct electricity in the solid state?
    [ ] Ionic solid
    [*] Metallic solid
    [ ] Most molecular solids
    [ ] Covalent network solid (diamond)
    Explanation: Metallic solids conduct electricity in the solid state because their delocalized electrons can flow freely. Ionic solids only conduct when melted or dissolved (ions must be mobile). Diamond is an insulator.

  Q10. What is the enthalpy of fusion (ΔH_fus)?
    [ ] The energy released when two nuclei fuse together
    [*] The energy required to convert one mole of a solid to liquid at its melting point
    [ ] The energy to break all bonds in a solid
    [ ] The energy released when a gas condenses
    Explanation: Enthalpy of fusion is the energy needed to melt one mole of a solid at its melting point. It is always less than ΔH_vap because melting only partially disrupts intermolecular forces while vaporization completely overcomes them.

  Q11. What is a 'crystalline solid'?
    [ ] A solid that has been grown in a laboratory, based on conventional assumptions about crystalline
    [ ] A solid that dissolves easily in water when considering all relevant crystalline factors
    [ ] Any solid that is transparent, especially during periods of significant change
    [*] A solid with atoms, ions, or molecules arranged in a highly ordered, repeating three-dimensional pattern (lattice)
    Explanation: Crystalline solids have a regular, repeating arrangement of particles forming a crystal lattice. They have sharp melting points and may exhibit characteristic geometric shapes. Examples include salt, quartz, and metals.

  Q12. What does 'amorphous solid' mean?
    [ ] A solid that is always opaque, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] A solid with a well-defined crystal structure, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] A solid with ionic bonds, since standard models of amorphous behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    [*] A solid that lacks long-range order in its atomic arrangement, softening gradually rather than melting at a sharp temperature
    Explanation: Amorphous solids have disordered particle arrangements without a repeating crystal lattice. They soften over a range of temperatures. Examples include glass, rubber, and many polymers.

  Q13. What is a 'covalent network solid'?
    [ ] A solid composed of discrete molecules held by weak forces
    [ ] A solid containing ionic bonds between atoms
    [*] A solid where atoms are connected by a continuous network of covalent bonds throughout the entire structure
    [ ] A solid with delocalized electrons
    Explanation: Covalent network solids have atoms bonded by covalent bonds in a continuous 3D network. There are no discrete molecules. Diamond (C), quartz (SiO₂), and silicon carbide (SiC) are examples with extremely high melting points.

  Q14. What does 'enthalpy of vaporization' (ΔH_vap) mean?
    [ ] The energy released when a liquid freezes
    [ ] The energy of the vapor above a liquid
    [ ] The difference in temperature between boiling and freezing points
    [*] The energy required to convert one mole of liquid to gas at its boiling point
    Explanation: ΔH_vap is the energy needed to vaporize one mole of a liquid at its boiling point. It is always positive (endothermic) and larger than ΔH_fus because vaporization completely separates molecules.

  Q15. What is a 'molecular solid'?
    [ ] A solid composed of metal atoms held by delocalized electrons
    [*] A solid composed of discrete molecules held together by intermolecular forces (LDF, dipole-dipole, or hydrogen bonds)
    [ ] A solid with a continuous network of covalent bonds
    [ ] A solid composed of ions in a lattice
    Explanation: Molecular solids consist of individual molecules held in a lattice by IMFs. They typically have low melting points and poor conductivity. Examples include ice, dry ice, and sugar.

  Q16. What does 'ionic solid' refer to?
    [ ] A solid that is always water-soluble
    [ ] A solid that conducts electricity in all states

--- Lesson 3.3: Solutions and Concentration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solutions and Concentration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the molarity of a solution made by dissolving 5.85 g of NaCl (58.44 g/mol) in water to make 500 mL of solution?
Moles NaCl = 5.85/58.44 = 0.100 mol. Molarity = 0.100 mol / 0.500 L = 0.200 M.
What is molality (m)?
Molality (m) = moles of solute / kg of solvent. Unlike molarity, molality doesn't change with temperature because it's based on mass, not volume.
What is a 'solution'?
A solution is a homogeneous mixture with uniform composition throughout. The solute (smaller amount) is dissolved in the solvent (larger amount). Solutions can be solid, liquid, or gas.
What is 'Raoult's law'?
Raoult's law: P_solvent = X_solvent × P°_solvent. This explains vapor pressure lowering: adding a nonvolatile solute reduces the solvent's mole fraction at the surface, proportionally lowering vapor pressure.
What is 'freezing point depression'?
Solute particles disrupt the orderly crystal formation of the solvent, requiring a lower temperature to freeze. ΔTf = iKfm, where i is the van't Hoff factor accounting for ion dissociation.
What is a 'van't Hoff factor (i)'?
The van't Hoff factor i accounts for solute dissociation. For nonelectrolytes (glucose), i = 1. For NaCl, i ≈ 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻). For CaCl₂, i ≈ 3. Actual i values may be slightly less due to ion pairing.
What is 'mole fraction'?
Mole fraction (X) = moles of component / total moles. All mole fractions in a mixture sum to 1. It is dimensionless and useful for Raoult's law and Dalton's law calculations.
A city road crew spreads calcium chloride (CaCl₂) on icy roads. A resident asks why CaCl₂ is more effective than table salt (NaCl) at melting ice. Using colligative properties, what is the best explanation?
ΔTf = iKfm. CaCl₂ produces 3 ions (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻, i ≈ 3) vs NaCl's 2 ions (i ≈ 2). At the same molality, CaCl₂ depresses the freezing point about 1.5 times more. It can also dissolve exothermically, providing additional melting.
A student dissolves 10.0 g of an unknown nonelectrolyte in 100 g of water and measures a freezing point of -1.86°C. Given Kf for water = 1.86°C/m, what is the molar mass of the unknown compound?
ΔTf = Kf × m. 1.86 = 1.86 × m, so m = 1.00 mol/kg. Moles solute = 1.00 × 0.100 kg = 0.100 mol. Molar mass = 10.0 g / 0.100 mol = 100 g/mol.
Real-World Applications
Example: A nurse prepares an IV solution for a patient. She must use isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl) to prevent damage to red blood cells. If she accidentally uses pure water instead, what would happen to the red blood cells and why?
In a hypotonic solution (pure water), the solute concentration inside cells is higher. Water moves by osmosis into the cells, causing them to swell and lyse (burst). Isotonic saline has the same osmotic pressure as blood, preventing this.

--- Lesson 3.4: Separation Techniques (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Separation Techniques
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'distillation'?
Distillation heats a liquid mixture, selectively vaporizing the component with the lower boiling point. The vapor is then condensed and collected separately. It exploits differences in vapor pressure/boiling point.
What is 'Rf value' in chromatography?
Rf = distance traveled by component / distance traveled by solvent front. Rf values range from 0 to 1 and are characteristic of a compound under specific conditions. They help identify unknown substances.
What is 'extraction' as a separation technique?
Extraction uses the principle 'like dissolves like' to selectively transfer a component from one phase to another. Liquid-liquid extraction uses two immiscible solvents, and the component preferentially dissolves in one.
What is 'recrystallization'?
Recrystallization exploits differences in solubility. The desired compound (more abundant) crystallizes as the solution cools, while impurities (in small amounts) remain dissolved. The key is choosing a solvent where solubility varies greatly with temperature.
What is 'electrophoresis'?
In electrophoresis, an electric field drives charged particles through a gel. Smaller and more charged molecules move faster. It is essential in molecular biology for separating DNA fragments and proteins.
Real-World Applications
Example: Paper chromatography of ink reveals multiple colored spots. What does this indicate?
Multiple spots indicate multiple components in the ink. Each pigment has different attraction to the paper (stationary phase) vs. the solvent (mobile phase), causing them to separate at different positions.
