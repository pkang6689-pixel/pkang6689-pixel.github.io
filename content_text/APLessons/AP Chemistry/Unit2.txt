--- Lesson 2.1: Stoichiometry (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Stoichiometry
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Avogadro's number?
Avogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³) is the number of particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) in one mole of a substance. It connects the macroscopic and atomic scales.
What is the molar mass of water (H₂O)?
The molar mass of H₂O = 2(1.01) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol. Each hydrogen contributes about 1.01 g/mol and oxygen contributes 16.00 g/mol.
What is the mass percent of oxygen in glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆, molar mass = 180.16 g/mol)?
Mass % of O = (6 × 16.00) / 180.16 × 100 = 96.00/180.16 × 100 = 53.3%. Six oxygen atoms contribute 96.00 g to the 180.16 g/mol molar mass.
In the reaction 2Al + 3Cl₂ → 2AlCl₃, what is the mole ratio of Al to Cl₂?
The coefficients in the balanced equation directly give the mole ratio. For every 2 moles of Al, 3 moles of Cl₂ are needed, giving a 2:3 mole ratio.
A compound is found to contain 40.0% C, 6.7% H, and 53.3% O by mass. What is its empirical formula?
Converting to moles: C = 40.0/12.01 = 3.33, H = 6.7/1.01 = 6.63, O = 53.3/16.00 = 3.33. Dividing by the smallest (3.33): C:H:O = 1:2:1, giving CH₂O. Wait - rechecking: 6.63/3.33 = 1.99 ≈ 2, so empirical formula is CH₂O.
If the empirical formula of a compound is CH₂O and its molar mass is 180 g/mol, what is the molecular formula?
Empirical formula mass of CH₂O = 12 + 2 + 16 = 30 g/mol. Multiplier = 180/30 = 6. Molecular formula = (CH₂O)₆ = C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose).
What is a 'mole' in chemistry?
A mole is the SI unit for amount of substance, defined as exactly 6.02214076 × 10²³ elementary entities. It bridges atomic masses (amu) to laboratory masses (grams).
What is 'stoichiometry'?
Stoichiometry uses the mole ratios from balanced equations to calculate the amounts of reactants and products. It is the mathematical foundation for quantitative chemistry.
What is a 'molecular formula'?
The molecular formula gives the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule. It is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula and requires knowledge of the molar mass to determine.
What is 'mass percent composition'?
Mass percent composition tells what fraction of a compound's total mass is contributed by each element. It is calculated by dividing the total mass of each element by the molar mass of the compound.
What is a 'formula unit'?
A formula unit is the simplest ratio of ions in an ionic compound. For NaCl, one formula unit consists of one Na⁺ and one Cl⁻. Ionic compounds don't form discrete molecules, so we use formula units.
A forensic chemist analyzes an unknown white powder and determines it contains 52.14% C, 13.13% H, and 34.73% O by mass. She suspects it might be ethanol (C₂H₆O) or MTBE (C₅H₁₂O). What is the empirical formula, and which compound could it be?
Converting to moles: C = 52.14/12.01 = 4.34, H = 13.13/1.008 = 13.02, O = 34.73/16.00 = 2.17. Dividing by 2.17: C₂H₆O₁. The empirical formula C₂H₆O matches ethanol.
A mining company extracts copper from chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂). The ore sample contains 34.5% CuFeS₂ by mass. If they process 1000 kg of ore, what is the maximum mass of copper (63.55 g/mol) they can extract? (CuFeS₂ molar mass = 183.52 g/mol)
Mass CuFeS₂ = 0.345 × 1000 = 345 kg. Mass fraction of Cu in CuFeS₂ = 63.55/183.52 = 0.3463. Mass Cu = 345 × 0.3463 = 119 kg maximum copper.
A combustion analysis lab burns 0.255 g of an unknown organic compound and collects 0.561 g of CO₂ and 0.306 g of H₂O. The compound contains only C, H, and O. What is its empirical formula?
Moles C = 0.561/44.01 = 0.01275 mol. Moles H = 2 × (0.306/18.02) = 0.03397 mol. Mass O = 0.255 - (0.01275×12.01) - (0.03397×1.008) = 0.0674 g; moles O = 0.0674/16.00 = 0.00421. Ratio C:H:O = 3.03:8.07:1 ≈ 3:8:1. Empirical formula: C₃H₈O.

--- Lesson 2.2: Equilibrium (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Equilibrium
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For the reaction N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), what is the equilibrium expression?
The equilibrium expression has products over reactants, each raised to their stoichiometric coefficient: Keq = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³).
What is the relationship between Kp and Kc?
Kp and Kc are related by Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn. When Δn = 0 (equal moles of gas on both sides), Kp = Kc.
If the equilibrium constant for A → B is K₁, what is the equilibrium constant for 2A → 2B?
When a balanced equation is multiplied by a factor n, the new equilibrium constant is the original K raised to the nth power. Multiplying by 2 gives K₁².
At 25°C, the Ksp of AgCl is 1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰. What is the molar solubility of AgCl?
AgCl → Ag⁺ + Cl⁻. Ksp = s² where s is molar solubility. s = √(1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 1.3 × 10⁻⁵ M.
What is 'chemical equilibrium'?
Chemical equilibrium is dynamic: reactions continue in both directions at equal rates. Macroscopic properties (concentrations) remain constant, but molecular-level reactions never stop.
What is the 'reaction quotient (Q)'?
Q has the same mathematical form as K but uses current concentrations rather than equilibrium concentrations. Comparing Q to K predicts whether a reaction will shift forward (Q < K) or reverse (Q > K).
What is 'Ksp' (solubility product constant)?
Ksp is the equilibrium constant specifically for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble salt. For AgCl: Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻]. A larger Ksp indicates greater solubility.
What is meant by 'homogeneous equilibrium'?
Homogeneous equilibrium occurs when all species are in the same phase (e.g., all gases or all in aqueous solution). Heterogeneous equilibrium involves species in different phases.
What is 'heterogeneous equilibrium'?
Heterogeneous equilibrium involves species in multiple phases. For example, CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) involves solids and a gas. Pure solids and liquids are omitted from the K expression.

--- Lesson 2.2: Equilibrium (Quiz) ---

  Q1. At equilibrium, which statement is true about a reversible reaction?
    [ ] All reactants have been converted to products
    [ ] The reaction has stopped completely
    [*] Both forward and reverse reactions occur at equal rates
    [ ] Only forward reaction occurs
    Explanation: At equilibrium, both forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates. Concentrations remain constant but the system is dynamic, not static.

  Q2. For the reaction N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), what is the equilibrium expression?
    [ ] Keq = 2[NH₃] / ([N₂] + 3[H₂])
    [ ] Keq = [N₂][H₂]³ / [NH₃]²
    [*] Keq = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³)
    [ ] Keq = [NH₃] / ([N₂][H₂])
    Explanation: The equilibrium expression has products over reactants, each raised to their stoichiometric coefficient: Keq = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³).

  Q3. If K >> 1 for a reaction at equilibrium, which statement is correct?
    [ ] The reaction does not reach equilibrium
    [ ] Reactants are favored
    [ ] Equal amounts of reactants and products exist
    [*] Products are favored
    Explanation: When K >> 1, the numerator (products) is much larger than the denominator (reactants), meaning the equilibrium position strongly favors product formation.

  Q4. Which of the following does NOT affect the value of Keq?
    [ ] Reversing the reaction
    [*] A catalyst
    [ ] Temperature
    [ ] Changing the balanced equation
    Explanation: A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally, reaching equilibrium faster without changing the equilibrium constant. Only temperature changes the value of K.

  Q5. Pure solids and pure liquids are excluded from the equilibrium expression because:
    [ ] They have no effect on equilibrium
    [*] Their concentrations (activities) are constant and equal to 1
    [ ] They don't participate in the reaction
    [ ] They are always in excess
    Explanation: Pure solids and liquids have constant concentrations regardless of amount present. Their activities are defined as 1 and are incorporated into the equilibrium constant itself.

  Q6. If Q < K for a reaction, in which direction will the reaction shift?
    [ ] No shift; the system is at equilibrium
    [ ] Toward reactants (reverse)
    [ ] The reaction stops
    [*] Toward products (forward)
    Explanation: When Q < K, there are relatively more reactants than at equilibrium. The system shifts forward (toward products) until Q = K and equilibrium is reestablished.

  Q7. For the equilibrium 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g), if the volume is decreased at constant temperature, which direction does equilibrium shift?
    [ ] Cannot be determined
    [ ] No shift
    [ ] Toward reactants (more moles of gas)
    [*] Toward products (fewer moles of gas)
    Explanation: Decreasing volume increases pressure. The system shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas to reduce pressure. Reactants have 3 moles of gas, products have 2, so the shift is toward products.

  Q8. What is the relationship between Kp and Kc?
    [ ] Kp = Kc / RT, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] Kp = Kc always, which is a common assumption in the field
    [*] Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn where Δn = moles gaseous products - moles gaseous reactants
    [ ] They are unrelated quantities, especially during periods
    Explanation: Kp and Kc are related by Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn. When Δn = 0 (equal moles of gas on both sides), Kp = Kc.

  Q9. If the equilibrium constant for A → B is K₁, what is the equilibrium constant for 2A → 2B?
    [ ] K₁/2
    [ ] K₁
    [*] K₁²
    [ ] 2K₁
    Explanation: When a balanced equation is multiplied by a factor n, the new equilibrium constant is the original K raised to the nth power. Multiplying by 2 gives K₁².

  Q10. At 25°C, the Ksp of AgCl is 1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰. What is the molar solubility of AgCl?
    [ ] 3.2 × 10⁻² M
    [ ] 1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰ M
    [*] 1.3 × 10⁻⁵ M
    [ ] 9.0 × 10⁻⁶ M
    Explanation: AgCl → Ag⁺ + Cl⁻. Ksp = s² where s is molar solubility. s = √(1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 1.3 × 10⁻⁵ M.

  Q11. What is 'chemical equilibrium'?
    [*] A dynamic state where the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, resulting in constant concentrations of reactants and products
    [ ] A state where all reactants have been consumed
    [ ] A state that can only be achieved in closed systems at 0°C
    [ ] A state where the reaction has stopped and no changes occur
    Explanation: Chemical equilibrium is dynamic: reactions continue in both directions at equal rates. Macroscopic properties (concentrations) remain constant, but molecular-level reactions never stop.

    [ ] The rate at which a reaction reaches equilibrium
    [ ] The total concentration of all species at equilibrium
    [ ] The time required for a reaction to reach equilibrium
    [*] A ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations (each raised to stoichiometric powers) at equilibrium at a given temperature
    Explanation: The equilibrium constant K is the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient. K depends only on temperature.

    [ ] The rate of the forward reaction divided by the rate of the reverse reaction
    [ ] The number of moles of product formed per mole of reactant consumed
    [*] The same expression as K but calculated using current (non-equilibrium) concentrations to predict the direction of shift
    Explanation: Q has the same mathematical form as K but uses current concentrations rather than equilibrium concentrations. Comparing Q to K predicts whether a reaction will shift forward (Q < K) or reverse (Q > K).

    [ ] Equilibrium constants change when pressure is applied
    [ ] Catalysts change the position of equilibrium
    [*] When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to partially counteract the change and establish a new equilibrium
    [ ] All reactions eventually reach equilibrium
    Explanation: Le Chatelier's Principle states that if a stress (concentration change, pressure change, or temperature change) is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts to partially offset that stress.

    [ ] The rate constant for dissolution reactions because conventional analysis of constant suggests this would be the expected result
    Explanation: Ksp is the equilibrium constant specifically for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble salt. For AgCl: Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻]. A larger Ksp indicates greater solubility.

    [ ] The system has reached maximum entropy
    [ ] The system is changing rapidly
    [ ] Only the forward reaction is occurring
    Explanation: 'Dynamic' means the reactions are still occurring (molecules are constantly reacting), while 'equilibrium' means the net concentrations don't change because the forward and reverse rates are equal.

    [*] An equilibrium where all reactants and products are in the same phase
    [ ] An equilibrium with only one reactant
    [ ] An equilibrium that only involves gases
    [ ] An equilibrium involving reactants and products in different phases
    Explanation: Homogeneous equilibrium occurs when all species are in the same phase (e.g., all gases or all in aqueous solution). Heterogeneous equilibrium involves species in different phases.

    [ ] An equilibrium where all species are gases
    [ ] An equilibrium that never reaches a stable state
    [ ] An equilibrium between two different reactions

--- Lesson 2.3: Acids and Bases (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acids and Bases
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the pH of a 0.001 M HCl solution?
HCl completely dissociates, so [H⁺] = 0.001 M = 10⁻³ M. pH = -log[H⁺] = -log(10⁻³) = 3.
What is the conjugate base of H₂SO₄?
A conjugate base is formed when an acid donates one proton. H₂SO₄ loses one H⁺ to form HSO₄⁻ (hydrogen sulfate ion), which is its conjugate base.
If a solution has a pOH of 3, what is its pH at 25°C?
At 25°C, pH + pOH = 14. Therefore, pH = 14 - 3 = 11. This solution is basic because its pH is above 7.
What is the relationship between Ka and Kb for a conjugate acid-base pair at 25°C?
For any conjugate acid-base pair at 25°C, Ka × Kb = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴. A strong acid has a weak conjugate base (large Ka × small Kb = Kw).
A 0.10 M solution of a weak acid HA has a pH of 3.0. What is the Ka of this acid?
[H⁺] = 10⁻³ M. Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA] = (10⁻³)(10⁻³)/(0.10 - 10⁻³) ≈ 10⁻⁶/0.099 ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻⁵.
What is the pH of pure water at 25°C?
Pure water autoionizes: [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M at 25°C. pH = -log(1.0 × 10⁻⁷) = 7.0, which is neutral.
What is an 'Arrhenius acid'?
An Arrhenius acid produces H⁺ (or H₃O⁺) ions when dissolved in water. This is the narrowest definition of an acid, limited to aqueous solutions.
What is a 'Lewis acid'?
A Lewis acid accepts an electron pair. This is the broadest definition of acidity. Examples include BF₃, AlCl₃, and metal cations like Fe³⁺, which accept electron pairs from Lewis bases.
What is 'Ka' (acid dissociation constant)?
Ka is the equilibrium constant for the reaction HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻. A larger Ka indicates a stronger acid (more dissociation). For acetic acid, Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵.
What is 'pH'?
pH = -log[H⁺] provides a convenient scale for expressing acidity. pH < 7 is acidic, pH = 7 is neutral, and pH > 7 is basic (at 25°C).
What is the 'leveling effect'?
The leveling effect means that in water, all strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, etc.) appear equally strong because they all donate their proton completely to water, forming H₃O⁺. To distinguish their strengths, a weaker base solvent is needed.
An industrial chemist works with concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄). She knows it is a diprotic acid with a strong first dissociation and a weak second dissociation (Ka2 = 0.012). If she dissolves H₂SO₄ in water to make a 1.0 M solution, what is the approximate [H⁺]?
The first dissociation is complete (Ka1 is very large), giving 1.0 M H⁺ and 1.0 M HSO₄⁻. The second dissociation (Ka2 = 0.012) is weak, contributing a small additional amount of H⁺. Total [H⁺] is slightly above 1.0 M.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Fluorapatite is thermodynamically more stable (lower Ksp) than hydroxyapatite. The fluoride ion is a weaker base than hydroxide, so it is less likely to react with H⁺ from acids, making fluorapatite more acid-resistant.
Real-World Applications
Example: A swimming pool manager notices the pH has dropped to 6.8 after a rainstorm. She adds sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) to raise it. NaHCO₃ is amphoteric but acts primarily as a base in this situation. What reaction raises the pool's pH?
The bicarbonate ion acts as a Bronsted-Lowry base, accepting H⁺ from the acidic water: HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ → H₂CO₃ (which decomposes to H₂O + CO₂). This removes excess H⁺, raising the pH.
Example: A marine biologist studies ocean acidification. As atmospheric CO₂ increases, more dissolves in seawater: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 over the past century. What percentage increase in [H⁺] does this represent?
[H⁺] at pH 8.2 = 6.31 × 10⁻⁹ M. [H⁺] at pH 8.1 = 7.94 × 10⁻⁹ M. Increase = (7.94-6.31)/6.31 × 100% ≈ 26%. A 0.1 pH drop represents a 26% increase in acidity.

    Explanation: Increased breathing removes CO₂ from the blood. By Le Chatelier's principle, the equilibrium CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ shifts left, consuming H⁺ and raising blood pH back toward normal.

  Q24. An industrial chemist works with concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄). She knows it is a diprotic acid with a strong first dissociation and a weak second dissociation (Ka2 = 0.012). If she dissolves H₂SO₄ in water to make a 1.0 M solution, what is the approximate [H⁺]?
    [*] Slightly greater than 1.0 M
    [ ] 0.50 M, even when
    [ ] 1.0 M, as conventional
    [ ] 2.0 M, despite what
    Explanation: The first dissociation is complete (Ka1 is very large), giving 1.0 M H⁺ and 1.0 M HSO₄⁻. The second dissociation (Ka2 = 0.012) is weak, contributing a small additional amount of H⁺. Total [H⁺] is slightly above 1.0 M.

  Q25. A food scientist measures the pH of lemon juice as 2.0 and orange juice as 3.5. A customer says the lemon juice is 'a little more acidic' than orange juice. By how many times is the [H⁺] in lemon juice greater than in orange juice?
    [ ] 1.5 times greater
    [ ] 150 times greater
    [*] About 32 times greater
    [ ] 15 times greater
    Explanation: pH is logarithmic. Difference = 3.5 - 2.0 = 1.5 pH units. [H⁺] ratio = 10^1.5 = 31.6 ≈ 32 times. Lemon juice is about 32 times more acidic than orange juice, not just 'a little more.'

  Q26. A dentist explains to a patient that tooth enamel (hydroxyapatite) dissolves in acid: Ca₅(PO₄)₃OH + H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + HPO₄²⁻ + H₂O. Fluoride treatments convert some hydroxyapatite to fluorapatite (Ca₅(PO₄)₃F). Why is fluorapatite more resistant to acid?
    [*] Fluorapatite has a lower Ksp than hydroxyapatite, making it less soluble in acid; F⁻ is a weaker base than OH⁻ and does not react as readily with H⁺
    [ ] Fluorine is a stronger base than hydroxide and neutralizes more acid
    [ ] Fluoride kills the bacteria that produce acid
    [ ] Fluorine atoms form a physical barrier over the tooth surface
    Explanation: Fluorapatite is thermodynamically more stable (lower Ksp) than hydroxyapatite. The fluoride ion is a weaker base than hydroxide, so it is less likely to react with H⁺ from acids, making fluorapatite more acid-resistant.

  Q27. A cleaning product label warns not to mix bleach (NaClO, basic) with toilet bowl cleaner (HCl, acidic). A student wonders what dangerous product forms. The reaction is: NaClO + 2HCl → Cl₂ + NaCl + H₂O. Identify the acid-base interaction that initiates this reaction.
    [*] HCl acts as an acid, donating protons that react with the basic hypochlorite ion, ultimately producing toxic Cl₂ gas
    [ ] The two substances combine to form a neutral salt only
    [ ] NaCl acts as a catalyst for the production of Cl₂
    [ ] NaClO acts as an acid, donating a proton to HCl
    Explanation: HCl is a strong acid that protonates the hypochlorite ion (ClO⁻, a weak base). The resulting HOCl reacts with more HCl to produce toxic Cl₂ gas. This is why mixing bleach and acid cleaners is extremely dangerous.

  Q28. A marine biologist studies ocean acidification. As atmospheric CO₂ increases, more dissolves in seawater: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 over the past century. What percentage increase in [H⁺] does this represent?
    [*] About 26%
    [ ] About 10%
    [ ] About 50%
    [ ] About 1%
    Explanation: [H⁺] at pH 8.2 = 6.31 × 10⁻⁹ M. [H⁺] at pH 8.1 = 7.94 × 10⁻⁹ M. Increase = (7.94-6.31)/6.31 × 100% ≈ 26%. A 0.1 pH drop represents a 26% increase in acidity.

  Q29. An antacid tablet contains 500 mg of CaCO₃. A patient with acid reflux has excess stomach acid (approximately 0.10 M HCl). The reaction is CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. How many mL of 0.10 M HCl can one tablet neutralize? (Molar mass CaCO₃ = 100.09 g/mol)

    [ ] 500 mL
    Explanation: Moles CaCO₃ = 0.500/100.09 = 0.00500 mol. Each mole neutralizes 2 mol HCl: 0.00500 × 2 = 0.0100 mol HCl. Volume = 0.0100/0.10 = 0.100 L = 100 mL.

  Q30. A wine maker monitors the acidity of her wine using tartaric acid (H₂C₄H₄O₆, a weak diprotic acid). She measures the pH at 3.2. She wants to reduce the acidity (raise pH) by adding potassium bicarbonate (KHCO₃). What type of reaction occurs between the tartaric acid and KHCO₃?

    [ ] A precipitation reaction forming an insoluble salt

--- Lesson 2.4: Intermolecular Forces (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Intermolecular Forces
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'hydrogen bond'?
A hydrogen bond is an especially strong dipole-dipole force occurring when H is bonded to F, O, or N (highly electronegative atoms with lone pairs). It is about 5-10% the strength of a typical covalent bond.
What is 'surface tension'?
Surface tension results from the imbalance of intermolecular forces on surface molecules (pulled inward but not outward). Liquids with stronger IMFs (like water with hydrogen bonds) have higher surface tension.
What is 'viscosity'?
Viscosity is the resistance of a fluid to flow. Liquids with strong IMFs and large, entangled molecules (like honey or motor oil) have high viscosity. Viscosity decreases with increasing temperature.

  Q1. Which intermolecular force is present in ALL molecules, regardless of polarity?
    [ ] Ion-dipole forces
    [ ] Hydrogen bonding
    [*] London dispersion forces
    [ ] Dipole-dipole forces
    Explanation: London dispersion forces (LDF) arise from temporary fluctuations in electron distribution and are present in all molecules and atoms. They are the only IMF in nonpolar substances.

  Q2. Hydrogen bonding occurs when hydrogen is bonded to which of the following elements?
    [ ] C, Si, or Ge
    [ ] Any halogen
    [*] F, O, or N
    [ ] S, P, or Cl
    Explanation: Hydrogen bonds form when H is bonded to highly electronegative atoms with lone pairs: fluorine, oxygen, or nitrogen (FON). These create a strong partial positive charge on H that attracts lone pairs on neighboring molecules.

  Q3. Which substance would have the highest boiling point?
    [ ] GeH₄ (77 g/mol)
    [ ] SiH₄ (32 g/mol)
    [*] H₂O (18 g/mol)
    [ ] CH₄ (16 g/mol)
    Explanation: Despite having the lowest molar mass, H₂O has the highest boiling point due to strong hydrogen bonding between molecules. The other substances only have London dispersion forces.

  Q4. What type of IMF exists between HCl molecules?
    [*] Dipole-dipole forces and London dispersion forces
    [ ] Ion-dipole forces
    [ ] London dispersion forces only
    [ ] Hydrogen bonding
    Explanation: HCl is a polar molecule (dipole-dipole forces) and, like all molecules, also has London dispersion forces. It does not have hydrogen bonding because Cl is not sufficiently electronegative (not F, O, or N).

  Q5. Why does hexane (C₆H₁₄) have a higher boiling point than methane (CH₄)?
    [ ] Hexane has ionic character
    [ ] Hexane is polar while methane is nonpolar
    [*] Hexane has stronger London dispersion forces due to more electrons and greater surface area
    [ ] Hexane has hydrogen bonds while methane does not
    Explanation: Both hexane and methane are nonpolar and only have LDF. However, hexane has more electrons (50 vs 10) and greater surface area, creating stronger temporary dipoles and therefore stronger London dispersion forces.

  Q6. Which of the following liquids has the highest surface tension?
    [ ] Acetone
    [ ] Hexane
    [ ] Ethanol
    [*] Water
    Explanation: Water has the highest surface tension among common liquids due to its extensive hydrogen bonding network. Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds, creating strong cohesive forces at the surface.

  Q7. Ion-dipole forces are most important in which situation?
    [ ] Between two identical polar molecules
    [*] When an ionic compound dissolves in a polar solvent like water
    [ ] Between two nonpolar molecules
    [ ] In metallic bonding
    Explanation: Ion-dipole forces occur between ions and polar molecules. They are the dominant force when ionic compounds dissolve in water, as water molecules surround and stabilize the separated ions.

  Q8. Which statement about the boiling points of noble gases is correct?
    [*] Boiling points increase going down the group due to increasing London dispersion forces
    [ ] All noble gases have the same boiling point
    [ ] Boiling points decrease going down the group
    [ ] Noble gases cannot be liquefied
    Explanation: Noble gases only experience London dispersion forces. Going down the group, atomic size and electron count increase, creating stronger temporary dipoles and increasing boiling points (He: -269°C to Rn: -62°C).

  Q9. Why is the boiling point of ethanol (78°C) much higher than that of dimethyl ether (−24°C), even though they have the same molecular formula C₂H₆O?
    [*] Ethanol can form hydrogen bonds through its O-H group, while dimethyl ether cannot
    [ ] Ethanol has a higher molecular weight
    [ ] Ethanol has stronger London dispersion forces
    [ ] Dimethyl ether is nonpolar
    Explanation: Ethanol has an -OH group that can form hydrogen bonds (H bonded to O). Dimethyl ether (CH₃-O-CH₃) has no O-H or N-H bonds, so it cannot form hydrogen bonds, resulting in a much lower boiling point.

  Q10. Viscosity of a liquid generally increases with:
    [ ] Increasing temperature and weaker IMFs
    [ ] Increasing temperature and stronger IMFs
    [ ] Decreasing pressure only
    [*] Decreasing temperature and stronger IMFs
    Explanation: Viscosity increases with stronger intermolecular forces (more resistance to flow) and decreases with higher temperature (molecules move faster and overcome IMFs more easily).

  Q11. What are 'London dispersion forces'?
    [ ] The force holding atoms together within a molecule
    [ ] Forces between permanently polar molecules
    [ ] Forces between ions and polar solvents
    [*] Temporary attractive forces caused by instantaneous dipoles from fluctuations in electron distribution, present in all molecules
    Explanation: London dispersion forces (also called van der Waals forces) arise from temporary, instantaneous dipoles created by random electron movement. They increase with molecular size, electron count, and surface area.

  Q12. What does 'dipole-dipole force' mean?
    [ ] The intramolecular force within a polar bond
    [ ] The force between two nonpolar molecules
    [ ] The attraction between an ion and a polar molecule
    [*] The attractive force between the positive end of one polar molecule and the negative end of another polar molecule
    Explanation: Dipole-dipole forces are attractions between the partial positive end (δ+) of one polar molecule and the partial negative end (δ-) of another. They are stronger than LDF for molecules of similar size.

  Q13. What is a 'hydrogen bond'?
    [ ] Any bond involving hydrogen within the broader context of bond analysis
    [ ] A covalent bond between two hydrogen atoms, even when conditions shift significantly
    [*] A strong dipole-dipole attraction between a hydrogen atom bonded to F, O, or N and a lone pair on a nearby F, O, or N atom
    [ ] An ionic bond between hydrogen and a halogen, as conventional bond theory would suggest
    Explanation: A hydrogen bond is an especially strong dipole-dipole force occurring when H is bonded to F, O, or N (highly electronegative atoms with lone pairs). It is about 5-10% the strength of a typical covalent bond.

  Q14. What does 'polarizability' mean?
    [ ] The electronegativity difference between bonded atoms
    [*] The ease with which the electron cloud of a molecule can be distorted to form temporary dipoles
    [ ] The permanent dipole moment of a molecule
    [ ] The ability of a substance to dissolve in water
    Explanation: Polarizability measures how easily an electron cloud can be distorted. Larger atoms/molecules with more electrons are more polarizable, leading to stronger London dispersion forces.

  Q15. What is 'surface tension'?
    [ ] The tendency of a liquid to climb up a narrow tube
    [ ] The force required to break a chemical bond
    [*] The energy required to increase the surface area of a liquid, caused by the net inward pull of intermolecular forces on surface molecules
    [ ] The pressure exerted by a gas on its container
    Explanation: Surface tension results from the imbalance of intermolecular forces on surface molecules (pulled inward but not outward). Liquids with stronger IMFs (like water with hydrogen bonds) have higher surface tension.

  Q16. What does 'capillary action' refer to?
    [ ] The spreading of a drop of oil on water, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] The boiling of a liquid at reduced pressure, even when conditions shift significantly
    [ ] The resistance of a liquid to flow, even when conditions shift significantly
    [*] The spontaneous rise or depression of a liquid in a narrow tube due to the interplay of adhesive and cohesive forces
    Explanation: Capillary action occurs when adhesive forces (liquid-container attraction) compete with cohesive forces (liquid-liquid attraction). In water, adhesion to glass exceeds cohesion, causing the meniscus to rise.

  Q17. What is 'viscosity'?
    [*] A measure of a liquid's resistance to flow, determined by the strength of its intermolecular forces
    [ ] The tendency of a liquid to form droplets
    [ ] The density of a liquid compared to water
    [ ] A measure of how easily a liquid evaporates
    Explanation: Viscosity is the resistance of a fluid to flow. Liquids with strong IMFs and large, entangled molecules (like honey or motor oil) have high viscosity. Viscosity decreases with increasing temperature.

  Q18. What does 'ion-dipole force' mean?
    [ ] The force between two polar molecules
    [*] The attractive force between an ion and the partial charge of a polar molecule
    [ ] The repulsion between like charges in solution
    [ ] The force between two ions in a crystal
    Explanation: Ion-dipole forces are the attraction between an ion (full charge) and a polar molecule (partial charge). They are the primary force responsible for dissolving ionic compounds in polar solvents like water.

    [ ] The atmospheric pressure at sea level
    [*] The pressure exerted by the vapor of a liquid in equilibrium with its liquid phase at a given temperature
    [ ] The pressure exerted by a gas above its boiling point
    [ ] The maximum pressure a container can withstand
    Explanation: Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by a vapor in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid. Substances with weak IMFs have high vapor pressures (volatile) because molecules escape the liquid easily.

  Q20. What does 'cohesion' versus 'adhesion' mean?
    [ ] Cohesion is attraction between unlike substances; adhesion is attraction between like substances
    [ ] Cohesion applies only to solids; adhesion applies only to liquids
    [ ] Both terms mean the same thing
    [*] Cohesion is the attraction between molecules of the same substance; adhesion is the attraction between molecules of different substances
    Explanation: Cohesion is the intermolecular attraction between identical molecules (water-water). Adhesion is the attraction between different molecules (water-glass). Their relative strength determines meniscus shape and capillary action.

    [ ] The alcohol reacts endothermically with the skin
    [ ] The alcohol is stored at a cold temperature
    Explanation: Evaporation is endothermic. The most energetic molecules escape the liquid, absorbing heat from the skin. Alcohol evaporates faster than water (weaker H-bonding), producing a noticeable cooling effect.

--- Lesson 2.5: Metallic Bonding and Structures (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Metallic Bonding and Structures
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an alloy?
An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of metals (and sometimes nonmetals). Examples include steel (Fe + C), brass (Cu + Zn), and bronze (Cu + Sn). Alloys often have improved properties over pure metals.
What is 'metallic bonding'?
Metallic bonding is the non-directional attraction between positive metal cations and the delocalized (free-moving) valence electrons shared among all atoms. This electron sea model explains metal properties.
What is a 'unit cell'?
A unit cell is the smallest repeating structural unit that, when translated in three dimensions, generates the entire crystal lattice. Common types include simple cubic, BCC, and FCC.
What is a 'substitutional alloy'?
In a substitutional alloy, atoms of similar size swap positions in the lattice. Brass (Cu and Zn) and sterling silver (Ag and Cu) are examples. The atoms must have similar radii (within ~15%).
What is 'close-packed structure'?
Close-packed structures (FCC and HCP) achieve the maximum packing efficiency of ~74%. Atoms are arranged in layers where each atom touches as many neighbors as possible (coordination number 12).
What is 'band theory' in metals?
Band theory explains metallic conductivity by showing how atomic orbitals from billions of atoms merge into continuous energy bands. In metals, the valence band is partially filled, allowing electrons to move freely.

  Q1. What holds metal atoms together in a metallic bond?

    [ ] Metals contain ionic bonds that carry charge
    Explanation: The sea of delocalized electrons in metals can flow freely in response to an electric field, making metals excellent electrical conductors. This is unique to metallic bonding.

    Explanation: When force is applied, layers of metal cations can slide over one another. The delocalized electron sea is non-directional and adjusts to the new positions, maintaining bonding. This prevents fracturing.

    [ ] Mercury (Hg)
    [ ] Aluminum (Al)
    [*] Tungsten (W)

    [*] A mixture of two or more metals (or a metal with a nonmetal) that retains metallic properties

    Explanation: An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of metals (and sometimes nonmetals). Examples include steel (Fe + C), brass (Cu + Zn), and bronze (Cu + Sn). Alloys often have improved properties over pure metals.

    [ ] Face-centered cubic
    [ ] Simple cubic
    Explanation: Body-centered cubic (BCC) has atoms at the 8 corners of the cube plus one atom in the exact center of the cube. Iron at room temperature, chromium, and tungsten have BCC structures.

  Q8. Why does adding carbon to iron make steel stronger than pure iron?
    [ ] Carbon increases the number of delocalized electrons
    [ ] Carbon atoms form covalent bonds with iron atoms
    [*] Small carbon atoms fit between iron atoms (interstitial alloy) and prevent layers from sliding easily
    [ ] Carbon replaces iron atoms in the lattice
    Explanation: Steel is an interstitial alloy where small carbon atoms fit in the gaps between larger iron atoms. This blocks the movement of dislocations (layer sliding), making steel harder and stronger than pure iron.

  Q9. Which property is NOT typical of metallic substances?
    [ ] High electrical conductivity
    [ ] Metallic luster
    [*] Brittleness
    [ ] Malleability
    Explanation: Brittleness is characteristic of ionic compounds, not metals. When force is applied to ionic crystals, ions of like charge align and repel, causing the crystal to shatter. Metals are malleable due to their delocalized electron sea.

  Q10. Metals generally have high thermal conductivity because:
    [ ] Metallic bonds absorb heat without transferring it
    [ ] Metal atoms are heavier and vibrate more
    [*] Delocalized electrons can transfer kinetic energy rapidly through the lattice
    [ ] Their atoms are tightly packed with no space between them
    Explanation: Free electrons in the electron sea can quickly transfer kinetic energy from hot regions to cool regions, making metals excellent thermal conductors. This is the same electron sea that conducts electricity.

  Q11. What is 'metallic bonding'?
    [ ] The sharing of electron pairs between two metal atoms
    [ ] The transfer of electrons from one metal to another
    [ ] The covalent bonding within metal molecules
    [*] The electrostatic attraction between a lattice of metal cations and a surrounding sea of delocalized valence electrons
    Explanation: Metallic bonding is the non-directional attraction between positive metal cations and the delocalized (free-moving) valence electrons shared among all atoms. This electron sea model explains metal properties.

  Q12. What does 'electron sea model' mean?
    [ ] A model describing electron flow in ionic compounds
    [*] A model of metallic bonding where valence electrons are delocalized and free to move throughout the entire metallic structure
    [ ] Electrons orbit individual metal atoms in fixed paths
    [ ] A description of how electrons move in water solutions
    Explanation: The electron sea model describes metallic bonding as metal cations surrounded by a 'sea' of free-moving valence electrons. These delocalized electrons belong to no single atom and account for conductivity, malleability, and luster.

  Q13. What is a 'unit cell'?
    [ ] A single atom in a crystal, since standard models of unit behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    [*] The smallest repeating unit of a crystal lattice that, when stacked in all directions, reproduces the entire crystal structure
    [ ] The space between atoms in a metal when considering all relevant unit factors
    [ ] A unit of measurement for crystal dimensions, at least according to standard theory
    Explanation: A unit cell is the smallest repeating structural unit that, when translated in three dimensions, generates the entire crystal lattice. Common types include simple cubic, BCC, and FCC.

  Q14. What does 'interstitial alloy' mean?
    [ ] An alloy formed only at high temperatures
    [*] An alloy where smaller atoms fit into the spaces (interstices) between larger metal atoms in the lattice
    [ ] An alloy that contains no metallic bonds
    [ ] An alloy where all atoms are the same size and substitute for each other
    Explanation: In an interstitial alloy, small atoms (like carbon, nitrogen, or boron) fit into the gaps between larger metal atoms. Steel (Fe + C) is the most common example. The interstitial atoms impede dislocation movement, increasing hardness.

  Q15. What is a 'substitutional alloy'?
    [ ] An alloy made by melting two metals that don't mix
    [ ] An alloy containing only nonmetals
    [ ] An alloy where smaller atoms occupy spaces between larger atoms
    [*] An alloy where atoms of similar size replace each other in the crystal lattice
    Explanation: In a substitutional alloy, atoms of similar size swap positions in the lattice. Brass (Cu and Zn) and sterling silver (Ag and Cu) are examples. The atoms must have similar radii (within ~15%).

  Q16. What does 'metallic luster' refer to?
    [ ] The transparency of thin metal sheets without accounting for additional influences
    [*] The shiny, reflective appearance of metals caused by the interaction of light with delocalized electrons at the surface
    [ ] The color of a metal compound in solution in most scenarios involving luster factors
    [ ] The glow emitted when metals are heated when considering all relevant luster factors
    Explanation: Metallic luster occurs because delocalized electrons at the metal surface absorb and re-emit light across a wide range of wavelengths. This gives metals their characteristic shiny, reflective appearance.

  Q17. What is 'close-packed structure'?
    [ ] An arrangement where atoms are as far apart as possible
    [*] A crystal arrangement where atoms are packed as efficiently as possible, occupying about 74% of the total volume
    [ ] An arrangement where all atoms are in a straight line
    [ ] A structure found only in ionic compounds
    Explanation: Close-packed structures (FCC and HCP) achieve the maximum packing efficiency of ~74%. Atoms are arranged in layers where each atom touches as many neighbors as possible (coordination number 12).

  Q18. What does 'coordination number' mean in crystal structures?
    [ ] The number of unit cells in a crystal
    [*] The number of nearest neighbor atoms surrounding a given atom in a crystal
    [ ] The charge on a metal ion in the lattice
    [ ] The total number of atoms in a unit cell
    Explanation: Coordination number is the number of nearest neighbors touching a given atom. Simple cubic = 6, BCC = 8, FCC = 12. Higher coordination numbers generally indicate closer packing.

  Q19. What is 'band theory' in metals?
    [ ] A theory about the frequency of light emitted by metals within the broader context of band analysis
    [ ] A model that only applies to semiconductors according to widely held views on this topic
    [ ] A theory about the arrangement of atoms in layers under most circumstances involving band
    [*] A model where overlapping atomic orbitals from many atoms create continuous bands of energy levels, with a partially filled band allowing electron flow
    Explanation: Band theory explains metallic conductivity by showing how atomic orbitals from billions of atoms merge into continuous energy bands. In metals, the valence band is partially filled, allowing electrons to move freely.

  Q20. What does 'packing efficiency' mean?
    [*] The percentage of total crystal volume actually occupied by atoms, with the rest being empty space
    [ ] The speed at which atoms can be packed into a container
    [ ] The number of atoms per unit cell
    [ ] The density of the metal
    Explanation: Packing efficiency = (volume of atoms in unit cell / total volume of unit cell) × 100%. Simple cubic ≈ 52%, BCC ≈ 68%, FCC and HCP ≈ 74%.

  Q21. A jeweler creates a rose gold ring by alloying gold with copper. Pure gold (24 karat) is too soft for everyday jewelry, so 18 karat rose gold (75% Au, 25% Cu) is used. Both Au and Cu have FCC crystal structures with similar atomic radii. What type of alloy is rose gold, and why is it harder than pure gold?
    [ ] A mixture that has no metallic bonding between the two metals
    [ ] An interstitial alloy; copper atoms fill holes in the gold lattice
    [ ] An ionic compound; gold and copper form ionic bonds
    [*] A substitutional alloy; copper atoms replace gold atoms and distort the lattice, impeding dislocation movement and increasing hardness
    Explanation: Au and Cu have similar atomic radii (~144 pm and ~128 pm), so Cu substitutes for Au in the FCC lattice. The size difference distorts the lattice, blocking dislocation movement and making the alloy harder.

  Q22. A car manufacturer uses aluminum (density 2.7 g/cm³) instead of steel (7.8 g/cm³) for body panels to reduce weight. However, pure aluminum is too soft. Engineers use aluminum alloy 6061, which contains small amounts of Mg and Si. Why does this alloy maintain aluminum's light weight while being much stronger?
    [ ] Magnesium and silicon are denser than aluminum, adding weight and strength
    [ ] Magnesium and silicon form ionic bonds with aluminum
    [*] The alloying elements create precipitates within the aluminum lattice that block dislocation movement, strengthening the metal without significantly changing density
    [ ] The alloy changes from metallic to covalent bonding
    Explanation: Small amounts of Mg and Si form Mg₂Si precipitates within the Al lattice during heat treatment. These precipitates block dislocation movement (precipitation hardening), dramatically increasing strength with minimal density change.

  Q23. A plumber uses copper pipes for water supply lines. When a copper pipe is bent around a corner, it deforms without cracking. When a cast iron pipe is bent, it fractures. Both are metals. Why does copper bend while cast iron breaks?
    [*] Pure copper has a ductile FCC structure where layers slide easily, while cast iron contains carbon inclusions (graphite flakes) that act as crack initiation points, making it brittle
    [ ] Copper has covalent bonds while cast iron has ionic bonds in most scenarios involving copper factors
    [ ] Cast iron is heavier, making it more brittle when the interaction between copper and cast factors is taken into full account
    [ ] Copper atoms are smaller and more flexible, since the relevant copper factors typically produce this outcome in practice
    Explanation: Copper's FCC structure allows smooth layer sliding (ductile). Cast iron contains graphite flakes (carbon inclusions) that disrupt the metallic lattice and create stress concentration points, leading to brittle fracture.

  Q24. A semiconductor company manufactures silicon chips. Pure silicon is a poor conductor at room temperature but becomes more conductive when heated. This behavior is opposite to metals, which become less conductive when heated. What explains this difference using band theory?
    [ ] Silicon has ionic bonds that break when heated due to the well-established relationship between band trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] Silicon atoms move faster when heated, conducting electricity through motion when considering all relevant band factors
    [ ] Metals and semiconductors respond identically to temperature because conventional analysis of band suggests this would be the expected result
    [*] In silicon, heating promotes electrons across the band gap into the conduction band, increasing conductivity; in metals, heating increases atomic vibrations that scatter electrons, decreasing conductivity
    Explanation: Silicon has a band gap between its valence and conduction bands. Heating provides energy for electrons to jump the gap, increasing conductivity. In metals, the conduction band is already partially filled, but heating causes lattice vibrations that scatter electrons.

  Q25. A dentist uses an amalgam filling made of mercury alloyed with silver, tin, and copper. The filling starts as a soft paste and hardens in the patient's mouth. Why is mercury liquid at room temperature while other metals are solid, despite all having metallic bonding?
    [ ] Mercury has too many electrons to form a solid lattice
    [*] Mercury's filled 6s² electrons are tightly held due to relativistic effects, weakening its metallic bonding and resulting in a very low melting point (-39°C)
    [ ] Mercury atoms are too large to pack into a crystal structure
    [ ] Mercury has covalent bonds instead of metallic bonds
    Explanation: Mercury's outermost 6s² electrons experience relativistic contraction, making them more tightly held and less available for metallic bonding. This weakens the metallic bond, giving mercury an unusually low melting point.

  Q26. An aerospace engineer selects titanium (Ti) for aircraft components because it has a high strength-to-weight ratio. Ti has an HCP crystal structure at room temperature but transforms to BCC above 882°C. Why does this phase transformation matter during manufacturing?
    [ ] The BCC phase is a liquid, making it easier to shape, as most introductory frameworks would predict when analyzing crystal dynamics
    [*] The BCC phase has different mechanical properties and is easier to work with; controlling the transformation temperature allows engineers to optimize the microstructure for desired properties
    [ ] The HCP and BCC phases have different chemical compositions, since the prevailing theory about crystal would support this particular interpretation
    [ ] Only the HCP phase has metallic bonding, since standard models of crystal behavior predict this outcome under normal conditions
    Explanation: Different crystal structures have different properties (BCC is less close-packed, more ductile at high T). Engineers use heat treatments to control the HCP/BCC ratio, tailoring strength, ductility, and toughness for specific applications.

  Q27. A bridge engineer specifies weathering steel (Cor-Ten) for a bridge. This steel contains small amounts of Cu, Cr, and Ni. Unlike ordinary steel that rusts destructively, weathering steel forms a stable, protective oxide layer. How does alloying affect the corrosion behavior?
    [*] Cu, Cr, and Ni atoms promote formation of a dense, adherent oxide layer that seals the surface and prevents further oxidation of the underlying iron
    [ ] The alloying elements make the steel nonmetallic and unreactive
    [ ] The alloying elements absorb all oxygen before it reaches the iron
    [ ] Weathering steel doesn't corrode at all
    Explanation: The alloying elements (especially Cr and Cu) form a stable, adherent oxide patina that physically blocks oxygen and moisture from reaching the underlying iron. This self-healing layer prevents progressive rusting.

  Q28. A student holds a metal spoon and a wooden spoon, both at room temperature (22°C). The metal spoon feels cold while the wooden spoon feels warm. Both are actually the same temperature. What property of metallic bonding explains this sensation?
    [ ] Metal atoms are colder than wood atoms at the same temperature, as conventional spoon theory would suggest
    [*] The delocalized electrons in metal rapidly conduct heat away from your hand, creating the sensation of cold; wood is a poor thermal conductor and transfers heat slowly
    [ ] Metal has a lower specific heat capacity based on the conventional understanding of how spoon processes interact in practice
    [ ] Wood absorbs heat while metal reflects it because the underlying forces at work tend to produce exactly this type of outcome
    Explanation: Your hand is warmer than 22°C. The metal spoon's delocalized electrons rapidly conduct heat away from your skin, making it feel cold. Wood conducts heat slowly, so heat loss from your hand is minimal and it feels warm.

  Q29. A research lab develops shape-memory alloys (like Nitinol, a Ni-Ti alloy) for medical stents. These stents are compressed into a small diameter for insertion, then expand to their original shape at body temperature. What structural phenomenon allows this shape-memory behavior?
    [ ] The alloy melts and resolidifies inside the body, particularly when shape considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    [ ] The alloy has elastic covalent bonds that spring back, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [*] A reversible phase transformation between two crystal structures (martensite at low T and austenite at body T) allows the alloy to return to its programmed shape when heated
    [ ] Hydrogen bonding between Ni and Ti atoms stores the original shape regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    Explanation: Nitinol undergoes a reversible martensitic phase transformation. At lower temperatures, it adopts a deformable martensite structure. When warmed to body temperature, it transforms to austenite and returns to its original shape.

  Q30. A nuclear engineer needs shielding material. Lead (Pb) is commonly used, but for applications requiring structural strength, depleted uranium (DU) is sometimes chosen. Both are dense metals. Why is density important for radiation shielding, and how does metallic bonding relate to the density of these metals?
    [ ] Density doesn't matter; only atomic number determines shielding considering that the key variables involved tend to move in this direction together
    [ ] Dense metals have more covalent bonds per volume to absorb radiation, although this interpretation oversimplifies the actual density mechanisms at work
    [*] Higher density means more atoms (and electrons) packed per unit volume; the many electrons in heavy metals interact with and absorb radiation through the electron sea, and close-packed structures maximize the number of atoms per volume
    [ ] Dense metals reflect radiation like a mirror as a result of how the dynamics of density interact with metals factors to produce measurable and consistent effects
    Explanation: Radiation shielding depends on electron density. Heavy metals like Pb and U have many electrons per atom, and their close-packed metallic structures maximize atoms per volume. The dense electron sea interacts with and attenuates gamma radiation.

--- Lesson 2.5: Metallic Bonding and Structures (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Atom
     A: The smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element

  2. Q: Define: Chemical Bond
     A: An attractive force between atoms that holds them together in molecules and compounds

  3. Q: Define: Oxidation-Reduction Reaction
     A: A reaction involving the transfer of electrons between atoms, changing their oxidation states

  4. Q: Define: Equilibrium
     A: A state in which the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations

  5. Q: Define: Molarity
     A: A measure of solution concentration defined as moles of solute per liter of solution

  6. Q: Define: Acid
     A: A substance that donates hydrogen ions (H+) in solution and has a pH less than 7

  7. Q: Define: Base
     A: A substance that accepts hydrogen ions (H+) or donates hydroxide ions (OH-) and has a pH greater than 7

  8. Q: Define: Electrochemistry
     A: The branch of chemistry dealing with chemical reactions produced by electrical current and vice versa

  9. Q: What is the antiderivative of $f(x) = x^4$?
     A: $\frac{x^5}{5} + C$

  10. Q: $\int e^x dx = ?$
     A: $e^x + C$
