--- Lesson 2.1: Photosynthesis: Light Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Photosynthesis: Light Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary pigment that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis?
Chlorophyll a is the primary photosynthetic pigment that directly participates in the light reactions. It absorbs mainly red and blue wavelengths and reflects green, giving plants their green color.
What is the correct sequence of electron flow in noncyclic (linear) electron transport?
In linear electron flow: water is split at PSII, electrons pass through the electron transport chain (including cytochrome b6f), then to PSI, and finally to NADP⁺ reductase, producing NADPH.
During cyclic electron flow, what is produced?
In cyclic electron flow, electrons from PSI are recycled back through the cytochrome b6f complex and return to PSI. This pumps H⁺ ions and generates ATP only, without producing NADPH or splitting water.
What is a photosystem?
A photosystem consists of a light-harvesting antenna complex (many pigment molecules) that funnels absorbed photon energy to a reaction center chlorophyll (P680 in PSII, P700 in PSI), which then donates excited electrons to an electron acceptor.
What is 'chemiosmosis'?
Chemiosmosis is the process by which the energy stored in a proton (H⁺) gradient across a membrane is used to drive ATP synthesis. H⁺ ions flow through ATP synthase from the thylakoid lumen to the stroma, powering ATP production.
What is an 'action spectrum'?
An action spectrum plots the rate of photosynthesis (or another biological process) against wavelengths of light. It closely matches the absorption spectrum of chlorophyll, confirming that absorbed light drives photosynthesis.
What is 'NADPH'?
NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate, reduced form) is a high-energy electron carrier. It is produced when NADP⁺ accepts electrons at the end of Photosystem I and delivers them to the Calvin cycle.
What is 'cyclic electron flow'?
Cyclic electron flow uses only PSI: excited electrons pass through ferredoxin to the cytochrome b6f complex and back to PSI via plastocyanin. It produces extra ATP (via chemiosmosis) without generating NADPH or O₂.
A student studying photosynthesis uses a light meter and an oxygen probe in a sealed aquarium with aquatic plants. At sunset, she notices oxygen levels begin to drop. What is happening at the cellular level?
Plants carry out both photosynthesis and cellular respiration. In the dark, photosynthesis stops (no light reactions, no O₂ production), but mitochondria continue consuming O₂ for respiration, causing a net decrease in dissolved oxygen.
Real-World Applications
Example: A researcher notices that under very high light intensity, some plants close their stomata and switch from the Calvin cycle to photorespiration. She hypothesizes that cyclic electron flow increases under these conditions. Why would this be advantageous?
When the Calvin cycle slows (e.g., closed stomata, low CO₂), NADPH builds up because it isn't being consumed. Cyclic electron flow generates ATP without NADPH, preventing over-reduction and potential photooxidative damage.

--- Lesson 2.2: Photosynthesis: Calvin Cycle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Photosynthesis: Calvin Cycle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the first stable product of carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle?
When RuBisCO fixes CO₂ onto RuBP (a 5-carbon molecule), the unstable 6-carbon intermediate immediately splits into two molecules of 3-PGA, a 3-carbon compound that is the first stable product.
What is carbon fixation?
Carbon fixation is the process of converting inorganic CO₂ into organic carbon compounds. In the Calvin cycle, RuBisCO catalyzes the attachment of CO₂ to the 5-carbon RuBP, producing two 3-carbon 3-PGA molecules.
What is G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate)?
G3P is a 3-carbon sugar phosphate formed when ATP and NADPH reduce 3-PGA in the Calvin cycle. It is the key output of the cycle—one G3P exits per three CO₂ fixed, and two G3P molecules can be combined to make glucose.
What is C4 photosynthesis?
C4 plants use spatial separation: PEP carboxylase fixes CO₂ into 4-carbon oxaloacetate in mesophyll cells, which is converted to malate and transported to bundle sheath cells where CO₂ is released for RuBisCO, concentrating CO₂ and reducing photorespiration.
What is CAM photosynthesis?
CAM plants use temporal separation: stomata open at night (when it's cooler) to fix CO₂ into malic acid, which is stored in vacuoles. During the day, stomata close to conserve water, and malic acid releases CO₂ for the Calvin cycle.
What is PEP carboxylase?
PEP carboxylase has a high affinity for CO₂ and does not react with O₂ (unlike RuBisCO). In C4 plants, it pre-fixes CO₂ into oxaloacetate (4-carbon) in mesophyll cells, which is then decarboxylated in bundle sheath cells to concentrate CO₂ for RuBisCO.
Real-World Applications
Example: What are the three main stages of the Calvin cycle in order?
The Calvin cycle has three stages: (1) carbon fixation (CO₂ + RuBP → 3-PGA via RuBisCO), (2) reduction (3-PGA → G3P using ATP and NADPH), and (3) regeneration of RuBP (using ATP to reform the CO₂ acceptor).

--- Lesson 2.3: Cellular Respiration: Glycolysis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cellular Respiration: Glycolysis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the net ATP yield from one molecule of glucose undergoing glycolysis?
Glycolysis produces 4 ATP by substrate-level phosphorylation but uses 2 ATP in the energy investment phase. The net yield is 2 ATP per glucose molecule.
What is the role of NAD⁺ in glycolysis?
NAD⁺ is an oxidizing agent that accepts electrons (and a hydrogen) from glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate during glycolysis, becoming NADH. The NADH carries these electrons to the electron transport chain (in aerobic conditions).
What is glycolysis?
Glycolysis (glyco = sugar, lysis = splitting) is a 10-step metabolic pathway in the cytoplasm that converts one molecule of glucose (6C) into two molecules of pyruvate (3C), with a net yield of 2 ATP and 2 NADH.
What is pyruvate?
Pyruvate is a 3-carbon organic acid (CH₃COCOO⁻) produced as the end product of glycolysis. With oxygen present, it is oxidized to acetyl-CoA; without oxygen, it undergoes fermentation to ethanol or lactate.
What is NAD⁺?
NAD⁺ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a crucial coenzyme that functions as an electron acceptor. When reduced to NADH, it carries high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain for ATP production.
What is 'lactic acid fermentation'?
In lactic acid fermentation, pyruvate is directly reduced by NADH to lactate, regenerating NAD⁺. This occurs in muscle cells during intense exercise (when O₂ supply is insufficient) and in certain bacteria used in yogurt production.
What is 'alcoholic fermentation'?
Alcoholic fermentation occurs in yeast and some bacteria: pyruvate is first decarboxylated to acetaldehyde (releasing CO₂), then acetaldehyde is reduced by NADH to ethanol, regenerating NAD⁺ for continued glycolysis.

--- Lesson 2.4: Cellular Respiration: Krebs Cycle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cellular Respiration: Krebs Cycle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary function of the Krebs cycle in cellular respiration?
The Krebs cycle's main role is to extract high-energy electrons from acetyl-CoA and transfer them to NAD⁺ and FAD, forming NADH and FADH₂. These electron carriers deliver their electrons to the ETC, where the bulk of ATP is produced.
What is the fate of the carbon atoms that enter the Krebs cycle as acetyl-CoA?
The two carbon atoms entering as acetyl-CoA are fully oxidized and released as two CO₂ molecules during the Krebs cycle. This CO₂ is the same carbon dioxide we exhale during breathing.
What is the Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle)?
The Krebs cycle (also called the citric acid cycle or TCA cycle) is a series of eight enzyme-catalyzed reactions that completely oxidize the acetyl group of acetyl-CoA. Per turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 GTP, and 2 CO₂.
What is oxaloacetate?
Oxaloacetate (OAA) is a 4-carbon molecule that regenerates at the end of each Krebs cycle turn. It combines with acetyl-CoA (2C) via citrate synthase to form citrate (6C), restarting the cycle.
What is FADH₂?
FADH₂ is the reduced form of FAD (flavin adenine dinucleotide). It carries high-energy electrons to Complex II of the ETC, bypassing Complex I. Because it enters at a lower energy level, FADH₂ produces approximately 1.5 ATP compared to NADH's ~2.5 ATP.
What is 'pyruvate dehydrogenase'?
Pyruvate dehydrogenase is a large multi-enzyme complex that links glycolysis to the Krebs cycle. It converts pyruvate (3C) to acetyl-CoA (2C), releasing one CO₂ and generating one NADH per pyruvate.
What is 'succinate dehydrogenase'?
Succinate dehydrogenase is unique among Krebs cycle enzymes because it is embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane rather than dissolved in the matrix. It directly links the Krebs cycle to the ETC as Complex II.
A nutritionist explains that both carbohydrates and fats can fuel the Krebs cycle. A patient asks how fat contributes. What is the biochemical connection between fat breakdown and the Krebs cycle?
Fatty acids are broken down by beta-oxidation in the mitochondrial matrix, which sequentially removes 2-carbon units as acetyl-CoA. Each acetyl-CoA enters the Krebs cycle just like acetyl-CoA from glucose metabolism.
An endocrinologist explains to a patient that amino acids from protein digestion can also fuel the Krebs cycle. A patient on a high-protein, low-carbohydrate diet asks how this works. What is the biochemical explanation?
After deamination, amino acid carbon skeletons enter the Krebs cycle at various points: some become pyruvate or acetyl-CoA, others become alpha-ketoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, fumarate, or oxaloacetate, depending on the amino acid.
A fitness trainer tells a client that 'slow cardio burns more fat than high-intensity exercise.' A physiologist explains this using the Krebs cycle. What is the biochemical basis for this claim?
At moderate intensity, aerobic metabolism can keep up with energy demand. Fatty acid beta-oxidation produces acetyl-CoA for the Krebs cycle. At high intensity, the faster but less efficient glycolytic pathway predominates because it can produce ATP more quickly.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The brain consumes about 20% of the body's oxygen and is highly dependent on aerobic respiration. Neurons have limited capacity for anaerobic ATP production, so Krebs cycle dysfunction causes severe energy deficits and neurological damage.

--- Lesson 2.5: Cellular Respiration: Electron Transport Chain (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cellular Respiration: Electron Transport Chain
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the final electron acceptor of the electron transport chain?
Molecular oxygen (O₂) is the final electron acceptor at Complex IV. It accepts electrons along with H⁺ ions to form water (H₂O). Without O₂, the entire ETC and oxidative phosphorylation halt.
What is the role of oxygen in aerobic respiration?
O₂ is the terminal electron acceptor at Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase). It accepts four electrons and combines with four H⁺ to form two H₂O. Without O₂, electrons cannot leave the chain and the entire ETC stalls.
What is the electron transport chain?
The ETC consists of Complexes I-IV plus mobile carriers (coenzyme Q, cytochrome c) in the inner mitochondrial membrane. Electrons flow from NADH/FADH₂ through these complexes to O₂, and the released energy pumps H⁺ for chemiosmotic ATP production.
What is ATP synthase?
ATP synthase (Complex V) is a rotary molecular motor. As H⁺ flows through its channel from the intermembrane space to the matrix, the rotor spins, driving conformational changes that catalyze the binding of ADP + Pi to form ATP.
What is 'coenzyme Q' (ubiquinone)?
Coenzyme Q (ubiquinone) is a small, hydrophobic molecule that moves freely within the lipid bilayer of the inner mitochondrial membrane. It collects electrons from both Complex I and Complex II and delivers them to Complex III.
What is 'cytochrome c'?
Cytochrome c is a small, heme-containing protein loosely associated with the outer surface of the inner membrane (facing the intermembrane space). It shuttles electrons one at a time from Complex III to Complex IV.
What is an 'electron carrier'?
Electron carriers are molecules that accept and donate electrons in redox reactions. In cellular respiration, NADH and FADH₂ are mobile electron carriers that deliver electrons to the ETC, while CoQ and cytochrome c shuttle electrons between ETC complexes.
Real-World Applications
Example: A cardiologist treating a patient with heart failure notes that the heart consumes approximately 6 kg of ATP per day, despite weighing only about 300 grams. The doctor explains that nearly all of this ATP comes from oxidative phosphorylation. What happens if a coronary artery blockage cuts off oxygen supply to part of the heart (myocardial infarction)?
Cardiac muscle depends on aerobic respiration for ~95% of its ATP. When a coronary blockage stops O₂ delivery, the ETC halts and ATP production plummets. Glycolysis alone cannot meet the heart's enormous energy demand, leading to ischemic cell death within minutes.
