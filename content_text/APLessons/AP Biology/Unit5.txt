--- Lesson 5.1: Mendelian Genetics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mendelian Genetics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In a cross between two heterozygous organisms (Aa x Aa), what is the expected phenotypic ratio of the offspring?
A monohybrid cross between two heterozygotes (Aa x Aa) produces a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 dominant phenotype : 1 recessive phenotype) and a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio (1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa).
In a dihybrid cross (AaBb x AaBb), what is the expected phenotypic ratio?
A dihybrid cross between two double heterozygotes produces a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio (9 both dominant : 3 first dominant/second recessive : 3 first recessive/second dominant : 1 both recessive), assuming independent assortment.
What is the genotypic ratio of offspring from a cross between Aa and aa?
Crossing Aa x aa produces 50% Aa and 50% aa offspring (1:1 ratio). This is the expected result of a testcross when the dominant-phenotype parent is heterozygous.
What is a 'genotype'?
The genotype refers to the specific alleles an organism carries at one or more gene loci (e.g., AA, Aa, or aa). The genotype, along with environmental influences, determines the phenotype.
What is a 'dominant allele'?
A dominant allele produces its phenotypic effect whether the organism is homozygous (AA) or heterozygous (Aa). It masks the expression of the recessive allele in heterozygotes. Dominance is a property of the allele interaction, not the allele's frequency.
What is a 'homozygous' organism?
Homozygous describes an organism that has two identical alleles at a gene locus. Homozygous dominant (AA) and homozygous recessive (aa) are both examples. Homozygous organisms are true-breeding for that trait.
What is the 'Law of Segregation'?
The Law of Segregation states that each diploid organism has two alleles per gene, and these alleles separate during meiosis so that each gamete receives only one allele. This results from the separation of homologous chromosomes during meiosis I.
What is an 'allele'?
An allele is one of two or more versions of a gene. Different alleles can produce different phenotypic effects. For example, a gene for flower color might have a purple allele (P) and a white allele (p).
What is a 'Punnett square'?
A Punnett square is a grid diagram invented by Reginald Punnett that lists all possible gamete combinations from two parents, allowing the prediction of offspring genotype and phenotype ratios.
A couple visits a genetic counselor. Both parents are carriers of cystic fibrosis (heterozygous, Cc). They ask about the probability that their first child will have cystic fibrosis. What is the probability?
Cystic fibrosis is autosomal recessive. A Cc x Cc cross produces 1/4 CC, 2/4 Cc, and 1/4 cc offspring. The probability of the child being homozygous recessive (cc) and having cystic fibrosis is 1/4 or 25%, regardless of birth order.
Huntington's disease is caused by a dominant allele (H). A man with Huntington's disease (Hh) marries a woman without the disease (hh). They have four children. What is the probability that all four children will be free of Huntington's disease?
Each child has a 1/2 probability of being hh (unaffected). Using the product rule for independent events, the probability that all four children are unaffected is (1/2)^4 = 1/16.
A couple with no family history of albinism has a child with albinism (an autosomal recessive condition). The parents are surprised because neither of them has albinism. What is the most likely explanation?
This is a classic pattern for autosomal recessive inheritance. Both parents are phenotypically normal but carry one recessive allele (Aa). Their child received the recessive allele from each parent, producing the aa genotype and expressing albinism.
Sickle cell disease is autosomal recessive. A man who is a carrier (Aa) for sickle cell disease marries a woman who also is a carrier (Aa). They plan to have three children. What is the probability that exactly one of the three children will have sickle cell disease?
Each child has a 1/4 chance of being affected (aa) and 3/4 chance of being unaffected. Using the binomial probability: C(3,1) x (1/4)^1 x (3/4)^2 = 3 x 1/4 x 9/16 = 27/64. There are three ways exactly one of three children can be affected.

--- Lesson 5.2: Genetic Linkage and Chromosome Mapping (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Genetic Linkage and Chromosome Mapping
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'genetic linkage'?
Genetic linkage is the phenomenon where genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together as a unit. Linkage reduces the frequency of recombinant offspring below the 50% expected from independent assortment.
What is a 'genetic map'?
A genetic map (linkage map) shows the relative positions and distances between genes on a chromosome, determined by recombination frequencies. Distances are measured in centimorgans (cM), where 1 cM = 1% recombination.
What is a 'centimorgan'?
A centimorgan (cM), also called a map unit, is a unit of genetic distance. One centimorgan corresponds to a 1% recombination frequency. It is named after geneticist Thomas Hunt Morgan.
What is a 'recombinant type' offspring?
Recombinant-type offspring have new combinations of alleles that differ from both parental chromosomes. They arise from crossing over between linked genes during meiosis and represent the minority class in a testcross.
What is a 'locus' (plural: loci)?
A locus is the specific position on a chromosome where a particular gene is located. Different alleles of the same gene occupy the same locus on homologous chromosomes.
What is a 'coefficient of coincidence'?
The coefficient of coincidence (c.o.c.) equals the observed number of double crossovers divided by the expected number. A c.o.c. of 1 means no interference; values less than 1 indicate positive interference (fewer double crossovers than expected).
A geneticist crosses a fruit fly heterozygous for two linked traits (AaBb, in coupling/cis configuration: AB/ab) with a fly homozygous recessive for both traits (aabb). She observes: 410 AB/ab, 395 ab/ab, 48 Ab/ab, 47 aB/ab. What is the recombination frequency?
Recombinant offspring are Ab and aB classes: 48 + 47 = 95. Total offspring: 410 + 395 + 48 + 47 = 900. Recombination frequency = 95/900 = 10.6%. This means genes A and B are approximately 10.6 cM apart.
A student maps three Drosophila genes: the distance between A and B is 8 cM, and the distance between B and C is 12 cM. She predicts 0.96% double crossovers (0.08 x 0.12 = 0.0096). However, she observes only 0.48% double crossovers. What is the coefficient of coincidence, and what does it indicate?
The coefficient of coincidence = observed double crossovers / expected double crossovers = 0.48% / 0.96% = 0.5. This means only 50% of expected double crossovers occurred, indicating positive interference of 0.5 (interference = 1 - c.o.c. = 0.5).
Real-World Applications
Example: What is 'genetic linkage'?
Genetic linkage is the phenomenon where genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together as a unit. Linkage reduces the frequency of recombinant offspring below the 50% expected from independent assortment.
Example: What does 'parental type' mean in the context of linkage analysis?
Parental-type offspring carry the same combination of linked alleles as the original parental chromosomes, meaning no crossing over occurred between the genes of interest. They are always the most frequent class when genes are linked.
Example: In humans, genetic linkage studies have helped identify disease genes. In a family study, a researcher finds that a disease allele consistently co-segregates with a specific DNA marker with only 2% recombination. What does this suggest about the location of the disease gene?
A 2% recombination frequency indicates the disease gene and the marker are very closely linked (approximately 2 cM apart) on the same chromosome. This information can be used to narrow the chromosomal region containing the disease gene for further study.
Example: Alfred Sturtevant, a student in Morgan's lab, was the first to construct a genetic linkage map. He reasoned that if genes A and B are 5 cM apart, and genes B and C are 3 cM apart, then genes A and C could be either 8 cM or 2 cM apart. Why are there two possible distances?
Two-point distances alone do not reveal gene order. If the order is A-B-C, then A-C = 5 + 3 = 8 cM. If the order is A-C-B, then A-C = 5 - 3 = 2 cM. A three-point cross or additional pairwise distances are needed to determine the correct order.
Example: Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) identify genetic variants associated with diseases by exploiting the principle of linkage disequilibrium. If a SNP marker is in linkage disequilibrium with a disease-causing mutation, what does this imply?
Linkage disequilibrium means specific alleles at two loci co-occur on the same chromosome more often than expected. In GWAS, a SNP in linkage disequilibrium with a disease variant serves as a marker indicating the nearby presence of the causal mutation.
Important Concepts
The recombination frequency between two genes is used to:
Recombination frequency is directly proportional to the physical distance between genes. A 1% recombination frequency equals 1 centimorgan (cM) or 1 map unit, providing a way to construct genetic maps.
If two genes have a recombination frequency of 50%, they behave as if they are:
A recombination frequency of 50% is the maximum possible and indicates that the two genes assort independently, either because they are on different chromosomes or so far apart on the same chromosome that at least one crossover always occurs between them.
One map unit (centimorgan) corresponds to a recombination frequency of:
One map unit, also called a centimorgan (cM), equals a 1% recombination frequency between two genes. This unit was named after Thomas Hunt Morgan, who pioneered genetic linkage studies using Drosophila.
What does 'recombination frequency' mean
Recombination frequency is the percentage of recombinant offspring among total offspring in a testcross. It reflects how often crossing over occurs between two gene loci and is used to estimate the genetic distance between them.
A geneticist crosses a fruit fly heterozygous for two linked traits (AaBb, in coupling/cis configuration: AB/ab) with a fly homozygous recessive for both traits (aabb). She observes: 410 AB/ab, 395 ab/ab, 48 Ab/ab, 47 aB/ab. What is the recombination frequency
Recombinant offspring are Ab and aB classes: 48 + 47 = 95. Total offspring: 410 + 395 + 48 + 47 = 900. Recombination frequency = 95/900 = 10.6%. This means genes A and B are approximately 10.6 cM apart.
A researcher discovers that two genes she expected to show independent assortment (50% recombination) actually have a recombination frequency of 35%. She later discovers both genes are on chromosome 2. Why is the recombination frequency 35% rather than close to 0% if they are on the same chromosome
Genes on the same chromosome can show recombination frequencies up to 50% depending on how far apart they are. At 35 cM apart, crossing over occurs frequently in the intervening region. Only genes very close together show very low recombination.
A researcher uses the mapping function to convert a recombination frequency of 25% into a corrected map distance, and finds it is actually about 30 cM. Why does the mapping function give a larger distance than the observed recombination frequency
When genes are far apart, double (or even) crossovers can occur, restoring the parental combination and making those meioses appear as non-recombinant. This causes the observed recombination frequency to underestimate the true genetic distance. Mapping functions (like the Haldane function) correct for this.

    [ ] Identical genes, based on conventional assumptions about frequency

    Explanation: A recombination frequency of 50% is the maximum possible and indicates that the two genes assort independently, either because they are on different chromosomes or so far apart on the same chromosome that at least one crossover always occurs between them.

  Q5. One map unit (centimorgan) corresponds to a recombination frequency of:
    [ ] 0.1%
    [ ] 10%
    [ ] 50%
    [*] 1%
    Explanation: One map unit, also called a centimorgan (cM), equals a 1% recombination frequency between two genes. This unit was named after Thomas Hunt Morgan, who pioneered genetic linkage studies using Drosophila.

  Q6. A testcross of a dihybrid organism produces 45% parental-type offspring and 5% recombinant offspring. What does this indicate about the two genes?
    [ ] The genes are on different chromosomes
    [ ] Independent assortment is occurring normally
    [*] The genes are linked on the same chromosome, approximately 10 map units apart
    [ ] The genes are identical
    Explanation: With 45% parental types per class (90% total parental) and 5% recombinant types per class (10% total recombinant), the recombination frequency is 10%, placing the genes approximately 10 map units (cM) apart on the same chromosome.

  Q7. Three-point testcrosses are used to determine:
    [ ] Whether three genes are on different chromosomes
    [ ] The DNA sequence of three genes simultaneously
    [ ] The dominance relationships among three alleles
    [*] The order and distances between three linked genes on a chromosome
    Explanation: A three-point testcross simultaneously maps three linked genes by analyzing the frequencies of all offspring classes. The rarest class identifies the double crossover, which reveals the gene in the middle, while recombination frequencies determine map distances.

  Q8. Thomas Hunt Morgan's experiments with Drosophila (fruit flies) provided the first evidence for:
    [ ] Polyploidy in plants
    [*] Genetic linkage and the chromosomal theory of inheritance
    [ ] Mendel's Law of Segregation
    [ ] The structure of DNA
    Explanation: Morgan's work with Drosophila showed that certain genes did not assort independently, providing evidence that genes are located on chromosomes and that genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together (linkage).

  Q9. Genes that are very close together on a chromosome will show:
    [*] A low recombination frequency
    [ ] No inheritance at all
    [ ] A high recombination frequency
    [ ] A recombination frequency of exactly 50%
    Explanation: Closely linked genes have a low recombination frequency because crossover events between them are rare. The closer two genes are, the less likely a crossover will occur in the short intervening region.

  Q10. In a genetic mapping experiment, which offspring class is used to identify double crossover events?
    [*] The least frequent class
    [ ] The most frequent class
    [ ] Only the parental class
    [ ] Any class with a recombination frequency above 25%
    Explanation: Double crossover events are the rarest because they require two independent crossovers between the three genes. The least frequent offspring class in a three-point cross represents the double crossover class, and comparing it to the parental class reveals which gene is in the middle.

  Q11. What is 'genetic linkage'?
    [ ] The tendency of genes on different chromosomes to be inherited together
    [*] The tendency of genes located on the same chromosome to be inherited together because they do not independently assort during meiosis
    [ ] The process by which genes are linked to proteins during translation
    [ ] The physical connection between two different chromosomes
    Explanation: Genetic linkage is the phenomenon where genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together as a unit. Linkage reduces the frequency of recombinant offspring below the 50% expected from independent assortment.

  Q12. What does 'recombination frequency' mean?
    [ ] The rate of independent assortment during meiosis II
    [ ] The frequency at which new mutations arise in a population
    [ ] The number of times a gene is expressed per cell cycle
    [*] The proportion of offspring from a cross that show new (recombinant) combinations of alleles, reflecting the frequency of crossing over between two linked genes
    Explanation: Recombination frequency is the percentage of recombinant offspring among total offspring in a testcross. It reflects how often crossing over occurs between two gene loci and is used to estimate the genetic distance between them.

  Q13. What is a 'genetic map'?
    [ ] A map showing the geographic distribution of alleles in a population
    [ ] A photograph of chromosomes arranged by size
    [ ] A diagram showing the physical structure of DNA bases in a gene
    [*] A diagram showing the relative positions of genes on a chromosome based on recombination frequencies
    Explanation: A genetic map (linkage map) shows the relative positions and distances between genes on a chromosome, determined by recombination frequencies. Distances are measured in centimorgans (cM), where 1 cM = 1% recombination.

  Q14. What is a 'centimorgan'?
    [ ] A unit of physical distance equal to one million base pairs
    [ ] A unit of measurement for chromosome length under a microscope
    [ ] A measure of gene expression levels
    [*] A unit of genetic map distance equal to a 1% recombination frequency between two gene loci
    Explanation: A centimorgan (cM), also called a map unit, is a unit of genetic distance. One centimorgan corresponds to a 1% recombination frequency. It is named after geneticist Thomas Hunt Morgan.

  Q15. What does 'parental type' mean in the context of linkage analysis?
    [ ] The dominant phenotype class in a testcross
    [ ] Offspring that show completely new traits not seen in either parent
    [ ] The generation of organisms used as parents in the original cross
    [*] Offspring that have the same combination of alleles as the parents' chromosomes (no recombination occurred between the linked genes)
    Explanation: Parental-type offspring carry the same combination of linked alleles as the original parental chromosomes, meaning no crossing over occurred between the genes of interest. They are always the most frequent class when genes are linked.

  Q16. What is a 'recombinant type' offspring?
    [ ] An offspring with a chromosomal mutation
    [*] An offspring carrying a new combination of alleles not seen in either parent's chromosomes, resulting from crossing over between linked genes
    [ ] An offspring produced by artificial insemination
    [ ] An offspring that has the exact same allele combination as the parent
    Explanation: Recombinant-type offspring have new combinations of alleles that differ from both parental chromosomes. They arise from crossing over between linked genes during meiosis and represent the minority class in a testcross.

  Q17. What does 'interference' mean in the context of genetic mapping?
    [ ] The interference of environmental factors with gene expression
    [*] The tendency of one crossover event to reduce the probability of another crossover occurring nearby on the same chromosome
    [ ] The blocking of gene transcription by repressor proteins
    [ ] The inability of two linked genes to be separated by any amount of crossing over
    Explanation: Interference (also called positive crossover interference) is the phenomenon where a crossover in one region of a chromosome inhibits additional crossovers in adjacent regions. It is calculated as 1 minus the coefficient of coincidence.

  Q18. What is a 'locus' (plural: loci)?
    [*] The specific physical position or location of a gene on a chromosome
    [ ] The centromere region of a chromosome
    [ ] A type of mutation that affects multiple genes simultaneously
    [ ] A cluster of related genes that are always expressed together
    Explanation: A locus is the specific position on a chromosome where a particular gene is located. Different alleles of the same gene occupy the same locus on homologous chromosomes.

  Q19. What does 'syntenic' mean?
    [ ] Genes that produce proteins with similar structures
    [*] Genes that are located on the same chromosome, regardless of whether they are genetically linked
    [ ] Genes that are involved in the same metabolic pathway
    [ ] Genes that are found in all species
    Explanation: Syntenic genes are located on the same chromosome. All syntenic genes are physically on the same chromosome, but they may or may not be genetically linked (tightly linked genes have low recombination frequencies; genes far apart may assort independently).

  Q20. What is a 'coefficient of coincidence'?
    [ ] The probability that two independent mutations will occur simultaneously
    [*] The ratio of observed double crossovers to expected double crossovers, used to measure crossover interference
    [ ] The frequency at which two genes coincidentally assort together
    [ ] A measure of genetic drift in a small population
    Explanation: The coefficient of coincidence (c.o.c.) equals the observed number of double crossovers divided by the expected number. A c.o.c. of 1 means no interference; values less than 1 indicate positive interference (fewer double crossovers than expected).

  Q21. A geneticist crosses a fruit fly heterozygous for two linked traits (AaBb, in coupling/cis configuration: AB/ab) with a fly homozygous recessive for both traits (aabb). She observes: 410 AB/ab, 395 ab/ab, 48 Ab/ab, 47 aB/ab. What is the recombination frequency?
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 0%
    [ ] Approximately 45%
    [*] Approximately 10.6%
    Explanation: Recombinant offspring are Ab and aB classes: 48 + 47 = 95. Total offspring: 410 + 395 + 48 + 47 = 900. Recombination frequency = 95/900 = 10.6%. This means genes A and B are approximately 10.6 cM apart.

  Q22. In a three-point testcross, the parental classes are ABC and abc (most frequent), and the double crossover classes are AbC and aBc (least frequent). Which gene is in the middle?
    [ ] Gene C
    [ ] Gene A
    [*] Gene B
    [ ] Cannot be determined from this information
    Explanation: To find the middle gene, compare the double crossover class to the parental class. The allele that switches position in the double crossover class relative to the parental class identifies the middle gene. Here, B switches (ABC to AbC and abc to aBc), so B is in the middle. The gene order is A-B-C.

  Q23. A student maps three Drosophila genes: the distance between A and B is 8 cM, and the distance between B and C is 12 cM. She predicts 0.96% double crossovers (0.08 x 0.12 = 0.0096). However, she observes only 0.48% double crossovers. What is the coefficient of coincidence, and what does it indicate?
    [*] c.o.c. = 0.5; positive interference is reducing double crossovers by 50%
    [ ] c.o.c. = 0.0; complete interference is occurring
    [ ] c.o.c. = 1.0; no interference is occurring
    [ ] c.o.c. = 2.0; negative interference is occurring
    Explanation: The coefficient of coincidence = observed double crossovers / expected double crossovers = 0.48% / 0.96% = 0.5. This means only 50% of expected double crossovers occurred, indicating positive interference of 0.5 (interference = 1 - c.o.c. = 0.5).

  Q24. A researcher discovers that two genes she expected to show independent assortment (50% recombination) actually have a recombination frequency of 35%. She later discovers both genes are on chromosome 2. Why is the recombination frequency 35% rather than close to 0% if they are on the same chromosome?
    [ ] One of the genes has been translocated to another chromosome
    [ ] The genes are on different arms of the chromosome and therefore assort independently
    [*] The genes are far enough apart that crossing over between them is very frequent, though not quite 50%
    [ ] The genes are actually on chromosome 2 and chromosome 3 simultaneously
    Explanation: Genes on the same chromosome can show recombination frequencies up to 50% depending on how far apart they are. At 35 cM apart, crossing over occurs frequently in the intervening region. Only genes very close together show very low recombination.

  Q25. In humans, genetic linkage studies have helped identify disease genes. In a family study, a researcher finds that a disease allele consistently co-segregates with a specific DNA marker with only 2% recombination. What does this suggest about the location of the disease gene?
    [ ] The marker is causing the disease
    [ ] The disease gene is on a different chromosome from the marker
    [*] The disease gene is very close to the DNA marker on the same chromosome, approximately 2 cM away
    [ ] The disease gene and marker are identical
    Explanation: A 2% recombination frequency indicates the disease gene and the marker are very closely linked (approximately 2 cM apart) on the same chromosome. This information can be used to narrow the chromosomal region containing the disease gene for further study.

  Q26. Alfred Sturtevant, a student in Morgan's lab, was the first to construct a genetic linkage map. He reasoned that if genes A and B are 5 cM apart, and genes B and C are 3 cM apart, then genes A and C could be either 8 cM or 2 cM apart. Why are there two possible distances?
    [ ] Because crossing over creates random distances between genes
    [ ] Because genetic distances are always uncertain
    [*] Because gene B could be between A and C (making A-C = 8 cM) or C could be between A and B (making A-C = 2 cM), depending on gene order
    [ ] Because map distances depend on the sex of the organism
    Explanation: Two-point distances alone do not reveal gene order. If the order is A-B-C, then A-C = 5 + 3 = 8 cM. If the order is A-C-B, then A-C = 5 - 3 = 2 cM. A three-point cross or additional pairwise distances are needed to determine the correct order.

  Q27. A cytogeneticist compares a genetic map (measured in cM) with a physical map (measured in base pairs) of the same chromosome. She notices that the relationship between genetic and physical distance is not uniform along the chromosome. Why?
    [ ] Physical maps are based on protein distances, not DNA distances
    [ ] Genetic maps are always inaccurate due to experimental error
    [ ] Genes are not evenly spaced along chromosomes
    [*] Crossing over frequency varies along the chromosome, with some regions (hotspots) having higher rates and some (near centromeres) having lower rates
    Explanation: Recombination frequency is not uniform along a chromosome. Certain regions (recombination hotspots) have elevated crossover rates, while others (e.g., near centromeres and in heterochromatin) have suppressed rates. This makes genetic distance and physical distance non-proportional.

  Q28. A couple suspects that genes for two inherited traits in their family are linked because the traits seem to always be inherited together. A genetic counselor explains that if the genes are linked, a testcross should produce mostly parental-type offspring. After analysis, the offspring ratio is approximately 25% for each of the four possible phenotype classes. What should the counselor conclude?
    [ ] Crossing over has occurred in 100% of meioses
    [ ] The genes are very tightly linked with 0% recombination
    [ ] The genes are linked with 50% recombination
    [*] The genes are not linked; they assort independently, producing the expected 1:1:1:1 testcross ratio
    Explanation: A 1:1:1:1 ratio in a testcross (equal numbers of parental and recombinant types) indicates 50% recombination, which means the genes are assorting independently. The genes are either on different chromosomes or so far apart on the same chromosome that they behave as unlinked.

  Q29. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) identify genetic variants associated with diseases by exploiting the principle of linkage disequilibrium. If a SNP marker is in linkage disequilibrium with a disease-causing mutation, what does this imply?
    [*] The SNP and the disease mutation are physically close on the same chromosome and are co-inherited more often than expected by chance
    [ ] The SNP is on a different chromosome from the disease gene
    [ ] The disease mutation has been completely mapped to a single base pair
    [ ] The SNP itself causes the disease
    Explanation: Linkage disequilibrium means specific alleles at two loci co-occur on the same chromosome more often than expected. In GWAS, a SNP in linkage disequilibrium with a disease variant serves as a marker indicating the nearby presence of the causal mutation.

  Q30. A researcher uses the mapping function to convert a recombination frequency of 25% into a corrected map distance, and finds it is actually about 30 cM. Why does the mapping function give a larger distance than the observed recombination frequency?
    [ ] Recombination frequencies cannot exceed 25%, so the function compensates for this limit
    [*] At larger distances, multiple crossovers can occur between the same two genes, and some of these restore the parental configuration, causing the observed recombination frequency to underestimate the true genetic distance
    [ ] The mapping function accounts for gene interactions (epistasis) between the two loci
    [ ] The mapping function always adds 5 cM to account for experimental error
    Explanation: When genes are far apart, double (or even) crossovers can occur, restoring the parental combination and making those meioses appear as non-recombinant. This causes the observed recombination frequency to underestimate the true genetic distance. Mapping functions (like the Haldane function) correct for this.

--- Lesson 5.3: Non-Mendelian Inheritance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Non-Mendelian Inheritance
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'incomplete dominance'?
Incomplete dominance is a non-Mendelian inheritance pattern where the heterozygous phenotype is an intermediate blend of the two homozygous phenotypes, because the single copy of the dominant allele does not produce enough gene product for full expression.
What is 'epistasis'?
Epistasis is the interaction between genes at different loci, where the expression of one gene (the epistatic gene) modifies or suppresses the phenotypic expression of another gene (the hypostatic gene). It alters expected Mendelian ratios.
What is 'polygenic inheritance'?
Polygenic inheritance involves multiple genes, each contributing a small amount to a single trait. The combined effects produce a continuous distribution of phenotypes (bell curve). Human height, skin color, and eye color are polygenic traits.
What is 'genomic imprinting'?
Genomic imprinting is an epigenetic process where chemical modifications (usually DNA methylation) silence one allele based on its parental origin. Only the allele from one specific parent is expressed, making the gene functionally hemizygous.
What is 'maternal inheritance'?
Maternal inheritance describes traits encoded by mitochondrial (or chloroplast) DNA, which is inherited exclusively from the mother. The egg cell contributes virtually all the cytoplasm and its organelles to the embryo, while sperm contribute almost none.
What is 'sex-limited inheritance'?
Sex-limited traits are encoded by autosomal genes but expressed in only one sex due to hormonal regulation. For example, male pattern baldness and milk production are influenced by sex hormones, so the trait appears only (or predominantly) in one sex.
Sickle cell disease is often described as a pleiotropic condition. A student asks why a single mutation in hemoglobin causes so many different symptoms (anemia, organ damage, pain crises, malaria resistance). What is the best explanation?
This is pleiotropy: one gene (HBB) with one mutation (glutamic acid to valine) produces abnormal hemoglobin that causes red blood cells to sickle under low oxygen. The sickled cells then cause a cascade of effects in multiple organ systems, producing the diverse symptoms of sickle cell disease.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
This is the hallmark of mitochondrial inheritance: affected mothers pass the trait to all their children, while affected fathers pass it to none. This is because mitochondria (and their DNA) come exclusively from the egg, not from sperm.
Real-World Applications
Example: A gene that has three or more alleles in the population is an example of:
Multiple alleles means that a single gene has more than two allelic forms in the population, although any individual still carries at most two alleles. The ABO blood group gene (I^A, I^B, i) is a classic example with three alleles.
Example: A woman who is a carrier for color blindness (X^C X^c) marries a man with normal color vision (X^C Y). What percentage of their sons would be expected to be color blind?
Sons receive their X chromosome from their mother. The carrier mother (X^C X^c) has a 50% chance of passing the X^c allele to each son. Sons who receive X^c Y will be color blind (50%), while sons who receive X^C Y will have normal vision (50%).
Example: Sickle cell disease is often described as a pleiotropic condition. A student asks why a single mutation in hemoglobin causes so many different symptoms (anemia, organ damage, pain crises, malaria resistance). What is the best explanation?
This is pleiotropy: one gene (HBB) with one mutation (glutamic acid to valine) produces abnormal hemoglobin that causes red blood cells to sickle under low oxygen. The sickled cells then cause a cascade of effects in multiple organ systems, producing the diverse symptoms of sickle cell disease.
Example: A breeder crosses two chickens: one with white feathers and one with black feathers. All F1 offspring have blue (grayish) feathers. When the F1 are crossed with each other, the F2 generation shows 1/4 white, 1/2 blue, and 1/4 black. However, examining the blue feathers closely reveals that they contain both white and black pigment granules side by side. Is this incomplete dominance or codominance?
Although the birds appear 'blue' from a distance, the presence of both distinct white and black pigment granules indicates codominance: both alleles are independently and fully expressed at the cellular level. In true incomplete dominance, a single intermediate pigment would be produced.

    [*] Inheritance of autosomal genes whose expression is limited to one sex due to hormonal or anatomical differences, even though both sexes carry the gene

    Explanation: Sex-limited traits are encoded by autosomal genes but expressed in only one sex due to hormonal regulation. For example, male pattern baldness and milk production are influenced by sex hormones, so the trait appears only (or predominantly) in one sex.

  Q21. A couple has a child with type AB blood. The mother has type A blood and the father has type B blood. Based on the ABO blood group system, what are the most likely genotypes of the parents?
    [*] Mother I^A i, Father I^B i
    [ ] Mother I^A I^B, Father ii
    [ ] Mother I^A I^A, Father I^B I^B
    [ ] Mother ii, Father I^A I^B
    Explanation: For a child to have type AB blood (I^A I^B), they must receive I^A from one parent and I^B from the other. The type A mother could be I^A I^A or I^A i, and the type B father could be I^B I^B or I^B i. However, since the question asks for the most likely genotypes (and both could also have a type O child if heterozygous), I^A i and I^B i are the most likely.

  Q22. A woman who is a carrier for color blindness (X^C X^c) marries a man with normal color vision (X^C Y). What percentage of their sons would be expected to be color blind?
    [ ] 100%
    [ ] 25%
    [*] 50%
    [ ] 0%
    Explanation: Sons receive their X chromosome from their mother. The carrier mother (X^C X^c) has a 50% chance of passing the X^c allele to each son. Sons who receive X^c Y will be color blind (50%), while sons who receive X^C Y will have normal vision (50%).

  Q23. Calico cats display patches of orange and black fur, with the orange gene (O) located on the X chromosome. Male calico cats are extremely rare. Why are most calico cats female?
    [*] The calico pattern requires two X chromosomes, one carrying each allele (X^O X^o), and random X-inactivation creates patches of each color
    [ ] Calico is caused by a mitochondrial gene inherited only from the mother
    [ ] The calico gene is autosomal but expressed only in females due to hormones
    [ ] Males cannot carry the orange allele
    Explanation: Calico coloring requires heterozygosity at the X-linked orange gene (X^O X^o). Random X-inactivation in each cell produces a mosaic pattern of orange and black patches. Males (XY) have only one X, so they express only one color (unless they have an XXY genotype).

  Q24. In Labrador retrievers, coat color depends on two genes: the B gene (B = black, b = brown) and the E gene (E = color deposited, e = no color deposited). A BbEe x BbEe cross produces puppies in a 9:3:4 ratio (9 black : 3 brown : 4 yellow). What type of gene interaction is occurring?
    [ ] Incomplete dominance at both loci
    [*] Recessive epistasis, where the ee genotype at the E locus masks the expression of the B gene
    [ ] Codominance between the B and E genes
    [ ] Polygenic inheritance of coat color
    Explanation: This is recessive epistasis: the ee genotype (homozygous recessive at the E locus) prevents any pigment from being deposited in the coat, producing a yellow dog regardless of the B genotype. The 9:3:4 ratio is a modification of the 9:3:3:1 ratio where the last two classes (3+1=4) are combined.

  Q25. Sickle cell disease is often described as a pleiotropic condition. A student asks why a single mutation in hemoglobin causes so many different symptoms (anemia, organ damage, pain crises, malaria resistance). What is the best explanation?
    [ ] The symptoms are caused by environmental factors rather than the mutation
    [ ] Each symptom is caused by a different mutation in a different gene

    [ ] Sickle cell disease involves mutations in multiple genes that happen to be linked
    Explanation: This is pleiotropy: one gene (HBB) with one mutation (glutamic acid to valine) produces abnormal hemoglobin that causes red blood cells to sickle under low oxygen. The sickled cells then cause a cascade of effects in multiple organ systems, producing the diverse symptoms of sickle cell disease.

  Q26. Human skin color is controlled by at least three genes, each with two alleles that contribute additively. A person with genotype AABBCC (darkest) crosses with a person with genotype aabbcc (lightest). What skin color would you expect in the F1 offspring?

    Explanation: In polygenic inheritance with additive alleles, the F1 generation from AABBCC x aabbcc would all be AaBbCc, with exactly 3 'contributing' alleles out of 6 possible. This produces a uniform intermediate phenotype in the F1 generation.

  Q27. A genetic disorder is traced through a family and shows the following pattern: all children of an affected mother are affected, but an affected father never passes the trait to his children. What type of inheritance is this?
    [ ] Autosomal recessive
    [ ] X-linked recessive
    [ ] Autosomal dominant
    [*] Mitochondrial (maternal) inheritance
    Explanation: This is the hallmark of mitochondrial inheritance: affected mothers pass the trait to all their children, while affected fathers pass it to none. This is because mitochondria (and their DNA) come exclusively from the egg, not from sperm.

  Q28. Two parents who both have intermediate height have children who range from very short to very tall. This wide range of offspring phenotypes from intermediate parents is best explained by:
    [ ] Incomplete dominance at a single gene locus when considering all relevant height factors
    [*] Polygenic inheritance, where many genes contribute additively and recombination produces offspring with different numbers of contributing alleles
    [ ] Autosomal recessive inheritance, which is consistent with how height mechanisms are understood to operate in theory
    [ ] X-linked inheritance of the height gene, as conventional height theory would suggest
    Explanation: Height is polygenic. When intermediate-height parents (heterozygous at multiple loci) cross, independent assortment produces offspring with many possible combinations of alleles, generating a continuous range from short to tall in the F2-like generation.

  Q29. Prader-Willi syndrome and Angelman syndrome are both caused by deletions of the same region on chromosome 15, yet they produce very different symptoms. The key difference is which parent the deletion is inherited from. What genetic mechanism explains this?
    [ ] X-linked inheritance, especially during periods of significant change

    Explanation: Genes in the chromosome 15q11-13 region are imprinted: some are only expressed from the paternal copy and others only from the maternal copy. Deletion of the paternal copy causes Prader-Willi syndrome; deletion of the maternal copy causes Angelman syndrome.

  Q30. A breeder crosses two chickens: one with white feathers and one with black feathers. All F1 offspring have blue (grayish) feathers. When the F1 are crossed with each other, the F2 generation shows 1/4 white, 1/2 blue, and 1/4 black. However, examining the blue feathers closely reveals that they contain both white and black pigment granules side by side. Is this incomplete dominance or codominance?
    [ ] Epistasis between a pigment gene and a modifier gene
    [ ] Incomplete dominance, because the phenotype appears intermediate
    [*] Codominance, because both white and black pigments are individually expressed in separate granules rather than being blended
    [ ] Complete dominance with variable expressivity
    Explanation: Although the birds appear 'blue' from a distance, the presence of both distinct white and black pigment granules indicates codominance: both alleles are independently and fully expressed at the cellular level. In true incomplete dominance, a single intermediate pigment would be produced.

--- Lesson 5.4: Mutations and Genetic Variation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mutations and Genetic Variation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'point mutation'?
A point mutation is the simplest type of mutation, involving the substitution, insertion, or deletion of a single nucleotide in the DNA. Point mutations include transitions (purine to purine), transversions (purine to pyrimidine), and single-base indels.
What is a 'frameshift mutation'?
A frameshift mutation occurs when insertions or deletions alter the triplet reading frame of the mRNA. Every codon downstream of the mutation is read differently, usually producing a completely different and nonfunctional amino acid sequence.
What is 'aneuploidy'?
Aneuploidy refers to having an abnormal number of chromosomes, such as trisomy (2n+1) or monosomy (2n-1). It usually results from nondisjunction during meiosis. Down syndrome (trisomy 21) is the most common viable aneuploidy in humans.
What is a 'silent mutation'?
A silent (synonymous) mutation changes a nucleotide but the new codon still specifies the same amino acid. This is possible because the genetic code has 64 codons but only 20 amino acids, so most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons.
What is a 'germline mutation'?
Germline mutations occur in the germ cells (cells that produce gametes) and are heritable, meaning they can be passed from parent to offspring. These mutations are the basis for inherited genetic diseases and are the raw material for evolution.
What is a 'nonsense mutation'?
A nonsense mutation changes a codon that normally specifies an amino acid into one of the three stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA). This causes the ribosome to release the incomplete polypeptide prematurely, producing a truncated and usually nonfunctional protein.
A conservation biologist studies two populations of cheetahs and finds that both have very low genetic variation. DNA analysis reveals that all cheetahs are nearly genetically identical. What is the most likely historical explanation for this low genetic diversity?
Cheetahs experienced one or more severe population bottlenecks (dramatic reductions in population size) that eliminated most genetic variation through genetic drift. The surviving small population carried only a fraction of the original diversity, and the species has remained genetically depauperate.
Real-World Applications

Frameshift mutations occur when insertions or deletions change the reading frame of the mRNA, altering every amino acid downstream of the mutation. This usually produces a completely nonfunctional protein.
Example: Which of the following is an example of a chromosomal inversion?
A chromosomal inversion occurs when a segment of a chromosome is excised, rotated 180 degrees, and reinserted. The gene order within the inverted segment is reversed, which can affect gene expression and suppress recombination in that region.
Example: A researcher discovers that two populations of the same species have very different allele frequencies for a gene controlling color pattern. One population lives in a forest (where dark coloration is advantageous) and the other in a desert (where light coloration is advantageous). Which evolutionary force most likely explains these differences?
Natural selection is the most likely explanation: in the forest, dark alleles provide a survival advantage (camouflage), while in the desert, light alleles are favored. Over time, selection increases the frequency of advantageous alleles in each environment.

--- Lesson 5.4: Mutations and Genetic Variation (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A point mutation that changes a single nucleotide and results in a different amino acid in the protein is called a:
    [ ] Frameshift mutation
    [ ] Nonsense mutation
    [ ] Silent mutation
    [*] Missense mutation
    Explanation: A missense mutation is a point mutation where a single nucleotide change results in a codon that specifies a different amino acid. The effect on protein function depends on the chemical properties of the new amino acid and its location in the protein.

  Q2. A nonsense mutation creates a premature stop codon, resulting in:
    [ ] A protein with one different amino acid
    [*] A truncated (shortened) protein that is usually nonfunctional
    [ ] A longer-than-normal protein
    [ ] No change in the protein
    Explanation: A nonsense mutation changes a sense codon into a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA), causing translation to terminate prematurely. The resulting truncated protein is usually nonfunctional because it lacks essential domains.

  Q3. Insertions and deletions of nucleotides that are not multiples of three cause:
    [*] Frameshift mutations that alter the reading frame of all downstream codons
    [ ] Polyploidy, as conventional deletions theory would suggest
    [ ] Translocations, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] Silent mutations according to widely held views on this topic
    Explanation: Frameshift mutations occur when insertions or deletions change the reading frame of the mRNA, altering every amino acid downstream of the mutation. This usually produces a completely nonfunctional protein.

  Q4. A silent mutation does not change the amino acid sequence because:
    [ ] DNA repair mechanisms always fix silent mutations
    [*] The genetic code is degenerate, so different codons can specify the same amino acid
    [ ] It occurs outside a gene
    [ ] The mutation is in the promoter region
    Explanation: The genetic code is degenerate (redundant), meaning most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon. A silent mutation changes a nucleotide but the new codon still codes for the same amino acid, so the protein is unaffected.

  Q5. Chromosomal mutations include all of the following EXCEPT:
    [ ] Translocations
    [*] Point mutations
    [ ] Inversions
    [ ] Deletions
    Explanation: Point mutations involve changes to individual nucleotides and are gene-level mutations. Chromosomal mutations are large-scale changes affecting whole segments of chromosomes and include deletions, duplications, inversions, and translocations.

  Q6. Which type of mutation is most likely to be harmful to an organism?
    [ ] Silent mutation
    [ ] A synonymous substitution
    [*] Frameshift mutation in the middle of a coding sequence
    [ ] A mutation in a non-coding intron
    Explanation: Frameshift mutations in coding regions alter every codon downstream of the insertion or deletion, typically producing a completely nonfunctional protein and often introducing premature stop codons. They are generally the most damaging type of point-level mutation.

  Q7. Genetic variation in sexually reproducing organisms arises from all of the following EXCEPT:
    [ ] Independent assortment of chromosomes
    [*] Mitotic cell division in somatic cells
    [ ] Crossing over during meiosis
    [ ] Random fertilization
    Explanation: Mitosis produces genetically identical daughter cells and does not generate genetic variation. Crossing over, independent assortment, and random fertilization all create new allele combinations, contributing to genetic diversity in populations.

  Q8. A duplication mutation involves:
    [ ] The movement of a segment from one chromosome to another
    [ ] The reversal of a segment within a chromosome
    [*] The repetition of a segment of a chromosome, resulting in extra copies of genes
    [ ] The loss of a segment of a chromosome
    Explanation: Gene duplication creates extra copies of a chromosomal segment. Over evolutionary time, duplicated genes can diverge and acquire new functions (neofunctionalization), making duplication an important source of genetic innovation.

  Q9. Somatic mutations differ from germline mutations in that somatic mutations:
    [ ] Are always lethal in most scenarios involving mutations factors
    [ ] Always affect the entire organism when viewed through a simplified
    [ ] Only occur during meiosis, based on conventional assumptions about
    [*] Are not passed to offspring because they occur in non-reproductive body cells
    Explanation: Somatic mutations occur in body cells (non-gamete cells) and affect only the individual and their descendant cells (e.g., cancer), but are not transmitted to offspring. Germline mutations occur in gamete-producing cells and can be inherited.

  Q10. Which of the following is an example of a chromosomal inversion?
    [ ] A segment of DNA is duplicated on the same chromosome
    [*] A segment of a chromosome is reversed in orientation (flipped 180 degrees)
    [ ] An entire extra set of chromosomes is present
    [ ] A segment of DNA is removed from a chromosome
    Explanation: A chromosomal inversion occurs when a segment of a chromosome is excised, rotated 180 degrees, and reinserted. The gene order within the inverted segment is reversed, which can affect gene expression and suppress recombination in that region.

  Q11. What is a 'point mutation'?
    [ ] A large-scale rearrangement of a chromosome
    [ ] The loss of an entire chromosome
    [ ] The insertion of an entire gene into a new chromosome
    [*] A change in a single nucleotide base pair in the DNA sequence
    Explanation: A point mutation is the simplest type of mutation, involving the substitution, insertion, or deletion of a single nucleotide in the DNA. Point mutations include transitions (purine to purine), transversions (purine to pyrimidine), and single-base indels.

  Q12. What does 'missense mutation' mean?
    [ ] A mutation that does not change the amino acid sequence
    [ ] A mutation that shifts the reading frame of the mRNA
    [ ] A mutation that creates a premature stop codon
    [*] A point mutation that changes a codon so that it specifies a different amino acid in the resulting protein
    Explanation: A missense mutation is a nucleotide substitution that results in a different amino acid being incorporated into the protein. The impact ranges from benign (conservative substitution) to severe (such as sickle cell disease, where glutamic acid is replaced by valine).

  Q13. What is a 'frameshift mutation'?
    [*] An insertion or deletion of nucleotides (not in multiples of three) that shifts the translational reading frame, altering all downstream codons
    [ ] A mutation in the promoter that shifts transcription to a new start site
    [ ] The reversal of a chromosomal segment
    [ ] A substitution of one nucleotide for another that changes the reading frame
    Explanation: A frameshift mutation occurs when insertions or deletions alter the triplet reading frame of the mRNA. Every codon downstream of the mutation is read differently, usually producing a completely different and nonfunctional amino acid sequence.

  Q14. What does 'translocation' mean in genetics?
    [ ] The movement of ribosomes along mRNA during protein synthesis
    [ ] The migration of DNA polymerase along the template strand
    [*] A chromosomal mutation in which a segment of one chromosome breaks off and attaches to a non-homologous chromosome
    [ ] The transport of proteins from the cytoplasm to the nucleus
    Explanation: A chromosomal translocation involves the transfer of a segment from one chromosome to a non-homologous chromosome. Reciprocal translocations (exchange between two chromosomes) and Robertsonian translocations (fusion of two acrocentric chromosomes) are the major types.

  Q15. What is 'aneuploidy'?
    [ ] A mutation that affects gene expression but not chromosome number
    [*] A condition in which the chromosome number is not an exact multiple of the haploid number, typically involving the gain or loss of individual chromosomes
    [ ] Having exactly the normal number of chromosomes
    [ ] Having three or more complete sets of chromosomes
    Explanation: Aneuploidy refers to having an abnormal number of chromosomes, such as trisomy (2n+1) or monosomy (2n-1). It usually results from nondisjunction during meiosis. Down syndrome (trisomy 21) is the most common viable aneuploidy in humans.

  Q16. What is a 'silent mutation'?
    [ ] A mutation that affects only introns and is always removed during splicing
    [*] A nucleotide substitution that does not change the amino acid sequence of the protein, due to the redundancy of the genetic code
    [ ] A mutation that silences a gene permanently through methylation
    [ ] A mutation that is never detected by DNA sequencing
    Explanation: A silent (synonymous) mutation changes a nucleotide but the new codon still specifies the same amino acid. This is possible because the genetic code has 64 codons but only 20 amino acids, so most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons.

  Q17. What does 'polyploidy' mean?
    [ ] A mutation affecting only a single base pair
    [*] A condition in which an organism has more than two complete sets of chromosomes (e.g., 3n, 4n)
    [ ] Having an extra copy of a single chromosome
    [ ] Having one fewer chromosome than normal
    Explanation: Polyploidy is the condition of having three or more complete sets of chromosomes (e.g., triploid 3n, tetraploid 4n). It is common in plants and is an important mechanism of speciation, as polyploid organisms are often reproductively isolated from their diploid relatives.

  Q18. What is a 'germline mutation'?
    [*] A mutation that occurs in cells that give rise to gametes (eggs or sperm), so it can be passed to offspring
    [ ] A mutation that only affects the immune system
    [ ] A mutation caused by exposure to germicidal agents
    [ ] A mutation that occurs in somatic cells during an individual's lifetime
    Explanation: Germline mutations occur in the germ cells (cells that produce gametes) and are heritable, meaning they can be passed from parent to offspring. These mutations are the basis for inherited genetic diseases and are the raw material for evolution.

  Q19. What does 'genetic variation' mean?
    [ ] The number of genes in an organism's genome
    [ ] The process by which organisms evolve over time
    [*] The differences in DNA sequences and traits among individuals within a population
    [ ] The total amount of DNA in a cell
    Explanation: Genetic variation refers to the diversity of alleles and genotypes within a population. It is generated by mutation, recombination, and sexual reproduction, and is essential for natural selection and adaptive evolution.

  Q20. What is a 'nonsense mutation'?
    [ ] A mutation that has no effect on the organism
    [ ] A mutation that inserts a new start codon into the coding region
    [ ] A mutation that changes one amino acid to a chemically similar one
    [*] A point mutation that converts a sense codon into a premature stop codon, resulting in a truncated protein
    Explanation: A nonsense mutation changes a codon that normally specifies an amino acid into one of the three stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA). This causes the ribosome to release the incomplete polypeptide prematurely, producing a truncated and usually nonfunctional protein.

  Q21. Sickle cell disease is caused by a single nucleotide substitution in the beta-globin gene (GAG to GTG), changing glutamic acid to valine at position 6. This is classified as what type of mutation, and why is it harmful?
    [*] A missense mutation that replaces a hydrophilic amino acid with a hydrophobic one, causing hemoglobin to polymerize and red blood cells to sickle
    [ ] A nonsense mutation that truncates the beta-globin protein
    [ ] A silent mutation that has no effect on protein structure

    Explanation: The sickle cell mutation is a missense mutation where hydrophilic glutamic acid is replaced by hydrophobic valine. This causes the hemoglobin molecules to stick together under low-oxygen conditions, forming stiff polymers that deform red blood cells into a sickle shape.

  Q22. Cystic fibrosis is most commonly caused by a three-nucleotide deletion (delta-F508) that removes a single phenylalanine amino acid from the CFTR protein. Why is this NOT a frameshift mutation, even though it involves a deletion?

  Q24. A researcher discovers that two populations of the same species have very different allele frequencies for a gene controlling color pattern. Which evolutionary force most likely explains these differences?

    Explanation: Natural selection is the most likely explanation: in the forest, dark alleles provide a survival advantage (camouflage), while in the desert, light alleles are favored. Over time, selection increases the frequency of advantageous alleles in each environment.

  Q26. A conservation biologist studies two populations of cheetahs and finds that both have very low genetic variation. DNA analysis reveals that all cheetahs are nearly genetically identical. What is the most likely historical explanation for this low genetic diversity?
    [ ] Cheetahs have an extremely high mutation rate that eliminates variation

    Explanation: Cheetahs experienced one or more severe population bottlenecks (dramatic reductions in population size) that eliminated most genetic variation through genetic drift. The surviving small population carried only a fraction of the original diversity, and the species has remained genetically depauperate.

  Q27. Gene duplication events have been important in evolution because duplicated genes can:
    [*] Accumulate mutations freely since one copy maintains the original function, potentially evolving new functions (neofunctionalization)
    [ ] Immediately create a new species through reproductive isolation
    [ ] Only produce identical proteins, providing no evolutionary advantage

  Q28. A mutation in a regulatory region upstream of a gene increases the gene's expression tenfold. Even though the protein's amino acid sequence is unchanged, the organism shows a dramatically different phenotype. What does this demonstrate about the nature of mutations?

  Q29. A population geneticist compares the DNA sequences of a gene in two related species and finds that the rate of synonymous (silent) substitutions is much higher than the rate of nonsynonymous (amino acid-changing) substitutions. What does this pattern suggest about the gene?

    Explanation: When synonymous substitutions exceed nonsynonymous substitutions (dN/dS < 1), it indicates purifying selection: most amino acid changes are deleterious and are removed by natural selection, while silent changes accumulate neutrally.   Q30. A student asks: 'If mutations are random and mostly harmful, how can they be the source of all genetic variation that drives evolution?' Which response best addresses this question?
    [ ] Organisms can direct their mutations toward beneficial changes in response to environmental needs

    Explanation: Mutations are indeed random and mostly neutral or harmful. However, across large populations and long time periods, the occasional beneficial mutation provides a selective advantage. Natural selection preserves these rare beneficial variants, and over generations, they accumulate, driving adaptive evolution.
