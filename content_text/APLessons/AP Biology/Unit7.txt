--- Lesson 7.1: Evolution and Evidence (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Evolution and Evidence
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a fossil?
A fossil is any preserved remains, trace, or impression of an organism from the past, typically found in sedimentary rock. Fossils document the history of life and provide direct evidence for evolution.
What is 'adaptive radiation'?
Adaptive radiation occurs when a single ancestor species rapidly diversifies into multiple new species, each adapted to different ecological niches. Darwin's finches on the Galapagos are a classic example.
What is a 'transitional fossil'?
A transitional fossil displays traits intermediate between an ancestral group and its descendants. Tiktaalik, for example, shows features of both fish and tetrapods, documenting the transition from water to land.
What is 'artificial selection'?
Artificial selection is the human-directed breeding of organisms to enhance specific traits. Darwin used artificial selection as evidence that populations can change over time, analogous to natural selection.
During embryonic development, human embryos temporarily develop pharyngeal pouches similar to those in fish embryos. A medical student asks why humans have these structures if they never develop into gills. What is the best evolutionary explanation?
Comparative embryology shows that vertebrates share developmental genes from a common ancestor. The pharyngeal pouches develop into gills in fish but are modified into middle ear bones, the Eustachian tube, and tonsils in humans, reflecting descent with modification.
Real-World Applications
Example: Similar embryonic development stages across vertebrate species provide evidence for:
Comparative embryology reveals that vertebrate embryos share similar developmental stages, such as pharyngeal pouches and post-anal tails, indicating that these organisms share a common ancestor.
Example: A farmer selectively breeds the cows that produce the most milk each generation. After 20 generations, the average milk production in the herd has doubled. How does this relate to Darwin's theory?
Darwin cited artificial selection as evidence that populations can be modified through selective breeding. The same principle operates in nature: individuals with traits that improve survival and reproduction contribute more offspring, gradually changing the population.
Example: On the Galapagos Islands, a severe drought kills most plants except those with hard, large seeds. Over several generations, researchers observe that the average beak size of a finch population increases. Which evidence for evolution does this real-time observation provide?
Peter and Rosemary Grant's research on Galapagos finches documented natural selection in real time. During drought, finches with larger beaks survived better because they could crack hard seeds, and this trait was passed to offspring, demonstrating observable evolutionary change.

--- Lesson 7.2: Mechanisms of Evolution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mechanisms of Evolution
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the ultimate source of all new genetic variation?
Mutation is the only mechanism that creates entirely new alleles. While sexual reproduction and gene flow reshuffle existing alleles, all genetic variation ultimately traces back to mutations in DNA.
What is 'fitness' in an evolutionary context?
Evolutionary fitness (Darwinian fitness) measures an organism's reproductive success relative to others in the population. It is not about strength but about contributing alleles to the next generation through surviving, fertile offspring.
What is 'gene flow'?
Gene flow (also called migration in genetics) is the transfer of alleles from one population to another through movement of individuals or their gametes. It tends to homogenize allele frequencies between connected populations.
What is the 'founder effect'?
The founder effect occurs when a few individuals colonize a new habitat and establish a population with allele frequencies that differ from the original population due to the small, non-representative sample of alleles they carry.
What is 'sexual selection'?
Sexual selection is a form of natural selection where traits that increase mating success are favored, even if they decrease survival. It includes intrasexual competition (contests between same-sex individuals) and intersexual selection (mate choice).
Human birth weight is subject to a type of selection where babies that are too small or too large have lower survival rates, while babies of intermediate weight have the highest survival. What type of selection is this, and what is its effect on the population?
This is stabilizing selection, the most common mode of natural selection. By selecting against extreme birth weights (both too low and too high), it reduces variation and maintains the population mean around an optimal value.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The antibiotic created a strong selective pressure. Bacteria with the pre-existing resistance allele survived and reproduced, while susceptible bacteria were killed. This is directional selection, increasing the frequency of the resistance allele in the population.
Real-World Applications
Example: The founder effect is an example of:
The founder effect occurs when a small group establishes a new population with allele frequencies that differ from the original population. It is a form of genetic drift because the change in allele frequencies results from chance sampling.
Example: Male peacocks have elaborate, colorful tails that make them more conspicuous to predators. However, males with the most impressive tails tend to mate more often. How do biologists explain the persistence of this seemingly disadvantageous trait?
This is intersexual selection: females preferentially choose males with elaborate tails. The mating advantage of having a large, colorful tail outweighs the survival cost of increased predation risk, maintaining the trait in the population.
Important Concepts
A population of beetles lives on a rocky hillside. Brown beetles blend in with the rocks while green beetles are easily spotted by birds. Over ten generations, the frequency of green beetles drops from 50% to 5%. Which mechanism of evolution is primarily responsible
This is directional natural selection. Birds preferentially prey on the more visible green beetles, giving brown beetles a survival advantage. Over generations, the allele for brown coloring increases in frequency while the green allele decreases.
A small group of birds is blown to an uninhabited island during a storm. This founding population has a much higher frequency of red feathers than the mainland population. After many generations, the island population remains mostly red-feathered. Which evolutionary concept explains this
The founder effect is a type of genetic drift. The small founding group carried a disproportionately high frequency of the red feather allele, and this initial allele frequency persisted in the new population independent of any selective advantage.

  Q1. Which of the following is required for natural selection to occur?

    Explanation: Natural selection requires heritable variation among individuals, differential survival and reproduction based on those traits, and that the traits are passed to offspring. Without variation, selection cannot act.

    [ ] Large populations with high genetic diversity

    [ ] Organisms within a population mate randomly
    [*] Alleles are transferred between populations through migration and interbreeding
    [ ] DNA is replicated within a cell

    Explanation: Gene flow is the movement of alleles between populations through the migration of individuals or their gametes. It tends to reduce genetic differences between populations and can introduce new alleles.

  Q4. Which type of selection favors individuals at both extremes of a phenotypic range over those with intermediate phenotypes?
    [ ] Sexual selection
    [ ] Directional selection
    [ ] Stabilizing selection
    [*] Disruptive selection

  Q5. The founder effect is an example of:
    [ ] Natural selection
    [*] Genetic drift
    [ ] Stabilizing selection
    [ ] Gene flow
    Explanation: The founder effect occurs when a small group establishes a new population with allele frequencies that differ from the original population. It is a form of genetic drift because the change in allele frequencies results from chance sampling.

    [ ] Natural selection
    [ ] Genetic drift

    [ ] Natural selection

--- Lesson 7.3: Population Genetics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Population Genetics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If the frequency of the recessive allele (q) in a population is 0.3, what is the frequency of the dominant allele (p)?
Since p + q = 1, if q = 0.3, then p = 1 - 0.3 = 0.7. This equation reflects that the two allele frequencies for a two-allele system must sum to 1 (100% of alleles in the gene pool).
In a population of 1000 individuals, 160 show the recessive phenotype (aa). What is the frequency of the recessive allele (q)?
The frequency of the recessive phenotype (q²) = 160/1000 = 0.16. Taking the square root: q = sqrt(0.16) = 0.4. Therefore, the frequency of the recessive allele is 0.4.
What is 'allele frequency'?
Allele frequency is the relative abundance of a particular allele at a genetic locus in a population, expressed as a proportion or percentage. Changes in allele frequencies over time define microevolution.
What is 'genotype frequency'?
Genotype frequency is the fraction of individuals in a population that carry a specific genotype (e.g., AA, Aa, or aa). Under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, genotype frequencies can be predicted from allele frequencies using p², 2pq, and q².
What is 'random mating'?
Random mating (panmixia) means every individual has an equal probability of mating with any other individual regardless of genotype. It is a requirement for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium and ensures genotype frequencies match expected proportions.
What is 'genetic variation'?
Genetic variation refers to the diversity of alleles and genotypes within a population. It is the raw material for evolution, arising through mutation, sexual reproduction, and gene flow, and is essential for natural selection to operate.
Real-World Applications
Example: If a population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium with p = 0.6 and q = 0.4, what percentage of the population is expected to be heterozygous?
The frequency of heterozygous individuals is 2pq = 2(0.6)(0.4) = 0.48, or 48%. This is the expected proportion of heterozygous individuals under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
Example: What does 'heterozygote advantage' mean?
Heterozygote advantage (overdominance) occurs when heterozygous individuals have higher fitness than either homozygote. The classic example is sickle cell trait: heterozygotes resist malaria better than either homozygote, maintaining both alleles.
Example: In a region of Africa where malaria is prevalent, the frequency of the sickle cell allele (HbS) is about 0.12, much higher than in populations without malaria. Scientists hypothesize that heterozygote advantage maintains this allele. Which prediction would support this hypothesis?
Heterozygote advantage (balancing selection) predicts that heterozygotes have the highest fitness. HbA/HbS heterozygotes resist malaria (unlike HbA/HbA) and avoid severe sickle cell disease (unlike HbS/HbS), maintaining both alleles at stable frequencies in malaria regions.
Example: A conservation biologist is managing a small, endangered population of 50 wolves. She is concerned about genetic drift and inbreeding. Which management strategy would best address both concerns?
Introducing individuals from other populations creates gene flow, which increases genetic diversity, reduces inbreeding, and counteracts the harmful effects of genetic drift in small populations. This strategy is called genetic rescue.

  Q1. According to the Hardy-Weinberg principle, allele frequencies in a population remain constant when:
    [ ] Organisms only reproduce asexually
    [ ] Natural selection is strongly favoring one allele
    [*] There is no mutation, selection, migration, genetic drift, or non-random mating
    [ ] The population is very small
    Explanation: Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium requires five conditions: no mutation, random mating, no natural selection, an infinitely large population (no genetic drift), and no gene flow. When all conditions are met, allele and genotype frequencies remain constant.

  Q2. In the Hardy-Weinberg equation p² + 2pq + q² = 1, what does 2pq represent?
    [*] The frequency of heterozygous individuals
    [ ] The frequency of homozygous recessive individuals
    [ ] The frequency of homozygous dominant individuals
    [ ] The total allele frequency in the population
    Explanation: In the Hardy-Weinberg equation, p² represents homozygous dominant frequency, q² represents homozygous recessive frequency, and 2pq represents the frequency of heterozygous individuals in the population.

  Q3. If the frequency of the recessive allele (q) in a population is 0.3, what is the frequency of the dominant allele (p)?

    [ ] 0.3
    [*] 0.7
    [ ] 0.09
    Explanation: Since p + q = 1, if q = 0.3, then p = 1 - 0.3 = 0.7. This equation reflects that the two allele frequencies for a two-allele system must sum to 1 (100% of alleles in the gene pool).

  Q4. A population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium for a particular gene. Which of the following must be true?
    [ ] The dominant allele is increasing in frequency
    [ ] The population is evolving rapidly
    [ ] All individuals are homozygous for the gene

    Explanation: Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium means allele and genotype frequencies are not changing, which is the definition of a non-evolving population. It serves as a null model against which real populations are compared to detect evolution.

    [ ] 0.84

--- Lesson 7.4: Macroevolution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Macroevolution
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is speciation?
Speciation is the evolutionary process by which populations diverge and develop reproductive isolation, eventually forming distinct species that can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring.
What is 'sympatric speciation'?
Sympatric speciation (sym = same, patric = homeland) occurs without geographic isolation. It can result from polyploidy, habitat differentiation within the same area, or sexual selection leading to assortative mating.
What is 'gradualism' in evolutionary biology?
Gradualism proposes that evolutionary change occurs through the slow, continuous accumulation of small modifications over many generations. Darwin favored this view, and it contrasts with the punctuated equilibrium model.
What is a 'prezygotic barrier'?
Prezygotic barriers act before fertilization to prevent hybrid zygote formation. They include habitat isolation, temporal isolation, behavioral isolation, mechanical isolation, and gametic isolation.
What is a 'postzygotic barrier'?
Postzygotic barriers act after a hybrid zygote forms. They include hybrid inviability (hybrid fails to develop), hybrid sterility (hybrid is infertile), and hybrid breakdown (F2 or later generations have reduced fitness).
Two closely related bird species occasionally mate and produce hybrid offspring that survive but are sterile. A scientist argues that these species are maintained as distinct biological species. What is the basis for this argument?
Under the biological species concept, species are defined by reproductive isolation. Even though mating occurs, the sterile hybrids cannot pass on genes, so no gene flow occurs between species. Hybrid sterility is an effective postzygotic barrier maintaining species boundaries.
Real-World Applications
Example: A mule is the offspring of a horse and a donkey. The mule is sterile. This is an example of which postzygotic barrier?
Hybrid sterility is a postzygotic barrier where the hybrid offspring is viable but unable to reproduce. Mules are sterile because horses and donkeys have different chromosome numbers, preventing proper meiosis.
Example: Which of the following is an example of a prezygotic barrier called mechanical isolation?
Mechanical isolation occurs when differences in the size or shape of reproductive organs prevent successful mating between species. This is a prezygotic barrier because it prevents the formation of a zygote.
Example: A researcher examining the fossil record of a marine invertebrate finds that the species shows little morphological change for 3 million years, then rapidly develops new shell features over about 50,000 years before stabilizing again. Which model of evolutionary change does this pattern support?
This pattern of long stasis followed by rapid morphological change is the hallmark of punctuated equilibrium. The rapid change may correspond to a speciation event, while the long stable period represents the species in equilibrium with its environment.

--- Lesson 7.4: Macroevolution (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is speciation?
    [ ] The extinction of a species
    [*] The formation of new species from an ancestral population
    [ ] The adaptation of a species to a new environment
    [ ] The migration of a species to a new location
    Explanation: Speciation is the evolutionary process by which populations diverge and develop reproductive isolation, eventually forming distinct species that can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring.

  Q2. Allopatric speciation occurs when populations are separated by:
    [*] A physical geographic barrier
    [ ] Different mating seasons
    [ ] Behavioral differences
    [ ] Chromosomal mutations
    Explanation: Allopatric speciation occurs when a geographic barrier (mountain range, river, ocean) physically separates populations, preventing gene flow and allowing independent evolution to produce reproductive isolation.

  Q3. Which of the following is a prezygotic reproductive barrier?
    [ ] Hybrid sterility
    [*] Temporal isolation due to different breeding seasons
    [ ] Hybrid inviability
    [ ] Reduced fitness of hybrid offspring
    Explanation: Temporal isolation is a prezygotic barrier that prevents mating because species breed at different times (different seasons, times of day, or years). Prezygotic barriers prevent the formation of hybrid zygotes.

  Q4. Sympatric speciation differs from allopatric speciation because it occurs:
    [*] Without geographic separation of populations
    [ ] Only in aquatic environments
    [ ] Over much longer time periods than allopatric speciation
    [ ] Only through natural selection
    Explanation: Sympatric speciation occurs within the same geographic area without a physical barrier. It can result from polyploidy (especially in plants), habitat differentiation, or disruptive selection that creates reproductive isolation.

  Q5. Polyploidy is most commonly associated with speciation in:
    [ ] Mammals
    [*] Plants
    [ ] Bacteria
    [ ] Insects
    Explanation: Polyploidy (having more than two complete sets of chromosomes) is a major mechanism of sympatric speciation in plants. Many crop species are polyploid. It is rare in animals because it disrupts sex determination.

  Q6. The biological species concept defines a species as a group of organisms that:
    [ ] Live in the same geographic area
    [*] Can interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring
    [ ] Look physically identical
    [ ] Share the same number of chromosomes
    Explanation: The biological species concept, proposed by Ernst Mayr, defines species as populations that can interbreed in nature to produce viable, fertile offspring and are reproductively isolated from other such groups.

  Q7. Mass extinctions are significant in macroevolution because they:
    [ ] Have no long-term effect on evolution
    [*] Open ecological niches that surviving lineages can diversify into through adaptive radiation
    [ ] Only affect organisms in the ocean
    [ ] Permanently reduce biodiversity with no recovery
    Explanation: Mass extinctions eliminate many species and open ecological niches. Surviving lineages often undergo adaptive radiation to fill these vacant niches, dramatically reshaping the diversity of life. For example, mammals radiated after the dinosaur extinction.

  Q8. A mule is the offspring of a horse and a donkey. The mule is sterile. This is an example of which postzygotic barrier?
    [ ] Mechanical isolation
    [ ] Temporal isolation
    [*] Hybrid sterility
    [ ] Gametic isolation
    Explanation: Hybrid sterility is a postzygotic barrier where the hybrid offspring is viable but unable to reproduce. Mules are sterile because horses and donkeys have different chromosome numbers, preventing proper meiosis.

  Q9. The punctuated equilibrium model proposes that:
    [*] Species remain largely unchanged for long periods, with rapid bursts of change associated with speciation events
    [ ] Extinction never occurs in evolution
    [ ] Evolution occurs at a constant, gradual rate
    [ ] All evolution occurs through genetic drift
    Explanation: Punctuated equilibrium, proposed by Gould and Eldredge, suggests that species experience long periods of stasis with little change, interrupted by brief episodes of rapid evolutionary change, typically associated with speciation events.

  Q10. Which of the following is an example of a prezygotic barrier called mechanical isolation?
    [ ] Two species use different courtship songs
    [ ] Hybrid offspring fail to develop properly
    [*] Two species have incompatible reproductive structures that prevent mating
    [ ] Two species breed at different times of year
    Explanation: Mechanical isolation occurs when differences in the size or shape of reproductive organs prevent successful mating between species. This is a prezygotic barrier because it prevents the formation of a zygote.

  Q11. What does 'reproductive isolation' mean?
    [ ] The inability of organisms within a species to reproduce
    [*] Barriers that prevent members of different species from interbreeding or producing fertile offspring
    [ ] The process by which organisms reproduce asexually
    [ ] The physical separation of organisms from their mates
    Explanation: Reproductive isolation encompasses all biological barriers that prevent members of different species from interbreeding or producing viable, fertile offspring. These barriers maintain species as distinct evolutionary lineages.

  Q12. What does 'allopatric speciation' mean?
    [ ] Speciation that occurs within the same geographic area
    [ ] Speciation caused exclusively by polyploidy
    [ ] The process by which two species merge into one
    [*] Speciation that occurs when a population is split by a geographic barrier, preventing gene flow and allowing independent evolution
    Explanation: Allopatric speciation (allo = other, patric = homeland) occurs when populations are geographically separated. Without gene flow, the isolated populations independently accumulate genetic differences that eventually lead to reproductive isolation.

  Q13. What is 'sympatric speciation'?
    [ ] Speciation that requires millions of years
    [ ] Speciation caused only by natural selection
    [*] Speciation that occurs within the same geographic area without physical separation of populations
    [ ] The extinction of one species replaced by another
    Explanation: Sympatric speciation (sym = same, patric = homeland) occurs without geographic isolation. It can result from polyploidy, habitat differentiation within the same area, or sexual selection leading to assortative mating.

  Q14. What does 'polyploidy' mean?
    [*] Having more than two complete sets of chromosomes in a cell
    [ ] Having exactly two sets of chromosomes
    [ ] Having fewer chromosomes than normal
    [ ] Having one set of chromosomes
    Explanation: Polyploidy is the condition of having more than two complete sets of chromosomes (e.g., triploid = 3n, tetraploid = 4n). It is a major mechanism of instant speciation in plants because polyploid individuals are often reproductively isolated from their diploid parents.

  Q15. What does 'adaptive radiation' mean?
    [ ] The spread of one species across many continents
    [ ] The gradual extinction of many species in a lineage
    [*] The rapid diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many species adapted to different ecological niches
    [ ] The evolution of radiation resistance in organisms
    Explanation: Adaptive radiation is the rapid evolutionary diversification of a single ancestral species into many descendant species, each filling different ecological niches. It typically occurs after mass extinctions or colonization of new habitats with open niches.

  Q16. What is 'gradualism' in evolutionary biology?
    [*] The idea that evolution occurs through slow, steady accumulation of small changes over long periods
    [ ] The gradual decrease in population size leading to extinction
    [ ] The process of grading organisms by their fitness
    [ ] The sudden appearance of new species without transitional forms
    Explanation: Gradualism proposes that evolutionary change occurs through the slow, continuous accumulation of small modifications over many generations. Darwin favored this view, and it contrasts with the punctuated equilibrium model.

  Q17. What does 'punctuated equilibrium' mean?
    [ ] A model stating that evolution always occurs at a constant rate
    [*] A model of evolution proposing long periods of little change (stasis) interrupted by brief periods of rapid speciation
    [ ] The extinction of organisms due to sudden environmental changes
    [ ] The process by which organisms reach a stable equilibrium with their environment
    Explanation: Punctuated equilibrium, proposed by Gould and Eldredge, suggests species are generally stable (in equilibrium) for most of their existence, with evolutionary change concentrated in relatively brief episodes associated with speciation events.

  Q18. What is a 'prezygotic barrier'?
    [ ] A mutation that prevents cell division
    [ ] A mechanism that prevents hybrid offspring from surviving
    [ ] A geographic barrier that separates populations
    [*] A reproductive barrier that prevents the formation of a zygote by impeding mating or fertilization between species
    Explanation: Prezygotic barriers act before fertilization to prevent hybrid zygote formation. They include habitat isolation, temporal isolation, behavioral isolation, mechanical isolation, and gametic isolation.

  Q19. What is a 'postzygotic barrier'?
    [ ] A physical barrier that separates two populations
    [ ] A barrier that prevents organisms from migrating to new habitats
    [ ] A barrier that prevents DNA replication
    [*] A reproductive barrier that acts after fertilization, reducing the viability or fertility of hybrid offspring
    Explanation: Postzygotic barriers act after a hybrid zygote forms. They include hybrid inviability (hybrid fails to develop), hybrid sterility (hybrid is infertile), and hybrid breakdown (F2 or later generations have reduced fitness).

  Q20. What does 'mass extinction' mean?
    [*] A dramatic, widespread event in which a large proportion of all species on Earth go extinct in a geologically short period
    [ ] The normal background rate of species extinction
    [ ] The extinction of a single species due to competition
    [ ] The local disappearance of a population from one habitat
    Explanation: A mass extinction is a global event where a significant proportion of species go extinct in a relatively short geological time span. Five major mass extinctions are recognized in Earth's history, each followed by periods of adaptive radiation.

  Q21. The Grand Canyon has separated two populations of squirrels for millions of years. The Kaibab squirrel on the North Rim has a white tail, while the Abert's squirrel on the South Rim has a gray tail. They can no longer interbreed successfully. What type of speciation does this illustrate?
    [ ] Artificial selection by humans living near the canyon
    [*] Allopatric speciation, where the geographic barrier of the canyon prevented gene flow and allowed independent evolution
    [ ] Sympatric speciation through habitat differentiation
    [ ] Parapatric speciation along the edge of the canyon
    Explanation: This is a classic example of allopatric speciation. The Grand Canyon physically separated the squirrel populations, preventing gene flow. Over millions of years, independent evolution led to distinct morphological and genetic differences and reproductive isolation.

  Q22. A plant species has 2n = 14 chromosomes. After an error in meiosis, a tetraploid individual (4n = 28) is produced. This individual can self-fertilize and produce fertile tetraploid offspring, but cannot produce fertile offspring with the original diploid species. What has occurred?
    [*] Sympatric speciation through autopolyploidy, creating instant reproductive isolation
    [ ] Extinction of the original species
    [ ] Allopatric speciation through geographic isolation
    [ ] Gradual divergence due to natural selection
    Explanation: This is sympatric speciation through autopolyploidy. The tetraploid cannot produce fertile offspring with the diploid parent species because crosses produce triploid offspring with unbalanced chromosome sets. A new species was instantly created without geographic isolation.

  Q23. Two species of frogs live in the same pond. Species A calls to mates in the evening, while Species B calls in the early morning. Even though they could physically interbreed, they never do. What type of reproductive barrier maintains their species identity?
    [ ] Hybrid sterility, a postzygotic barrier
    [*] Temporal isolation, a prezygotic barrier based on different mating times
    [ ] Mechanical isolation due to incompatible body sizes
    [ ] Habitat isolation because they live in different ponds
    Explanation: This is temporal isolation, a prezygotic barrier. The two species are active and calling at different times, so they never encounter potential mates from the other species. No geographic barrier is needed; timing alone prevents interbreeding.

  Q24. After the mass extinction event that killed the dinosaurs 66 million years ago, mammals rapidly diversified into many ecological roles previously occupied by dinosaurs. Within 10 million years, ancestral mammals had given rise to bats, whales, primates, and many other groups. What macroevolutionary pattern does this represent?
    [*] Adaptive radiation following a mass extinction that opened ecological niches
    [ ] Convergent evolution of unrelated lineages
    [ ] Stabilizing selection maintaining mammalian body forms
    [ ] Genetic drift in small mammalian populations
    Explanation: This is adaptive radiation: the rapid diversification of mammals into many new species occupying diverse ecological niches vacated by extinct dinosaurs. Mass extinctions often trigger adaptive radiations by removing dominant competitors and opening resources.

  Q25. Two closely related bird species occasionally mate and produce hybrid offspring that survive but are sterile. A scientist argues that these species are maintained as distinct biological species. What is the basis for this argument?
    [ ] The species must be in different geographic areas
    [ ] The hybrid offspring look different from both parent species
    [ ] The hybrids will eventually evolve to become fertile
    [*] The postzygotic barrier of hybrid sterility prevents gene flow between the species, maintaining reproductive isolation
    Explanation: Under the biological species concept, species are defined by reproductive isolation. Even though mating occurs, the sterile hybrids cannot pass on genes, so no gene flow occurs between species. Hybrid sterility is an effective postzygotic barrier maintaining species boundaries.

  Q26. A researcher examining the fossil record of a marine invertebrate finds that the species shows little morphological change for 3 million years, then rapidly develops new shell features over about 50,000 years before stabilizing again. Which model of evolutionary change does this pattern support?
    [*] Punctuated equilibrium, with long stasis interrupted by rapid change
    [ ] The species was not evolving at any point during this time
    [ ] Constant directional selection throughout the entire period
    [ ] Gradualism, with slow and steady change
    Explanation: This pattern of long stasis followed by rapid morphological change is the hallmark of punctuated equilibrium. The rapid change may correspond to a speciation event, while the long stable period represents the species in equilibrium with its environment.

  Q27. Apple maggot flies originally laid eggs on hawthorn fruits. When apple trees were introduced to North America, some flies began laying eggs on apples instead. These two populations now have different host preferences and breed at different times (apples fruit earlier). Genetic analysis shows the populations are diverging. What type of speciation may be in progress?
    [*] Sympatric speciation through habitat differentiation and temporal isolation
    [ ] Speciation caused exclusively by genetic drift
    [ ] Allopatric speciation due to a mountain range separating the populations
    [ ] Artificial selection by farmers
    Explanation: Apple maggot flies are a textbook example of sympatric speciation in progress. The two populations live in the same area but are reproductively isolated by host preference (habitat isolation) and different fruiting times (temporal isolation), leading to genetic divergence.

  Q28. Eastern and western meadowlarks look nearly identical and have overlapping ranges, but they have different songs and rarely interbreed. What type of prezygotic barrier maintains their species identity?
    [ ] Geographic isolation since they live in different areas
    [*] Behavioral isolation through species-specific courtship songs
    [ ] Hybrid breakdown in later generations
    [ ] Gametic isolation preventing fertilization
    Explanation: This is behavioral isolation (also called ethological isolation). Despite living in the same areas and looking similar, these species recognize mates through species-specific songs. Females only respond to the correct song, preventing interbreeding.

  Q29. Cichlid fish in Lake Victoria, Africa, have diversified into over 500 species in less than 15,000 years. Species differ in jaw shape, diet, coloration, and habitat use. What combination of evolutionary mechanisms best explains this extraordinary diversification?
    [*] Adaptive radiation driven by natural and sexual selection exploiting diverse ecological niches in the lake
    [ ] Gene flow between all 500 species maintaining diversity
    [ ] Artificial selection by local fishermen
    [ ] Only genetic drift operating on isolated island populations
    Explanation: Lake Victoria cichlids represent one of the fastest known adaptive radiations. Natural selection drove adaptation to different food sources and habitats, while sexual selection based on coloration promoted reproductive isolation, together producing remarkable species diversity.

  Q30. A wheat farmer discovers a plant in the field that is larger and has twice the normal number of chromosomes. This plant can produce seeds when self-pollinated but cannot cross with normal wheat plants. The farmer breeds this plant and creates a new variety. What evolutionary process occurred in the field?
    [ ] Allopatric speciation through geographic isolation
    [ ] Extinction of the original wheat variety
    [ ] Gradual divergence through many generations of natural selection
    [*] Instantaneous sympatric speciation through autopolyploidy, creating a new species in one generation
    Explanation: This is autopolyploidy, where chromosome doubling within a single species creates a new polyploid individual that is reproductively isolated from the diploid parent species. This is one of the few mechanisms that can produce a new species in a single generation, and it is common in plants.

--- Lesson 7.5: Phylogenetics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Phylogenetics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'cladogram'?
A cladogram is a type of phylogenetic tree that depicts evolutionary relationships based on shared derived characters (synapomorphies). Branch lengths in a cladogram do not necessarily represent time or the amount of evolutionary change.
What is a 'paraphyletic group'?
A paraphyletic group includes a common ancestor and some but not all descendants. For example, 'reptiles' is paraphyletic if birds are excluded, since birds descended from reptilian ancestors. Modern systematics avoids paraphyletic groups.
What is 'molecular phylogenetics'?
Molecular phylogenetics uses sequence data from DNA, RNA, or proteins to reconstruct evolutionary relationships. It has revolutionized systematics by providing abundant, objective data for tree construction.
What is 'horizontal gene transfer'?
Horizontal (lateral) gene transfer is the movement of genetic material between organisms outside the normal parent-to-offspring transmission. It is common in bacteria (via conjugation, transformation, and transduction) and complicates phylogenetic analysis.
Researchers sequencing genomes of different bacterial species find that a gene for antibiotic resistance appears in distantly related species scattered across the bacterial tree of life. The gene sequences are nearly identical. What is the most likely explanation?
Nearly identical gene sequences in distantly related bacterial species are best explained by horizontal gene transfer. Bacteria frequently exchange genes via conjugation, transformation, and transduction, spreading traits like antibiotic resistance across species boundaries.
Real-World Applications
Example: A conservation biologist needs to prioritize species for protection. She uses phylogenetic diversity as a criterion, aiming to preserve the maximum evolutionary history. Of these four species, which should receive the highest priority: Species A (one of 50 closely related species in its clade), Species B (the sole surviving member of an ancient lineage), Species C (a common species with many close relatives), or Species D (a recently diverged subspecies)?
Phylogenetic diversity prioritizes species representing unique evolutionary lineages. Species B, as the sole survivor of an ancient lineage, represents more unique evolutionary history. Its extinction would remove an entire branch from the tree of life, whereas losing one of 50 similar species has less impact on total evolutionary diversity.

--- Lesson 7.5: Phylogenetics (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A phylogenetic tree is used to represent:
    [*] The evolutionary relationships among species or other taxa
    [ ] The geographic distribution of species
    [ ] The life cycle of a single organism
    [ ] The food web in an ecosystem
    Explanation: A phylogenetic tree (phylogeny) is a branching diagram that depicts the hypothesized evolutionary relationships among groups of organisms, showing how lineages diverged from common ancestors over time.

  Q2. On a cladogram, a node represents:
    [ ] An extant species
    [ ] The extinction of a species
    [ ] A geographic location
    [*] A hypothetical common ancestor where lineages diverged
    Explanation: Each node (branch point) on a cladogram represents a hypothetical common ancestor from which two or more descendant lineages diverged. The node is where speciation is hypothesized to have occurred.

  Q3. A shared derived character (synapomorphy) is used in cladistics to:
    [ ] Measure the rate of evolution
    [*] Group organisms into clades based on common ancestry
    [ ] Show that two species live in the same habitat
    [ ] Determine the age of a fossil
    Explanation: Shared derived characters (synapomorphies) are traits that evolved in a common ancestor and are shared by its descendants. They are the key evidence used to construct cladograms and identify monophyletic groups (clades).

  Q4. An outgroup in phylogenetic analysis is:
    [ ] A species that has gone extinct in most scenarios involving outgroup factors
    [ ] The species with the most derived characters according to widely held views on this topic
    [*] A taxon closely related to but not part of the group being studied, used to determine which characters are ancestral
    [ ] The most derived species in the tree regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    Explanation: An outgroup is a taxon that diverged before the group of interest (ingroup). By comparing the outgroup's characters to those of the ingroup, scientists determine which traits are ancestral (shared with the outgroup) and which are derived (unique to the ingroup).

  Q5. Maximum parsimony in phylogenetics assumes that:
    [ ] Convergent evolution never occurs
    [*] The simplest explanation requiring the fewest evolutionary changes is most likely correct
    [ ] Evolution always occurs at a constant rate
    [ ] All species evolved from a single common ancestor at the same time
    Explanation: Maximum parsimony selects the phylogenetic tree that requires the fewest evolutionary changes (character state transitions). It applies Occam's razor, assuming the simplest explanation is the best hypothesis.

  Q6. Molecular phylogenetics uses which type of data to construct evolutionary trees?
    [*] DNA, RNA, or protein sequences
    [ ] Behavioral observations only
    [ ] Fossil morphology only
    [ ] Geographic distribution data only
    Explanation: Molecular phylogenetics constructs evolutionary trees using DNA, RNA, or protein sequence data. Sequence comparisons reveal patterns of similarity and difference that reflect evolutionary relationships and divergence times.

  Q7. A monophyletic group (clade) includes:
    [ ] Only the most recently evolved species
    [ ] Only species with identical DNA sequences
    [*] An ancestor and all of its descendants
    [ ] All species that share the same habitat
    Explanation: A monophyletic group or clade consists of a common ancestor plus all of its descendants. Valid clades can be identified on a phylogenetic tree by making a single cut on a branch and collecting everything above the cut.

  Q8. Which of the following would be most useful for determining the phylogenetic relationship between two species of bacteria?
    [ ] Comparing their growth rate on agar plates
    [ ] Comparing their size under a microscope
    [ ] Comparing their fossil records
    [*] Comparing their ribosomal RNA gene sequences
    Explanation: Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes are highly conserved across all life and are the standard molecular marker for bacterial phylogenetics. Bacteria rarely fossilize and have simple morphology, making molecular data essential for determining relationships.

  Q9. On a phylogenetic tree, species that share a more recent common ancestor are:
    [ ] Always in the same geographic location
    [*] More closely related to each other
    [ ] Less closely related to each other
    [ ] Always the same species
    Explanation: On a phylogenetic tree, the more recent the common ancestor shared by two taxa, the more closely related they are. Closeness of relationship is determined by recency of common ancestry, not by physical similarity.

  Q10. Horizontal gene transfer in bacteria can make phylogenetic analysis difficult because:
    [ ] Bacteria have no evolutionary history
    [ ] Bacteria do not have DNA
    [ ] Bacteria reproduce too quickly to study
    [*] Genes can be transferred between unrelated species, creating conflicting evolutionary signals
    Explanation: Horizontal (lateral) gene transfer moves genes between unrelated organisms outside of reproduction. This means different genes in the same organism may have different evolutionary histories, making it difficult to reconstruct a single, accurate tree.

  Q11. What is a 'cladogram'?
    [ ] A chart comparing the physical characteristics of animals
    [ ] A graph of population size over time
    [ ] A map showing where different species live
    [*] A branching diagram that represents hypothesized evolutionary relationships among taxa based on shared derived characters
    Explanation: A cladogram is a type of phylogenetic tree that depicts evolutionary relationships based on shared derived characters (synapomorphies). Branch lengths in a cladogram do not necessarily represent time or the amount of evolutionary change.

  Q12. What does 'synapomorphy' mean?
    [ ] A trait that evolved independently in unrelated lineages
    [ ] A primitive character that has not changed since the origin of life
    [ ] A trait that is found in all living organisms
    [*] A shared derived character that evolved in the common ancestor of a clade and is inherited by all members of that clade
    Explanation: A synapomorphy is a shared derived character that originated in the most recent common ancestor of a clade. Synapomorphies are the basis for defining clades in cladistic analysis. For example, feathers are a synapomorphy of birds.

  Q13. What does 'monophyletic group' mean?
    [*] A taxonomic group consisting of a single common ancestor and all of its descendants
    [ ] A group of unrelated organisms that look similar
    [ ] A group of organisms that are all the same species
    [ ] A group of species that live on the same continent
    Explanation: A monophyletic group (clade) includes a common ancestor and all organisms descended from that ancestor. It represents a complete branch on the tree of life. Valid phylogenetic classifications should consist only of monophyletic groups.

  Q14. What is a 'paraphyletic group'?
    [ ] A group of organisms from different kingdoms
    [ ] A group that includes a common ancestor and all of its descendants
    [*] A group that includes a common ancestor and some, but not all, of its descendants
    [ ] A group of organisms that have no common ancestor
    Explanation: A paraphyletic group includes a common ancestor and some but not all descendants. For example, 'reptiles' is paraphyletic if birds are excluded, since birds descended from reptilian ancestors. Modern systematics avoids paraphyletic groups.

  Q15. What does 'outgroup' mean in phylogenetics?
    [ ] The most recently evolved species in a clade
    [ ] An organism that does not belong to any taxonomic group
    [*] A taxon that is closely related to but not part of the group being studied, used as a reference to determine character polarity
    [ ] A species that has recently gone extinct
    Explanation: An outgroup is a reference taxon that branched off before the group being studied (ingroup). It helps determine which character states are ancestral (plesiomorphic) versus derived (apomorphic) for the ingroup.

  Q16. What does 'homoplasy' mean?
    [ ] A type of fossil preservation based on the conventional understanding of how evolution processes interact in practice
    [*] Similar traits in different lineages that did not arise from a common ancestor, resulting from convergent evolution, parallelism, or reversal
    [ ] The complete genome of an organism, particularly when evolution considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    [ ] The inheritance of traits from a common ancestor in most scenarios involving evolution factors
    Explanation: Homoplasy refers to similarities between organisms that are not due to inheritance from a common ancestor. It includes convergent evolution, parallel evolution, and evolutionary reversals, and it can mislead phylogenetic analysis.

  Q17. What does 'maximum parsimony' mean in phylogenetics?
    [*] A principle that selects the phylogenetic tree requiring the fewest evolutionary changes as the best hypothesis
    [ ] A method that always produces the longest possible phylogenetic tree
    [ ] A technique for dating fossils using radioactive isotopes
    [ ] A rule that the most complex explanation is always correct
    Explanation: Maximum parsimony is a tree-building method based on Occam's razor: among all possible trees, the one requiring the fewest character state changes is preferred as the simplest and most likely hypothesis of evolutionary relationships.

  Q18. What is 'molecular phylogenetics'?
    [ ] The process of extracting DNA from fossils
    [ ] The classification of organisms based only on physical appearance
    [*] The use of DNA, RNA, or protein sequence data to infer evolutionary relationships and construct phylogenetic trees
    [ ] The study of molecules within a single cell
    Explanation: Molecular phylogenetics uses sequence data from DNA, RNA, or proteins to reconstruct evolutionary relationships. It has revolutionized systematics by providing abundant, objective data for tree construction.

  Q19. What is 'horizontal gene transfer'?
    [*] The transfer of genetic material between organisms that is not from parent to offspring, often occurring between unrelated species
    [ ] The passage of genes from parent to offspring
    [ ] The movement of genes along a chromosome during recombination
    [ ] The expression of genes at different developmental stages
    Explanation: Horizontal (lateral) gene transfer is the movement of genetic material between organisms outside the normal parent-to-offspring transmission. It is common in bacteria (via conjugation, transformation, and transduction) and complicates phylogenetic analysis.

  Q20. What does 'clade' mean?
    [ ] A classification based on body size
    [ ] Any group of organisms that look similar
    [ ] A group of organisms that live in the same ecosystem
    [*] A monophyletic group consisting of an ancestor and all of its descendants
    Explanation: A clade is a monophyletic group that includes a single common ancestor and every descendant of that ancestor. Clades are the fundamental units of modern phylogenetic classification and are identified by shared derived characters.

  Q21. A scientist constructs a phylogenetic tree for five mammal species using DNA sequences and finds that whales are more closely related to hippopotamuses than to any other mammal. A colleague who used morphological data grouped whales with fish. Which approach is more reliable for determining evolutionary relationships, and why?
    [ ] Morphological data is always more reliable because physical features are directly observable
    [ ] Both approaches are equally unreliable
    [*] Molecular data is more reliable here because the whale-fish similarity is due to convergent evolution, while DNA reflects true ancestry
    [ ] Neither approach can determine evolutionary relationships
    Explanation: Whales and fish look similar because of convergent evolution (similar body shape for aquatic life), not common ancestry. Molecular data correctly reveals that whales are mammals closely related to hippos, demonstrating that morphology can be misleading when convergent evolution has occurred.

  Q22. A student is given a cladogram showing the relationships among vertebrates. She notices that birds are nested within the reptile clade, yet her textbook classifies birds and reptiles as separate classes. How does cladistic classification resolve this discrepancy?
    [*] Cladistics recognizes that traditional 'Reptilia' without birds is paraphyletic, and proper classification should include birds within the reptile clade to make it monophyletic
    [ ] Cladistics places reptiles within the bird clade instead
    [ ] The cladogram must be incorrect because birds cannot be reptiles
    [ ] Cladistics rejects the idea that birds are related to reptiles
    Explanation: Traditional 'Reptilia' excluding birds is paraphyletic because it includes some but not all descendants of the common ancestor. Cladistic classification requires monophyletic groups, so birds must be included within Reptilia (or reptiles reclassified) to form a valid clade.

  Q23. Researchers sequencing genomes of different bacterial species find that a gene for antibiotic resistance appears in distantly related species scattered across the bacterial tree of life. The gene sequences are nearly identical. What is the most likely explanation?
    [ ] All bacteria inherited the gene from a single common ancestor
    [ ] The researchers made a sequencing error
    [ ] The gene evolved independently in each species by convergent evolution
    [*] Horizontal gene transfer spread the resistance gene between unrelated bacterial species
    Explanation: Nearly identical gene sequences in distantly related bacterial species are best explained by horizontal gene transfer. Bacteria frequently exchange genes via conjugation, transformation, and transduction, spreading traits like antibiotic resistance across species boundaries.

  Q24. A taxonomist discovers that two insect species classified in the same genus based on wing shape actually have very different DNA sequences, while a third species with different wing shape has DNA nearly identical to one of them. What should the taxonomist conclude?
    [*] Wing shape similarity between the first two species likely results from convergent evolution (homoplasy), and the classification should be revised based on molecular evidence
    [ ] DNA evidence should be ignored in favor of morphology when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [ ] The third species must have recently evolved from the first without accounting for additional influences
    [ ] All three species should be placed in the same genus, particularly when wing considerations are weighed against competing pressures
    Explanation: This scenario illustrates homoplasy: similar wing shapes evolved independently in unrelated lineages. Molecular data provides a more reliable signal of common ancestry, and the classification should be revised to reflect true phylogenetic relationships.

  Q25. A research team constructs two different phylogenetic trees for the same group of organisms: one using maximum parsimony and one using maximum likelihood. The trees disagree on the placement of one species. What should the researchers do?
    [ ] Discard both trees and conclude that phylogenetics is unreliable
    [ ] Combine the two trees into one
    [ ] Always choose the parsimony tree because it is simpler
    [*] Use additional data (more genes, morphological characters, or both) and evaluate statistical support for each tree topology
    Explanation: When different methods produce conflicting trees, scientists collect additional independent data and assess statistical support (such as bootstrap values) for each topology. Discrepancies highlight areas of uncertainty that require more evidence to resolve.

  Q26. An evolutionary biologist compares ribosomal RNA sequences across all three domains of life (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya) and finds that Archaea share more sequence similarity with Eukarya than with Bacteria. What does this finding suggest about the tree of life?
    [*] Archaea and Eukarya share a more recent common ancestor with each other than either does with Bacteria
    [ ] Ribosomal RNA is not useful for determining relationships between domains
    [ ] All three domains evolved independently with no common ancestor
    [ ] Archaea evolved from Eukarya recently
    Explanation: Greater rRNA similarity between Archaea and Eukarya indicates a more recent common ancestor. Carl Woese's pioneering work comparing rRNA sequences established the three-domain system and showed that Archaea and Eukarya are sister groups.

  Q27. A student looks at a cladogram and mistakenly concludes that species positioned to the right of the tree are more evolved than species on the left. Why is this interpretation incorrect?
    [ ] Only species at the top of the tree are more evolved
    [*] Branches on a cladogram can be rotated at any node without changing the relationships; position does not indicate level of evolution
    [ ] Cladograms do not represent evolution at all
    [ ] Species on the left are actually more evolved
    Explanation: Branches on a cladogram can be freely rotated around any node like a mobile. Left-right position is arbitrary and does not indicate evolutionary advancement. All extant species at the tips have been evolving for the same amount of time since their common ancestor.

  Q28. Scientists discover a new deep-sea organism with unusual features. To determine its phylogenetic placement, they sequence its 18S rRNA gene and compare it to known sequences. The analysis places the organism within the fungal clade, despite its superficial resemblance to a protist. What principle of phylogenetics does this illustrate?
    [ ] 18S rRNA is unreliable for determining evolutionary relationships
    [ ] Fungi and protists are the same kingdom
    [*] Molecular data can reveal evolutionary relationships that are obscured by convergent morphological evolution
    [ ] Morphological similarity always indicates close evolutionary relationships
    Explanation: This illustrates that morphological similarity can be misleading due to convergent evolution. Molecular phylogenetics can correctly identify evolutionary relationships even when organisms have converged on similar physical forms in response to similar environments.

  Q29. A phylogenetic analysis of great apes using molecular data produces this tree: ((gorilla, (chimpanzee, human)), orangutan). A student concludes that chimpanzees are ancestral to humans. Why is this conclusion wrong?
    [ ] Molecular data cannot determine relationships among primates
    [ ] The tree actually shows that humans are ancestral to chimpanzees
    [ ] Gorillas are actually more closely related to humans than chimpanzees are
    [*] The tree shows that chimpanzees and humans share a common ancestor; neither is ancestral to the other, and both have been evolving independently since their lineages diverged
    Explanation: A phylogenetic tree shows sister-group relationships, not ancestor-descendant relationships. Chimpanzees and humans are sister taxa that diverged from a common ancestor. Both have evolved independently since that divergence, and neither is the ancestor of the other.

  Q30. A conservation biologist needs to prioritize species for protection. She uses phylogenetic diversity as a criterion, aiming to preserve the maximum evolutionary history. Of these four species, which should receive the highest priority: Species A (one of 50 closely related species in its clade), Species B (the sole surviving member of an ancient lineage), Species C (a common species with many close relatives), or Species D (a recently diverged subspecies)?
    [ ] Species D, because recently evolved species have the most genetic diversity
    [ ] Species A, because it has the most relatives
    [ ] Species C, because common species are more important
    [*] Species B, because it represents a unique evolutionary lineage and its loss would eliminate millions of years of evolutionary history
    Explanation: Phylogenetic diversity prioritizes species representing unique evolutionary lineages. Species B, as the sole survivor of an ancient lineage, represents more unique evolutionary history. Its extinction would remove an entire branch from the tree of life, whereas losing one of 50 similar species has less impact on total evolutionary diversity.

--- Lesson 7.5: Phylogenetics (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Photosynthesis
     A: The process by which organisms use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce glucose and oxygen

  2. Q: Define: Cellular Respiration
     A: The metabolic process that breaks down glucose to release energy stored in the form of ATP

  3. Q: Define: Homeostasis
     A: The process by which organisms maintain stable internal conditions despite external environmental changes

  4. Q: Define: Natural Selection
     A: The evolutionary mechanism by which organisms with advantageous traits are more likely to survive and reproduce

  5. Q: Define: Enzyme
     A: A protein that catalyzes biochemical reactions by lowering activation energy without being permanently altered

  6. Q: Define: Chromosome
     A: A structure containing DNA composed of genes that carries genetic information passed from parent to offspring

  7. Q: Define: Mitosis
     A: The process of cell division that produces two identical daughter cells for growth and asexual reproduction

  8. Q: Define: Meiosis
     A: The process of cell division that produces four genetically distinct haploid cells for sexual reproduction

  9. Q: The first law of thermodynamics in ecosystems means:
     A: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between forms

  10. Q: In a marine ecosystem, which organisms form the base of the food chain?
     A: Phytoplankton (photosynthetic organisms)
