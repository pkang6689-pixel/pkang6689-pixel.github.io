--- Lesson 3.1: Energy and Metabolism (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Photosynthesis
     A: The process by which organisms use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce glucose and oxygen

  2. Q: Define: Cellular Respiration
     A: The metabolic process that breaks down glucose to release energy stored in the form of ATP

  3. Q: Define: Homeostasis
     A: The process by which organisms maintain stable internal conditions despite external environmental changes

  4. Q: Define: Natural Selection
     A: The evolutionary mechanism by which organisms with advantageous traits are more likely to survive and reproduce

  5. Q: Define: Enzyme
     A: A protein that catalyzes biochemical reactions by lowering activation energy without being permanently altered

  6. Q: Define: Chromosome
     A: A structure containing DNA composed of genes that carries genetic information passed from parent to offspring

  7. Q: Define: Mitosis
     A: The process of cell division that produces two identical daughter cells for growth and asexual reproduction

  8. Q: Define: Meiosis
     A: The process of cell division that produces four genetically distinct haploid cells for sexual reproduction

  9. Q: The first law of thermodynamics in ecosystems means:
     A: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between forms

  10. Q: In a marine ecosystem, which organisms form the base of the food chain?
     A: Phytoplankton (photosynthetic organisms)

--- Lesson 3.4: Cellular Respiration (Summary) ---

  Understanding Cellular Respiration
Cellular respiration is the metabolic pathway that breaks down glucose to release energy stored as ATP (adenosine triphosphate). Complete aerobic respiration of one glucose molecule yields approximately 30-32 ATP through a series of coordinated stages. Respiration occurs in three main stages: glycolysis (cytoplasm), the Krebs cycle (mitochondrial matrix), and the electron transport chain (inner mitochondrial membrane).

  Key Concepts and Vocabulary
Glycolysis splits one 6-carbon glucose molecule into two 3-carbon pyruvate molecules, producing 2 ATP and 2 NADH. Pyruvate oxidation converts pyruvate to Acetyl-CoA (2-carbon unit). The Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle) oxidizes Acetyl-CoA to CO₂, producing NADH, FADH₂, and GTP. The electron transport chain couples electron transfer through NADH and FADH₂ to proton pumping across the inner mitochondrial membrane, creating a gradient that drives ATP synthase to produce the majority of ATP (about 28-32 per glucose).

  Real-World Applications and Examples
Real-world examples: athletes experience fatigue when muscles switch to anaerobic respiration (fermentation) during intense exercise, producing only 2 ATP per glucose; lactic acid fermentation produces lactate, contributing to muscle soreness; cancer cells often rely on glycolysis despite oxygen availability (Warburg effect); cellular respiration rates differ among organisms and vary with activity level; mitochondrial diseases involve defective electron transport, reducing ATP production.

  Deeper Understanding
Cellular respiration is staged because energy is released gradually—if all energy released at once, it would burn as heat. The multi-step sequence allows cells to capture energy efficiently in ATP chemical bonds. NADH and FADH₂ are electron carriers that transfer high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain. The proton gradient created by electron transport is the mechanism powering ATP synthesis.
