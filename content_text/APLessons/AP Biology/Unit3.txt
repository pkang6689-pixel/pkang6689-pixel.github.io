--- Lesson 3.1: Energy and Metabolism (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy and Metabolism
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary energy currency molecule used by cells?
ATP is the universal energy currency of cells. Energy released from ATP hydrolysis (ATP → ADP + Pi, ΔG = -30.5 kJ/mol) is used to drive endergonic reactions and cellular work.
What is the relationship between anabolic and catabolic reactions?
Catabolism breaks down complex molecules into simpler ones, releasing energy (exergonic). Anabolism builds complex molecules from simpler ones, requiring energy (endergonic). These processes are coupled through ATP.
What is free energy (G)?
Gibbs free energy (G) measures the energy available to do work. A negative ΔG indicates a spontaneous (exergonic) reaction. A positive ΔG indicates a non-spontaneous (endergonic) reaction that requires energy input.
What is metabolism?
Metabolism encompasses all chemical reactions in an organism. Catabolic pathways release energy by breaking down molecules; anabolic pathways consume energy to build complex molecules. Together they maintain life.
What is an 'exergonic reaction'?
Exergonic reactions release free energy (negative ΔG) and are thermodynamically spontaneous. However, 'spontaneous' does not mean 'fast'—many exergonic reactions still require activation energy to begin.
What is 'activation energy'?
Activation energy (Ea) is the energy barrier that must be overcome for a reaction to proceed. Enzymes lower Ea by stabilizing the transition state, dramatically increasing reaction rates without changing the overall ΔG.
What is 'anabolism'?
Anabolism is the constructive branch of metabolism that builds complex molecules (proteins, nucleic acids, polysaccharides) from smaller precursors. Anabolic reactions consume energy, often supplied by ATP.
What is 'phosphorylation'?
Phosphorylation adds a phosphate group (from ATP) to a protein or other molecule, changing its conformation and activity. Kinase enzymes catalyze phosphorylation, which regulates many cellular processes.
Real-World Applications
Example: A biologist calculates that a typical human cell uses approximately 10 million ATP molecules per second and recycles its entire pool of ATP roughly every 1-2 minutes. What does this rapid ATP turnover tell us about how cells manage energy?
Cells maintain only a small pool of ATP that is rapidly cycled between ATP and ADP. A human body recycles its weight in ATP each day. This continuous turnover keeps the cell far from equilibrium, enabling work.
Example: A biochemist studying metabolic regulation notices that when cells have abundant ATP, catabolic pathways slow down. When ATP levels drop and ADP/AMP rise, catabolism accelerates. What principle of energy management does this illustrate?
Cells regulate ATP production through feedback mechanisms. Key catabolic enzymes (like PFK in glycolysis) are allosterically inhibited by ATP (high energy signal) and activated by AMP/ADP (low energy signal), maintaining a balance between energy supply and demand.

--- Lesson 3.2: Enzyme Structure and Function (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Enzyme Structure and Function
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the effect of extreme pH on enzyme activity?
Extreme pH values alter the ionization states of amino acid R groups, disrupting ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds that maintain the enzyme's shape. Each enzyme has an optimal pH (e.g., pepsin at pH 2, trypsin at pH 8).
What is a cofactor?
Cofactors are non-protein molecules required for enzyme function. Inorganic cofactors include metal ions (Zn²⁺, Fe²⁺, Mg²⁺). Organic cofactors (coenzymes) include NAD⁺, FAD, and coenzyme A, often derived from vitamins.
What is an enzyme?
Enzymes are biological catalysts that dramatically accelerate reaction rates (often by 10⁶ to 10¹² times) by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. They are specific, reusable, and regulatable.
What is the 'induced fit model'?
The induced fit model (proposed by Koshland) is the modern view of enzyme-substrate interaction. Upon substrate binding, the enzyme's active site changes shape to embrace the substrate more closely, positioning catalytic residues optimally and straining substrate bonds.
What is 'Vmax'?
Vmax is the maximum rate of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction, reached when every enzyme molecule has its active site occupied by substrate. At Vmax, adding more substrate cannot increase the rate because no free active sites are available.
What is a 'coenzyme'?
Coenzymes are organic molecules that assist enzymes by accepting or donating chemical groups during reactions. Many are derived from vitamins (NAD⁺ from niacin, FAD from riboflavin, coenzyme A from pantothenic acid).
What is a 'transition state'?
The transition state is the fleeting, high-energy configuration that reactant molecules must pass through to become products. Enzymes bind the transition state more tightly than the substrate, lowering the energy barrier (activation energy).
Real-World Applications
Example: An industrial chemist wants to immobilize an enzyme on a solid support so it can be reused multiple times for manufacturing. After immobilization, the enzyme's Km increases but Vmax remains unchanged. What does this suggest?
An increased Km with unchanged Vmax suggests the enzyme is intact but substrate diffusion to the active site is partially hindered by the solid support. At saturating substrate, all active sites work at full capacity (same Vmax), but half-maximal rate requires more substrate (higher Km).
Example: A pharmaceutical company designs a drug that binds to an allosteric site on a bacterial enzyme essential for cell wall synthesis. The drug locks the enzyme in an inactive conformation. Why might this approach be advantageous compared to a competitive inhibitor that targets the active site?
Competitive inhibitors can be outcompeted by excess substrate. Allosteric inhibitors bind at a separate site, changing the enzyme's shape regardless of substrate concentration. Allosteric sites are often structurally unique, providing greater drug selectivity and reduced side effects.

--- Lesson 3.3: Regulation of Enzyme Activity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Regulation of Enzyme Activity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an allosteric site?
An allosteric site is a specific region on an enzyme, distinct from the active site, where regulatory molecules (activators or inhibitors) bind and induce conformational changes that affect enzyme activity.
What is feedback inhibition?
Feedback inhibition (also called end-product inhibition) is a self-regulatory mechanism where the final product of a pathway allosterically inhibits an earlier enzyme, maintaining homeostasis and conserving resources.
What is a zymogen?
A zymogen (or proenzyme) is an inactive enzyme precursor that must undergo a specific biochemical change, typically proteolytic cleavage, to become catalytically active. This prevents premature enzymatic activity.
What is a kinase?
A kinase is an enzyme that catalyzes the transfer of a phosphate group, usually from ATP, to a target molecule (substrate). Phosphorylation by kinases is one of the most common mechanisms of enzyme regulation in cells.
What is an allosteric activator?
An allosteric activator is a molecule that binds to an allosteric site on an enzyme and induces a conformational change that stabilizes the enzyme's active form, thereby increasing its catalytic efficiency.
What is a phosphatase?
A phosphatase is an enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis (removal) of phosphate groups from its substrates. It acts as the counterpart to kinases, reversing phosphorylation and thus switching enzyme activity off (or on).
Real-World Applications
Example: Phosphorylation of an enzyme by a kinase is an example of what type of regulation?
Phosphorylation is a covalent modification in which a kinase transfers a phosphate group to an enzyme, changing its shape and activity. This is a common reversible on/off switch for enzyme regulation.
Example: Compartmentalization of enzymes within organelles is an example of what type of regulation?
Compartmentalization is a form of spatial regulation where enzymes are sequestered in specific organelles (e.g., lysosomes, mitochondria), ensuring they only act on substrates when and where appropriate.
Example: During a cell signaling cascade, a growth factor receptor activates a kinase, which phosphorylates a downstream enzyme, switching it from an inactive to an active state. Later, a phosphatase removes the phosphate group, returning the enzyme to its inactive form. What advantage does this phosphorylation-dephosphorylation cycle provide?
The phosphorylation-dephosphorylation cycle provides rapid, reversible regulation of enzyme activity. Cells can quickly toggle enzymes between active and inactive states in response to extracellular signals without synthesizing or degrading protein.

--- Lesson 3.4: Cellular Respiration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cellular Respiration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the net gain of ATP molecules produced directly during glycolysis from one molecule of glucose?
Glycolysis produces 4 ATP total but consumes 2 ATP in the energy-investment phase, yielding a net gain of 2 ATP per glucose molecule through substrate-level phosphorylation.
What is the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain during aerobic respiration?
Molecular oxygen (O2) is the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain. It accepts electrons and combines with hydrogen ions to form water, which is why this process requires oxygen and is called aerobic respiration.
What is the role of NADH and FADH2 in cellular respiration?
NADH and FADH2 are electron carriers that shuttle high-energy electrons from glycolysis and the citric acid cycle to the electron transport chain, where the energy from these electrons drives ATP synthesis.
What is cellular respiration?
Cellular respiration is the set of catabolic metabolic reactions that break down organic molecules (primarily glucose) to produce ATP, releasing carbon dioxide and water as byproducts.
What is the meaning of 'substrate-level phosphorylation'?
Substrate-level phosphorylation is the formation of ATP by the direct enzymatic transfer of a phosphate group from a phosphorylated intermediate to ADP, occurring in both glycolysis and the citric acid cycle.
What is 'fermentation'?
Fermentation is an anaerobic metabolic pathway that converts pyruvate to either ethanol or lactate while regenerating NAD+ from NADH, allowing glycolysis to continue producing ATP when oxygen is unavailable.
What is the 'electron transport chain'?
The electron transport chain is a series of protein complexes (Complexes I-IV) and mobile electron carriers embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that pass electrons in a stepwise fashion, using the energy released to pump H+ ions and generate the proton gradient.
What is 'pyruvate oxidation'?
Pyruvate oxidation (also called the pyruvate dehydrogenase reaction) is the transition step where pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix, loses a CO2, and is converted to acetyl-CoA while reducing NAD+ to NADH.
What is 'ATP synthase'?
ATP synthase is a large enzyme complex embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that acts as a molecular turbine, using the flow of protons down their electrochemical gradient to catalyze the formation of ATP from ADP and Pi.
During a biology experiment, students measure oxygen consumption in germinating seeds versus non-germinating seeds at room temperature. They find germinating seeds consume significantly more oxygen. What is the best explanation?
Germinating seeds are actively growing and require large amounts of ATP. They have elevated rates of aerobic cellular respiration, which consumes oxygen as the final electron acceptor in the ETC, explaining the higher oxygen consumption rate.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which stage of cellular respiration produces the most ATP?
Oxidative phosphorylation, which includes the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis, generates approximately 26-28 ATP per glucose, far more than glycolysis (2 ATP) or the citric acid cycle (2 ATP).

--- Lesson 3.4: Cellular Respiration (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is the net gain of ATP molecules produced directly during glycolysis from one molecule of glucose?
    [ ] 1 ATP
    [ ] 36 ATP
    [ ] 4 ATP
    [*] 2 ATP
    Explanation: Glycolysis produces 4 ATP total but consumes 2 ATP in the energy-investment phase, yielding a net gain of 2 ATP per glucose molecule through substrate-level phosphorylation.

  Q2. In which cellular compartment does the citric acid (Krebs) cycle take place?
    [ ] Outer mitochondrial membrane
    [ ] Intermembrane space
    [ ] Cytoplasm
    [*] Mitochondrial matrix
    Explanation: The citric acid cycle occurs in the mitochondrial matrix, where acetyl-CoA is oxidized and CO2 is released. The matrix contains all the enzymes needed for this cycle.

  Q3. What is the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain during aerobic respiration?
    [*] Oxygen
    [ ] Carbon dioxide
    [ ] FAD
    [ ] NAD+
    Explanation: Molecular oxygen (O2) is the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain. It accepts electrons and combines with hydrogen ions to form water, which is why this process requires oxygen and is called aerobic respiration.

  Q4. Which molecule directly links glycolysis to the citric acid cycle?
    [ ] Oxaloacetate
    [ ] Citrate
    [ ] Glucose
    [*] Acetyl-CoA
    Explanation: Pyruvate from glycolysis is converted to acetyl-CoA by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, and acetyl-CoA then enters the citric acid cycle by combining with oxaloacetate to form citrate.

  Q5. Which stage of cellular respiration produces the most ATP?
    [*] Oxidative phosphorylation (electron transport chain and chemiosmosis)
    [ ] Citric acid cycle, based on conventional assumptions about acid
    [ ] Glycolysis when considering all relevant acid factors
    [ ] Pyruvate oxidation within the broader context of acid analysis
    Explanation: Oxidative phosphorylation, which includes the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis, generates approximately 26-28 ATP per glucose, far more than glycolysis (2 ATP) or the citric acid cycle (2 ATP).

  Q6. During fermentation in human muscle cells, pyruvate is converted to what product?
    [ ] Acetyl-CoA
    [ ] Carbon dioxide
    [ ] Ethanol
    [*] Lactate (lactic acid)
    Explanation: In anaerobic conditions, human muscle cells perform lactic acid fermentation, converting pyruvate to lactate while regenerating NAD+ so that glycolysis can continue to produce ATP.

  Q7. How many molecules of CO2 are released during one complete turn of the citric acid cycle?
    [*] 2
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 6
    Explanation: Each turn of the citric acid cycle releases 2 molecules of CO2, one during the conversion of isocitrate to alpha-ketoglutarate and another during the conversion of alpha-ketoglutarate to succinyl-CoA.

  Q8. What is the role of NADH and FADH2 in cellular respiration?
    [ ] They convert glucose to pyruvate
    [*] They carry high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain
    [ ] They directly phosphorylate ADP to form ATP
    [ ] They serve as the final electron acceptors
    Explanation: NADH and FADH2 are electron carriers that shuttle high-energy electrons from glycolysis and the citric acid cycle to the electron transport chain, where the energy from these electrons drives ATP synthesis.

  Q9. ATP synthase generates ATP by utilizing what driving force?
    [ ] A concentration gradient of electrons
    [*] A proton (H+) gradient across the inner mitochondrial membrane
    [ ] Direct phosphorylation from substrate molecules
    [ ] The breakdown of NADH in the matrix
    Explanation: ATP synthase uses the proton-motive force created by the electrochemical gradient of H+ ions across the inner mitochondrial membrane. As protons flow back through ATP synthase down their concentration gradient, the enzyme catalyzes the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP.

  Q10. Where does glycolysis occur in the cell?
    [ ] Nucleus
    [ ] Mitochondrial matrix
    [*] Cytoplasm (cytosol)
    [ ] Inner mitochondrial membrane
    Explanation: Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm (cytosol) and does not require a mitochondrion. This ancient pathway can function in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions.

  Q11. What is cellular respiration?
    [*] The set of metabolic reactions that convert biochemical energy from nutrients into ATP, releasing waste products
    [ ] The synthesis of glucose from carbon dioxide and water using sunlight
    [ ] The process of inhaling oxygen and exhaling carbon dioxide
    [ ] The passive diffusion of gases across the cell membrane
    Explanation: Cellular respiration is the set of catabolic metabolic reactions that break down organic molecules (primarily glucose) to produce ATP, releasing carbon dioxide and water as byproducts.

  Q12. What does 'oxidative phosphorylation' mean?
    [ ] The direct transfer of a phosphate group from a substrate to ADP
    [*] The production of ATP driven by the transfer of electrons through the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis
    [ ] The phosphorylation of glucose at the beginning of glycolysis
    [ ] The oxidation of FADH2 in the cytoplasm
    Explanation: Oxidative phosphorylation is the process in which ATP is formed as electrons are transferred from NADH and FADH2 through the electron transport chain to oxygen, creating a proton gradient that drives ATP synthase.

  Q13. What is the meaning of 'substrate-level phosphorylation'?
    [ ] The addition of a phosphate group to a protein by a kinase
    [*] The direct transfer of a phosphate group from a phosphorylated substrate to ADP to form ATP
    [ ] ATP production powered by the electron transport chain
    [ ] The phosphorylation of glucose by hexokinase
    Explanation: Substrate-level phosphorylation is the formation of ATP by the direct enzymatic transfer of a phosphate group from a phosphorylated intermediate to ADP, occurring in both glycolysis and the citric acid cycle.

  Q14. What does 'chemiosmosis' mean?
    [ ] The diffusion of oxygen into the mitochondrial matrix
    [*] The synthesis of ATP driven by the flow of ions (typically H+) down their electrochemical gradient through ATP synthase
    [ ] The movement of electrons through membrane-bound protein complexes
    [ ] The chemical reaction between NADH and oxygen
    Explanation: Chemiosmosis is the process by which the flow of hydrogen ions (protons) through ATP synthase, driven by the electrochemical gradient established by the electron transport chain, powers the synthesis of ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate.

  Q15. What is 'fermentation'?
    [ ] The aerobic breakdown of glucose in the mitochondria
    [*] An anaerobic pathway that regenerates NAD+ from NADH so glycolysis can continue in the absence of oxygen
    [ ] The synthesis of glucose from CO2
    [ ] The transport of electrons from NADH to oxygen
    Explanation: Fermentation is an anaerobic metabolic pathway that converts pyruvate to either ethanol or lactate while regenerating NAD+ from NADH, allowing glycolysis to continue producing ATP when oxygen is unavailable.

  Q16. What is the 'electron transport chain'?
    [ ] A series of enzymatic reactions in the cytoplasm that produce NADH
    [ ] A shuttle system that transports ATP out of the mitochondria
    [*] A series of membrane-bound protein complexes that transfer electrons from donors to acceptors, using the released energy to pump protons across a membrane
    [ ] The chain of reactions that converts glucose to pyruvate
    Explanation: The electron transport chain is a series of protein complexes (Complexes I-IV) and mobile electron carriers embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that pass electrons in a stepwise fashion, using the energy released to pump H+ ions and generate the proton gradient.

  Q17. What does 'anaerobic' mean in the context of metabolism?
    [ ] Requiring oxygen to proceed
    [*] Occurring without the use of oxygen
    [ ] Occurring exclusively in the mitochondria
    [ ] Producing carbon dioxide as a waste product
    Explanation: Anaerobic means 'without oxygen.' Anaerobic metabolic processes, such as glycolysis and fermentation, do not require molecular oxygen and can occur when oxygen is absent or limited.

  Q18. What is 'pyruvate oxidation'?
    [*] The conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, releasing CO2 and producing NADH, linking glycolysis to the citric acid cycle
    [ ] The conversion of glucose to two molecules of pyruvate in the cytoplasm
    [ ] The reduction of pyruvate to lactate during fermentation
    [ ] The direct phosphorylation of pyruvate to form ATP
    Explanation: Pyruvate oxidation (also called the pyruvate dehydrogenase reaction) is the transition step where pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix, loses a CO2, and is converted to acetyl-CoA while reducing NAD+ to NADH.

  Q19. What does 'proton-motive force' mean?
    [ ] The attraction between protons and electrons within an atom
    [ ] The movement of protons through aquaporins in the cell membrane
    [ ] The force generated by ATP hydrolysis that drives muscle contraction
    [*] The potential energy stored in the electrochemical gradient of protons across a membrane, used to drive ATP synthesis
    Explanation: The proton-motive force is the electrochemical gradient of hydrogen ions (protons) across the inner mitochondrial membrane. It consists of both a concentration gradient (pH difference) and an electrical gradient, and its energy drives ATP synthase.

  Q20. What is 'ATP synthase'?
    [ ] A protein that breaks down ATP to release energy
    [ ] A cytoplasmic enzyme that catalyzes substrate-level phosphorylation
    [*] An enzyme complex in the inner mitochondrial membrane that synthesizes ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate using the energy of the proton gradient
    [ ] A carrier protein that shuttles electrons through the ETC
    Explanation: ATP synthase is a large enzyme complex embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane that acts as a molecular turbine, using the flow of protons down their electrochemical gradient to catalyze the formation of ATP from ADP and Pi.

  Q21. A marathon runner's leg muscles begin to burn and fatigue during the last miles of a race. Blood tests reveal elevated lactate levels. Which metabolic shift explains the lactate accumulation?
    [ ] The muscles switched from the citric acid cycle to beta-oxidation of fatty acids
    [*] Oxygen delivery could not keep up with demand, so muscles shifted to lactic acid fermentation to regenerate NAD+
    [ ] The electron transport chain began using lactate as the final electron acceptor
    [ ] Glycolysis shut down and the Krebs cycle produced lactate directly
    Explanation: During intense exercise, oxygen supply to muscles cannot match demand. Muscles shift to lactic acid fermentation, converting pyruvate to lactate to regenerate NAD+ so glycolysis can continue producing ATP anaerobically.

  Q22. Cyanide is a potent poison that binds to Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) of the electron transport chain, blocking electron transfer to oxygen. Which of the following would be an immediate consequence of cyanide exposure in a cell?
    [ ] The citric acid cycle would increase its rate to compensate
    [ ] Glycolysis would immediately stop
    [ ] NADH would accumulate only in the cytoplasm
    [*] The proton gradient would dissipate and oxidative phosphorylation would cease, drastically reducing ATP production
    Explanation: Cyanide blocks electron flow at Complex IV, halting the ETC. Without electron flow, protons are no longer pumped and the gradient collapses. ATP synthase stops producing ATP, and NADH/FADH2 cannot be reoxidized, ultimately stalling the citric acid cycle as well.

  Q23. A researcher treats mitochondria with a chemical that makes the inner mitochondrial membrane freely permeable to protons. Which of the following would result?
    [ ] ATP production by ATP synthase would increase
    [*] The proton gradient would be disrupted, ATP synthase would stop, and energy would be released as heat
    [ ] The electron transport chain would stop functioning
    [ ] Oxygen consumption would decrease immediately
    Explanation: If the inner membrane becomes freely permeable to protons (an uncoupling effect), the proton gradient cannot be maintained. ATP synthase has no driving force, so ATP is not produced. The energy is instead released as heat, which is how biological uncouplers like thermogenin work in brown fat.

  Q24. Yeast cells are placed in a sealed container with glucose but no oxygen. After several hours, the culture produces ethanol and carbon dioxide. If the container is then opened to allow oxygen in, what change in metabolism would you predict?
    [ ] The yeast would stop using glucose entirely
    [ ] Ethanol production would increase due to the extra energy from oxygen
    [ ] The yeast would begin producing lactate instead of ethanol
    [*] The yeast would shift from alcoholic fermentation to aerobic respiration, greatly increasing ATP yield per glucose
    Explanation: This describes the Pasteur effect: when oxygen becomes available, yeast shift from fermentation (2 ATP per glucose) to aerobic respiration (approximately 30-32 ATP per glucose), dramatically increasing energy efficiency and reducing ethanol production.

  Q25. A patient is diagnosed with a mitochondrial disease that impairs Complex I of the electron transport chain. The patient's cells can still produce some ATP. Which pathway is most likely responsible for this residual ATP production?
    [ ] Beta-oxidation of fatty acids in peroxisomes
    [ ] Photosynthesis occurring in the patient's cells
    [*] Glycolysis and fermentation, which do not require functional mitochondria
    [ ] The citric acid cycle alone
    Explanation: With a defective Complex I, the electron transport chain is severely impaired and cannot efficiently produce ATP via oxidative phosphorylation. The cell can still generate ATP through glycolysis and fermentation in the cytoplasm, as these pathways are independent of mitochondrial function.

  Q26. During a biology experiment, students measure oxygen consumption in germinating seeds versus non-germinating seeds at room temperature. They find germinating seeds consume significantly more oxygen. What is the best explanation?
    [ ] Non-germinating seeds are performing more fermentation than germinating seeds
    [ ] Germinating seeds are performing photosynthesis, which consumes oxygen
    [ ] Germinating seeds release oxygen from stored water molecules
    [*] Germinating seeds have a much higher rate of aerobic cellular respiration to fuel growth, requiring more oxygen as the final electron acceptor
    Explanation: Germinating seeds are actively growing and require large amounts of ATP. They have elevated rates of aerobic cellular respiration, which consumes oxygen as the final electron acceptor in the ETC, explaining the higher oxygen consumption rate.

  Q27. A biochemist adds the drug oligomycin to isolated mitochondria. Oligomycin blocks the proton channel of ATP synthase. She observes that electron transport also slows significantly. Why does blocking ATP synthase affect the ETC?
    [ ] ATP is a required substrate for the electron transport chain
    [ ] ATP synthase directly donates electrons to Complex I
    [ ] Oligomycin also directly inhibits Complex III
    [*] Without proton flow through ATP synthase, the proton gradient becomes too steep, making it energetically difficult for the ETC to continue pumping protons
    Explanation: When ATP synthase is blocked, protons cannot flow back into the matrix. The proton gradient builds up to a point where the ETC complexes cannot pump additional protons against the increasingly steep gradient, slowing or halting electron transport.

  Q28. A cell biologist compares ATP production in a cell with normal mitochondria versus a cell lacking mitochondria entirely. Both cells are given glucose in aerobic conditions. Approximately how much less ATP would the cell without mitochondria produce per glucose molecule?
    [ ] About 10 fewer ATP
    [ ] There would be no difference
    [ ] About 2 fewer ATP
    [*] About 28-30 fewer ATP
    Explanation: Without mitochondria, the cell can only perform glycolysis and fermentation (yielding 2 ATP per glucose). Normal aerobic respiration yields approximately 30-32 ATP, so the cell without mitochondria produces about 28-30 fewer ATP per glucose molecule.

  Q29. A food scientist notices that bread dough rises faster in a warm kitchen than in a cold one. Using your knowledge of cellular respiration and fermentation, which explanation best accounts for this observation?
    [ ] Cold temperatures cause yeast to switch from fermentation to aerobic respiration
    [*] Higher temperatures increase the rate of yeast alcoholic fermentation, producing CO2 faster and causing the dough to rise more quickly
    [ ] Warmer temperatures cause the gluten proteins to expand, trapping more air
    [ ] Warm temperatures allow oxygen to dissolve more easily in the dough
    Explanation: Yeast undergo alcoholic fermentation in the anaerobic interior of dough, producing CO2 and ethanol. Higher temperatures increase the metabolic rate of the yeast enzymes (up to a point), resulting in faster CO2 production and faster rising.

  Q30. A researcher feeds cells glucose molecules in which all carbon atoms are labeled with radioactive carbon-14. After complete aerobic respiration, where will the radioactive carbon-14 atoms be found?
    [ ] In the ATP molecules produced
    [*] In the carbon dioxide (CO2) released during pyruvate oxidation and the citric acid cycle
    [ ] In the NADH and FADH2 electron carriers
    [ ] In the water (H2O) produced by the ETC
    Explanation: The carbon atoms from glucose are released as CO2 during pyruvate oxidation (1 CO2 per pyruvate) and the citric acid cycle (2 CO2 per acetyl-CoA). All six carbons from glucose ultimately end up in six CO2 molecules, which is where the radioactive carbon-14 would be detected.
