--- Lesson 1.1: Properties of Water (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Properties of Water
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the specific heat capacity of water?
Water has a specific heat capacity of 4.18 J/g·°C, which is unusually high compared to most substances and allows water to absorb or release large amounts of heat with minimal temperature change.
What is a hydrogen bond?
A hydrogen bond is a weak intermolecular force that forms when a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to an electronegative atom is attracted to another electronegative atom on a nearby molecule.
What is adhesion?
Adhesion is the attraction between different types of molecules. Water adheres to polar surfaces like glass or the cellulose walls of plant xylem, which helps drive capillary action.
What is meant by a 'hydrophobic interaction'?
Hydrophobic interactions occur when nonpolar molecules are excluded from the hydrogen bonding network of water and aggregate together, minimizing their contact with water.
What is 'surface tension'?
Surface tension is the elastic tendency of a fluid surface to minimize its area. In water, strong cohesive forces from hydrogen bonding make the surface behave like a stretched membrane.
What is 'capillary action'?
Capillary action is the movement of liquid through narrow tubes or porous materials against gravity, driven by adhesive forces between the liquid and tube walls combined with cohesive forces between liquid molecules.

--- Lesson 1.1: Properties of Water (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Water molecules are held together by which type of bond between their atoms?
    [*] Polar covalent bonds
    [ ] Metallic bonds
    [ ] Nonpolar covalent bonds
    [ ] Ionic bonds
    Explanation: Water molecules consist of oxygen and hydrogen atoms joined by polar covalent bonds, where electrons are shared unequally due to oxygen's higher electronegativity.

  Q2. What causes water to have a bent molecular geometry?
    [ ] The hydrogen atoms repel each other equally
    [*] The two lone pairs on oxygen repel the bonding pairs
    [ ] Water contains double bonds
    [ ] Hydrogen atoms are too large to be linear
    Explanation: Oxygen has two lone pairs of electrons that push the two O-H bonds closer together, giving water its characteristic bent shape with a bond angle of about 104.5 degrees.

  Q3. Which property of water allows insects like water striders to walk on its surface?
    [ ] Adhesion
    [ ] Capillary action
    [*] Surface tension
    [ ] High specific heat
    Explanation: Surface tension results from cohesion between water molecules at the surface, creating a film-like barrier strong enough to support lightweight objects like water striders.

  Q4. Why does ice float on liquid water?
    [*] Ice has more hydrogen bonds, forming an open lattice that is less dense than liquid water
    [ ] Ice molecules move faster than liquid water molecules
    [ ] Ice molecules are smaller than liquid water molecules
    [ ] Ice contains fewer hydrogen bonds than liquid water
    Explanation: When water freezes, hydrogen bonds lock the molecules into a crystalline lattice with more space between molecules, making ice about 9% less dense than liquid water.

  Q5. Water's high heat of vaporization is directly useful for which biological process?
    [ ] Osmosis across cell membranes
    [*] Evaporative cooling through sweating
    [ ] DNA replication
    [ ] Protein folding
    Explanation: Water's high heat of vaporization (2,260 J/g) means a large amount of heat energy is removed when water evaporates from the skin, making sweating an effective cooling mechanism.

  Q6. What is the specific heat capacity of water?
    [ ] 2.09 J/g·°C
    [*] 4.18 J/g·°C
    [ ] 1.00 J/g·°C
    [ ] 9.81 J/g·°C
    Explanation: Water has a specific heat capacity of 4.18 J/g·°C, which is unusually high compared to most substances and allows water to absorb or release large amounts of heat with minimal temperature change.

  Q7. Which property of water explains why it can travel upward through narrow plant xylem vessels?
    [ ] Low density of ice
    [ ] High specific heat
    [ ] Surface tension alone
    [*] Capillary action due to cohesion and adhesion
    Explanation: Capillary action occurs because adhesion between water and the polar walls of xylem vessels pulls water upward, while cohesion between water molecules drags neighboring molecules along.

  Q8. Water is considered a universal solvent primarily because it can dissolve which type of substances?
    [ ] Nonpolar molecules
    [*] Polar and ionic compounds
    [ ] Only gases
    [ ] Only organic molecules
    Explanation: Water's polarity allows it to surround and interact with polar molecules and ions through hydrogen bonding and electrostatic attraction, effectively dissolving them.

  Q9. A hydrogen bond in water forms between which atoms?
    [ ] Two hydrogen atoms on different molecules
    [ ] A hydrogen atom and a nitrogen atom
    [ ] Two oxygen atoms on different molecules
    [*] A hydrogen atom on one molecule and an oxygen atom on another molecule
    Explanation: In water, hydrogen bonds form between the partially positive hydrogen atom of one water molecule and the partially negative oxygen atom of a neighboring water molecule.

  Q10. Which of the following is NOT a property of water that supports life?
    [ ] Cohesion and surface tension
    [ ] Expansion upon freezing
    [*] Ability to dissolve nonpolar lipids easily
    [ ] High specific heat
    Explanation: Water does not easily dissolve nonpolar lipids because water is polar and nonpolar substances are hydrophobic. The other options are all genuine life-supporting properties of water.

  Q11. What is a hydrogen bond?
    [ ] The bond within a water molecule between hydrogen and oxygen
    [ ] A bond that only forms in ice, not in liquid water
    [ ] A strong covalent bond between two hydrogen atoms
    [*] A weak intermolecular attraction between a partially positive hydrogen and a nearby electronegative atom such as oxygen, nitrogen, or fluorine
    Explanation: A hydrogen bond is a weak intermolecular force that forms when a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to an electronegative atom is attracted to another electronegative atom on a nearby molecule.

  Q12. What does the term 'cohesion' mean in the context of water?
    [*] The mutual attraction between water molecules due to hydrogen bonding
    [ ] The tendency of water to evaporate at high temperatures
    [ ] The attraction of water molecules to other polar surfaces
    [ ] The ability of water to resist changes in temperature
    Explanation: Cohesion refers to the attraction between molecules of the same substance. In water, hydrogen bonds between neighboring water molecules create strong cohesive forces responsible for surface tension.

  Q13. What is adhesion?
    [*] The attraction between water molecules and molecules of a different substance, such as the walls of a glass tube or plant xylem
    [ ] The formation of hydrogen bonds within a single water molecule
    [ ] The process by which water evaporates
    [ ] The clumping of water molecules to form droplets
    Explanation: Adhesion is the attraction between different types of molecules. Water adheres to polar surfaces like glass or the cellulose walls of plant xylem, which helps drive capillary action.

  Q14. What does 'specific heat' mean?
    [ ] The total thermal energy in a body of water
    [*] The amount of heat energy required to raise 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance changes from liquid to gas
    [ ] The rate at which a substance releases heat to its surroundings
    Explanation: Specific heat is defined as the amount of heat energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius. Water's high specific heat (4.18 J/g·°C) helps moderate environmental temperatures.

  Q15. What is meant by a 'hydrophobic interaction'?
    [*] The tendency of nonpolar molecules to cluster together in an aqueous environment to minimize disruption of hydrogen bonds among water molecules
    [ ] The attraction between water and polar molecules
    [ ] A chemical reaction where water is a product
    [ ] The breaking of hydrogen bonds when temperature increases
    Explanation: Hydrophobic interactions occur when nonpolar molecules are excluded from the hydrogen bonding network of water and aggregate together, minimizing their contact with water.

  Q16. What is 'surface tension'?
    [*] The measure of how difficult it is to stretch or break the surface of a liquid, resulting from cohesion among surface molecules
    [ ] The force that pulls water molecules apart at the surface
    [ ] The tendency of water to form ice at the surface first
    [ ] The pressure exerted by water on the walls of a container
    Explanation: Surface tension is the elastic tendency of a fluid surface to minimize its area. In water, strong cohesive forces from hydrogen bonding make the surface behave like a stretched membrane.

  Q17. What does 'hydrophilic' mean?
    [*] Water-loving; describes molecules that have an affinity for water and dissolve readily in it
    [ ] A molecule that is neutral and does not interact with water
    [ ] A type of chemical bond found only in water
    [ ] Water-fearing; describes molecules that repel water
    Explanation: Hydrophilic literally means 'water-loving' and describes polar or charged molecules that interact favorably with water through hydrogen bonds or electrostatic attractions.

  Q18. What is 'capillary action'?
    [ ] The flow of water downhill due to gravity
    [ ] The process of water changing from liquid to vapor
    [ ] The movement of water from areas of high concentration to low concentration across a membrane
    [*] The ability of a liquid to flow in narrow spaces against gravity, driven by adhesion and cohesion forces
    Explanation: Capillary action is the movement of liquid through narrow tubes or porous materials against gravity, driven by adhesive forces between the liquid and tube walls combined with cohesive forces between liquid molecules.

  Q19. What does 'heat of vaporization' mean?
    [ ] The maximum temperature water can reach before boiling
    [ ] The heat required to raise the temperature of water by one degree
    [*] The quantity of heat a liquid must absorb per gram to be converted from liquid to gas
    [ ] The heat released when water freezes into ice
    Explanation: The heat of vaporization is the energy required to convert one gram of liquid to gas at the boiling point. Water's high heat of vaporization (2,260 J/g) means significant energy is needed to break hydrogen bonds during evaporation.

  Q20. What does the term 'polarity' mean when describing a water molecule?
    [ ] Water has equal distribution of electrical charge across the molecule
    [ ] Water carries a full positive charge at one end and a full negative charge at the other
    [*] Water has an uneven distribution of electrical charge, with a partial negative charge on oxygen and partial positive charges on hydrogen atoms
    [ ] Water molecules repel each other due to identical charges
    Explanation: Polarity in water means the molecule has an asymmetric charge distribution. Oxygen's greater electronegativity pulls shared electrons closer, creating partial negative and positive poles that enable hydrogen bonding.

  Q21. A researcher places a thin glass tube into a beaker of water and observes that the water level inside the tube rises above the water level in the beaker. Which combination of water properties best explains this observation?
    [ ] Hydrophobic interactions and high heat of vaporization
    [*] Adhesion of water to the glass and cohesion between water molecules
    [ ] High specific heat and low density of ice
    [ ] Evaporative cooling and surface tension
    Explanation: Capillary action results from adhesion (water sticking to the polar glass surface) pulling water upward and cohesion (water molecules pulling each other along via hydrogen bonds). Together these overcome gravity in narrow tubes.

  Q22. During a marathon on a hot day, a runner's body temperature stays relatively stable at 37°C despite intense heat production. Which property of water is most directly responsible for this temperature regulation?
    [ ] Adhesion to skin surfaces without accounting
    [ ] Low density of ice, at least according to standard theory
    [ ] Cohesion, especially during periods of significant change
    [*] High heat of vaporization enabling evaporative cooling from sweat
    Explanation: When sweat evaporates, each gram of water removes 2,260 joules of heat from the body. This high heat of vaporization makes sweating an extremely efficient cooling mechanism during exercise.

  Q23. A lake in Minnesota freezes on the surface during winter, but fish survive in the liquid water below. Which property of water is most critical to the survival of these aquatic organisms?
    [ ] Water's high heat of vaporization
    [*] Ice being less dense than liquid water, so it floats and insulates the water below
    [ ] Water's ability to dissolve oxygen
    [ ] Water's high surface tension preventing heat loss
    Explanation: Because ice is less dense than liquid water (0.917 g/cm³ vs. ~1.0 g/cm³), it floats and forms an insulating layer on the surface. This prevents the entire lake from freezing solid, allowing aquatic life to survive underneath.

  Q24. A coastal city experiences smaller temperature fluctuations between day and night compared to an inland desert city at the same latitude. Which property of water best explains this moderating effect of the nearby ocean?
    [ ] Water's polarity, since the prevailing theory about water would support this particular interpretation
    [ ] The density of saltwater relative to freshwater without accounting for additional influences
    [ ] The cohesion of water molecules in ocean waves when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [*] Water's high specific heat, allowing it to absorb and release large amounts of heat with minimal temperature change
    Explanation: Water's high specific heat (4.18 J/g·°C) means large bodies of water absorb heat during the day and release it at night with relatively small temperature changes, moderating the climate of nearby coastal areas.

  Q25. A student adds a drop of oil to a glass of water and notices the oil forms a distinct sphere floating on the surface. Which properties of water account for both the shape and position of the oil droplet?
    [*] Hydrophobic exclusion forces nonpolar oil molecules together, and oil is less dense than water
    [ ] Capillary action pulls the oil to the surface
    [ ] Hydrogen bonds between oil and water push the oil upward
    [ ] Adhesion causes oil to stick to itself and float
    Explanation: Oil is nonpolar and cannot form hydrogen bonds with water, so water molecules exclude it through hydrophobic interactions, forcing oil molecules to cluster together. Oil also floats because it is less dense than water.

  Q26. A biologist observes that a tall redwood tree can transport water over 100 meters from its roots to its uppermost leaves. Which mechanism, relying on water's properties, best explains this feat?
    [ ] Root pressure alone pushes water to the top
    [ ] Water's high specific heat generates thermal convection currents inside the tree
    [ ] Ice formation in the trunk creates pressure that drives water upward
    [*] Transpiration-driven pull combined with cohesion of water molecules in the xylem creates a continuous water column
    Explanation: As water evaporates from leaves (transpiration), cohesion between water molecules in the xylem transmits a pulling force down the continuous water column. Adhesion to xylem walls also helps resist gravity.

  Q27. A pharmaceutical company needs to design a drug that can dissolve in the bloodstream to reach its target organ. Based on water's properties as a solvent, which molecular characteristic should the drug have?
    [*] It should contain polar or charged functional groups that can form hydrogen bonds with water
    [ ] It should be as nonpolar as possible to avoid interacting with blood cells
    [ ] It should have a molecular weight exceeding 100,000 daltons
    [ ] It should be entirely composed of hydrocarbon chains
    Explanation: Blood plasma is an aqueous solution, so drugs must be hydrophilic (water-soluble) to dissolve in it. Polar or charged functional groups form hydrogen bonds and electrostatic interactions with water molecules.

  Q28. An ecologist studying a pond ecosystem finds that water temperature at the bottom of the pond in winter is approximately 4°C while the surface is frozen at 0°C. What explains this temperature gradient?
    [ ] Geothermal energy from the Earth heats the bottom water to exactly 4°C
    [ ] Decomposing organisms at the bottom generate metabolic heat that warms the water
    [ ] Dissolved salts at the bottom lower the freezing point to 4°C
    [*] Water is densest at 4°C, so the coldest liquid water sinks to the bottom before ice forms on top
    Explanation: Water reaches its maximum density at approximately 4°C. As surface water cools below 4°C it becomes less dense and stays on top, eventually freezing. The denser 4°C water remains at the bottom, protecting aquatic organisms.

  Q29. A student places a paper towel vertically with its bottom edge dipping into colored water and observes the water climbing upward through the towel. Which of the following best explains this observation?
    [ ] Evaporative cooling pulls water upward through the towel fibers
    [ ] Hydrophobic interactions between the dye and the paper push water through the towel
    [*] Adhesion of water to the cellulose fibers combined with cohesion between water molecules draws the water upward via capillary action
    [ ] Osmotic pressure forces the water through the towel
    Explanation: The paper towel contains tiny cellulose fibers with polar surfaces. Adhesion between water and these fibers pulls water into the spaces, while cohesion ensures that neighboring water molecules follow, enabling capillary action.

  Q30. A climate scientist notes that large bodies of water near cities help reduce the severity of both heat waves and cold snaps. If global warming reduces the total ocean surface area through land reclamation, what effect would this most likely have on nearby urban climates?
    [ ] Urban temperatures would become more stable because less evaporation means less cooling variability
    [ ] The city would become permanently warmer because water only absorbs heat, never releases it
    [*] Urban temperatures would become more extreme, with hotter summers and colder winters, due to loss of water's temperature-moderating effect
    [ ] There would be no effect because air temperature is determined solely by solar radiation
    Explanation: Water's high specific heat moderates temperatures by absorbing excess heat in warm periods and releasing stored heat in cool periods. Reducing nearby water surface area would remove this buffering effect, leading to greater temperature extremes.

--- Lesson 1.2: Carbohydrates: Structure and Function (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Carbohydrates: Structure and Function
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the general molecular formula for a monosaccharide?
Monosaccharides follow the general formula (CH₂O)n, where n is typically 3-7. For example, glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) fits this formula with n = 6.
What is a monosaccharide?
A monosaccharide is the simplest carbohydrate unit, a single sugar molecule with the general formula (CH₂O)n. Common examples include glucose, fructose, and galactose.
What is a disaccharide?
A disaccharide is formed when two monosaccharides are linked by a glycosidic bond through dehydration synthesis. Examples include sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose (glucose + galactose), and maltose (glucose + glucose).
What is a polysaccharide?
A polysaccharide is a complex carbohydrate polymer composed of long chains of monosaccharide units joined by glycosidic bonds. Examples include starch, glycogen, cellulose, and chitin.
What is starch?
Starch is the main energy storage polysaccharide in plants, composed of alpha-glucose. It exists in two forms: amylose (linear, helical) and amylopectin (branched), both stored in plastids.
What is glycogen?
Glycogen is the main energy storage polysaccharide in animals, consisting of extensively branched chains of alpha-glucose. Its high degree of branching allows rapid mobilization of glucose when energy is needed.
Cows can obtain energy from grass (cellulose), but humans cannot. A veterinary scientist is studying how ruminant digestion makes this possible. What is the key biological mechanism?
Cows harbor symbiotic bacteria and protists in their rumen that produce cellulase, the enzyme that hydrolyzes beta-1,4-glycosidic bonds in cellulose. Humans lack these microorganisms and the cellulase enzyme, so cellulose passes through as indigestible fiber.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Cellulose's beta-1,4-glycosidic bonds cause alternating glucose monomers to flip, producing straight chains. These chains form extensive hydrogen-bonded microfibrils that resist the enzymes (like human amylase) that easily digest starch's alpha linkages.
Resistant starch resists digestion in the small intestine, and soluble fiber forms a gel that slows the rate of enzymatic starch breakdown and glucose absorption. This produces a gradual, sustained release of glucose rather than a rapid spike.
Real-World Applications
Example: A food scientist is developing a low-glycemic snack bar for diabetic patients. She chooses to include resistant starch and soluble fiber rather than simple sugars. How do these complex carbohydrates help manage blood sugar?
Resistant starch resists digestion in the small intestine, and soluble fiber forms a gel that slows the rate of enzymatic starch breakdown and glucose absorption. This produces a gradual, sustained release of glucose rather than a rapid spike.

    Explanation: Monosaccharides follow the general formula (CH₂O)n, where n is typically 3-7. For example, glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) fits this formula with n = 6.

  Q2. Which type of bond links monosaccharides together to form disaccharides and polysaccharides?
    [ ] Phosphodiester bond
    [ ] Peptide bond
    [ ] Hydrogen bond
    [*] Glycosidic bond
    Explanation: Glycosidic bonds form via dehydration synthesis (condensation) between the hydroxyl groups of two monosaccharides, releasing a molecule of water.

  Q3. Which polysaccharide serves as the primary structural component of plant cell walls?
    [ ] Starch
    [ ] Chitin
    [ ] Glycogen
    [*] Cellulose
    Explanation: Cellulose is composed of beta-glucose monomers linked by beta-1,4-glycosidic bonds, forming straight chains that bundle into strong microfibrils in plant cell walls.

  Q4. What are the two monosaccharides that combine to form sucrose?
    [*] Glucose and fructose
    [ ] Glucose and galactose
    [ ] Fructose and galactose
    [ ] Two glucose molecules
    Explanation: Sucrose (table sugar) is a disaccharide formed by a dehydration synthesis reaction between glucose and fructose, joined by an alpha-1,2-glycosidic bond.

  Q5. Where is glycogen primarily stored in the human body?
    [*] Liver and skeletal muscles
    [ ] Adipose tissue only
    [ ] Brain and spinal cord
    [ ] Blood plasma
    Explanation: Glycogen is the main storage polysaccharide in animals and is concentrated in the liver (for blood glucose regulation) and skeletal muscles (for quick energy during activity).

  Q6. How does the structure of starch differ from that of cellulose?
    [ ] Both are identical except starch is longer
    [ ] Starch is branched; cellulose is a protein-based polymer
    [ ] Starch uses phosphodiester bonds; cellulose uses glycosidic bonds
    [*] Starch contains alpha-glucose monomers; cellulose contains beta-glucose monomers
    Explanation: Starch is made of alpha-glucose units (alpha-1,4 and alpha-1,6 bonds in amylopectin), forming helical or branched structures. Cellulose uses beta-glucose with beta-1,4 bonds, producing straight, rigid chains.

  Q7. Which reaction breaks a disaccharide into its component monosaccharides?
    [*] Hydrolysis
    [ ] Oxidation
    [ ] Phosphorylation
    [ ] Dehydration synthesis
    Explanation: Hydrolysis uses a water molecule to break the glycosidic bond between two monosaccharides, restoring the hydroxyl groups on each monomer.

  Q8. Which monosaccharide is the primary energy source for cellular respiration in most organisms?
    [ ] Ribose
    [*] Glucose
    [ ] Galactose
    [ ] Fructose
    Explanation: Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is the primary fuel for cellular respiration, where it is oxidized through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation to produce ATP.

  Q9. What type of polysaccharide makes up the exoskeletons of arthropods?
    [ ] Starch
    [*] Chitin
    [ ] Cellulose
    [ ] Glycogen
    Explanation: Chitin is a structural polysaccharide made of N-acetylglucosamine monomers linked by beta-1,4-glycosidic bonds. It provides strength and flexibility to arthropod exoskeletons and fungal cell walls.

  Q10. Which of the following correctly lists carbohydrates from simplest to most complex?
    [ ] Monosaccharide → Polysaccharide → Disaccharide
    [ ] Disaccharide → Monosaccharide → Polysaccharide
    [ ] Polysaccharide → Disaccharide → Monosaccharide
    [*] Monosaccharide → Disaccharide → Polysaccharide
    Explanation: Monosaccharides are single sugar units, disaccharides are two monosaccharides joined together, and polysaccharides are long chains of many monosaccharides. Complexity increases with chain length.

  Q11. What is a monosaccharide?
    [ ] A polymer of amino acids used for energy storage
    [*] A simple sugar that is the basic monomer unit of carbohydrates, such as glucose, fructose, or galactose
    [ ] A two-sugar molecule formed by dehydration synthesis
    [ ] A lipid molecule with a sugar head group
    Explanation: A monosaccharide is the simplest carbohydrate unit, a single sugar molecule with the general formula (CH₂O)n. Common examples include glucose, fructose, and galactose.

  Q12. What does the term 'glycosidic bond' mean?
    [ ] A hydrogen bond that holds polysaccharide chains together
    [ ] An ionic bond between glucose and water
    [ ] A bond between an amino acid and a sugar molecule
    [*] A covalent bond between two monosaccharides formed through dehydration synthesis
    Explanation: A glycosidic bond is a covalent linkage formed when the hydroxyl group of one monosaccharide reacts with the hydroxyl group of another, releasing water. It can be alpha or beta depending on the orientation.

  Q13. What is a disaccharide?
    [ ] A long chain of repeating sugar units
    [*] A carbohydrate composed of two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond
    [ ] A single sugar molecule with six carbon atoms
    [ ] A sugar alcohol used in food processing
    Explanation: A disaccharide is formed when two monosaccharides are linked by a glycosidic bond through dehydration synthesis. Examples include sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose (glucose + galactose), and maltose (glucose + glucose).

  Q14. What does 'dehydration synthesis' mean in the context of carbohydrate chemistry?
    [*] A condensation reaction that removes a water molecule while forming a covalent bond between two monomers
    [ ] A reaction that removes water to join molecules together
    [ ] The evaporation of water from a sugar solution
    [ ] A reaction that breaks polymers apart by adding water
    Explanation: Dehydration synthesis (also called condensation) is a reaction in which two molecules are joined by removing a water molecule, forming a covalent bond. This is how monosaccharides link to build disaccharides and polysaccharides.

  Q15. What is a polysaccharide?
    [ ] A molecule consisting of exactly two sugar units
    [ ] A sugar molecule found only in nucleic acids
    [*] A macromolecule composed of many monosaccharide monomers linked by glycosidic bonds
    [ ] A small organic molecule that acts as an enzyme cofactor
    Explanation: A polysaccharide is a complex carbohydrate polymer composed of long chains of monosaccharide units joined by glycosidic bonds. Examples include starch, glycogen, cellulose, and chitin.

  Q16. What does 'hydrolysis' mean?
    [ ] The addition of hydrogen atoms to a carbon chain
    [*] A reaction that uses water to break covalent bonds between monomers in a polymer
    [ ] A reaction that builds polymers by removing water
    [ ] The process of dissolving a solute in water
    Explanation: Hydrolysis literally means 'water splitting.' In this reaction, water is added across a covalent bond, breaking a polymer into its component monomers. It is the reverse of dehydration synthesis.

  Q17. What is starch?
    [ ] A five-carbon sugar used in DNA synthesis according to widely held views on this topic
    [*] A storage polysaccharide in plants made of alpha-glucose monomers, existing as amylose (unbranched) and amylopectin (branched)
    [ ] A structural carbohydrate in animal cells, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] A disaccharide found in milk based on the conventional understanding of how starch processes interact in practice
    Explanation: Starch is the main energy storage polysaccharide in plants, composed of alpha-glucose. It exists in two forms: amylose (linear, helical) and amylopectin (branched), both stored in plastids.

  Q18. What does the term 'isomer' mean in carbohydrate chemistry?
    [ ] Different elements that occupy the same position in the periodic table
    [ ] Identical molecules with different atomic masses
    [*] Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements
    [ ] Molecules that have different molecular formulas but the same structure
    Explanation: Isomers are molecules that share the same molecular formula but differ in the arrangement of their atoms. For example, glucose and fructose are both C₆H₁₂O₆ but have different structures and properties.

  Q19. What is glycogen?
    [ ] A structural polysaccharide in plant cell walls
    [ ] A monosaccharide that serves as the building block of chitin
    [ ] A disaccharide found in table sugar
    [*] A storage polysaccharide in animals composed of highly branched alpha-glucose chains, stored primarily in liver and muscle cells
    Explanation: Glycogen is the main energy storage polysaccharide in animals, consisting of extensively branched chains of alpha-glucose. Its high degree of branching allows rapid mobilization of glucose when energy is needed.

  Q20. What does the term 'reducing sugar' mean?
    [ ] A sugar that has been chemically reduced in size through digestion
    [ ] A low-calorie sugar substitute
    [ ] A sugar that only exists in reduced oxygen environments
    [*] A sugar that can act as a reducing agent because it has a free anomeric carbon capable of donating electrons
    Explanation: A reducing sugar has a free aldehyde or ketone group (free anomeric carbon) that can reduce other molecules by donating electrons. Glucose, fructose, and maltose are reducing sugars; sucrose is not.

--- Lesson 1.3: Lipids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Lipids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the key structural difference between a saturated and an unsaturated fatty acid?
Saturated fatty acids have no double bonds between carbon atoms and are fully 'saturated' with hydrogen atoms. Unsaturated fatty acids have one or more C=C double bonds that create kinks in the chain.
What is a triglyceride?
A triglyceride is the most common form of fat in the body, consisting of a glycerol backbone esterified to three fatty acid chains. It stores approximately 9 kcal of energy per gram.
What is a phospholipid?
A phospholipid is an amphipathic lipid with a hydrophilic phosphate head group and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails. These molecules self-assemble into bilayers, forming the core structure of all biological membranes.
What is a steroid?
Steroids are lipids with a distinctive structure of four fused carbon rings. Cholesterol is the most common steroid in animal cells and serves as a precursor for steroid hormones and vitamin D.
What is meant by 'trans fat'?
Trans fats have hydrogen atoms on opposite sides (trans configuration) of the carbon-carbon double bond, unlike natural cis fats. This arrangement makes the chains straighter, increasing packing and raising health risks like cardiovascular disease.
What is a wax?
Waxes are highly hydrophobic lipids consisting of a long-chain fatty acid linked to a long-chain alcohol by an ester bond. They form protective, waterproof coatings on leaves, feathers, and fruit surfaces.

--- Lesson 1.4: Proteins (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Proteins
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a peptide bond?
A peptide bond is a covalent C-N bond formed between the carboxyl group (-COOH) of one amino acid and the amino group (-NH₂) of the next during a dehydration synthesis reaction that releases water.
What is an enzyme?
Enzymes are biological catalysts, mostly proteins, that accelerate chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy. They are highly specific, reusable, and essential for virtually all metabolic processes.
What is a polypeptide?
A polypeptide is a linear chain of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. A functional protein may consist of one or more polypeptides that fold into specific three-dimensional shapes.
What is a 'chaperone protein'?
Chaperone proteins (chaperonins) assist newly synthesized polypeptides in folding into their correct 3D conformations. They provide a protected environment that prevents inappropriate interactions and aggregation.
What is a 'disulfide bridge'?
A disulfide bridge (disulfide bond) is a strong covalent S-S bond formed by oxidation of the sulfhydryl groups of two cysteine amino acids. These bonds provide extra stability to proteins, especially those secreted outside the cell.
A cook fries an egg and notices the clear egg white turns opaque and solid. What is happening to the proteins at the molecular level, and why is this change irreversible under normal conditions?
Heat breaks the noncovalent bonds (hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds) that maintain albumin's globular shape. The unfolded proteins expose hydrophobic R groups that aggregate into an insoluble mass, making the process practically irreversible.
A geneticist studies collagen deficiency in a patient with Ehlers-Danlos syndrome. Collagen is a structural protein with a triple-helix quaternary structure. If a mutation replaces glycine (the smallest amino acid) with a bulkier amino acid in one of the three chains, what is the likely effect?
Glycine's small size is essential at every third position in collagen because it fits in the crowded interior of the triple helix. A bulkier amino acid disrupts this tight arrangement, destabilizing the triple helix and weakening connective tissues.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Thermophilic enzymes maintain their structure at high temperatures through increased intramolecular interactions: tighter hydrophobic core packing, more ionic interactions (salt bridges), additional disulfide bonds, and shorter surface loops that resist unfolding.
Real-World Applications
Example: Alpha helices and beta pleated sheets are examples of which level of protein structure?
Secondary structure consists of local folding patterns stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms. Alpha helices are coiled and beta pleated sheets are zigzag folded arrangements.

In normal cells, p53 activates DNA repair genes and triggers apoptosis if damage is irreparable. How does a mutation that disrupts p53's tertiary structure contribute to cancer development?
p53 is a transcription factor whose function depends on its 3D structure to bind DNA. Mutations that disrupt its shape prevent it from activating DNA repair or apoptosis pathways, allowing damaged cells to proliferate and accumulate mutations leading to cancer.

--- Lesson 1.5: Nucleic Acids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nucleic Acids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If a DNA strand reads 5'-ATCGGA-3', what is the sequence of the complementary strand?
The complementary strand runs antiparallel and follows base pairing rules (A-T, C-G). For 5'-ATCGGA-3', the complementary strand is 3'-TAGCCT-5'.
What is a nucleotide?
A nucleotide is the basic building block (monomer) of nucleic acids. It has three parts: a nitrogenous base, a five-carbon sugar (ribose or deoxyribose), and one or more phosphate groups.
What is a phosphodiester bond?
A phosphodiester bond is the covalent linkage in the nucleic acid backbone, connecting the 3'-OH of one nucleotide's sugar to the 5'-phosphate of the adjacent nucleotide through a phosphate group.
What is a purine?
Purines are nitrogenous bases with a two-ring (double-ring) structure. Adenine (A) and guanine (G) are purines. They are larger than pyrimidines and always pair with a pyrimidine to maintain the uniform width of the double helix.
What is a pyrimidine?
Pyrimidines are nitrogenous bases with a single-ring structure. Cytosine (C), thymine (T, in DNA), and uracil (U, in RNA) are pyrimidines. They pair with the larger purines to keep the DNA helix width constant.
What is ATP (adenosine triphosphate)?
ATP is a modified nucleotide with adenine, ribose sugar, and three phosphate groups. Hydrolysis of the terminal phosphate bond releases energy (approximately -30.5 kJ/mol) used to drive endergonic cellular processes.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does the term 'antiparallel' mean in the context of DNA structure?
Antiparallel means the two strands of the DNA double helix run in opposite 5'-to-3' directions. This arrangement is critical for complementary base pairing and has important implications for DNA replication.
Example: A forensic scientist analyzes DNA from a crime scene and finds that the sample contains 22% adenine. What percentage of cytosine does the sample contain?
By Chargaff's rules, A = T = 22%, so A + T = 44%. Since the four bases total 100%, G + C = 56%. Since G = C, cytosine = 28%.

--- Lesson 1.6: Cell Structure (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cell Structure
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary function of ribosomes?
Ribosomes are the molecular machines responsible for translating mRNA into polypeptide chains. They consist of a large and small subunit made of rRNA and proteins, with A, P, and E sites for tRNA binding.
What is the main difference between rough and smooth endoplasmic reticulum?
Rough ER is studded with ribosomes that synthesize membrane-bound and secretory proteins. Smooth ER lacks ribosomes and functions in lipid synthesis, carbohydrate metabolism, calcium storage, and detoxification.
What is the function of the cytoskeleton?
The cytoskeleton consists of microfilaments (actin), intermediate filaments, and microtubules (tubulin). It maintains cell shape, anchors organelles, enables cell motility, and serves as tracks for intracellular transport.
What is the cell membrane (plasma membrane)?
The cell membrane is a fluid phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins (the fluid mosaic model). It is selectively permeable, controlling what enters and exits the cell through passive and active transport mechanisms.
What is the endoplasmic reticulum?
The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is an extensive network of membrane-enclosed tubules and flattened sacs connected to the nuclear envelope. Rough ER synthesizes proteins; smooth ER synthesizes lipids and detoxifies drugs.
What is the endomembrane system?
The endomembrane system includes the nuclear envelope, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, vacuoles, and plasma membrane. These components are connected through direct physical contact or vesicle trafficking.
What is the central vacuole?
The central vacuole is a large, tonoplast-enclosed compartment occupying up to 90% of a mature plant cell's volume. It stores water, nutrients, and waste, and its turgor pressure provides structural support to the cell.
What is a chloroplast?
Chloroplasts are double-membrane organelles in plant cells and algae. They contain thylakoid membranes (site of light reactions) and stroma (site of the Calvin cycle) and have their own DNA, consistent with endosymbiotic origin.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which organelle modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport?
The Golgi apparatus (Golgi complex) receives proteins from the endoplasmic reticulum, further modifies them (glycosylation, phosphorylation), sorts them, and packages them into vesicles for delivery to their destinations.
Example: A cancer researcher observes that rapidly dividing tumor cells are significantly smaller than normal cells of the same tissue type. Using the concept of surface area-to-volume ratio, why might smaller cell size be advantageous for rapidly dividing cells?
Smaller cells have a greater surface area relative to their volume, allowing more efficient exchange of nutrients and waste across the membrane. This supports the high metabolic rate needed for rapid cell division in tumors.

  Q19. What is a chloroplast?
