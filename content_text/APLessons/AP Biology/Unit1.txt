--- Lesson 1.1: Properties of Water (Summary) ---

  Understanding Properties of Water
Water's unique properties result from hydrogen bonding: the attraction between hydrogen atoms (slightly positive) of one water molecule and oxygen atoms (slightly negative) of another. Water is a polar molecule—its electrons are distributed unevenly, creating partial positive and negative charges. This polarity underlies water's remarkable properties and makes it essential for life.

  Key Concepts and Vocabulary
Key properties: cohesion (water molecules stick together through hydrogen bonds, allowing water to travel against gravity in plant xylem); adhesion (water adheres to other polar materials); high heat capacity (water resists temperature change, moderating climate and preventing rapid body temperature shifts); density anomaly (ice is less dense than liquid water, so ice floats—unusual among substances); universal solvent (water dissolves many substances because of its polarity). Each property depends on hydrogen bonding.

  Real-World Applications and Examples
Real-world applications: cohesion enables capillary action that transports water from plant roots to leaves against gravity; water's heat capacity prevents rapid temperature fluctuations in oceans and lakes, moderating global climate; ice floating means aquatic life can survive under ice in winter (fish and organisms exist under the frozen surface); water as solvent enables nutrient transport in plants and animals and allows biochemical reactions to occur in aqueous environments.

  Deeper Understanding
Understanding water requires appreciating how each property emerges from hydrogen bonding. Asking 'what would happen without this property?' reveals why each is essential—without cohesion, plants couldn't transport water; without high heat capacity, life couldn't survive temperature extremes; without the density anomaly, aquatic ecosystems would freeze solid. Water properties enable life's fundamental processes.
