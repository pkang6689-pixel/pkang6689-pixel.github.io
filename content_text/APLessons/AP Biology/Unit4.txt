--- Lesson 4.1: Cell Signaling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cell Signaling
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the function of a second messenger in a signaling pathway?
Second messengers such as cyclic AMP (cAMP) and calcium ions (Ca2+) are small intracellular molecules that relay and amplify the signal received at the cell surface, activating downstream effector proteins.
What is a ligand?
A ligand is any molecule (such as a hormone, neurotransmitter, or growth factor) that binds specifically to a receptor protein, initiating a signaling event. The binding is based on complementary shapes.
What is a 'second messenger'?
A second messenger is a small intracellular molecule (such as cAMP, Ca2+, or IP3) generated in response to an extracellular signal (the 'first messenger'). Second messengers amplify and propagate the signal inside the cell.
What is a 'phosphorylation cascade'?
A phosphorylation cascade (or kinase cascade) is a signal transduction mechanism where a series of protein kinases sequentially phosphorylate and activate each other, amplifying the initial signal at each step.
What is a 'G protein'?
A G protein is a molecular switch that alternates between an active GTP-bound state and an inactive GDP-bound state. When a GPCR is activated by a ligand, the associated G protein exchanges GDP for GTP and relays the signal to downstream effectors.
What is 'apoptosis'?
Apoptosis is a tightly regulated form of programmed cell death activated by specific signaling pathways. It plays essential roles in development, tissue homeostasis, and immune function by eliminating damaged or unnecessary cells.
What is a 'receptor tyrosine kinase' (RTK)?
Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) are enzyme-linked transmembrane receptors that dimerize upon ligand binding and autophosphorylate tyrosine residues on each other. The phosphorylated residues serve as docking sites for relay proteins that activate pathways such as Ras-MAPK.
A researcher discovers that a cancer cell has a mutation in the Ras protein that prevents it from hydrolyzing GTP to GDP. What is the most likely effect on cell signaling?
Ras normally acts as a molecular switch, turning off when it hydrolyzes GTP to GDP. A mutation preventing GTP hydrolysis locks Ras in the active state, continuously activating the MAPK pathway and promoting uncontrolled cell division, a hallmark of many cancers.
Researchers find that certain cancer cells overexpress growth factor receptors (RTKs) on their surface. These cells proliferate even in low concentrations of growth factors. What is the most likely explanation?
Overexpression of RTKs increases the cell's sensitivity to growth factors. Even low concentrations of ligand can activate enough receptors to trigger the downstream MAPK signaling cascade that promotes proliferation, contributing to uncontrolled growth.
Real-World Applications
Example: What are the three stages of cell signaling in order?
Cell signaling occurs in three sequential stages: reception (ligand binds to receptor), transduction (signal is relayed and often amplified through a cascade), and response (the cell carries out a specific action).

--- Lesson 4.1: Cell Signaling (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which type of signaling involves cells communicating over long distances through hormones carried in the bloodstream?
    [ ] Autocrine signaling
    [*] Endocrine signaling
    [ ] Juxtacrine signaling
    [ ] Paracrine signaling
    Explanation: Endocrine signaling involves the release of hormones into the bloodstream, allowing communication between cells that may be far apart in the body. This contrasts with paracrine signaling, which acts locally.

  Q2. What are the three stages of cell signaling in order?
    [ ] Transduction, reception, response
    [ ] Response, reception, transduction
    [*] Reception, transduction, response
    [ ] Reception, response, transduction
    Explanation: Cell signaling occurs in three sequential stages: reception (ligand binds to receptor), transduction (signal is relayed and often amplified through a cascade), and response (the cell carries out a specific action).

  Q3. Which type of receptor spans the cell membrane and activates intracellular G proteins when a ligand binds?
    [ ] Ion channel receptor
    [*] G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR)
    [ ] Intracellular receptor
    [ ] Enzyme-linked receptor
    Explanation: G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) are transmembrane receptors that activate associated G proteins upon ligand binding, triggering downstream signaling cascades. They are one of the largest families of cell-surface receptors.

  Q4. What is the function of a second messenger in a signaling pathway?
    [ ] It directly binds to DNA to turn on gene expression
    [ ] It serves as the original signal molecule released by the signaling cell
    [ ] It degrades the ligand after reception
    [*] It relays and amplifies signals from receptors to target molecules inside the cell
    Explanation: Second messengers such as cyclic AMP (cAMP) and calcium ions (Ca2+) are small intracellular molecules that relay and amplify the signal received at the cell surface, activating downstream effector proteins.

  Q5. Steroid hormones like testosterone can pass through the cell membrane because they are:
    [ ] Small enough to fit through membrane channel proteins
    [*] Hydrophobic (lipid-soluble) and can diffuse through the phospholipid bilayer
    [ ] Composed of amino acids that interact with membrane proteins
    [ ] Actively transported by pumps in the membrane
    Explanation: Steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol and are hydrophobic, allowing them to freely diffuse across the phospholipid bilayer to bind intracellular receptors, often acting as transcription factors in the nucleus.

  Q6. Signal amplification in a phosphorylation cascade occurs because:
    [ ] The ligand concentration increases with each step of the cascade
    [*] Each activated kinase can phosphorylate many substrate molecules, multiplying the signal at each step
    [ ] Phosphatases add phosphate groups to amplify the signal
    [ ] Each kinase molecule can activate only one target molecule
    Explanation: In a kinase cascade, one activated kinase can phosphorylate many copies of the next kinase in the pathway. This one-to-many activation at each step produces exponential amplification of the original signal.

  Q7. Which molecule is a common second messenger produced from ATP by the enzyme adenylyl cyclase?
    [ ] IP3
    [ ] Calcium ions
    [*] Cyclic AMP (cAMP)
    [ ] DAG
    Explanation: Cyclic AMP (cAMP) is synthesized from ATP by the membrane-bound enzyme adenylyl cyclase. When a GPCR activates a stimulatory G protein, adenylyl cyclase is activated and produces cAMP, which then activates protein kinase A.

  Q8. Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) initiate signaling by:
    [ ] Directly entering the nucleus to activate genes
    [ ] Releasing G proteins into the cytoplasm
    [ ] Opening ion channels in the membrane
    [*] Dimerizing and cross-phosphorylating tyrosine residues on each other upon ligand binding
    Explanation: When a ligand binds to an RTK, two receptor monomers dimerize and cross-phosphorylate tyrosine residues on each other's intracellular domains. These phosphotyrosines then serve as docking sites for downstream signaling proteins.

  Q9. Which of the following terminates a signaling response by breaking down cAMP?
    [ ] Protein kinase A
    [*] Phosphodiesterase
    [ ] Adenylyl cyclase
    [ ] GTPase
    Explanation: Phosphodiesterase is the enzyme that hydrolyzes cAMP to AMP, terminating the signal. This ensures that the cellular response is temporary and proportional to the continued presence of the signaling molecule.

  Q10. In paracrine signaling, the signaling molecule acts on:
    [*] Nearby cells in the local environment
    [ ] Cells that are in direct physical contact through gap junctions
    [ ] Distant cells via the bloodstream
    [ ] Only the cell that secreted it
    Explanation: Paracrine signaling involves the release of signaling molecules that diffuse locally to affect nearby target cells. Growth factors and neurotransmitters are common examples of paracrine signals.

  Q11. What is a ligand?
    [ ] A second messenger produced inside the cell
    [ ] A membrane protein that receives signals
    [*] A molecule that binds specifically to a receptor, triggering a cellular response
    [ ] An intracellular enzyme that phosphorylates proteins
    Explanation: A ligand is any molecule (such as a hormone, neurotransmitter, or growth factor) that binds specifically to a receptor protein, initiating a signaling event. The binding is based on complementary shapes.

  Q12. What does 'signal transduction' mean?
    [ ] The degradation of a signaling molecule after it has been used
    [*] The process by which an extracellular signal is converted into an intracellular response through a series of molecular steps
    [ ] The release of a hormone into the bloodstream
    [ ] The binding of a ligand to its receptor on the cell surface
    Explanation: Signal transduction is the multi-step process by which a signal received at the cell surface is relayed and converted into a functional change within the cell, often through a cascade of protein activations and second messengers.

  Q13. What is a 'second messenger'?
    [*] A small, non-protein intracellular signaling molecule released in response to receptor activation that helps relay and amplify the signal
    [ ] A backup receptor that activates when the primary receptor fails
    [ ] A protein that carries signals from one cell to another
    [ ] The second signaling molecule released by an endocrine gland
    Explanation: A second messenger is a small intracellular molecule (such as cAMP, Ca2+, or IP3) generated in response to an extracellular signal (the 'first messenger'). Second messengers amplify and propagate the signal inside the cell.

  Q14. What is a 'phosphorylation cascade'?
    [ ] The process by which ATP is broken down during glycolysis
    [ ] A series of ion channels that open in sequence
    [*] A series of reactions in which each protein kinase phosphorylates and activates the next kinase in the sequence, amplifying the signal
    [ ] The removal of phosphate groups from a chain of enzymes
    Explanation: A phosphorylation cascade (or kinase cascade) is a signal transduction mechanism where a series of protein kinases sequentially phosphorylate and activate each other, amplifying the initial signal at each step.

  Q15. What does 'autocrine signaling' mean?
    [*] A type of signaling in which a cell produces a signal that binds to receptors on its own surface, stimulating itself
    [ ] Long-distance signaling through the nervous system
    [ ] Signaling between cells that are in direct physical contact
    [ ] Signaling that occurs only during embryonic development
    Explanation: Autocrine signaling occurs when a cell secretes a signaling molecule that binds to receptors on the same cell (or same cell type), stimulating its own activity. This is commonly seen in immune cell activation and some cancer cells.

  Q16. What is a 'G protein'?
    [ ] A protein that directly binds to DNA to regulate gene expression
    [ ] A transport protein that moves glucose across the membrane
    [*] A signal-relaying protein associated with the cytoplasmic side of the membrane that is active when bound to GTP and inactive when bound to GDP
    [ ] A glycoprotein that forms part of the extracellular matrix
    Explanation: A G protein is a molecular switch that alternates between an active GTP-bound state and an inactive GDP-bound state. When a GPCR is activated by a ligand, the associated G protein exchanges GDP for GTP and relays the signal to downstream effectors.

  Q17. What does 'signal amplification' mean?
    [*] The process by which a single extracellular signal molecule triggers an increasingly large intracellular response through sequential activation steps
    [ ] The increase in the concentration of signaling molecules released by a cell over time
    [ ] The process of making multiple copies of a receptor gene
    [ ] The strengthening of a signal as it travels through the bloodstream
    Explanation: Signal amplification is the process by which a small number of signaling events at the cell surface (e.g., one ligand binding one receptor) generates a progressively larger response inside the cell through cascading activation of many downstream molecules.

  Q18. What is 'apoptosis'?
    [*] Programmed cell death, a genetically controlled process triggered by specific signaling pathways
    [ ] Uncontrolled cell growth leading to tumor formation
    [ ] The bursting of a cell due to osmotic pressure
    [ ] The fusion of two cells during fertilization
    Explanation: Apoptosis is a tightly regulated form of programmed cell death activated by specific signaling pathways. It plays essential roles in development, tissue homeostasis, and immune function by eliminating damaged or unnecessary cells.

  Q19. What does 'receptor specificity' mean?
    [ ] The tendency of receptors to become desensitized over time
    [ ] The ability of a receptor to amplify the signal independently
    [ ] The ability of a single receptor to bind any signaling molecule in the body
    [*] The property by which a receptor recognizes and binds only to a particular ligand or class of ligands due to its specific shape
    Explanation: Receptor specificity refers to the ability of a receptor to selectively bind only certain ligands based on complementary molecular shapes. This specificity ensures that cells respond only to appropriate signals.

  Q20. What is a 'receptor tyrosine kinase' (RTK)?
    [*] A transmembrane receptor that, upon ligand binding, dimerizes and phosphorylates tyrosine residues on its intracellular domain, activating downstream signaling pathways
    [ ] A receptor that opens an ion channel in response to a ligand, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    [ ] An intracellular receptor for steroid hormones due to the well-established relationship between tyrosine trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] A G protein that directly activates adenylyl cyclase, particularly in the context of tyrosine
    Explanation: Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) are enzyme-linked transmembrane receptors that dimerize upon ligand binding and autophosphorylate tyrosine residues on each other. The phosphorylated residues serve as docking sites for relay proteins that activate pathways such as Ras-MAPK.

  Q21. A patient with type 2 diabetes has cells that no longer respond effectively to insulin, even though insulin levels in the blood are normal or elevated. Which aspect of cell signaling is most likely impaired?
    [ ] The blood-brain barrier is preventing insulin from reaching cells
    [ ] The pancreatic beta cells have stopped producing insulin
    [ ] Insulin is being converted to glucagon in the bloodstream
    [*] The insulin receptors or their downstream signaling pathway in target cells are defective, reducing signal transduction
    Explanation: In type 2 diabetes, the problem is insulin resistance: target cells have defects in their insulin receptors or in the downstream signaling cascade (e.g., impaired receptor tyrosine kinase activity or defective GLUT4 transporter trafficking), so they fail to respond properly to insulin.

  Q22. A researcher discovers that a cancer cell has a mutation in the Ras protein that prevents it from hydrolyzing GTP to GDP. What is the most likely effect on cell signaling?
    [ ] Second messengers will no longer be produced in the cell
    [*] The Ras protein will be stuck in its active (GTP-bound) state, continuously stimulating cell division
    [ ] The cell will undergo immediate apoptosis
    [ ] The Ras pathway will be permanently shut off because GTP cannot bind
    Explanation: Ras normally acts as a molecular switch, turning off when it hydrolyzes GTP to GDP. A mutation preventing GTP hydrolysis locks Ras in the active state, continuously activating the MAPK pathway and promoting uncontrolled cell division, a hallmark of many cancers.

  Q23. Epinephrine (adrenaline) binds to receptors on liver cells, triggering a cascade that activates glycogen phosphorylase and breaks down glycogen into glucose. The same epinephrine binds to receptors on heart muscle cells and increases heart rate. Why does the same signal produce different responses in different cells?
    [ ] Liver cells and heart cells have identical signaling pathways but different nuclei
    [ ] Heart cells convert epinephrine to a different hormone before responding
    [ ] Epinephrine changes its molecular shape depending on the target tissue
    [*] Different cell types express different receptor subtypes and/or have different intracellular signaling proteins, leading to different responses
    Explanation: Different cell types can respond differently to the same ligand because they may express different receptor subtypes (e.g., alpha vs. beta adrenergic receptors) and possess different complements of intracellular signaling proteins and effectors.

  Q24. Scientists studying a neurotransmitter find that it binds to a ligand-gated ion channel on the postsynaptic neuron, causing an influx of Na+ ions and rapid depolarization. Unlike GPCR signaling, this response is nearly instantaneous. What property of ligand-gated ion channels explains the speed?
    [ ] Ion channels convert the chemical signal into a hormonal signal for faster transmission
    [ ] The ion channel produces cAMP to rapidly activate downstream targets
    [*] The channel directly opens upon ligand binding without requiring intermediate signal transduction steps, allowing immediate ion flow
    [ ] Ligand-gated ion channels amplify the signal through a phosphorylation cascade before responding
    Explanation: Ligand-gated ion channels provide the fastest type of cell signaling because ligand binding directly opens the channel pore, allowing ions to flow immediately without the delay of multi-step signal transduction cascades.

  Q25. A pharmaceutical company develops a drug that inhibits phosphodiesterase, the enzyme that degrades cAMP. What would be the predicted effect on cells treated with this drug when they receive a signal that normally activates the cAMP pathway?
    [ ] The receptor would be unable to bind its ligand
    [ ] G proteins would be permanently inactivated
    [*] cAMP would accumulate to higher levels and the signal response would be prolonged and intensified
    [ ] cAMP levels would decrease, weakening the cellular response
    Explanation: Phosphodiesterase normally degrades cAMP to AMP, terminating the signal. Inhibiting this enzyme causes cAMP to accumulate, prolonging and amplifying the cellular response. This is the mechanism of drugs like caffeine and sildenafil (Viagra).

  Q26. During embryonic development, a group of cells secretes a signaling molecule that causes neighboring cells to differentiate into nerve tissue, while the signaling cells themselves also respond to the molecule and differentiate. Which types of signaling are involved?
    [ ] Juxtacrine signaling only
    [ ] Synaptic signaling only
    [ ] Endocrine signaling only
    [*] Both paracrine and autocrine signaling
    Explanation: The signaling molecule acts on nearby cells (paracrine) and also on the cells that produced it (autocrine). Both modes of signaling frequently operate simultaneously during development to coordinate tissue differentiation.

  Q27. A laboratory experiment shows that a single molecule of epinephrine binding to a GPCR on a liver cell ultimately activates millions of glycogen phosphorylase enzymes, releasing a massive amount of glucose. What principle of signal transduction does this demonstrate?
    [ ] Signal specificity
    [*] Signal amplification through a multi-step cascade
    [ ] Signal termination by phosphatases
    [ ] Receptor desensitization
    Explanation: This is a textbook example of signal amplification. At each step of the cascade (G protein to adenylyl cyclase to cAMP to protein kinase A to phosphorylase kinase to glycogen phosphorylase), the number of activated molecules increases dramatically.

  Q28. A cell biologist observes that cells treated with cholera toxin have continuously elevated cAMP levels because the G protein alpha subunit cannot hydrolyze GTP. As a result, these intestinal cells secrete large amounts of chloride and water, causing severe diarrhea. Which step in the signaling pathway has been disrupted?
    [ ] The activation of receptor tyrosine kinases
    [ ] The production of IP3 by phospholipase C
    [*] The GTPase activity of the G protein that normally shuts off the signal
    [ ] Ligand binding to the receptor
    Explanation: Cholera toxin modifies the Gs alpha subunit so it cannot hydrolyze GTP to GDP, keeping it permanently active. This leads to continuous activation of adenylyl cyclase, elevated cAMP, and excessive ion and water secretion.

  Q29. Researchers find that certain cancer cells overexpress growth factor receptors (RTKs) on their surface. These cells proliferate even in low concentrations of growth factors. What is the most likely explanation?
    [ ] The extra receptors block the signal from entering the cell
    [ ] The overexpressed receptors prevent apoptosis by degrading all growth factors
    [*] With more receptors, even small amounts of growth factor can trigger sufficient signaling to stimulate cell division
    [ ] The cancer cells no longer require any signaling to divide
    Explanation: Overexpression of RTKs increases the cell's sensitivity to growth factors. Even low concentrations of ligand can activate enough receptors to trigger the downstream MAPK signaling cascade that promotes proliferation, contributing to uncontrolled growth.

  Q30. A geneticist studies a family with a mutation that eliminates the function of a key pro-apoptotic signaling protein. Members of this family are at increased risk for developing tumors. How does the loss of apoptotic signaling contribute to cancer?
    [*] Without apoptosis, cells accumulate mutations and damaged DNA without being eliminated, increasing the chance of tumor formation
    [ ] The loss of apoptosis causes cells to divide more slowly, allowing mutations to accumulate passively
    [ ] Without apoptosis, cells lose the ability to communicate through gap junctions
    [ ] The absence of apoptotic signaling causes all cells to become stem cells
    Explanation: Apoptosis normally eliminates damaged or abnormal cells. When pro-apoptotic signaling is lost, cells with oncogenic mutations survive instead of being destroyed, allowing them to accumulate further mutations and eventually form tumors.

--- Lesson 4.2: Cell Division (Mitosis and Meiosis) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Cell Division (Mitosis and Meiosis)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'mitosis'?
Mitosis is the division of the nucleus that results in two daughter nuclei, each genetically identical to the parent. It is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in eukaryotic organisms.
What is 'crossing over'?
Crossing over is the reciprocal exchange of DNA segments between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during prophase I. It creates recombinant chromosomes with new combinations of alleles, increasing genetic diversity.
What is a 'homologous pair'?
Homologous chromosomes are a pair of chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal) that have the same length, centromere position, and gene loci, though they may carry different alleles. They pair up during meiosis I.
What is 'cytokinesis'?
Cytokinesis is the final step of cell division in which the cytoplasm is physically divided to produce two separate daughter cells. It typically overlaps with the last stages of mitosis or meiosis.
What is 'interphase'?
Interphase is the longest phase of the cell cycle, comprising G1 (growth), S (DNA synthesis), and G2 (preparation for division). During interphase, the cell performs normal functions, grows, and duplicates its chromosomes.
What is a 'sister chromatid'?
Sister chromatids are the two identical copies of a single replicated chromosome that are connected by a centromere. They are produced during DNA replication in S phase and are separated during anaphase of mitosis (or anaphase II of meiosis).
An error in meiosis causes both copies of chromosome 21 to move to the same pole during anaphase I. What is this error called, and what is its most likely consequence if the resulting gamete is fertilized?
Nondisjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes (or sister chromatids) to separate properly during cell division. If both copies of chromosome 21 go to one gamete and that gamete is fertilized, the resulting zygote has three copies of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21).
A researcher observes that cancer cells in a tumor are dividing much faster than normal cells and appear to skip the G1 and G2 checkpoints. What is the most likely consequence of bypassing these checkpoints?
G1 and G2 checkpoints normally ensure that DNA is undamaged and fully replicated before the cell proceeds to division. Bypassing these checkpoints allows cells to divide with errors, leading to mutations, chromosomal abnormalities, and further cancer progression.
Real-World Applications
Example: Crossing over occurs during which stage of meiosis?
Crossing over occurs during prophase I of meiosis, when homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) and exchange segments of DNA at points called chiasmata. This increases genetic variation in the resulting gametes.
Example: A scientist treats a cell culture with colchicine, a drug that prevents microtubule assembly. At which stage would cells treated with colchicine become arrested?
Colchicine prevents microtubule polymerization, so spindle fibers cannot form. Chromosomes may condense and reach the metaphase plate, but without spindle fibers to separate them, cells become arrested at metaphase.
Example: A biology student examines cells under a microscope and sees a cell with chromosomes lined up in pairs at the center of the cell. Is this cell in mitosis or meiosis, and which specific phase?
Chromosomes lined up in homologous pairs (bivalents/tetrads) at the metaphase plate is characteristic of metaphase I of meiosis. In mitosis or meiosis II, individual chromosomes (not pairs) align at the metaphase plate.

--- Lesson 4.2: Cell Division (Mitosis and Meiosis) (Quiz) ---

  Q1. How many daughter cells are produced at the end of mitosis?
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 4
    [ ] 8
    [*] 2
    Explanation: Mitosis produces 2 genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell. Each daughter cell has the same chromosome number (diploid) as the parent.

  Q2. During which phase of mitosis do chromosomes align along the metaphase plate?
    [ ] Prophase
    [ ] Telophase
    [ ] Anaphase
    [*] Metaphase
    Explanation: During metaphase, the spindle fibers align all chromosomes at the cell's equator (metaphase plate). This ensures that each daughter cell receives one copy of each chromosome when they separate in anaphase.

  Q3. Meiosis produces how many haploid daughter cells from a single diploid parent cell?
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 2
    [*] 4
    [ ] 8
    Explanation: Meiosis consists of two consecutive divisions (meiosis I and meiosis II) that produce 4 genetically unique haploid cells from one diploid cell. These cells can develop into gametes (sperm or eggs).

  Q4. Crossing over occurs during which stage of meiosis?
    [ ] Metaphase I
    [ ] Telophase II
    [ ] Anaphase II
    [*] Prophase I
    Explanation: Crossing over occurs during prophase I of meiosis, when homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) and exchange segments of DNA at points called chiasmata. This increases genetic variation in the resulting gametes.

  Q5. What separates during anaphase I of meiosis?
    [ ] Sister chromatids
    [*] Homologous chromosomes
    [ ] Nuclear envelopes
    [ ] Centrioles
    Explanation: During anaphase I, homologous chromosome pairs are pulled to opposite poles of the cell. Sister chromatids remain joined at the centromere until anaphase II.

  Q6. Which structure is responsible for pulling chromosomes apart during cell division?
    [ ] Ribosomes
    [*] Spindle fibers (microtubules)
    [ ] Cell membrane
    [ ] Endoplasmic reticulum
    Explanation: Spindle fibers, composed of microtubule proteins, attach to the kinetochores of chromosomes and pull them toward opposite poles of the cell during anaphase of mitosis and meiosis.

  Q7. DNA replication occurs during which phase of the cell cycle?
    [*] S phase
    [ ] G1 phase
    [ ] G2 phase
    [ ] M phase
    Explanation: DNA replication takes place during the S (synthesis) phase of interphase. After S phase, each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere.

  Q8. Independent assortment during meiosis refers to:
    [ ] The exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes
    [*] The random orientation and separation of homologous chromosome pairs during meiosis I
    [ ] The alignment of chromosomes on the metaphase plate during mitosis
    [ ] The separation of sister chromatids during anaphase II
    Explanation: Independent assortment is the random orientation of homologous pairs at the metaphase plate during meiosis I. This means the maternal and paternal chromosomes are distributed randomly, producing 2^n possible combinations of chromosomes in gametes.

  Q9. Cytokinesis in animal cells occurs by:
    [ ] Dissolution of the nuclear envelope
    [*] Constriction of a cleavage furrow
    [ ] Extension of the cell membrane outward
    [ ] Formation of a cell plate
    Explanation: In animal cells, cytokinesis involves a contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments that pinches the cell membrane inward, forming a cleavage furrow that eventually divides the cytoplasm into two daughter cells.

  Q10. Which of the following is a key difference between mitosis and meiosis?
    [ ] Mitosis produces haploid cells and meiosis produces diploid cells
    [*] Meiosis includes crossing over and independent assortment, producing genetically diverse cells, while mitosis produces genetically identical cells
    [ ] Meiosis occurs only in prokaryotes
    [ ] Mitosis requires DNA replication but meiosis does not
    Explanation: Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells, while meiosis produces four genetically unique haploid daughter cells through crossing over, independent assortment, and two rounds of division.

  Q11. What is 'mitosis'?
    [ ] The division of the cytoplasm following nuclear division
    [ ] The replication of DNA during the S phase of the cell cycle
    [ ] The process of cell division that produces gametes with half the chromosome number
    [*] A type of nuclear division that produces two genetically identical daughter nuclei, each with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell
    Explanation: Mitosis is the division of the nucleus that results in two daughter nuclei, each genetically identical to the parent. It is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in eukaryotic organisms.

  Q12. What does 'meiosis' mean?
    [ ] A type of cell division that doubles the chromosome number
    [ ] The growth phase of the cell cycle before division
    [*] A special type of cell division that reduces the chromosome number by half, producing four genetically unique haploid cells for sexual reproduction
    [ ] The process of DNA repair after replication errors
    Explanation: Meiosis is a specialized form of cell division that reduces the diploid (2n) chromosome number to haploid (n), producing four genetically diverse daughter cells. It is essential for sexual reproduction and occurs in germ cells.

  Q13. What is 'crossing over'?
    [*] The exchange of genetic material between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis
    [ ] The crossing of spindle fibers at the cell equator
    [ ] The transfer of genes from one species to another
    [ ] The movement of chromosomes to opposite poles during anaphase
    Explanation: Crossing over is the reciprocal exchange of DNA segments between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during prophase I. It creates recombinant chromosomes with new combinations of alleles, increasing genetic diversity.

  Q14. What does 'diploid' mean?
    [ ] A cell that has undergone meiosis
    [ ] A cell with extra copies of a single chromosome
    [*] A cell containing two complete sets of chromosomes, one from each parent
    [ ] A cell containing only one set of chromosomes
    Explanation: Diploid (2n) describes a cell that contains two complete sets of homologous chromosomes, one inherited from each parent. In humans, diploid cells have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).

  Q15. What is a 'homologous pair'?
    [ ] A pair of sex chromosomes only
    [ ] Two different genes that control the same trait
    [*] A pair of chromosomes, one from each parent, that are the same size, shape, and carry genes for the same traits at corresponding loci
    [ ] Two identical copies of a chromosome produced by DNA replication (sister chromatids)
    Explanation: Homologous chromosomes are a pair of chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal) that have the same length, centromere position, and gene loci, though they may carry different alleles. They pair up during meiosis I.

  Q16. What does 'haploid' mean?
    [*] A cell containing only one complete set of chromosomes
    [ ] A cell containing two complete sets of chromosomes
    [ ] A cell that is about to undergo mitosis
    [ ] A cell with no chromosomes at all
    Explanation: Haploid (n) describes a cell that contains only one complete set of chromosomes. In humans, haploid cells (gametes) have 23 chromosomes. Fertilization restores the diploid number.

  Q17. What is 'cytokinesis'?
    [ ] The condensation of chromatin into chromosomes
    [*] The physical division of the cytoplasm into two daughter cells following nuclear division
    [ ] The replication of DNA before cell division
    [ ] The division of the nucleus during cell division
    Explanation: Cytokinesis is the final step of cell division in which the cytoplasm is physically divided to produce two separate daughter cells. It typically overlaps with the last stages of mitosis or meiosis.

  Q18. What is 'interphase'?
    [ ] The phase of the cell cycle during which the cell actively divides
    [ ] The phase when chromosomes are visible under a microscope
    [ ] The brief pause between meiosis I and meiosis II
    [*] The period between cell divisions during which the cell grows, replicates its DNA, and prepares for division
    Explanation: Interphase is the longest phase of the cell cycle, comprising G1 (growth), S (DNA synthesis), and G2 (preparation for division). During interphase, the cell performs normal functions, grows, and duplicates its chromosomes.

  Q19. What does 'independent assortment' mean?
    [ ] Genes on the same chromosome always segregate together during meiosis
    [*] The random orientation and separation of homologous chromosome pairs during meiosis I, so that the distribution of one pair does not influence another
    [ ] Daughter cells independently choose which chromosomes to keep after division
    [ ] Each chromosome independently replicates during S phase
    Explanation: Independent assortment states that during meiosis I, each pair of homologous chromosomes sorts into gametes independently of other pairs. This random distribution is a major source of genetic variation.

  Q20. What is a 'sister chromatid'?
    [ ] One chromosome from each parent that forms a homologous pair
    [*] One of two identical copies of a replicated chromosome, joined at the centromere
    [ ] The protein structure that holds chromosomes to the spindle
    [ ] A fragment of chromosome produced during crossing over
    Explanation: Sister chromatids are the two identical copies of a single replicated chromosome that are connected by a centromere. They are produced during DNA replication in S phase and are separated during anaphase of mitosis (or anaphase II of meiosis).

  Q21. A couple undergoes genetic counseling and learns that the woman carries a translocation between chromosomes 14 and 21. Their genetic counselor explains that this increases the risk of having a child with Down syndrome. During which process in the mother's cells would this translocation most likely cause problems?
    [ ] DNA replication during S phase
    [ ] Crossing over during prophase I, which repairs the translocation
    [ ] Cytokinesis of the fertilized egg during the first mitotic division
    [*] Segregation of chromosomes during meiosis I, where the translocation can lead to unbalanced gametes with extra chromosome 21 material
    Explanation: A Robertsonian translocation between chromosomes 14 and 21 can cause abnormal segregation during meiosis I, producing gametes with an extra copy of chromosome 21 material. If such a gamete is fertilized, the resulting embryo will have trisomy 21 (Down syndrome).

  Q22. A scientist treats a cell culture with colchicine, a drug that prevents microtubule assembly. At which stage would cells treated with colchicine become arrested?
    [ ] S phase, because DNA replication requires microtubules
    [ ] Telophase, because the nuclear envelope cannot reform
    [ ] G1 phase, because the cell cannot grow without microtubules
    [*] Metaphase, because chromosomes cannot be pulled to the poles without functional spindle fibers
    Explanation: Colchicine prevents microtubule polymerization, so spindle fibers cannot form. Chromosomes may condense and reach the metaphase plate, but without spindle fibers to separate them, cells become arrested at metaphase.

  Q23. Identical twins arise from a single fertilized egg that splits into two embryos early in development. Which type of cell division is responsible for this splitting?
    [ ] Meiosis, because two haploid cells are produced
    [ ] Binary fission, because the embryo is unicellular
    [*] Mitosis, because the zygote divides to produce genetically identical cells that separate into two embryos
    [ ] A second round of meiosis occurring after fertilization
    Explanation: After fertilization, the zygote undergoes mitosis. If the early embryonic cells separate into two groups, each group can develop into a complete organism. Since mitosis produces genetically identical cells, the resulting twins are genetically identical.

  Q24. A human cell has 46 chromosomes. After meiosis I is complete, how many chromosomes does each resulting cell contain?
    [ ] 23, each as a single chromatid
    [*] 23, each consisting of two sister chromatids
    [ ] 92 within the broader context of cell
    [ ] 46 within the broader context of cell
    Explanation: After meiosis I, each daughter cell has 23 chromosomes (haploid number), but each chromosome still consists of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere. The sister chromatids are separated later in meiosis II.

  Q25. A farmer breeds two plant varieties and notices that offspring show far more trait combinations than expected from independent assortment alone. The farmer's genetic advisor suggests that crossing over during meiosis significantly increased genetic variation. How does crossing over achieve this?
    [*] It creates new combinations of alleles on the same chromosome by exchanging DNA segments between homologs
    [ ] It introduces entirely new genes from the environment
    [ ] It eliminates harmful mutations from the chromosomes
    [ ] It doubles the chromosome number, providing more genetic material
    Explanation: Crossing over breaks and rejoins DNA segments between homologous chromosomes, creating recombinant chromosomes with new allele combinations. This generates genetic diversity beyond what independent assortment alone can produce.

  Q26. An error in meiosis causes both copies of chromosome 21 to move to the same pole during anaphase I. What is this error called, and what is its most likely consequence if the resulting gamete is fertilized?
    [ ] Crossing over; the offspring will have increased genetic diversity
    [ ] Deletion; the offspring will be missing chromosome 21 entirely
    [*] Nondisjunction; the offspring may have trisomy 21 (Down syndrome)
    [ ] Translocation; the offspring will have a rearranged chromosome
    Explanation: Nondisjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes (or sister chromatids) to separate properly during cell division. If both copies of chromosome 21 go to one gamete and that gamete is fertilized, the resulting zygote has three copies of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21).

  Q27. A researcher observes that cancer cells in a tumor are dividing much faster than normal cells and appear to skip the G1 and G2 checkpoints. What is the most likely consequence of bypassing these checkpoints?
    [ ] The cells will become haploid and lose the ability to divide
    [ ] The cells will undergo apoptosis more frequently
    [ ] The cells will only undergo meiosis instead of mitosis
    [*] The cells may divide with damaged DNA or incomplete replication, accumulating mutations and genomic instability
    Explanation: G1 and G2 checkpoints normally ensure that DNA is undamaged and fully replicated before the cell proceeds to division. Bypassing these checkpoints allows cells to divide with errors, leading to mutations, chromosomal abnormalities, and further cancer progression.

  Q28. In human males, meiosis produces four haploid sperm cells from one diploid spermatocyte. In human females, meiosis produces one large egg cell and three small polar bodies. Why does female meiosis produce cells of unequal size?
    [ ] Female meiosis undergoes only one division instead of two
    [ ] Female cells have fewer chromosomes to distribute
    [*] Unequal cytokinesis directs most of the cytoplasm and nutrients to one cell (the egg) to support early embryonic development
    [ ] Polar bodies are actually diploid cells that failed to complete meiosis
    Explanation: During oogenesis, unequal cytokinesis concentrates nearly all the cytoplasm and stored nutrients into the single egg cell. The polar bodies receive minimal cytoplasm and eventually degenerate. This ensures the egg has sufficient resources for early development after fertilization.

  Q29. A biology student examines cells under a microscope and sees a cell with chromosomes lined up in pairs at the center of the cell. Is this cell in mitosis or meiosis, and which specific phase?
    [*] Meiosis I, metaphase I, because homologous pairs (tetrads) are aligned at the metaphase plate

    Explanation: Chromosomes lined up in homologous pairs (bivalents/tetrads) at the metaphase plate is characteristic of metaphase I of meiosis. In mitosis or meiosis II, individual chromosomes (not pairs) align at the metaphase plate.

  Q30. An organism has a diploid number of 8 (2n = 8). How many different combinations of chromosomes are possible in its gametes due to independent assortment alone (without crossing over)?

    [*] 16
    [ ] 8

--- Lesson 4.3: Regulation of Cell Cycle (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Regulation of Cell Cycle
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'cell cycle checkpoint'?
Cell cycle checkpoints are regulatory mechanisms at specific points (G1, G2, and M) where the cell assesses conditions like DNA integrity, cell size, and proper chromosome attachment before committing to the next phase of division.
What is a 'tumor suppressor gene'?
Tumor suppressor genes encode proteins that normally restrain cell division (e.g., p53, Rb). When both copies of a tumor suppressor gene are lost or inactivated by mutation, the cell loses a critical brake on proliferation, increasing cancer risk.
What is a 'proto-oncogene'?
Proto-oncogenes are normal genes encoding proteins that promote cell growth, division, and survival (e.g., growth factors, signal transduction proteins, transcription factors). Mutations converting them to oncogenes result in overactive proteins that drive uncontrolled proliferation.
What is 'apoptosis' in the context of cell cycle regulation?
Apoptosis is a regulated form of cell death that eliminates damaged, infected, or unnecessary cells. In cell cycle regulation, apoptosis acts as a fail-safe: if DNA damage cannot be repaired, p53 and other proteins trigger apoptosis to prevent the propagation of mutations.
What is a 'growth factor'?
Growth factors are proteins secreted by cells that stimulate proliferation of target cells by binding to cell-surface receptors and activating intracellular signaling pathways (e.g., PDGF, EGF). They are important external regulators of the cell cycle.
What is 'G0 phase'?
G0 is a quiescent phase where cells exit the cell cycle and stop dividing. Some cells (like neurons) remain in G0 permanently, while others (like liver cells) can re-enter the cycle in response to appropriate stimuli.
A molecular biologist finds that a cancer cell line has a mutation that causes Rb protein to be permanently phosphorylated. What is the predicted effect on cell cycle regulation?
Rb normally inhibits E2F when unphosphorylated. If Rb is permanently phosphorylated, it can never bind and inhibit E2F, so E2F continuously activates genes required for DNA replication and S phase entry, promoting uncontrolled cell proliferation.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which tumor suppressor protein is known as the 'guardian of the genome' because it halts the cell cycle when DNA damage is detected?
p53 is a transcription factor that responds to DNA damage by activating genes that halt the cell cycle (e.g., p21), giving the cell time to repair DNA or, if damage is irreparable, triggering apoptosis. Mutations in p53 are found in over 50% of human cancers.
Example: Cancer typically requires mutations in multiple genes. Why is a single mutation usually insufficient to cause cancer?
Cells possess multiple layers of defense against uncontrolled division, including growth factor requirements, checkpoint controls, DNA repair enzymes, tumor suppressors, and apoptotic pathways. Cancer usually develops only after several of these safeguards are disabled by accumulated mutations.
Example: What does 'anchorage dependence' mean in cell biology?
Anchorage dependence is a property of normal cells that requires them to be attached to a surface (extracellular matrix or other substratum) to grow and divide. Cancer cells often lose anchorage dependence, allowing them to grow in suspension and metastasize.

    [ ] They directly replicate DNA
    [*] They act as external signals that stimulate cells to enter the cell cycle and divide

    Explanation: Growth factors are extracellular signaling proteins that bind to receptors on target cells, activating intracellular signaling cascades that promote entry into the cell cycle and cell division.

    Explanation: Rb is a tumor suppressor that, in its unphosphorylated form, binds and inhibits E2F transcription factors needed for S phase gene expression. When cyclin-Cdk complexes phosphorylate Rb, E2F is released and the cell proceeds to S phase.

    Explanation: Cyclin-dependent kinases (Cdks) are enzymes that phosphorylate target proteins to advance the cell cycle, but they are only active when bound to their cyclin partners. Different cyclin-Cdk complexes regulate different phases of the cycle.

--- Lesson 4.4: DNA Replication and Repair (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: DNA Replication and Repair
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'semiconservative replication'?
Semiconservative replication describes how each parental DNA strand serves as a template for a new complementary strand, so each daughter molecule retains ('conserves') one of the original strands paired with one new strand.
What is an 'origin of replication'?
An origin of replication (ORI) is a specific sequence in the DNA where proteins bind to initiate the unwinding of the helix and the assembly of the replication machinery. Eukaryotic chromosomes have multiple origins to speed replication.
What is 'DNA ligase'?
DNA ligase joins breaks (nicks) in the DNA backbone by catalyzing the formation of a phosphodiester bond between the 3'-OH of one fragment and the 5'-phosphate of the adjacent fragment. It is essential for joining Okazaki fragments and completing DNA repair.
What is 'nucleotide excision repair'?

--- Lesson 4.5: DNA Damage and Repair Mechanisms (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: DNA Damage and Repair Mechanisms
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'base excision repair' (BER)?
Base excision repair begins when a DNA glycosylase recognizes and removes the damaged base, leaving an abasic site. An AP endonuclease then nicks the backbone, and DNA polymerase fills the gap using the complementary strand, followed by ligation.
What is 'homologous recombination' in the context of DNA repair?
Homologous recombination repair uses sequence homology from an undamaged template (usually the sister chromatid) to restore the original sequence at a double-strand break. It is highly accurate but requires the template, so it is most active during S and G2 phases.
What is a 'DNA glycosylase'?
DNA glycosylases are a family of enzymes that scan DNA for specific types of damaged or modified bases. Each glycosylase recognizes particular lesions (e.g., uracil-DNA glycosylase removes uracil) and cleaves the glycosidic bond between the base and sugar.
What is an 'abasic site'?
An abasic site (also called an AP site, for apurinic/apyrimidinic) is a location in DNA where a base has been lost or removed. The backbone remains intact but the missing base creates a non-coding lesion that must be repaired to prevent mutations.
What is a 'mutagen'?
A mutagen is any agent that causes mutations. Physical mutagens include UV radiation and ionizing radiation. Chemical mutagens include alkylating agents, base analogs, and intercalating agents. Biological mutagens include certain viruses and transposable elements.
A biochemist discovers that a colony of bacteria living near a hydrothermal vent has an exceptionally high rate of depurination due to the extreme heat. Despite this, the bacteria show a normal mutation rate. What is the most likely explanation?
Abasic sites from depurination are repaired by base excision repair (BER), initiated by AP endonuclease. Thermophilic bacteria exposed to constant high temperatures have likely evolved highly efficient BER systems to handle the increased rate of spontaneous damage.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
Transcription-coupled NER preferentially repairs lesions on the transcribed strand of active genes, which is critical for neurons and other non-dividing, transcriptionally active cells. The global genome NER pathway (which repairs lesions throughout the genome) remains functional, preventing the mutagenesis that leads to cancer.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which type of DNA damage is most commonly caused by ultraviolet (UV) radiation?
UV radiation, especially UVB, causes adjacent pyrimidine bases (most commonly thymines) to form covalent dimers. These dimers distort the DNA helix and block normal replication and transcription.
Example: Depurination is a type of spontaneous DNA damage that involves:
Depurination occurs when the bond between a purine base and its deoxyribose sugar spontaneously breaks, leaving an abasic (AP) site. This is the most common type of spontaneous DNA damage, occurring thousands of times per cell per day.
Example: Reactive oxygen species (ROS) cause DNA damage primarily through:
Reactive oxygen species oxidize DNA bases, with guanine being particularly vulnerable, producing 8-oxoguanine. This modified base can mispair with adenine instead of cytosine during replication, causing G-to-T transversion mutations if not repaired.
Example: Which protein is activated in response to DNA damage to halt the cell cycle and allow time for repair?
p53 is a transcription factor activated in response to DNA damage. It induces expression of cell cycle inhibitors (like p21) to arrest the cell cycle at checkpoints, giving the cell time to repair damage before replication or division.
Example: Alkylating agents cause DNA damage by:
Alkylating agents transfer alkyl groups (such as methyl or ethyl groups) to DNA bases, particularly guanine. This can cause base mispairing, replication blocks, or strand breaks, and is the mechanism by which many chemotherapy drugs and environmental mutagens damage DNA.
Example: What does 'deamination' mean in the context of DNA damage?
Deamination is the loss of an amino group from a DNA base. The most common example is deamination of cytosine to uracil. If unrepaired, uracil pairs with adenine during replication, resulting in a C-to-T transition mutation.
Example: What is a 'mutagen'?
A mutagen is any agent that causes mutations. Physical mutagens include UV radiation and ionizing radiation. Chemical mutagens include alkylating agents, base analogs, and intercalating agents. Biological mutagens include certain viruses and transposable elements.
Example: A dermatologist explains to a patient that their chronic sun exposure has led to the accumulation of thousands of thymine dimers in their skin cell DNA. The patient asks why their body cannot simply fix all the damage. Which repair pathway is primarily responsible, and why might it be overwhelmed?
NER is the primary pathway for removing bulky DNA lesions like thymine dimers. However, extensive UV exposure produces dimers at a rate faster than NER can repair them, especially if cells replicate before repair is complete, leading to permanent mutations and skin cancer risk.
Example: A radiation oncologist treats a tumor with ionizing radiation, which induces double-strand breaks. The treatment is more effective when given in multiple small doses rather than one large dose. Why does fractionated dosing improve the outcome?
Fractionated radiation allows normal cells to repair double-strand breaks (using HR and NHEJ) between sessions. Cancer cells, which often have defective DNA repair pathways and divide rapidly, are less able to repair damage between doses, accumulating lethal levels of DSBs.
Example: CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology creates targeted double-strand breaks in DNA. Researchers rely on the cell's own repair mechanisms to either disrupt a gene or insert new sequences. Which repair pathway would a researcher want to exploit if the goal is to precisely insert a new gene at the cut site?
For precise gene insertion, researchers supply a DNA donor template with the desired sequence flanked by homology arms. The cell's homologous recombination machinery uses this template to repair the Cas9-induced break, incorporating the new sequence precisely.

    [ ] Breaking the sugar-phosphate backbone directly
    [ ] Cross-linking histone proteins to DNA
    [ ] Removing entire nucleotides from the DNA
    Explanation: Alkylating agents transfer alkyl groups (such as methyl or ethyl groups) to DNA bases, particularly guanine. This can cause base mispairing, replication blocks, or strand breaks, and is the mechanism by which many chemotherapy drugs and environmental mutagens damage DNA.

  Q11. What is 'base excision repair' (BER)?
    [ ] The replacement of RNA bases with DNA bases during replication
    [*] A DNA repair mechanism in which a damaged or incorrect base is removed by a glycosylase, and the resulting gap is filled using the intact complementary strand as a template
    [ ] A repair pathway that removes large segments of DNA surrounding a damaged base
    [ ] A process that excises entire genes from a chromosome
    Explanation: Base excision repair begins when a DNA glycosylase recognizes and removes the damaged base, leaving an abasic site. An AP endonuclease then nicks the backbone, and DNA polymerase fills the gap using the complementary strand, followed by ligation.

  Q12. What does 'double-strand break' mean?
    [ ] A break in a single strand of RNA during transcription
    [*] A type of DNA damage in which both strands of the DNA double helix are severed at the same or nearby locations
    [ ] The separation of sister chromatids during anaphase
    [ ] A break in the hydrogen bonds between base pairs
    Explanation: A double-strand break (DSB) occurs when both phosphodiester backbones of the DNA helix are broken. DSBs can be caused by ionizing radiation, reactive oxygen species, or errors during replication, and are among the most dangerous forms of DNA damage.

  Q13. What is 'homologous recombination' in the context of DNA repair?
    [ ] A process that repairs single-base mismatches after replication
    [ ] The crossing over of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during meiosis only
    [ ] The random joining of broken DNA ends without a template
    [*] A high-fidelity repair mechanism that uses a homologous DNA sequence (typically a sister chromatid) as a template to accurately repair double-strand breaks
    Explanation: Homologous recombination repair uses sequence homology from an undamaged template (usually the sister chromatid) to restore the original sequence at a double-strand break. It is highly accurate but requires the template, so it is most active during S and G2 phases.

  Q14. What does 'non-homologous end joining' (NHEJ) mean?
    [*] A repair mechanism that directly ligates the two broken ends of a double-strand break together without requiring a homologous template, often introducing small errors
    [ ] The end-to-end fusion of telomeres on different chromosomes
    [ ] The joining of Okazaki fragments during normal replication
    [ ] A DNA repair pathway that uses the complementary strand to fix mismatches
    Explanation: Non-homologous end joining is a fast but error-prone repair pathway that re-ligates the broken ends of a double-strand break without using a template. It can function in any cell cycle phase but may introduce small insertions or deletions.

  Q15. What is a 'DNA glycosylase'?
    [*] An enzyme that recognizes and removes a damaged or inappropriate base from DNA by cleaving the glycosidic bond, initiating base excision repair
    [ ] An enzyme that synthesizes new DNA strands
    [ ] A protein that stabilizes the DNA helix during replication
    [ ] An enzyme that adds sugar molecules to DNA bases
    Explanation: DNA glycosylases are a family of enzymes that scan DNA for specific types of damaged or modified bases. Each glycosylase recognizes particular lesions (e.g., uracil-DNA glycosylase removes uracil) and cleaves the glycosidic bond between the base and sugar.

  Q16. What does 'depurination' mean?
    [ ] The enzymatic addition of purine bases during DNA repair
    [ ] The conversion of a purine base into a pyrimidine base
    [ ] The removal of amino groups from purine bases
    [*] The spontaneous loss of a purine base (adenine or guanine) from the DNA backbone, creating an abasic site
    Explanation: Depurination is the spontaneous hydrolysis of the glycosidic bond between a purine base and deoxyribose, causing the base to be lost and leaving an abasic (apurinic) site. It occurs approximately 10,000 times per cell per day in human cells.

  Q17. What does 'deamination' mean in the context of DNA damage?
    [*] The spontaneous removal of an amino group from a DNA base, converting it to a different base that can cause mutations
    [ ] The addition of amino groups to DNA bases by repair enzymes
    [ ] The transfer of an amine group from one base to another within the same strand
    [ ] The breakdown of amino acids near DNA
    Explanation: Deamination is the loss of an amino group from a DNA base. The most common example is deamination of cytosine to uracil. If unrepaired, uracil pairs with adenine during replication, resulting in a C-to-T transition mutation.

  Q18. What is an 'abasic site'?
    [ ] A base that has been modified by methylation
    [*] A location in DNA where a base has been lost, leaving the sugar-phosphate backbone intact but without a base
    [ ] A region of DNA that lacks any sugar molecules
    [ ] A site where the DNA double helix is opened during replication
    Explanation: An abasic site (also called an AP site, for apurinic/apyrimidinic) is a location in DNA where a base has been lost or removed. The backbone remains intact but the missing base creates a non-coding lesion that must be repaired to prevent mutations.

  Q19. What is a 'mutagen'?
    [*] A physical or chemical agent that increases the rate of mutations by damaging DNA or interfering with DNA repair
    [ ] A gene that controls the rate of cell division
    [ ] An enzyme that checks DNA for errors during replication
    [ ] A protein that repairs DNA damage
    Explanation: A mutagen is any agent that causes mutations. Physical mutagens include UV radiation and ionizing radiation. Chemical mutagens include alkylating agents, base analogs, and intercalating agents. Biological mutagens include certain viruses and transposable elements.

  Q20. What does 'photolyase' mean?
    [ ] An enzyme that synthesizes DNA during photosynthesis
    [*] A light-activated enzyme that directly reverses UV-induced pyrimidine dimers by cleaving the covalent bonds between them
    [ ] A protein that degrades DNA in the presence of light
    [ ] An enzyme that creates thymine dimers using light energy
    Explanation: Photolyase is an enzyme found in many organisms (but not placental mammals) that uses energy from visible light to directly split pyrimidine dimers, restoring the bases to their original monomeric form in a process called photoreactivation.

  Q21. A dermatologist explains to a patient that their chronic sun exposure has led to the accumulation of thousands of thymine dimers in their skin cell DNA. The patient asks why their body cannot simply fix all the damage. Which repair pathway is primarily responsible, and why might it be overwhelmed?
    [ ] Mismatch repair; it normally handles UV damage but becomes saturated
    [ ] Homologous recombination handles all UV damage but requires the cell to be in S phase
    [ ] Base excision repair removes dimers but only works in the presence of sunlight
    [*] Nucleotide excision repair (NER) is the primary pathway for removing thymine dimers, but chronic high-dose UV exposure can produce more lesions than NER can repair before replication, allowing mutations to accumulate
    Explanation: NER is the primary pathway for removing bulky DNA lesions like thymine dimers. However, extensive UV exposure produces dimers at a rate faster than NER can repair them, especially if cells replicate before repair is complete, leading to permanent mutations and skin cancer risk.

  Q22. A radiation oncologist treats a tumor with ionizing radiation, which induces double-strand breaks. The treatment is more effective when given in multiple small doses rather than one large dose. Why does fractionated dosing improve the outcome?
    [ ] Fractionated dosing converts double-strand breaks into single-strand breaks
    [*] Normal cells can repair DSBs between doses, while rapidly dividing cancer cells have less time to repair, making them more susceptible to accumulated damage
    [ ] Multiple small doses activate telomerase in normal cells only
    [ ] Small doses completely prevent DNA repair in all cells
    Explanation: Fractionated radiation allows normal cells to repair double-strand breaks (using HR and NHEJ) between sessions. Cancer cells, which often have defective DNA repair pathways and divide rapidly, are less able to repair damage between doses, accumulating lethal levels of DSBs.

  Q23. CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology creates targeted double-strand breaks in DNA. Researchers rely on the cell's own repair mechanisms to either disrupt a gene or insert new sequences. Which repair pathway would a researcher want to exploit if the goal is to precisely insert a new gene at the cut site?
    [ ] Mismatch repair, because it can insert new genes during replication
    [ ] Base excision repair, because it can replace single bases with any desired sequence
    [ ] Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), because it is error-prone and creates random insertions
    [*] Homologous recombination (HR), because providing a DNA template with the desired sequence allows precise insertion at the break site
    Explanation: For precise gene insertion, researchers supply a DNA donor template with the desired sequence flanked by homology arms. The cell's homologous recombination machinery uses this template to repair the Cas9-induced break, incorporating the new sequence precisely.

  Q24. A biochemist discovers that a colony of bacteria living near a hydrothermal vent has an exceptionally high rate of depurination due to the extreme heat. Despite this, the bacteria show a normal mutation rate. What is the most likely explanation?
    [ ] The bacteria have extra chromosomes to compensate for damaged ones
    [*] The bacteria have evolved highly efficient base excision repair and AP endonuclease systems to rapidly repair abasic sites before replication
    [ ] The heat destroys all mutant bacteria, leaving only unmutated survivors
    [ ] Depurination does not actually cause mutations
    Explanation: Abasic sites from depurination are repaired by base excision repair (BER), initiated by AP endonuclease. Thermophilic bacteria exposed to constant high temperatures have likely evolved highly efficient BER systems to handle the increased rate of spontaneous damage.

  Q25. A patient with Fanconi anemia has defective repair of DNA interstrand crosslinks. These crosslinks prevent the two DNA strands from being separated. Why is this particularly problematic during DNA replication?
    [ ] Interstrand crosslinks activate telomerase, causing uncontrolled growth
    [ ] Interstrand crosslinks prevent the cell from producing RNA
    [*] Crosslinks block the replication fork because helicase cannot separate the two strands, leading to fork collapse and double-strand breaks
    [ ] Crosslinks cause the cell to duplicate its chromosomes without dividing
    Explanation: Interstrand crosslinks covalently link the two DNA strands, preventing their separation by helicase. When the replication fork encounters a crosslink, it stalls and can collapse, generating double-strand breaks that lead to chromosomal instability and bone marrow failure in Fanconi anemia patients.

  Q26. A laboratory mouse is engineered to lack the gene for uracil-DNA glycosylase. What type of mutations would you expect to accumulate in this mouse's cells over time?
    [*] C-to-T transition mutations, because spontaneous deamination of cytosine produces uracil that cannot be removed
    [ ] Large chromosomal deletions
    [ ] Transversion mutations (purine to pyrimidine changes)
    [ ] Frameshift mutations from inserted nucleotides
    Explanation: Uracil-DNA glycosylase normally removes uracil that arises from cytosine deamination. Without this enzyme, uracil persists in the DNA and pairs with adenine during replication, converting the original C:G base pair to a T:A pair (a C-to-T transition mutation).

  Q27. A researcher treats cells with a chemical that generates large amounts of 8-oxoguanine in their DNA. If left unrepaired, what type of mutation would result during the next round of replication?
    [ ] A G-to-C mutation due to pairing with cytosine on both strands
    [ ] A deletion of the affected guanine
    [*] A G-to-T transversion mutation, because 8-oxoguanine mispairs with adenine
    [ ] An insertion of an extra guanine
    Explanation: 8-oxoguanine is an oxidized form of guanine that can mispair with adenine instead of cytosine. After one round of replication, the mispairing produces an A:T base pair where there was originally a G:C, resulting in a G-to-T transversion mutation.

  Q28. PARP inhibitors are a class of cancer drugs that block poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase, an enzyme involved in single-strand break repair. These drugs are particularly effective against cancers with BRCA1/2 mutations. Why are BRCA-mutant cancer cells especially sensitive to PARP inhibition?
    [ ] PARP inhibitors directly restore BRCA1/2 function given that prevailing Brca conditions tend to reinforce this particular trajectory
    [ ] BRCA mutations make cells divide faster, increasing drug uptake in most scenarios involving Brca factors
    [ ] BRCA-mutant cells rely entirely on PARP for DNA replication when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [*] BRCA-mutant cells cannot perform homologous recombination, so when PARP-dependent single-strand break repair is also blocked, unrepaired breaks convert to lethal double-strand breaks
    Explanation: This is an example of synthetic lethality. BRCA-mutant cells already lack homologous recombination repair. When PARP inhibitors block the backup single-strand break repair pathway, unrepaired SSBs become DSBs during replication that cannot be fixed, causing cancer cell death while sparing normal cells with intact BRCA.

  Q29. A microbiologist discovers that certain bacteria near Chernobyl have developed extraordinary resistance to ionizing radiation. These bacteria can survive radiation doses that would be lethal to most organisms. What DNA repair adaptations would most likely explain this resistance?
    [ ] They have evolved DNA that does not form double-strand breaks
    [ ] They have replaced DNA with a radiation-resistant polymer
    [ ] They lack DNA entirely and use only RNA for genetic information
    [*] They have highly efficient and redundant double-strand break repair systems with enhanced homologous recombination and multiple copies of their genome for templates
    Explanation: Radiation-resistant bacteria like Deinococcus radiodurans survive extreme radiation through highly efficient DSB repair, multiple genome copies providing repair templates, enhanced RecA-mediated homologous recombination, and protective mechanisms that shield repair enzymes from oxidative damage.

  Q30. A clinical geneticist counsels a family affected by Cockayne syndrome, a condition in which a specific subpathway of nucleotide excision repair (transcription-coupled NER) is defective. Patients exhibit growth failure and neurodegeneration but, surprisingly, do not have elevated cancer rates. Why might defective transcription-coupled NER cause neurodegeneration without increasing cancer risk?
    [ ] Cockayne syndrome patients die too young to develop cancer due to the well-established relationship between cancer trends and broader outcomes
    [ ] Neurons are immune to all forms of DNA damage, as the combined effect of cancer dynamics and coupled changes creates predictable patterns that point in this direction
    [ ] Transcription-coupled NER only repairs mitochondrial DNA considering that the key variables involved tend to move in this direction together
    [*] Transcription-coupled NER repairs damage only in actively transcribed genes, so its loss primarily affects highly active non-dividing cells like neurons, while global NER still prevents cancer-causing mutations in dividing cells
    Explanation: Transcription-coupled NER preferentially repairs lesions on the transcribed strand of active genes, which is critical for neurons and other non-dividing, transcriptionally active cells. The global genome NER pathway (which repairs lesions throughout the genome) remains functional, preventing the mutagenesis that leads to cancer.
