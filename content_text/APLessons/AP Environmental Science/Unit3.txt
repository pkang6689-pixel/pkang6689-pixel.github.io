--- Lesson 3.1: Population Growth Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Population Growth Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is exponential growth?
Exponential growth occurs when a population grows at a constant rate per individual per unit time. The result is ever-faster growth as the expanding population base multiplies at the same per capita rate.
What is 'carrying capacity (K)'?
Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population an environment can support long-term with its available resources. It can change over time as conditions change.
What is meant by 'biotic potential'?
Biotic potential is the theoretical maximum rate of population increase under perfect conditions. Real populations never achieve biotic potential because environmental resistance always limits growth.
What is 'doubling time'?
Doubling time uses the Rule of 70: dividing 70 by the annual growth rate percentage gives the approximate number of years for a population to double. A 1% growth rate means doubling in about 70 years.
What is 'zero population growth (ZPG)'?
Zero population growth occurs when the population size is stable because additions (births + immigration) exactly balance losses (deaths + emigration), resulting in no net change.
Real-World Applications
Example: An invasive carp species is introduced to a river system. In the first year, the population doubles. In subsequent years, growth continues at a similar rate. Native fish populations begin to decline. A wildlife manager asks how quickly the carp population will reach problematic levels. What model should be used for the initial projection, and what are its limitations?
During early invasion, exponential growth is appropriate because resources are abundant. However, the model becomes unrealistic as the population grows and competition, predation, and resource depletion begin to slow growth.
Example: A conservation biologist is managing a population of 200 endangered tortoises in a reserve. The tortoises are K-selected (live 100+ years, mature at age 20, lay 5-10 eggs per year). A drought kills 50% of the population. Compared to a similarly-sized r-selected species, how would recovery differ?
K-selected species recover slowly from population declines because they have low intrinsic growth rates (r), long generation times, and few offspring. An r-selected species could rebound within a few generations.

--- Lesson 3.2: Population Limiting Factors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Population Limiting Factors
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between disease transmission and population density?
Higher population density means more frequent contact between individuals, increasing the rate of disease transmission. This makes disease a powerful density-dependent population regulator.
What is Liebig's Law of the Minimum?
Liebig's Law states that population growth is constrained by the single resource that is most scarce relative to need (the limiting factor), even if all other resources are abundant.
What is the Allee effect?
The Allee effect occurs when very small populations experience reduced growth rates because of difficulty finding mates, reduced cooperative defense, or loss of genetic diversity, making recovery harder.
What is a limiting factor in ecology?
A limiting factor is any biotic or abiotic condition that constrains the growth, abundance, or distribution of an organism or population within an ecosystem.
What is a 'density-independent factor'?
Density-independent factors impact populations irrespective of size or density. Floods, fires, droughts, earthquakes, and extreme temperatures kill organisms regardless of how many are present.
What is meant by 'interspecific competition'?
Interspecific competition occurs when two or more different species compete for the same limited resources. It can lead to competitive exclusion or niche partitioning.
What is 'niche partitioning'?
Niche partitioning (resource partitioning) is an evolutionary response to competition where coexisting species diverge in their resource use, reducing overlap and allowing coexistence.
What is 'negative feedback' in population regulation?
Negative feedback occurs when a population increase triggers density-dependent factors (more competition, disease, predation) that slow growth, while a decrease reduces these pressures, allowing recovery toward K.

Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the demographic transition model?
The demographic transition model describes four stages of population change: (1) high birth and death rates, (2) declining death rates with high birth rates, (3) declining birth rates, and (4) low birth and death rates.
What is the total fertility rate (TFR) needed for population replacement in developed countries?
Replacement-level fertility is approximately 2.1 children per woman in developed countries (slightly above 2 to account for childhood mortality). Below this, the population eventually declines.
What is a population pyramid (age structure diagram)?
A population pyramid is a bar chart showing the number or percentage of people in each age group, with males on one side and females on the other. Its shape reveals growth trends.
What is meant by 'population momentum'?
Population momentum means that even when fertility reaches replacement level, a population with many young people will continue growing for decades as those large cohorts move through their reproductive years.
What is the 'demographic transition'?
The demographic transition describes the shift countries undergo as they develop: from pre-industrial high birth/death rates through a growth phase to post-industrial low birth/death rates.
What is 'infant mortality rate'?
Infant mortality rate (IMR) measures deaths of children under age 1 per 1,000 live births. It is a key indicator of a country's health care quality and development level.
What is 'replacement-level fertility'?
Replacement-level fertility is the TFR needed for each generation to exactly replace itself. In developed nations, this is about 2.1 (slightly above 2 to account for mortality before reproductive age).
A developing nation has a TFR of 5.5 and a population pyramid with a very wide base. The government implements a program providing free education for girls through secondary school. Based on demographic evidence, what is the most likely long-term outcome?
Research consistently shows female education is the strongest predictor of fertility decline. Educated women have better access to family planning, delay marriage, participate in the workforce, and typically choose smaller families.
A country in Stage 2 of the demographic transition has a crude death rate that dropped from 30 to 10 per thousand in 20 years, while the crude birth rate remained at 40 per thousand. What is the annual natural increase rate, and what are its implications?
Natural increase = CBR - CDR = 40 - 10 = 30 per thousand = 3.0% per year. Using the Rule of 70: doubling time = 70/3 = ~23 years. This rapid growth strains food, water, education, and healthcare systems.

--- Lesson 3.4: Population Dynamics and Conservation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Population Dynamics and Conservation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a Population Viability Analysis (PVA)?
PVA uses demographic data, genetic information, and environmental variability to model future population trajectories and estimate extinction risk under different management scenarios.
What is the concept of minimum viable population (MVP)?
MVP is the smallest number of individuals needed for a population to have a specified probability (typically 95%) of persisting for a specified time (typically 100 years), accounting for all sources of variability.
What is the metapopulation concept?
A metapopulation is a network of subpopulations in separate habitat patches connected by dispersal. Local populations may go extinct, but empty patches can be recolonized from neighboring populations.
What is the difference between a source population and a sink population?
Source habitats have birth rates exceeding death rates, producing emigrating surplus. Sink habitats have death rates exceeding birth rates and would go extinct without constant immigration from source populations.
What is an edge effect in ecology?
Edge effects include changes in temperature, humidity, light, wind, and species composition at habitat boundaries. Small or elongated fragments have proportionally more edge, negatively affecting interior-dependent species.
What is population dynamics?
Population dynamics encompasses the study of changes in population size, density, age structure, and distribution over time, including the biological and environmental factors that cause these changes.
What is a 'metapopulation'?

--- Lesson 3.5: Population Regulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Population Regulation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the most common outcome when a population exceeds its carrying capacity?
When a population overshoots its carrying capacity, resources become scarce, leading to increased mortality and decreased reproduction, which causes the population to decline back toward carrying capacity.
What is a population crash?
A population crash is a sudden and steep decline in the number of individuals, often occurring after a population overshoots its carrying capacity and resources are depleted.
What is carrying capacity?
Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely given the available resources, space, and other environmental conditions.
What is a negative feedback loop in population regulation?
A negative feedback loop is a self-correcting mechanism in which a change in population size triggers responses that push the population back toward its carrying capacity, maintaining stability.
What is the meaning of 'population overshoot'?
Population overshoot occurs when a population temporarily grows beyond the carrying capacity of its environment, often leading to resource depletion and a subsequent population crash.
What is meant by 'environmental resistance'?
Environmental resistance encompasses all the biotic and abiotic factors that prevent a population from growing at its biotic potential, including predation, disease, limited resources, and unfavorable weather.
What is the definition of 'density-independent factor'?
A density-independent factor affects a population's size irrespective of how dense the population is; examples include natural disasters, extreme weather events, and seasonal changes.
A wildlife biologist notices that a deer population on an island has grown rapidly over several years after wolves were removed. The deer have now stripped most vegetation bare, and many deer are visibly thin. What is the most likely next phase for this population?
When a population overshoots its carrying capacity and depletes its food supply, a population crash typically follows due to starvation, weakened immune systems, and increased mortality, as seen in classic cases like the Kaibab deer.
A population of lemmings in the Arctic tundra experiences regular boom-and-bust cycles approximately every 3-4 years. During boom years, lemmings consume most available vegetation; during bust years, the population drops dramatically. Researchers find that both predator numbers and food availability play roles. What is the best explanation for this cycling pattern?
Lemming population cycles are driven by time-lagged density-dependent factors: as the population grows, it depletes food resources and attracts more predators, but because these effects are delayed, the population overshoots and then crashes in a regular cyclical pattern.
Real-World Applications
Example: Researchers studying a rabbit population observe that when population density is low, each female produces an average of 8 offspring per year. When density is high, females average only 3 offspring per year. Which population regulation mechanism is most likely responsible?
The reduction in offspring at high densities is a classic example of density-dependent regulation; as population density increases, greater competition for food and space leads to physiological stress and reduced reproductive output.
Example: A farmer notices that a field mouse population in a grain storage barn has exploded to very high numbers. Shortly after, many mice begin dying from a bacterial infection. A nearby barn with only a few mice shows no signs of disease. What does this scenario demonstrate about population regulation?
This scenario demonstrates that disease is a density-dependent regulating factor; in the crowded barn, close contact between many mice facilitated rapid disease transmission, while the sparse population in the other barn limited spread.
Example: A national park manager is concerned that the elk population has grown too large and is damaging grasslands. The manager proposes reintroducing mountain lions rather than allowing sport hunting. Which ecological principle supports this approach?
Reintroducing natural predators applies the principle of top-down regulation, where predators at higher trophic levels control herbivore populations, creating a sustainable and self-maintaining balance rather than relying on ongoing human management.

    Explanation: Biotic potential is the maximum rate at which a population can grow when resources are unlimited and environmental conditions are ideal, representing the highest possible reproductive output.

  Q17. What is meant by 'environmental resistance'?
    [ ] The resistance of a habitat to human development
    [ ] The ability of organisms to resist environmental change
    [*] The combination of factors that limit population growth below biotic potential
    [ ] The physical strength of an ecosystem
    Explanation: Environmental resistance encompasses all the biotic and abiotic factors that prevent a population from growing at its biotic potential, including predation, disease, limited resources, and unfavorable weather.

  Q18. What does the term 'trophic cascade' mean?
    [*] An indirect effect on lower trophic levels caused by changes at the top of a food web
    [ ] The decomposition of organic matter by bacteria
    [ ] The physical movement of organisms between habitats
    [ ] The flow of energy from the sun to producers
    Explanation: A trophic cascade occurs when changes in the population of a top predator ripple down through the food web, affecting populations at multiple lower trophic levels through indirect ecological interactions.

  Q19. What is the definition of 'density-independent factor'?
    [*] A factor that affects population size regardless of population density
    [ ] A factor that depends on the number of species present
    [ ] A factor that only operates in marine environments
    [ ] A factor that only affects large populations
    Explanation: A density-independent factor affects a population's size irrespective of how dense the population is; examples include natural disasters, extreme weather events, and seasonal changes.

  Q20. What does 'compensatory mortality' mean in population ecology?
    [ ] The process by which dead organisms are decomposed
    [ ] Mortality that always leads to population extinction
    [*] Deaths caused by one factor replace deaths that would have occurred from another factor
    [ ] A population compensates for low birth rates by living longer
    Explanation: Compensatory mortality occurs when deaths from one cause simply substitute for deaths that would have happened from another cause, meaning the overall mortality rate remains relatively unchanged.

  Q21. A wildlife biologist notices that a deer population on an island has grown rapidly over several years after wolves were removed. The deer have now stripped most vegetation bare, and many deer are visibly thin. What is the most likely next phase for this population?
    [ ] The vegetation will grow back faster to meet the deer's demand
    [*] The deer population will crash due to starvation and resource depletion
    [ ] The deer will evolve to eat different food sources within one generation
    [ ] The deer population will continue growing because there are no predators
    Explanation: When a population overshoots its carrying capacity and depletes its food supply, a population crash typically follows due to starvation, weakened immune systems, and increased mortality, as seen in classic cases like the Kaibab deer.

  Q22. A fish hatchery releases thousands of trout into a small lake each spring. Despite the large stocking numbers, the trout population by autumn is always about the same size. Which concept best explains this observation?
    [ ] The trout are all genetically identical
    [ ] Density-independent mortality from pollution
    [ ] The trout migrate to a different lake
    [*] The lake's carrying capacity limits the population through density-dependent factors
    Explanation: The lake has a finite carrying capacity determined by food, dissolved oxygen, and space; density-dependent factors like competition and limited resources reduce the stocked population back to sustainable levels each year.

  Q23. Researchers studying a rabbit population observe that when population density is low, each female produces an average of 8 offspring per year. When density is high, females average only 3 offspring per year. Which population regulation mechanism is most likely responsible?
    [*] Density-dependent regulation through intraspecific competition for resources
    [ ] Density-independent regulation caused by weather
    [ ] Random genetic drift affecting reproduction
    [ ] Density-independent regulation caused by predators
    Explanation: The reduction in offspring at high densities is a classic example of density-dependent regulation; as population density increases, greater competition for food and space leads to physiological stress and reduced reproductive output.

  Q24. After the reintroduction of wolves into Yellowstone National Park, elk populations declined, which allowed willow and aspen trees to recover along stream banks. This recovery of vegetation then improved habitat for beavers and songbirds. What ecological concept does this scenario illustrate?
    [ ] Primary ecological succession
    [ ] Density-independent population control
    [ ] Bottom-up regulation
    [*] A trophic cascade caused by top-down regulation
    Explanation: The Yellowstone wolf reintroduction is a classic example of a trophic cascade driven by top-down regulation: predators controlled herbivore populations, allowing vegetation to recover, which in turn benefited other species throughout the ecosystem.

  Q25. A farmer notices that a field mouse population in a grain storage barn has exploded to very high numbers. Shortly after, many mice begin dying from a bacterial infection. A nearby barn with only a few mice shows no signs of disease. What does this scenario demonstrate about population regulation?
    [ ] The mice in the second barn are immune to all diseases
    [*] Disease acts as a density-dependent factor because it spreads more in crowded conditions
    [ ] Disease acts as a density-independent factor
    [ ] Bacterial infections only occur in grain storage facilities
    Explanation: This scenario demonstrates that disease is a density-dependent regulating factor; in the crowded barn, close contact between many mice facilitated rapid disease transmission, while the sparse population in the other barn limited spread.

  Q26. On a remote island, a population of seabirds nests on cliffsides. Each pair defends a nesting territory and drives away intruders. When the population grows, some pairs cannot find nesting sites and fail to reproduce. What type of population regulation does this represent?
    [ ] Density-independent regulation through volcanic activity
    [ ] Density-independent regulation through weather events
    [ ] Bottom-up regulation through nutrient limitation
    [*] Density-dependent regulation through territoriality
    Explanation: Territoriality is a density-dependent mechanism; as the seabird population grows, limited nesting sites mean that some individuals are excluded from breeding, effectively capping the reproductive population and regulating overall numbers.

  Q27. A marine biologist observes that a coral reef fish population remains stable despite significant variation in predator numbers from year to year. However, the fish population plummets after a severe hurricane destroys most of the reef. Which statement best explains the relative importance of these two factors?
    [ ] The fish were not affected by the hurricane at all
    [*] Density-independent factors like hurricanes can have a larger immediate impact than density-dependent factors like predation
    [ ] Density-dependent factors never play a role in marine ecosystems
    [ ] Predation is always more important than abiotic factors in regulating fish populations
    Explanation: While density-dependent factors like predation help fine-tune population size over time, density-independent events like hurricanes can cause sudden, catastrophic population declines regardless of population density.

  Q28. Ecologists studying a lake ecosystem find that when phosphorus runoff from nearby farms increases, algae populations boom, which supports more zooplankton, which in turn feeds a larger fish population. What type of population regulation is driving this chain of effects?
    [ ] Density-independent regulation by seasonal temperature change
    [ ] Top-down regulation by apex predators
    [ ] Negative feedback regulation by the fish population
    [*] Bottom-up regulation driven by nutrient availability
    Explanation: This is bottom-up regulation because increased nutrient input at the base of the food web (phosphorus supporting algae) cascades upward through trophic levels, increasing populations of zooplankton and then fish.

  Q29. A national park manager is concerned that the elk population has grown too large and is damaging grasslands. The manager proposes reintroducing mountain lions rather than allowing sport hunting. Which ecological principle supports this approach?
    [ ] Elk populations will naturally decline without any intervention
    [ ] Density-independent control is more effective than density-dependent control
    [ ] Bottom-up regulation will solve the problem once grasses recover
    [*] Top-down regulation by natural predators can sustainably control herbivore populations
    Explanation: Reintroducing natural predators applies the principle of top-down regulation, where predators at higher trophic levels control herbivore populations, creating a sustainable and self-maintaining balance rather than relying on ongoing human management.

  Q30. A population of lemmings in the Arctic tundra experiences regular boom-and-bust cycles approximately every 3-4 years. During boom years, lemmings consume most available vegetation; during bust years, the population drops dramatically. Researchers find that both predator numbers and food availability play roles. What is the best explanation for this cycling pattern?
    [ ] Only density-independent factors like harsh winters cause the cycles
    [*] The cycles result from time-lagged interactions between density-dependent factors including food depletion and predation
    [ ] Lemmings deliberately control their own population through mass migration
    [ ] The population is always at carrying capacity and only appears to fluctuate
    Explanation: Lemming population cycles are driven by time-lagged density-dependent factors: as the population grows, it depletes food resources and attracts more predators, but because these effects are delayed, the population overshoots and then crashes in a regular cyclical pattern.

  1. Q: Define: Ecosystem
     A: A community of organisms and their physical environment interacting together in an area

  2. Q: Define: Biome
     A: A large geographic area characterized by distinctive climate, vegetation, and animal populations

  3. Q: Define: Population Growth
     A: The change in the size of a population over time due to births, deaths, immigration, and emigration

  4. Q: Define: Carrying Capacity
     A: The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely given its available resources

  5. Q: Define: Photosynthesis
     A: The process by which plants convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in glucose molecules

  6. Q: Define: Nutrient Cycling
     A: The movement of chemical elements like carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus between organisms and the environment

  7. Q: Define: Succession
     A: The sequential and predictable change in species composition of a community over time

  8. Q: Define: Conservation
     A: The protection and sustainable management of natural resources to ensure their availability for future generations

  9. Q: Which factor would most directly cause a population to increase?
     A: High birth rate and low death rate

  10. Q: Carrying capacity refers to:
     A: The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely
