--- Lesson 2.1: Population Ecology (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Population Ecology
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A population of deer in a forest has 500 individuals in a 50 km² area. What is the population density?
Population density = total individuals / area = 500 / 50 = 10 deer per km². This measure helps ecologists assess crowding and resource competition.
What is the biotic potential of a species?
Biotic potential is the maximum rate at which a population could grow under ideal conditions with unlimited resources, no predation, and no disease. Actual growth is always less due to environmental resistance.
What is population ecology?
Population ecology is the branch of ecology that studies the dynamics of populations, including their size, growth, density, distribution, and the factors that regulate these characteristics.
What is a 'survivorship curve'?
A survivorship curve plots the proportion of a cohort (group born at the same time) still alive at each age. The three main types (I, II, III) reflect different life history strategies.
What is meant by 'emigration'?
Emigration is the permanent movement of individuals out of a population, reducing population size. It differs from immigration, which is the movement of individuals into a population.
What is 'environmental resistance'?
Environmental resistance is the collective set of factors (predation, competition, disease, limited resources, climate) that prevent a population from growing at its biotic potential and keep it near carrying capacity.
What is a 'population bottleneck'?
A population bottleneck occurs when a population's size is drastically reduced by a catastrophic event, leaving a small surviving group with reduced genetic diversity, which can affect long-term viability.
Wildlife biologists use mark-recapture to study a frog population in a pond. They capture and mark 100 frogs, then release them. A week later, they capture 80 frogs, 20 of which are marked. What is the estimated total population?
Using the Lincoln-Petersen formula: N = (number marked x total recaptured) / number of marked recaptured = (100 x 80) / 20 = 400 frogs estimated in the population.
An oyster population releases millions of eggs into the ocean, but very few survive to adulthood. Which survivorship curve does this represent, and what is the ecological advantage of this reproductive strategy?
Oysters exhibit a Type III survivorship curve with very high mortality in early life stages. By producing millions of offspring, the species ensures that enough survive to maintain the population despite massive early losses.
Ecologists monitoring a small island find that the population of a native bird species is declining at 5% per year. Analysis shows that 15 birds die and 5 emigrate annually, while only 12 are born and 3 immigrate. What is the net population change per year?
Net change = (Births + Immigration) - (Deaths + Emigration) = (12 + 3) - (15 + 5) = 15 - 20 = -5. The population decreases by 5 individuals per year.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which survivorship curve describes species where most individuals survive to old age and die in a short period?
Type I survivorship curves show high survival rates throughout most of life, with most mortality occurring in old age. Humans and large mammals typically exhibit this pattern.
Example: Two neighboring towns report their deer population densities as 15 deer/km² and 45 deer/km², respectively. The second town experiences significant crop damage and deer-vehicle collisions. Using population ecology, what management approach might help?
When deer density exceeds the level at which coexistence with humans is manageable, population reduction through regulated hunting can decrease density-dependent conflicts like crop damage and vehicle collisions.
Example: An oyster population releases millions of eggs into the ocean, but very few survive to adulthood. Which survivorship curve does this represent, and what is the ecological advantage of this reproductive strategy?
Oysters exhibit a Type III survivorship curve with very high mortality in early life stages. By producing millions of offspring, the species ensures that enough survive to maintain the population despite massive early losses.

--- Lesson 2.2: Genetic Diversity and Population Genetics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Genetic Diversity and Population Genetics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is inbreeding depression?
Inbreeding depression occurs when related individuals mate, increasing homozygosity and the expression of harmful recessive alleles, leading to reduced survival, fertility, and overall fitness.
What is genetic drift?
Genetic drift is the random fluctuation of allele frequencies due to chance events in reproduction. Its effects are strongest in small populations, where it can lead to the loss of alleles and reduced genetic diversity.
What is the founder effect?
The founder effect occurs when a small number of individuals establish a new population, carrying only a fraction of the genetic diversity of the source population, which can lead to unusual allele frequencies.
What is a minimum viable population (MVP)?
The minimum viable population is the smallest number of individuals needed for a population to have a high probability of surviving over a specified time period, typically considering genetic diversity, demographic stochasticity, and environmental variation.
What is genetic diversity?
Genetic diversity refers to the total variety of genes, alleles, and genotypes within a population or species. It is one of three levels of biodiversity (genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity).
What is 'allele frequency'?
Allele frequency is the relative proportion of a particular allele at a specific gene locus in a population. Changes in allele frequencies over time indicate that evolution is occurring.
What is meant by 'population bottleneck'?
A population bottleneck is an event that drastically reduces population size, eliminating many alleles from the gene pool. The surviving population has much lower genetic diversity than the original.
What is 'natural selection'?
Natural selection is the evolutionary mechanism where individuals with traits better suited to their environment have higher survival and reproductive success, causing beneficial alleles to increase in frequency.
What is 'genetic bottleneck'?
A genetic bottleneck occurs when a population's size is drastically reduced, causing a random loss of alleles. The surviving population has reduced genetic variation regardless of which individuals happened to survive.
Climate change is causing warmer temperatures, and a tree species at its southern range limit is showing signs of heat stress. The northern edge of the range, however, shows healthy growth. A conservation geneticist proposes 'assisted gene flow' by planting seeds from heat-tolerant southern populations into central populations. What is the genetic rationale?
Assisted gene flow introduces pre-adapted genetic variation from warm-adapted southern populations, providing central populations with alleles that may help them survive as conditions warm, boosting their adaptive potential.
Real-World Applications
Example: A zoo breeding program for endangered California condors carefully tracks the pedigree of every individual to decide which birds should mate. Why is pedigree management so important in captive breeding?
Pedigree management ensures that the most genetically unrelated individuals are paired, maximizing heterozygosity and preventing inbreeding depression, which is crucial for the long-term viability of the captive population.
Important Concepts
What is 'allele frequency'
Allele frequency is the relative proportion of a particular allele at a specific gene locus in a population. Changes in allele frequencies over time indicate that evolution is occurring.
After a devastating hurricane, a small island population of lizards is reduced from 10,000 to just 50 survivors. Genetic analysis shows the survivors happen to have an unusually high frequency of a rare allele for darker coloration. What two population genetics concepts explain this situation
The hurricane caused a population bottleneck (drastic population reduction). The unusual allele frequency in survivors is due to genetic drift (random sampling effect), not selection for darker coloration.

--- Lesson 2.3: Speciation and Extinction (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Speciation and Extinction
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary mechanism that leads to speciation?
Speciation occurs when populations become reproductively isolated and evolve independently. Over time, genetic differences accumulate until the populations can no longer successfully interbreed.
What is the current estimated rate of species extinction compared to the natural background rate?
Current extinction rates are estimated at 100 to 1,000 times the natural background rate due to human activities, leading scientists to identify this as the sixth mass extinction.
What is the difference between allopatric and sympatric speciation?
Allopatric speciation occurs when populations are physically separated by a geographic barrier, while sympatric speciation occurs without geographic separation, often through ecological specialization or polyploidy.
What is the background extinction rate?
The background extinction rate is the steady rate at which species naturally go extinct through geological time, estimated from the fossil record at roughly 1-5 species per year (or 0.1-1 per million species per year).
What is an extinction vortex?
An extinction vortex describes the downward spiral where small populations face increased inbreeding, genetic drift, reduced reproductive success, and greater vulnerability to random events, each factor worsening the others.
What is speciation?
Speciation is the formation of new and distinct species through evolutionary processes, typically involving reproductive isolation followed by genetic divergence between separated populations.
What is meant by 'allopatric speciation'?
Allopatric speciation occurs when a geographic barrier (mountain range, river, ocean) physically separates populations, cutting off gene flow and allowing them to evolve independently until they become distinct species.
What is an 'endemic species'?
An endemic species is one that naturally occurs exclusively in a specific, often limited, geographic area. Endemic species are especially vulnerable to extinction because their entire range can be affected by a single event.
What is the 'HIPPO' acronym in conservation biology?
HIPPO summarizes the five major human-caused threats to biodiversity: Habitat loss, Invasive species, Pollution, Population growth (human), and Overexploitation, in roughly decreasing order of impact.
What is meant by 'functional extinction'?
Functional extinction occurs when a species' population becomes so small that it can no longer perform its ecological role (like pollination or seed dispersal) or maintain a viable breeding population, even though individuals still exist.

--- Lesson 2.4: Conservation of Biodiversity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Conservation of Biodiversity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between in situ and ex situ conservation?
In situ conservation protects species in their natural habitats (national parks, reserves), while ex situ conservation protects them in controlled environments (zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks).
What is the international agreement that regulates trade in endangered species?
CITES is an international agreement between governments that aims to ensure international trade in specimens of wild animals and plants does not threaten the survival of the species.
What is the purpose of a captive breeding program?
Captive breeding programs maintain insurance populations of critically endangered species, carefully managing genetics, with the ultimate goal of reintroducing individuals to restored wild habitats.
What is the role of seed banks in conservation?
Seed banks store seeds under controlled conditions to preserve plant genetic diversity. The Svalbard Global Seed Vault and national seed banks protect both crop varieties and wild plant species for future use.
What is the concept of an umbrella species in conservation?
An umbrella species requires large areas of habitat, so protecting it also protects the many other species that share that habitat. The northern spotted owl is a classic umbrella species for old-growth forest ecosystems.
What is the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)?
The CBD, adopted at the 1992 Earth Summit in Rio, is the key international treaty addressing biodiversity conservation, sustainable use, and equitable sharing of benefits from genetic resources.
What is biodiversity conservation?
Biodiversity conservation is the systematic effort to protect the variety of life at all levels (genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity) to maintain ecological health and the benefits humans derive from nature.
What is 'ex situ conservation'?
Ex situ (Latin for 'out of place') conservation maintains species outside their natural habitats, serving as insurance populations and providing opportunities for research, education, and eventual reintroduction.
What is a 'wildlife corridor'?
Wildlife corridors are linear habitats that connect fragmented populations, enabling animal movement for feeding, breeding, and migration, which maintains gene flow and reduces isolation effects.
What is 'ecotourism'?
Ecotourism is environmentally responsible travel to natural areas that promotes conservation, provides economic benefits to local communities, and educates visitors about the environment.
What is 'sustainable use' in conservation?
Sustainable use means harvesting natural resources at rates that allow natural replenishment, balancing human needs with ecological health to ensure resources remain available for future generations.
An island nation proposes creating a large marine protected area (MPA) that bans commercial fishing in 30% of its territorial waters. Fishing companies argue this will destroy their industry. Scientists present data showing that MPAs can increase fish populations in surrounding areas. What is this phenomenon called?
The spillover effect occurs when fish populations within marine protected areas grow and surplus individuals move into surrounding fishable waters, actually boosting catches in adjacent areas over time.
Real-World Applications
Example: A developing tropical nation has a large area of pristine rainforest that contains many endemic species. International conservation organizations want to create a national park, but local communities depend on the forest for food and income. What conservation approach could address both goals?
Community-based conservation balances biodiversity protection with human livelihoods by involving local people in decision-making, creating economic incentives through ecotourism or sustainable harvest, and respecting traditional land rights.

--- Lesson 2.5: Biodiversity Hotspots (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Biodiversity Hotspots
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the main threat to most biodiversity hotspots?
Habitat destruction driven by agricultural expansion, logging, and urban development is the primary threat to biodiversity hotspots. Most are in tropical regions experiencing rapid population growth and economic development.
What is the relationship between biodiversity hotspots and human population density?
Many hotspots are in developing tropical regions with dense, growing human populations that depend on natural resources. This creates tensions between conservation needs and human livelihood requirements.
What is a biodiversity hotspot?
A biodiversity hotspot is a region that meets two criteria: it contains at least 1,500 endemic vascular plant species and has lost at least 70% of its original habitat, making it a priority for conservation.
What is 'species richness'?
Species richness is simply the count of different species in a given area. It is one of the most basic and commonly used measures of biodiversity.
What is meant by 'irreplaceability' in conservation?
Irreplaceability measures how unique a site is for conservation. Areas with many endemic species have high irreplaceability because if those habitats are destroyed, the species they contain are lost forever.
What is 'conservation prioritization'?
Conservation prioritization uses criteria like species richness, endemism, threat level, and cost-effectiveness to allocate limited resources where they will have the greatest impact on biodiversity protection.
What is 'gap analysis' in conservation?
Gap analysis identifies 'gaps' in conservation coverage: species, ecosystems, or areas that are not adequately protected by existing reserves, helping planners decide where new protection is needed most.
The Mediterranean Basin hotspot spans 22 countries across Southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. Conservation in this hotspot requires international cooperation across countries with very different economies and political systems. What is the biggest challenge this presents?
Transboundary hotspots require international cooperation, which is complicated by differing environmental laws, economic development levels, political instability, and competing national priorities across multiple sovereign nations.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a biodiversity hotspot?
Madagascar is one of the most critical biodiversity hotspots, containing species like lemurs that are found nowhere else on Earth. Over 90% of its original habitat has been destroyed.
Example: What percentage of Earth's land surface do biodiversity hotspots cover in their remaining intact area?
The remaining intact vegetation of all 36 hotspots covers only about 2.5% of Earth's land surface, yet these areas contain more than 50% of the world's plant species and 43% of vertebrate species as endemics.

    [ ] Sea-level rise will create new habitat for species when the interaction between change and climate factors is taken into full account
    Explanation: Island hotspots face a double threat: existing habitat destruction plus climate change. Sea-level rise shrinks already-small habitats while warming alters ecological conditions, and endemic island species have nowhere to migrate.

  Q25. Critics of the biodiversity hotspot approach argue that it neglects important ecosystems like the Amazon rainforest and Congo basin, which are not technically hotspots because they haven't lost 70% of their habitat yet. How would you respond to this criticism?
    [ ] The Amazon and Congo have no conservation value, since the prevailing theory about Biodiversity would support this particular interpretation

    [ ] The hotspot approach is perfect and needs no supplementation, despite what intuition might initially suggest
    Explanation: The hotspot approach is one tool among several. Complementary frameworks like High Biodiversity Wilderness Areas and Key Biodiversity Areas address the limitations of focusing solely on hotspots.

  Q26. The Mediterranean Basin hotspot spans 22 countries across Southern Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. Conservation in this hotspot requires international cooperation across countries with very different economies and political systems. What is the biggest challenge this presents?

  Q27. An ecologist discovers a new species of frog in a cloud forest in Ecuador that is part of the Tropical Andes hotspot.     Explanation: New species are regularly discovered in hotspot remnants, indicating that current species counts underestimate true biodiversity. This means the conservation urgency is even greater than assessments based on known species suggest.

  Q28. Madagascar, which separated from Africa 160 million years ago, has over 90% endemism for many taxonomic groups. Over 90% of its original forest has been destroyed.     Explanation: Madagascar's extreme isolation means there are no 'backup' populations elsewhere. If a species goes extinct on Madagascar, it is extinct globally. Corridors are essential to maintain viable populations across fragmented habitat.

  Q29. Some scientists argue that focusing conservation resources only on hotspots creates a 'conservation bias' that ignores important but species-poor ecosystems like Arctic tundra and deep-sea environments. A student asks: if these areas have fewer species, why should we protect them?
    [*] Species-poor ecosystems provide critical global services (carbon storage, climate regulation) and contain unique species adapted to extreme conditions, warranting protection alongside hotspots

    [ ] Arctic ecosystems are not threatened because the underlying forces at work tend to produce exactly this type of outcome
    Explanation: Ecosystem services like carbon storage (tundra permafrost, deep-sea carbon burial) and climate regulation are provided by species-poor ecosystems. A comprehensive conservation strategy must protect both species-rich hotspots and functionally important ecosystems.

  Q30. A mining company proposes an open-pit gold mine in the heart of the Cerrado hotspot in Brazil, a tropical savanna with over 4,400 endemic plant species.     Explanation: Informed decisions require understanding the irreplaceability of species at risk, the value of ecosystem services (water purification, carbon storage, soil health), and whether alternative development approaches could minimize biodiversity loss.

--- Lesson 2.5: Biodiversity Hotspots (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Ecosystem
     A: A community of organisms and their physical environment interacting together in an area

  2. Q: Define: Biome
     A: A large geographic area characterized by distinctive climate, vegetation, and animal populations

  3. Q: Define: Population Growth
     A: The change in the size of a population over time due to births, deaths, immigration, and emigration

  4. Q: Define: Carrying Capacity
     A: The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely given its available resources

  5. Q: Define: Photosynthesis
     A: The process by which plants convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in glucose molecules

  6. Q: Define: Nutrient Cycling
     A: The movement of chemical elements like carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus between organisms and the environment

  7. Q: Define: Succession
     A: The sequential and predictable change in species composition of a community over time

  8. Q: Define: Conservation
     A: The protection and sustainable management of natural resources to ensure their availability for future generations

  9. Q: Which factor would most directly cause a population to increase?
     A: High birth rate and low death rate

  10. Q: Carrying capacity refers to:
     A: The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely
