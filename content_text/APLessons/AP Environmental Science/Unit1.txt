--- Lesson 1.1: Energy Flow Through Ecosystems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Energy Flow Through Ecosystems
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the relationship between Net Primary Productivity (NPP) and Gross Primary Productivity (GPP)?
NPP equals GPP minus the energy used by producers for their own respiration. NPP represents the energy actually available to consumers.
What is a trophic level?
A trophic level is the position an organism occupies in a food chain, such as producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, or tertiary consumer.
What is biomass?
Biomass refers to the total dry mass of living organic matter in a particular trophic level, community, or area, and is often used to quantify energy stored in living systems.
What is meant by 'net primary productivity (NPP)'?
NPP is the energy that remains after producers use some of their GPP for cellular respiration. It represents the energy actually available to be consumed by herbivores and other organisms.
What is an energy pyramid?
An energy pyramid is a diagram that shows the relative amount of energy available at each trophic level, always widest at the producer level and narrowing toward top predators.
What is meant by 'ecological efficiency'?
Ecological efficiency is the proportion of energy at one trophic level that is incorporated into biomass at the next trophic level, typically averaging around 10%.
A marine biologist measures that phytoplankton in a coastal bay fix 10,000 kcal/m²/year through photosynthesis and use 6,000 kcal/m²/year for their own respiration. What is the net primary productivity available to zooplankton and other consumers?
NPP = GPP - Respiration = 10,000 - 6,000 = 4,000 kcal/m²/year. This is the energy actually available for zooplankton and higher trophic levels to consume.
Real-World Applications
Example: Approximately what percentage of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next in a typical ecosystem?
Only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed to the next; the rest is lost primarily as heat through cellular respiration.
Example: A wildlife manager notices that after the removal of wolves from a national park, deer populations exploded and vegetation declined sharply. How does this scenario relate to energy flow concepts?
When top predators are removed, unchecked herbivore populations consume excessive producer biomass, reducing NPP and destabilizing energy flow through the entire food web.

    [*] 10%
    [ ] 50%
    [ ] 1%
    [ ] 90%
    Explanation: Only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is passed to the next; the rest is lost primarily as heat through cellular respiration.

  Q2. Which organisms form the base of nearly all food chains by converting solar energy into chemical energy?
    [ ] Primary consumers
    [*] Producers (autotrophs)
    [ ] Decomposers
    [ ] Secondary consumers
    Explanation: Producers such as plants and algae use photosynthesis to convert solar energy into chemical energy, forming the foundation of food chains.

  Q3. What happens to most of the energy consumed by organisms at each trophic level?
    [ ] It is stored as biomass
    [*] It is lost as heat through cellular respiration
    [ ] It is recycled back to producers
    [ ] It is converted to inorganic nutrients
    Explanation: At each trophic level, roughly 90% of the energy is lost as heat during cellular respiration, which is why energy flow is one-directional and decreases at higher levels.

  Q4. Which law of thermodynamics explains why energy is lost as heat at each trophic level?
    [ ] Third law of thermodynamics
    [ ] Law of conservation of mass
    [ ] First law of thermodynamics
    [*] Second law of thermodynamics
    Explanation: The second law of thermodynamics states that energy transformations increase entropy; during each transfer, some energy is degraded to heat, making it unavailable for work.

  Q5. In an energy pyramid, which trophic level contains the most energy?
    [ ] Secondary consumers
    [ ] Tertiary consumers
    [ ] Primary consumers
    [*] Producers
    Explanation: Producers at the base of the energy pyramid contain the most energy because energy is lost at each successive trophic level.

  Q6. Why are there typically fewer trophic levels in aquatic ecosystems compared to terrestrial ecosystems?
    [ ] Water conducts energy better than air
    [*] Energy losses at each level limit the number of levels an ecosystem can support
    [ ] Aquatic producers are more efficient
    [ ] Sunlight cannot penetrate deep water
    Explanation: Because only about 10% of energy transfers between levels, ecosystems have a limited number of trophic levels they can sustain; this constraint applies universally but is especially visible in aquatic systems with lower overall productivity.

  Q7. What is the relationship between Net Primary Productivity (NPP) and Gross Primary Productivity (GPP)?
    [ ] NPP and GPP are the same thing
    [ ] NPP = GPP + Respiration
    [*] NPP = GPP - Respiration
    [ ] NPP = GPP x Respiration
    Explanation: NPP equals GPP minus the energy used by producers for their own respiration. NPP represents the energy actually available to consumers.

  Q8. Decomposers play a critical role in energy flow because they:
    [ ] Increase the total energy in an ecosystem
    [ ] Create new energy from dead organic matter
    [*] Transfer energy from dead organisms back into the nutrient pool
    [ ] Convert heat energy back into chemical energy
    Explanation: Decomposers break down dead organic matter, releasing nutrients back into the ecosystem and utilizing the remaining chemical energy, though they also lose energy as heat.

  Q9. Which biome typically has the highest net primary productivity?
    [ ] Temperate grassland
    [ ] Tundra
    [ ] Desert
    [*] Tropical rainforest
    Explanation: Tropical rainforests have the highest NPP due to abundant sunlight, warmth, and rainfall year-round, which maximizes photosynthetic output.

  Q10. Energy flow in an ecosystem is described as one-directional because:
    [*] Energy enters as sunlight and exits as heat, never cycling back
    [ ] Energy flows from consumers back to producers
    [ ] Energy only moves through one food chain
    [ ] Energy can be recycled like nutrients
    Explanation: Unlike nutrients, which cycle through ecosystems, energy flows in one direction: it enters as solar radiation, passes through trophic levels, and is ultimately lost as heat.

  Q11. What is a trophic level?
    [ ] The amount of solar radiation an ecosystem receives
    [ ] The geographic range of a species
    [*] The feeding position of an organism in a food chain or food web
    [ ] A measure of biodiversity in an ecosystem
    Explanation: A trophic level is the position an organism occupies in a food chain, such as producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, or tertiary consumer.

  Q12. What does the term 'primary productivity' mean in ecology?
    [ ] The rate at which consumers convert food into biomass
    [ ] The total number of producers in an ecosystem
    [ ] The speed at which decomposition occurs
    [*] The rate at which producers capture and store energy through photosynthesis
    Explanation: Primary productivity is the rate at which producers (autotrophs) capture solar energy and convert it to chemical energy via photosynthesis, forming the energy base of the ecosystem.

  Q13. What is biomass?
    [*] The total dry weight of organic matter in organisms at a given trophic level or area
    [ ] The amount of water in living organisms
    [ ] The total energy consumed by all organisms
    [ ] The total number of organisms in an ecosystem
    Explanation: Biomass refers to the total dry mass of living organic matter in a particular trophic level, community, or area, and is often used to quantify energy stored in living systems.

  Q14. What does 'gross primary productivity (GPP)' refer to?
    [ ] The energy stored in fossil fuels over geological time
    [ ] The energy available to herbivores after producers respire
    [*] The total energy captured by producers through photosynthesis before any is used for respiration
    [ ] The net energy gain of an entire ecosystem
    Explanation: GPP is the total amount of energy captured by producers through photosynthesis, including the portion they will use for their own metabolic processes.

  Q15. What is meant by 'net primary productivity (NPP)'?
    [*] The energy available to consumers after producers have used some for respiration
    [ ] The gross energy minus the energy lost through decomposition
    [ ] The productivity of secondary consumers only
    [ ] The total energy captured by all organisms in an ecosystem
    Explanation: NPP is the energy that remains after producers use some of their GPP for cellular respiration. It represents the energy actually available to be consumed by herbivores and other organisms.

  Q16. What does the term 'food web' mean?
    [ ] A single linear pathway showing who eats whom
    [ ] The total amount of food available in a habitat
    [ ] A diagram showing nutrient cycling only
    [*] A complex network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem
    Explanation: A food web is a complex, interconnected network of multiple food chains that shows the many feeding relationships among organisms in an ecosystem.

  Q17. What is an energy pyramid?
    [ ] A model showing how energy is created by producers
    [ ] A diagram showing the cycling of nutrients through an ecosystem
    [ ] A chart comparing energy use across different biomes
    [*] A graphical representation of the amount of energy at each trophic level, with the most energy at the base
    Explanation: An energy pyramid is a diagram that shows the relative amount of energy available at each trophic level, always widest at the producer level and narrowing toward top predators.

  Q18. What does the term 'autotroph' mean?
    [*] An organism that produces its own food from inorganic substances using light or chemical energy
    [ ] An organism that lives in symbiosis with another species
    [ ] An organism that breaks down dead organic matter
    [ ] An organism that feeds on other organisms for energy
    Explanation: An autotroph is a self-feeding organism that produces its own organic molecules from inorganic raw materials, using energy from sunlight (photoautotroph) or chemical reactions (chemoautotroph).

  Q19. What is meant by 'ecological efficiency'?
    [*] The percentage of energy transferred from one trophic level to the next
    [ ] The total number of species in an ecosystem
    [ ] The speed at which an ecosystem recovers from disturbance
    [ ] The rate of nutrient recycling in a community
    Explanation: Ecological efficiency is the proportion of energy at one trophic level that is incorporated into biomass at the next trophic level, typically averaging around 10%.

    [ ] An organism that only lives in aquatic environments
    [ ] An organism that can produce its own food
    [*] An organism that obtains energy by consuming other organisms
    [ ] An organism that decomposes inorganic matter
    Explanation: A heterotroph is an organism that cannot make its own food and must obtain energy by consuming other organisms or organic matter, including herbivores, carnivores, and decomposers.

  Q21. A marine biologist measures that phytoplankton in a coastal bay fix 10,000 kcal/m²/year through photosynthesis and use 6,000 kcal/m²/year for their own respiration. What is the net primary productivity available to zooplankton and other consumers?

    [ ] 16,000 kcal/m²/year
    [*] 4,000 kcal/m²/year
    [ ] 10,000 kcal/m²/year

  Q22. A farmer is deciding whether to grow corn to feed cattle or to grow corn for direct human consumption. Based on the 10% rule of energy transfer, which approach would feed more people and why?
    [ ] Both approaches feed the same number of people
    [ ] Feeding cattle, because beef has more calories per gram
    [*] Growing corn for direct human consumption, because eating at a lower trophic level captures more of the original energy
    [ ] Feeding cattle, because animals concentrate nutrients more efficiently
    Explanation: Eating lower on the food chain is more energy-efficient. When humans eat corn directly, they access the full energy of that trophic level; routing it through cattle loses about 90% of the energy at each transfer.

  Q23. An ecologist studying a lake ecosystem finds that the producer level contains 20,000 kcal of energy. Using the 10% rule, how much energy would be available to secondary consumers (third trophic level)?
    [ ] 2,000 kcal
    [ ] 20 kcal
    [*] 200 kcal
    [ ] 1,000 kcal
    Explanation: Producers have 20,000 kcal. Primary consumers get 10% = 2,000 kcal. Secondary consumers get 10% of that = 200 kcal. Energy decreases by a factor of 10 at each trophic level.

  Q24. A coastal city is experiencing algal blooms due to agricultural runoff. Fishermen notice that while algae biomass has increased dramatically, the populations of large predatory fish have not increased proportionally. Which concept best explains this observation?
    [ ] Habitat fragmentation
    [*] The 10% rule of energy transfer between trophic levels
    [ ] Competitive exclusion principle
    [ ] Genetic drift in fish populations
    Explanation: Due to the 10% rule, a large increase in producer biomass translates to progressively smaller increases at higher trophic levels. Predatory fish are several levels removed from algae, so they receive only a tiny fraction of the additional energy.

  Q25. Researchers comparing two ecosystems find that a tropical rainforest has a GPP of 8,000 g C/m²/year and a tundra has a GPP of 200 g C/m²/year. If both lose about 50% to respiration, which ecosystem supports more consumer biomass and why?

    [ ] The tundra, because fewer species means less competition
    [ ] They support equal consumer biomass since both lose 50%
    [ ] The tundra, because cold temperatures preserve energy better
    Explanation: The rainforest NPP is 4,000 g C/m²/year versus only 100 g C/m²/year for tundra. Since consumer biomass depends on NPP, the rainforest can support vastly more consumer biomass.

  Q26. A wildlife manager notices that after the removal of wolves from a national park, deer populations exploded and vegetation declined sharply. How does this scenario relate to energy flow concepts?
    [ ] The removal of wolves increased GPP in the ecosystem
    [ ] Wolves were the main producers in the ecosystem
    [*] Without top predators, primary consumers overgraze producers, disrupting the energy base of the ecosystem
    [ ] Deer populations create more energy for the ecosystem
    Explanation: When top predators are removed, unchecked herbivore populations consume excessive producer biomass, reducing NPP and destabilizing energy flow through the entire food web.

  Q27. A fisheries scientist recommends harvesting anchovies (primary consumers) rather than tuna (tertiary consumers) to maximize the food yield from the ocean. Which principle supports this recommendation?
    [ ] The principle of competitive exclusion
    [*] The ecological efficiency of energy transfer between trophic levels
    [ ] The law of conservation of mass
    [ ] The theory of island biogeography
    Explanation: Harvesting at lower trophic levels captures more of the available energy. By the time energy reaches tuna (tertiary consumers), about 99.9% of the original producer energy has been lost as heat.

  Q28. After a volcanic eruption covers a forest in ash, scientists observe that the area's productivity drops to nearly zero. Over the following decades, lichens colonize bare rock, followed by mosses and eventually trees. How does energy flow change during this process?
    [ ] Energy flow remains constant throughout succession
    [ ] Energy flow decreases as more species compete for resources
    [ ] Energy flow only increases once tertiary consumers arrive
    [*] GPP and NPP gradually increase as more producers establish and grow, supporting increasingly complex food webs
    Explanation: During ecological succession, as producer biomass increases, GPP and NPP rise. This growing energy base supports progressively more trophic levels and a more complex food web over time.

  Q29. A city plans to convert vacant lots into urban gardens. An environmental consultant explains that growing vegetables directly for residents is more energy-efficient than using the same land to grow feed for livestock. What ecological principle underlies this advice?
    [ ] Biomagnification of toxins
    [ ] The greenhouse effect
    [ ] Nitrogen fixation by soil bacteria
    [*] The second law of thermodynamics and the 10% rule of trophic energy transfer
    Explanation: Eating plants directly (one trophic transfer) retains far more energy than routing through livestock (two transfers). The 10% rule means only about 10% of plant energy becomes animal biomass, so direct consumption is more efficient.

  Q30. Scientists studying deep-sea hydrothermal vent communities discover thriving ecosystems with no sunlight. These communities rely on chemosynthetic bacteria as producers. How does this challenge and support the concept of energy flow through ecosystems?
    [ ] It disproves the 10% rule entirely
    [*] It supports the concept because energy still flows one-directionally from producers to consumers, even though the energy source is chemical rather than solar
    [ ] It shows that consumers can produce their own energy
    [ ] It challenges the concept because energy does not flow through these communities
    Explanation: Hydrothermal vent ecosystems demonstrate that while the energy source may differ (chemical vs. solar), the fundamental principle of one-directional energy flow from producers through consumers still applies, with energy lost as heat at each level.

--- Lesson 1.2: Nutrient Cycles (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Nutrient Cycles
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
In the phosphorus cycle, what is notably absent compared to the carbon and nitrogen cycles?
Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus does not have a significant gaseous phase. It cycles primarily through rocks, soil, water, and organisms, making it a sedimentary cycle.
What is the primary natural source of phosphorus entering ecosystems?
The natural source of phosphorus is the weathering and erosion of rocks containing phosphate minerals, which slowly releases phosphorus into soils and waterways.
What is a biogeochemical cycle?
A biogeochemical cycle is the movement of a nutrient or element through living organisms (bio-), the earth (geo-), and chemical processes, cycling between biotic and abiotic reservoirs.
What is nitrification?
Nitrification is a two-step aerobic process in which nitrifying bacteria first convert ammonia to nitrite and then nitrite to nitrate, the form most readily absorbed by plants.
What is meant by a 'nutrient sink'?
A nutrient sink is a reservoir that absorbs and stores more of a substance than it releases. For example, growing forests act as carbon sinks by absorbing more CO2 through photosynthesis than they release.
What is a 'limiting nutrient'?
A limiting nutrient is the essential element whose scarcity most constrains the growth of organisms. In freshwater systems, phosphorus is often limiting, while in marine systems, nitrogen is typically limiting.
What is 'eutrophication'?
Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus) enter water bodies, stimulating excessive algae growth that depletes dissolved oxygen when it decomposes, creating hypoxic or dead zones.
Real-World Applications
Example: Researchers studying coral reefs near a developing coastline find that increased sewage discharge is elevating nitrogen and phosphorus levels. Which sequence of events would they most likely predict?
Excess nutrients from sewage promote algal growth that outcompetes and smothers corals. The algae block sunlight needed by the symbiotic zooxanthellae in coral tissues, leading to coral decline.
Example: A wetland restoration project aims to reduce nitrogen pollution in a watershed. Engineers design shallow, low-oxygen basins connected to drainage channels. Which step of the nitrogen cycle are they trying to enhance?
Low-oxygen (anaerobic) conditions promote denitrification, where bacteria convert nitrate to nitrogen gas, effectively removing excess reactive nitrogen from the water and returning it harmlessly to the atmosphere.
Example: An organic farmer plants clover (a legume) as a cover crop between corn-growing seasons. How does this practice leverage the nitrogen cycle to benefit soil fertility?
Legumes like clover have a symbiotic relationship with Rhizobium bacteria that fix atmospheric N2 into ammonium in root nodules. When the clover is tilled into the soil, this nitrogen becomes available for the next crop.

--- Lesson 1.2: Nutrient Cycles (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which of the following is a major reservoir of carbon in the carbon cycle?
    [*] Oceans, atmosphere, fossil fuels, and biomass
    [ ] The atmosphere only
    [ ] Only living organisms
    [ ] Soil and freshwater only
    Explanation: Carbon is stored in multiple major reservoirs including oceans (the largest), the atmosphere as CO2, underground as fossil fuels, and in living and dead biomass.

  Q2. What process converts atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into forms usable by plants?
    [ ] Denitrification
    [ ] Photosynthesis
    [*] Nitrogen fixation
    [ ] Transpiration
    Explanation: Nitrogen fixation, carried out by certain bacteria (including Rhizobium in legume root nodules) and cyanobacteria, converts atmospheric N2 into ammonia (NH3) or ammonium (NH4+), which plants can absorb.

  Q3. In the phosphorus cycle, what is notably absent compared to the carbon and nitrogen cycles?
    [ ] A soil component
    [ ] An aquatic component
    [*] A significant atmospheric (gaseous) phase
    [ ] A biological component
    Explanation: Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus does not have a significant gaseous phase. It cycles primarily through rocks, soil, water, and organisms, making it a sedimentary cycle.

  Q4. Which process in the water cycle moves water from plants into the atmosphere?
    [ ] Condensation
    [ ] Runoff
    [ ] Percolation
    [*] Transpiration
    Explanation: Transpiration is the process by which water absorbed by plant roots is released as water vapor through stomata in the leaves, returning water to the atmosphere.

  Q5. What role do decomposers play in nutrient cycling?
    [ ] They fix atmospheric nitrogen into soil
    [ ] They convert phosphorus into a gaseous form
    [*] They break down dead organic matter and release nutrients back into the soil and atmosphere
    [ ] They create new elements from organic matter
    Explanation: Decomposers break down dead organisms and waste products, releasing nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon back into the environment where they can be reused by producers.

  Q6. How does the burning of fossil fuels affect the carbon cycle?
    [ ] It converts atmospheric carbon into rock
    [*] It releases stored carbon into the atmosphere as CO2, increasing atmospheric concentrations
    [ ] It removes carbon from the atmosphere
    [ ] It has no effect on the carbon cycle
    Explanation: Burning fossil fuels releases carbon that was stored underground for millions of years back into the atmosphere as CO2, disrupting the natural balance of the carbon cycle and contributing to climate change.

  Q7. Which process returns nitrogen from the soil back to the atmosphere as N2 gas?
    [ ] Nitrification
    [*] Denitrification
    [ ] Assimilation
    [ ] Ammonification
    Explanation: Denitrification is performed by anaerobic bacteria that convert nitrate (NO3-) back into nitrogen gas (N2), which is released into the atmosphere, completing the nitrogen cycle.

  Q8. What is the primary natural source of phosphorus entering ecosystems?
    [ ] Volcanic eruptions
    [ ] Atmospheric deposition
    [ ] Lightning strikes
    [*] Weathering of phosphate-containing rocks
    Explanation: The natural source of phosphorus is the weathering and erosion of rocks containing phosphate minerals, which slowly releases phosphorus into soils and waterways.

  Q9. Unlike energy, nutrients in ecosystems are:
    [ ] Continuously created by living organisms
    [ ] Only available to producers
    [*] Recycled and reused within the ecosystem
    [ ] Lost as heat at each trophic level
    Explanation: Nutrients cycle through ecosystems repeatedly, being absorbed by organisms, passed through food chains, released by decomposers, and then reused. This contrasts with energy, which flows one-directionally.

  Q10. Which human activity has most significantly disrupted the nitrogen cycle?
    [ ] Overfishing, particularly in the context of nitrogen
    [ ] Deforestation under most circumstances involving nitrogen
    [*] Use of synthetic nitrogen fertilizers via the Haber-Bosch process
    [ ] Dam construction under most circumstances involving
    Explanation: The Haber-Bosch process, which synthesizes ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen for fertilizer, has more than doubled the amount of reactive nitrogen entering ecosystems, causing widespread eutrophication and dead zones.

  Q11. What is a biogeochemical cycle?
    [*] The pathway by which a chemical element moves through the biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem
    [ ] The migration patterns of organisms between biomes
    [ ] The one-way flow of energy through an ecosystem
    [ ] The process of speciation in isolated populations
    Explanation: A biogeochemical cycle is the movement of a nutrient or element through living organisms (bio-), the earth (geo-), and chemical processes, cycling between biotic and abiotic reservoirs.

  Q12. What does the term 'nitrogen fixation' mean?
    [ ] The release of nitrogen gas from soil into the atmosphere
    [ ] The absorption of nitrate by plant roots
    [*] The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen gas (N2) into biologically usable forms like ammonia
    [ ] The breakdown of proteins into amino acids
    Explanation: Nitrogen fixation is the process by which certain bacteria and cyanobacteria convert inert atmospheric N2 gas into reactive forms (NH3/NH4+) that organisms can incorporate into biological molecules.

  Q13. What is nitrification?
    [*] The bacterial conversion of ammonia (NH3) to nitrite (NO2-) and then to nitrate (NO3-)
    [ ] The uptake of nitrogen by plant roots
    [ ] The conversion of nitrogen gas into ammonia
    [ ] The return of nitrogen to the atmosphere
    Explanation: Nitrification is a two-step aerobic process in which nitrifying bacteria first convert ammonia to nitrite and then nitrite to nitrate, the form most readily absorbed by plants.

  Q14. What does 'carbon sequestration' mean?
    [ ] The process of photosynthesis in aquatic algae
    [ ] The cycling of carbon through food webs
    [ ] The release of carbon from fossil fuels during combustion
    [*] The long-term storage of carbon in plants, soils, geologic formations, or the ocean to reduce atmospheric CO2
    Explanation: Carbon sequestration is the capture and long-term storage of atmospheric CO2, either through natural processes (forests, oceans, soils) or technological methods, to mitigate climate change.

  Q15. What is meant by a 'nutrient sink'?
    [ ] A place where nutrients are destroyed
    [ ] An organism that does not require nutrients
    [*] A reservoir or process that absorbs and stores more of a nutrient than it releases
    [ ] A location that releases more of a nutrient than it absorbs
    Explanation: A nutrient sink is a reservoir that absorbs and stores more of a substance than it releases. For example, growing forests act as carbon sinks by absorbing more CO2 through photosynthesis than they release.

  Q16. What does 'ammonification' refer to?
    [ ] The absorption of ammonia by aquatic organisms
    [ ] The oxidation of ammonia to nitrate
    [*] The decomposition of organic nitrogen compounds into ammonia (NH3) or ammonium (NH4+)
    [ ] The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen to ammonia by bacteria
    Explanation: Ammonification (also called mineralization) is the process by which decomposers convert organic nitrogen from dead organisms and waste into inorganic ammonia or ammonium, making it available for nitrification or direct plant uptake.

  Q17. What is a 'limiting nutrient'?
    [ ] A nutrient that is toxic in any amount
    [ ] A nutrient that cannot be recycled
    [ ] The most abundant nutrient in an ecosystem
    [*] A nutrient that is in shortest supply relative to demand, thereby restricting growth and productivity
    Explanation: A limiting nutrient is the essential element whose scarcity most constrains the growth of organisms. In freshwater systems, phosphorus is often limiting, while in marine systems, nitrogen is typically limiting.

  Q18. What does 'denitrification' mean?
    [ ] The conversion of ammonia to nitrate in soil
    [ ] The process of converting nitrogen gas to ammonia
    [*] The bacterial conversion of nitrate (NO3-) back to nitrogen gas (N2) released to the atmosphere
    [ ] The uptake of nitrogen by plant roots
    Explanation: Denitrification is the microbial process that reduces nitrate to nitrogen gas under anaerobic conditions, returning biologically available nitrogen to the atmosphere and completing the nitrogen cycle.

  Q19. What is 'eutrophication'?
    [ ] The process by which rivers deposit sediment at their mouths
    [ ] The natural filtration of pollutants by wetlands
    [ ] The depletion of all nutrients from a water body
    [*] The excessive enrichment of a water body with nutrients, leading to algal blooms and oxygen depletion
    Explanation: Eutrophication occurs when excess nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus) enter water bodies, stimulating excessive algae growth that depletes dissolved oxygen when it decomposes, creating hypoxic or dead zones.

  Q20. What does 'assimilation' mean in the nitrogen cycle?
    [*] The process by which organisms absorb and incorporate inorganic nitrogen into organic molecules
    [ ] The conversion of ammonia to nitrate by bacteria
    [ ] The release of nitrogen from organisms during decomposition
    [ ] The evaporation of nitrogen compounds from soil
    Explanation: Assimilation is the process by which organisms take up inorganic nitrogen (as ammonium or nitrate) and incorporate it into organic compounds like amino acids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

  Q21. A farmer applies large quantities of nitrogen fertilizer to cornfields adjacent to a river. After heavy rains, residents downstream notice green, murky water and fish kills. Which disruption to the nitrogen cycle best explains this chain of events?

    Explanation: Excess nitrogen from fertilizer runoff stimulates algal blooms (eutrophication). When the algae die and decompose, bacteria consume dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic conditions that kill fish and other aquatic life.

--- Lesson 1.3: Succession (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Succession
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the correct order of ecological succession in a terrestrial ecosystem?
Ecological succession proceeds from bare substrate to pioneer species (like lichens and mosses), then intermediate species (grasses and shrubs), and finally a stable climax community (mature forest).
What is the key difference between primary and secondary succession?
Primary succession starts on newly formed or exposed surfaces with no soil (like lava flows or retreating glaciers), while secondary succession occurs where disturbance has removed vegetation but soil remains.
What is ecological succession?
Ecological succession is the process by which the species composition and community structure of an ecosystem change over time, typically following a somewhat predictable sequence.
What is meant by 'primary succession'?
Primary succession is ecological succession that begins in an area with no pre-existing soil or life, such as after a volcanic eruption or glacial retreat, and takes a very long time because soil must form first.
What is a 'climax community'?
A climax community is the final, stable stage of ecological succession where the community is in equilibrium with environmental conditions and species composition changes little over time.
What is meant by 'inhibition' in ecological succession?
Inhibition occurs when established species prevent or slow the establishment of later-arriving species through competition for resources, allelopathy, or physical space occupation.
What is a 'seral stage'?
A seral stage (or sere) is any of the intermediate communities that develop during ecological succession between the initial colonization and the climax community.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of primary succession?
A newly formed volcanic island has no soil or pre-existing life, so ecological development there represents primary succession, starting from bare rock with pioneer organisms.
Example: What is a 'seral stage'?
A seral stage (or sere) is any of the intermediate communities that develop during ecological succession between the initial colonization and the climax community.
Example: A national forest experiences a severe wildfire that burns most of the trees but leaves the soil and root systems intact. Within two years, grasses and wildflowers cover the ground. Five years later, shrubs and young trees begin to appear. What stage of succession is the forest in after five years?
Since soil remained after the fire, this is secondary succession. After five years with shrubs and young trees establishing, the ecosystem is in an intermediate seral stage, progressing toward a mature forest community.

--- Lesson 1.4: Ecosystem Services and Biomes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ecosystem Services and Biomes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a biome?
A biome is a major ecological community type defined by its characteristic climate and dominant vegetation, such as tropical rainforest, desert, tundra, or temperate grassland.
What is meant by 'biodiversity hotspot'?
A biodiversity hotspot is defined as a region that hosts at least 1,500 endemic vascular plant species (more than 0.5% of the world's total) and has lost at least 70% of its original habitat.
What is the definition of 'biotic factors' in an ecosystem?
Biotic factors are all the living components of an ecosystem, including plants, animals, fungi, and microorganisms, as well as their interactions such as predation, competition, and symbiosis.
What is a 'keystone species'?
A keystone species has a disproportionately large effect on its environment relative to its population size. Removing a keystone species causes significant changes to the ecosystem, like sea otters controlling sea urchin populations to protect kelp forests.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a provisioning ecosystem service?
Provisioning services are tangible products obtained from ecosystems, such as food, fresh water, timber, and fiber. Clean drinking water is a direct material benefit provided by watersheds.
Example: Which is an example of a cultural ecosystem service?
Cultural services are non-material benefits people obtain from ecosystems, including recreational, aesthetic, spiritual, and educational values. National parks and sacred natural sites provide cultural ecosystem services.
Example: A coastal city is debating whether to preserve its remaining mangrove forests or develop them for waterfront condominiums. An environmental economist estimates the mangroves provide $15,000 per hectare annually in storm surge protection, fishery nursery habitat, and carbon storage. Which approach to decision-making does this represent?
Placing economic value on ecosystem services allows decision-makers to compare the ongoing benefits of natural systems against the one-time revenue from development, often revealing that intact ecosystems provide greater long-term value.
Example: A coffee farmer in Colombia notices that after clearing nearby forest for more cropland, pollination of coffee plants has declined and pest damage has increased. Which ecosystem services were lost due to deforestation?
The nearby forest provided habitat for wild pollinators and natural pest predators. Clearing the forest eliminated these regulating services, resulting in reduced pollination and increased pest damage to crops.
Example: A researcher studying the Sahel region of Africa documents that overgrazing and drought have caused grasslands to become barren, sandy landscapes. This biome shift from savanna to desert is an example of:
Desertification is the degradation of dryland ecosystems due to human activities like overgrazing and poor land management, often compounded by drought. The Sahel is one of the most well-documented cases of this biome transition.
Example: A government considers draining a large wetland to create farmland. Environmental scientists argue that the wetland provides flood protection, water purification, and wildlife habitat worth more than the agricultural output. The government proceeds with drainage. The following year, devastating floods damage nearby towns. What does this scenario illustrate about ecosystem services?
This illustrates the hidden economic value of regulating services. Wetlands absorb and store floodwaters; destroying them eliminates this free natural infrastructure, often resulting in costly damages that far exceed development profits.

--- Lesson 1.5: Succession and Restoration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Succession and Restoration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the primary goal of ecological restoration?
Ecological restoration aims to assist the recovery of a degraded, damaged, or destroyed ecosystem by returning it to its historical trajectory, including native species composition, structure, and function.
What is the role of prescribed (controlled) burns in ecological restoration?
Prescribed burns replicate natural fire regimes that many ecosystems depend on. Fire releases nutrients, opens canopy gaps, stimulates seed germination in fire-adapted species, and controls woody invasives.
What is the difference between restoration and remediation?
Remediation addresses contamination (removing pollutants from soil or water), while restoration is broader, aiming to re-establish the entire ecosystem's species composition, structure, and function.
What is a reference ecosystem in the context of restoration?
A reference ecosystem is an existing, intact ecosystem of the same type that provides a benchmark for what the restored ecosystem should look like in terms of species composition, structure, and function.
What is ecological restoration?
Ecological restoration is an active, intentional process of helping a degraded ecosystem recover its composition, structure, and function, guided by the principles of ecology and successional theory.
What is 'phytoremediation'?
Phytoremediation uses plants to remove, transfer, stabilize, or destroy contaminants in soil and water. Sunflowers, for example, can absorb heavy metals like lead, and willows can take up excess nitrogen.
What is meant by 'ecosystem resilience'?
Ecosystem resilience is the ability of an ecosystem to recover from disturbance and return to a functional state. Higher biodiversity generally increases resilience by providing redundancy in ecological roles.
What is a 'seed bank' in the context of restoration?
A seed bank refers to both the natural reserve of dormant seeds in the soil and to facilities (like the Svalbard Global Seed Vault) that store seeds for use in restoration and conservation.
What is 'rewilding'?
Rewilding is a progressive approach to conservation that aims to restore natural processes and keystone species (like wolves or beavers) to allow ecosystems to manage themselves with minimal human intervention.
A developer wants to build a shopping center on a wetland. Regulations require no net loss of wetland function, so the developer proposes creating a new wetland twice the size on a nearby upland site. An ecologist argues this mitigation approach has limitations. What is the primary concern?
Studies show created wetlands often fail to achieve the biodiversity and ecological functions of natural wetlands. Complex soil chemistry, hydrology, and biological communities that took centuries to develop cannot be easily replicated.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the primary goal of ecological restoration?
Ecological restoration aims to assist the recovery of a degraded, damaged, or destroyed ecosystem by returning it to its historical trajectory, including native species composition, structure, and function.
Example: What does 'adaptive management' mean in restoration ecology?
Adaptive management treats restoration as an ongoing experiment: practitioners monitor results, learn from successes and failures, and adjust their strategies accordingly to improve outcomes over time.
Example: A restoration team discovers that a prairie restoration project is failing because invasive species keep returning after removal. They decide to implement an adaptive management approach. What does this involve?
Adaptive management involves systematically learning from results and modifying strategies. The team might try different removal timing, add prescribed burns, or overseed with competitive natives to suppress reinvasion.
Example: A city on the Great Lakes is converting a former industrial waterfront into a public green space. Soil tests reveal petroleum and heavy metal contamination. The restoration plan includes soil remediation, native plantings, and creating wetland areas for stormwater management. Which combination of restoration approaches is being used?
This project combines remediation (cleaning up contamination) with ecological restoration (establishing native plant communities and restoring ecosystem services like stormwater management through constructed wetlands).
Example: Climate change is making some restoration targets unrealistic because the historical climate that supported the original ecosystem no longer exists. For example, a prairie restoration in Kansas may face hotter, drier conditions than the original prairie experienced. How should restoration practitioners address this challenge?
Forward-looking or climate-adapted restoration acknowledges that historical baselines may be unreachable. Practitioners may include drought-tolerant genotypes or species from warmer regions to build resilience to projected future conditions.

    Explanation: When invasive species have displaced natives so thoroughly that natural recovery is unlikely, active restoration (invasive removal plus native replanting) is necessary to overcome the invasion barrier and re-establish the native community.

  Q29. A city on the Great Lakes is converting a former industrial waterfront into a public green space. Soil tests reveal petroleum and heavy metal contamination. The restoration plan includes soil remediation, native plantings, and creating wetland areas for stormwater management. Which combination of restoration approaches is being used?
    [ ] Rewilding with large predator reintroduction, which is a common assumption in the field
    [*] Remediation to address contamination combined with ecological restoration to re-establish native communities and ecosystem services
    [ ] Only bioremediation, which represents a common but incomplete view of how these restoration dynamics operate
    [ ] Only reclamation with no ecological component without accounting for additional influences
    Explanation: This project combines remediation (cleaning up contamination) with ecological restoration (establishing native plant communities and restoring ecosystem services like stormwater management through constructed wetlands).

  Q30. Climate change is making some restoration targets unrealistic because the historical climate that supported the original ecosystem no longer exists. For example, a prairie restoration in Kansas may face hotter, drier conditions than the original prairie experienced. How should restoration practitioners address this challenge?
    [ ] Restore to exact historical conditions regardless of current climate, based on conventional assumptions about restoration
    [ ] Abandon all restoration projects because they are futile, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] Only use species from colder climates, as most introductory frameworks would predict when analyzing restoration dynamics
    [*] Use forward-looking restoration that considers future climate projections, selecting species and creating conditions suited to anticipated rather than historical conditions
    Explanation: Forward-looking or climate-adapted restoration acknowledges that historical baselines may be unreachable. Practitioners may include drought-tolerant genotypes or species from warmer regions to build resilience to projected future conditions.

--- Lesson 1.5: Succession and Restoration (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Ecosystem
     A: A community of organisms and their physical environment interacting together in an area

  2. Q: Define: Biome
     A: A large geographic area characterized by distinctive climate, vegetation, and animal populations

  3. Q: Define: Population Growth
     A: The change in the size of a population over time due to births, deaths, immigration, and emigration

  4. Q: Define: Carrying Capacity
     A: The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely given its available resources

  5. Q: Define: Photosynthesis
     A: The process by which plants convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in glucose molecules

  6. Q: Define: Nutrient Cycling
     A: The movement of chemical elements like carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus between organisms and the environment

  7. Q: Define: Succession
     A: The sequential and predictable change in species composition of a community over time

  8. Q: Define: Conservation
     A: The protection and sustainable management of natural resources to ensure their availability for future generations

  9. Q: Which factor would most directly cause a population to increase?
     A: High birth rate and low death rate

  10. Q: Carrying capacity refers to:
     A: The maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely
