--- Lesson 3.1: Sampling Methods (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sampling Methods
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of 'Simple random sample (SRS)'?
"Simple random sample (SRS)" means: A sample selected so that every possible sample of size n from the population has an equal probability of being chosen. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Stratified random sample'?
"Stratified random sample" means: Dividing the population into homogeneous subgroups (strata) and taking an SRS from each stratum to ensure representation. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Cluster sample'?
"Cluster sample" means: Randomly selecting entire groups (clusters) and surveying all individuals within chosen clusters; practical when a population list is unavailable. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Systematic sample'?
"Systematic sample" means: Selecting every kth individual from a list after a random starting point between 1 and k. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Sampling frame'?
"Sampling frame" means: The list of all individuals in the population from which the sample is actually drawn; ideally matches the target population. The other options describe different concepts.

Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of 'Experiment'?
"Experiment" means: A study in which the researcher deliberately imposes one or more treatments on subjects to observe the effect on a response variable. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Random assignment'?
"Random assignment" means: Allocating experimental units to treatment groups using a chance mechanism to balance confounding variables across groups. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Control group'?
"Control group" means: A group that receives no treatment or a standard treatment, providing a baseline for comparison with the treatment group. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Placebo'?
"Placebo" means: An inactive treatment (e.g., sugar pill) given to the control group to eliminate the psychological effect of receiving treatment. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Double-blind experiment'?
"Double-blind experiment" means: Neither the subjects nor the researchers measuring outcomes know which treatment each subject received, minimizing both placebo effect and experimenter bias. The other options describe different concepts.

What is the definition of 'Observational study'?
"Observational study" means: A study in which the researcher observes and records data without imposing a treatment or intervention on the subjects. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Confounding variable'?
"Confounding variable" means: A variable associated with both the explanatory variable and the response variable that distorts the apparent relationship between them. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Lurking variable'?
"Lurking variable" means: A variable not included in the study that may influence the relationship between the variables being studied. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Causation vs. association'?
"Causation vs. association" means: Association means two variables are related; causation requires that changing one variable directly produces a change in the other. The other options describe different concepts.
What is the definition of 'Retrospective vs. prospective study'?
"Retrospective vs. prospective study" means: Retrospective studies look backward at existing data; prospective studies follow subjects forward in time from the start. The other options describe different concepts.
