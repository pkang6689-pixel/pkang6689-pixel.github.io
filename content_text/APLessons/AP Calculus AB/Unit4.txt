--- Lesson 4.1: Related Rates (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Related Rates
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If PV = nRT (ideal gas law) with n and R constant, T = 300K, P = 100 kPa, and dP/dt = 5 kPa/s, what is dV/dt when V = 2 m^3?
PV = C (constant T). P*(dV/dt) + V*(dP/dt) = 0. 100*(dV/dt) + 2(5) = 0. dV/dt = -10/100 = -0.1 m^3/s.
What is a 'constraint equation' in related rates?
A constraint equation is the geometric or physical relationship connecting the variables (like the Pythagorean theorem for a ladder problem or volume formulas). It is differentiated with respect to time.

--- Lesson 4.1: Related Rates (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A square's side length increases at 2 cm/s. How fast is the area increasing when the side is 10 cm?
    [*] 40 cm^2/s
    [ ] 20 cm^2/s
    [ ] 4 cm^2/s
    [ ] 100 cm^2/s
    Explanation: A = s^2. dA/dt = 2s(ds/dt) = 2(10)(2) = 40 cm^2/s.

  Q2. A balloon's volume increases at 20 cm^3/s. How fast is the radius increasing when r = 10 cm?
    [*] 1/(20pi) cm/s
    [ ] 20/(400pi) cm/s
    [ ] 1/(10pi) cm/s
    [ ] 20pi cm/s
    Explanation: V = (4/3)pi*r^3. dV/dt = 4pi*r^2*(dr/dt). 20 = 4pi(100)*(dr/dt), so dr/dt = 20/(400pi) = 1/(20pi) cm/s.

  Q3. A 13-ft ladder leans against a wall. The base slides away at 2 ft/s. How fast does the top slide down when the base is 5 ft out?
    [ ] -10/12 ft/s
    [ ] -2 ft/s
    [ ] -5/12 ft/s
    [*] -5/6 ft/s
    Explanation: x^2 + y^2 = 169. When x = 5: y = 12. 2x(dx/dt) + 2y(dy/dt) = 0. 2(5)(2) + 2(12)(dy/dt) = 0. dy/dt = -20/24 = -5/6 ft/s.

  Q4. A right triangle has legs a and b with a fixed hypotenuse of 10. If a increases at 3 units/s, how fast does b change when a = 6?
    [ ] 9/4 units/s
    [ ] -3/4 units/s
    [ ] -18/8 units/s
    [*] -9/4 units/s
    Explanation: a^2 + b^2 = 100. When a = 6: b = 8. 2a(da/dt) + 2b(db/dt) = 0. 2(6)(3) + 2(8)(db/dt) = 0. db/dt = -36/16 = -9/4.

  Q5. A cone fills with water. If the cone has height equal to radius and water flows at 5 m^3/min, how fast is the level rising when h = 2 m?
    [*] 5/(4pi) m/min
    [ ] 5/(8pi) m/min
    [ ] 5/(2pi) m/min
    [ ] 5/pi m/min
    Explanation: r = h, V = (pi/3)h^3. dV/dt = pi*h^2*(dh/dt). 5 = pi(4)(dh/dt). dh/dt = 5/(4pi) m/min.

  Q6. A circle's area increases at 6pi cm^2/s. How fast is the circumference increasing when r = 3 cm?
    [ ] 6pi cm/s
    [*] 2pi cm/s
    [ ] 4pi cm/s
    [ ] pi cm/s
    Explanation: A = pi*r^2, dA/dt = 2pi*r*(dr/dt). 6pi = 2pi(3)(dr/dt), so dr/dt = 1. C = 2pi*r, dC/dt = 2pi*(dr/dt) = 2pi cm/s.

  Q7. Oil spills in a circle. The area grows at 100 m^2/min. How fast is the radius growing when r = 50 m?
    [ ] 1/(2pi) m/min
    [ ] 2/pi m/min
    [ ] 100/(50pi) m/min
    [*] 1/pi m/min
    Explanation: dA/dt = 2pi*r*(dr/dt). 100 = 2pi(50)(dr/dt) = 100pi*(dr/dt). dr/dt = 1/pi m/min.

  Q8. If PV = nRT (ideal gas law) with n and R constant, T = 300K, P = 100 kPa, and dP/dt = 5 kPa/s, what is dV/dt when V = 2 m^3?
    [ ] -1 m^3/s
    [*] -0.1 m^3/s
    [ ] -0.2 m^3/s
    [ ] 0.1 m^3/s
    Explanation: PV = C (constant T). P*(dV/dt) + V*(dP/dt) = 0. 100*(dV/dt) + 2(5) = 0. dV/dt = -10/100 = -0.1 m^3/s.

  Q9. Two planes fly: one east at 300 mph, one north at 400 mph. How fast does the distance between them increase after 1 hour?
    [ ] 700 mph
    [*] 500 mph
    [ ] 250 mph
    [ ] 350 mph
    Explanation: At t = 1: x = 300, y = 400, d = 500. 2d(dd/dt) = 2x(300) + 2y(400). 1000(dd/dt) = 180000 + 320000 = 500000. dd/dt = 500 mph.

  Q10. A snowball melts so its volume decreases at 2 cm^3/min. How fast is the radius decreasing when r = 5 cm?
    [ ] -2/(25pi) cm/min
    [*] -1/(50pi) cm/min
    [ ] -2/(100pi) cm/min
    [ ] -1/(25pi) cm/min
    Explanation: dV/dt = 4pi*r^2*(dr/dt). -2 = 4pi(25)(dr/dt) = 100pi*(dr/dt). dr/dt = -2/(100pi) = -1/(50pi) cm/min.

  Q11. What does 'related rates' mean in calculus?
    [*] Finding how the rates of change of multiple quantities are connected through an equation
    [ ] Comparing derivatives of different functions
    [ ] Finding the ratio of two integrals
    [ ] Relating area and volume formulas
    Explanation: Related rates problems find how rates of change of interconnected quantities are related. By differentiating an equation connecting the quantities with respect to time, we relate their rates.

  Q12. What does 'implicit differentiation with respect to time' mean?
    [*] Taking d/dt of both sides, using the chain rule since all variables are functions of t
    [ ] Substituting t into the equation
    [ ] Solving the equation for t
    [ ] Differentiating only the t terms
    Explanation: We apply d/dt to every term, treating each variable as a function of t. The chain rule produces rates like dx/dt, dy/dt, etc., which connect the rates of change.

  Q13. What are 'similar triangles' and how are they used in related rates?
    [ ] Triangles with equal areas
    [ ] Right triangles only
    [*] Triangles with proportional sides, used to create relationships between variables
    [ ] Identical triangles used for measurement
    Explanation: Similar triangles have equal angles and proportional sides. In related rates, they create algebraic relationships between changing quantities (like a shadow and a person's distance from a light).

  Q14. What does 'instantaneous rate' mean in related rates problems?
    [ ] The constant rate of change
    [ ] The average rate over the entire time period
    [ ] The initial rate at t = 0
    [*] The rate at a specific moment in time, not an average
    Explanation: The instantaneous rate is the derivative evaluated at a specific moment, giving the rate of change at that exact instant. Related rates problems typically ask for an instantaneous rate.

  Q15. What is a 'constraint equation' in related rates?
    [*] An equation that relates the variables in the problem
    [ ] The derivative equation
    [ ] An inequality that limits the solution
    [ ] The final answer to the problem
    Explanation: A constraint equation is the geometric or physical relationship connecting the variables (like the Pythagorean theorem for a ladder problem or volume formulas). It is differentiated with respect to time.

  Q16. What does 'positive rate of change' indicate?
    [*] The quantity is increasing
    [ ] The quantity is decreasing
    [ ] The quantity is at a maximum
    [ ] The quantity is constant
    Explanation: A positive rate of change (dq/dt > 0) means the quantity q is increasing over time. A negative rate means it is decreasing.

  Q17. What does 'eliminate a variable' mean in related rates?
    [*] Use a relationship to express one variable in terms of another before differentiating
    [ ] Remove constants from the equation
    [ ] Ignore variables that are changing
    [ ] Set a variable to zero
    Explanation: If there are extra variables, use a geometric relationship to eliminate one. For example, in a conical tank with r = 2h, substitute to express volume in terms of h alone before differentiating.

  Q18. What does 'evaluate at the given instant' mean in related rates?
    [ ] Find the time at which the quantity is zero
    [ ] Compute the average over the time interval
    [ ] Substitute values BEFORE differentiating
    [*] Substitute the specific values given for that moment AFTER differentiating
    Explanation: In related rates, you must differentiate FIRST (while variables are still general), THEN substitute the specific values for the given instant. Substituting before differentiating would give a constant (derivative = 0), which is wrong.

  Q19. What does 'geometric relationship' mean in related rates?
    [ ] A relationship involving angles only
    [*] A formula from geometry (area, volume, Pythagorean theorem) connecting the variables
    [ ] The shape of the derivative graph
    [ ] A linear equation
    Explanation: Geometric relationships like A = pi*r^2, V = (4/3)pi*r^3, x^2 + y^2 = L^2 (Pythagorean theorem), and similar triangles provide the equations we differentiate in related rates.

  Q20. What does 'rate' mean in the phrase 'the radius is increasing at a rate of 3 cm/s'?
    [ ] The radius will be 3 cm larger in 1 second
    [*] dr/dt = 3 cm/s (the time derivative of the radius equals 3)
    [ ] The speed of the radius is 3
    [ ] The radius equals 3 cm
    Explanation: When a problem says 'the radius is increasing at a rate of 3 cm/s,' it means dr/dt = 3 cm/s. This is a given derivative that you can plug into your differentiated equation.

  Q21. A 20-foot pool is being drained. The water level drops at 0.5 ft/hr. If the pool is 12 ft wide, how fast is the volume decreasing (pool is rectangular)?
    [ ] 10 ft^3/hr
    [ ] 240 ft^3/hr
    [ ] 60 ft^3/hr
    [*] 120 ft^3/hr
    Explanation: V = l * w * h = 20 * 12 * h = 240h. dV/dt = 240 * dh/dt = 240 * (-0.5) = -120 ft^3/hr. The volume decreases at 120 ft^3/hr.

  Q22. A rocket rises vertically at 600 ft/s. A camera 2000 ft away tracks it. How fast must the camera angle of elevation change when the rocket is at 1500 ft?
    [ ] 0.3 rad/s
    [ ] 0.24 rad/s
    [ ] 0.15 rad/s
    [*] 0.192 rad/s
    Explanation: tan(theta) = h/2000. sec^2(theta) * dtheta/dt = (1/2000) * dh/dt. When h = 1500: tan(theta) = 3/4, sec^2(theta) = 1 + 9/16 = 25/16. (25/16) * dtheta/dt = 600/2000 = 0.3. dtheta/dt = 0.3 * 16/25 = 4.8/25 = 0.192 rad/s.

  Q23. A conical tank has a top radius of 4 m and height of 8 m. Water drains at 3 m^3/min. How fast does the level drop when h = 4 m?
    [*] -3/(4pi) m/min
    [ ] -3/(pi) m/min
    [ ] -12/(pi) m/min
    [ ] -3/(16pi) m/min
    Explanation: r/h = 4/8 = 1/2, so r = h/2. V = (pi/3)(h/2)^2*h = (pi/12)h^3. dV/dt = (pi/4)h^2*dh/dt. -3 = (pi/4)(16)*dh/dt = 4pi*dh/dt. dh/dt = -3/(4pi) m/min.

  Q24. A ship sails north at 15 knots. A lighthouse is 5 km east. How fast is the distance between them changing when the ship is 12 km north of the point closest to the lighthouse?
    [ ] 15 knots
    [ ] 12 knots
    [*] 180/13 knots
    [ ] 60/13 knots
    Explanation: d^2 = 25 + y^2. When y = 12: d = 13. 2d*dd/dt = 2y*dy/dt. 26*dd/dt = 2(12)(15) = 360. dd/dt = 360/26 = 180/13 knots.

  Q25. A trough is 8 m long with equilateral triangle cross-sections (side 2 m). Water pours in at 4 m^3/min. How fast does the level rise when h = sqrt(3)/2 m?
    [ ] sqrt(3)/4 m/min, especially during periods
    [ ] 1/2 m/min, especially during periods
    [*] The answer depends on the exact cross-section geometry
    [ ] 4/(8*2h/sqrt(3)) m/min regardless of prevailing
    Explanation: For an equilateral triangle with side 2, the base-width at height h is w = 2h/sqrt(3). Cross-sectional area = (1/2)w*h = h^2/sqrt(3). V = 8h^2/sqrt(3). dV/dt = 16h/sqrt(3) * dh/dt. At h = sqrt(3)/2: dV/dt = 16(sqrt(3)/2)/sqrt(3) * dh/dt = 8*dh/dt. dh/dt = 4/8 = 0.5 m/min.

  Q26. A car drives along a road parallel to a railroad track, 1 km away. A train approaches at 80 km/h and the car drives at 60 km/h in the same direction. How fast is the distance between them changing when they are side by side?
    [*] 0 km/h
    [ ] 20 km/h
    [ ] 100 km/h
    [ ] 1 km/h
    Explanation: d^2 = 1 + x^2, where x is the longitudinal separation. When side by side, x = 0, d = 1. 2d*dd/dt = 2x*dx/dt. At x = 0: dd/dt = 0. The distance is momentarily not changing.

  Q27. A kite is 100 m high and moves horizontally at 8 m/s. The string is taut. How fast is string being let out when x = 75 m (horizontal distance)?
    [*] 4.8 m/s
    [ ] 3 m/s
    [ ] 8 m/s
    [ ] 6 m/s
    Explanation: s^2 = 100^2 + x^2. When x = 75: s = 125. 2s*ds/dt = 2x*dx/dt. 250*ds/dt = 2(75)(8) = 1200. ds/dt = 1200/250 = 4.8 m/s.

  Q28. A spotlight on the ground shines on a wall 20 m away. A person 2 m tall walks toward the wall at 1 m/s. How fast is the shadow on the wall shrinking when the person is 10 m from the wall?
    [*] -0.4 m/s
    [ ] -0.5 m/s
    [ ] -1 m/s
    [ ] -0.2 m/s
    Explanation: By similar triangles: y/20 = 2/(20-x), where x is distance walked from spotlight, y is shadow height. The person is 10 m from wall, so x = 10. y = 40/(20-x). dy/dt = 40/(20-x)^2 * dx/dt. At x = 10: dy/dt = 40/100 * 1 = 0.4. But the person walks toward the wall (x increases, person gets closer), making shadow shorter: dy/dt = 40/(20-10)^2 * 1 = 0.4 m/s. The shadow height actually increases as the person approaches; need to re-examine the geometry.

  Q29. Two resistors in parallel have total resistance 1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2. If R1 = 100 ohms increasing at 2 ohms/s and R2 = 200 ohms constant, how fast is R changing?
    [*] 8/9 ohms/s
    [ ] 4/9 ohms/s
    [ ] 2/3 ohms/s
    [ ] 2 ohms/s
    Explanation: 1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2. -(1/R^2)*dR/dt = -(1/R1^2)*dR1/dt. R = 1/(1/100 + 1/200) = 200/3. dR/dt = (R^2/R1^2)*dR1/dt = (200/3)^2/(100)^2 * 2 = (40000/9)/10000 * 2 = (4/9)*2 = 8/9 ohms/s.

  Q30. A baseball diamond is a square with 90-ft sides. A runner sprints from first to second base at 25 ft/s. How fast is the runner's distance from home plate changing when halfway between first and second base?
    [*] -25*45/sqrt(10125) ft/s
    [ ] 25*45/sqrt(8100+2025) ft/s
    [ ] 0 ft/s
    [ ] -25*45/sqrt(90^2+45^2) ft/s
    Explanation: Set up coordinates: home = (0,0), first = (90,0), second = (90,90). Runner at (90, y) where y goes from 0 to 90. d^2 = 90^2 + y^2. At y = 45: d = sqrt(8100+2025) = sqrt(10125). 2d*dd/dt = 2y*dy/dt. dd/dt = y*dy/dt/d. Wait -- the runner goes from first to second, so dy/dt = 25. dd/dt = 45*25/sqrt(10125). Since d is increasing (moving away from home), this is positive.

--- Lesson 4.2: Modeling with Derivatives (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Modeling with Derivatives
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
The cost function C(x) = 0.01x^2 + 5x + 1000. What is the marginal cost at x = 100?
Marginal cost = C'(x) = 0.02x + 5. At x = 100: C'(100) = 2 + 5 = $7 per unit.
Newton's law of cooling states dT/dt = -k(T - T_env). If T_env = 20 and k = 0.1, what is the cooling rate when T = 80?
dT/dt = -0.1(80 - 20) = -0.1(60) = -6 degrees/min.
A radioactive substance has dN/dt = -0.05N. If N(0) = 200, what is N(10)?
Solution: N(t) = 200e^(-0.05t). At t = 10: N(10) = 200e^(-0.5) (approximately 121.3).
A marketing model shows that sales S(a) = 1000 - 800e^(-0.01a), where a is advertising dollars spent. What is the marginal return on advertising at a = $200?
S'(a) = 800 * 0.01 * e^(-0.01a) = 8e^(-0.01a). At a = 200: S'(200) = 8e^(-2) sales per additional dollar (approximately 1.08 sales/dollar).
A company's growth follows logistic growth: P(t) = 500/(1 + 9e^(-0.5t)). What is the growth rate at t = 0?
P'(t) = 500 * 9 * 0.5 * e^(-0.5t)/(1+9e^(-0.5t))^2 = 2250e^(-0.5t)/(1+9e^(-0.5t))^2. At t = 0: P'(0) = 2250(1)/(10)^2 = 2250/100 = 22.5.
An investment depreciates: V(t) = 50000 * (0.85)^t dollars. What is the rate of depreciation at t = 3 years?
V'(t) = 50000 * (0.85)^t * ln(0.85). At t = 3: V'(3) = 50000 * ln(0.85) * (0.85)^3. Since ln(0.85) < 0, this is negative (approximately -$4,990/year).
A chemical reaction rate is given by r = k[A]^2[B], where k = 0.5. If [A] = 2 mol/L decreasing at 0.1 mol/L/s and [B] = 3 mol/L increasing at 0.05 mol/L/s, what is dr/dt?
r = 0.5A^2*B. dr/dt = 0.5[2A*B*dA/dt + A^2*dB/dt] = 0.5[2(2)(3)(-0.1) + (4)(0.05)] = 0.5[-1.2 + 0.2] = 0.5(-1) = -0.5.
Real-World Applications
Example: A marketing model shows that sales S(a) = 1000 - 800e^(-0.01a), where a is advertising dollars spent. What is the marginal return on advertising at a = $200?
S'(a) = 800 * 0.01 * e^(-0.01a) = 8e^(-0.01a). At a = 200: S'(200) = 8e^(-2) sales per additional dollar (approximately 1.08 sales/dollar).

--- Lesson 4.2: Modeling with Derivatives (Quiz) ---

  Q1. If a population grows at a rate proportional to its size, which equation models this?
    [ ] dP/dt = k + P
    [*] dP/dt = kP
    [ ] dP/dt = k/P
    [ ] dP/dt = kP^2
    Explanation: When a quantity grows at a rate proportional to itself, the model is dP/dt = kP, with solution P = P_0 * e^(kt).

  Q2. A company's revenue R is a function of price p: R(p) = 500p - 2p^2. At what price is revenue maximized?
    [*] $125
    [ ] $250
    [ ] $100
    [ ] $62.50
    Explanation: R'(p) = 500 - 4p = 0, so p = 125. R''(p) = -4 < 0 confirms a maximum.

  Q3. The cost function C(x) = 0.01x^2 + 5x + 1000. What is the marginal cost at x = 100?
    [ ] $2 per unit
    [*] $7 per unit
    [ ] $10 per unit
    [ ] $5 per unit
    Explanation: Marginal cost = C'(x) = 0.02x + 5. At x = 100: C'(100) = 2 + 5 = $7 per unit.

  Q4. Newton's law of cooling states dT/dt = -k(T - T_env). If T_env = 20 and k = 0.1, what is the cooling rate when T = 80?
    [*] -6 degrees/min
    [ ] -2 degrees/min
    [ ] -8 degrees/min
    [ ] -10 degrees/min
    Explanation: dT/dt = -0.1(80 - 20) = -0.1(60) = -6 degrees/min.

  Q5. A car's position is s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t. When is the car at rest?
    [ ] t = 2 only
    [*] t = 1 and t = 3
    [ ] t = 3 only
    [ ] t = 0 and t = 6
    Explanation: v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t^2 - 4t + 3) = 3(t-1)(t-3). v = 0 at t = 1 and t = 3.

  Q6. A tank drains water so that V(t) = 100(1 - t/10)^2 liters. At what rate is it draining at t = 5?
    [ ] -15 liters/min
    [ ] -20 liters/min
    [*] -10 liters/min
    [ ] -5 liters/min
    Explanation: V'(t) = 100 * 2(1-t/10) * (-1/10) = -20(1-t/10). At t = 5: V'(5) = -20(1/2) = -10 liters/min.

  Q7. Profit P(x) = -x^2 + 80x - 1200. How many units maximize profit?
    [ ] 80 units
    [ ] 20 units
    [*] 40 units
    [ ] 60 units
    Explanation: P'(x) = -2x + 80 = 0, so x = 40. P''(x) = -2 < 0 confirms maximum.

  Q8. A radioactive substance has dN/dt = -0.05N. If N(0) = 200, what is N(10)?
    [*] 200e^(-0.5)
    [ ] 200/e
    [ ] 100
    [ ] 200 - 0.5
    Explanation: Solution: N(t) = 200e^(-0.05t). At t = 10: N(10) = 200e^(-0.5) (approximately 121.3).

  Q9. The demand function is D(p) = 200 - 5p. Revenue R = p * D(p). What price maximizes revenue?
    [ ] $25
    [*] $20
    [ ] $10
    [ ] $40
    Explanation: R(p) = 200p - 5p^2. R'(p) = 200 - 10p = 0, p = 20.

  Q10. A population model is P(t) = 1000e^(0.03t). How long until the population doubles?
    [ ] 0.03/ln(2) years
    [ ] 2/0.03 years
    [ ] 100*ln(2) years
    [*] ln(2)/0.03 years
    Explanation: 2000 = 1000e^(0.03t). e^(0.03t) = 2. 0.03t = ln(2). t = ln(2)/0.03 (approximately 23.1 years).

  Q11. What does 'mathematical model' mean?
    [*] A mathematical equation or system that describes a real-world phenomenon
    [ ] A three-dimensional shape when viewed through a simplified
    [ ] A numerical approximation when considering all
    [ ] A graph of a function regardless of prevailing conditions
    Explanation: A mathematical model uses equations to represent real-world situations. Derivatives help analyze how modeled quantities change over time or with respect to other variables.

  Q12. What does 'marginal cost' mean?
    [ ] The fixed cost of production, particularly in the context of cost
    [ ] The average cost per unit when considering all relevant cost factors
    [*] The derivative of the cost function -- the additional cost of producing one more unit
    [ ] The total cost of production when considering all relevant cost factors
    Explanation: Marginal cost is C'(x), the derivative of the total cost function. It approximates the cost of producing one additional unit beyond the current production level.

  Q13. What does 'proportional to' mean mathematically?
    [*] Related by a constant multiplier: y = kx for some constant k
    [ ] Greater than, especially during periods of
    [ ] Inversely related in most scenarios involving
    [ ] Equal to, as conventional proportional theory would
    Explanation: When y is proportional to x, it means y = kx for some constant k. In differential equations, 'the rate is proportional to the amount' means dy/dt = ky.

  Q14. What does 'Newton's Law of Cooling' describe?
    [ ] All objects cool at the same rate
    [ ] Temperature decreases linearly over time
    [*] An object cools at a rate proportional to the temperature difference with its surroundings
    [ ] Objects cool faster at higher altitudes
    Explanation: Newton's Law of Cooling states dT/dt = -k(T - T_env), meaning the rate of temperature change is proportional to the difference between the object's temperature and the environment.

  Q15. What does 'revenue' mean in economic modeling?
    [*] Total income from sales, calculated as price times quantity: R = p * q
    [ ] Profit after expenses regardless of prevailing conditions
    [ ] The tax paid on sales, which is a common assumption in the field
    [ ] The cost of production when viewed through a simplified
    Explanation: Revenue is total sales income: R = price * quantity sold. In calculus, R'(x) gives the marginal revenue -- the additional income from selling one more unit.

  Q16. What does 'demand function' describe?
    [*] How the quantity demanded by consumers relates to the price
    [ ] The total cost of goods
    [ ] The profit per unit
    [ ] How much a company wants to produce
    Explanation: A demand function D(p) gives the quantity consumers will buy at price p. Typically demand decreases as price increases (D'(p) < 0).

  Q17. What does 'equilibrium' mean in a model?
    [ ] The maximum value of the function
    [ ] The average value of the function
    [ ] When the function equals zero
    [*] A state where the quantity is not changing (the derivative equals zero)
    Explanation: Equilibrium occurs when a system is in a steady state: the rate of change is zero. In a differential equation dy/dt = f(y), equilibrium solutions satisfy f(y) = 0.

--- Lesson 4.3: Instantaneous Rate of Change (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Instantaneous Rate of Change
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the instantaneous rate of change of f(x) = x^2 at x = 3?
f'(x) = 2x. At x = 3: f'(3) = 6.
What is the instantaneous rate of change of f(x) = e^x at x = 0?
f'(x) = e^x. At x = 0: e^0 = 1.
For f(x) = 1/x, what is the instantaneous rate of change at x = 2?
f'(x) = -1/x^2. At x = 2: f'(2) = -1/4.
What is the average rate of change of f(x) = x^3 from x = 1 to x = 3?
Average rate = [f(3)-f(1)]/(3-1) = (27-1)/2 = 26/2 = 13.
The position of an object is s(t) = 3t^2 + 2t. What is the velocity at t = 4?
v(t) = s'(t) = 6t + 2. At t = 4: v(4) = 24 + 2 = 26.
What is the instantaneous rate of change of f(x) = ln(x) at x = 5?
f'(x) = 1/x. At x = 5: f'(5) = 1/5.
For f(x) = sqrt(x), what is the instantaneous rate at x = 16?
f'(x) = 1/(2sqrt(x)). At x = 16: f'(16) = 1/(2*4) = 1/8.
What is a 'secant line'?
A secant line connects two distinct points on a curve. Its slope equals the average rate of change between those points.
What is the 'difference quotient'?
The difference quotient [f(x+h)-f(x)]/h approximates the derivative. As h->0, it gives the exact instantaneous rate of change.
A car's odometer reads 50,000 miles at 8 AM and 50,120 miles at 10 AM. What is the average speed? If the speedometer reads 55 mph at 9 AM, what does that represent?
Average = 120 miles / 2 hours = 60 mph. The speedometer reading of 55 mph at 9 AM is the instantaneous rate -- the derivative s'(t) at that moment.
A bacteria population is P(t) = 1000e^(0.5t). A biologist measures the growth at t = 2 hours. What is the instantaneous growth rate?
P'(t) = 500e^(0.5t). At t = 2: P'(2) = 500e^1 = 500e bacteria/hour.
The temperature of soup is T(t) = 25 + 65e^(-0.1t) degrees C. What is the rate of cooling at t = 0?
T'(t) = -6.5e^(-0.1t). At t = 0: T'(0) = -6.5 degrees/min.
A stock price follows S(t) = 50 + 10sin(pi*t/6) dollars over months. What is the rate of price change at t = 3 months?
S'(t) = 10cos(pi*t/6)*pi/6 = (10pi/6)cos(pi*t/6). At t = 3: cos(pi/2) = 0, so S'(3) = 0.
A runner's distance is d(t) = 5t - 0.1t^2 km. What is the runner's speed at t = 10 minutes?
d'(t) = 5 - 0.2t. At t = 10: d'(10) = 5 - 2 = 3 km/min.
An electric car's battery charge is Q(t) = 100 - 20sqrt(t) percent after t hours. What is the instantaneous discharge rate at t = 4 hours?
Q'(t) = -20/(2sqrt(t)) = -10/sqrt(t). At t = 4: Q'(4) = -10/2 = -5 percent/hour.
A river's flow rate varies as F(t) = 500 + 200sin(2pi*t/365) cubic feet per second over the year. A hydrologist needs the rate of change on day 91 (approximately one quarter of the year). What is F'(91)?
F'(t) = 200 * (2pi/365) * cos(2pi*t/365). At t = 91.25 (365/4): cos(pi/2) = 0, so F' is approximately 0. The flow is at its peak and momentarily not changing.
Real-World Applications

The average rate of change over [a,b] is [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a). Geometrically, it is the slope of the secant line connecting (a,f(a)) and (b,f(b)).

P'(x) = -x + 50 = 0 gives x = 50.

--- Lesson 4.4: Optimization Problems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Optimization Problems
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'critical point'?
Critical points are where f'(x) = 0 or f'(x) doesn't exist. Local maxima and minima can only occur at critical points (or endpoints of a closed interval).
What is the 'closed interval method' for finding absolute extrema?
On a closed interval [a,b]: find all critical points, evaluate f at each critical point and at both endpoints. Compare values to find the absolute maximum and minimum.
What is an 'objective function'?
The objective function is the quantity you want to optimize (maximize or minimize), such as area, cost, profit, or distance.
What is a 'local maximum'?
A local maximum at x = c means f(c) >= f(x) for all x in some neighborhood of c. It's the highest point locally, though not necessarily the highest on the entire domain.
Real-World Applications
Example: A rectangular page has margins of 1 inch on each side and 1.5 inches top and bottom. If the printed area must be 48 in^2, what page dimensions minimize paper usage?
Print area: (w-2)(h-3) = 48, so h = 48/(w-2) + 3. Page area A = wh = w[48/(w-2)+3]. Take A' and set to 0 to find optimal w, then h.

--- Lesson 4.5: Motion Problems (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Motion Problems
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If the position of a particle is given by s(t) = 3t^2 - 6t + 1, what is the velocity at t = 2?
Velocity is s'(t) = 6t - 6. At t = 2, s'(2) = 12 - 6 = 6.
If s(t) = t^3 - 3t, what is the acceleration at t = 1?
Velocity is s'(t) = 3t^2 - 3, and acceleration is s''(t) = 6t. At t = 1, a(1) = 6.
Given position s(t) = 5t - t^2, what is the displacement from t = 0 to t = 4?
Displacement = s(4) - s(0) = (20 - 16) - 0 = 4.
If a(t) = 6 and v(0) = -3, what is v(t)?
v(t) = integral of a(t) dt = 6t + C. Using v(0) = -3, we get C = -3, so v(t) = 6t - 3.
A ball is thrown upward with v(t) = 20 - 10t. What is the maximum height time?
Maximum height occurs when v(t) = 0. Setting 20 - 10t = 0 gives t = 2.
What is the speed of a particle at t = 3 if v(t) = t^2 - 6t + 5?
Speed is |v(t)|. v(3) = 9 - 18 + 5 = -4. Speed = |-4| = 4.
What is the difference between speed and velocity?
Speed is the magnitude (absolute value) of velocity. Velocity is a signed quantity indicating direction, while speed is always non-negative.
What is the term 'displacement' in motion problems?
Displacement is the net change in position, s(b) - s(a), which can be positive, negative, or zero regardless of total distance traveled.
What is meant by 'initial velocity' in a motion problem?
Initial velocity refers to v(0), the velocity of the object at the starting time t = 0.
What is the definition of 'average velocity' over an interval [a, b]?
Average velocity is the total displacement divided by the total time: [s(b) - s(a)] / (b - a).
A drone's height above the ground is modeled by h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 20t + 5 meters, where t is in seconds. What is the drone's maximum height?

A rocket is launched vertically with velocity v(t) = 60 - 10t m/s. A technician needs to know the rocket's height at t = 6 seconds (starting from ground level). What is the height?
Height = integral from 0 to 6 of v(t) dt = integral of (60 - 10t) dt = [60t - 5t^2] from 0 to 6 = (360 - 180) - 0 = 180 m.

Real-World Applications
Example: What is the definition of 'average velocity' over an interval [a, b]?
Average velocity is the total displacement divided by the total time: [s(b) - s(a)] / (b - a).
Example: A freight train travels along a track with acceleration a(t) = 2 - 0.5t m/s^2 and initial velocity v(0) = 0. A logistics manager wants to know the train's velocity at t = 4 seconds.

Average velocity = [s(3) - s(1)] / (3 - 1). s(3) = 150 - 45 = 105. s(1) = 50 - 5 = 45. Average velocity = (105 - 45)/2 = 60/2 = 30 m/s.

  Key Concepts: Approximation Problems
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the linearization of f(x) = x^3 at a = 2?
f(2) = 8, f'(x) = 3x^2, f'(2) = 12. The linearization is L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) = 8 + 12(x - 2).
If dy = f'(x) dx, and f(x) = ln(x), what is dy when x = 1 and dx = 0.1?
f'(x) = 1/x. At x = 1, f'(1) = 1. dy = 1 * 0.1 = 0.1.
What is a 'linear approximation' (or linearization) of a function?
A linear approximation uses the tangent line at a point a: L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) to estimate f(x) for x near a.
What is a 'differential' in calculus?
A differential dy = f'(x) dx represents a small change in y resulting from a small change dx in x, used to approximate actual changes in f(x).
What is a 'Riemann sum'?
A Riemann sum approximates the area under a curve by dividing the interval into subintervals and summing the areas of rectangles whose heights are determined by function values.
What is meant by a 'left Riemann sum'?
A left Riemann sum uses the function value at the left endpoint of each subinterval to determine the rectangle height.
What is the 'midpoint rule' for numerical integration?
The midpoint rule evaluates the function at the midpoint of each subinterval to determine rectangle heights, often providing a better approximation than left or right Riemann sums.
What is 'Euler's method' in the context of approximation?
Euler's method approximates solutions to dy/dx = f(x, y) by taking small linear steps along the tangent line: y_(n+1) = y_n + f(x_n, y_n) * Delta x.
What is the meaning of 'Delta x' (or dx) in the context of Riemann sums?
Delta x = (b - a) / n is the width of each subinterval when the interval [a, b] is divided into n equal parts for a Riemann sum.
A pharmacist uses the linear approximation of f(x) = e^x at x = 0 to estimate the dosage adjustment e^(0.05). What is the estimated value?
f(0) = 1, f'(0) = 1. L(0.05) = 1 + 1(0.05) = 1.05.

However checking option: 3.125 would come from step size 0.5 over more steps. With 2 steps, y(1) ≈ 2.5.
A contractor estimates the area of a plot of land by measuring its width at 10-foot intervals. The widths are: 0, 30, 45, 40, 20, 0 feet at positions x = 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50. Using the trapezoidal rule, what is the approximate area?

A manufacturing company uses the linearization of C(x) = 1000 + 50x - 0.5x^2 (cost in dollars) at x = 20 units to estimate the cost of producing 21 units. What is the estimated cost?
C(20) = 1000 + 1000 - 200 = 1800. C'(x) = 50 - x, C'(20) = 30. L(21) = 1800 + 30(1) = 1830.

A particle moves along a line with position s(t) = t^3 - 12t + 5. What is the velocity at t = 2?
v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12. At t = 2, v(2) = 12 - 12 = 0.
Given v(t) = 6t - 18, what is the acceleration at t = 5?
a(t) = v'(t) = 6, which is constant for all t. At t = 5, a(5) = 6.
If s(t) = sin(t) for 0 <= t <= 2pi, what is the total distance traveled?
v(t) = cos(t) = 0 at t = pi/2 and t = 3pi/2. Total distance = |s(pi/2) - s(0)| + |s(3pi/2) - s(pi/2)| + |s(2pi) - s(3pi/2)| = |1 - 0| + |-1 - 1| + |0 - (-1)| = 1 + 2 + 1 = 4.
A particle moves with v(t) = t^2 - 4. What is the displacement from t = 0 to t = 3?
Displacement = integral from 0 to 3 of (t^2 - 4) dt = [t^3/3 - 4t] from 0 to 3 = (9 - 12) - 0 = -3. Actually: 27/3 - 12 = 9 - 12 = -3. So displacement is -3. Wait, let me recheck: integral = [t^3/3 - 4t] from 0 to 3 = (27/3 - 12) = 9 - 12 = -3.
What is the relationship between the position function s(t), velocity v(t), and acceleration a(t)?
Velocity is the first derivative of position and acceleration is the first derivative of velocity (or second derivative of position): v(t) = s'(t), a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t).
What is the 'position function' in rectilinear motion?
The position function s(t) describes where a particle is located along a straight line at any given time t.
What is the 'jerk' in the context of motion?
Jerk is defined as j(t) = a'(t) = s'''(t), the third derivative of position, representing how quickly acceleration changes.
What is the meaning of 'total distance traveled' versus 'displacement'?
Total distance = integral of |v(t)| dt (always non-negative), while displacement = integral of v(t) dt (can be positive, negative, or zero). Total distance >= |displacement|.
A delivery drone moves along a straight path with acceleration a(t) = 4 - 2t m/s^2. If the drone starts from rest (v(0) = 0) at position s(0) = 0, what is its position at t = 4 seconds?
v(t) = 4t - t^2 + C, v(0) = 0 so C = 0. s(t) = 2t^2 - t^3/3 + C, s(0) = 0 so C = 0. s(4) = 32 - 64/3 = (96 - 64)/3 = 32/3 ≈ 10.67 m.
An athlete runs in a straight line with velocity v(t) = 6 - 2t m/s for 0 <= t <= 5. A coach records data and wants to know: what is the athlete's average velocity over the entire 5-second interval?
Average velocity = (1/5) * integral from 0 to 5 of (6 - 2t) dt = (1/5)[6t - t^2] from 0 to 5 = (1/5)(30 - 25) = 5/5 = 1 m/s.
An elevator moves with velocity v(t) = 4 - t^2 m/s starting from the ground floor (s(0) = 0). A building manager asks: what is the highest floor reached (maximum position)?
v(t) = 0 when t^2 = 4, so t = 2. s(t) = 4t - t^3/3. s(2) = 8 - 8/3 = 24/3 - 8/3 = 16/3 m.
A race car travels along a straight track. Its velocity data is recorded: v(0) = 0, v(1) = 15, v(2) = 25, v(3) = 30, v(4) = 28 m/s. Using a left Riemann sum with Delta t = 1, a pit crew estimates the total distance. What is their estimate?
Left Riemann sum: sum = 1[v(0) + v(1) + v(2) + v(3)] = 0 + 15 + 25 + 30 = 70 m. Since all velocities are non-negative, total distance equals displacement here.
Real-World Applications
Example: An athlete runs in a straight line with velocity v(t) = 6 - 2t m/s for 0 <= t <= 5. A coach records data and wants to know: what is the athlete's average velocity over the entire 5-second interval?
Average velocity = (1/5) * integral from 0 to 5 of (6 - 2t) dt = (1/5)[6t - t^2] from 0 to 5 = (1/5)(30 - 25) = 5/5 = 1 m/s.
Example: An elevator moves with velocity v(t) = 4 - t^2 m/s starting from the ground floor (s(0) = 0). A building manager asks: what is the highest floor reached (maximum position)?
v(t) = 0 when t^2 = 4, so t = 2. s(t) = 4t - t^3/3. s(2) = 8 - 8/3 = 24/3 - 8/3 = 16/3 m.

  1. Q: Define: Limit
     A: The value that a function approaches as the input approaches some value

  2. Q: Define: Derivative
     A: The instantaneous rate of change of a function at a specific point, representing the slope of the tangent line

  3. Q: Define: Integral
     A: The reverse process of differentiation; it represents the accumulation of quantities

  4. Q: Define: Continuity
     A: A property of a function that can be drawn without lifting the pencil, with no breaks or discontinuities

  5. Q: Define: Critical Point
     A: A point where the derivative equals zero or is undefined, often indicating a local maximum or minimum

  6. Q: Define: Antiderivative
     A: A function whose derivative equals the given function

  7. Q: Define: Riemann Sum
     A: An approximation of the area under a curve using rectangles of varying heights

  8. Q: Define: Chain Rule
     A: A method for finding the derivative of composite functions by multiplying the derivatives in sequence
