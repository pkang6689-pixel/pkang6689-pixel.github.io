--- Lesson 1.1: Limits and Continuity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Limits and Continuity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lim(x->3) (2x + 1)?
For a polynomial, you can directly substitute: lim(x->3)(2x+1) = 2(3)+1 = 7.
For f(x) = x^2, what is lim(x->-2) f(x)?
Since f(x) = x^2 is a polynomial, direct substitution gives (-2)^2 = 4.
If lim(x->4^-) f(x) = 5 and lim(x->4^+) f(x) = 5, what is lim(x->4) f(x)?
A two-sided limit exists when both one-sided limits exist and are equal. Since both equal 5, lim(x->4) f(x) = 5.
What is a limit in calculus?
A limit describes the value that f(x) approaches as x approaches a specific value c, written lim(x->c) f(x) = L. The function need not be defined or equal to L at x = c.
What is a one-sided limit?
A one-sided limit considers the behavior of f(x) as x approaches c from only one direction: lim(x->c^-) from the left or lim(x->c^+) from the right.
What is a removable discontinuity?
A removable discontinuity occurs when lim(x->c) f(x) exists but f(c) is undefined or f(c) does not equal the limit. It can be 'removed' by redefining f(c) to equal the limit.
What is a jump discontinuity?
A jump discontinuity occurs when lim(x->c^-) f(x) and lim(x->c^+) f(x) both exist but have different values, so the two-sided limit does not exist.
What is an infinite discontinuity?
An infinite discontinuity occurs when f(x) increases or decreases without bound as x approaches a value c, often corresponding to a vertical asymptote in the graph.

--- Lesson 1.2: Derivatives Definition (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Derivatives Definition
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Using the limit definition, what is f'(x) if f(x) = 3x + 5?
f'(x) = lim(h->0) [(3(x+h)+5 - (3x+5))/h] = lim(h->0) [3h/h] = 3. The derivative of a linear function is its slope.
What is lim(h->0) [(x+h)^2 - x^2] / h?
Expanding: [(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) - x^2]/h = (2xh + h^2)/h = 2x + h. As h->0, this equals 2x, confirming d/dx(x^2) = 2x.
If f(x) = x^2 - 4x, what is f'(2) using the limit definition?
f'(x) = 2x - 4, so f'(2) = 2(2) - 4 = 0. Alternatively, using the definition: lim(h->0)[(2+h)^2-4(2+h)-(-4)]/h = lim(h->0)(h^2)/h = 0.
What is the average rate of change of f(x) = x^2 on [1, 3]?
Average rate of change = [f(3) - f(1)]/(3 - 1) = (9 - 1)/2 = 4.
If f(x) = 1/x, what is f'(x) using the limit definition?
f'(x) = lim(h->0) [1/(x+h) - 1/x]/h = lim(h->0) -1/[x(x+h)] = -1/x^2.
What is the derivative of a function?
The derivative f'(x) = lim(h->0) [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h is the instantaneous rate of change of f at x.
What is the tangent line to a curve at a point?
The tangent line at (a, f(a)) has slope f'(a) and best approximates the curve near x = a. Its equation is y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a).
What is the 'instantaneous rate of change' of a function?
The instantaneous rate of change at x = a is f'(a). It gives the rate at which f is changing at that exact moment.
What is a secant line?
A secant line passes through two distinct points on a curve. Its slope is the difference quotient, representing the average rate of change.
What is the relationship between differentiability and continuity?
If a function is differentiable at a point, it is continuous there. However, f(x) = |x| shows continuity does not guarantee differentiability.
A ball is thrown upward and its height in feet after t seconds is h(t) = -16t^2 + 48t + 6. What is the ball's instantaneous velocity at t = 1 second?
Velocity is the derivative of position: h'(t) = -32t + 48, so h'(1) = -32 + 48 = 16 ft/s upward.
A car travels along a highway and its odometer reads 120 miles at 2:00 PM and 180 miles at 3:00 PM. What is the car's average velocity over this interval?
Average velocity = (180 - 120)/(3 - 2) = 60/1 = 60 mph. This is the slope of the secant line connecting the two time-position points.
A bacterial colony's population is P(t) = 500 + 20t^2, where t is in hours. What is the instantaneous growth rate at t = 3 hours?
P'(t) = 40t, so P'(3) = 120 bacteria per hour. This is the instantaneous rate of population growth.
A stone is dropped from 400 feet. Its height is h(t) = 400 - 16t^2 feet. What is the velocity when it hits the ground?
Setting h(t) = 0: t = 5 seconds. Velocity h'(t) = -32t, so h'(5) = -160 ft/s. The negative indicates downward motion.
An engineer measures temperature T(t) = sqrt(t + 1) degrees C at time t minutes. What is the rate of temperature change at t = 3 minutes?
T'(t) = 1/(2*sqrt(t+1)). At t = 3: T'(3) = 1/(2*2) = 1/4 deg C per minute.
A city's population P(t) = 10000/(1 + t) is modeled for t >= 0 years. What is the rate of population change at t = 4?
P'(t) = -10000/(1+t)^2. At t = 4: P'(4) = -10000/25 = -400 people per year, declining.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the average rate of change of f(x) = x^2 on [1, 3]?
Average rate of change = [f(3) - f(1)]/(3 - 1) = (9 - 1)/2 = 4.
Example: What does 'average rate of change' mean?
The average rate of change of f on [a, b] is [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a), the slope of the secant line between the two endpoints.
Example: A car travels along a highway and its odometer reads 120 miles at 2:00 PM and 180 miles at 3:00 PM. What is the car's average velocity over this interval?
Average velocity = (180 - 120)/(3 - 2) = 60/1 = 60 mph. This is the slope of the secant line connecting the two time-position points.

    [ ] A line intersecting the curve at exactly two points

    [ ] The mean of all function values on an interval
    [ ] The instantaneous slope at a single point
    Explanation: The average rate of change of f on [a, b] is [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a), the slope of the secant line between the two endpoints.

    [ ] The function is positive there
    [ ] The function has a local extremum there
    [ ] The function is defined at that point
    [*] The limit of the difference quotient exists (is finite) at that point
    Explanation: A function is differentiable at x = a when lim(h->0) [f(a+h) - f(a)]/h exists and is finite.

    [ ] The change in y divided by the change in x over a finite interval
    [ ] The average of f over an interval
    [ ] The second derivative
    Explanation: The instantaneous rate of change at x = a is f'(a). It gives the rate at which f is changing at that exact moment.

    [*] The derivative of y with respect to x, in Leibniz notation
    [ ] The second derivative of y
    [ ] The integral of y
    Explanation: dy/dx is Leibniz notation for the derivative, emphasizing the ratio of infinitesimal changes in y and x. It is equivalent to f'(x).

    [ ] A line touching a curve at exactly one point
    [ ] A line perpendicular to the tangent
    [ ] A horizontal line through the maximum
    [*] A line passing through two points on a curve
    Explanation: A secant line passes through two distinct points on a curve. Its slope is the difference quotient, representing the average rate of change.

  Q19. What is the relationship between differentiability and continuity?
    [ ] They are completely independent properties
    [*] Differentiability implies continuity, but continuity does not imply differentiability
    [ ] A function must be differentiable to be continuous
    [ ] Continuity implies differentiability
    Explanation: If a function is differentiable at a point, it is continuous there. However, f(x) = |x| shows continuity does not guarantee differentiability.

  Q20. What does the alternate form of the derivative definition, f'(a) = lim(x->a) [f(x) - f(a)]/(x - a), represent?

    [ ] The area under the curve near x = a
    [ ] The second derivative of f at a
    Explanation: This alternate form replaces h with x - a. As x->a, this quotient gives the same derivative f'(a). It is useful when evaluating specific derivative values.

--- Lesson 1.3: Derivative Rules (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Derivative Rules
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is d/dx(x^5)?
By the power rule, d/dx(x^n) = nx^(n-1). So d/dx(x^5) = 5x^4.
What is d/dx(7x^3 - 2x + 9)?
Using the power rule and sum rule: 7(3x^2) - 2(1) + 0 = 21x^2 - 2. Constants differentiate to zero.
What is the derivative of f(x) = 4/x^2?
Rewrite as 4x^(-2). By the power rule: 4(-2)x^(-3) = -8x^(-3) = -8/x^3.
If f(x) = sqrt(x), what is f'(x)?
sqrt(x) = x^(1/2), so f'(x) = (1/2)x^(-1/2) = 1/(2*sqrt(x)).
What is d/dx(3x^4 + 2x^3 - x + 5) evaluated at x = 1?
f'(x) = 12x^3 + 6x^2 - 1. At x = 1: f'(1) = 12 + 6 - 1 = 17.
What is the derivative of a constant function f(x) = 8?
The derivative of any constant is 0 because a constant function has zero rate of change everywhere.
What is d/dx(x^(-3))?
By the power rule: d/dx(x^(-3)) = -3x^(-3-1) = -3x^(-4).
If f(x) = 2x^(3/2), what is f'(4)?
f'(x) = 2*(3/2)*x^(1/2) = 3*sqrt(x). At x = 4: f'(4) = 3*2 = 6.
What is d/dx(6x^2 - 3x + 1) at x = 0?
f'(x) = 12x - 3. At x = 0: f'(0) = 0 - 3 = -3.
If f(x) = x^4/4 - x^3/3, what is f'(x)?
f'(x) = (1/4)(4x^3) - (1/3)(3x^2) = x^3 - x^2.
What is the power rule for derivatives?
The power rule states d/dx(x^n) = nx^(n-1). It works for any real exponent n, including fractions and negatives.
What is meant by a 'derivative rule'?
Derivative rules (power rule, sum rule, constant multiple rule, etc.) are formulas derived from the limit definition that provide efficient shortcuts for finding derivatives.
What is a polynomial function?
A polynomial is a sum of terms of the form a_n*x^n where n is a non-negative integer and a_n is a constant. Examples: 3x^2 + 2x - 1.
What is a 'higher-order derivative'?
Higher-order derivatives are obtained by differentiating repeatedly. f''(x) is the second derivative, f'''(x) the third, and so on.
A manufacturer's cost function is C(x) = 0.01x^3 - 0.6x^2 + 15x + 100 dollars for x units. What is the marginal cost (the derivative of cost) when 20 units are produced?
C'(x) = 0.03x^2 - 1.2x + 15. At x = 20: C'(20) = 0.03(400) - 1.2(20) + 15 = 12 - 24 + 15 = $3 per unit.
A ball is launched so that its height is h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 20t + 1.5 meters. What is the ball's velocity at t = 2 seconds, and is it going up or down?
h'(t) = -9.8t + 20. At t = 2: h'(2) = -19.6 + 20 = 0.4 m/s. Positive means the ball is still going up.
An oil spill spreads in a circle. The radius grows as r(t) = 3*sqrt(t) meters after t hours. The area is A = pi*r^2. What is dA/dt in terms of t?
A(t) = pi*(3*sqrt(t))^2 = 9*pi*t. Then dA/dt = 9*pi, a constant. The area grows at a constant rate of 9*pi square meters per hour.
A bridge cable hangs in a shape modeled by y = 0.005x^4 - 0.1x^2 + 10 feet, where x is horizontal distance in feet from the center. What is the slope of the cable at x = 3 feet?
y' = 0.02x^3 - 0.2x. At x = 3: y' = 0.02(27) - 0.2(3) = 0.54 - 0.6 = -0.06.
A cyclist's distance from her starting point is d(t) = t^(3/2) miles after t hours. What is her speed at t = 4 hours?
d'(t) = (3/2)t^(1/2) = (3/2)*sqrt(t). At t = 4: d'(4) = (3/2)(2) = 3 mph.
A tank's water level is h(t) = 50 - 2t - 0.5t^2 centimeters after t minutes of draining. What is the rate of change of water level at t = 6 minutes?
h'(t) = -2 - t. At t = 6: h'(6) = -2 - 6 = -8 cm/min. The water level drops at 8 cm per minute.
A spacecraft's fuel remaining is F(t) = 1000 - 5t^2 kg after t minutes of burn. The mission requires knowing the fuel consumption rate at t = 8 minutes. What is it?
F'(t) = -10t. At t = 8: F'(8) = -80 kg/min. The negative sign means fuel is being consumed at 80 kg per minute.
A roller coaster track follows y = x^4/100 - x^2/5 + 10 meters. An engineer needs the steepness of the track at x = 5 meters from center. What is dy/dx at x = 5?
dy/dx = 4x^3/100 - 2x/5 = x^3/25 - 2x/5. At x = 5: 125/25 - 10/5 = 5 - 2 = 3.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does the constant multiple rule state?
The constant multiple rule says you can factor out constants when differentiating: d/dx[c*f(x)] = c*f'(x).

Overview

Key Definitions
What is lim(x->infinity) (3x^2 + 1)/(5x^2 - 2)?
When the degrees are equal, the limit is the ratio of leading coefficients: 3/5.
What is the horizontal asymptote of f(x) = (2x + 3)/(x - 1)?
Both numerator and denominator are degree 1, so the horizontal asymptote is y = 2/1 = 2.
What is lim(x->infinity) (4x^3 - x)/(2x^2 + 7)?
The numerator's degree (3) exceeds the denominator's degree (2), so the limit is infinity.
If -x^2 + 1 <= f(x) <= x^2 + 1 and lim(x->0) (-x^2+1) = lim(x->0) (x^2+1) = 1, what is lim(x->0) f(x)?
By the Squeeze Theorem, since both bounding functions approach 1, f(x) must also approach 1.
What is lim(x->0) x*cos(1/x)?
Since -1 <= cos(1/x) <= 1, we have -|x| <= x*cos(1/x) <= |x|. Both bounds approach 0, so by the Squeeze Theorem the limit is 0.
What is lim(x->-infinity) (5x^2 - 3)/(x^2 + 2)?
Same-degree rational function: leading coefficients give 5/1 = 5. The limit is the same at +infinity and -infinity.
What is the Squeeze Theorem (Sandwich Theorem)?
The Squeeze Theorem says if f is trapped between g and h, and both g and h approach the same limit L, then f must also approach L.
What is a horizontal asymptote?
A horizontal asymptote y = L exists when lim(x->infinity) f(x) = L or lim(x->-infinity) f(x) = L. The graph approaches but may cross this line.
What is a 'limit at infinity'?
A limit at infinity, lim(x->infinity) f(x) = L, means f(x) approaches L as x becomes arbitrarily large. This determines horizontal asymptotes.
What is 'dominance' when comparing functions at infinity?
Dominance refers to growth rate hierarchy: exponentials dominate polynomials, which dominate logarithms. This determines limits of ratios at infinity.
What is the 'leading term test' for limits at infinity?
For rational functions, the behavior at infinity is governed by the leading terms. Divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of x to see this clearly.

--- Lesson 1.6: Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Infinite Limits and Vertical Asymptotes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lim(x->0) 1/x?
The left-hand limit is -infinity and the right-hand limit is +infinity. Since they disagree, the two-sided limit does not exist.
What is an infinite limit?
An infinite limit means f(x) -> +infinity or f(x) -> -infinity as x approaches some finite value c. Technically the limit 'does not exist' as a real number, but we describe the behavior as infinity.
What is a vertical asymptote?
A vertical asymptote at x = a exists when at least one of the one-sided limits is +infinity or -infinity as x approaches a.
What is the difference between a vertical asymptote and a removable discontinuity?
If the common factor fully cancels and the limit is finite, the discontinuity is removable. If the denominator has a zero that does not cancel, the function blows up, creating a vertical asymptote.
What is meant by 'sign analysis' near a vertical asymptote?
Sign analysis involves checking whether the numerator and denominator are each positive or negative as x approaches the asymptote from the left and right, determining the direction of the infinite limit.
What is the relationship between vertical asymptotes and the domain of a function?
Vertical asymptotes occur at domain exclusions where the denominator is zero and the zero does not cancel with the numerator, causing the function to approach infinity.
What is an 'even vertical asymptote' versus an 'odd vertical asymptote'?
When the factor (x-a) appears to an even power in the denominator, both one-sided limits go in the same direction. An odd power makes them go in opposite directions.
Real-World Applications
Example: A lens maker uses the thin lens equation 1/f = 1/do + 1/di, where do is object distance and f = 10 cm. As an object is placed at the focal point (do -> 10), what happens to the image distance di?
1/di = 1/10 - 1/do. As do -> 10, 1/di -> 1/10 - 1/10 = 0, so di -> infinity. The image forms at infinity when the object is at the focal point.
Example: A traffic engineer models average commute time as T(v) = 60/(v - 10) + 15 minutes, where v is average speed in mph and v > 10. As average speed drops toward 10 mph, what happens?
As v -> 10^+, 60/(v-10) -> +infinity, so T -> +infinity. The vertical asymptote represents gridlock conditions.

--- Lesson 1.7: Comparing Limit Behaviors (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Comparing Limit Behaviors
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lim(x->infinity) e^x / x^100?
Exponential functions grow faster than any polynomial. No matter how large the polynomial degree, e^x eventually dominates.
What is lim(x->infinity) ln(x)/x?
Logarithmic functions grow slower than any positive power of x. So ln(x)/x -> 0 as x -> infinity.
What is lim(x->infinity) (2x^2 + 1)/(3x^2 - 5)?
Equal-degree rational function: the limit is the ratio of leading coefficients, 2/3.
What is lim(x->infinity) (x + sin(x))/x?
Rewrite as 1 + sin(x)/x. Since |sin(x)/x| <= 1/x -> 0, the limit is 1 + 0 = 1.
What is lim(x->infinity) sqrt(x)/x?
sqrt(x)/x = 1/sqrt(x) -> 0 as x -> infinity. The linear function x grows faster than sqrt(x).
What is lim(x->infinity) (3^x)/(2^x)?
(3/2)^x -> infinity since 3/2 > 1. Exponentials with larger bases grow faster.
What is lim(x->infinity) x/(x + 100)?
Divide top and bottom by x: 1/(1 + 100/x) -> 1/(1+0) = 1 as x -> infinity.
What is the 'growth rate hierarchy' for common functions?
The standard hierarchy is: logarithms grow slowest, then polynomials (by degree), then exponentials. This determines limits of ratios at infinity.
What is 'exponential growth'?
Exponential growth involves a constant multiplicative factor per unit input change. It dominates polynomial growth for any degree.
What is 'polynomial growth'?
Polynomial growth of degree n means f(x) ~ cx^n. It sits between logarithmic (slower) and exponential (faster) in the growth hierarchy.
What is 'logarithmic growth'?
Logarithmic growth goes to infinity, but extremely slowly. lim(x->inf) ln(x)/x^p = 0 for any p > 0.

--- Lesson 1.8: Continuity and Discontinuity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Continuity and Discontinuity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a removable discontinuity?
A removable discontinuity is a 'hole' in the graph. The limit exists at c, but f(c) is either undefined or does not match the limit.
What is a jump discontinuity?
A jump discontinuity has lim(x->c^-) != lim(x->c^+), so the two-sided limit does not exist. The graph 'jumps' from one value to another.
What is an infinite discontinuity?
An infinite discontinuity occurs when at least one one-sided limit is +infinity or -infinity, typically at a vertical asymptote.
What is a piecewise function?
A piecewise function uses different rules on different intervals. Continuity must be checked at the boundary points between pieces.
What is an 'oscillating discontinuity'?
An oscillating discontinuity (like sin(1/x) at x = 0) occurs when the function oscillates between values infinitely often near the point, preventing the limit from existing.
Real-World Applications
Example: A postal service charges $5 for packages up to 1 lb, $8 for over 1 lb up to 3 lb, and $12 for over 3 lb up to 5 lb. Is the cost function C(w) continuous?
The cost jumps from 5 to 8 at w = 1 and from 8 to 12 at w = 3. These sudden price changes are jump discontinuities.
Example: A coffee shop models daily revenue as R(p) = p(200 - 10p) where p is price per cup in dollars. The model applies for 0 <= p <= 20. A manager uses the IVT to determine: is there a price that generates exactly $800 in revenue?
R is a polynomial (continuous). R(0) = 0 and R(10) = 1000. Since 800 is between 0 and 1000, IVT guarantees some price p in (0,10) with R(p) = 800.

--- Lesson 1.9: One-to-One Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: One-to-One Functions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If f(3) = 7 and f is one-to-one, what is f^(-1)(7)?
If f(3) = 7 and f is one-to-one, then f^(-1) reverses the mapping: f^(-1)(7) = 3.
What is the inverse of f(x) = 3x - 6?
Swap x and y: x = 3y - 6, then solve: y = (x + 6)/3. So f^(-1)(x) = (x + 6)/3.
If f and g are inverses, what is f(g(x))?
Inverse functions undo each other: f(g(x)) = x and g(f(x)) = x for all x in the appropriate domains.
What is the inverse of f(x) = x^3?
If y = x^3, then x = y^(1/3). So f^(-1)(x) = x^(1/3), the cube root function.
If f is one-to-one, what is the domain of f^(-1)?
The domain of f^(-1) is the range of f, and the range of f^(-1) is the domain of f. They swap roles.
What is a one-to-one (injective) function?
A one-to-one function maps distinct inputs to distinct outputs. No two different x-values produce the same y-value.
What is the horizontal line test?
The horizontal line test determines if a function is one-to-one: if any horizontal line hits the graph more than once, the function is not one-to-one.
What is an inverse function?
The inverse function reverses the input-output pairs of f. Note: f^(-1)(x) is NOT 1/f(x); the superscript -1 denotes the inverse, not a reciprocal.
What is the relationship between the domain of f and the range of f^(-1)?
Domain of f = Range of f^(-1), and Range of f = Domain of f^(-1). Inverse functions swap domain and range.
What is the process for finding an inverse function algebraically?
The steps are: (1) replace f(x) with y, (2) interchange x and y, (3) solve for y. The result is y = f^(-1)(x).
What is the vertical line test, and how does it differ from the horizontal line test?
Vertical line test: is it a function? Horizontal line test: is it one-to-one? Both use intersections of lines with the graph.
What is a 'monotonic' function?
A strictly monotonic function is always increasing or always decreasing. This guarantees it is one-to-one and therefore has an inverse.
A cryptographer uses f(x) = 5x + 3 (mod 26) to encode messages. To decode, the recipient needs the inverse function. If f(x) = 18, what is the original value x? (Assume the function is one-to-one in context.)
Solving 5x + 3 = 18: 5x = 15, x = 3. The inverse function 'decrypts' by reversing the encoding.
A temperature conversion from Celsius to Fahrenheit is F(C) = (9/5)C + 32. A weather station receives 86 degrees F and needs the Celsius equivalent. Using the inverse, what is the temperature in Celsius?
F^(-1)(F) = (5/9)(F - 32). At F = 86: C = (5/9)(86 - 32) = (5/9)(54) = 30 degrees C.
A pharmaceutical company models drug dosage as d(w) = 0.5w + 10 mg, where w is patient weight in kg. If a patient requires a 35 mg dose, the pharmacist needs to determine the weight. What is w?
d^(-1)(d) = (d - 10)/0.5 = 2(d - 10). At d = 35: w = 2(35 - 10) = 2(25) = 50 kg.
A fitness tracker converts steps to calories burned via C(s) = 0.04s + 50 calories. After burning 250 calories, a user wants to know how many steps were taken. Using the inverse, what is the answer?
s = C^(-1)(C) = (C - 50)/0.04. At C = 250: s = (250 - 50)/0.04 = 200/0.04 = 5,000 steps.
A logistics company uses distance-to-time conversion t(d) = d/60 + 0.5 hours for deliveries (d in miles). A dispatcher receives an ETA of 2 hours and needs to find the distance. What is d?
d = t^(-1)(t) = 60(t - 0.5). At t = 2: d = 60(2 - 0.5) = 60(1.5) = 90 miles.
An audio engineer uses a decibel function dB(I) = 10*log10(I/I_0) where I_0 = 10^(-12) W/m^2. If a sound measures 80 dB, what is the intensity I? (Inverse of the decibel function.)
80 = 10*log10(I/10^(-12)). So log10(I/10^(-12)) = 8, meaning I/10^(-12) = 10^8, giving I = 10^(-12)*10^8 = 10^(-4) W/m^2.
Real-World Applications
Example: A cryptographer uses f(x) = 5x + 3 (mod 26) to encode messages. To decode, the recipient needs the inverse function. If f(x) = 18, what is the original value x? (Assume the function is one-to-one in context.)
Solving 5x + 3 = 18: 5x = 15, x = 3. The inverse function 'decrypts' by reversing the encoding.
Example: A pharmaceutical company models drug dosage as d(w) = 0.5w + 10 mg, where w is patient weight in kg. If a patient requires a 35 mg dose, the pharmacist needs to determine the weight. What is w?
d^(-1)(d) = (d - 10)/0.5 = 2(d - 10). At d = 35: w = 2(35 - 10) = 2(25) = 50 kg.
Example: A company's revenue function R(p) = 100p - 2p^2 (for 0 <= p <= 25) is not one-to-one on this interval. A manager asks: on what restricted domain is R one-to-one and increasing?
R'(p) = 100 - 4p = 0 at p = 25. On [0, 25], R is increasing (R' > 0), so R is one-to-one there.

--- Lesson 1.10: Introducing Limits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Introducing Limits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If f(x) = x^2 + 1, what is lim(x->3) f(x)?
f(3) = 9 + 1 = 10. Since polynomials are continuous, the limit equals the function value.
What is the intuitive meaning of a limit?
Intuitively, a limit describes the tendency or trend of f(x) as x gets closer to a target value, regardless of what f actually equals there.
What is a left-hand limit?
The left-hand limit, lim(x->c^-) f(x), considers only x-values approaching c from below (x < c).
What is a right-hand limit?
The right-hand limit, lim(x->c^+) f(x), considers only x-values approaching c from above (x > c).
What is the difference between 'f(c) is defined' and 'lim(x->c) f(x) exists'?
The function value f(c) and the limit at c are independent concepts. For example, f(x) = (x^2-1)/(x-1) has limit 2 at x=1 but is undefined there.
What is the limit of a constant function?
For f(x) = k, lim(x->c) k = k for any c. A constant function approaches the same constant no matter what.
Real-World Applications
Example: A savings account compounds interest. After year 1: $1050, year 2: $1102.50, year 5: $1276.28, year 10: $1628.89. If instead of yearly compounding, the bank compounds continuously, the account approaches what multiplier of the principal after 1 year at 5% interest?
Continuous compounding gives lim(n->inf)(1 + 0.05/n)^n = e^(0.05) which is approximately 1.05127, slightly more than simple 5%.

--- Lesson 1.11: Introducing Limits (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Introducing Limits
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is lim(x->0) (x^3 + 4)?
Since x^3 + 4 is continuous, lim(x->0) = 0 + 4 = 4.
If f(x) = {x + 2 for x < 1, 5 for x = 1, 2x for x > 1}, what is lim(x->1) f(x)?
lim(x->1^-) = 1+2 = 3. lim(x->1^+) = 2(1) = 2. Since 3 != 2, the two-sided limit does not exist.
What is lim(x->4) sqrt(x)?
sqrt(x) is continuous at x = 4, so the limit is sqrt(4) = 2.
Using a graph, if the curve approaches the point (3, 8) from both sides but has a hole there, what is lim(x->3) f(x)?
A hole at (3, 8) means the function approaches 8 as x approaches 3. The limit is determined by the trend, not the function value.
What is lim(x->2^+) floor(x), where floor(x) is the greatest integer function?
For x slightly greater than 2 (like 2.01, 2.001), floor(x) = 2. So lim(x->2^+) floor(x) = 2.
What is the purpose of studying limits?
Limits are the conceptual foundation upon which derivatives (instantaneous rates of change) and integrals (accumulated quantities) are built.
What is a 'limit at a point' versus a 'limit at infinity'?
Limit at a point: what does f(x) approach as x -> c? Limit at infinity: what does f(x) approach as x -> infinity? Both are limits but examine different regimes.
What is the 'trend' of a function near a point?
The trend or tendency of f(x) near x = c is precisely what the limit captures. It tells us what value f is heading toward.
What is 'numerical estimation of limits'?
Numerical estimation involves computing f at x-values like c-0.1, c-0.01, c-0.001, c+0.001, c+0.01, c+0.1 to observe what value f approaches.
What is the 'graphical interpretation of limits'?
On a graph, the limit at x = c is the y-coordinate the curve approaches from both sides of x = c, even if there is a hole or discontinuity.
What is 'algebraic computation of limits'?
Algebraic computation uses symbolic manipulation to find exact limit values, especially when direct substitution gives an indeterminate form like 0/0.

The limit is determined by the trend of d(t) as t approaches 30, which is 5 km. The actual value d(30) = 4.8 km is irrelevant to the limit.

--- Lesson 1.11: Introducing Limits (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Limit
     A: The value that a function approaches as the input approaches some value

  2. Q: Define: Derivative
     A: The instantaneous rate of change of a function at a specific point, representing the slope of the tangent line

  3. Q: Define: Integral
     A: The reverse process of differentiation; it represents the accumulation of quantities

  4. Q: Define: Continuity
     A: A property of a function that can be drawn without lifting the pencil, with no breaks or discontinuities

  5. Q: Define: Critical Point
     A: A point where the derivative equals zero or is undefined, often indicating a local maximum or minimum

  6. Q: Define: Antiderivative
     A: A function whose derivative equals the given function

  7. Q: Define: Riemann Sum
     A: An approximation of the area under a curve using rectangles of varying heights

  8. Q: Define: Chain Rule
     A: A method for finding the derivative of composite functions by multiplying the derivatives in sequence

  9. Q: When creating a mathematical model to represent a real-world scenario, which is the most important f
     A: Identify the variables and relationships between them

  10. Q: When validating a mathematical model against real data, what does a large discrepancy typically indi
     A: The model may need adjustment or additional variables
