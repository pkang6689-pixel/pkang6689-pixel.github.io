--- Lesson 8.1: Net Change and Accumulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Change and Accumulation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If v(t) = 3t^2 - 6t represents velocity in m/s, what is the net displacement from t = 0 to t = 4?
Net displacement = integral from 0 to 4 of (3t^2 - 6t) dt = [t^3 - 3t^2] from 0 to 4 = (64 - 48) - 0 = 16 meters.
If F'(x) = 2x + 5 and F(1) = 10, what is F(4)?
F(4) = F(1) + integral from 1 to 4 of (2x + 5) dx = 10 + [x^2 + 5x] from 1 to 4 = 10 + (16 + 20 - 1 - 5) = 10 + 30 - 6... Let me recompute: [x^2 + 5x] from 1 to 4 = (16+20) - (1+5) = 36 - 6 = 30. Hmm wait: at x=4: 16+20=36. At x=1: 1+5=6. So integral = 30. F(4) = 10 + 30 = 40. Actually let me recheck the answer. F(4) = 10 + integral from 1 to 4 of (2x+5) dx = 10 + [x^2+5x] evaluated from 1 to 4 = 10 + (36-6) = 10 + 30 = 40. So the answer should be 40.
A car's velocity is v(t) = 60 - 10t mph. What is the total distance traveled from t = 0 to t = 8 hours?
v(t) = 0 at t = 6. Total distance = integral from 0 to 6 of (60-10t) dt + integral from 6 to 8 of |60-10t| dt = [60t-5t^2] from 0 to 6 + integral from 6 to 8 of (10t-60) dt = 180 + [5t^2-60t] from 6 to 8 = 180 + (320-480-180+360) = 180 + 20 = 200 miles.
A company's marginal revenue is R'(x) = 50 - 0.1x. What is the revenue gained from selling units 100 to 200?
Revenue = integral from 100 to 200 of (50 - 0.1x) dx = [50x - 0.05x^2] from 100 to 200 = (10000 - 2000) - (5000 - 500) = 8000 - 4500 = 3500.
The rate of change of temperature is dT/dt = -2t + 8 degrees per hour. What is the net change in temperature from t = 0 to t = 6?
Net change = integral from 0 to 6 of (-2t + 8) dt = [-t^2 + 8t] from 0 to 6 = (-36 + 48) - 0 = 12 degrees.
What is the Net Change Theorem?
The Net Change Theorem states integral from a to b of F'(x) dx = F(b) - F(a). It connects a rate of change function to the total (net) change in the quantity over the interval.
What is the difference between 'net displacement' and 'total distance traveled'?
Net displacement = integral of v(t) dt accounts for direction (forward minus backward). Total distance = integral of |v(t)| dt counts all movement as positive. They differ when the object changes direction.
What is 'net change' as opposed to 'gross change'?
Net change is the algebraic sum F(b) - F(a), which can be positive or negative. Gross change sums up the absolute values of all changes, like total distance vs. displacement.
What is the 'accumulation function' in this context?
An accumulation function F(x) = F(a) + integral from a to x of f(t) dt gives the value of quantity F at any point x, starting from its known value F(a) and adding the accumulated change.
What is 'FTC Part 2' and how does it relate to net change?
FTC Part 2 provides the evaluation method: find an antiderivative F and compute F(b) - F(a). This directly gives the net change in F over [a, b], which is the foundation of the Net Change Theorem.
A student's typing speed improves over a semester at rate W'(t) = 2 - 0.1t words per minute per week. If initial speed is 30 wpm, what is the speed after 10 weeks?
W(10) = 30 + integral from 0 to 10 of (2 - 0.1t) dt = 30 + [2t - 0.05t^2] from 0 to 10 = 30 + (20 - 5) = 30 + 15 = 45 wpm.

--- Lesson 8.2: Area Between Curves (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Area Between Curves
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'intersection point' in the context of area between curves?
Intersection points are where the two curves meet: f(x) = g(x). These points define the boundaries of the enclosed region and serve as the limits of integration.
What is 'right curve minus left curve' when integrating with respect to y?
When integrating with respect to y, the integrand is (x_right - x_left) where x_right is the curve farther to the right at each y-value. This gives the width of horizontal slices through the region.
What is a 'representative rectangle' for area between curves?
A representative rectangle is a thin strip (vertical with width dx or horizontal with width dy) whose height or length equals the distance between the curves. Summing all such rectangles (via integration) gives the total area.
What is a 'vertical slice' method for area?
The vertical slice method uses thin rectangles of width dx at each x. The height of each rectangle is f(x) - g(x) (top minus bottom). Area = integral from a to b of [f(x) - g(x)] dx.
What is a 'horizontal slice' method for area?
The horizontal slice method uses thin rectangles of width dy at each y. The length of each rectangle is x_right(y) - x_left(y). Area = integral from c to d of [x_right - x_left] dy.
The cross-section of a valley is modeled by the curves y = x^2/4 (valley floor) and y = 8 (surface level of a proposed reservoir). What is the cross-sectional area of water if the valley is filled?
x^2/4 = 8 at x = +/-4sqrt(2). Area = 2 integral from 0 to 4sqrt(2) of (8-x^2/4) dx = 2[8x-x^3/12] from 0 to 4sqrt(2) = 2[32sqrt(2) - (128sqrt(2))/12] = 2[32sqrt(2) - 32sqrt(2)/3] = 2 * 64sqrt(2)/3 = 128sqrt(2)/3.

    [*] 9/2
    [ ] 6 (an integer)
    [ ] 4 (a square)
    [ ] 3 (an integer)

  Q7. Find the area between y = x^3 and y = x from x = -1 to x = 1.
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 0

--- Lesson 8.3: Volume of Solids of Revolution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Volume of Solids of Revolution
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'disk method' for finding volumes of revolution?
The disk method slices the solid into thin circular disks perpendicular to the axis. Each disk has radius f(x) and thickness dx, giving volume pi*[f(x)]^2 dx. Summing via integration gives the total volume.
What is the 'washer method'?
The washer method extends the disk method to regions with a hole. Each cross-section is a washer (annulus) with outer radius R(x) and inner radius r(x). The volume is pi * integral of (R^2 - r^2) dx.
What is a 'solid of revolution'?
A solid of revolution is created by rotating a planar region 360 degrees about a line. Examples include spheres (semicircle rotated), cones (triangle rotated), and cylinders.
What is the 'axis of revolution'?
The axis of revolution is the line about which the region rotates. Common axes include the x-axis, y-axis, or other horizontal/vertical lines like y = -1 or x = 3.
What is the 'cross-sectional area' in the context of volumes?
A cross-sectional area A(x) is the area of a thin slice cut perpendicular to the axis at position x. The volume is found by integrating A(x) over the length of the solid: V = integral of A(x) dx.
What is the 'shell method'?
The shell method uses thin cylindrical shells of radius x, height f(x), and thickness dx. Each shell has volume 2pi*x*f(x)*dx. This method is especially useful when integrating with respect to a variable parallel to the axis.
What is meant by 'volume by known cross-sections'?
Volume by known cross-sections is a general method: V = integral of A(x) dx, where A(x) can be any shape (square, equilateral triangle, semicircle, etc.) as long as the area formula is known.
What is the 'general volume formula' using cross-sections?
The general volume formula V = integral of A(x) dx works for any solid whose cross-sectional area A(x) is a known function of position x. Both the disk/washer methods and known cross-section problems use this idea.
An engineer designs a funnel by revolving y = 1/x from x = 1 to x = 4 around the x-axis, then capping both ends. What is the interior volume?
V = pi * integral from 1 to 4 of (1/x)^2 dx = pi[-1/x] from 1 to 4 = pi(-1/4 + 1) = 3pi/4.
A wine glass is modeled by revolving y = x^3 from x = 0 to x = 2 around the y-axis. What is the volume?
Using the shell method around y-axis: V = 2pi * integral from 0 to 2 of x * x^3 dx = 2pi[x^5/5] from 0 to 2 = 2pi(32/5) = 64pi/5.
Real-World Applications
Example: A storage silo is formed by revolving the region between y = 10 - x^2 and y = 0 (for x >= 0) around the y-axis. Find the volume.
x = 0 to x = sqrt(10). Using shells: V = 2pi * integral from 0 to sqrt(10) of x(10-x^2) dx = 2pi * integral of (10x-x^3) dx = 2pi[5x^2-x^4/4] from 0 to sqrt(10) = 2pi(50-25) = 50pi.

--- Lesson 8.4: Arc Length and Surface Area (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Arc Length and Surface Area
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the arc length formula for a function y = f(x) from x = a to x = b?
The arc length of a smooth curve y = f(x) on [a, b] is given by L = integral from a to b of sqrt(1 + [f'(x)]^2) dx. This formula derives from the Pythagorean theorem applied to infinitesimal segments of the curve.
If a curve has a constant slope of 0 (i.e., y = c), what is its arc length from x = a to x = b?
For y = c (a horizontal line), f'(x) = 0, so the integrand is sqrt(1 + 0) = 1. The arc length = integral from a to b of 1 dx = b - a. The arc length of a horizontal line segment equals its horizontal extent.
What is arc length in the context of calculus?
Arc length is the total distance measured along the curve itself between two points. Unlike the straight-line (chord) distance, arc length accounts for all bends and turns in the curve.
What is a surface of revolution?
A surface of revolution is a three-dimensional surface created by rotating a two-dimensional curve around a fixed axis (typically the x-axis or y-axis). Common examples include spheres, cones, and tori.
What is the Pythagorean theorem's role in deriving the arc length formula?
The arc length formula is derived by approximating small pieces of the curve as straight-line segments. The Pythagorean theorem gives the length of each segment as sqrt(dx^2 + dy^2), and summing these infinitesimal lengths via integration yields the total arc length.
What is meant by the 'arc length parameterization' of a curve?
Arc length parameterization uses the arc length s measured from a starting point as the parameter for the curve. This means the speed along the curve is always 1 (|dr/ds| = 1), making it a natural parameterization useful in differential geometry.
What is the difference between arc length and displacement?
Arc length measures the total distance traveled along the curve and is always non-negative. Displacement is the straight-line (vector) distance from the starting point to the ending point. Arc length is always greater than or equal to the magnitude of displacement.
An engineer designs a curved ramp described by y = x^(3/2)/3 from x = 0 to x = 5 meters. What is the total length of the ramp surface along the curve?
f'(x) = (1/2)*x^(1/2), so [f'(x)]^2 = x/4 (note: f(x) = x^(3/2)/3, so f'(x) = (3/2)x^(1/2)/3 = x^(1/2)/2). Wait -- actually f'(x) = (1/2)*sqrt(x), so [f'(x)]^2 = x/4. The arc length = integral from 0 to 5 of sqrt(1 + x/4) dx. Let u = 1 + x/4, du = dx/4. L = 4 * (2/3)u^(3/2) from u=1 to u=1+5/4 = (8/3)[(9/4)^(3/2) - 1] = (8/3)(27/8 - 1) = (8/3)(19/8) = 19/3. Checking: (8/27)[(1+9(5)/4)^(3/2)-1] uses a different derivative. With f'(x) = (1/2)x^(1/2), [f']^2 = x/4. L = integral sqrt(1+x/4)dx from 0 to 5 = (8/3)[(9/4)^(3/2)-1] = (8/3)(27/8-1) = (8/3)(19/8) = 19/3 meters.
A ski jump has the profile y = 20 - x^2/5 for 0 <= x <= 10 meters. What is the arc length of the ski jump surface?
f(x) = 20 - x^2/5, so f'(x) = -2x/5 and [f'(x)]^2 = 4x^2/25. The arc length = integral from 0 to 10 of sqrt(1 + 4x^2/25) dx. This integral can be evaluated using a trigonometric substitution or numerically.
A decorative bowl is created by revolving y = sqrt(x) from x = 0 to x = 4 about the x-axis. What is the surface area of the bowl's interior?
f(x) = sqrt(x), f'(x) = 1/(2*sqrt(x)), so [f']^2 = 1/(4x). The surface area = 2*pi * integral from 0 to 4 of sqrt(x) * sqrt(1 + 1/(4x)) dx = 2*pi * integral of sqrt(x + 1/4) dx. With u = x + 1/4: S = 2*pi * (2/3)u^(3/2) from 1/4 to 17/4 = (4*pi/3)[(17/4)^(3/2) - (1/4)^(3/2)] = (4*pi/3)[(17*sqrt(17) - 1)/8] = (pi/6)(17*sqrt(17) - 1).

--- Lesson 8.5: Applications to Physics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Applications to Physics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the work-energy theorem?
The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy: W_net = (1/2)mv_f^2 - (1/2)mv_i^2. This connects the integral of force over distance to the object's motion.
What is the spring constant in Hooke's Law?
The spring constant k measures the stiffness of the spring. A larger k means the spring is stiffer and requires more force to stretch or compress by the same amount. Its units are force per length (e.g., N/m).
What is potential energy in the context of physics applications of integration?
Potential energy is energy stored in an object due to its position or configuration. For example, gravitational PE = mgh and elastic PE = (1/2)kx^2. The work done against a conservative force equals the gain in potential energy.
What is the unit of work in the SI system?
The SI unit of work is the joule (J). One joule equals one newton-meter (N*m), which is the work done when a force of one newton moves an object one meter in the direction of the force.
What is the difference between 'work' and 'power' in physics?
Work measures the total energy transferred by a force over a distance (W = integral of F dx, measured in joules). Power is the rate at which work is done (P = dW/dt, measured in watts). Power tells you how quickly energy is being transferred.
A rocket burns fuel at a rate that decreases its mass. If the thrust force is a constant 5000 N and the rocket moves from x = 0 to x = 100 m in a straight line, what is the work done by the thrust?
Since the thrust force is constant at 5000 N, W = integral from 0 to 100 of 5000 dx = 5000 * 100 = 500,000 J. The changing mass affects acceleration but not the work calculation when force is given directly.
A car accelerates from rest with velocity v(t) = 3t^2 m/s. What is the total distance traveled in the first 4 seconds?
Since v(t) = 3t^2 >= 0 for all t >= 0, total distance = integral from 0 to 4 of 3t^2 dt = [t^3] from 0 to 4 = 64 m. Because velocity is always non-negative, distance equals displacement.
A ball is thrown upward with initial velocity 20 m/s. Ignoring air resistance, its velocity is v(t) = 20 - 9.8t m/s. What is the maximum height reached?
The ball reaches max height when v(t) = 0, giving t = 20/9.8 approximately 2.04 s. Height = integral from 0 to 20/9.8 of (20 - 9.8t) dt = [20t - 4.9t^2] from 0 to 2.04 = 20(2.04) - 4.9(2.04)^2 = 40.8 - 20.4 = 20.4 m.
A particle moves along the x-axis with velocity v(t) = sin(pi*t) m/s. What is the total distance traveled from t = 0 to t = 2?
v(t) = sin(pi*t) is positive on (0,1) and negative on (1,2). Total distance = integral from 0 to 1 of sin(pi*t) dt + integral from 1 to 2 of |sin(pi*t)| dt = [-cos(pi*t)/pi] from 0 to 1 + [cos(pi*t)/pi] from 1 to 2 = (1/pi + 1/pi) + (1/pi + 1/pi) = 4/pi m.

A spring has a natural length of 20 cm. It takes 12 J of work to stretch it to 26 cm. What is the spring constant?
The stretch is 0.06 m. Work = (1/2)k*x^2, so 12 = (1/2)k(0.06)^2 = (1/2)k(0.0036). Therefore k = 24/0.0036 = 6667 N/m (approximately).
If the work required to stretch a spring 0.2 m from natural length is 4 J, what is the spring constant k?
W = (1/2)kx^2, so 4 = (1/2)k(0.2)^2 = (1/2)k(0.04) = 0.02k. Therefore k = 4/0.02 = 200 N/m.
What is the elastic potential energy stored in a spring compressed 0.3 m with spring constant k = 500 N/m?
Elastic PE = (1/2)kx^2 = (1/2)(500)(0.3)^2 = (1/2)(500)(0.09) = 22.5 J. This equals the work done to compress the spring by 0.3 m.
A force F(x) = 12 - 2x N acts on an object from x = 0 to x = 6. What is the net work done?
W = integral from 0 to 6 of (12 - 2x) dx = [12x - x^2] from 0 to 6 = (72 - 36) - 0 = 36 J.
What is 'work' in the physics sense as computed by integration?
In physics, work is defined as the integral of force with respect to displacement: W = integral of F(x) dx. For a constant force, this reduces to W = F * d. Work represents the energy transferred by a force.
What is the 'work-energy theorem'?
The work-energy theorem states W_net = delta KE = (1/2)mv_f^2 - (1/2)mv_i^2. The net work done on an object by all forces equals the change in the object's kinetic energy.
What is 'gravitational potential energy'?
Gravitational potential energy (near Earth's surface) is the energy stored due to an object's height in a gravitational field: PE = mgh. It equals the work done against gravity to lift the object to height h from a reference level.
What is the meaning of 'foot-pound' as a unit?
A foot-pound (ft-lb) is the British/imperial unit of work or energy. It is the amount of work done when a force of one pound moves an object a distance of one foot in the direction of the force. It is analogous to the newton-meter (joule) in SI.
What is 'power' in the context of work and energy?
Power is the rate at which work is done: P = dW/dt. It can also be expressed as P = F*v (force times velocity). The SI unit is the watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.
A 500-kg wrecking ball swings down from a height of 4 m. Using conservation of energy (g = 9.8 m/s^2), what is its speed at the bottom of the swing?
By conservation of energy, mgh = (1/2)mv^2, so v = sqrt(2gh) = sqrt(2*9.8*4) = sqrt(78.4) = 8.85 m/s (approximately). Mass cancels, so the speed is independent of the mass of the wrecking ball.

Real-World Applications
Example: A factory conveyor belt moves packages using a force F(x) = 20*e^(-x/5) N. How much work is done moving a package from x = 0 to x = 10 m?

--- Lesson 8.7: Fluid Pressure and Force (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Fluid Pressure and Force
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the hydrostatic force on a rectangular plate 3 ft wide submerged vertically with its top edge at the water surface and its bottom edge at 5 ft depth?
F = 62.4 * integral from 0 to 5 of 3h dh = 62.4 * 3 * [h^2/2] from 0 to 5 = 187.2 * 12.5 = 2340 lb.
What is 'hydrostatic pressure'?
Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid at rest due to gravity. At a depth h below the surface, the hydrostatic pressure is P = rho*g*h (or P = w*h where w is weight density). It increases linearly with depth.
What is 'pressure' in physics?
Pressure is defined as force per unit area: P = F/A. Its SI unit is the pascal (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m^2. In fluid mechanics, pressure at a point acts equally in all directions.
What is the 'centroid' of a submerged plate and why is it relevant to fluid force?
The centroid is the geometric center of the plate. For a flat plate submerged in a fluid, the total hydrostatic force equals the pressure at the centroid multiplied by the plate's area: F = (w * h_centroid) * A. This is a useful shortcut for uniform-density fluids.
What is a 'pascal' as a unit of pressure?
A pascal (Pa) is the SI unit of pressure, defined as one newton per square meter (1 Pa = 1 N/m^2). Standard atmospheric pressure is approximately 101,325 Pa.
A rectangular swimming pool is 10 m long, 4 m wide, and 3 m deep. What is the hydrostatic force on one of the 10 m by 3 m walls? (Use water weight density = 9800 N/m^3.)
F = 9800 * integral from 0 to 3 of 10*h dh = 98,000 * [h^2/2] from 0 to 3 = 98,000 * 4.5 = 441,000 N.
A triangular gate in a dam has its vertex at the water surface and base of 8 ft at a depth of 6 ft. What is the hydrostatic force on the gate? (w = 62.4 lb/ft^3)
The width at depth h is (8/6)*h = 4h/3 (by similar triangles, 0 at vertex/surface, 8 at depth 6). F = 62.4 * integral from 0 to 6 of h*(4h/3) dh = 62.4*(4/3) * integral from 0 to 6 of h^2 dh = 83.2 * [h^3/3] from 0 to 6 = 83.2 * 72 = 5990.4 lb.

At depth h, the width of the semicircle is 2*sqrt(r^2 - h^2) = 2*sqrt(4 - h^2). The force = 7840 * integral from 0 to 2 of h * 2*sqrt(4 - h^2) dh. Using substitution u = 4 - h^2, this integral evaluates to 7840*(2/3)(4)^(3/2) = 7840*16/3.

    Explanation: For a horizontal plate, the pressure is constant across the surface: P = 62.4*h. The force is F = P * A = 62.4*h*A. Integration is not needed because the depth is the same everywhere on a horizontal plate.

    [ ] Because the plate is curved

    Explanation: On a vertical plate, different horizontal strips are at different depths, so each strip experiences a different pressure. Integration sums the forces on all these infinitesimally thin horizontal strips to find the total hydrostatic force.

--- Lesson 8.8: Probability Distributions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Probability Distributions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If f(x) = 2x for 0 <= x <= 1 and f(x) = 0 otherwise, what is P(0.5 <= X <= 1)?
P(0.5 <= X <= 1) = integral from 0.5 to 1 of 2x dx = [x^2] from 0.5 to 1 = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75.
If f(x) = 3x^2 for 0 <= x <= 1, what is E(X)?
E(X) = integral from 0 to 1 of x * 3x^2 dx = integral from 0 to 1 of 3x^3 dx = 3[x^4/4] from 0 to 1 = 3/4.
If f(x) = (1/4)e^(-x/4) for x >= 0, what is P(X > 8)?
P(X > 8) = integral from 8 to infinity of (1/4)e^(-x/4) dx = [-e^(-x/4)] from 8 to infinity = 0 - (-e^(-2)) = e^(-2). This is an exponential distribution with mean 4.
For the pdf f(x) = 6x(1-x) on [0, 1], what is P(X < 0.5)?
P(X < 0.5) = integral from 0 to 0.5 of 6x(1-x) dx = 6 * integral of (x - x^2) dx = 6[x^2/2 - x^3/3] from 0 to 0.5 = 6(0.125 - 0.04167) = 6(0.08333) = 0.5. This pdf is symmetric about x = 0.5.
What is a probability density function (pdf)?
A probability density function (pdf) describes the relative likelihood of a continuous random variable taking on given values. The probability of the variable falling in a range [a, b] is the integral of the pdf from a to b. The pdf must be non-negative and integrate to 1 over its entire domain.
What is a cumulative distribution function (CDF)?
The CDF F(x) = P(X <= x) gives the probability that the random variable takes a value less than or equal to x. It is obtained by integrating the pdf from -infinity to x. It is non-decreasing, starting at 0 and approaching 1.
What is an 'exponential distribution'?
The exponential distribution has pdf f(x) = (1/lambda)*e^(-x/lambda) for x >= 0, where lambda is the mean. It is commonly used to model time between events in a Poisson process, such as waiting times. Its mean and standard deviation are both lambda.
What is a 'uniform distribution'?
A uniform distribution on [a, b] assigns equal probability density to all values in the interval: f(x) = 1/(b-a). The mean is (a+b)/2, the midpoint. Examples include randomly choosing a real number between a and b.
What is the 'mode' of a continuous probability distribution?
The mode is the value of x at which the pdf reaches its maximum value. It represents the most likely value (highest probability density). A distribution can have more than one mode (bimodal, multimodal) or no mode.
The lifetime of a lightbulb follows an exponential distribution with a mean of 1000 hours. What is the probability that a randomly selected bulb lasts more than 1500 hours?
For an exponential distribution with mean lambda = 1000, the pdf is f(x) = (1/1000)*e^(-x/1000). P(X > 1500) = integral from 1500 to infinity of (1/1000)*e^(-x/1000) dx = e^(-1500/1000) = e^(-1.5) = approximately 0.2231.
A bus arrives at a stop uniformly at random between 8:00 and 8:30 AM. What is the probability that a commuter arriving at 8:10 waits less than 5 minutes?
Given the commuter arrives at 8:10, the bus must arrive between 8:10 and 8:15 for a wait of less than 5 minutes. But the bus arrives uniformly between 8:00 and 8:30. Given that the bus has not yet arrived by 8:10, we need P(bus arrives in [8:10, 8:15] | bus arrives in [8:10, 8:30]) = 5/20 = 1/4.
The time (in minutes) a customer spends on hold follows an exponential distribution with mean 5 minutes. A customer service manager wants to ensure that 90% of callers wait at most T minutes. What is T?
We need P(X <= T) = 0.9. For exponential with mean 5: P(X <= T) = 1 - e^(-T/5) = 0.9, so e^(-T/5) = 0.1, giving -T/5 = ln(0.1) = -ln(10). Therefore T = 5*ln(10) = approximately 11.51 minutes.
An online store models daily revenue (in thousands of dollars) with pdf f(x) = (3/8)x^2 for 0 <= x <= 2. What is the expected daily revenue?
E(X) = integral from 0 to 2 of x * (3/8)x^2 dx = (3/8) * integral from 0 to 2 of x^3 dx = (3/8)[x^4/4] from 0 to 2 = (3/8)(4) = 3/2 = 1.5 (thousands). So the expected daily revenue is $1,500.
A medical researcher finds that the duration (in days) of a certain illness follows a distribution with pdf f(t) = (1/9)t*e^(-t/3) for t >= 0. What is the expected duration of the illness?
This is a Gamma distribution with shape parameter 2 and rate parameter 1/3. The mean is shape * scale = 2 * 3 = 6 days. Equivalently, E(T) = integral from 0 to infinity of t * (1/9)*t*e^(-t/3) dt = (1/9) * integral of t^2*e^(-t/3) dt = (1/9)*2*(3)^3 = (1/9)*54 = 6.
A machine component has a failure time modeled by the pdf f(t) = 2e^(-2t) for t >= 0 (time in years). What is the probability the component survives past 1 year?
P(T > 1) = integral from 1 to infinity of 2e^(-2t) dt = [-e^(-2t)] from 1 to infinity = 0 - (-e^(-2)) = e^(-2) = approximately 0.1353. This exponential distribution has mean 1/2 year, so surviving past 1 year is relatively unlikely.
A teacher models test scores with the pdf f(x) = (1/50)(x - 50) for 50 <= x <= 60 and f(x) = (1/50)(70 - x) for 60 <= x <= 70. What is the median score?
This is a triangular distribution symmetric about x = 60. By symmetry, P(X <= 60) = 0.5, so the median is 60. The pdf forms a triangle with peak at x = 60 and base from 50 to 70.
A weather station records daily rainfall amounts following f(x) = 3(1-x)^2 for 0 <= x <= 1 (in inches). What is the probability of getting more than half an inch of rain?
P(X > 0.5) = integral from 0.5 to 1 of 3(1-x)^2 dx = 3 * [-(1-x)^3/3] from 0.5 to 1 = [-(1-x)^3] from 0.5 to 1 = (0 - (-1/8)) = 1/8.

Example: A pizza delivery company promises delivery within 30 minutes. Delivery times follow a distribution with pdf f(t) = (1/25)(10-t) for 20 <= t <= 30 minutes. What percentage of deliveries arrive late (after 30 minutes)?
Since the pdf is zero for t > 30 (the distribution only spans [20, 30]), P(T > 30) = 0. This means 0% of deliveries are late. The distribution is defined to have no probability mass beyond 30 minutes.
Example: A teacher models test scores with the pdf f(x) = (1/50)(x - 50) for 50 <= x <= 60 and f(x) = (1/50)(70 - x) for 60 <= x <= 70. What is the median score?

--- Lesson 8.9: More Applications of Integration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: More Applications of Integration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'average value' of a function over an interval?
The average value of f on [a, b] is the constant c such that c*(b-a) = integral from a to b of f(x) dx. In other words, a rectangle of height c and width (b-a) has the same area as the region under f(x). It is computed as (1/(b-a)) * integral of f(x) dx.
What is 'center of mass' in the context of integration?
The center of mass is the balance point of an object. For a one-dimensional object with variable density rho(x), it is found using integration: x_bar = integral of x*rho(x) dx / integral of rho(x) dx. It accounts for how mass is distributed.
What is a 'Riemann sum'?
A Riemann sum approximates a definite integral by dividing the interval into subintervals and summing the areas of rectangles whose heights are determined by the function value at sample points. As the number of rectangles increases, the Riemann sum approaches the integral.
What is the 'moment' of a mass distribution about the origin?
The first moment about the origin is M = integral of x * rho(x) dx. It measures the tendency of the mass distribution to rotate about the origin. The center of mass is the moment divided by total mass.
What is the 'Trapezoidal Rule'?
The Trapezoidal Rule approximates a definite integral by dividing the interval into subintervals and using trapezoids (rather than rectangles) to approximate the area. It generally provides better accuracy than basic Riemann sums for the same number of subintervals.
A city's temperature over a 12-hour day (6 AM to 6 PM) follows T(t) = 60 + 15*sin(pi*t/12) degrees F, where t is hours after 6 AM. What is the average temperature during this period?
T_avg = (1/12) * integral from 0 to 12 of [60 + 15*sin(pi*t/12)] dt = (1/12)[60t - 15*(12/pi)*cos(pi*t/12)] from 0 to 12 = (1/12)[720 - (180/pi)(-1 - 1)] = (1/12)[720 + 360/pi] = 60 + 30/pi degrees F.
A population of bacteria grows so that the rate of growth is r(t) = 500*e^(0.1t) bacteria per hour. If the initial population is 2000, what is the population after 10 hours?
Population at time 10 = initial + integral from 0 to 10 of 500*e^(0.1t) dt = 2000 + 500[10*e^(0.1t)] from 0 to 10 = 2000 + 5000(e^1 - e^0) = 2000 + 5000(e - 1).
An engineer uses the Trapezoidal Rule with n = 4 to estimate the integral of f(x) from x = 0 to x = 4, using data points f(0) = 1, f(1) = 3, f(2) = 2, f(3) = 4, f(4) = 1. What is the estimate?
With h = 1 and n = 4: T = (1/2)[f(0) + 2f(1) + 2f(2) + 2f(3) + f(4)] = (1/2)[1 + 6 + 4 + 8 + 1] = (1/2)(20) = 10.
A farmer measures the width of an irregularly shaped field at 10-meter intervals along a 60-meter baseline. The widths are: 0, 12, 18, 22, 20, 14, 0 meters. Using Simpson's Rule, what is the approximate area of the field?
With h = 10 and n = 6 (even), Simpson's Rule gives: S = (10/3)[0 + 4(12) + 2(18) + 4(22) + 2(20) + 4(14) + 0] = (10/3)[0 + 48 + 36 + 88 + 40 + 56 + 0] = (10/3)(268) = 2680/3 = 893.3 m^2. Let me recheck: (10/3)[f(0) + 4f(10) + 2f(20) + 4f(30) + 2f(40) + 4f(50) + f(60)] = (10/3)[0 + 48 + 36 + 88 + 40 + 56 + 0] = (10/3)(268) = 893.3. Closest to 913.3 in options.
A chemical reaction produces heat at a rate H(t) = 100*e^(-0.5t) joules per minute. What is the total heat produced in the first 4 minutes?
Total heat = integral from 0 to 4 of 100*e^(-0.5t) dt = 100[-2*e^(-0.5t)] from 0 to 4 = -200(e^(-2) - 1) = 200(1 - e^(-2)) J.
A factory produces a product at rate P(t) = 200 - 4t units per hour. If the factory operates from t = 0 to t = 10 hours, what is the average production rate?
Average rate = (1/10) * integral from 0 to 10 of (200 - 4t) dt = (1/10) * [200t - 2t^2] from 0 to 10 = (1/10)(2000 - 200) = (1/10)(1800) = 180 units per hour.

--- Lesson 8.10: Net Change and Accumulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Change and Accumulation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
The acceleration of a car is a(t) = 6 m/s^2 (constant). If v(0) = 10 m/s, what is the velocity at t = 5?
v(5) = v(0) + integral from 0 to 5 of 6 dt = 10 + 6(5) = 10 + 30 = 40 m/s.
What is the 'Net Change Theorem'?
The Net Change Theorem states that integral from a to b of F'(x) dx = F(b) - F(a). In other words, integrating the rate of change of a quantity over an interval yields the total (net) change in that quantity over that interval.
What is 'net displacement' vs. 'total distance'?
Net displacement = integral of v(t) dt (can be positive, negative, or zero, as it accounts for direction). Total distance = integral of |v(t)| dt (always non-negative, counting all movement regardless of direction). Total distance >= |net displacement|.
What is the 'Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 1'?
FTC Part 1 states that if f is continuous on [a, b] and g(x) = integral from a to x of f(t) dt, then g is differentiable and g'(x) = f(x). This theorem establishes that differentiation and integration are inverse processes.
An online store's revenue grows at a rate of R'(t) = 2000*e^(0.05t) dollars per month. If the initial cumulative revenue is $0, what is the total revenue after 12 months?
Total revenue = integral from 0 to 12 of 2000*e^(0.05t) dt = 2000[20*e^(0.05t)] from 0 to 12 = 40,000(e^(0.6) - 1) dollars.

--- Lesson 8.11: Net Change and Accumulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Change and Accumulation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
The rate of temperature change in a room is T'(t) = -2 + 0.5t degrees per hour. If T(0) = 72 degrees F, what is the temperature at t = 4?
T(4) = 72 + integral from 0 to 4 of (-2 + 0.5t) dt = 72 + [-2t + 0.25t^2] from 0 to 4 = 72 + (-8 + 4) = 72 - 4 = 68 degrees F.
What is 'marginal cost' and how does it relate to integration?
Marginal cost C'(x) is the cost of producing one additional unit (the derivative of total cost). By the Net Change Theorem, integral from a to b of C'(x) dx = C(b) - C(a), the total cost of producing units a through b.
What is the 'additive property' of definite integrals?
The additive property states that for any point c between a and b: integral from a to c of f(x) dx + integral from c to b of f(x) dx = integral from a to b of f(x) dx. This allows splitting integrals at convenient points.
What is a 'rate function' in applied calculus?
A rate function describes how a quantity changes over time (or another variable). Examples include velocity (position rate), growth rate, flow rate, and marginal cost. Integrating a rate function gives the total accumulation or net change.
A city's population changes at rate P'(t) = 200*cos(pi*t/6) people per month. Starting with 50,000 people, what is the population after 3 months?
P(3) = 50,000 + integral from 0 to 3 of 200*cos(pi*t/6) dt = 50,000 + 200*(6/pi)*sin(pi*t/6) from 0 to 3 = 50,000 + (1200/pi)(sin(pi/2) - sin(0)) = 50,000 + 1200/pi.
A financial account earns interest at a continuous rate of r(t) = 500*e^(0.03t) dollars per year. If the initial balance is $10,000, what is the balance after 5 years?
Balance = 10,000 + integral from 0 to 5 of 500*e^(0.03t) dt = 10,000 + 500*(1/0.03)*[e^(0.03t)] from 0 to 5 = 10,000 + (500/0.03)(e^(0.15) - 1).
A train travels with velocity v(t) = 20 + 5*sin(t) m/s. What is the net displacement in the interval [0, 2*pi]?
Displacement = integral from 0 to 2*pi of (20 + 5*sin(t)) dt = [20t - 5*cos(t)] from 0 to 2*pi = (40*pi - 5) - (0 - 5) = 40*pi. The sine term integrates to zero over a full period.
A car accelerates from rest with acceleration a(t) = 12 - 2t m/s^2. What is the car's maximum velocity, and when does it occur?
v(t) = integral of a(t) dt = 12t - t^2 + C. Since v(0) = 0, C = 0. Maximum velocity occurs when a(t) = 0: t = 6. v(6) = 12(6) - 36 = 72 - 36 = 36 m/s.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student studies at a rate of L(t) = 10*e^(-0.2t) pages per hour. How many pages does the student read in 5 hours?
Pages = integral from 0 to 5 of 10*e^(-0.2t) dt = 10[-5*e^(-0.2t)] from 0 to 5 = -50(e^(-1) - 1) = 50(1 - e^(-1)) = approximately 31.6 pages.

--- Lesson 8.12: Net Change and Accumulation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Change and Accumulation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A function satisfies f'(x) = sqrt(x + 1) and f(0) = 2. What is f(3)?
f(3) = 2 + integral from 0 to 3 of sqrt(x+1) dx. Let u = x+1: integral = (2/3)(x+1)^(3/2) from 0 to 3 = (2/3)(8 - 1) = 14/3. So f(3) = 2 + 14/3 = 20/3.
What is the 'definite integral' of a function?
A definite integral has specified upper and lower limits [a, b] and evaluates to a number: integral from a to b of f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a). It represents the net signed area between the curve and the x-axis.
What is 'marginal analysis' in economics?
Marginal analysis uses calculus to study incremental changes. The marginal cost C'(x), marginal revenue R'(x), and marginal profit P'(x) are derivatives that represent per-unit rates. Integrating these marginals gives total changes in cost, revenue, or profit.
A patient receives medication intravenously at rate R_in(t) = 20 mg/hr. The body metabolizes the drug at rate R_out(t) = 0.4*C(t) mg/hr, where C(t) is the current amount. If C(0) = 0 and after a long time the drug level approaches equilibrium, what is the equilibrium level?
At equilibrium, R_in = R_out: 20 = 0.4*C, so C = 50 mg. The net rate dC/dt = 20 - 0.4C = 0 at equilibrium. This is the steady-state drug level.
A river's flow rate past a measuring station varies as Q(t) = 1000 + 500*sin(pi*t/6) cubic feet per second, where t is in hours. What is the total volume of water that flows past in 12 hours?
Volume = integral from 0 to 12 of Q(t) * 3600 dt (converting hours to seconds for the rate). Actually, since Q is in ft^3/s and t is in hours, we need: Volume = integral from 0 to 12 of Q(t) dt * 3600 s/hr. Integral of (1000 + 500sin(pi*t/6)) from 0 to 12 = [1000t - 500*(6/pi)*cos(pi*t/6)] from 0 to 12 = 12,000 - (3000/pi)(cos(2pi) - cos(0)) = 12,000 - 0 = 12,000 ft^3*hr. Converting: 12,000 * 3600 = 43,200,000 cubic feet.
A balloon is inflated so its volume increases at a rate V'(t) = 12t - t^2 cm^3/s. Starting from empty, what is the maximum volume the balloon reaches before it starts deflating?
V'(t) = 0 when 12t - t^2 = 0, so t(12 - t) = 0, giving t = 12 (deflation starts). V(12) = integral from 0 to 12 of (12t - t^2) dt = [6t^2 - t^3/3] from 0 to 12 = 864 - 576 = 288 cm^3.
A taxi charges at a rate of C'(d) = 2 + 0.5/(d+1) dollars per mile, where d is distance in miles. What is the total fare for a 4-mile ride if the base fare is $3?
Fare = 3 + integral from 0 to 4 of [2 + 0.5/(d+1)] dd = 3 + [2d + 0.5*ln(d+1)] from 0 to 4 = 3 + (8 + 0.5*ln(5)) - 0 = 3 + 8 + 0.5*ln(5) dollars, which is approximately $11.80.
A cyclist's speed varies as v(t) = 15 - 3|t - 3| mph for 0 <= t <= 6 hours. What is the total distance traveled?
Since v(t) >= 0 for all t in [0, 6] (minimum is v(0) = v(6) = 6), total distance = integral from 0 to 6 of v(t) dt. Split at t = 3: integral from 0 to 3 of (15 - 3(3-t)) dt + integral from 3 to 6 of (15 - 3(t-3)) dt = integral from 0 to 3 of (6 + 3t) dt + integral from 3 to 6 of (24 - 3t) dt = [6t + 3t^2/2] from 0 to 3 + [24t - 3t^2/2] from 3 to 6 = (18 + 13.5) + (144 - 54 - 72 + 13.5) = 31.5 + 31.5 = 63 miles.
A dam controls water release. During a 10-hour flood event, inflow is R_in(t) = 500 + 100*sin(pi*t/5) ft^3/s and outflow is set to a constant 550 ft^3/s. What is the net change in reservoir volume over the 10 hours (in ft^3)?
Net rate = (500 + 100sin(pi*t/5)) - 550 = -50 + 100sin(pi*t/5) ft^3/s. Integral from 0 to 10 of [-50 + 100sin(pi*t/5)] dt = [-50t - 100*(5/pi)*cos(pi*t/5)] from 0 to 10 = (-500 - (500/pi)(cos(2pi) - cos(0))) = -500 - 0 = -500 ft^3*hr. Converting: but t is in hours and rates are ft^3/s, so we need to convert: -500 ft^3*hr... Actually the net rate integral in hours gives -500 ft^3/s * hr. Converting to ft^3: -500 * 3600 = -1,800,000 ft^3.
Real-World Applications
Example: A company's daily sales rate follows S(t) = 500 - 20t + t^2 units per day for a 10-day period. What are the total units sold?
Total = integral from 0 to 10 of (500 - 20t + t^2) dt = [500t - 10t^2 + t^3/3] from 0 to 10 = 5000 - 1000 + 1000/3 = 4000 + 333.3 = 4333.3 units (approximately 4333).
