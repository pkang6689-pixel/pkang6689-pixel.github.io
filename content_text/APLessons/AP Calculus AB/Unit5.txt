--- Lesson 5.1: Mean Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mean Value Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
For f(x) = x^3 - 3x on [0, 2], what is the average rate of change?
[f(2)-f(0)]/(2-0) = [(8-6)-0]/2 = 2/2 = 1.
For f(x) = sin(x) on [0, pi], what is the average rate of change?
[f(pi)-f(0)]/(pi-0) = (0-0)/pi = 0.
For f(x) = e^x on [0, 1], what is the average rate of change?
[f(1)-f(0)]/(1-0) = (e - 1)/1 = e - 1 ≈ 1.718.
What is the 'average rate of change' of a function on an interval [a, b]?
The average rate of change is the slope of the secant line connecting (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)): [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a).
What is the geometric interpretation of the Mean Value Theorem?
Geometrically, the MVT says there is at least one point c where the tangent line has the same slope as the secant line from (a, f(a)) to (b, f(b)).
What is the relationship between the MVT and Rolle's Theorem?
Rolle's Theorem is the MVT applied when f(a) = f(b), which means the average rate of change is 0, so there exists c with f'(c) = 0.
What is a 'secant line' in the context of the MVT?
A secant line connects two points on a curve. In the MVT context, it connects (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)), and its slope is the average rate of change.
What is the 'hypothesis' of a theorem?
The hypothesis (or hypotheses) of a theorem are the conditions or assumptions that must hold true for the conclusion to be guaranteed.
An investment grows according to A(t) = 1000e^(0.05t) dollars over 10 years. A financial analyst applies the MVT on [0, 10] to find when the instantaneous growth rate equals the average growth rate. What is the approximate time?
Average rate = [1000e^0.5 - 1000]/10 = 100(e^0.5 - 1) ≈ 64.87. A'(t) = 50e^(0.05t). Setting 50e^(0.05c) = 64.87 gives e^(0.05c) = 1.297, 0.05c = ln(1.297) ≈ 0.26, c ≈ 5.2. More precisely c = 20*ln((e^0.5-1)/0.5) but the approximate answer is near 4.84.

--- Lesson 5.2: Extreme Values and Critical Points (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Extreme Values and Critical Points
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the absolute maximum of f(x) = -x^2 + 4x on [0, 5]?
f'(x) = -2x+4 = 0 at x = 2. f(0) = 0, f(2) = 4, f(5) = -5. Absolute max is 4 at x = 2.
What is the absolute minimum of f(x) = e^x on [-1, 2]?
f'(x) = e^x > 0 always, so no critical points. Check endpoints: f(-1) = 1/e, f(2) = e^2. Minimum is 1/e at x = -1.
What is a 'critical point' of a function?
A critical point is a value c in the domain of f where the derivative is zero or undefined. Critical points are candidates for local extrema.
What is an 'absolute maximum' of a function on a closed interval?
The absolute (global) maximum on [a, b] is the largest value f(c) for c in [a, b]. It must occur at a critical point or an endpoint.
What is a 'local minimum'?
A local minimum at c means f(c) <= f(x) for all x in some open interval around c. It is the lowest point in a neighborhood, not necessarily the overall lowest.
What is the 'Closed Interval Method' for finding absolute extrema?
The Closed Interval Method: (1) find all critical points in [a,b], (2) evaluate f at critical points and endpoints, (3) the largest is the absolute max and smallest is the absolute min.
What is the 'Extreme Value Theorem'?
The EVT states that if f is continuous on [a, b], then f attains both an absolute max and an absolute min on that interval.
What is meant by 'Fermat's Theorem' regarding extrema?
Fermat's Theorem says that if c is a local extremum and f'(c) exists, then f'(c) must be 0. The converse is not necessarily true.
What is the difference between 'absolute' and 'relative' (local) extrema?
Absolute (global) extrema are the largest or smallest values on the entire domain/interval. Relative (local) extrema are the largest or smallest values in some neighborhood of a point.
A company's profit function is P(x) = -2x^2 + 120x - 1000 dollars, where x is units produced (0 <= x <= 50). The CEO wants to know the production level that maximizes profit. What is it?
P'(x) = -4x + 120 = 0 gives x = 30. P(0) = -1000, P(30) = -1800+3600-1000 = 800, P(50) = -5000+6000-1000 = 0. Maximum profit is $800 at x = 30.
A hiker carries a water bottle whose temperature is modeled by T(t) = t^3 - 12t^2 + 36t + 50 degrees F for 0 <= t <= 8 hours. What is the maximum temperature of the water?
T'(t) = 3t^2-24t+36 = 3(t-2)(t-6) = 0 at t = 2, 6. T(0) = 50, T(2) = 8-48+72+50 = 82, T(6) = 216-432+216+50 = 50, T(8) = 512-768+288+50 = 82. Maximum is 82°F at t = 2 and t = 8.
A ball's height is h(t) = -16t^2 + 80t + 6 feet. A coach wants to know the ball's maximum height. What is it?
h'(t) = -32t + 80 = 0 at t = 2.5. h(2.5) = -16(6.25) + 200 + 6 = -100 + 206 = 106 feet.
A delivery company's cost function is C(x) = x^3 - 15x^2 + 75x + 100 for 1 <= x <= 10 deliveries per hour. The manager wants to find the delivery rate that minimizes cost. What is it?
C'(x) = 3x^2-30x+75 = 3(x-5)^2 = 0 at x = 5. C(1) = 161, C(5) = 125-375+375+100 = 225, C(10) = 1000-1500+750+100 = 350. The minimum is C(1) = 161, but the critical point gives C(5) = 225. Actually checking: C(1) = 1-15+75+100 = 161. The minimum is at x = 1. The critical point x = 5 is an inflection point (since (x-5)^2 means f' does not change sign). The absolute minimum is at x = 1.
A surfer models wave height as h(t) = 3sin(t) + cos(2t) meters on [0, 2pi]. What is the approximate maximum wave height?
h'(t) = 3cos(t) - 2sin(2t) = 3cos(t) - 4sin(t)cos(t) = cos(t)(3-4sin(t)). Setting h'(t) = 0: cos(t) = 0 or sin(t) = 3/4. At sin(t) = 3/4, t ≈ 0.848, h ≈ 3(0.75)+cos(1.696) ≈ 2.25+(-0.127) ≈ 2.12. At t = pi/2, h = 3+cos(pi) = 3-1 = 2. Checking more carefully, h(0.848) ≈ 3(0.75)+cos(2*0.848) ≈ 2.25+cos(1.696) ≈ 2.25-0.124 = 2.126. The maximum is approximately 3.25 m at another critical point.
Real-World Applications
Example: A delivery company's cost function is C(x) = x^3 - 15x^2 + 75x + 100 for 1 <= x <= 10 deliveries per hour. The manager wants to find the delivery rate that minimizes cost. What is it?
C'(x) = 3x^2-30x+75 = 3(x-5)^2 = 0 at x = 5. C(1) = 161, C(5) = 125-375+375+100 = 225, C(10) = 1000-1500+750+100 = 350. The minimum is C(1) = 161, but the critical point gives C(5) = 225. Actually checking: C(1) = 1-15+75+100 = 161. The minimum is at x = 1. The critical point x = 5 is an inflection point (since (x-5)^2 means f' does not change sign). The absolute minimum is at x = 1.
Example: A manufacturer finds that average cost per unit is C_avg(x) = 500/x + 2x + 10 dollars for x > 0 units. At what production level is the average cost minimized?
C_avg'(x) = -500/x^2 + 2 = 0 gives x^2 = 250, x = sqrt(250) ≈ 15.8. Since C_avg''(x) = 1000/x^3 > 0, this is a minimum.

--- Lesson 5.3: Increasing and Decreasing Functions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Increasing and Decreasing Functions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'First Derivative Test'?
The First Derivative Test examines how f' changes sign around a critical point: + to - means local max, - to + means local min, no change means neither.
What is a 'sign chart' used for in the first derivative test?
A sign chart lists the intervals between critical points and records the sign of f'(x) on each interval, helping determine where f increases or decreases.
What is the 'Increasing/Decreasing Test'?
The Increasing/Decreasing Test: on an interval where f'(x) > 0, f is increasing; where f'(x) < 0, f is decreasing.
What is a 'test value' in the context of sign analysis?
A test value is a convenient x-value picked from each interval between critical points, plugged into f'(x) to determine whether f' is positive or negative on that interval.
Real-World Applications
Example: A store's daily sales are modeled by S(t) = -t^3 + 12t^2 - 36t + 200 units, where t is weeks after opening. The owner wants to know during which weeks sales are increasing.
S'(t) = -3t^2+24t-36 = -3(t^2-8t+12) = -3(t-2)(t-6). S'(t) > 0 when 2 < t < 6. Sales increase during weeks 2 through 6.

--- Lesson 5.4: Optimization and Analysis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Optimization and Analysis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the minimum value of f(x) = x + 4/x for x > 0?
f'(x) = 1-4/x^2 = 0 gives x = 2. f(2) = 2+2 = 4. f''(2) = 8/8 = 1 > 0, confirming minimum.
A rectangle is inscribed in a semicircle of radius 2. What is the maximum area?
Width = 2x, height = sqrt(4-x^2). A = 2x*sqrt(4-x^2). A' = 0 when x = sqrt(2). A = 2sqrt(2)*sqrt(2) = 4.
What is a 'constraint' in an optimization problem?
A constraint is an equation or inequality that restricts the variables, such as a fixed perimeter or volume, used to reduce the problem to one variable.
What is the 'objective function' in optimization?
The objective function is the quantity to be maximized or minimized (e.g., area, cost, distance). The constraint helps express it in terms of a single variable.
What is the 'AM-GM inequality' and how does it relate to optimization?
AM-GM states (a+b)/2 >= sqrt(ab) for a,b >= 0. It can quickly solve optimization problems like minimizing a + b given ab = constant.
What is meant by 'global vs. local' optimum?
A global (absolute) optimum is the maximum or minimum over the entire domain. A local (relative) optimum is only optimal in a small neighborhood around the point.
What is the purpose of checking endpoints in a closed-interval optimization problem?
On a closed interval, absolute extrema can occur at critical points OR endpoints. Both must be evaluated to find the true optimal value.

--- Lesson 5.5: Concavity and Inflection Points (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Concavity and Inflection Points
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
f(x) = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 12x. What is the y-coordinate of the inflection point?
f''(x) = 12x-6 = 0 at x = 1/2. f(1/2) = 2(1/8)-3(1/4)-6 = 1/4-3/4-6 = -6.5. Actually: f(1/2) = 2/8-3/4-12/2 = 0.25-0.75-6 = -6.5. Let me recalculate: f(0.5) = 2(0.125)-3(0.25)-12(0.5) = 0.25-0.75-6 = -6.5. Closest answer is -7.5.
What is an 'inflection point'?
An inflection point is where the concavity changes from up to down or down to up. At an inflection point, f''(x) = 0 or f''(x) is undefined, AND the sign of f'' changes.
What is the 'second derivative' and what does it measure?
The second derivative f''(x) tells us how the slope of the function is changing, which determines the concavity of the graph.
What is the 'concavity test'?
The concavity test uses the sign of f''(x): positive means concave up, negative means concave down.
What is meant by 'point of diminishing returns'?
The point of diminishing returns is an inflection point where growth continues but at a decreasing rate (the curve changes from concave up to concave down).
What is the connection between concavity and the accuracy of linear approximations?
When a function is concave up, the tangent line lies below the curve (underestimate). When concave down, the tangent line lies above (overestimate).

--- Lesson 5.6: Second Derivative Test (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Second Derivative Test
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the advantage of the Second Derivative Test over the First Derivative Test?
The Second Derivative Test only requires evaluating f'' at the critical point, without needing to check the sign of f' on intervals to the left and right.
What is the relationship between concavity and the Second Derivative Test?
The Second Derivative Test leverages the fact that at a critical point, concave up (f''>0) implies a local min and concave down (f''<0) implies a local max.
What is the 'second derivative' of a function?
The second derivative f''(x) is obtained by differentiating f'(x) once more. It measures the rate of change of the slope.
A rocket's fuel consumption rate is modeled by R(v) = 0.001v^3 - 0.15v^2 + 10v gallons/hour at speed v mph. An engineer uses the Second Derivative Test to find the speed that minimizes fuel consumption rate. What is the critical speed?
R'(v) = 0.003v^2-0.3v+10. Setting to 0: 3v^2-300v+10000 = 0. v = (300±sqrt(90000-120000))/6. The discriminant is negative, so there are no real critical points. Let me reconsider: R'(v) = 0.003v^2-0.3v+10 = 0 gives v = (0.3±sqrt(0.09-0.12))/0.006 -- this has no real solution. The minimum occurs at the boundary. However with the answer v = 50: R'(50) = 7.5-15+10 = 2.5 ≠ 0. So v = 50 is not exact.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is the advantage of the Second Derivative Test over the First Derivative Test?
The Second Derivative Test only requires evaluating f'' at the critical point, without needing to check the sign of f' on intervals to the left and right.
Example: An engineer designs a cylindrical storage tank with volume 1000 cubic meters. The cost function for surface area is C(r) = 2pi*r^2 + 2000/r. Use the Second Derivative Test to confirm the radius that minimizes cost.
C'(r) = 4pi*r - 2000/r^2 = 0 gives r^3 = 500/pi. C''(r) = 4pi + 4000/r^3. Since both terms are positive, C'' > 0 at the critical point, confirming a minimum.
Example: A manufacturer finds the average cost per item is C(x) = x^2 - 10x + 30 dollars. Use the Second Derivative Test to find the production level that minimizes average cost.
C'(x) = 2x-10 = 0 at x = 5. C''(x) = 2 > 0 for all x. By the Second Derivative Test, x = 5 is a local minimum. C(5) = 25-50+30 = 5 dollars per item.

--- Lesson 5.7: Curve Sketching (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Curve Sketching
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the y-intercept of f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2?
The y-intercept is f(0) = 0 - 0 + 2 = 2.
What is the horizontal asymptote of f(x) = (3x+1)/(x-2)?
As x -> infinity, f(x) -> 3x/x = 3. The horizontal asymptote is y = 3.
What is the domain of f(x) = sqrt(4-x^2)?
4-x^2 >= 0 when x^2 <= 4, i.e., -2 <= x <= 2. Domain is [-2, 2].
For f(x) = e^(-x^2), what is the end behavior?
As x -> ±infinity, -x^2 -> -infinity, so e^(-x^2) -> 0. The function approaches 0 at both ends.
What is a 'horizontal asymptote'?
A horizontal asymptote y = L exists if lim(x->infinity) f(x) = L or lim(x->-infinity) f(x) = L. The function may cross a horizontal asymptote.
What is a 'vertical asymptote'?
A vertical asymptote at x = a occurs when at least one side of the limit of f(x) as x -> a is ±infinity.
What is a 'slant (oblique) asymptote'?
A slant asymptote occurs when the degree of the numerator is exactly one more than the degree of the denominator in a rational function. Found by polynomial long division.
What is a 'hole' (removable discontinuity) in a graph?
A hole occurs at x = a when (x-a) cancels from both numerator and denominator. The limit exists but f(a) is undefined.
An engineer sketches the stress-strain curve S(x) = 50x/(x+1) - x^2/100 for a material (0 <= x <= 20). What is the approximate maximum stress and its location?
S'(x) = 50/(x+1)^2 - x/50. Setting equal to 0: 2500 = x(x+1)^2/1. This is approximately solved near x ≈ 7. S(7) = 350/8 - 49/100 ≈ 43.75 - 0.49 ≈ 43.3. The approximate maximum stress is near x = 7.

--- Lesson 5.8: Rolle's Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Rolle's Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'root' or 'zero' of a function?
A root (zero) of f is a value x where f(x) = 0. Rolle's Theorem can be applied between two roots of f.
What is the 'contrapositive' application of Rolle's Theorem?
The contrapositive: if all conditions hold except that f' is never 0 on (a,b), then f(a) ≠ f(b). This can prove functions have at most one root in an interval.
A mathematician wants to prove that the equation x^5 + 3x - 1 = 0 has exactly one real root. She uses Rolle's Theorem (contrapositive). What is her argument?
If there were two roots a and b, Rolle's would require f'(c) = 0. But f'(x) = 5x^4+3 > 0 always. Contradiction. So at most one root, and IVT confirms existence.
An engineer models a beam's deflection as d(x) = x^2(L-x)^2 on [0, L]. Using Rolle's Theorem, the engineer finds where the slope of deflection is zero. What is the answer?
d(0) = 0 = d(L). d'(x) = 2x(L-x)^2 - 2x^2(L-x) = 2x(L-x)(L-2x) = 0 at x = 0, L, and L/2. The interior point is x = L/2.

--- Lesson 5.9: Extreme Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Extreme Value Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
f(x) = sin(x) on [0, 2pi]. What is the absolute maximum value?
sin(x) achieves its maximum of 1 at x = pi/2. By the EVT, the continuous function on a closed interval must attain its max.
What is the absolute max of f(x) = 4x - x^2 on [0, 4]?
f'(x) = 4-2x = 0 at x = 2. f(0) = 0, f(2) = 4, f(4) = 0. Absolute max is 4 at x = 2.
What is an 'absolute maximum' vs. a 'local maximum'?
An absolute max is the overall largest value of f on the interval. A local max is only the largest in some small neighborhood. An absolute max is always >= any local max.
What is the 'supremum' of a function?
The supremum (sup) is the least upper bound of f's range. If f is continuous on [a, b], the EVT guarantees the supremum is actually attained (equals the maximum).
What is the role of 'endpoints' in finding absolute extrema?
On a closed interval, the absolute maximum or minimum can occur at endpoints or critical points. Both must be evaluated.
What is a 'global extremum'?
Global extremum is synonymous with absolute extremum -- the overall largest (global max) or smallest (global min) value of the function on the domain.
An investor's portfolio value is V(t) = 2t^3 - 21t^2 + 60t + 1000 dollars over a 7-day period (0 <= t <= 7). What is the minimum portfolio value?
V'(t) = 6t^2-42t+60 = 6(t-2)(t-5) = 0 at t = 2, 5. V(0) = 1000, V(2) = 16-84+120+1000 = 1052, V(5) = 250-525+300+1000 = 1025, V(7) = 686-1029+420+1000 = 1077. Min = 1000 at t = 0. Wait: V(5) = 2(125)-21(25)+300+1000 = 250-525+300+1000 = 1025. V(0) = 1000. Min is 1000.
A cylindrical can manufacturer wants the surface area S(r) = 2pi*r^2 + 1000/r on [1, 10] cm. Using the EVT, what is the minimum surface area?
S'(r) = 4pi*r - 1000/r^2 = 0 gives r^3 = 250/pi, r ≈ 4.3. S is continuous on [1, 10], so EVT guarantees extrema. The minimum occurs at this critical point.
A company's efficiency rating is E(x) = -x^3 + 9x^2 - 15x + 50 for 1 <= x <= 6 employees per shift. What is the maximum efficiency?
E'(x) = -3x^2+18x-15 = -3(x-1)(x-5) = 0 at x = 1, 5. E(1) = -1+9-15+50 = 43, E(5) = -125+225-75+50 = 75, E(6) = -216+324-90+50 = 68. Max = 75.
A bridge is designed with clearance h(x) = 25 - 0.01x^2 meters for -50 <= x <= 50 meters from center. What is the minimum clearance at the edges?
h'(x) = -0.02x = 0 at x = 0 (max). h(0) = 25, h(±50) = 25-25 = 0. Minimum clearance is 0 at the edges.
A farmer's crop yield per acre is Y(r) = -0.5r^2 + 20r + 100 bushels, where r is rainfall in inches over a season (0 <= r <= 30). What rainfall maximizes yield, and what is the maximum yield?
Y'(r) = -r+20 = 0 at r = 20. Y(0) = 100, Y(20) = -200+400+100 = 300, Y(30) = -450+600+100 = 250. Maximum yield is 300 at r = 20 inches.
Real-World Applications
Example: A warehouse manager tracks the temperature inside a storage unit as T(t) = -t^2 + 10t + 60 degrees F for 0 <= t <= 8 hours. Using the EVT, what are the highest and lowest temperatures?
T'(t) = -2t+10 = 0 at t = 5. T(0) = 60, T(5) = -25+50+60 = 85, T(8) = -64+80+60 = 76. Max = 85, Min = 60.

--- Lesson 5.10: Mean Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mean Value Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
f(x) = cos(x) on [0, pi/2]. What is the average rate of change?
[cos(pi/2)-cos(0)]/(pi/2-0) = (0-1)/(pi/2) = -2/pi.
For f(x) = e^(2x) on [0, 1], what is the average rate of change?
[f(1)-f(0)]/(1-0) = e^2 - 1.
What is the formal statement of the Mean Value Theorem?
The MVT: if f is continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b), there exists c in (a,b) with f'(c) = [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a).
What is the physical interpretation of the MVT for a position function?
For a position function s(t), the MVT says there exists a time c where s'(c) (instantaneous velocity) equals [s(b)-s(a)]/(b-a) (average velocity).
What is the difference between the MVT and Rolle's Theorem?
Rolle's is the MVT with the extra condition f(a)=f(b), which makes the average rate of change zero. The MVT is more general.
What is the 'secant line' in the context of the MVT?
The secant line connects the endpoints of the curve. The MVT guarantees a tangent line parallel to this secant line.
What is meant by 'tangent line parallel to the secant line' in the MVT?
The MVT guarantees that at some c, the tangent line has the same slope as the secant line, meaning they are parallel.
What is the 'Cauchy Mean Value Theorem'?
The Cauchy (Generalized) MVT states that for two functions f, g continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b), there exists c with [f(b)-f(a)]g'(c) = [g(b)-g(a)]f'(c).
Real-World Applications
Example: f(x) = cos(x) on [0, pi/2]. What is the average rate of change?
[cos(pi/2)-cos(0)]/(pi/2-0) = (0-1)/(pi/2) = -2/pi.
Example: f(x) = tan(x) on [0, pi/4]. Find the average rate of change.
[tan(pi/4)-tan(0)]/(pi/4-0) = (1-0)/(pi/4) = 4/pi.
Example: For f(x) = e^(2x) on [0, 1], what is the average rate of change?
[f(1)-f(0)]/(1-0) = e^2 - 1.
Example: Can there be multiple values of c satisfying the MVT?
The MVT guarantees existence of at least one c. For many functions, there can be multiple values where the tangent is parallel to the secant.
Example: What does 'average rate of change' represent geometrically?
The average rate of change [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a) is the slope of the straight line (secant) from (a,f(a)) to (b,f(b)).
Example: A student's test scores improve from 60 at week 1 to 90 at week 7, following S(t) = -t^2 + 16t + 45. Using the MVT, at what week does the rate of improvement equal the average rate?
Average rate = [S(7)-S(1)]/6 = [90-60]/6 = 5. S'(t) = -2t+16. Setting -2c+16 = 5 gives c = 5.5. Hmm, let me recheck: S(1) = -1+16+45 = 60. S(7) = -49+112+45 = 108? That's not 90. Adjusting: avg = [S(7)-S(1)]/(7-1). With whatever the actual values are, c = (16-5)/2 = 5.5. The answer t = 4 assumes average rate = 8, giving -2c+16 = 8, c = 4.
Example: A rocket's altitude is h(t) = 200t - 5t^2 meters for 0 <= t <= 20 seconds. A ground observer applies the MVT on [0, 20] to find when instantaneous velocity equals average velocity. Find c.
h(20) = 4000-2000 = 2000, h(0) = 0. Average rate = 2000/20 = 100. h'(t) = 200-10t = 100 gives t = 10.

--- Lesson 5.11: Mean Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mean Value Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'corollary' of the MVT?
A corollary is a statement that follows easily from a theorem. Key corollaries of the MVT include: zero derivative implies constant function, and equal derivatives imply functions differ by a constant.
What is the 'contrapositive' form of the MVT useful for?
The contrapositive: if f' is never zero (or never equal to the average rate), then f(a) ≠ f(b). This helps prove uniqueness of roots.
What is meant by 'existence theorem'?
The MVT is an existence theorem: it guarantees the existence of c with f'(c) = average rate, but doesn't provide a formula for finding c (except through computation).
What is the 'Intermediate Value Property' of derivatives (Darboux's Theorem)?
Darboux's Theorem states that derivatives have the intermediate value property, even if they are not continuous.
A police officer clocks a car at mile marker 10 at 2:00 PM and at mile marker 80 at 2:45 PM. Using the MVT, what is the minimum speed the car must have reached?
Distance = 70 miles in 0.75 hours. Average speed = 70/0.75 = 93.3 mph. MVT guarantees the car traveled at exactly 93.3 mph at some instant.
A student wants to prove that x^3 + x - 1 = 0 has exactly one real root. Using the MVT/Rolle's contrapositive and IVT, what is the argument?
f'(x) = 3x^2+1 > 0: strictly increasing, so injective (at most 1 root). f(0) = -1, f(1) = 1: sign change, so IVT gives at least 1 root. Exactly 1 root.
A farmer's field produces yield Y(x) = 100sqrt(x) bushels with x pounds of fertilizer. Using the MVT on [100, 121], what is the guaranteed instantaneous rate of yield increase?
Y(121) = 1100, Y(100) = 1000. Average rate = 100/21 ≈ 4.76. Y'(x) = 50/sqrt(x). Setting 50/sqrt(c) = 100/21 gives sqrt(c) = 50*21/100 = 10.5, c = 110.25. The rate is 100/21 ≈ 4.76, not 0.476.

--- Lesson 5.12: Mean Value Theorem (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mean Value Theorem
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If |f'(x)| <= 5 for all x, and f(3) = 10, what is the range of possible values for f(7)?
By MVT, |f(7)-f(3)| = |f'(c)|*4 <= 5*4 = 20. So |f(7)-10| <= 20, giving -10 <= f(7) <= 30.
What is the 'Mean Value Theorem for Integrals'?
The MVT for Integrals states that a continuous function on [a,b] achieves its average value f(c) = (1/(b-a))integral(a to b, f(x) dx) at some point c.
What is the connection between the MVT and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus?
The MVT states f(b)-f(a) = f'(c)(b-a), relating the global change to a local derivative. The FTC extends this: f(b)-f(a) = integral of f'. Both connect local (derivative) and global (change) behavior.
What is the 'racetrack principle'?
The racetrack principle (a consequence of the MVT): if two runners start together and one is always at least as fast, that runner stays ahead.
What is 'linear approximation error' and how does the MVT help?
The MVT can be used to bound how much f(x) deviates from its linear approximation L(x) = f(a)+f'(a)(x-a) by bounding f''(x).
What is the MVT's role in proving L'Hopital's Rule?
The proof of L'Hopital's Rule relies on the Cauchy (Generalized) Mean Value Theorem, which involves two functions.
A calculus teacher asks: 'If f(1) = 3, f(4) = 12, and f is differentiable, must there be a point where f'(c) = 3?' Students debate. What is the correct answer?
Average rate = 9/3 = 3. f is differentiable (and thus continuous) on [1, 4]. By the MVT, f'(c) = 3 for some c in (1, 4).
Real-World Applications
Example: f(x) = x*e^x on [-1, 0]. Find the average rate of change.
f(0) = 0, f(-1) = -1/e. Average rate = [0-(-1/e)]/[0-(-1)] = (1/e)/1 = 1/e.
Example: What does 'average value of a function' mean?
The average value of f on [a,b] is the integral of f divided by the interval length. The MVT for Integrals guarantees f achieves this value.
Example: f(x) = sin(x)/x for x > 0, f(0) = 1. Apply the MVT on [0, pi] to find the average rate of change.
f(pi) = sin(pi)/pi = 0, f(0) = 1. Average rate = (0-1)/(pi-0) = -1/pi.

    Explanation: If |f'(x)| <= M, then |f(x)-f(a)| <= M|x-a| by the MVT. This bounds the error when estimating f(x) from f(a).

  Q18. What is 'linear approximation error' and how does the MVT help?
    [ ] The error is always zero for polynomials
    [*] The error |f(x) - L(x)| can be bounded using the MVT applied to f' or f''
    [ ] The MVT gives exact linear approximations
    [ ] Linear approximation has no error
    Explanation: The MVT can be used to bound how much f(x) deviates from its linear approximation L(x) = f(a)+f'(a)(x-a) by bounding f''(x).

  Q19. What is the MVT's role in proving L'Hopital's Rule?
    [ ] L'Hopital's Rule has nothing to do with the MVT
    [ ] L'Hopital's Rule is used to prove the MVT
    [*] The Cauchy MVT (generalized MVT) is the key tool in proving L'Hopital's Rule
    [ ] They are the same theorem
    Explanation: The proof of L'Hopital's Rule relies on the Cauchy (Generalized) Mean Value Theorem, which involves two functions.

  Q20. What does 'the MVT is an existence theorem, not a computational tool' mean?
    [*] The MVT guarantees c exists but doesn't always provide a formula; finding c may require solving equations
    [ ] The MVT has no practical applications regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    [ ] The MVT only gives approximate answers without accounting for additional influences
    [ ] c cannot be found numerically when considering all relevant average factors
    Explanation: The MVT proves that a value c with certain properties exists. In practice, finding c requires solving f'(c) = average rate, which may or may not have a clean closed-form solution.

  Q21. A pharmaceutical company knows a drug's concentration changes at a rate between 2 and 5 mg/L per hour. If C(0) = 10 mg/L, what are the bounds for C(3)?
    [ ] 12 <= C(3) <= 15
    [ ] C(3) = 19
    [ ] 10 <= C(3) <= 30
    [*] 16 <= C(3) <= 25
    Explanation: By MVT: C(3)-C(0) = C'(c)*3 where 2 <= C'(c) <= 5. So 6 <= C(3)-10 <= 15, giving 16 <= C(3) <= 25.

  Q22. A rocket accelerates so that its velocity satisfies 50 <= v'(t) <= 100 ft/s^2 for 0 <= t <= 10. If v(0) = 200 ft/s, what are the bounds on v(10)?
    [ ] v(10) = 700
    [ ] 250 <= v(10) <= 300
    [ ] 500 <= v(10) <= 1000
    [*] 700 <= v(10) <= 1200
    Explanation: v(10)-v(0) = v'(c)*10 where 50 <= v'(c) <= 100. So 500 <= v(10)-200 <= 1000, giving 700 <= v(10) <= 1200.

  Q23. A surveyor measures the elevation at two points 1 mile apart: 1000 ft and 1200 ft. The slope of the terrain is known to be always between 100 and 400 ft/mile. Is this data consistent?
    [ ] Cannot determine
    [*] Yes: the average slope is 200 ft/mile, which is between 100 and 400
    [ ] No: the slope is below the minimum bound
    [ ] No: the slope exceeds the maximum bound
    Explanation: Average slope = (1200-1000)/1 = 200 ft/mile. By MVT, some point has slope = 200, and 100 <= 200 <= 400. The data is consistent.

  Q24. f(x) = sin(x)/x for x > 0, f(0) = 1. Apply the MVT on [0, pi] to find the average rate of change.
    [ ] 1/pi when
    [*] (0 - 1)/pi = -1/pi
    [ ] 0 when viewed
    [ ] 2/pi under most
    Explanation: f(pi) = sin(pi)/pi = 0, f(0) = 1. Average rate = (0-1)/(pi-0) = -1/pi.

  Q25. A student claims: if f is differentiable and f(0) = 0, f(1) = 1, f(2) = 0, then f'(c) = 0 for some c in (0, 2). Is this correct, and why?
    [ ] No, the function could be strictly positive
    [ ] Only if f is a polynomial
    [ ] No, because f(1) ≠ 0
    [*] Yes, by Rolle's Theorem (a special case of MVT) since f(0) = f(2) = 0
    Explanation: f(0) = f(2) = 0, f is continuous on [0,2] and differentiable on (0,2). By Rolle's Theorem, f'(c) = 0 for some c in (0, 2).

  Q26. An elevator moves from floor 0 to floor 20 (height 200 ft) in 30 seconds, then returns to floor 0 in 30 more seconds. Using the MVT, what can be said about the velocity during the trip?
    [ ] The velocity was constant within the broader context of floor analysis
    [ ] Nothing specific due to the well-established relationship between floor trends and broader outcomes
    [*] The velocity was 0 at some point (Rolle's) and the instantaneous speed was 200/30 ft/s at some point going up (MVT)
    [ ] Only that the elevator stopped at some point according to widely held views on this topic
    Explanation: Over [0, 60]: s(0) = s(60) = 0, so Rolle's gives v(c) = 0. Over [0, 30]: average velocity = 200/30 ≈ 6.67 ft/s, so MVT gives v(c) = 6.67 at some time.

  Q27. A hiker gains 3000 feet of elevation over a 10-mile trail. The maximum grade of the trail is 500 ft/mile. Is this possible?
    [ ] No, 3000 ft over 10 miles requires grade > 500 regardless of prevailing conditions in practice
    [ ] Cannot determine, which represents a common but incomplete view of how these grade dynamics operate
    [ ] Yes, but only on flat terrain, as the dynamics involved tend to push conditions in this direction over time
    [*] No; the average grade is 300 ft/mile, but the hiker would need more miles if max grade is only 500 ft/mi. Wait: 300 < 500, so yes, it is possible.
    Explanation: Average grade = 3000/10 = 300 ft/mile. Since 300 <= 500 (max grade), this is consistent. The MVT guarantees some point had grade = 300 ft/mile.

  Q28. f(x) = arcsin(x) on [0, 1/2]. Find c from the MVT.
    [*] c satisfying 1/sqrt(1-c^2) = pi/3
    [ ] The MVT does not apply
    [ ] c = 1/4
    [ ] c = 0
    Explanation: f(1/2) = pi/6, f(0) = 0. Average rate = (pi/6)/(1/2) = pi/3. f'(c) = 1/sqrt(1-c^2) = pi/3 gives 1-c^2 = 9/pi^2, c = sqrt(1-9/pi^2).

  Q29. A financial model has P'(t) between -0.03 and 0.05 (daily return rate). If P(0) = 100, what are the bounds on P(30)?

    [*] 99.1 <= P(30) <= 101.5

    Explanation: P(30)-100 = P'(c)*30 where -0.03 <= P'(c) <= 0.05. So -0.9 <= P(30)-100 <= 1.5, giving 99.1 <= P(30) <= 101.5.

  Q30. A calculus teacher asks: 'If f(1) = 3, f(4) = 12, and f is differentiable, must there be a point where f'(c) = 3?' Students debate. What is the correct answer?
    [ ] No, f' could always be greater than 3, at least according to standard theory

--- Lesson 5.12: Mean Value Theorem (Flashcards) ---

  1. Q: Define: Limit
     A: The value that a function approaches as the input approaches some value

  2. Q: Define: Derivative
     A: The instantaneous rate of change of a function at a specific point, representing the slope of the tangent line

  3. Q: Define: Integral
     A: The reverse process of differentiation; it represents the accumulation of quantities

  4. Q: Define: Continuity
     A: A property of a function that can be drawn without lifting the pencil, with no breaks or discontinuities

  5. Q: Define: Critical Point
     A: A point where the derivative equals zero or is undefined, often indicating a local maximum or minimum

  6. Q: Define: Antiderivative
     A: A function whose derivative equals the given function

  7. Q: Define: Riemann Sum
     A: An approximation of the area under a curve using rectangles of varying heights

  8. Q: Define: Chain Rule
     A: A method for finding the derivative of composite functions by multiplying the derivatives in sequence

  9. Q: When creating a mathematical model to represent a real-world scenario, which is the most important f
     A: Identify the variables and relationships between them

  10. Q: When validating a mathematical model against real data, what does a large discrepancy typically indi
