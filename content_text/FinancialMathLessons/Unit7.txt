--- Lesson 7.1: Starting a Business (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Starting a Business
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'sole proprietorship'?
A sole proprietorship is the default business structure for a single owner. It requires no formal filing (except licenses), but offers no liability protection — the owner is personally liable for all business obligations.
What is an 'LLC' (Limited Liability Company)?
An LLC is a hybrid entity combining corporate liability protection with partnership-style pass-through taxation. It is the most popular structure for small businesses due to its flexibility and protection.
What is an 'EIN' (Employer Identification Number)?
An EIN is like a Social Security number for a business. It is required for filing business taxes, hiring employees, and opening business bank accounts. It is free to obtain from the IRS.
What is a 'business plan'?
A business plan serves as both a roadmap for the business and a document for stakeholders. It typically includes an executive summary, market analysis, financial projections, and operational plans.
What is a 'franchise'?
Franchises provide a proven model, brand recognition, and operational support. In exchange, franchisees pay initial fees ($10,000-$100,000+) and ongoing royalties (typically 4-8% of revenue).
Kim leaves an $80,000/year job to start a business, investing $40,000 in savings that was earning 6% returns. What is her total first-year opportunity cost?
Opportunity cost = foregone salary ($80,000) + foregone investment return ($40,000 x 0.06 = $2,400) = $82,400. The business must generate at least this amount in value to justify the switch.
A franchise requires $50,000 upfront, $2,000/month royalties (6% of revenue), and generates $600,000 in annual revenue with $400,000 in expenses (before royalties). What is the franchisee's annual profit?
Revenue: $600,000. Expenses: $400,000. Royalties: 6% of $600,000 = $36,000. Profit = $600,000 - $400,000 - $36,000 = $164,000. The upfront fee is a separate capital cost, not an annual expense.
A new e-commerce business has $2,000/month fixed costs and $15 variable cost per item, selling at $45 each. In month one, they sell 50 items. In month three, they sell 150 items. What is the profit in each month?
Month 1: Revenue = 50 x $45 = $2,250. Costs = $2,000 + (50 x $15) = $2,750. Loss = -$500. Month 3: Revenue = 150 x $45 = $6,750. Costs = $2,000 + (150 x $15) = $4,250. Profit = $2,500.
Two friends are deciding between starting a partnership and forming an LLC. They each invest $25,000. In a partnership, both have unlimited personal liability. The LLC costs $500/year to maintain. If a customer sues for $500,000 and the business has only $50,000 in assets, what is each partner's personal exposure under each structure?
In a partnership, after the business's $50,000 in assets are exhausted, the remaining $450,000 can be collected from partners' personal assets. In an LLC, personal assets are protected — the maximum loss is the $25,000 each invested.
Maria runs a successful service business earning $200,000/year. She estimates she needs 6 months of working capital ($60,000) in reserve. She currently has $40,000 in business reserves. A major client asks her to expand, requiring $80,000 in upfront investment. What is the most financially prudent approach?
Depleting working capital is dangerous — unexpected expenses could cripple operations. Financing the expansion preserves the $40,000 buffer while she rebuilds toward the $60,000 target. Alternatively, phased expansion reduces risk.
Real-World Applications
Example: A sole proprietorship's biggest disadvantage is:
In a sole proprietorship, there is no legal separation between the owner and the business. Personal assets (home, savings) can be seized to satisfy business debts or lawsuits.
Example: A bank loan for a business is an example of _____ financing.
Debt financing (loans, lines of credit) must be repaid with interest on a fixed schedule. The lender does not share in profits but has priority claim if the business fails.
Example: Approximately what percentage of small businesses fail within the first 5 years?
Approximately 50% of small businesses fail within five years, often due to inadequate planning, insufficient capital, and poor financial management. This highlights the importance of sound financial planning.

--- Lesson 7.2: Profit & Loss Analysis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Profit & Loss Analysis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'revenue' in the context of a profit and loss statement?
Revenue is the total income generated from the sale of goods or services before any costs or expenses are subtracted. It is the starting point of the income statement.
What is 'depreciation' in accounting?
Depreciation is an accounting method that systematically spreads the cost of a tangible long-term asset (like machinery or vehicles) over its estimated useful life, reducing reported profit without an actual outflow of cash.
What is the 'bottom line' on an income statement?
Net income is called the 'bottom line' because it literally appears as the last line on the income statement, representing the final profit or loss after all revenues and expenses are accounted for.
A clothing store has $350,000 in annual revenue, COGS of $140,000, and operating expenses of $120,000. What is the store's net profit before taxes?
Gross profit = $350,000 - $140,000 = $210,000. Net profit before taxes = $210,000 - $120,000 = $90,000.
A tech company has $800,000 in revenue and a 70% gross margin. If operating expenses are $300,000, what is the net income before taxes?
Gross profit = $800,000 x 0.70 = $560,000. Net income before taxes = $560,000 - $300,000 = $260,000.
A restaurant earns $500,000 in revenue. COGS is $175,000 and operating expenses total $225,000. What is the restaurant's net profit margin?
Gross profit = $500,000 - $175,000 = $325,000. Net profit = $325,000 - $225,000 = $100,000. Net profit margin = $100,000 / $500,000 = 20%.
A SaaS company has monthly revenue of $120,000, COGS of $18,000, and operating expenses of $85,000. What is its gross margin percentage and net profit?
Gross profit = $120,000 - $18,000 = $102,000. Gross margin = $102,000 / $120,000 = 85%. Net profit = $102,000 - $85,000 = $17,000.
A coffee shop has $8,000/month fixed costs. Each drink has a selling price of $5 and a variable cost of $1.50. The shop currently sells 3,000 drinks per month. What is its monthly profit and margin of safety in units?
CM per drink = $5.00 - $1.50 = $3.50. Total CM = 3,000 x $3.50 = $10,500. Profit = $10,500 - $8,000 = $2,500. BEP = $8,000 / $3.50 ≈ 2,286 units. Margin of safety = 3,000 - 2,286 = about 714 units above break-even.
A company buys equipment for $60,000 with a 5-year useful life and no salvage value (straight-line depreciation). If annual revenue is $200,000, COGS is $90,000, and other operating expenses total $80,000, what is the annual net profit before taxes?
Annual depreciation = $60,000 / 5 = $12,000. Gross profit = $200,000 - $90,000 = $110,000. Total operating expenses = $80,000 + $12,000 = $92,000. Net profit before taxes = $110,000 - $92,000 = $18,000.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'operating leverage' refer to?
Operating leverage describes how a company's cost structure (specifically its mix of fixed vs. variable costs) affects profitability. High operating leverage means small revenue changes lead to large profit swings.
Example: A SaaS company has monthly revenue of $120,000, COGS of $18,000, and operating expenses of $85,000. What is its gross margin percentage and net profit?
Gross profit = $120,000 - $18,000 = $102,000. Gross margin = $102,000 / $120,000 = 85%. Net profit = $102,000 - $85,000 = $17,000.
Example: A company buys equipment for $60,000 with a 5-year useful life and no salvage value (straight-line depreciation). If annual revenue is $200,000, COGS is $90,000, and other operating expenses total $80,000, what is the annual net profit before taxes?
Annual depreciation = $60,000 / 5 = $12,000. Gross profit = $200,000 - $90,000 = $110,000. Total operating expenses = $80,000 + $12,000 = $92,000. Net profit before taxes = $110,000 - $92,000 = $18,000.
Example: A business with high operating leverage has $200,000 in fixed costs and low variable costs (10% of revenue). If revenue increases from $500,000 to $600,000, by how much does profit increase?
At $500K revenue: Variable costs = $50K, Fixed costs = $200K, Profit = $500K - $50K - $200K = $250K. At $600K revenue: Variable costs = $60K, Fixed costs = $200K, Profit = $600K - $60K - $200K = $340K. Profit increase = $340K - $250K = $90K. A 20% revenue increase caused a 36% profit increase, demonstrating high operating leverage.

--- Lesson 7.3: Break-Even Analysis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Break-Even Analysis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'break-even point' in business finance?
The break-even point is the sales volume (in units or dollars) at which total revenue equals total costs (fixed plus variable), meaning the business earns zero profit and incurs zero loss.
What is a 'target profit analysis'?
Target profit analysis extends break-even analysis by adding the desired profit to fixed costs before dividing by contribution margin: Units = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) / CM per unit.
A gym has $20,000 in monthly fixed costs. Each membership is $80/month with a variable cost of $20/member. The gym currently has 350 members. What is its monthly profit and margin of safety percentage?
CM per member = $80 - $20 = $60. Total CM = 350 x $60 = $21,000. Profit = $21,000 - $20,000 = $1,000. BEP = $20,000 / $60 = 333.33, rounded up to 334 members. Margin of safety = (350 - 334) / 350 = about 4.76%.
A toy store sells two products: Product A ($20 price, $8 variable cost, 60% of sales) and Product B ($35 price, $20 variable cost, 40% of sales). What is the weighted-average contribution margin?
CM for A = $20 - $8 = $12. CM for B = $35 - $20 = $15. Weighted-average CM = ($12 x 0.60) + ($15 x 0.40) = $7.20 + $6.00 = $13.20.
A software company sells licenses at $100 each with a variable cost of $10 (mostly hosting). Monthly fixed costs are $36,000. It currently sells 500 licenses/month. What is the DOL (Degree of Operating Leverage)?
CM per unit = $100 - $10 = $90. Total CM = 500 x $90 = $45,000. Net income = $45,000 - $36,000 = $9,000. DOL = Total CM / Net Income = $45,000 / $9,000 = 5.0. A high DOL means profits are very sensitive to revenue changes.
A food truck has $6,000 monthly fixed costs and sells meals for $15 each with a variable cost of $6. If the owner wants to earn $3,000 profit per month, what is the minimum number of meals needed?
CM per meal = $15 - $6 = $9. Units for target profit = (Fixed costs + Target profit) / CM = ($6,000 + $3,000) / $9 = $9,000 / $9 = 1,000 meals.

--- Lesson 7.4: Small Business Financing (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Small Business Financing
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'bootstrapping' in business finance?
Bootstrapping refers to building a business from the ground up with personal savings and operating revenue rather than relying on outside investors or borrowed money.
What is 'cost of capital'?
Cost of capital is the blended rate of return a business must earn on its investments to satisfy both its debt holders (interest) and equity investors (expected returns). It serves as a hurdle rate for evaluating new projects.
Maria invests $50,000 of personal savings to start a bakery. After one year, the bakery generates $18,000 in net profit. What is her ROI?
ROI = Net Profit / Investment = $18,000 / $50,000 = 0.36 = 36%.
A business takes a $150,000 bank loan at 8% annual interest for 5 years. The monthly payment is approximately $3,042. What is the total interest paid over the life of the loan?
Total payments = $3,042 x 60 months = $182,520. Total interest = $182,520 - $150,000 = $32,520.
An entrepreneur gives up 25% equity to an angel investor when the business is valued at $400,000. If the business later grows to $2,000,000, what is the value of the equity the investor now holds?
The investor holds 25% of the business. At $2,000,000 valuation, their stake = 25% x $2,000,000 = $500,000. The original equity cost was $100,000 (25% of $400K), so the true cost of that equity financing grew to $500,000.
A small business factors $80,000 in invoices at a 15% discount to receive immediate cash. How much cash does the business receive, and what is the cost of factoring?
Cash received = $80,000 x (1 - 0.15) = $80,000 x 0.85 = $68,000. The cost of factoring = $80,000 - $68,000 = $12,000. The business trades $12,000 for immediate cash flow.
A business chooses to lease equipment for $800/month for 5 years instead of buying it for $35,000. What is the total lease cost, and how does it compare to buying?
Total lease cost = $800 x 60 months = $48,000. Compared to buying at $35,000, leasing costs $13,000 more over the full term. However, leasing preserves cash upfront and may offer tax advantages.
A restaurant owner needs $200,000 to open. She uses $60,000 personal savings (bootstrapping), a $100,000 SBA loan at 6.5%, and a $40,000 angel investment for 10% equity. If the restaurant is worth $800,000 after 5 years, what is the total cost of the angel investment?
The angel investor holds 10% equity. At $800,000 valuation, that stake is worth 10% x $800,000 = $80,000. The cost of the equity financing is $80,000 in ownership value, which is double the original $40,000 investment.
Real-World Applications
Example: The main advantage of bootstrapping is:
Bootstrapping lets the owner retain full ownership and control of the business without owing debt payments or answering to outside investors.
Example: A business with $15,000 monthly revenue and a $100,000 loan at 8% (monthly payment ~$2,028) has what percentage of revenue going to debt service? Is this considered healthy?
Debt service ratio = $2,028 / $15,000 = 13.5%. Generally, keeping debt service below 25% of revenue is considered manageable. At 13.5%, the business has a comfortable margin for repayment.

--- Lesson 7.5: Entrepreneurship Case Studies (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Entrepreneurship Case Studies
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'payback period' in business analysis?
Payback period measures how long it takes for an investment to 'pay for itself' through the profits it generates. It is calculated as Initial Investment / Annual Profit.
A food truck owner is considering adding a catering service. Monthly catering adds $2,000 in fixed costs but generates $6,000 in revenue with 50% contribution margin. What is the monthly profit from catering?
Contribution from catering = $6,000 x 50% = $3,000. Profit = $3,000 - $2,000 fixed costs = $1,000 per month. The catering expansion is profitable.
An online store spends $3,000 on a marketing campaign and gains 120 new customers. Each customer has an average LTV of $150. What is the CAC, and is the campaign profitable?
CAC = $3,000 / 120 = $25 per customer. LTV:CAC = $150 / $25 = 6:1, which is well above the 3:1 healthy threshold. Total LTV from campaign = 120 x $150 = $18,000 vs. $3,000 spent.
A food truck earns contribution margin of $6,000/month in summer (May-August) and $2,500/month the rest of the year. Monthly fixed costs are $3,000 year-round. What is the annual profit?
Summer profit = ($6,000 - $3,000) x 4 months = $12,000. Off-season: ($2,500 - $3,000) x 8 months = -$4,000 loss. Annual profit = $12,000 - $4,000 = $8,000. The strong summer months more than offset the off-season losses.
A subscription box company acquires 200 subscribers at $35/month. Average customer stays 8 months. CAC is $50. What is the LTV:CAC ratio?
LTV = $35/month x 8 months = $280 per customer. LTV:CAC = $280 / $50 = 5.6:1, which is excellent.
An online tutoring startup has virtually zero variable costs (digital delivery). Monthly fixed costs are $2,000. Each student pays $100/month. If the startup has 30 students, what is the monthly profit and margin of safety?
With zero variable costs, CM = $100/student. Revenue = 30 x $100 = $3,000. Profit = $3,000 - $2,000 = $1,000. BEP = $2,000 / $100 = 20 students. Margin of safety = 30 - 20 = 10 students (10/30 = 33%).
A craft business sells items for $25 each online. Variable cost is $10. Etsy charges a 6.5% transaction fee on sales. Fixed costs are $1,200/month. What is the true CM and monthly BEP?
Etsy fee = $25 x 6.5% = $1.625 per item. True variable cost = $10 + $1.625 = $11.625. CM = $25 - $11.625 = $13.375. BEP = $1,200 / $13.375 = about 90 items per month.
Two friends start a lawn care business. Startup cost: $8,000 for equipment. They charge $50 per lawn with $12 in variable costs (gas, supplies). Weekly fixed costs are $200. In their first summer (16 weeks), they average 25 lawns/week. What is their total summer profit, and how long to pay back the equipment?
CM per lawn = $50 - $12 = $38. Weekly CM = 25 x $38 = $950. Weekly profit = $950 - $200 = $750. Summer profit = $750 x 16 = $12,000. Payback = $8,000 / $750 per week = about 10.7 weeks, well within the 16-week summer.

--- Lesson 7.7: Business Risk Management (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Business Risk Management
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A business with $500,000 in annual revenue depends on one client for 60% of sales. If that client leaves, remaining revenue is $200,000 against $350,000 in fixed costs. What is the annual shortfall?
If the main client leaves, remaining revenue = $200,000. With $350,000 in fixed costs, the shortfall = $350,000 - $200,000 = $150,000. This illustrates the severe danger of concentration risk.
A company has $300,000 in total debt, $150,000 in equity, and generates $80,000 in annual net income. What is the D/E ratio, and can the company comfortably service its debt assuming 8% interest?
D/E = $300,000 / $150,000 = 2.0. Annual interest = $300,000 x 8% = $24,000. Interest coverage ratio = $80,000 / $24,000 = 3.3x, meaning income covers interest payments comfortably.
A restaurant has monthly operating expenses of $25,000. The recommended cash reserve is 3-6 months. What is the minimum recommended reserve?
Minimum recommended reserve = 3 months x $25,000 = $75,000. This provides a buffer to weather temporary revenue disruptions like equipment failures, slow seasons, or unexpected closures.
A manufacturing company relies on one overseas supplier. Shipping delays cause a 2-month production stoppage. Monthly revenue is $100,000 and fixed costs continue at $40,000/month. What is the total financial impact?
Lost revenue = $100,000 x 2 = $200,000. Fixed costs that continue = $40,000 x 2 = $80,000. Total impact = $200,000 lost revenue + $80,000 in costs still owed = $280,000. Having backup suppliers would mitigate this risk.
A business pays $2,400/year for business interruption insurance. A fire forces closure for 3 months, during which the insurance pays $15,000/month for lost income. What is the net benefit of the insurance?
Insurance payout = $15,000 x 3 = $45,000. Annual premium = $2,400. Net benefit = $45,000 - $2,400 = $42,600. The $2,400 annual premium was an excellent investment against the $45,000 loss.
A tech company suffers a data breach. Cyber insurance covers $200,000 in damages. Without insurance, the company would pay $200,000 in notification costs, legal fees, and fines, plus an estimated $100,000 in lost customer trust. If the annual cyber insurance premium is $8,000, what is the ROI on the insurance?
Covered damages = $200,000. Premium = $8,000. ROI = ($200,000 - $8,000) / $8,000 x 100% = $192,000 / $8,000 = 2,400%. Note that the $100K in lost customer trust is not covered by insurance but would still be incurred.
A business runs scenario analysis with three outcomes: Best case (profit $120,000, probability 20%), Most likely (profit $60,000, probability 60%), Worst case (loss $30,000, probability 20%). What is the expected value?
Expected value = ($120,000 x 0.20) + ($60,000 x 0.60) + (-$30,000 x 0.20) = $24,000 + $36,000 - $6,000 = $54,000.
A company with $150,000 in annual profit has two key employees. Key person insurance for each costs $3,000/year and pays $250,000 if either dies or becomes disabled. If one key person leaves and it takes $150,000 to replace them and recover lost business, what is the net insurance benefit?
Insurance payout = $250,000. Cost to replace and recover = $150,000. Net insurance benefit = $250,000 - $150,000 = $100,000 remaining to help the business through the transition (minus the $3,000 annual premium).
Real-World Applications

Cash flow risk management involves maintaining adequate reserves, diversifying revenue streams, actively monitoring accounts receivable, and planning for seasonal fluctuations.

If the main client leaves, remaining revenue = $200,000. With $350,000 in fixed costs, the shortfall = $350,000 - $200,000 = $150,000. This illustrates the severe danger of concentration risk.
Example: A manufacturing company relies on one overseas supplier. Shipping delays cause a 2-month production stoppage. Monthly revenue is $100,000 and fixed costs continue at $40,000/month. What is the total financial impact?
Lost revenue = $100,000 x 2 = $200,000. Fixed costs that continue = $40,000 x 2 = $80,000. Total impact = $200,000 lost revenue + $80,000 in costs still owed = $280,000. Having backup suppliers would mitigate this risk.
Example: A tech company suffers a data breach. Cyber insurance covers $200,000 in damages. Without insurance, the company would pay $200,000 in notification costs, legal fees, and fines, plus an estimated $100,000 in lost customer trust. If the annual cyber insurance premium is $8,000, what is the ROI on the insurance?
Covered damages = $200,000. Premium = $8,000. ROI = ($200,000 - $8,000) / $8,000 x 100% = $192,000 / $8,000 = 2,400%. Note that the $100K in lost customer trust is not covered by insurance but would still be incurred.
Example: A construction company has a D/E ratio of 3.0 and annual interest expense of $45,000. If net income drops from $90,000 to $50,000, how does the interest coverage ratio change, and is this concerning?
Old interest coverage = $90,000 / $45,000 = 2.0x. New coverage = $50,000 / $45,000 = 1.1x. A coverage ratio below 1.5x is considered dangerous because there is almost no margin for further income decline before the business cannot cover its interest payments.

--- Lesson 7.8: Technology in Business Finance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Technology in Business Finance
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'cloud accounting'?
Cloud accounting refers to accounting software hosted on remote servers (the 'cloud') that can be accessed from any internet-connected device, enabling real-time collaboration and automatic data backup.
Using Excel's PMT function, a business calculates a $200,000 loan at 6% annual rate for 15 years. The monthly payment is approximately $1,688. What is the total interest paid?
Total payments = $1,688 x 180 months = $303,840. Total interest = $303,840 - $200,000 = $103,840.
A business pays $65/month for QuickBooks, $45/month for payroll service, and $30/month for POS software. What is the total annual cost of financial technology?
Monthly total = $65 + $45 + $30 = $140. Annual cost = $140 x 12 = $1,680. These tools are highly cost-effective compared to hiring a full-time bookkeeper.
A business creates a financial model in Excel projecting 3 scenarios: Base case ($80K profit), Optimistic ($120K profit, 25% probability), and Pessimistic ($30K profit, 35% probability). Base case probability is 40%. What is the expected profit?
Expected profit = ($120K x 0.25) + ($80K x 0.40) + ($30K x 0.35) = $30,000 + $32,000 + $10,500 = $72,500.
Real-World Applications
Example: Cloud accounting advantages include:
Cloud-based accounting provides remote access, automatic data syncing with bank accounts, real-time collaboration with bookkeepers/accountants, and automatic data backups.
Example: What does 'Goal Seek' do in Excel?
Goal Seek is an Excel what-if analysis tool that works backward: you specify a target result (e.g., a specific profit level), and it calculates what input value (e.g., price or volume) is needed to achieve it.
Example: A company uses Excel's Goal Seek to find what price is needed to achieve $50,000 profit. Fixed costs are $80,000, variable cost is $15/unit, and projected volume is 5,000 units. What price does Goal Seek return?
Required total CM = Fixed costs + Target profit = $80,000 + $50,000 = $130,000. CM per unit = $130,000 / 5,000 = $26. Price = Variable cost + CM = $15 + $26 = $41 per unit.
Example: An online store receives payments via Stripe. In January, it processes $45,000 in sales across 600 transactions. What are the total processing fees for January?
Percentage fee = $45,000 x 2.9% = $1,305. Per-transaction fee = 600 x $0.30 = $180. Total = $1,305 + $180 = $1,485.
Example: A business uses automated invoicing and reduces its average collection time from 45 days to 20 days. With $30,000 in monthly revenue, how much additional cash is available at any given time?
Daily revenue = $30,000 / 30 = $1,000. Days saved = 45 - 20 = 25 days. Additional cash freed up = 25 x $1,000 = $25,000 that is no longer tied up in outstanding receivables.
Example: A business evaluates two payment processors: Processor A charges 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction. Processor B charges a flat 2.5% with no per-transaction fee. The business averages 500 transactions/month at $80 each. Which is cheaper?
Processor A: ($80 x 0.029 + $0.30) x 500 = ($2.32 + $0.30) x 500 = $2.62 x 500 = $1,310/month. Processor B: $80 x 0.025 x 500 = $2.00 x 500 = $1,000/month. Processor B saves $310/month ($3,720/year).

  Q22. Using Excel's PMT function, a business calculates a $200,000 loan at 6% annual rate for 15 years. The monthly payment is approximately $1,688. What is the total interest paid?
    [*] $103,840
    [ ] $80,000
    [ ] $200,000
    [ ] $60,000
    Explanation: Total payments = $1,688 x 180 months = $303,840. Total interest = $303,840 - $200,000 = $103,840.

  Q23. A business switches from manual bookkeeping (10 hours/week at $30/hour) to accounting software at $50/month. Assuming the software reduces bookkeeping time to 2 hours/week, what are the approximate annual savings?
    [*] $11,880
    [ ] $7,200
    [ ] $10,000
    [ ] $15,000
    Explanation: Old cost = 10 hrs x $30 x 52 weeks = $15,600/year. New cost = (2 hrs x $30 x 52) + ($50 x 12) = $3,120 + $600 = $3,720/year. Annual savings = $15,600 - $3,720 = $11,880.

  Q24. A business pays $65/month for QuickBooks, $45/month for payroll service, and $30/month for POS software. What is the total annual cost of financial technology?
    [ ] $2,100
    [ ] $1,440
    [*] $1,680
    [ ] $1,200
    Explanation: Monthly total = $65 + $45 + $30 = $140. Annual cost = $140 x 12 = $1,680. These tools are highly cost-effective compared to hiring a full-time bookkeeper.

  Q25. A company uses Excel's Goal Seek to find what price is needed to achieve $50,000 profit. Fixed costs are $80,000, variable cost is $15/unit, and projected volume is 5,000 units. What price does Goal Seek return?
    [ ] $35
    [ ] $26
    [*] $41
    [ ] $31
    Explanation: Required total CM = Fixed costs + Target profit = $80,000 + $50,000 = $130,000. CM per unit = $130,000 / 5,000 = $26. Price = Variable cost + CM = $15 + $26 = $41 per unit.

  Q26. An online store receives payments via Stripe. In January, it processes $45,000 in sales across 600 transactions. What are the total processing fees for January?
    [ ] $1,305
    [*] $1,485
    [ ] $1,125
    [ ] $1,350
    Explanation: Percentage fee = $45,000 x 2.9% = $1,305. Per-transaction fee = 600 x $0.30 = $180. Total = $1,305 + $180 = $1,485.

  Q27. A business uses automated invoicing and reduces its average collection time from 45 days to 20 days. With $30,000 in monthly revenue, how much additional cash is available at any given time?
    [ ] $10,000
    [ ] $30,000
    [ ] $15,000
    [*] $25,000
    Explanation: Daily revenue = $30,000 / 30 = $1,000. Days saved = 45 - 20 = 25 days. Additional cash freed up = 25 x $1,000 = $25,000 that is no longer tied up in outstanding receivables.

  Q28. A business evaluates two payment processors: Processor A charges 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction. Processor B charges a flat 2.5% with no per-transaction fee. The business averages 500 transactions/month at $80 each. Which is cheaper?
    [ ] Both cost the same
    [ ] Processor A ($1,310/month) is cheaper
    [ ] Processor A ($1,000/month) is cheaper
    [*] Processor B ($1,000/month) is cheaper by $310/month
    Explanation: Processor A: ($80 x 0.029 + $0.30) x 500 = ($2.32 + $0.30) x 500 = $2.62 x 500 = $1,310/month. Processor B: $80 x 0.025 x 500 = $2.00 x 500 = $1,000/month. Processor B saves $310/month ($3,720/year).

  Q29. A business creates a financial model in Excel projecting 3 scenarios: Base case ($80K profit), Optimistic ($120K profit, 25% probability), and Pessimistic ($30K profit, 35% probability). Base case probability is 40%. What is the expected profit?
    [ ] $76,667
    [ ] $65,000
    [ ] $80,000
    [*] $72,500
    Explanation: Expected profit = ($120K x 0.25) + ($80K x 0.40) + ($30K x 0.35) = $30,000 + $32,000 + $10,500 = $72,500.

  Q30. A payroll service costs $40/month base plus $6/employee. A business with 12 employees pays manual payroll processing of 8 hours/month at $35/hour. Is the payroll service cost-effective?
    [ ] The costs are exactly equal

    [*] Yes, the service costs $112/month vs. $280/month manual, saving $168/month
    [ ] No, the service costs more than manual processing
    Explanation: Service cost = $40 + (12 x $6) = $40 + $72 = $112/month. Manual cost = 8 hours x $35 = $280/month. Monthly savings = $280 - $112 = $168 ($2,016/year), plus the service reduces error risk.
