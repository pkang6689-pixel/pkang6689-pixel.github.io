--- Lesson 9.1: Time Value of Money (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Time Value of Money
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is present value (PV)?
Present value is the current equivalent of a future amount of money, calculated by discounting it back at an appropriate interest or discount rate using the formula PV = FV / (1 + r)^n.
What is future value (FV)?
Future value is the amount that a present sum of money will be worth at a future date, given a specific rate of return, calculated using FV = PV x (1 + r)^n.
What is an annuity?
An annuity is a series of equal payments occurring at regular intervals, such as monthly mortgage payments, annual pension income, or regular contributions to a retirement account.
What is a perpetuity?
A perpetuity is an annuity with no end date -- it pays a fixed amount forever. Its present value is simply PV = Payment / r, where r is the discount rate.
What is a nominal interest rate?
The nominal rate is the stated or quoted rate that does not account for inflation. For example, a bank advertising 6% on a savings account is quoting the nominal rate.
What is the real rate of return?
The real rate of return is the nominal rate minus inflation. It measures how much your purchasing power actually increases. If you earn 8% nominally but inflation is 3%, your real return is approximately 5%.
What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math shortcut: divide 72 by the annual interest rate to estimate the number of years required for an investment to double in value.
A home purchased for $250,000 appreciates at 3% annually for 20 years. What is its approximate value?
FV = $250,000 x (1.03)^20 = $250,000 x 1.8061 = $451,525, approximately $452,000. Even a modest 3% annual growth compounds significantly over two decades.
$10,000 is invested at 6% for 20 years, and another $10,000 is invested at 8% for 20 years. What is the approximate difference in final value?
At 6%: $10,000 x (1.06)^20 = $32,071. At 8%: $10,000 x (1.08)^20 = $46,610. The difference is $14,539, approximately $14,500. A 2-percentage-point rate difference compounds into a significant gap over 20 years.
Real-World Applications
Example: If inflation averages 3% per year, what will be the purchasing power of $50,000 in 20 years?
Purchasing power = $50,000 x (1 - 0.03)^20 = $50,000 x (0.97)^20 = $50,000 x 0.5438 = $27,190, approximately $27,200. Inflation silently erodes nearly half the value over 20 years.
Example: Person A invests $10,000 at age 25 at 8% annually until age 65. Person B invests $10,000 at age 45 at 8% annually until age 65. Approximately how much more does Person A have at age 65?
Person A: $10,000 x (1.08)^40 = $217,245. Person B: $10,000 x (1.08)^20 = $46,610. Difference = $170,635, approximately $170,600. Starting 20 years earlier with the same amount results in nearly 5 times the final value, powerfully illustrating the time value of money.

--- Lesson 9.2: Net Present Value (NPV) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Present Value (NPV)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Net Present Value (NPV)?
NPV is the sum of all future cash flows discounted to present value minus the initial investment outlay. It tells you how much value a project adds or subtracts in today's dollars.
What is WACC, and why is it commonly used as the discount rate in NPV?
WACC is the Weighted Average Cost of Capital, combining the cost of debt and equity weighted by their proportions. It is used as the discount rate because a project must earn at least the WACC to create value for investors.
What is sensitivity analysis in the context of NPV?
Sensitivity analysis tests how robust the NPV conclusion is by varying key inputs. If small changes in assumptions flip NPV from positive to negative, the investment decision is risky.
What is the NPV decision rule?
The NPV decision rule is straightforward: accept all independent projects with positive NPV (they create value) and reject those with negative NPV (they destroy value).
What is the Excel formula for calculating NPV?
Excel's NPV function discounts cash flows starting at period 1, so you must add the initial investment (as a negative value at time zero) separately: =NPV(rate, Year1:Year5) + Initial_Investment.
A company invests $50,000 in a project that generates $15,000 per year for 5 years. At a 10% discount rate, the sum of all discounted cash flows is $56,862. What is the NPV?
NPV = Total PV of cash flows minus initial investment = $56,862 - $50,000 = $6,862. Since NPV is positive, the project earns more than the 10% required return and should be accepted.
The same $50,000 project ($15,000/year for 5 years) is re-evaluated at a 15% discount rate. The sum of discounted cash flows drops to $50,282. What is the new NPV, and what does this indicate?
NPV = $50,282 - $50,000 = $282. The project is still marginally acceptable, but the NPV dropped from $6,862 to $282 when the discount rate rose from 10% to 15%, showing strong rate sensitivity.
If the discount rate is further increased to 20%, the sum of discounted cash flows for the same project falls to $44,859. What is the NPV?
NPV = $44,859 - $50,000 = -$5,141. At a 20% discount rate, the project destroys value and should be rejected. This demonstrates how the same project can go from acceptable to unacceptable depending on the required return.
A startup requires a $30,000 investment and projects uneven cash flows: Year 1 = $5,000, Year 2 = $10,000, Year 3 = $15,000, Year 4 = $20,000. At an 8% discount rate, the total PV of cash flows is approximately $39,811. What is the NPV?
NPV = $39,811 - $30,000 = $9,811. Even though cash flows are uneven (growing over time), NPV handles each year's cash flow individually by discounting each to present value.
A company buys equipment for $60,000 that saves $18,000 per year in labor costs for 5 years. At a 12% discount rate, the PV of savings is approximately $64,890. What is the NPV, and what does it tell the manager?
NPV = $64,890 - $60,000 = $4,890. The positive NPV means the labor savings, when discounted at 12%, are worth more than the equipment cost. The purchase creates $4,890 in value.
A real estate investor buys a rental property for $200,000. Annual net operating income is $14,000 for 10 years, and the property will sell for $269,000 after 10 years. At an 8% discount rate, the PV of rental income is approximately $93,935 and the PV of the sale is approximately $124,600. What is the NPV?
NPV = PV of rental income + PV of sale - purchase price = $93,935 + $124,600 - $200,000 = $18,535. Real estate NPV must include both periodic cash flows and the terminal sale value.
Real-World Applications
Example: A company buys equipment for $60,000 that saves $18,000 per year in labor costs for 5 years. At a 12% discount rate, the PV of savings is approximately $64,890. What is the NPV, and what does it tell the manager?
NPV = $64,890 - $60,000 = $4,890. The positive NPV means the labor savings, when discounted at 12%, are worth more than the equipment cost. The purchase creates $4,890 in value.

--- Lesson 9.3: Internal Rate of Return (IRR) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Internal Rate of Return (IRR)?
IRR is the annualized rate of return that sets NPV to zero. It represents the project's true percentage return, accounting for the time value of money and the pattern of cash flows.
What is a hurdle rate?
The hurdle rate is the minimum acceptable rate of return for an investment. Projects with IRR above the hurdle rate are accepted; those below are rejected. It typically equals or exceeds the company's cost of capital.
What is the payback period?
The payback period measures how long it takes to recoup the initial investment from cash inflows. It is simple to calculate but ignores the time value of money and cash flows beyond the payback date.
What is the discounted payback period?
The discounted payback period improves upon the simple payback by discounting each cash flow before accumulating them. It tells you how long until you recover your investment in present-value terms.
What is the 'scale problem' with IRR?
Since IRR is a percentage, it does not reflect the dollar amount of value created. A 30% return on $1,000 ($300) creates far less wealth than a 15% return on $1,000,000 ($150,000).
A venture capitalist invests $1 million in a startup that returns $5 million after 5 years. What is the annualized IRR?
IRR = (Exit Value / Entry Price)^(1/years) - 1 = ($5M / $1M)^(1/5) - 1 = 5^0.2 - 1 = 1.3797 - 1 = 0.3797, approximately 38%. This is the annualized return over the 5-year holding period.
Another VC invests $1 million for a $10 million exit in 7 years. What is the approximate IRR?
IRR = (10/1)^(1/7) - 1 = 10^(0.1429) - 1 = 1.3895 - 1 = 0.3895, approximately 39%. Despite a 10x return vs. 5x, the IRR is similar because it took 7 years instead of 5.
Real-World Applications
Example: Multiple IRRs can occur when:
When cash flows alternate between positive and negative more than once (non-conventional cash flows), the NPV equation can have multiple solutions, producing multiple IRRs and making the metric unreliable.

--- Lesson 9.4: Financial Modeling (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Financial Modeling
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a financial model?
A financial model is a quantitative tool (typically built in a spreadsheet) that translates business assumptions into financial projections, enabling valuation, planning, and decision-making.
What is terminal value in a DCF model?
Terminal value captures the business's value beyond the explicit forecast period (e.g., years 6+ in a 5-year model). It represents the ongoing value of the business and often comprises the majority of total enterprise value.
What is Monte Carlo simulation?
Monte Carlo simulation uses random sampling from probability distributions to run thousands of model iterations, producing a distribution of possible outcomes rather than a single point estimate.
What is break-even analysis in a financial model?
Break-even analysis identifies the sales volume (or revenue level) at which a business covers all its costs -- both fixed and variable -- with zero profit or loss. It is a critical viability threshold.
What is the perpetuity growth method for calculating terminal value?
The perpetuity growth method assumes free cash flows grow at a constant rate forever beyond the forecast period. The formula TV = FCF x (1+g)/(r-g) capitalizes the growing cash stream into a single terminal value.
What is the output of a Monte Carlo simulation?
Monte Carlo produces a distribution of outcomes, allowing statements like 'there is a 90% probability that NPV exceeds zero' or 'the expected valuation range is $50M to $80M with 80% confidence.'
A company's Year 3 projection shows: Revenue = $132,250, COGS (40%) = $52,900, Operating Expenses = $15,000, and Tax Rate = 25%. What is the projected net income?
Gross profit = $132,250 - $52,900 = $79,350. Operating income = $79,350 - $15,000 = $64,350. Net income = $64,350 x (1 - 0.25) = $64,350 x 0.75 = $48,262.50, approximately $48,263.
Real-World Applications
Example: A modeler projects a SaaS company with 50% gross margins, but the industry average is 70-80%. This discrepancy suggests the model should:
Validation against industry benchmarks is essential. A 50% gross margin for a SaaS company (where 70-80% is typical) signals either an error in the model or a company-specific issue that should be documented and explained.
Example: A company projects free cash flow of $500,000 in Year 5, with a terminal growth rate of 2.5% and a WACC of 9%. The terminal value is approximately $7.88 million. If the present value of Years 1-5 cash flows totals $1.8 million and the PV of the terminal value is $5.12 million, what percentage of total enterprise value comes from the terminal value?
Total enterprise value = $1.8M + $5.12M = $6.92M. Terminal value percentage = $5.12M / $6.92M = 74%. This illustrates why terminal value assumptions are critical -- they often represent the majority of a company's valuation.

--- Lesson 9.5: Corporate Finance Fundamentals (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Corporate Finance Fundamentals
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is capital structure?
Capital structure describes how a company funds itself through a combination of debt (loans, bonds) and equity (stock, retained earnings). Different industries have different typical capital structures.
What is WACC?
WACC is the weighted average of the cost of debt (after tax) and the cost of equity, with weights based on the company's target capital structure. It represents the minimum return the company must earn to satisfy all investors.
What is dividend policy?
Dividend policy determines how much of a company's profits are paid to shareholders versus retained for growth. Growth companies often retain most earnings, while mature companies pay higher dividends.
What is Free Cash Flow (FCF)?
FCF measures the cash a company generates after accounting for capital expenditures needed to maintain or grow its asset base. It represents cash truly available for distribution to investors or reinvestment.
What is Enterprise Value (EV)?
EV = Market Cap + Debt - Cash. It represents the total value of a company to all capital providers. Debt is added because an acquirer assumes it; cash is subtracted because the acquirer effectively gets it back.
What is working capital management?
Working capital management ensures a company can meet short-term obligations and fund daily operations by efficiently managing inventory, accounts receivable, and accounts payable.
A company has market capitalization of $500 million, total debt of $100 million, and cash of $50 million. What is the Enterprise Value?
EV = Market Cap + Debt - Cash = $500M + $100M - $50M = $550M. This is what it would theoretically cost to acquire the entire company, including assuming its debt and receiving its cash.
A company borrows at 7% with a 30% tax rate. What is the after-tax cost of debt?
After-tax cost of debt = 7% x (1 - 0.30) = 7% x 0.70 = 4.9%. The 30% tax rate means the government effectively subsidizes 30% of the interest cost through tax deductions.
Using CAPM: risk-free rate = 2%, beta = 0.8, expected market return = 9%. What is the cost of equity?
Cost of equity = 2% + 0.8 x (9% - 2%) = 2% + 0.8 x 7% = 2% + 5.6% = 7.6%. The lower beta (0.8) reflects less market risk, resulting in a lower required return than the overall market.
A company has 50% debt at 4.9% after-tax cost and 50% equity at 7.6% cost. What is the WACC?
WACC = (0.50 x 4.9%) + (0.50 x 7.6%) = 2.45% + 3.80% = 6.25%. This is the minimum return the company's projects must earn to create value for investors.
A company reports net income of $2 million, depreciation of $500,000, a $200,000 increase in working capital, and capital expenditures of $800,000. What is the Free Cash Flow?
Operating CF = Net Income + Depreciation - Increase in Working Capital = $2M + $0.5M - $0.2M = $2.3M. FCF = Operating CF - CapEx = $2.3M - $0.8M = $1.5M. This is the cash truly available to investors.
Real-World Applications
Example: Debt financing has a tax advantage because:
Interest expenses on debt are tax-deductible, creating a 'tax shield' that reduces the company's taxable income. Dividends paid to equity holders are not tax-deductible.
Example: What is working capital management?
Working capital management ensures a company can meet short-term obligations and fund daily operations by efficiently managing inventory, accounts receivable, and accounts payable.
Example: A company has $10 million in total assets and $4 million in total equity. Its financial leverage ratio is:
Financial leverage ratio = Total Assets / Total Equity = $10M / $4M = 2.5. This means for every $1 of equity, the company controls $2.50 in assets. The remaining $6M of assets is financed by debt.

--- Lesson 9.6: Risk Analysis & Quantification (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Risk Analysis & Quantification
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is standard deviation in the context of investment risk?
Standard deviation measures the dispersion of returns around the mean. A stock with 20% average return and 30% standard deviation might return anywhere from -10% to +50% in a typical year (one standard deviation range).
What is the Sharpe ratio?
The Sharpe ratio = (Portfolio return - Risk-free rate) / Portfolio standard deviation. It quantifies how much excess return you receive per unit of risk taken, allowing comparison across different investments.
What is Value at Risk (VaR)?
VaR answers the question: 'What is the most I could lose, at a given confidence level, over a given period?' For example, a daily VaR of $50,000 at 99% confidence means there is only a 1% chance of losing more than $50,000 in a day.
What is the efficient frontier?
The efficient frontier is a curve on a risk-return graph showing the best possible portfolios. Any portfolio below the frontier is suboptimal because you could achieve either more return for the same risk or less risk for the same return.
What is Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)?
MPT, developed by Harry Markowitz in 1952, revolutionized investing by mathematically proving that combining assets with different correlations can reduce portfolio risk below the risk of any individual asset.
What is systematic risk?
Systematic risk (market risk) affects the entire economy or market: recessions, inflation, interest rate changes, and geopolitical events. It cannot be eliminated through diversification and is measured by beta.
What is unsystematic risk?
Unsystematic risk is specific to individual companies: a CEO departure, product failure, or lawsuit. Holding 20-30 diversified stocks eliminates most unsystematic risk because individual company events tend to cancel out.
What is maximum drawdown?
Maximum drawdown measures the largest percentage drop from a peak to the subsequent trough. A 40% maximum drawdown means the portfolio lost 40% of its value at its worst point before recovering.
What is the risk-return tradeoff?
The risk-return tradeoff is a fundamental principle: investors demand higher expected returns as compensation for taking on greater risk. Without this premium, no rational investor would choose riskier assets.
An investor allocates 60% to stocks (expected 10% return), 30% to bonds (expected 4% return), and 10% to cash (expected 2% return). What is the portfolio's expected return?
Expected return = (0.60 x 10%) + (0.30 x 4%) + (0.10 x 2%) = 6.0% + 1.2% + 0.2% = 7.4%. The portfolio's expected return is the weighted average of each asset class's expected return.
Real-World Applications
Example: Investment A has a 10% average return with 15% standard deviation. Investment B has a 10% average return with 5% standard deviation. Which is riskier?
Both investments offer 10% average returns, but A has a 15% standard deviation versus B's 5%. Investment A's returns swing much more widely, making it riskier for the same expected reward.

--- Lesson 9.7: AP Economics & Finance Connections (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: AP Economics & Finance Connections
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Gross Domestic Product (GDP)?
GDP is the broadest measure of economic activity, capturing the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders during a specific time period.
What is inflation?
Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices rises, eroding purchasing power. If inflation is 3%, something that costs $100 today will cost $103 next year, and your $100 buys less.
What is monetary policy?
Monetary policy refers to the Federal Reserve's use of tools like interest rate adjustments, open market operations, and reserve requirements to control the money supply and achieve economic objectives.
What is fiscal policy?
Fiscal policy involves government spending and taxation decisions made by Congress and signed by the President. It can be expansionary (more spending, lower taxes) or contractionary (less spending, higher taxes).
What is the yield curve?
The yield curve plots interest rates (yields) of bonds with different maturities. Normally it slopes upward because longer-term bonds demand higher yields to compensate for greater uncertainty.
What is the Consumer Price Index (CPI)?
CPI tracks the price changes of a representative basket of consumer goods and services over time. It is the most widely used measure of inflation and influences Fed policy, Social Security adjustments, and cost-of-living calculations.
What is opportunity cost?
Opportunity cost is what you sacrifice by choosing one option over another. If you invest $10,000 in stocks and could have earned 4% in a savings account, the opportunity cost of your stock investment is that 4% guaranteed return.
What is comparative advantage?
Comparative advantage means producing something at a lower opportunity cost than others. It drives specialization and trade: countries (and individuals) benefit by focusing on what they do relatively best and trading for the rest.
CPI increases from 280 to 290 over one year. What is the approximate annual inflation rate?
Inflation rate = (New CPI - Old CPI) / Old CPI x 100 = (290 - 280) / 280 x 100 = 10/280 x 100 = 3.57%, approximately 3.6%. This measures how much the price of a typical basket of goods increased over the year.
Real-World Applications
Example: What is comparative advantage?
Comparative advantage means producing something at a lower opportunity cost than others. It drives specialization and trade: countries (and individuals) benefit by focusing on what they do relatively best and trading for the rest.

--- Lesson 9.8: International Finance Case Studies (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: International Finance Case Studies
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a currency exchange rate?
An exchange rate tells you how much one currency is worth in terms of another. For example, EUR/USD = 1.10 means 1 euro costs $1.10. Exchange rates fluctuate based on trade flows, interest rate differentials, and economic conditions.
What is a carry trade?
Carry trades exploit interest rate differentials between countries. The strategy is profitable as long as exchange rates remain stable, but adverse currency movements can quickly wipe out gains.
What is sovereign risk?
Sovereign risk is the danger that a national government will fail to meet its financial obligations. This can include outright default, forced currency devaluation, or restrictions on capital leaving the country.
What is Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)?
PPP suggests that in the long run, exchange rates should equalize the price of an identical basket of goods across countries. The Big Mac Index is a popular informal PPP measure comparing burger prices worldwide.
What is transfer pricing in international finance?
Transfer pricing allows multinational corporations to allocate profits across countries by setting prices on internal transactions. Companies may use this to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions, which is why tax authorities closely monitor transfer pricing.
What is the benefit of international diversification?
International diversification reduces portfolio risk because economic conditions differ across countries. When one market struggles, others may thrive, smoothing overall portfolio returns.
What is a forward contract in currency hedging?
A forward contract locks in a future exchange rate today, allowing businesses and investors to eliminate currency uncertainty. The tradeoff is giving up potential gains if the currency moves favorably.
A US investor considering Samsung (South Korean company) can buy ADRs on the NYSE or buy shares directly on the Korean exchange. What is the main advantage of the ADR?
ADRs provide convenience: they trade in dollars, on familiar exchanges, during US hours, and with standard US settlement. However, the underlying value still depends on the foreign stock price and exchange rate, so currency risk remains.
Real-World Applications
Example: Financial advisors typically recommend that US investors allocate what percentage of their equity portfolio to international stocks?
Most financial advisors recommend 20-40% international equity allocation. This provides meaningful diversification benefits from different economic cycles while maintaining a domestic bias for currency stability and familiarity.
