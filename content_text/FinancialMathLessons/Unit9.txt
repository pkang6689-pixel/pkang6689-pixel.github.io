--- Lesson 9.1: Time Value of Money (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Time Value of Money
Time Value of Money (TVM)Core Principle
A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because it can be invested to earn a return.
Key Formulas  • Future Value: FV = PV × (1 + r)^n  • Present Value: PV = FV / (1 + r)^n  • FV of Annuity: FV = PMT × [((1+r)^n − 1) / r]  • PV of Annuity: PV = PMT × [(1 − (1+r)^−n) / r]

--- Lesson 9.2: Net Present Value (NPV) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Present Value (NPV)
Net Present ValueNPV Formula
NPV = −Initial Investment + Σ (Cash Flow_t / (1+r)^t)

If NPV > 0, the investment adds value. If NPV Decision Rule
Accept projects with NPV > 0. Among multiple projects, choose the highest NPV.

--- Lesson 9.3: Internal Rate of Return (IRR) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
Internal Rate of ReturnIRR Definition
The discount rate that makes NPV = 0. It's the project's effective annual return.
Decision Rule
If IRR > required return (hurdle rate): accept. If IRR < hurdle rate: reject.

--- Lesson 9.5: Corporate Finance Fundamentals (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Corporate Finance Fundamentals
Corporate FinanceCapital Structure
The mix of debt (loans, bonds) and equity (stock, retained earnings) used to finance a company.
Cost of Capital  • Cost of Debt: Interest rate on borrowing × (1 − tax rate) — interest is tax-deductible.  • Cost of Equity: Return required by shareholders (typically estimated via CAPM).  • WACC: Weighted average of debt and equity costs.

    Explanation: While some debt is beneficial (interest is tax-deductible and leverages equity returns), too much debt creates existential risk. Interest payments are fixed obligations regardless of revenue — if sales drop, the company still owes the same amount, potentially leading to default or bankruptcy. This is why debt-to-equity ratios and interest coverage ratios are closely watched by analysts and creditors.

  Q10. Optimal capital structure balances:
    [ ] Only debt
    [ ] Only equity
    [*] The tax benefits of debt against the increased bankruptcy risk of too much debt
    [ ] Revenue and expenses
    Explanation: Tradeoff theory.

  Q11. Modigliani-Miller theorem (with taxes) states:

    [ ] All debt is bad
    [ ] All equity is best
    Explanation: MM theorem.

  Q12. Retained earnings are:
    [ ] External equity

    [*] Internal equity — profits reinvested in the business rather than paid as dividends (cheapest form of equity)
    [ ] Cash only
    Explanation: Internal financing.

  Q13. Dividend policy decisions involve:
    [ ] Only paying dividends
    [*] Whether to pay profits as dividends (returning cash to shareholders) or retain them for growth and reinvestment
    [ ] Only retaining profits
    [ ] Only stock buybacks
    Explanation: Payout decisions.

  Q14. Stock buybacks reduce:
    [ ] Revenue
    [ ] Debt
    [*] Shares outstanding (increasing EPS and often stock price — an alternative to dividends)
    [ ] Expenses
    Explanation: Share repurchase.

  Q15. Free Cash Flow (FCF) = Operating cash flow minus:
    [ ] Revenue
    [*] Capital expenditures (the cash available to pay debt holders and equity holders)
    [ ] Dividends
    [ ] Stock price
    Explanation: FCF definition.

  Q16. Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Debt − Cash. If market cap = $500M, debt = $100M, cash = $50M:
    [ ] $500M
    [ ] $600M
    [*] $550M
    [ ] $450M

  Q17. EV/EBITDA ratio is used to:
    [ ] Calculate revenue
    [*] Value and compare companies (a lower ratio may indicate undervaluation)
    [ ] Only measure debt
    [ ] Only measure cash flow
    Explanation: Valuation multiple.

  Q18. Financial leverage ratio = Total Assets / Total Equity. $10M assets / $4M equity =
    [ ] 0.4

--- Lesson 9.6: Risk Analysis & Quantification (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Risk Analysis & Quantification
Risk AnalysisMeasuring Risk  • Standard Deviation: Measures volatility of returns (higher = more risk).  • Sharpe Ratio: (Return − Risk-free Rate) / Standard Deviation — risk-adjusted return.  • Value at Risk (VaR): Maximum expected loss at a given confidence level.

--- Lesson 9.6: Risk Analysis & Quantification (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Standard deviation in finance measures:
    [ ] Average return
    [ ] Total return
    [*] Volatility — how much returns deviate from the average (higher σ = more uncertainty)
    [ ] Only losses
    Explanation: Dispersion of returns.

  Q2. Investment A: 10% avg return, 15% std dev. Investment B: 10% avg return, 5% std dev. Which is riskier?
    [ ] B
    [ ] They're equal
    [*] A (same return but 3× the volatility — wider range of possible outcomes)
    [ ] Neither
    Explanation: Same return, different risk.

  Q3. The Sharpe ratio for A (10% return, 3% risk-free, 15% std dev) =
    [ ] 0.67
    [*] 0.47 ((10−3)/15)
    [ ] 0.33
    [ ] 1.0
    Explanation: Sharpe A.

  Q4. The Sharpe ratio for B (10% return, 3% risk-free, 5% std dev) =
    [ ] 0.47
    [ ] 1.0
    [*] 1.4 ((10−3)/5)
    [ ] 2.0
    Explanation: Sharpe B.

  Q5. Investor should choose _____ because it has a higher Sharpe ratio.
    [ ] A
    [*] B (same return with much less risk — more efficient risk-adjusted return)
    [ ] Either
    [ ] Neither
    Explanation: Risk-adjusted choice.

  Q6. A Sharpe ratio above 1.0 is generally considered:
    [ ] Poor
    [ ] Average
    [*] Good (the investment's excess return exceeds its risk)
    [ ] Impossible
    Explanation: Sharpe benchmark.

  Q7. VaR at 95% confidence of $100,000 means:
    [ ] You'll lose exactly $100K
    [ ] You'll definitely lose
    [*] There's a 95% chance you'll lose LESS than $100K in the specified period (5% chance of losing more)
    [ ] You'll gain $100K
    Explanation: VaR interpretation.

  Q8. Correlation of +1 between two stocks means:
    [ ] They're unrelated
    [*] They move perfectly together (no diversification benefit)
    [ ] They move oppositely
    [ ] They're uncorrelated
    Explanation: Perfect positive.

  Q9. Correlation of −1 means:
    [ ] They move together
    [*] They move in exactly opposite directions (maximum diversification benefit)
    [ ] They're unrelated
    [ ] Correlation is zero
    Explanation: Perfect negative.

  Q10. A portfolio of stocks with low correlation to each other has:
    [ ] Higher total risk
    [*] Lower total risk than the average of individual risks (diversification effect)
    [ ] Same total risk
    [ ] No return
    Explanation: Diversification benefit.

  Q11. The efficient frontier shows:
    [ ] One perfect portfolio
    [*] The set of portfolios offering maximum return for each level of risk (or minimum risk for each return level)
    [ ] All possible portfolios
    [ ] Only high-risk portfolios
    Explanation: Optimal portfolios.

  Q12. Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) was developed by:
    [ ] Warren Buffett
    [ ] Benjamin Graham
    [*] Harry Markowitz (1952) — foundational theory that diversification reduces portfolio risk
    [ ] John Bogle
    Explanation: MPT originator.

  Q13. Portfolio A: 12% return, 18% std dev. Portfolio B: 10% return, 10% std dev. If risk-free = 3%:
    [ ] A is clearly better
    [*] A Sharpe = 0.50, B Sharpe = 0.70 — B is better risk-adjusted despite lower absolute return
    [ ] B is clearly worse
    [ ] They're equal
    Explanation: Comparing portfolios.

  Q14. Systematic risk (market risk) can _____ be diversified away.
    [ ] Always
    [*] Never (it affects the entire market: interest rates, recessions, geopolitical events)
    [ ] Sometimes
    [ ] Only with bonds
    Explanation: Non-diversifiable.

  Q15. Unsystematic risk (company-specific) can _____ be diversified away.
    [ ] Never
    [*] Usually (holding 20-30 diversified stocks eliminates most company-specific risk)
    [ ] Partially, but requires 1000+ stocks
    [ ] Only with options
    Explanation: Diversifiable.

  Q16. Beta measures only _____ risk.
    [ ] Total
    [ ] Unsystematic
    [*] Systematic (market-related volatility that cannot be diversified away)
    [ ] No
    Explanation: Beta and systematic risk.

  Q17. The risk-return tradeoff states:
    [ ] Higher risk, lower return
    [*] Higher risk investments must offer higher EXPECTED returns to attract investors (compensation for bearing risk)
    [ ] Risk and return are unrelated
    [ ] Lower risk, higher return
    Explanation: Fundamental tradeoff.

  Q18. Stress testing a portfolio involves:
    [ ] Normal conditions only
    [*] Simulating extreme scenarios (market crash, interest rate spike, pandemic) to see how the portfolio performs under stress
    [ ] Only past performance
    [ ] Only one scenario
    Explanation: Extreme scenario analysis.

  Q19. Maximum drawdown measures:
    [ ] Average loss
    [*] The largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value (how bad the worst period was)
    [ ] Daily volatility
    [ ] Only single-day losses
    Explanation: Worst decline.

  Q20. For financial math, risk analysis provides:
    [ ] Subjective judgment
    [*] Quantitative tools (σ, Sharpe, VaR, correlation) to objectively compare investments and construct optimal portfolios

    [ ] Only intuition
    Explanation: Quantitative risk assessment.

  Key Concepts: International Finance Case Studies
International Finance Case StudiesCase 1: Currency Impact on Investment Returns
A US investor buys European stock at €100 when 1 EUR = 1.10 USD (cost: $110). Stock rises 10% to €110. If EUR weakens to 1 EUR = 1.05 USD: $110 × 1.05 = $115.50 return on $110 cost = 5% return (vs. 10% in euros).
Case 2: Emerging Market Opportunities
Higher growth potential (7-8% GDP) vs. developed markets (2-3%), but with currency risk, political risk, and volatility.
