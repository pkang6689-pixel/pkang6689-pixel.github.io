--- Lesson 3.1: Loan Terminology & Concepts (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Loan Terminology & Concepts
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is APR (Annual Percentage Rate) for a loan?
APR includes the interest rate plus origination fees, closing costs, and other charges, expressed as an annual percentage. It provides a more complete picture of borrowing costs than the interest rate alone and allows for comparison across lenders.
What is a 'co-signer' on a loan?
A co-signer is equally responsible for the debt. If the primary borrower defaults, the co-signer must repay. Co-signing can help a borrower with limited credit qualify for a loan, but it carries significant risk for the co-signer.
What is the 'debt-to-income ratio' (DTI)?
DTI measures what portion of your income goes toward debt payments. Lenders use it to assess whether you can afford additional debt. Most lenders prefer a DTI below 36%, and 43% is typically the maximum for qualified mortgages.
What is an 'origination fee'?
An origination fee is an upfront charge from the lender to cover the cost of processing the loan. It is usually expressed as a percentage of the loan amount (e.g., 0.5% to 1%) and is included in APR calculations.
What is a 'prepayment penalty'?
Some lenders charge prepayment penalties to recoup lost interest income when borrowers pay off loans early. It is important to check for this provision before signing a loan agreement, as it can make refinancing or early payoff costly.
What is 'refinancing' a loan?
Refinancing involves taking out a new loan to pay off the existing one. Borrowers refinance to lower their interest rate, change their loan term, or access home equity. Refinancing involves closing costs, so a break-even analysis is essential.
Kevin earns $6,000 per month gross income and has the following monthly debt payments: $300 car loan, $200 student loan, and $150 credit card minimum. What is his debt-to-income ratio, and would he likely qualify for a mortgage under the 43% DTI threshold?
Current DTI = ($300 + $200 + $150) / $6,000 = $650 / $6,000 = 10.8%. Since 43% of $6,000 is $2,580, Kevin could add up to $1,930 in mortgage payments ($2,580 - $650) and still remain under the 43% threshold.
Maria has a $250,000 mortgage at 7% and is considering refinancing to 5.5%. Closing costs are $5,000, and the refinance would save her $250 per month. What is her break-even point?
Break-even = closing costs / monthly savings = $5,000 / $250 = 20 months. If Maria plans to stay in her home for more than 20 months, refinancing saves her money. After the break-even point, she saves $250 every month.
Lisa cosigns a $20,000 car loan for her nephew. After six months, her nephew stops making payments and the lender demands payment. What is Lisa's legal obligation?
As a co-signer, Lisa is equally liable for the full loan balance. The missed payments and potential default will damage her credit score. This illustrates why co-signing carries significant financial risk even without receiving any benefit from the loan.
A couple earns $10,000 per month gross income. They have $800 in existing monthly debt payments and want to buy a home. Under the 28% housing rule, what is the maximum they should spend on total housing costs (PITI)?
Under the 28% rule: $10,000 x 0.28 = $2,800 maximum for housing costs (PITI). Additionally, their total DTI should stay below 36%: $10,000 x 0.36 = $3,600 total debt allowed, minus $800 existing debt = $2,800 available for housing, which happens to match.

PMI protects the lender (not the borrower) if you default. It is required on conventional mortgages with less than 20% down and can be removed once you reach 20% equity.

  Q1. The principal of a loan refers to:

    Explanation: Secured loans are backed by collateral such as a house (mortgage) or car (auto loan). If the borrower fails to repay, the lender can seize the collateral.

    [ ] Zero, despite what intuition might initially suggest

    Explanation: Without collateral backing the loan, lenders charge higher rates to compensate for the increased risk of non-repayment. Credit cards and personal loans are common unsecured debts.

--- Lesson 3.2: Amortization Schedules (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Amortization Schedules
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'amortization schedule'?
An amortization schedule shows the payment number, interest portion, principal portion, and remaining balance for every payment. It reveals how the interest-to-principal ratio shifts over time, with early payments being mostly interest.
What is a 'balloon payment'?
A balloon payment is a large sum due at the end of a loan where regular payments did not fully amortize the balance. This can be risky because the borrower must either pay the lump sum, refinance, or sell the asset.
A $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years has a monthly payment of $1,199.10. If the first month's interest is $1,000, how much principal is paid in the first month, and what is the remaining balance after payment 1?
Principal paid = payment - interest = $1,199.10 - $1,000 = $199.10. Remaining balance = $200,000 - $199.10 = $199,800.90. Only about 17% of the first payment reduces the principal.
Compare two mortgages on $200,000: Mortgage A is 6% for 30 years (payment: $1,199, total interest: $231,676). Mortgage B is 6% for 15 years (payment: $1,688, total interest: $103,788). What is the monthly payment difference and total interest savings of choosing the 15-year option?
Monthly difference: $1,688 - $1,199 = $489. Interest savings: $231,676 - $103,788 = $127,888. By paying $489 more per month, you save nearly $128,000 in interest and own your home free and clear 15 years sooner.
A homeowner has a $250,000 mortgage at 7% for 30 years (payment: $1,663). After 5 years, rates drop and she refinances the remaining balance of approximately $238,000 to 5% for 25 years. Closing costs are $4,000. What is her new payment and approximate monthly savings?
New payment on $238,000 at 5% for 25 years: approximately $1,391. Monthly savings: $1,663 - $1,391 = $272. Break-even: $4,000 / $272 = approximately 15 months. Over 25 years, net savings (after closing costs) exceed $77,000.
A $350,000 mortgage at 5.5% for 30 years has a monthly payment of $1,987.26. The total interest over the life of the loan is approximately $365,414. What is the ratio of total interest to original principal?
Interest-to-principal ratio: $365,414 / $350,000 = 1.044. The borrower pays slightly more in interest than the original loan amount. Total cost: $350,000 + $365,414 = $715,414, more than double the purchase price.
Real-World Applications
Example: For a $200,000 mortgage at 6% annual rate for 30 years, the monthly rate r is:
When making monthly payments, the annual rate must be divided by 12 to get the monthly rate: 6% / 12 = 0.5% = 0.005 as a decimal.
Example: The total number of payments (n) for a 30-year monthly mortgage is:
Total monthly payments = 30 years x 12 months per year = 360 payments.
Example: In early mortgage payments, the majority of each payment goes toward:
Since interest is calculated on the outstanding balance, early payments are interest-heavy. As the principal is gradually paid down, more of each subsequent payment goes toward principal.
Example: The monthly payment on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years is approximately $1,199.10. Total payments over 30 years equal:
$1,199.10 x 360 payments = $431,676. This means the total interest paid is $431,676 - $200,000 = $231,676, which is more than the original loan amount.
Example: Biweekly mortgage payments (26 half-payments per year) effectively result in how many full monthly payments per year?
26 half-payments / 2 = 13 full monthly payments per year instead of 12. This one extra payment per year goes entirely to principal, significantly reducing total interest and loan duration.
Example: The monthly payment on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 15 years is approximately $1,688. Total interest paid is approximately:
Total payments: $1,688 x 180 = $303,840. Total interest: $303,840 - $200,000 = $103,840 (approximately $103,788). This is $127,888 less than the 30-year option, saving over half the interest despite higher monthly payments.
Example: Making an extra $100 per month toward principal on a $200,000 mortgage at 6% for 30 years saves approximately:
An extra $100/month significantly accelerates principal paydown. Since early payments are mostly interest, even small additional principal payments compound into massive savings over time.
Example: What does 'equity' mean in the context of a mortgage?
Home equity equals the current market value minus what you still owe. Equity builds through principal payments and property appreciation. On a $300,000 home with a $200,000 remaining mortgage, your equity is $100,000.

Due to front-loaded interest, principal paydown is much slower in the first half of the loan. After 15 years of a 30-year mortgage at 6%, only about 30% of the original principal has been repaid, with roughly $140,000 still owed on a $200,000 loan.

--- Lesson 3.3: Mortgage Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mortgage Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'escrow account' in mortgage banking?
An escrow account ensures that property taxes and insurance are paid on time. The lender collects 1/12 of the annual tax and insurance amounts with each mortgage payment and makes those payments when they are due.
What is 'PMI' (Private Mortgage Insurance)?
PMI protects the lender if you default. It is an additional monthly cost, typically 0.5-1% of the loan amount annually. PMI can be removed once you reach 20% equity in the home.
What is 'title insurance'?
Title insurance protects against legal challenges to property ownership, such as undisclosed liens, forgery, or errors in public records. It is a one-time cost paid at closing and is typically required by lenders.
Refinancing a $280,000 mortgage from 6.5% to 5% (remaining 25 years) changes the monthly payment from approximately $1,770 to approximately $1,500. If closing costs are $5,000, what is the break-even point and total savings over the remaining term?
Monthly savings: $1,770 - $1,500 = $270. Break-even: $5,000 / $270 = 18.5 months. Total savings over 25 years: ($270 x 300) - $5,000 = $81,000 - $5,000 = $76,000.
A family's home is appraised at $450,000. They owe $290,000 on their mortgage. Their annual property tax is 1.5% of the assessed value, and homeowner's insurance is $2,400/year. What is their monthly escrow payment for taxes and insurance?
Annual property tax: $450,000 x 0.015 = $6,750. Monthly tax: $6,750 / 12 = $562.50. Monthly insurance: $2,400 / 12 = $200. Total monthly escrow: $562.50 + $200 = $762.50. This is added to the principal and interest payment.
A homeowner purchased their home for $350,000 with a $280,000 mortgage. After 10 years, the home is worth $420,000 and they owe $220,000. What is their current home equity and LTV ratio?
Equity = market value - mortgage balance = $420,000 - $220,000 = $200,000. LTV = mortgage balance / market value = $220,000 / $420,000 = 52.4%. The homeowner has built significant equity through both principal payments and home appreciation.
A couple is considering a 5/1 ARM at 4.5% initial rate versus a 30-year fixed at 6.0% on a $350,000 mortgage. They plan to stay 7 years. If the ARM adjusts to 7.5% in year 6, what is the approximate total cost difference over 7 years?
ARM: 5 years at ~$1,773/month + 2 years at ~$2,350/month = ($1,773 x 60) + ($2,350 x 24) = $106,380 + $56,400 = $162,780. Fixed: $2,098 x 84 = $176,232. ARM savings: approximately $13,452 plus additional savings from faster principal paydown in years 1-5. Even with the rate jump, the ARM is cheaper for a 7-year hold.
Real-World Applications
Example: One discount point on a $280,000 mortgage costs:

Example: A 5/1 ARM (Adjustable Rate Mortgage) means:
In a 5/1 ARM, the rate is fixed for the first 5 years (the initial period) and then adjusts once per year (the adjustment period). ARMs typically have rate caps limiting how much the rate can increase.

--- Lesson 3.4: Credit Card Mathematics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Credit Card Mathematics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'annual fee' on a credit card?
Annual fees are charged by some cards, especially premium rewards cards with travel perks or high cashback rates. The card is only worthwhile if the value of rewards and benefits exceeds the annual fee.
A $500 impulse purchase on a 20% APR credit card is paid off over 2 years with monthly payments. The total interest paid is approximately $111. What is the effective markup on the original purchase?
Total cost: $500 + $111 = $611. Markup: $111 / $500 = 22.2%. Every purchase financed on a credit card effectively costs more than the sticker price. This is the hidden cost of carrying a credit card balance.
A cardholder earns 2% cashback on all purchases and spends $3,000/month on the card. They pay the full balance every month. What is their annual cashback earnings, and what would happen if they carried even a $1,000 balance at 20% APR instead?
Annual cashback: $3,000 x 12 x 0.02 = $720. If carrying $1,000 at 20%: $200/year interest. Net: $720 - $200 = $520. While still positive, the $200 in interest significantly reduces the reward value. Paying in full maximizes the benefit.
A college student gets their first credit card with a $1,000 limit. They charge $800 in the first month. What is their credit utilization, and how does this affect their credit score?
Utilization: $800 / $1,000 = 80%. Recommended: below 30%, ideally below 10%. At 80%, this heavily impacts credit score since utilization is about 30% of the FICO score. Even if paid in full by the due date, the high utilization may be reported to credit bureaus before the payment is made.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'average daily balance' mean for credit card interest calculations?
Most credit cards use the average daily balance method. Each day's balance is added up and divided by the number of days in the billing cycle. Interest = average daily balance x daily periodic rate x number of days. Paying early in the cycle reduces the average and thus the interest.
Example: Compare: $10,000 credit card debt at 22% APR versus $10,000 mortgage at 6%. How much more annual interest does the credit card cost?
Credit card: $10,000 x 0.22 = $2,200 annual interest. Mortgage: $10,000 x 0.06 = $600 annual interest. Difference: $1,600 per year. The credit card costs 3.67 times more in interest, making it the most expensive common debt.
Example: A college student gets their first credit card with a $1,000 limit. They charge $800 in the first month. What is their credit utilization, and how does this affect their credit score?
Utilization: $800 / $1,000 = 80%. Recommended: below 30%, ideally below 10%. At 80%, this heavily impacts credit score since utilization is about 30% of the FICO score. Even if paid in full by the due date, the high utilization may be reported to credit bureaus before the payment is made.

--- Lesson 3.5: Student Loan Analysis (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Student Loan Analysis
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is interest capitalization in the context of student loans?
Interest capitalization occurs when accrued but unpaid interest is added to the loan's principal balance, meaning the borrower subsequently pays interest on a larger amount.
What is the difference between a subsidized and unsubsidized federal student loan?
The key distinction is that the government covers interest on subsidized loans during enrollment, grace period, and deferment, while unsubsidized loans accrue interest from the day funds are disbursed.
What is the meaning of 'discretionary income' as used in income-driven repayment plans?
For federal income-driven plans, discretionary income is specifically defined as the borrower's adjusted gross income minus 150% of the poverty guideline for their family size and state.
What is a 'Master Promissory Note' (MPN)?
The MPN is a binding legal document that outlines the borrower's obligation to repay the loan, the interest rate, repayment terms, and borrower rights. It can cover multiple loan disbursements over up to 10 years.

--- Lesson 3.7: Case Studies in Debt Management (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Debt Management
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'debt avalanche' method?
The debt avalanche strategy involves making minimum payments on all debts except the one with the highest interest rate, which receives all available extra funds. Once that debt is paid off, the freed-up money rolls to the next highest rate.
What is 'lifestyle inflation' (lifestyle creep)?
Lifestyle inflation occurs when people upgrade their spending habits every time their income increases, channeling raises into upgraded expenses rather than debt payoff or savings, keeping them in the same financial position.
What is 'Chapter 13 bankruptcy'?
Chapter 13 allows individuals to keep their property while repaying debts through a court-approved 3-5 year plan. It stays on credit reports for 7 years (vs. 10 for Chapter 7) and is suited for those with regular income.
What is 'debt fatigue'?
Debt fatigue is a psychological phenomenon where borrowers become discouraged by slow progress and abandon their repayment plan. The snowball method's quick wins help combat this by providing early motivation.
What is a 'hardship program' offered by creditors?
Many creditors offer hardship programs for borrowers experiencing job loss, medical emergencies, or other financial difficulties. These may temporarily reduce rates or payments but typically require documentation of hardship.
Real-World Applications
Example: A debt management plan (DMP) through a nonprofit credit counselor:
A DMP through a reputable nonprofit counselor negotiates reduced interest rates with creditors, combines multiple payments into one, and typically takes 3-5 years to complete. It is far less damaging to credit than bankruptcy.
Example: A couple earns $7,000/month gross and has the following monthly debt payments: mortgage $1,800, car loan $400, student loans $300, credit card minimums $200. Their DTI ratio is:
Total monthly debt payments: $1,800 + $400 + $300 + $200 = $2,700. DTI = $2,700 / $7,000 = 38.6%. This is below the 43% QM threshold but above the preferred 36% level, suggesting they should reduce non-housing debt.
Example: David consolidated $30,000 in credit card debt (average 22% APR) into a personal loan at 10% for 5 years. His monthly payment is $637. After 2 years of on-time payments, he has accumulated $8,000 in new credit card debt. What went wrong?
David fell into the consolidation trap: he freed up credit card capacity by paying them off with the loan, then accumulated $8,000 in new charges. His total debt is now the remaining consolidation loan balance plus $8,000 — more than the original $30,000.

--- Lesson 3.8: Loan Comparison & Decision-Making (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Loan Comparison & Decision-Making
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'HELOC' (Home Equity Line of Credit)?
A HELOC works like a credit card secured by your home. It has a draw period (often 10 years) where you can borrow and repay flexibly, followed by a repayment period. The risk is that your home is collateral.
What is 'TRID' in mortgage lending?
TRID combined two older disclosure requirements (Truth in Lending Act and RESPA) into two standardized forms: the Loan Estimate (provided within 3 days of application) and the Closing Disclosure (provided 3 days before closing).
What is 'payment anchoring' as a cognitive bias?
Payment anchoring causes borrowers to evaluate loans primarily by monthly payment rather than total cost. A $189/month payment looks better than $291/month, but the total cost difference can be hundreds or thousands of dollars.
Real-World Applications
Example: If your mortgage rate is 4% and investments return 8%, extra money is mathematically better:
Mathematically, investing at 8% earns more than the 4% cost of the mortgage. However, investment returns are uncertain while the mortgage interest is guaranteed, making this a risk-adjusted decision.
Example: Paying off a 4% mortgage gives a _____ return compared to uncertain 8% investment returns.
Paying off a 4% mortgage is equivalent to earning a guaranteed 4% return on your money. While investments may yield more, the certainty of eliminating debt has psychological and financial value.
Example: When comparing adjustable-rate vs. fixed-rate mortgages, you should consider:
ARM evaluation requires analyzing the worst case (maximum rate after adjustments), your ownership timeline (ARMs may save money if you sell before the rate adjusts), and whether rates are expected to rise or fall.
Example: Biweekly mortgage payments (26 half-payments per year) vs. monthly payments save money because:
26 biweekly half-payments equal 13 full monthly payments per year. That extra annual payment goes directly to principal, reducing the balance faster. On a $300,000 mortgage at 6%, this can save ~$60,000 in interest and cut 4-5 years off the term.
Example: What does 'APR' (Annual Percentage Rate) represent on a loan?
APR reflects the true annual cost of borrowing by including the interest rate plus origination fees, discount points, and other loan charges. It is always equal to or higher than the stated interest rate and allows apples-to-apples comparison.
Example: What is 'TRID' in mortgage lending?
TRID combined two older disclosure requirements (Truth in Lending Act and RESPA) into two standardized forms: the Loan Estimate (provided within 3 days of application) and the Closing Disclosure (provided 3 days before closing).
Example: What does 'rate lock' mean in mortgage lending?
A rate lock protects the borrower from rate increases during the loan processing period. If rates rise, you keep the locked rate. However, if rates fall significantly, you may want to renegotiate or use a float-down option.
Example: Two mortgage offers: Loan A is $200,000 at 6% for 30 years (monthly payment $1,199, total $431,676). Loan B is $200,000 at 5.75% with $6,000 in fees (monthly payment $1,167, total $426,233). Which is the better deal if you keep the loan the full 30 years?
Loan A total: $431,676. Loan B total: $426,233 + $6,000 fees = $432,233. Loan A is cheaper by $557 over 30 years. However, Loan B has a lower monthly payment ($32 less), which might matter for monthly cash flow.

Break-even: $8,000 / $330 = ~24 months. Total savings over 10 years: $330 x 120 = $39,600 minus $8,000 costs = $31,600 net savings. Since they plan to stay 10 years (well beyond the 24-month break-even), this refinance is clearly worthwhile.
Example: A borrower is comparing a 5/1 ARM starting at 4% vs. a 30-year fixed at 5.5% on a $300,000 mortgage. The ARM saves $282/month for the first 5 years. If the ARM adjusts to 7% after 5 years, the monthly payment increases by approximately $595. If the borrower plans to sell after 7 years, which is cheaper?
ARM savings years 1-5: $282 x 60 = $16,920. Extra ARM cost years 6-7: $595 x 24 = $14,280. Net savings with ARM: $16,920 - $14,280 = $2,640. For a 7-year hold, the ARM still saves money, but the margin is narrow and depends on actual rate adjustments.
Example: A buyer pays 2 points ($6,000) on a $300,000 mortgage to reduce the rate from 6% to 5.5%. This lowers the monthly payment by $99. How many months to break even, and how much does the buyer save over 30 years?
Break-even: $6,000 / $99 = ~61 months (about 5 years). Total savings over 30 years: $99 x 360 = $35,640 minus $6,000 cost = $29,640 net savings. If you plan to stay longer than 5 years, buying points is a strong move.

Over 20 years, investing the $20,000 would grow to approximately $77,394 while paying off the mortgage saves approximately $30,000 in avoided interest. The mathematical advantage of investing is:

Example: A comprehensive loan decision should consider multiple factors. Rank these factors from most to least important for most borrowers:
Total cost is the primary measure of a loan's expense. Monthly affordability ensures you can sustain payments. Flexibility (no prepayment penalties, hardship options) protects against life changes. Opportunity cost considers what else you could do with the money.

    [*] Total cost (APR + fees + term) > monthly affordability > flexibility (prepayment options, hardship provisions) > opportunity cost of the funds
    [ ] Interest rate > brand of lender > down payment size > loan term
    Explanation: Total cost is the primary measure of a loan's expense. Monthly affordability ensures you can sustain payments. Flexibility (no prepayment penalties, hardship options) protects against life changes. Opportunity cost considers what else you could do with the money.
