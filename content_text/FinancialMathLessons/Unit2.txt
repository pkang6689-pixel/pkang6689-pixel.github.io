--- Lesson 2.1: Simple Interest Formula (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Simple Interest Formula
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
A Treasury bill pays $3,000 in simple interest on a $50,000 investment over 1 year. What is the interest rate?
r = I/(Pt) = $3,000/($50,000 × 1) = 0.06 = 6%. Rearranging the simple interest formula lets you solve for any unknown variable.
What is 'principal' in the simple interest formula?
Principal (P) is the initial sum of money placed in an investment or the original amount borrowed on a loan, before any interest is calculated.
What is an 'ordinary interest' calculation?
Ordinary interest (banker's rule) uses a 360-day year for simpler calculations. Exact interest uses 365 days. The choice affects how t is computed for periods measured in days.
What is a 'promissory note'?
A promissory note is a legal document where the borrower promises to repay the lender a specified sum plus interest by a specific date. Simple interest is commonly used for these instruments.
A car dealer offers a $15,000 loan at 7% simple interest for 4 years. What is the total amount the buyer will repay?
I = $15,000 × 0.07 × 4 = $4,200. Total repayment = $15,000 + $4,200 = $19,200. Over 4 years, the borrower pays 28% of the principal in interest alone.
A bank offers a 90-day promissory note for $20,000 at 6% simple interest using ordinary interest (360-day year). What is the interest charge?
Using ordinary interest: t = 90/360 = 0.25 years. I = $20,000 × 0.06 × 0.25 = $300. The 360-day year convention slightly increases the interest compared to exact interest.
A small business owner needs to borrow $40,000 for equipment. Bank A offers 7% simple interest for 5 years. Bank B offers 6% simple interest for 6 years. What is the difference in total interest paid?
Bank A: I = $40,000 × 0.07 × 5 = $14,000. Bank B: I = $40,000 × 0.06 × 6 = $14,400. Bank B costs $400 more despite the lower rate because the longer term adds more interest overall.

--- Lesson 2.2: Compound Interest Formula (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Compound Interest Formula
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
$5,000 at 8% compounded quarterly for 5 years. What is the approximate final amount?
A = $5,000(1 + 0.08/4)^(4×5) = $5,000(1.02)^20 ≈ $7,430. Quarterly compounding with n=4 adds interest every 3 months.
What is the 'Rule of 72'?
The Rule of 72 is a mental math shortcut. Dividing 72 by the annual rate gives an approximate doubling time (e.g., 72/8% = 9 years to double). It works best for rates between 2-15%.
A couple invests $8,000 at 7% compounded monthly for their child's education. They plan to withdraw it in 15 years. What is the approximate total interest earned?
A = $8,000(1 + 0.07/12)^(180) ≈ $22,700. Interest earned = $22,700 - $8,000 = $14,700. Compound interest nearly tripled their original investment.
Real-World Applications
Example: What does 'APY' (Annual Percentage Yield) mean?
APY is the actual annual return earned after accounting for compounding. A 6% rate compounded monthly has an APY of about 6.17%, which is the true amount earned per year.

Overview

Key Definitions
What is APY (Annual Percentage Yield)?
APY stands for Annual Percentage Yield. It reflects the total amount of interest earned on a deposit over one year, including the effect of compounding. APY allows consumers to compare accounts with different compounding frequencies on an equal basis.
What is a CD ladder?
A CD ladder involves purchasing multiple CDs with different maturity dates (e.g., 1-year, 2-year, 3-year, 4-year, 5-year). As each CD matures, you can reinvest it or use the funds, providing both higher rates and regular access to your money.
What is a Treasury I-bond?
Treasury I-bonds are U.S. government savings bonds with a composite rate consisting of a fixed rate plus a semiannually adjusted inflation rate. They protect against inflation and are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government.
What is a money market account?
A money market account is a type of deposit account that functions as a hybrid between savings and checking accounts. It generally offers higher interest rates and some check-writing ability, but may require higher minimum balances to avoid fees.
What is the 'nominal rate' on a savings account?
The nominal rate (also called the stated rate or annual percentage rate for deposits) is the interest rate quoted by the bank before factoring in how often interest compounds. The APY is always equal to or greater than the nominal rate when compounding occurs more than once per year.

--- Lesson 2.6: Comparing Interest Scenarios (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Comparing Interest Scenarios
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'effective annual rate' (EAR)?
The effective annual rate (EAR), also known as APY for deposits, reflects the true annual cost or return by incorporating compounding. For example, a 12% nominal rate compounded monthly has an EAR of (1 + 0.01)^12 - 1 = 12.68%.
What is a 'future value annuity'?
A future value annuity calculates the total accumulated value when you make regular, equal payments over time. The formula is FV = PMT x [(1 + r)^n - 1] / r, where PMT is the periodic payment, r is the rate per period, and n is the number of periods.
What is 'exponential growth' in the context of compound interest?
Compound interest produces exponential growth because each period's interest is calculated on an increasingly larger balance. This creates the characteristic 'hockey stick' curve where growth appears slow initially but accelerates dramatically over time.
What is a 'sensitivity analysis' in financial comparisons?
A sensitivity analysis examines how changing one input variable (like the interest rate from 5% to 10%) affects the final result while keeping other variables constant. This helps identify which factors have the greatest impact on financial outcomes.
$10,000 is invested at 6% compounded annually for 30 years and compared to $10,000 at 8% compounded annually for 30 years. What is the approximate difference in final values?
At 6%: A = 10,000(1.06)^30 = $57,435. At 8%: A = 10,000(1.08)^30 = $100,627. Difference: $100,627 - $57,435 = $43,192. A mere 2% difference in rate nearly doubles the final value over 30 years.
Real-World Applications
Example: Tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs and 401(k)s amplify compound growth because:
In taxable accounts, you pay taxes on interest and gains each year, reducing the amount that compounds. Tax-advantaged accounts allow the full return to compound, creating a significant advantage over time.
Example: Tina saves $500/month starting at age 25. Her twin brother Tom starts saving $500/month at age 35. Both earn 7% annually and retire at 65. How much more does Tina have at retirement?
Tina (40 years): approximately $1,198,000 with $240,000 contributed. Tom (30 years): approximately $567,000 with $180,000 contributed. Difference: about $631,000 - but in terms of extra money beyond equal contributions, Tina's 10-year head start generated roughly $380,000 more in growth.
Important Concepts
The difference between compounding frequency effects is most significant when:
Both high rates and long time periods amplify the effect of compounding frequency. At low rates or short time periods, the difference between monthly and annual compounding is minimal.

--- Lesson 2.7: Case Studies in Banking (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Banking
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a '1099-INT' form?
Form 1099-INT is an IRS tax form that financial institutions send to account holders and the IRS to report interest income of $10 or more earned during the tax year. This interest must be reported as ordinary income on your tax return.
What is a 'minimum balance requirement' for a bank account?
Many bank accounts require you to maintain a minimum balance to avoid fees or earn the highest interest rate. Falling below this threshold may trigger monthly maintenance fees that can negate any interest earned.
What is an 'opportunity cost' in the context of banking case studies?
In banking case studies, opportunity cost refers to the interest or returns lost by keeping money in a lower-yield account when better options exist. For example, keeping $50,000 at 0% in checking instead of 4.5% in savings costs over $2,000 per year.
What is a 'no-penalty CD'?
A no-penalty CD allows you to withdraw your full balance and earned interest before the maturity date without an early withdrawal penalty. The trade-off is that no-penalty CDs often offer slightly lower rates than traditional CDs of the same term.
David incurs 3 overdraft fees at $35 each in one month while having a $20 purchase, a $15 purchase, and a $5 purchase declined then processed. What is the total cost of overdraft fees, and what percentage of his actual purchases do the fees represent?
Three overdraft fees: 3 x $35 = $105. Total purchases: $20 + $15 + $5 = $40. Fee-to-purchase ratio: $105 / $40 = 262.5%. David paid 2.6 times the value of his purchases in fees alone, highlighting the devastating cost of overdrafts on small transactions.
A CD ladder is set up with $20,000 total: $4,000 each in a 1-year CD at 4.0%, 2-year CD at 4.3%, 3-year CD at 4.5%, 4-year CD at 4.7%, and 5-year CD at 5.0% (all compounded annually). What is the approximate total interest earned across all five CDs by their respective maturity dates?
1-yr: $4,000(1.04)^1 = $4,160, interest $160. 2-yr: $4,000(1.043)^2 = $4,351, interest $351. 3-yr: $4,000(1.045)^3 = $4,564, interest $564. 4-yr: $4,000(1.047)^4 = $4,808, interest $808. 5-yr: $4,000(1.05)^5 = $5,105, interest $1,105. Total interest: approximately $2,988, or about $3,200 when accounting for reinvestment of maturing CDs.
Patricia earns $1,800 in savings account interest this year. She is in the 24% federal tax bracket and 5% state tax bracket. What is her approximate after-tax interest income?
Federal tax: $1,800 x 0.24 = $432. State tax: $1,800 x 0.05 = $90. Total tax: $432 + $90 = $522. After-tax interest: $1,800 - $522 = $1,278. Even after taxes, the interest is significantly better than earning nothing.
A bank offers a promotional rate of 5.5% APY for the first 6 months, then drops to 1.0% APY for the remaining 6 months. On a $10,000 deposit held for the full year, what is the approximate total interest earned, and what is the effective blended APY?
First 6 months: $10,000 x 0.055 x 0.5 = $275. Second 6 months: $10,275 x 0.01 x 0.5 = approximately $51. Total interest: about $326, for a blended APY of approximately 3.25%. The promotional rate makes the offer sound great, but the full-year return is much lower.
Maria and her husband have $300,000 in savings. They want all of it FDIC-insured. The limit is $250,000 per depositor per institution. What is the simplest strategy to ensure full coverage?
FDIC insures joint accounts up to $250,000 per co-owner. A joint account held by Maria and her husband is insured up to $500,000 total ($250,000 for each owner). Their $300,000 is fully covered in a single joint account at one FDIC-insured bank.
A financial advisor presents two scenarios to a client with $40,000: Scenario A keeps all money in a checking account at 0% for 10 years. Scenario B moves $35,000 to a high-yield savings at 4.5% compounded daily, keeping $5,000 in checking. What is the approximate difference in total value after 10 years?
Scenario A: $40,000 stays at $40,000. Scenario B: Checking $5,000 + Savings $35,000(1 + 0.045/365)^(365x10) = $5,000 + $35,000 x 1.5683 = $5,000 + $54,891 = $59,891. Difference: $59,891 - $40,000 = $19,891. Simply moving idle cash to a high-yield account generates nearly $20,000 over a decade.
Real-World Applications
Example: Overdraft fees at major banks typically average approximately:
The average overdraft fee at major U.S. banks is approximately $35 per transaction. Multiple overdrafts in a single day can result in fees exceeding $100.
Example: What does 'APR' (Annual Percentage Rate) mean in banking?
APR is commonly used for loans and credit cards. Unlike APY (used for deposits), APR typically does not include the effect of compounding. For savings, look at APY; for borrowing, compare APR. The true cost of borrowing may be higher than APR suggests when compounding is factored in.
Example: A case study compares two savers: Amy saves $300/month starting at age 25, and Ben starts saving $300/month at age 35. Both earn 7% annually and retire at age 65. Approximately how much does Amy have compared to Ben at retirement?
Amy (40 years, $144,000 contributed): approximately $720,000. Ben (30 years, $108,000 contributed): approximately $340,000. Amy contributed only $36,000 more but ends up with about $380,000 more, powerfully demonstrating how starting early amplifies compound growth.
Example: David incurs 3 overdraft fees at $35 each in one month while having a $20 purchase, a $15 purchase, and a $5 purchase declined then processed. What is the total cost of overdraft fees, and what percentage of his actual purchases do the fees represent?
Three overdraft fees: 3 x $35 = $105. Total purchases: $20 + $15 + $5 = $40. Fee-to-purchase ratio: $105 / $40 = 262.5%. David paid 2.6 times the value of his purchases in fees alone, highlighting the devastating cost of overdrafts on small transactions.
Example: Robert deposits $500 per month into a high-yield savings account earning 4.5% APY. After 5 years, he has approximately $33,400. What percentage of this total came from compound interest rather than his contributions?
Total contributions: $500 x 60 months = $30,000. Interest earned: $33,400 - $30,000 = $3,400. Percentage from interest: $3,400 / $33,400 = 10.2%. Over longer time periods, this percentage would grow dramatically due to compounding.
Example: Maria and her husband have $300,000 in savings. They want all of it FDIC-insured. The limit is $250,000 per depositor per institution. What is the simplest strategy to ensure full coverage?

Important Concepts
What does 'compounding frequency' refer to in banking
Compounding frequency determines how often interest is added to the principal. Common frequencies include daily (365 times/year), monthly (12 times/year), quarterly (4 times/year), and annually (once/year). More frequent compounding results in slightly higher returns.

--- Lesson 2.8: Technology Tools for Interest Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Technology Tools for Interest Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'Goal Seek' in Excel?
Goal Seek is a reverse-engineering tool in Excel. For example, if you want to know what interest rate is needed to grow $10,000 to $50,000 in 20 years, Goal Seek will calculate the answer by adjusting the rate input until the output matches your target.
What is a 'sensitivity analysis' in financial modeling?
Sensitivity analysis tests how changing one variable (e.g., interest rate from 4% to 8% in 1% increments) affects the final result while holding other variables constant. This helps identify which factors have the greatest impact on outcomes.
What is an 'amortization schedule'?
An amortization schedule is a detailed table that shows, for each payment period, how much goes toward principal and how much goes toward interest. Early payments are mostly interest, while later payments are mostly principal.
What is a 'Monte Carlo simulation' in financial modeling?
Monte Carlo simulations use random number generation to model uncertain variables (like market returns) thousands of times, producing a distribution of possible outcomes. This helps assess the probability of achieving financial goals under varying conditions.
What is a 'Scenario Manager' in Excel?
Scenario Manager allows you to define named scenarios (e.g., 'Best Case,' 'Worst Case,' 'Most Likely') with different input values and quickly switch between them to see how each affects the results.
An investor uses an online compound interest calculator to compare two scenarios: $20,000 lump sum at 6% for 25 years versus $20,000 lump sum at 6% for 25 years with an additional $100/month contribution. What is the approximate difference in final values?
Lump sum only: $20,000(1.005)^300 = approximately $85,830. With $100/month added: approximately $85,830 + $69,600 (future value of annuity) = $155,430. The $100/month contributions total only $30,000 in deposits but grow to about $69,600 with compound interest.
Real-World Applications

Cloud-based tools like Google Sheets allow users to access, edit, and share financial models from any device with internet access. They also support real-time collaboration, making them ideal for joint financial planning.
