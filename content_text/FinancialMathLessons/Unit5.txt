--- Lesson 5.1: Creating a Personal Budget (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Creating a Personal Budget
Creating a Personal BudgetThe 50/30/20 Rule  • 50% — Needs: Housing, food, utilities, transportation, insurance.  • 30% — Wants: Entertainment, dining out, hobbies, subscriptions.  • 20% — Savings & debt: Retirement, emergency fund, extra debt payments.Budget Equation
Income − Expenses = Surplus (or Deficit)

  Q1. The 50/30/20 rule allocates 50% of income to:
    [ ] Savings
    [ ] Wants
    [*] Needs (essential expenses: housing, food, utilities, transportation)

    [ ] Entertainment

    [ ] $1,200

    [ ] Income × Expenses

--- Lesson 5.2: Tracking Income & Expenses (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Tracking Income & Expenses
Tracking Income & ExpensesIncome Sources  • Gross income: Total earnings before deductions.  • Net income: Take-home pay after taxes and deductions.Expense Categories  • Essential: Housing (25-35%), food (10-15%), transportation (10-15%), insurance/health.  • Discretionary: Entertainment, subscriptions, dining, shopping.  • Financial: Savings, investments, debt payments.

--- Lesson 5.3: Emergency Funds & Savings Goals (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Emergency Funds & Savings Goals
Emergency Funds & Savings GoalsEmergency Fund  • Target: 3–6 months of essential expenses.  • Where: High-yield savings (liquid, FDIC-insured).  • Purpose: Job loss, medical emergency, major car repair, home emergency.Savings Goals
Use the formula: Monthly savings = Goal amount ÷ Months until deadline (or use FV/PV for interest).

--- Lesson 5.4: Short- & Long-Term Financial Planning (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Short- & Long-Term Financial Planning
Short- & Long-Term Financial PlanningTime Horizons  • Short-term (0–3 years): Emergency fund, vacation, minor purchases.  • Medium-term (3–10 years): Home down payment, car, graduate school.  • Long-term (10+ years): Retirement, children's college, wealth building.Planning Framework
1. Define goals. 2. Set timelines. 3. Calculate required savings. 4. Choose appropriate accounts. 5. Automate. 6. Review and adjust.

  Key Concepts: Budget Adjustments & Life Changes
Budget Adjustments & Life ChangesTriggers for Budget Revision  • Income changes: Raise, job loss, side income, retirement.  • Life events: Marriage, baby, home purchase, health crisis.  • Goal changes: New priorities, completed goals, shifting timelines.Adjustment Strategy
1. Reassess income. 2. Re-prioritize goals. 3. Adjust allocations. 4. Implement and monitor.

--- Lesson 5.8: Budget Adjustments & Life Changes (Quiz) ---

  Q1. You receive a 10% raise on a $50,000 salary, giving you $5,000 more per year. Which allocation strategy builds the most long-term wealth?
    [ ] Use it all for lifestyle upgrades since you earned it
    [*] Split it: increase your savings rate with about half and allow modest lifestyle improvements with the rest
    [ ] Put 100% into savings and make no lifestyle changes at all
    [ ] Add it all to your checking account and decide later
    Explanation: A balanced approach avoids 'lifestyle creep' (spending every raise) while staying sustainable. Saving 100% sounds ideal but often isn't maintained long-term. Having no plan (leaving it in checking) typically results in unintentional spending. The 50/50 split builds wealth while rewarding your progress.

  Q2. After an unexpected job loss, which set of actions should be taken first?
    [ ] Immediately take on credit card debt to maintain your current lifestyle
    [*] Cut discretionary spending, file for unemployment benefits, assess your emergency fund runway, and begin seeking new income
    [ ] Wait several months before making any changes to see if the situation resolves
    [ ] Withdraw all retirement savings to cover expenses right away
    Explanation: The priority during job loss is to extend your financial runway: reduce non-essential spending, claim available benefits, calculate how long your emergency fund lasts, and actively seek new income. Borrowing on credit cards creates high-interest debt. Withdrawing retirement funds incurs penalties and taxes. Waiting too long depletes savings unnecessarily.

  Q3. A baby adds approximately _____ per year in expenses.

    Explanation: Child cost reality.

    [ ] Budgets remain separate and independent after marriage
    Explanation: Marriage creates a financial partnership that affects nearly every budget category: combined incomes change cash flow, shared housing/insurance can reduce costs, but spending habits may conflict. Tax status changes (married filing jointly can lower rates), and goals must be aligned (savings targets, retirement plans, major purchases).
