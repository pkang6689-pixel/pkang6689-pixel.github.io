--- Lesson 1.1: Importance of Financial Literacy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Importance of Financial Literacy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is compound interest?
Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods, creating exponential growth over time.
What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 is a quick estimation tool where dividing 72 by the annual interest rate gives the approximate number of years needed for an investment to double.
What is an 'asset' in personal finance?
An asset is anything of value that you own, such as cash, investments, real estate, or vehicles, which can be converted to money or generate income.
What is 'financial literacy'?
Financial literacy is the possession of knowledge and skills that allow an individual to make informed and effective decisions about money management, including budgeting, saving, investing, and borrowing.
James has assets worth $150,000 (home equity, car, savings) and liabilities of $95,000 (mortgage balance, student loans, credit card debt). What is his net worth?
Net worth = Total Assets - Total Liabilities = $150,000 - $95,000 = $55,000. This figure represents James's actual financial position after accounting for all debts.
A family has a monthly income of $6,000. They spend $4,800 on expenses and save $1,200 in a high-yield savings account at 4.5% APY. What is their approximate annual interest earned on savings after 1 year?
Total saved in 1 year = $1,200 × 12 = $14,400. Average balance over the year is roughly $14,400/2 = $7,200. Interest ≈ 4.5% × $14,400 (simplified) ≈ $648. Even modest savings earn meaningful returns in a high-yield account.
Real-World Applications
Example: Approximately what percentage of Americans cannot cover a $1,000 emergency expense from savings?
Surveys consistently show that roughly 56% of Americans lack sufficient savings to handle a $1,000 emergency, highlighting widespread financial vulnerability.
Example: Financial illiteracy costs the average American approximately how much per year?
Research estimates that lack of financial knowledge costs the average American over $1,500 annually through unnecessary fees, poor interest rates, and suboptimal financial decisions.
Example: James has assets worth $150,000 (home equity, car, savings) and liabilities of $95,000 (mortgage balance, student loans, credit card debt). What is his net worth?
Net worth = Total Assets - Total Liabilities = $150,000 - $95,000 = $55,000. This figure represents James's actual financial position after accounting for all debts.
Example: Priya invests $5,000 at age 22 in a retirement account earning 9% annually. Without adding another dollar, approximately how much will this single investment be worth at age 62?
FV = $5,000 × (1.09)^40 ≈ $157,250. Even a single investment of $5,000, left to compound at 9% for 40 years, grows to over 31 times the original amount.

--- Lesson 1.2: Sources of Income (wages, salaries, investments) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sources of Income (wages, salaries, investments)
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'salary'?
A salary is a fixed annual compensation amount divided into regular pay periods (weekly, biweekly, or monthly), paid regardless of the exact number of hours worked.
What is 'commission-based income'?
Commission-based income is compensation calculated as a percentage of the sales an individual generates, commonly found in real estate, car sales, and financial services.
What is a '1099 form' used for?
A 1099 form reports various types of non-employment income to the IRS, including independent contractor earnings, bank interest, investment dividends, and other miscellaneous income.
What is 'investment income'?
Investment income is money earned from assets you own, including stock dividends, bond interest, capital gains from selling investments, and net rental income from property.
Sarah works 40 hours per week at $18/hour. She also works 8 hours of overtime this week. What is her gross pay for the week?
Regular pay = 40 × $18 = $720. Overtime pay = 8 × ($18 × 1.5) = 8 × $27 = $216. Total gross pay = $720 + $216 = $936.
Marcus earns a $65,000 annual salary. His employer matches 100% of his 401(k) contributions up to 5% of salary. If Marcus contributes 5%, what is his total annual compensation including the match?
Marcus's 401(k) contribution = 5% × $65,000 = $3,250. Employer match = $3,250. Total compensation = $65,000 salary + $3,250 match = $68,250. Not taking the full match is leaving free money on the table.
A real estate agent earns a 3% commission on each home sale. She sells a house for $350,000. What is her commission?
Commission = 3% × $350,000 = 0.03 × $350,000 = $10,500. Commission-based income can be highly variable since it depends entirely on sales volume.
Tom earns $55,000/year. His FICA deduction (7.65%), federal tax (12%), and state tax (4%) are his only deductions. What is his approximate annual net income?
Total deduction rate = 7.65% + 12% + 4% = 23.65%. Deductions = 23.65% × $55,000 = $12,992.50. Net income = $55,000 - $12,993 ≈ $42,008. Over 23% of gross pay goes to taxes.
Lisa owns rental property that generates $1,800/month in rent. After $600/month in expenses (mortgage, maintenance, taxes), what is her annual net rental income?
Monthly net rental income = $1,800 - $600 = $1,200. Annual net rental income = $1,200 × 12 = $14,400. This passive income continues as long as she owns the property.
Alex has the following income sources: $45,000 salary, $2,000 in stock dividends, $500 in savings interest, and $3,600 from a side gig. What is his total gross income?
Total gross income = $45,000 + $2,000 + $500 + $3,600 = $51,100. All income sources, including investment returns and side gig earnings, must be reported and are generally taxable.
Jennifer works at a restaurant earning $2.13/hr base plus tips. In a 30-hour week, she earns $450 in tips. What is her total weekly income?
Base pay = $2.13 × 30 = $63.90. Tips = $450. Total = $63.90 + $450 = $513.90. Tipped employees often receive a lower base wage with the expectation that tips bring total compensation above minimum wage.
Mike bought 100 shares of stock at $25 per share and sold them a year later at $32 per share. What is his capital gain?
Purchase price = 100 × $25 = $2,500. Sale price = 100 × $32 = $3,200. Capital gain = $3,200 - $2,500 = $700. This gain is taxable as investment income.
A couple earns combined salaries of $95,000/year. They also receive $4,000 in dividends, $1,200 in savings interest, and $6,000 from a rental property. If their combined effective tax rate is 22%, what is their approximate annual net income?
Total gross income = $95,000 + $4,000 + $1,200 + $6,000 = $106,200. Taxes = 22% × $106,200 = $23,364. Net income = $106,200 - $23,364 = $82,836. Diversified income sources improve financial resilience.
Real-World Applications
Example: Wages are typically paid on a(n) _____ basis.
Wages are compensation calculated on a per-hour basis, meaning workers earn a set dollar amount for each hour worked.
Example: FICA taxes fund Social Security and Medicare, totaling what percentage of gross wages for an employee?
Employees pay 7.65% in FICA taxes: 6.2% for Social Security and 1.45% for Medicare. Employers match this amount, making the combined rate 15.3%.
Example: The federal minimum wage as of 2024 is:
The federal minimum wage is $7.25 per hour, though many states and cities have set higher minimum wages that supersede the federal floor.
Example: A real estate agent earns a 3% commission on each home sale. She sells a house for $350,000. What is her commission?
Commission = 3% × $350,000 = 0.03 × $350,000 = $10,500. Commission-based income can be highly variable since it depends entirely on sales volume.
Example: Lisa owns rental property that generates $1,800/month in rent. After $600/month in expenses (mortgage, maintenance, taxes), what is her annual net rental income?
Monthly net rental income = $1,800 - $600 = $1,200. Annual net rental income = $1,200 × 12 = $14,400. This passive income continues as long as she owns the property.
Example: Jennifer works at a restaurant earning $2.13/hr base plus tips. In a 30-hour week, she earns $450 in tips. What is her total weekly income?
Base pay = $2.13 × 30 = $63.90. Tips = $450. Total = $63.90 + $450 = $513.90. Tipped employees often receive a lower base wage with the expectation that tips bring total compensation above minimum wage.

--- Lesson 1.3: Budgeting Basics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Budgeting Basics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'zero-based budgeting'?
Zero-based budgeting means giving every dollar a job so that your income minus all planned expenditures (including savings) equals zero, ensuring no money goes unaccounted for.
What is an 'envelope budgeting system'?
The envelope system is a cash-based budgeting method where you physically divide cash into labeled envelopes for each spending category, creating a tangible spending limit.
What is a 'sinking fund' in budgeting?
A sinking fund is money set aside regularly over time for a specific known future expense, allowing you to pay for irregular costs without disrupting your monthly budget.
What is 'cash flow' in personal finance?
Cash flow tracks the inflow (income from all sources) and outflow (all expenses and spending) of money over a period, with positive cash flow meaning more comes in than goes out.
What is a 'fixed expense'?
A fixed expense is a cost that remains constant from period to period, making it predictable and easy to plan for in a budget. Common examples include rent, mortgage payments, and insurance.
A family has the following monthly expenses: rent $1,500, car payment $350, groceries $600, utilities $200, entertainment $300, and insurance $250. Which are their fixed expenses and what is the fixed expense total?
Fixed expenses remain the same each month: rent ($1,500) + car payment ($350) + insurance ($250) = $2,100. Groceries, utilities, and entertainment are variable expenses that fluctuate.
A couple earns $8,000/month combined. They track expenses and find: housing $2,400, food $800, transportation $600, utilities $300, insurance $400, debt payments $500, entertainment $400, and miscellaneous $300. What is their monthly surplus?
Total expenses = $2,400 + $800 + $600 + $300 + $400 + $500 + $400 + $300 = $5,700. Surplus = $8,000 - $5,700 = $2,300 available for savings and investments.
Real-World Applications
Example: The 50/30/20 rule allocates what percentages to needs, wants, and savings respectively?
The 50/30/20 rule is a widely recommended budgeting guideline that allocates 50% of after-tax income to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt repayment.
Example: Housing costs should ideally not exceed approximately what percentage of gross income?
Most financial experts and mortgage lenders recommend keeping housing costs (rent or mortgage, insurance, taxes) at or below 28-30% of gross monthly income to maintain financial health.
Example: Kevin earns $3,800/month. His fixed expenses total $2,000 and variable expenses average $1,200. He wants to build a $6,000 emergency fund. At his current surplus, how many months will it take?
Monthly surplus = $3,800 - $2,000 - $1,200 = $600. Months to reach $6,000 = $6,000 / $600 = 10 months. A clear budget helps you calculate exactly how long goals will take.

--- Lesson 1.4: Needs vs. Wants (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Needs vs. Wants
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'need' in personal finance?
A need is an expense essential for basic living and survival, including food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, basic transportation, and utilities without which you cannot safely function.
What is a 'want' in personal finance?
A want is a non-essential good or service that improves comfort or enjoyment but is not necessary for basic survival, such as entertainment, dining out, vacations, and luxury upgrades.
What is the 'needs vs. wants spectrum'?
Many expenses exist on a spectrum: for example, basic shelter is a need, but a luxury apartment in a premium location represents the want end of that spectrum.
What is the '24-hour rule' for purchases?
The 24-hour rule is a personal finance strategy where you wait at least 24 hours before buying a non-essential item, allowing emotions to subside and rational evaluation to determine if you truly want or need it.
The Rodriguez family is deciding between a basic internet plan ($50/month) and a premium plan ($120/month). They only need basic internet for work and school. What is the annual opportunity cost of choosing the premium plan?
Monthly difference = $120 - $50 = $70. Annual opportunity cost = $70 × 12 = $840. The basic plan meets their needs; the premium adds wants (faster streaming, more channels) at $840/year.
Real-World Applications
Example: Sarah's monthly income is $5,000. She categorizes her expenses: rent $1,400 (need), car payment $350 (need), groceries $500 (need), dining out $300 (want), phone $80 (need), gym $60 (want), streaming $45 (want), clothing $200 (want). What percentage of her income goes to wants?
Total wants = $300 + $60 + $45 + $200 = $605. Percentage = $605 / $5,000 = 12.1%. This is well below the 30% guideline, leaving room for savings and financial goals.

--- Lesson 1.5: Opportunity Cost in Financial Decisions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Opportunity Cost in Financial Decisions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'opportunity cost'?
Opportunity cost is a fundamental economic concept representing the value of the next best option you sacrifice when choosing one course of action over another.
What is a 'sunk cost'?
A sunk cost is an expense that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered. Rational decision-making requires ignoring sunk costs and focusing only on future costs and benefits.
Maria has $20,000 and must choose: Option A is paying off her car loan (4% interest, 3 years remaining), and Option B is investing in an index fund (expected 9% return). What is the approximate annual opportunity cost of choosing Option A?
If she pays off the car, she saves 4% ($800/year in interest). If she invests, she expects 9% ($1,800/year). Opportunity cost of paying off the car = $1,800 - $800 = $1,000/year in foregone returns, though investing carries more risk.
Kevin spent $2,000 on a non-refundable vacation deposit. The trip now conflicts with a work opportunity worth $5,000. Kevin decides to go on vacation because he already paid. What is the total cost of his decision?
The $2,000 deposit is a sunk cost — gone regardless. The opportunity cost of going on vacation is the $5,000 in foregone work income. Rationally, Kevin should take the work opportunity since the deposit cannot be recovered either way.
A couple is deciding between renting for $1,500/month and buying a home (mortgage $2,200/month but building $700/month in equity). They plan to stay 10 years. Ignoring other factors, what is the approximate equity opportunity cost of renting for 10 years?
By renting, they forgo building $700/month in home equity. Over 10 years: $700 × 12 × 10 = $84,000 in equity not built. However, the full rent-vs-buy analysis includes many more factors like maintenance and investment of the payment difference.
Jessica is offered a promotion requiring relocation. Current salary: $60,000. New salary: $80,000. Moving costs: $8,000. Her spouse would lose their $45,000 job for approximately 6 months. What is the first-year net financial impact?
Salary increase: +$20,000. Moving costs: -$8,000. Spouse's lost income (6 months): -$22,500. Net first-year impact: $20,000 - $8,000 - $22,500 = -$10,500. The opportunity cost analysis shows a first-year loss, though future years improve.
A student has $10,000 and must choose: pay off a 6% student loan or invest in an index fund expected to earn 10%. Over 5 years, what is the approximate opportunity cost of paying off the loan instead?
Investing: $10,000 × (1.10)^5 ≈ $16,105. Paying loan saves: $10,000 × (1.06)^5 ≈ $13,382 in avoided interest. Opportunity cost ≈ $16,105 - $13,382 = $2,723. However, loan payoff carries zero risk while investing carries market risk.
Tom spends 2 hours commuting daily (round trip) instead of working remotely. If his time is worth $30/hour and he commutes 250 days/year, what is the annual opportunity cost of his commute in time alone?
Time opportunity cost = 2 hours × $30/hour × 250 days = $15,000. This doesn't include gas, car wear, tolls, and parking. Tom's commute has a substantial hidden cost in lost productive time.
A business owner uses $50,000 of her own savings to start a business instead of investing it. The business earns $8,000 profit in the first year. If the investment would have returned 8%, what is the economic profit of the business?
Accounting profit = $8,000. Opportunity cost = 8% × $50,000 = $4,000 (foregone investment return). Economic profit = $8,000 - $4,000 implicit cost - any other implicit costs. The business earned less than the investment alternative.
Rachel is choosing between two job offers: Job A pays $70,000 with no retirement match. Job B pays $65,000 with a 6% 401(k) match. If Rachel contributes 6% to her 401(k) in Job B, what is the true total compensation difference?
Job A total comp = $70,000. Job B total comp = $65,000 salary + $3,900 employer match (6% × $65,000) = $68,900. Difference = $70,000 - $68,900 = $1,100 in favor of Job A, but the match is essentially guaranteed return on retirement savings, making Job B more competitive than the salary alone suggests.

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Personal Finance
Overview

Key Definitions
What is the 'debt snowball' method?
The debt snowball method involves listing debts from smallest to largest balance, making minimum payments on all but the smallest, and throwing extra money at the smallest debt until it is eliminated, then moving to the next.
What is the 'debt avalanche' method?
The debt avalanche method targets the debt with the highest interest rate first, regardless of balance size. This approach saves the most money in total interest over time.
What is an 'amortization schedule'?
An amortization schedule is a detailed table showing for each payment how much goes to principal (reducing the balance) and how much goes to interest, revealing that early payments are mostly interest.
In a mortgage case study, a $250,000 home with 20% down ($50,000) results in a $200,000 mortgage. At 6.5% for 30 years, the monthly payment is approximately $1,264. What is the approximate total interest paid over the life of the loan?
Total payments = $1,264 × 360 months = $455,040. Total interest = $455,040 - $200,000 principal = $255,040. You pay more in interest than the original loan amount over 30 years.
A case study follows someone who received a $50,000 inheritance. Option A: invest it all at 9% for 20 years. Option B: spend $30,000 and invest $20,000 at 9% for 20 years. What is the approximate opportunity cost of Option B?
Option A: $50,000 × (1.09)^20 ≈ $280,000. Option B: $20,000 × (1.09)^20 ≈ $112,000. Opportunity cost = $280,000 - $112,000 = $168,000. The $30,000 spent actually cost $168,000 in foregone growth.

--- Lesson 1.8: Financial Decision-Making Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Financial Decision-Making Models
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Jake invested $10,000 in a stock that dropped to $6,000. He holds on because 'it has to come back to break even.' A financial advisor suggests selling and investing in an index fund expected to return 9%. If the stock stays flat for 3 years while the index fund grows, what is Jake's opportunity cost?
If Jake moves $6,000 to the index fund at 9% for 3 years: $6,000 × (1.09)^3 ≈ $7,770. By holding the flat stock, he misses ~$1,770 in growth. The $4,000 loss is a sunk cost; the real decision is what to do with the $6,000 going forward.
Maria uses mental accounting: she keeps $5,000 in a savings account earning 1% while carrying $5,000 in credit card debt at 22%. What is the net annual cost of this irrational behavior?
Interest earned on savings: 1% × $5,000 = $50. Interest paid on credit card: 22% × $5,000 = $1,100. Net cost = $1,100 - $50 = $1,050/year. Mental accounting treats the savings as sacred while ignoring the much larger debt cost.
Two investors each have $50,000. Investor A uses a decision matrix comparing 5 options across cost, risk, return, and liquidity. Investor B follows a friend's hot stock tip (herd behavior). After 3 years, Investor A's diversified portfolio returns 8%/year while Investor B's single stock drops 40%. What is the difference in outcomes?
Investor A: $50,000 × (1.08)^3 ≈ $63,000. Investor B: $50,000 × 0.60 = $30,000. Difference = $63,000 - $30,000 = $33,000. Structured decision-making vastly outperformed emotional herd behavior.
