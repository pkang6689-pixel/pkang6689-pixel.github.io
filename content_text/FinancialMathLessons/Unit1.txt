--- Lesson 1.1: Importance of Financial Literacy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Importance of Financial Literacy
Importance of Financial Literacy
Financial literacy is the ability to understand and effectively manage personal finances. It includes budgeting, investing, debt management, and planning for the future.
Why It Matters  • ~56% of Americans cannot cover a $1,000 emergency expense from savings.  • Average US household credit card debt: ~$6,500.  • Financial stress is the #1 source of stress for adults.  • Financially literate individuals save more, invest better, and retire earlier.

--- Lesson 1.2: Sources of Income (wages, salaries, investments) (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sources of Income (wages, salaries, investments)
Sources of IncomeTypes  • Earned income: Wages (hourly), salaries (annual), tips, commissions, bonuses.  • Investment income: Dividends, interest, capital gains, rental income.  • Passive income: Income requiring minimal ongoing effort (royalties, rental, business ownership).  • Transfer payments: Government benefits (Social Security, unemployment, disability).Gross vs. Net Income
Gross income = total earnings before deductions. Net income = take-home pay after taxes, insurance, retirement contributions.

--- Lesson 1.3: Budgeting Basics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Budgeting Basics
Budgeting Basics
A budget is a plan for how to allocate income to expenses, savings, and investments.
The 50/30/20 Rule  • 50% Needs: housing, food, utilities, insurance, minimum debt payments.  • 30% Wants: entertainment, dining out, hobbies, subscriptions.  • 20% Savings/Debt repayment: emergency fund, retirement, extra debt payments.Zero-Based Budgeting
Every dollar is assigned a purpose: Income − All allocations = $0. Forces intentional spending.

--- Lesson 1.4: Needs vs. Wants (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Needs vs. Wants
Needs vs. Wants
Needs are essential for survival and basic functioning: food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, transportation to work.

Wants are things that improve quality of life but aren't essential: dining out, streaming services, designer clothing, vacations.
Gray Areas
Many expenses fall between needs and wants. A smartphone may be a need (for work) but the latest model is a want. Basic transportation is a need; a luxury car is a want.

--- Lesson 1.5: Opportunity Cost in Financial Decisions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Opportunity Cost in Financial Decisions
Opportunity Cost
Opportunity cost is the value of the best alternative forgone when making a choice. Every financial decision has an opportunity cost.
Examples  • Spending $5,000 on vacation vs. investing it (at 8% for 30 years: ~$50,000).  • Attending college: tuition + 4 years of potential wages vs. higher lifetime earnings.  • Buying a car with cash vs. investing and financing at low interest.

--- Lesson 1.7: Case Studies in Personal Finance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Case Studies in Personal Finance
Case Studies in Personal FinanceCase 1: The Power of Starting Early
Investor A saves $200/month from age 22–32 (10 years, $24,000 total) then stops. Investor B saves $200/month from age 32–62 (30 years, $72,000 total). At 8% return, Investor A has MORE at 62 (~$470,000 vs. ~$300,000) despite investing less, because of compound interest and time.
Case 2: Debt Spiral
$5,000 credit card balance at 20% APR, paying only minimums: takes ~20 years to pay off, total paid: ~$12,000 (more than double the original debt).

--- Lesson 1.8: Financial Decision-Making Models (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Financial Decision-Making Models
Financial Decision-Making ModelsRational Decision-Making Process  • Step 1: Identify the decision (e.g., buy vs. lease a car).  • Step 2: Gather information (costs, terms, alternatives).  • Step 3: Identify alternatives and their opportunity costs.  • Step 4: Evaluate using criteria (cost, risk, time, alignment with goals).  • Step 5: Make and implement the decision.  • Step 6: Review and learn from the outcome.
