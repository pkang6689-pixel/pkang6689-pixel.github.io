--- Lesson 8.1: Stock Market Basics (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Stock Market Basics
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'market capitalization'?
Market capitalization (market cap) is the total dollar value of all outstanding shares, representing the market's assessment of a company's total equity value.
What is 'NYSE'?
The NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) is the world's largest stock exchange by total market capitalization of its listed companies, located on Wall Street in New York City.
Sarah buys 200 shares of a tech company at $45 per share. After one year, the stock is at $54 per share and she received $1.50 per share in dividends. What is her total return (dollar and percentage)?
Capital gain = ($54 - $45) x 200 = $1,800. Dividends = $1.50 x 200 = $300. Total return = $1,800 + $300 = $2,100. Percentage = $2,100 / ($45 x 200) = $2,100 / $9,000 = 23.3%.
An investor buys 50 shares at $80 and the stock drops to $64. What is the loss in dollars and percentage?
Loss per share = $80 - $64 = $16. Total loss = $16 x 50 = $800. Percentage loss = $16 / $80 = 20%.
A stock pays quarterly dividends of $0.85 per share. If you own 300 shares, what is your total annual dividend income?
Annual dividend per share = $0.85 x 4 quarters = $3.40. Total annual income = $3.40 x 300 shares = $1,020.
Company XYZ has a market cap of $50 billion and 500 million shares outstanding. An investor owns 1,000 shares. What percentage of the company does the investor own, and what is their investment worth?
Ownership = 1,000 / 500,000,000 = 0.000002 = 0.0002%. Price per share = $50B / 500M = $100. Investment value = 1,000 x $100 = $100,000.
An investor buys 150 shares at $30 each. The stock rises to $37.50 and pays $0.60 in total dividends per share over the holding period. What is the total return in dollars and as a percentage?
Capital gain = ($37.50 - $30) x 150 = $1,125. Dividends = $0.60 x 150 = $90. Total return = $1,125 + $90 = $1,215. Percentage = $1,215 / ($30 x 150) = $1,215 / $4,500 = 27%.
Real-World Applications

The S&P 500 has historically returned about 10% per year on average (nominal), or approximately 7% after accounting for inflation.

--- Lesson 8.1: Stock Market Basics (Quiz) ---

  Q1. A stock represents:
    [ ] A guaranteed return on investment
    [ ] A type of bond
    [ ] A loan to a company
    [*] Ownership (equity) in a company
    Explanation: A stock represents a share of ownership (equity) in a publicly traded company. Stockholders are partial owners of the business.

  Q2. Market capitalization of a company with 50 million shares at $80/share =
    [ ] $50 million
    [ ] $400 million
    [ ] $80 million
    [*] $4 billion
    Explanation: Market cap = Shares outstanding x Price per share = 50,000,000 x $80 = $4,000,000,000 ($4 billion).

  Q3. A large-cap stock has a market cap of:
    [ ] $2-10 billion
    [ ] Under $500 million
    [ ] Under $2 billion
    [*] Over $10 billion
    Explanation: Large-cap stocks have a market capitalization over $10 billion. Mid-cap is $2-10 billion, and small-cap is under $2 billion.

  Q4. A dividend yield of 3% on a $100 stock means annual dividends of:
    [*] $3 per share
    [ ] $100 accord
    [ ] $30,
    [ ] $0.30,
    Explanation: Dividend yield = Annual dividend / Stock price. So annual dividend = 3% x $100 = $3 per share.

  Q5. A bull market is characterized by:
    [ ] Extreme day-to-day volatility
    [ ] Flat prices with no clear direction
    [*] Rising prices (generally 20%+ sustained increase) with investor optimism
    [ ] Falling prices and investor pessimism
    Explanation: A bull market is a sustained period of rising stock prices, typically defined as a 20% or greater increase from recent lows, accompanied by investor confidence.

  Q6. A bear market occurs when prices fall:
    [ ] Exactly 50%
    [ ] 5% from recent highs
    [ ] 10% from recent highs
    [*] 20% or more from recent highs
    Explanation: A bear market is defined as a decline of 20% or more from recent highs, often accompanied by widespread pessimism and negative investor sentiment.

  Q7. An IPO (Initial Public Offering) is when:
    [ ] A company goes bankrupt
    [ ] A stock splits into multiple shares
    [*] A private company sells shares to the public for the first time on a stock exchange
    [ ] A public company goes private again
    Explanation: An IPO is the process by which a privately held company offers its shares to the public for the first time, becoming listed on a stock exchange.

  Q8. Market orders execute:
    [ ] Never, they are just price requests
    [ ] Only at a specific price you set
    [*] Immediately at the current best available market price
    [ ] Only after market hours
    Explanation: A market order executes immediately at the best available price. It guarantees execution but not price, which may differ slightly from the last quoted price.

  Q9. Limit orders execute:
    [ ] Only on weekends
    [*] Only at your specified price or better
    [ ] Immediately at any available price
    [ ] At the worst available price
    Explanation: A limit order specifies the maximum price you will pay (buy) or minimum price you will accept (sell). It may not execute if the market never reaches your price.

  Q10. The stock market has historically returned approximately _____ per year (long-term S&P 500 average).
    [*] ~10% nominal (~7% after inflation)
    [ ] 15% regardless of prevailing
    [ ] 5%, based on conventional

  Q11. What is 'market capitalization'?
    [ ] The amount of capital a company has raised from investors
    [*] The total market value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated as share price times shares outstanding
    [ ] The total profit a company has earned over its lifetime
    [ ] The total revenue a company earns annually
    Explanation: Market capitalization (market cap) is the total dollar value of all outstanding shares, representing the market's assessment of a company's total equity value.

  Q12. What does 'dividend' mean?
    [*] A portion of a company's profits distributed to shareholders, usually quarterly
    [ ] A fee charged by the stock exchange for trading
    [ ] A tax on stock transactions
    [ ] The price increase of a stock over time
    Explanation: A dividend is a cash payment made by a company to its shareholders from its profits. Dividends are typically paid quarterly and represent a return on investment beyond stock price appreciation.

  Q13. What does 'dividend yield' mean?

    [ ] The interest rate on a bond

--- Lesson 8.2: Reading Financial Tables (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Reading Financial Tables
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'ticker symbol' in financial tables?
A ticker symbol is a unique short alphabetic code (such as TSLA for Tesla or AMZN for Amazon) used to identify a specific publicly traded security on an exchange.
What is the 'PEG ratio'?
The PEG ratio equals P/E divided by the earnings growth rate. A PEG below 1 may indicate the stock is undervalued relative to its growth, while above 1 may suggest it is overvalued.
What is an 'income statement' in financial reporting?
An income statement (also called a profit and loss statement) reports a company's revenue, expenses, and resulting net income over a specific period, showing profitability.
What is a 'balance sheet'?
A balance sheet provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific date, listing what it owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the residual value for shareholders (equity).
A stock table shows: ACME Corp (ACME) -- Price: $120, EPS: $6.00, Annual Dividend: $2.40, Shares Outstanding: 50 million. What is ACME's P/E ratio?
P/E = Price / EPS = $120 / $6.00 = 20. Investors are paying $20 for every $1 of ACME's earnings.
Using the same ACME data (Price: $120, Annual Dividend: $2.40), what is ACME's dividend yield?
Dividend yield = Annual Dividend / Price = $2.40 / $120 = 0.02 = 2.0%.
TechGrow Inc. has a P/E of 30 and an earnings growth rate of 25% per year. What is TechGrow's PEG ratio, and what does it suggest?
PEG = P/E / Growth Rate = 30 / 25 = 1.20. A PEG above 1 suggests the stock may be slightly overvalued relative to its earnings growth rate.
SafeBank Corp. has total assets of $800 million, total liabilities of $500 million, and 30 million shares outstanding. Its stock trades at $8. What is the P/B ratio?
Book value = Assets - Liabilities = $800M - $500M = $300M. Book value per share = $300M / 30M shares = $10. P/B = $8 / $10 = 0.80, meaning the stock trades below book value.
A company's income statement shows: Revenue $2 million, COGS $1.1 million, Operating Expenses $400,000, Interest $50,000, Taxes $90,000. What is the net income?
Net income = Revenue - COGS - Operating Expenses - Interest - Taxes = $2,000,000 - $1,100,000 - $400,000 - $50,000 - $90,000 = $360,000.
MegaCorp trades at $75, has 200 million shares outstanding, and pays a quarterly dividend of $0.50 per share. What is the annual dividend yield and market cap?
Annual dividend = $0.50 x 4 quarters = $2.00. Dividend yield = $2.00 / $75 = 0.0267 = 2.67%. Market cap = $75 x 200M = $15 billion.
A financial table shows Stock X opened at $92, hit a high of $97, a low of $90, and closed at $95 with volume of 8 million shares. The previous close was $93. What is the day's percentage change from the previous close?
Day's change is measured from previous close to today's close: ($95 - $93) / $93 x 100 = +2.15%.
Real-World Applications

A higher P/E relative to the industry average means investors are paying a premium, typically because they expect above-average earnings growth from that company.
Example: What does 'market capitalization' mean on a stock quote page?
Market capitalization (market cap) equals the current share price multiplied by total shares outstanding. It represents the market's total valuation of the company's equity.

--- Lesson 8.3: Market Indices & Benchmarks (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Market Indices & Benchmarks
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'market index'?
A market index is a statistical measure that tracks the performance of a selected group of stocks or other assets, serving as a benchmark for market or sector performance.
What is an 'expense ratio' on a fund?
The expense ratio is the annual fee expressed as a percentage of assets under management. A 0.10% expense ratio means $1 per year for every $1,000 invested.
What is a 'Total Stock Market index'?
A Total Stock Market index (like the CRSP US Total Market Index tracked by VTI) includes stocks of all sizes -- large, mid, small, and micro-cap -- providing exposure to the entire domestic stock market.
What is the 'MSCI EAFE' index?
MSCI EAFE stands for Morgan Stanley Capital International Europe, Australasia, and Far East. It tracks developed international stock markets excluding the US and Canada.
What is a 'bond index'?
A bond index (like the Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index) tracks the performance of a diversified basket of bonds and serves as a fixed-income benchmark for bond fund performance.
The Dow Jones goes from 30,000 to 33,000. What is the percentage gain?
Percentage gain = (33,000 - 30,000) / 30,000 x 100 = 3,000 / 30,000 x 100 = 10%.
A portfolio is allocated 60% stocks and 40% bonds. If stocks return 12% and bonds return 4%, what is the blended portfolio return?
Blended return = (0.60 x 12%) + (0.40 x 4%) = 7.2% + 1.6% = 8.8%.
An investor puts $500 per month into an S&P 500 index fund. In Month 1 the price is $50/share, Month 2 is $40/share, and Month 3 is $60/share. What is the average cost per share using DCA?
Shares bought: Month 1 = 10, Month 2 = 12.5, Month 3 = 8.33. Total shares = 30.83. Total invested = $1,500. Average cost = $1,500 / 30.83 = $48.65, which is less than the simple average price of $50.

The DJIA tracks 30 large blue-chip companies and is price-weighted, meaning stocks with higher share prices have more influence on the index.

The SPIVA scorecard shows roughly 80-90% of actively managed funds fail to beat their benchmark index over a 20-year period, which is a key reason why index investing has become so popular.

The S&P 500 has returned roughly 10% annually on average in nominal terms since 1926, or about 7% after adjusting for inflation.

Active funds typically charge 0.5-1.5% in expense ratios to cover fund managers, research analysts, and higher trading costs. Over decades, this fee difference compounds significantly.

Underperformance = Benchmark return - Fund return = 10% - 8% = 2 percentage points. The fund trailed its benchmark by 2 percentage points for the year.

Original index value = ($100 + $200 + $300) / 3 = $200. New value = ($100 + $200 + $270) / 3 = $190. Decline = ($200 - $190) / $200 x 100 = 5%. The $300 stock's 10% drop caused a 5% index decline because it represented half the index weight.

--- Lesson 8.4: Trading Strategies (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Trading Strategies
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'dollar cost averaging' (DCA)?
DCA is the practice of investing a fixed dollar amount on a regular schedule (e.g., $500/month). This systematic approach reduces timing risk and usually results in a lower average cost per share.
What is 'momentum investing'?
Momentum investing is a trend-following strategy that buys stocks with positive price trends and sells those with negative trends, based on the idea that recent performance tends to continue in the short term.
What is 'contrarian investing'?
Contrarian investors go against the herd. They buy undervalued stocks during market pessimism and sell overvalued holdings during market euphoria, following Buffett's advice to 'be greedy when others are fearful.'
What is the 'buy and hold' strategy?
Buy and hold means purchasing investments and keeping them for years or decades. This strategy benefits from compound growth and avoids the costs and risks of frequent trading.
What is a '40/60 stock/bond allocation'?
A 40/60 allocation puts only 40% in stocks and 60% in bonds. This conservative mix is designed for investors nearing retirement who need capital preservation and steady income over aggressive growth.
An investor uses DCA and invests $500/month when prices are $50, $40, and $60. What is the average cost per share, and is it lower than the simple average price of $50?
Shares: 10 + 12.5 + 8.33 = 30.83 total. Cost: $1,500 total. Average cost = $1,500 / 30.83 = ~$48.65. This is lower than the simple average ($50) because DCA buys more shares at lower prices.

Return = ($100 - $80) / $80 = 25%. While the momentum trade worked here, the key risk is that trends can reverse suddenly. Momentum stocks can crash quickly when sentiment shifts, making this a higher-risk strategy.

--- Lesson 8.5: Global Finance & Currency (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Global Finance & Currency
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an 'exchange rate'?
An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. For example, 1 USD = 0.92 EUR means one US dollar can be exchanged for 0.92 euros.
What is 'currency hedging'?
Currency hedging uses financial instruments (forwards, options, futures) to lock in exchange rates and protect against adverse currency movements that could reduce profits from international operations.
What is a 'forward contract' in currency trading?
A forward contract locks in an exchange rate for a future transaction. This allows businesses to know exactly how much they will receive or pay in their home currency, regardless of market fluctuations.
What is the 'Big Mac Index'?
The Big Mac Index, created by The Economist, uses the price of a Big Mac across countries as a lighthearted way to test purchasing power parity and estimate whether currencies are over- or undervalued.
What is 'international diversification' in investing?
International diversification means investing in markets across different countries. Since global markets don't move in perfect lockstep, losses in one country may be offset by gains in another.
What is a 'trade surplus'?
A trade surplus occurs when a country's exports exceed its imports. Countries like Germany and China have historically run large trade surpluses.
An investor holds $50,000 in a European stock fund. The stocks gain 10% in euro terms, but the euro depreciates 5% against the dollar during the same period. What is the approximate return in US dollar terms?
Approximate USD return = (1 + 10%) x (1 - 5%) - 1 = 1.10 x 0.95 - 1 = 1.045 - 1 = 4.5%. The euro's 5% depreciation reduces the investor's dollar-denominated return from 10% to about 4.5%.
An investor converts $100,000 to euros at 1 USD = 0.90 EUR, invests in European bonds earning 5% for one year, then converts back at 1 USD = 0.85 EUR. What is the total return in US dollars?
Convert to EUR: $100,000 x 0.90 = €90,000. After 5% return: €90,000 x 1.05 = €94,500. Convert back: €94,500 / 0.85 = $111,176. Total return = ($111,176 - $100,000) / $100,000 = 11.18%. The euro appreciated against the dollar, boosting returns.

--- Lesson 8.6: Market Case Studies (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Market Case Studies
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'recency bias' in investing?
Recency bias causes investors to overweight recent experiences. After a crash, they expect more crashes and avoid investing. After a bull market, they expect endless gains and take on too much risk.
What is 'herd mentality' in financial markets?
Herd mentality drives investors to follow the crowd rather than think independently. It amplifies bubbles (everyone rushes to buy) and crashes (everyone rushes to sell), often at the worst possible times.
What is a 'market bubble'?
A market bubble occurs when prices far exceed fundamental value driven by speculation, euphoria, and herd mentality. Examples include the dot-com bubble (2000) and the housing bubble (2007-2008).
What is a 'market correction'?
A market correction is a 10-20% decline from recent highs. Corrections occur frequently (roughly once per year on average) and are a normal part of market behavior, not necessarily a sign of crisis.
What is 'subprime lending' as it relates to the 2008 crisis?
Subprime lending refers to loans made to borrowers with weak credit who have a higher risk of default. Widespread subprime mortgage lending with relaxed standards was a key cause of the 2008 financial crisis.
An investor who stayed fully invested in the S&P 500 from 2000-2020 earned about 6% annually. If they missed the 10 best days, their return dropped to approximately 2% annually. On a $100,000 initial investment over 20 years, what is the approximate dollar difference?
Fully invested at 6%: $100,000 x (1.06)^20 = $320,714. Missed 10 best days at 2%: $100,000 x (1.02)^20 = $148,595. Difference = $320,714 - $148,595 = $172,119. Missing just 10 days over 20 years cost about $172,000.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
The dot-com bubble was concentrated in technology stocks. The NASDAQ Composite fell roughly 78% from its March 2000 peak as speculative internet company valuations collapsed.
Real-World Applications

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered the fastest bear market in history, with the S&P 500 dropping 34% in just 23 trading days from mid-February to late March 2020.
Example: Market crashes (declines of 20% or more) happen on average every:
Bear markets (20%+ declines) occur roughly every 3-5 years on average. They are a normal part of market cycles, and long-term investors will experience several during their investing lifetime.

Recovery needed = ($200,000 - $86,000) / $86,000 = $114,000 / $86,000 = 132.6%, approximately 133%. This illustrates why avoiding large losses is so important -- a 57% loss requires a 133% gain just to break even.
Example: During the COVID crash, an investor continued dollar cost averaging $2,000/month. They invested $2,000 each in March (S&P at 2,300), April (2,500), and May (2,900). What was their average purchase price?

Recovery needed = (5,049 - 1,114) / 1,114 = 3,935 / 1,114 = 353%. A 78% decline requires a staggering 353% gain just to break even, illustrating the asymmetric math of losses.

--- Lesson 8.7: Technology in Markets (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Technology in Markets
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'robo-advisor'?
A robo-advisor is an automated platform that uses algorithms to build, manage, and rebalance diversified portfolios based on each user's risk profile and investment goals, typically at a fraction of the cost of human advisors.
What is 'payment for order flow' (PFOF)?
PFOF is how commission-free brokerages make money. They route customer orders to market makers who pay for the privilege of executing those trades, which raises some concerns about whether investors get the best execution prices.
What is an 'ETF' (Exchange-Traded Fund)?
An ETF is a pooled investment fund that trades on exchanges throughout the day like a stock. Most ETFs track an index, sector, or asset class and offer low-cost diversification.
What is 'algorithmic trading'?
Algorithmic trading uses computer programs to execute trades based on pre-programmed rules (price, volume, timing patterns). It accounts for the majority of stock market trading volume today.
What is a 'flash crash' in the context of market technology?
A flash crash is an extremely rapid market decline and often quick recovery, sometimes within minutes. The May 2010 flash crash saw the Dow drop ~1,000 points in minutes, largely due to algorithmic trading interactions.
What is 'social trading'?
Social trading platforms allow users to see and copy other investors' trades. While it can help beginners learn, past performance of copied traders does not guarantee future results.
A robo-advisor charges 0.25% on $50,000 while a traditional advisor charges 1% on the same amount. What is the annual fee difference, and approximately how much would that difference grow to over 30 years at 8% returns?
Robo fee: $50,000 x 0.0025 = $125/year. Advisor fee: $50,000 x 0.01 = $500/year. Difference = $375/year. Compounded at 8% for 30 years: $375 x [(1.08^30 - 1)/0.08] = ~$42,400.
An ETF tracking the S&P 500 (like VOO) has an expense ratio of 0.03%. On a $200,000 investment, what is the annual fee?

--- Lesson 8.8: Behavioral Finance (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Behavioral Finance
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 'loss aversion' in behavioral finance?
Loss aversion is a cognitive bias discovered by Kahneman and Tversky showing people feel losses roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains. It drives irrational behaviors like panic selling and refusing to realize losses.
What is the 'disposition effect'?
The disposition effect, driven by loss aversion, causes investors to sell winners too soon (to feel the pleasure of a realized gain) and hold losers too long (to avoid the pain of realizing a loss).
What is 'anchoring bias' in investing?
Anchoring bias means fixating on an irrelevant reference point. Investors anchored to their $100 purchase price may irrationally hold a stock at $60, waiting to 'break even' rather than evaluating current value.
What is 'mental accounting' in behavioral finance?
Mental accounting causes people to treat a $5,000 tax refund differently than $5,000 in salary, perhaps spending the refund more freely. Rationally, all money is the same regardless of source.
What is 'availability bias'?
Availability bias causes people to judge probability based on how easily examples come to mind. After a dramatic crash, people overestimate the likelihood of another crash because the event is vivid and memorable.
What is the 'endowment effect'?
The endowment effect causes people to place a higher value on things simply because they own them. An investor might refuse to sell a stock at $80 that they would never buy at $80, simply because they already own it.
What is the 'framing effect'?
The framing effect shows that presentation matters. '90% success rate' and '10% failure rate' are identical, but people respond much more positively to the first framing. This bias affects investment decisions and risk perception.
What is 'herd mentality' in investing?
Herd mentality drives investors to follow the crowd rather than think independently. It amplifies bubbles (everyone rushes to buy) and crashes (everyone rushes to sell), typically at the worst times.
What is 'overconfidence bias'?
Overconfidence bias causes investors to overestimate their ability to pick stocks and time the market. Surveys show 74% of fund managers believe they are above average, which is statistically impossible.
What is a 'behavioral finance' approach to investing?
Behavioral finance studies how cognitive biases and emotional reactions cause investors to make irrational decisions. Understanding these biases helps investors recognize and counteract them for better outcomes.
Mental accounting: An investor treats a $10,000 inheritance as 'play money' and puts it all into a speculative stock. Meanwhile, they keep their $10,000 savings in a low-interest account. If the speculative stock drops 50%, what is the real cost of this mental accounting?
The $5,000 loss hurts equally regardless of the money's source. Mental accounting tricks people into taking excessive risks with 'found money' (bonuses, inheritances, winnings) while being overly conservative with earned income.
An index investor who automates $500/month contributions and never checks their portfolio earned 9% over 20 years. A behavioral-bias-prone investor who checked daily and reacted emotionally earned 5%. On a $500/month investment, what is the approximate dollar difference?
At 9%: $500/month for 20 years = ~$334,000. At 5%: $500/month for 20 years = ~$206,000. Difference = ~$128,000. Behavioral biases cost the emotional investor approximately $130,000 over 20 years.
Real-World Applications
Example: An overconfident investor makes 300 trades per year with an average cost of $0.02 per share in slippage and a 25% short-term capital gains tax rate. If they trade an average of 100 shares per trade at $50/share, how much do trading costs and taxes reduce their returns compared to a buy-and-hold investor?
Slippage: 300 trades x 100 shares x $0.02 = $600. Plus, frequent trading generates short-term gains taxed at 25% instead of the 15% long-term rate, adding thousands more in taxes. Combined, these costs significantly erode returns.
Example: Investor A follows a systematic plan investing $1,000/month regardless of market conditions. Investor B tries to time the market based on feelings, investing $12,000 in lump sums when they feel confident. Over 20 years at 8% average return, Investor A's consistent approach typically results in:
Research shows that systematic investing (DCA) typically matches or beats emotional market timing. Investor B tends to invest when feeling euphoric (near peaks) and hold cash when fearful (near bottoms), hurting returns.
Example: Herd mentality drove investors to buy tech stocks at peak valuations in March 2000. The NASDAQ subsequently fell 78%. An investor who put $50,000 into tech stocks at the peak would need the NASDAQ to rise what percentage just to break even?
After a 78% decline, $50,000 becomes $11,000. To recover: ($50,000 - $11,000) / $11,000 = 354%. The asymmetric math of losses means herd-driven buying at peaks requires enormous gains just to break even.
Example: An investor keeps a journal and notices they have made 15 emotional trades in the past year, each costing them an average of $200 in poor execution or bad timing. They decide to switch to an automated DCA plan. How much will they save annually, and what does this illustrate about behavioral finance?
15 trades x $200 = $3,000 in annual behavioral costs. By switching to automated DCA, the investor eliminates emotional trading decisions. This illustrates the core lesson of behavioral finance: awareness plus systematic rules is the best defense against costly biases.

    [ ] Securing a fixed interest rate on bond investments

    [*] Fixating on a specific reference point (like a purchase price or past high) and making decisions based on that anchor rather than current fundamentals
    Explanation: Anchoring bias means fixating on an irrelevant reference point. Investors anchored to their $100 purchase price may irrationally hold a stock at $60, waiting to 'break even' rather than evaluating current value.

  Q14. What is 'mental accounting' in behavioral finance?
    [ ] Using accounting software to track investments
    [ ] Mentally calculating investment returns without a calculator
    [ ] The practice of keeping separate bank accounts for different goals
    [*] The tendency to treat money differently based on its source or intended purpose, even though all money is fungible and has equal value
    Explanation: Mental accounting causes people to treat a $5,000 tax refund differently than $5,000 in salary, perhaps spending the refund more freely. Rationally, all money is the same regardless of source.

  Q15. What is 'availability bias'?
    [ ] Only investing in stocks that are easily available to purchase
    [ ] Making investments available to all income levels
    [ ] Researching all available data before making investment decisions
    [*] Overestimating the probability of events that come easily to mind, such as recent crashes or headline-grabbing events
    Explanation: Availability bias causes people to judge probability based on how easily examples come to mind. After a dramatic crash, people overestimate the likelihood of another crash because the event is vivid and memorable.

  Q16. What is the 'endowment effect'?
    [ ] The practice of leaving investments to heirs
    [*] The tendency to overvalue things you already own, demanding a higher price to sell than you would pay to buy the same thing
    [ ] The tendency to undervalue things you already own
    [ ] A financial endowment fund established by universities
    Explanation: The endowment effect causes people to place a higher value on things simply because they own them. An investor might refuse to sell a stock at $80 that they would never buy at $80, simply because they already own it.

  Q17. What is the 'framing effect'?
    [ ] Setting a time frame for investment goals
    [ ] The impact of how a picture is framed on its value at auction
    [ ] A technical analysis pattern on stock charts
    [*] The way information is presented (framed) can significantly change how people respond to it, even when the underlying facts are identical
    Explanation: The framing effect shows that presentation matters. '90% success rate' and '10% failure rate' are identical, but people respond much more positively to the first framing. This bias affects investment decisions and risk perception.

  Q18. What is 'herd mentality' in investing?
    [*] The tendency to follow the crowd, buying when everyone buys and selling when everyone sells, which amplifies market swings
    [ ] An organized group investing strategy when considering all relevant everyone factors
    [ ] A contrarian investment approach, especially during periods of significant change
    [ ] An independent analysis strategy according to widely held views on this topic
    Explanation: Herd mentality drives investors to follow the crowd rather than think independently. It amplifies bubbles (everyone rushes to buy) and crashes (everyone rushes to sell), typically at the worst times.

  Q19. What is 'overconfidence bias'?
    [*] The tendency to overestimate one's own abilities, knowledge, and the accuracy of one's predictions, leading to excessive risk-taking and trading
    [ ] Being confident that index investing will outperform active management
    [ ] Having appropriate confidence in well-researched investment decisions
    [ ] Having confidence in the long-term growth of the stock market
    Explanation: Overconfidence bias causes investors to overestimate their ability to pick stocks and time the market. Surveys show 74% of fund managers believe they are above average, which is statistically impossible.

  Q20. What is a 'behavioral finance' approach to investing?
    [*] A field studying how psychological biases and emotions influence financial decision-making, often leading to irrational choices that deviate from pure economic theory
    [ ] A theory that emotions never affect investment decisions
    [ ] A field that assumes all investors are perfectly rational
    [ ] A strategy that only uses behavioral data from social media
    Explanation: Behavioral finance studies how cognitive biases and emotional reactions cause investors to make irrational decisions. Understanding these biases helps investors recognize and counteract them for better outcomes.

  Q21. An investor bought Stock A at $100 and Stock B at $50. Stock A is now $130 (up 30%) and Stock B is now $35 (down 30%). Due to the disposition effect, the investor is most likely to:
    [*] Sell Stock A (the winner) to lock in gains and hold Stock B (the loser) hoping it recovers
    [ ] Sell both stocks immediately
    [ ] Buy more of both stocks
    [ ] Sell Stock B (the loser) and hold Stock A (the winner)
    Explanation: The disposition effect causes investors to sell winners too soon (to lock in the pleasure of a gain) and hold losers too long (to avoid the pain of realizing a loss). The investor sells the $130 winner and holds the $35 loser.

  Q22. An overconfident investor makes 300 trades per year with an average cost of $0.02 per share in slippage and a 25% short-term capital gains tax rate. If they trade an average of 100 shares per trade at $50/share, how much do trading costs and taxes reduce their returns compared to a buy-and-hold investor?
    [ ] Trading costs only matter if you lose money when viewed through a simplified analytical lens
    [*] About $600 in slippage alone, plus significantly higher tax bills from short-term gains taxed at 25% instead of long-term rates of 15%
    [ ] Trading costs are negligible when the interaction between term and average factors is taken into full account
    [ ] About $100 total in extra costs when the interaction between term and average factors is taken into full account
    Explanation: Slippage: 300 trades x 100 shares x $0.02 = $600. Plus, frequent trading generates short-term gains taxed at 25% instead of the 15% long-term rate, adding thousands more in taxes. Combined, these costs significantly erode returns.

  Q23. Investor A follows a systematic plan investing $1,000/month regardless of market conditions. Investor B tries to time the market based on feelings, investing $12,000 in lump sums when they feel confident. Over 20 years at 8% average return, Investor A's consistent approach typically results in:
    [*] Similar or better returns with far less stress, because systematic investing removes the emotional timing decisions that usually hurt performance
    [ ] Lower returns because they buy at high prices too
    [ ] Significantly worse returns since market timing always works
    [ ] Exactly the same returns as timing the market
    Explanation: Research shows that systematic investing (DCA) typically matches or beats emotional market timing. Investor B tends to invest when feeling euphoric (near peaks) and hold cash when fearful (near bottoms), hurting returns.

  Q24. An investor anchored to their $80 purchase price holds a stock now at $50. An analyst estimates the stock's fair value at $40 and declining. How much could the investor save by selling now rather than holding to their anchor?
    [ ] About $30 per share
    [ ] The purchase price is the best indicator of when to sell
    [ ] Nothing -- the stock will return to $80
    [*] About $10 per share by selling at $50 instead of waiting until it drops to $40
    Explanation: By selling at $50 instead of holding until $40, the investor saves $10/share. The anchor price of $80 is irrelevant to future value. Rational investors evaluate whether a stock is worth holding at its current price based on future prospects.

  Q25. During the COVID crash, Investor A panic sold $100,000 in stocks at the March 2020 bottom. The market recovered 70% over the next 12 months. How much did panic selling cost Investor A?
    [ ] $100,000 in missed gains
    [ ] $30,000 in missed gains
    [*] $70,000 in missed gains (the portfolio would have been worth $170,000)
    [ ] Nothing -- selling at the bottom preserved capital
    Explanation: If Investor A had stayed invested, the $100,000 would have grown by 70% to $170,000. By panic selling at the bottom, they locked in the loss and missed $70,000 in recovery gains -- a textbook case of loss aversion harming returns.

  Q26. Mental accounting: An investor treats a $10,000 inheritance as 'play money' and puts it all into a speculative stock. Meanwhile, they keep their $10,000 savings in a low-interest account. If the speculative stock drops 50%, what is the real cost of this mental accounting?
    [ ] $10,000 total loss
    [ ] $5,000 -- but it doesn't matter because it was 'free money'
    [ ] $0 -- inheritance money doesn't count
    [*] $5,000 -- it matters equally because all money has the same value regardless of source
    Explanation: The $5,000 loss hurts equally regardless of the money's source. Mental accounting tricks people into taking excessive risks with 'found money' (bonuses, inheritances, winnings) while being overly conservative with earned income.

  Q27. A framing experiment: Fund A has a '95% chance of positive returns over 20 years.' Fund B has 'a 5% chance of negative returns over 20 years.' Both describe the same fund. Which framing attracts more investors, and what does this illustrate?
    [ ] Fund B attracts more investors because people focus on risk
    [ ] Both attract equal numbers since the information is the same
    [*] Fund A attracts more investors, illustrating the framing effect -- identical information presented positively is perceived more favorably
    [ ] Neither attracts investors since both mention risk
    Explanation: Fund A's positive framing ('95% chance of positive returns') attracts more investors than Fund B's negative framing ('5% chance of negative returns') even though both convey identical information. This demonstrates the framing effect.

  Q28. An index investor who automates $500/month contributions and never checks their portfolio earned 9% over 20 years. A behavioral-bias-prone investor who checked daily and reacted emotionally earned 5%. On a $500/month investment, what is the approximate dollar difference?
    [ ] About $20,000 difference
    [ ] About $50,000 difference
    [*] About $130,000 difference
    [ ] About $300,000 difference
    Explanation: At 9%: $500/month for 20 years = ~$334,000. At 5%: $500/month for 20 years = ~$206,000. Difference = ~$128,000. Behavioral biases cost the emotional investor approximately $130,000 over 20 years.

  Q29. Herd mentality drove investors to buy tech stocks at peak valuations in March 2000. The NASDAQ subsequently fell 78%. An investor who put $50,000 into tech stocks at the peak would need the NASDAQ to rise what percentage just to break even?
    [ ] 200% gain to break even
    [*] ~353% gain to break even
    [ ] 78% gain to break even
    [ ] 100% gain to break even
    Explanation: After a 78% decline, $50,000 becomes $11,000. To recover: ($50,000 - $11,000) / $11,000 = 354%. The asymmetric math of losses means herd-driven buying at peaks requires enormous gains just to break even.

  Q30. An investor keeps a journal and notices they have made 15 emotional trades in the past year, each costing them an average of $200 in poor execution or bad timing. They decide to switch to an automated DCA plan. How much will they save annually, and what does this illustrate about behavioral finance?
    [ ] $1,500 annually; illustrates that emotional trading is sometimes beneficial
    [ ] $0 because emotional trades eventually balance out
    [ ] $200 annually; illustrates that individual trades don't add up
    [*] $3,000 annually; illustrates that awareness of biases combined with systematic rules can significantly reduce their cost
    Explanation: 15 trades x $200 = $3,000 in annual behavioral costs. By switching to automated DCA, the investor eliminates emotional trading decisions. This illustrates the core lesson of behavioral finance: awareness plus systematic rules is the best defense against costly biases.
