--- Lesson 11.1: Heat Conversion (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heat Conversion
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is heat (q) in thermochemistry?
Heat (q) is the transfer of energy between objects or systems due to a temperature difference. It flows from hotter objects to cooler ones.
What is the difference between a calorie and a Calorie?
A calorie (lowercase, cal) is the energy to raise 1 g of water by 1 degree C. A Calorie (capital C, kcal) = 1,000 cal and is used on food labels.
What is a joule defined as?
A joule (J) is the SI unit of energy, defined as kg*m^2/s^2. It is named after physicist James Prescott Joule.
What is a conversion factor?
A conversion factor is a ratio that expresses the relationship between two units. For heat: 1 cal / 4.184 J or 4.184 J / 1 cal.
What is the prefix 'kilo' equal to in the metric system?
The prefix 'kilo' means 1,000. So 1 kilojoule = 1,000 joules, and 1 kilocalorie = 1,000 calories.
What is 'dimensional analysis' as used in heat conversions?
Dimensional analysis is a problem-solving method where you multiply by conversion factors so unwanted units cancel, leaving the desired units. For example: 100 cal x (4.184 J / 1 cal) = 418.4 J.
What is the definition of a calorie (lowercase c)?
One calorie (lowercase c) is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1 degree Celsius.

--- Lesson 11.2: Specific Heat (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Specific Heat
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is specific heat capacity?
Specific heat capacity (c) is the amount of energy (in joules) required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius. It is an intensive property.
What is an 'intensive property' and how does specific heat relate?
An intensive property does not depend on sample size. Specific heat is intensive: 1 g of water and 100 g of water both have c = 4.184 J/g*C.
What is 'thermal equilibrium'?
Thermal equilibrium is reached when two objects in contact reach the same temperature and net heat flow between them stops.
What is an 'extensive property' and is heat (q) extensive or intensive?
An extensive property depends on sample size. Heat (q) is extensive: heating 100 g of water requires more energy than heating 10 g by the same DeltaT.

  Q3. A substance with a high specific heat:

  Q6. Metals generally have:

  Q7. Water's high specific heat makes it useful for:
    [ ] Making strong materials
    [*] Regulating temperature in organisms and environments
    [ ] Producing light energy
    [ ] Conducting electricity
    Explanation: Water's high specific heat (4.184 J/g*C) allows it to absorb and release large amounts of heat without large temperature changes, making it ideal for regulating body and environmental temperatures.

  Q8. In q = mcDeltaT, DeltaT represents:
    [*] The change in temperature (final minus initial)
    [ ] The total temperature of the substance
    [ ] The temperature at which the substance boils
    [ ] The room temperature during the experiment
    Explanation: DeltaT = T_final - T_initial. It is the change in temperature, not the absolute temperature.

  Q9. If 50 g of water absorbs 2,092 J, the temperature change is:
    [*] 10 degrees C
    [ ] 5 degrees C
    [ ] 50 degrees C
    [ ] 20 degrees C
    Explanation: DeltaT = q / (mc) = 2,092 / (50 x 4.184) = 2,092 / 209.2 = 10 degrees C.

  Q10. The specific heat of copper is 0.385 J/g*C. Compared to water, copper:

    [ ] Has the same heating rate as water
    [ ] Cannot change temperature at all
    Explanation: Copper's specific heat (0.385) is much lower than water's (4.184), so copper needs less energy per gram per degree, heating and cooling much faster.

    [ ] The amount of mass that one gram of a substance can dissolve in solution
    Explanation: Specific heat capacity (c) is the amount of energy (in joules) required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius. It is an intensive property.

    [ ] The number of calories in one gram of the substance being tested

    Explanation: An intensive property does not depend on sample size. Specific heat is intensive: 1 g of water and 100 g of water both have c = 4.184 J/g*C.

--- Lesson 11.3: Heat Capacity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heat Capacity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is heat capacity?
Heat capacity (C) is the total energy needed to raise the temperature of an entire object (not per gram) by 1 degree Celsius.
What is the difference between heat capacity (C) and specific heat (c)?
Heat capacity (C, in J/C) depends on total mass. Specific heat (c, in J/g*C) is per gram and is independent of mass.
What is meant by 'temperature regulation' in relation to heat capacity?
Temperature regulation means keeping temperatures stable. High heat capacity substances (like water) absorb and release large amounts of energy with small temperature changes, buffering temperature swings.
What is the relationship between heat capacity and the amount of heat needed?
q = C x DeltaT. The heat needed equals the heat capacity times the temperature change.
What is 'molar heat capacity'?
Molar heat capacity is the energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 mole of a substance by 1 degree C. Its units are J/(mol*C).

  Key Concepts: Calorimetry
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a calorimeter?
A calorimeter is an insulated device that measures the heat exchanged during chemical or physical processes by tracking temperature changes.
What is the definition of calorimetry?
Calorimetry is the branch of science concerned with measuring the heat of chemical reactions or physical changes.
What is a 'coffee-cup calorimeter'?
A coffee-cup calorimeter is a simple, inexpensive calorimeter made from insulated cups (often Styrofoam). It operates at constant atmospheric pressure.
What is a 'bomb calorimeter'?
A bomb calorimeter is a sturdy, sealed device used to measure the heat of combustion reactions at constant volume. The 'bomb' is the sealed reaction chamber.
What is the 'surroundings' in a calorimetry experiment?
In calorimetry, the surroundings are the water and calorimeter that exchange heat with the reaction (the system).
What is 'constant pressure calorimetry'?
Constant pressure calorimetry is done in an open container (like a coffee cup) at atmospheric pressure. The heat measured equals the enthalpy change.

--- Lesson 11.5: Enthalpy, Entropy, Free Energy (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Enthalpy, Entropy, Free Energy
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is enthalpy (DeltaH)?
Enthalpy (DeltaH) is the heat change at constant pressure. Negative DeltaH = exothermic; positive DeltaH = endothermic.
What is entropy (DeltaS)?
Entropy (S) is a measure of disorder or randomness. DeltaS is the change in entropy during a process. Positive DeltaS means increased disorder.
What is Gibbs free energy (DeltaG)?
Gibbs free energy combines enthalpy and entropy: DeltaG = DeltaH - TDeltaS. It predicts spontaneity.
What is the 'first law of thermodynamics'?
The first law of thermodynamics (conservation of energy) states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or converted.
What is 'nonspontaneous' in thermodynamics?
A nonspontaneous process has DeltaG > 0 and requires continuous energy input to occur. The reverse of a nonspontaneous process is spontaneous.
What is 'absolute zero' in thermodynamics?
Absolute zero is 0 K (-273.15 degrees C), the theoretical lowest temperature where molecular motion approaches its minimum.

--- Lesson 11.6: Hess's Law (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Hess's Law
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is Hess's Law?
Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the pathway between the reactants and products.
What is a 'state function'?
A state function depends only on the current state (like elevation). Enthalpy, entropy, and free energy are state functions. Work and heat are not.
What is 'standard enthalpy of formation' (DeltaHf)?
DeltaHf is the enthalpy change when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its elements, each in their standard states, at 25 degrees C and 1 atm.
What is a 'target reaction' in Hess's Law problems?
The target reaction is the overall reaction whose DeltaH you want to find. You manipulate and add known reactions to match it.
What is 'standard conditions' in thermochemistry?
Standard conditions in thermochemistry are 25 degrees C (298 K), 1 atm pressure, and 1 M for solutions. The degree symbol (°) indicates standard conditions.

If you multiply all coefficients by a factor n, DeltaH is also multiplied by n.

    Explanation: Reverse reaction 2: CO -> C + 1/2 O2, DeltaH = +110.5. Add to reaction 1: C + O2 -> CO2, DeltaH = -393.5. Total: CO + 1/2 O2 -> CO2, DeltaH = -283.0 kJ.

  Q23. A scientist cannot directly measure the DeltaH for the formation of methane (C + 2H2 -> CH4). Using Hess's Law with combustion data is useful because:
    [*] Combustion reactions are easy to measure in a calorimeter and can be combined
    [ ] Formation reactions always have the same DeltaH as combustion reactions
    [ ] Combustion reactions do not involve enthalpy changes at all during the process
    [ ] Methane cannot be formed from its elements under any conditions in the lab
    Explanation: Some reactions cannot be directly measured. Hess's Law lets us combine easily measurable reactions (like combustion) to calculate DeltaH for difficult reactions.

  Q24. A student is told the DeltaHf of CO2 = -393.5 kJ/mol, H2O = -285.8 kJ/mol, and C3H8 = -103.8 kJ/mol. The DeltaH for combustion of propane (C3H8 + 5O2 -> 3CO2 + 4H2O) is:
    [*] -2,220 kJ because DeltaH = [3(-393.5) + 4(-285.8)] - [-103.8 + 0]
    [ ] +2,220 kJ because the combustion of propane absorbs heat from surroundings
    [ ] -679.3 kJ because you add all three DeltaHf values together directly
    [ ] -103.8 kJ because the DeltaHf of propane is the answer by itself alone
    Explanation: DeltaH = [3(-393.5) + 4(-285.8)] - [-103.8 + 5(0)] = [-1180.5 + (-1143.2)] - [-103.8] = -2323.7 + 103.8 = -2219.9 kJ, approximately -2,220 kJ.

  Q25. A student reverses a reaction (DeltaH = -250 kJ) and multiplies it by 3. The new DeltaH is:
    [*] +750 kJ because reversing changes sign and multiplying by 3 triples it
    [ ] -750 kJ because multiplying by 3 triples but the sign stays the same
    [ ] +250 kJ because reversing only changes the sign and nothing else
    [ ] -250 kJ because the enthalpy never changes regardless of manipulation
    Explanation: Reverse: DeltaH becomes +250 kJ. Then multiply by 3: 3 x (+250) = +750 kJ.

  Q26. An engineer needs to calculate the heat released by an industrial process that involves three steps with DeltaH values of -120, +80, and -200 kJ. The total heat change is:
    [*] -240 kJ because DeltaH_total = -120 + 80 + (-200) = -240 kJ
    [ ] +400 kJ because you add the absolute values of all three steps
    [ ] -400 kJ because you add the absolute values then make it negative
    [ ] +160 kJ because -120 + 80 - 200 rounds to +160 kJ from the total
    Explanation: DeltaH_total = -120 + 80 + (-200) = -240 kJ. Using Hess's Law, you add the DeltaH values of each step.

  Q27. A student asks: 'Why do we need Hess's Law if we can just measure DeltaH directly?' The best answer is:
    [*] Some reactions are too slow, dangerous, or impractical to measure directly in a lab
    [ ] All reactions must use Hess's Law because direct measurement is never accurate
    [ ] Hess's Law gives different results than direct measurement for the same reaction
    [ ] Direct measurement always gives negative values while Hess's Law gives positive
    Explanation: Many reactions cannot be measured directly because they are too slow, too dangerous, or produce side reactions. Hess's Law lets us calculate DeltaH from measurable reactions.

  Q28. A textbook lists the DeltaHf of liquid water as -285.8 kJ/mol and gaseous water as -241.8 kJ/mol. The enthalpy of vaporization of water is:
    [*] +44.0 kJ/mol because DeltaH_vap = (-241.8) - (-285.8) = +44.0 kJ/mol
    [ ] -44.0 kJ/mol because liquid water has more energy than gaseous water
    [ ] -527.6 kJ/mol because you add both DeltaHf values together for the total
    [ ] +527.6 kJ/mol because you add the absolute values of both DeltaHf values
    Explanation: H2O(l) -> H2O(g). DeltaH = DeltaHf(products) - DeltaHf(reactants) = -241.8 - (-285.8) = +44.0 kJ/mol. Vaporization is endothermic.

  Q29. A chemistry class is calculating the enthalpy of a reaction using three given equations. A student cancels out intermediate species that appear on both sides. This cancellation works because:
    [*] Hess's Law treats reactions like algebraic equations that can be added and cancelled
    [ ] Intermediate species always have zero enthalpy and do not contribute to the total
    [ ] The cancelled species were errors in the original equations that need to be removed
    [ ] Only the first and last species in a series of reactions affect the final DeltaH
    Explanation: Hess's Law allows reactions to be treated like algebraic equations. Species appearing on both sides of the summed equations cancel out, leaving only the target reaction.

  Q30. A environmental scientist calculates that converting CO2 back to carbon and oxygen (CO2 -> C + O2) has DeltaH = +393.5 kJ. This tells us that carbon capture technology:
    [*] Requires significant energy input because the reverse of combustion is endothermic
    [ ] Would release energy because breaking CO2 apart produces free oxygen gas
    [ ] Is impossible because Hess's Law does not allow reactions to be reversed
    [ ] Has no energy cost because the enthalpy of formation of O2 is zero by definition
    Explanation: The decomposition of CO2 is the reverse of its formation, so DeltaH = +393.5 kJ (endothermic). Carbon capture requires substantial energy input, which is a real engineering challenge.
