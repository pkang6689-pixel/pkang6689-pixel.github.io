--- Lesson 5B.1: Covalent Bonds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Covalent Bonds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a covalent bond?
A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This type of bonding typically occurs between two nonmetal atoms.
What is a nonpolar covalent bond?
A nonpolar covalent bond occurs when two atoms share electrons equally, typically between two identical atoms like in H2 or O2.
What is a polar covalent bond?
A polar covalent bond forms when electrons are shared unequally because one atom has a higher electronegativity than the other, creating partial charges.
What is electronegativity?
Electronegativity is the measure of an atom's ability to attract shared electrons in a covalent bond. Differences in electronegativity determine bond polarity.
What is a lone pair of electrons?
A lone pair (or nonbonding pair) is a pair of valence electrons on an atom that is not shared with another atom in a covalent bond.
What is the octet rule in covalent bonding?
The octet rule states that atoms tend to form bonds so that each atom has eight electrons in its valence shell, achieving a stable noble gas configuration.
What is a molecular compound?
A molecular compound is formed when atoms share electrons through covalent bonds. They are typically composed of nonmetals and have lower melting points than ionic compounds.
What is the term for the minimum energy needed to break a covalent bond?
Bond dissociation energy is the amount of energy required to break one mole of a specific covalent bond. Stronger bonds have higher bond dissociation energies.

  Q26. A student is given two unknown substances. Substance A dissolves in water and conducts electricity. Substance B dissolves in water but does not conduct. Which is likely covalent?

  Q28. A student blows up a balloon and notices it holds its shape. The rubber in the balloon is a polymer. What type of bonding holds the atoms together within each rubber molecule?

  Q29. In a lab, a student heats a covalent compound (candle wax) and an ionic compound (table salt). The wax melts at about 60 degrees C, but the salt requires over 800 degrees C. What conclusion can the student draw?

    Explanation: Covalent (molecular) compounds generally have lower melting points than ionic compounds because the intermolecular forces between molecules are much weaker than the ionic bonds in a crystal lattice.

  Q30. A student wants to determine if a mystery liquid is a covalent or ionic compound dissolved in water. She tests it with a conductivity meter and gets no reading. She also notes it has a low boiling point. What should she conclude?
    [ ] The compound is ionic because it dissolves in water easily
    [*] The compound is covalent because it does not conduct and has a low boiling point
    [ ] The compound is metallic because it has a low boiling point
    [ ] No conclusion can be drawn from these two observations
    Explanation: A lack of electrical conductivity in solution and a low boiling point are both characteristic of covalent (molecular) compounds, which do not form ions when dissolved and have weak intermolecular forces.

--- Lesson 5B.2: Naming Covalent Compounds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Covalent Compounds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the correct name for CO2?
CO2 is carbon dioxide. Carbon has no prefix (mono- is dropped for the first element), and dioxide indicates two oxygen atoms.
What is the correct name for N2O5?
N2O5 is dinitrogen pentoxide. Di- indicates two nitrogen atoms and penta- indicates five oxygen atoms, with -ide ending on oxide.
What is the correct formula for dinitrogen monoxide?
Dinitrogen means two nitrogen atoms and monoxide means one oxygen atom, giving the formula N2O, also known as laughing gas.
What is the correct name for PCl5?
PCl5 is phosphorus pentachloride. There is one phosphorus (mono- is dropped for the first element) and five chlorines (penta- prefix).
What is the definition of a covalent compound in terms of naming?
Covalent compounds are made of two nonmetals and are named using Greek numerical prefixes to indicate the number of each type of atom in the molecule.
What is the meaning of the suffix '-ide' in covalent compound naming?
The suffix -ide is always added to the root of the second element's name in a covalent compound, such as chlor-ide, ox-ide, or sulfide.
What is the difference between naming ionic compounds and naming covalent compounds?
Covalent compounds use Greek numerical prefixes (mono-, di-, tri-, etc.) to indicate atom counts, while ionic compounds use the metal name and charge (sometimes Roman numerals).
What is the correct order for writing a covalent compound name?
In covalent compound naming, the element that appears farther left or lower on the periodic table is named first, and the second element gets the -ide suffix.
What is the systematic name for the compound with the formula SF6?
SF6 is sulfur hexafluoride. Mono- is dropped from sulfur (first element), and hexa- indicates six fluorine atoms with the -ide suffix.
In a lab, a student has a bottle labeled 'dinitrogen tetroxide.' What is the chemical formula for this compound?
Dinitrogen means two nitrogen atoms and tetroxide means four oxygen atoms, so the formula is N2O4.
In a lab report, a student writes the name 'disulfur decaoxide' for S2O10. A classmate says the prefix for 10 is wrong. What is the correct prefix for 10?
Deca- is the correct Greek prefix meaning ten. The name disulfur decaoxide would actually be correct if the formula were S2O10.
A student needs to name the compound IF7 for a quiz. What is the correct name?
IF7 is iodine heptafluoride. Mono- is not used on the first element (iodine), and hepta- indicates seven fluorine atoms.
A student is asked why CO is called carbon monoxide and not just carbon oxide. What is the best explanation?
The prefix mono- on the second element is important to distinguish compounds. Without it, 'carbon oxide' could refer to either CO or CO2. The mono- clarifies there is only one oxygen.

--- Lesson 5B.3: Metallic Bonds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Metallic Bonds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a metallic bond?
A metallic bond involves metal cations surrounded by a 'sea' of delocalized (freely moving) electrons that are shared among all the metal atoms in the structure.
What is the 'electron sea' model in metallic bonding?
The electron sea model describes metallic bonding as a lattice of positive metal cations surrounded by a 'sea' of freely moving delocalized valence electrons.
What is malleability?
Malleability is the ability of a material to be deformed under pressure (hammered, rolled, or pressed) into thin sheets without breaking.
What is ductility?
Ductility is the property that allows a metal to be pulled or drawn into thin wires. Copper and gold are highly ductile metals.
What is metallic luster?
Metallic luster is the shiny, reflective appearance of metals. It occurs because delocalized electrons can absorb and re-emit photons of light.
What is electrical conductivity?
Electrical conductivity is the measure of how well a material allows electric current (the flow of electrons) to pass through it. Metals are excellent conductors.
What is the term for the positive metal ions in a metallic bond?
In metallic bonding, metal atoms lose their valence electrons and become positively charged cations. These cations are surrounded by the sea of delocalized electrons.
What is a lattice structure in the context of metallic bonding?
In metallic bonding, the metal cations are arranged in a regular, repeating three-dimensional pattern called a lattice, with delocalized electrons filling the spaces between them.

--- Lesson 5B.4: Organic Compounds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Organic Compounds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an organic compound?
An organic compound is a chemical compound that contains carbon atoms, typically bonded to hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, or other carbon atoms through covalent bonds.
What is a hydrocarbon?
A hydrocarbon is an organic compound consisting entirely of hydrogen and carbon atoms. They are the simplest organic compounds and include alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes.
What is the difference between a saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbon?
Saturated hydrocarbons (alkanes) contain only single C-C bonds and are 'saturated' with hydrogen. Unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes/alkynes) have double or triple bonds.
What is a functional group in organic chemistry?
A functional group is a specific arrangement of atoms within an organic molecule that determines the compound's chemical properties and reactions, such as -OH (hydroxyl) or -COOH (carboxyl).
What is a polymer?
A polymer is a large organic molecule made up of many repeating units called monomers linked together in a chain. Examples include plastics, DNA, and proteins.
What is an isomer?
Isomers are molecules that have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements of atoms, giving them different properties.
What is the term for a small repeating unit that forms a polymer?
A monomer is the small repeating unit that links together to form a polymer. For example, glucose monomers link to form starch (a polymer).
A student is asked why there are millions of organic compounds but far fewer inorganic ones. What is the best explanation?
Carbon's ability to form four bonds and bond to itself in chains, branches, and rings of virtually unlimited length creates an enormous diversity of possible organic structures.

--- Lesson 5B.5: Mixed Nomenclature Practice (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mixed Nomenclature Practice
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the name of CaCl2?
CaCl2 is calcium chloride. Since calcium is a metal and chlorine is a nonmetal, this is ionic. Ionic names don't use Greek prefixes, and calcium always has a +2 charge so no Roman numeral is needed.
What is the name of N2O4?
N2O4 is dinitrogen tetroxide. Both elements are nonmetals, so this is covalent and uses Greek prefixes: di- for two nitrogen and tetra- for four oxygen.
What is the correct name for MgO?
MgO is magnesium oxide. Magnesium is a metal and oxygen is a nonmetal, making this ionic. No prefixes are needed, and magnesium always has a +2 charge so no Roman numeral is needed.
What is the formula for iron(III) oxide?
Iron(III) means Fe3+ and oxide is O2-. To balance charges: 2(+3) + 3(-2) = 0, giving the formula Fe2O3, which is rust.
What is the key difference between ionic and covalent nomenclature?
Covalent naming uses Greek prefixes (di-, tri-, tetra-) to indicate atom numbers. Ionic naming uses the metal name followed by the nonmetal with -ide, sometimes with Roman numerals.
What is a polyatomic ion?
A polyatomic ion is a group of two or more atoms that are covalently bonded together and carry an overall electrical charge, such as sulfate (SO4 2-) or nitrate (NO3-).
What is a cation?
A cation is a positively charged ion formed when an atom (usually a metal) loses one or more electrons. In ionic naming, the cation is always named first.
What is an anion?
An anion is a negatively charged ion formed when an atom (usually a nonmetal) gains one or more electrons. In ionic naming, the anion name ends in -ide.
What is the term for a transition metal that can have more than one charge?
A multivalent metal can form ions with different charges. For example, iron can be Fe2+ or Fe3+, and copper can be Cu+ or Cu2+. Roman numerals distinguish them.
A student encounters the compound PbO2 and needs to name it. Lead (Pb) is a transition metal. What is the correct name?
PbO2 is ionic (metal + nonmetal). Each O is 2-, so two O = 4-. Lead must be 4+ to balance. The name is lead(IV) oxide. Greek prefixes are not used for ionic compounds.
A student is asked to name the compound Cr2O3. Chromium is a transition metal with multiple possible charges. What is the correct name?
Cr2O3 is ionic. Three O2- ions total -6, so two Cr ions must total +6, meaning each Cr is 3+. The name is chromium(III) oxide using the Stock system.
A student must decide how to name SnO2. Tin (Sn) is a metal with multiple charges. What is the correct approach?
SnO2 is ionic (metal + nonmetal). Two O2- = -4 total, so Sn must be +4. Since tin has multiple possible charges, a Roman numeral is needed: tin(IV) oxide.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student is asked to name the compound Cr2O3. Chromium is a transition metal with multiple possible charges. What is the correct name?
Cr2O3 is ionic. Three O2- ions total -6, so two Cr ions must total +6, meaning each Cr is 3+. The name is chromium(III) oxide using the Stock system.
Example: A student must decide how to name SnO2. Tin (Sn) is a metal with multiple charges. What is the correct approach?
SnO2 is ionic (metal + nonmetal). Two O2- = -4 total, so Sn must be +4. Since tin has multiple possible charges, a Roman numeral is needed: tin(IV) oxide.
