--- Lesson 9.1: Solution Nomenclature (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solution Nomenclature
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of a solution in chemistry?
A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances where the solute is evenly distributed throughout the solvent at the molecular level.
What is an amalgam?
An amalgam is an alloy (solid solution) that contains mercury. Dental amalgam, used for fillings, is a mixture of mercury with silver, tin, and copper.
What is the 'universal solvent' and why is it called that?
Water is called the universal solvent because it dissolves more substances than any other liquid, due to its polar nature allowing it to interact with many different solutes.
A student opens a bottle of soda and sees bubbles forming. What is the solute and what is the solvent in the unopened soda?
In carbonated soda, CO2 gas is dissolved in water (the solvent), making it the solute. When the bottle is opened, pressure decreases and CO2 comes out of solution as bubbles.
A student reads a drink label that says 'contains 10% juice.' In this beverage, what is the solute?
The juice components (sugars, flavors, acids) are the solutes dissolved in water, which is the solvent making up the bulk of the beverage.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of these is an example of a solution?
Sweet tea is a solution because the sugar is completely dissolved in the water, creating a homogeneous mixture. The other examples are heterogeneous mixtures.
Example: A student reads a drink label that says 'contains 10% juice.' In this beverage, what is the solute?
The juice components (sugars, flavors, acids) are the solutes dissolved in water, which is the solvent making up the bulk of the beverage.

--- Lesson 9.1: Solution Nomenclature (Quiz) ---

  Q1. In a solution, the solvent is the substance that:
    [*] Dissolves the solute and is present in greater amount
    [ ] Is dissolved in the other substance in the mixture
    [ ] Settles to the bottom of the container over time
    [ ] Is always a solid material in the solution mixture
    Explanation: The solvent is the substance that does the dissolving and is usually present in the greater amount. Water is the most common solvent.

  Q2. The solute is the substance that:
    [ ] Is always present in the greater amount overall
    [ ] Does the dissolving of the other substance present
    [*] Is dissolved in the solvent in the solution mixture
    [ ] Is always a liquid material in the solution mixture
    Explanation: The solute is the substance that gets dissolved in the solvent. It is usually present in the smaller amount in a solution.

  Q3. An aqueous solution uses what as the solvent?
    [ ] Mercury as the primary solvent
    [ ] Alcohol as the primary solvent
    [*] Water as the primary solvent
    [ ] Oil as the primary dissolving agent
    Explanation: An aqueous solution is one where water is the solvent. The symbol (aq) in chemical equations indicates a substance is dissolved in water.

  Q4. The notation (aq) after a formula means:
    [ ] The substance is in the gas phase state
    [ ] The substance is a pure undissolved liquid
    [*] The substance is dissolved in water (aqueous)
    [ ] The substance exists as a solid in the reaction
    Explanation: The notation (aq) stands for aqueous, meaning the substance is dissolved in water. Other state symbols include (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, and (g) for gas.

  Q5. A solution is a type of:
    [*] Homogeneous mixture with uniform composition
    [ ] Heterogeneous mixture with visible particles
    [ ] Pure element with only one type of atom
    [ ] Pure substance that cannot be separated
    Explanation: A solution is a homogeneous mixture where the solute is evenly distributed throughout the solvent at the molecular level, giving it a uniform composition.

  Q6. In salt water, the solute is:
    [ ] Water (H2O) because it is the liquid
    [ ] Both salt and water contribute equally
    [*] Salt (NaCl) because it is dissolved in water
    [ ] Neither substance is the solute in this case
    Explanation: In salt water, salt (NaCl) is the solute because it is the substance being dissolved, and water is the solvent because it does the dissolving.

  Q7. A tincture is a solution that uses what as the solvent?
    [*] Alcohol is the solvent in a tincture
    [ ] Oil is the solvent used in a tincture
    [ ] Water is the solvent used in a tincture
    [ ] Gasoline is the solvent in a tincture
    Explanation: A tincture is a solution in which alcohol is the solvent. Tincture of iodine, for example, is iodine dissolved in alcohol.

  Q8. Which of these is an example of a solution?
    [ ] Sand and water mixed together in a jar
    [ ] Oil and vinegar layered in a salad bowl
    [*] Sweet tea with sugar fully dissolved in it
    [ ] Chunky peanut butter with visible nut pieces
    Explanation: Sweet tea is a solution because the sugar is completely dissolved in the water, creating a homogeneous mixture. The other examples are heterogeneous mixtures.

  Q9. Can gases dissolve in liquids to form solutions?
    [ ] No, only solids can dissolve in liquids to form solutions
    [*] Yes, like CO2 dissolved in water to make soda water
    [ ] No, gases and liquids always remain in separate layers
    [ ] Yes, but only noble gases can dissolve in water solutions
    Explanation: Gases can dissolve in liquids. Carbonated beverages are solutions of CO2 gas dissolved in water. Oxygen dissolved in water is essential for fish to breathe.

  Q10. An alloy like brass is a solution of:
    [ ] A gas dissolved in another gas at high pressure
    [ ] A solid dissolved in a liquid at room temperature
    [*] A solid dissolved in another solid metal (metal mixture)
    [ ] A liquid dissolved in a gas at very high temperature
    Explanation: Brass is a solid solution (alloy) of zinc dissolved in copper. Solutions can exist in all phases: solid, liquid, and gas.

  Q11. What is the definition of a solution in chemistry?
    [ ] A heterogeneous mixture where particles settle to the bottom
    [*] A homogeneous mixture of two or more substances evenly mixed
    [ ] A pure substance made of only one type of atom or molecule
    [ ] A chemical reaction that produces a new compound as product
    Explanation: A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances where the solute is evenly distributed throughout the solvent at the molecular level.

  Q12. What does the term 'soluble' mean?
    [ ] A substance that cannot dissolve in a given solvent at all
    [*] A substance that is able to dissolve in a given solvent
    [ ] A substance that sinks to the bottom of a liquid quickly
    [ ] A substance that floats on top of water at room temperature
    Explanation: Soluble means capable of being dissolved in a particular solvent. For example, sugar is soluble in water, meaning it dissolves when mixed.

  Q13. What does the term 'insoluble' mean?
    [ ] A substance that dissolves completely in a given solvent easily
    [*] A substance that cannot dissolve in a given solvent under conditions
    [ ] A substance that evaporates quickly when exposed to open air
    [ ] A substance that reacts chemically with the solvent to form gas
    Explanation: Insoluble means not capable of dissolving in a particular solvent. Sand is insoluble in water, meaning it does not dissolve no matter how much you stir.

  Q14. What does 'miscible' mean when referring to two liquids?
    [ ] The two liquids cannot mix and form separate layers always
    [*] The two liquids can mix in any proportion to form a solution
    [ ] One liquid evaporates when the other liquid is added to it
    [ ] The two liquids react chemically to produce a gas product
    Explanation: Miscible means two liquids can mix together in any proportion to form a homogeneous solution. Ethanol and water are miscible liquids.

  Q15. What does 'immiscible' mean when referring to two liquids?
    [ ] The liquids mix completely to form a clear uniform solution
    [*] The liquids do not mix and instead form separate visible layers
    [ ] The liquids react with each other to form a solid precipitate
    [ ] The liquids both evaporate when combined at room temperature
    Explanation: Immiscible means two liquids cannot mix and instead form separate layers. Oil and water are immiscible because oil is nonpolar and water is polar.

  Q16. What is an amalgam?
    [ ] A solution of a gas dissolved in a liquid solvent
    [*] A solution (alloy) containing mercury as one component
    [ ] A solution of water and alcohol mixed together evenly
    [ ] A solution where the solute is present in larger amount
    Explanation: An amalgam is an alloy (solid solution) that contains mercury. Dental amalgam, used for fillings, is a mixture of mercury with silver, tin, and copper.

  Q17. What is the 'universal solvent' and why is it called that?
    [ ] Alcohol, because it dissolves both polar and nonpolar substances
    [*] Water, because it dissolves more substances than any other liquid
    [ ] Gasoline, because it dissolves all organic compounds completely
    [ ] Mercury, because it dissolves all metals to form amalgam alloys
    Explanation: Water is called the universal solvent because it dissolves more substances than any other liquid, due to its polar nature allowing it to interact with many different solutes.

  Q18. Define the term 'dissolve' in the context of solutions.
    [ ] To physically break a solid into smaller visible pieces or chunks
    [*] To incorporate a substance into a solvent at the molecular level
    [ ] To chemically react two substances to form a new compound
    [ ] To heat a substance until it melts and changes into a liquid
    Explanation: Dissolving is the process where a solute is incorporated into a solvent at the molecular level, forming a homogeneous mixture. It is a physical process, not a chemical reaction.

  Q19. What does the term 'concentration' mean in solution chemistry?
    [ ] The temperature at which a solution begins to boil over
    [*] The amount of solute present in a given amount of solution
    [ ] The total volume of solvent that is used to make a solution
    [ ] The speed at which a solute dissolves in a solvent material
    Explanation: Concentration refers to the amount of solute dissolved in a given quantity of solution or solvent. It can be expressed in various units like molarity, percent, or ppm.

  Q20. What does 'homogeneous' mean when describing a mixture?
    [ ] The components are unevenly distributed and visibly separate
    [*] The components are evenly distributed and appear as one phase
    [ ] The mixture can only contain one type of element or compound
    [ ] The mixture separates into layers when left standing for a time
    Explanation: Homogeneous means the components are uniformly distributed throughout the mixture, so every sample has the same composition. Solutions are homogeneous mixtures.

  Q21. A student dissolves sugar in hot tea and notices it disappears completely. The tea looks clear and uniform. What type of mixture has the student made?
    [ ] A heterogeneous mixture because the sugar changed temperature
    [*] A solution, which is a homogeneous mixture of sugar in water
    [ ] A suspension because the sugar will settle to the bottom later
    [ ] A pure substance because the sugar is no longer visible at all
    Explanation: When sugar dissolves completely in tea, it forms a solution (homogeneous mixture). The sugar molecules are evenly distributed throughout the liquid at the molecular level.

  Q22. A student opens a bottle of soda and sees bubbles forming. What is the solute and what is the solvent in the unopened soda?
    [ ] Water is the solute and CO2 gas is the solvent in the soda
    [*] CO2 gas is the solute and water is the solvent in the soda
    [ ] Sugar is the only solute and there is no gas dissolved at all
    [ ] The bubbles are the solvent and the water is the main solute
    Explanation: In carbonated soda, CO2 gas is dissolved in water (the solvent), making it the solute. When the bottle is opened, pressure decreases and CO2 comes out of solution as bubbles.

  Q23. A student mixes oil and water and shakes the container vigorously. After a few minutes, the oil floats to the top in a separate layer. What can the student conclude?
    [ ] Oil and water formed a solution but then separated over time
    [*] Oil and water are immiscible and do not form a true solution
    [ ] Oil dissolved in water but the solution became supersaturated
    [ ] Oil reacted chemically with water to produce a new compound
    Explanation: Oil and water are immiscible liquids. Oil is nonpolar and water is polar, so they do not dissolve in each other and always separate into distinct layers.

  Q24. A student needs to prepare an aqueous solution of sodium chloride for a lab experiment. What should the student use as the solvent?
    [ ] Ethanol because it dissolves ionic compounds efficiently
    [*] Water because aqueous means dissolved in water specifically
    [ ] Vegetable oil because it is a common liquid in the laboratory
    [ ] Any liquid will work since all liquids can serve as solvents
    Explanation: Aqueous specifically means dissolved in water. To make an aqueous NaCl solution, the student must dissolve sodium chloride in water.

  Q25. In a lab, a student dissolves a small amount of iodine in a large amount of alcohol. In this tincture, which is the solute and which is the solvent?
    [ ] Alcohol is the solute and iodine is the solvent in this tincture
    [*] Iodine is the solute and alcohol is the solvent in this tincture
    [ ] Both are solutes because neither is water in this mixture
    [ ] Neither is a solute because tinctures are not true solutions
    Explanation: In tincture of iodine, iodine is the solute (dissolved substance, smaller amount) and alcohol is the solvent (does the dissolving, larger amount).

  Q26. A student is asked whether air is a solution. What should the student answer?
    [ ] No, air is not a solution because it is a gas mixture not liquid
    [*] Yes, air is a gaseous solution of oxygen and other gases in nitrogen
    [ ] No, air is a pure substance made of only one type of gas molecule
    [ ] Yes, but only when the humidity is above a certain threshold level
    Explanation: Air is a gaseous solution. Nitrogen is the solvent (about 78%) and oxygen, argon, CO2, and other gases are solutes dissolved uniformly throughout.

  Q27. A student reads a drink label that says 'contains 10% juice.' In this beverage, what is the solute?
    [ ] Water is the solute because it is the most abundant component
    [*] Juice components are the solute dissolved in water as the solvent
    [ ] The container itself is the solute holding everything together
    [ ] There is no solute because this is not a true solution mixture
    Explanation: The juice components (sugars, flavors, acids) are the solutes dissolved in water, which is the solvent making up the bulk of the beverage.

  Q28. A student notices that when salt is added to water, the salt crystals seem to disappear. The student's classmate says the salt 'melted.' Is this correct?
    [ ] Yes, the salt melted because water is at a higher temperature
    [*] No, the salt dissolved, meaning it separated into ions in water
    [ ] Yes, melting and dissolving are exactly the same physical process
    [ ] No, the salt evaporated because it turned into a gas in solution
    Explanation: The salt dissolved, not melted. Dissolving means the solute particles separate and become surrounded by solvent molecules. Melting is a phase change from solid to liquid caused by heat.

  Q29. A student mixes rubbing alcohol and water together. They mix completely with no layers. How should the student describe this pair of liquids?
    [ ] They are immiscible because they are two different liquids
    [*] They are miscible because they mix completely in all proportions
    [ ] They are insoluble because neither one dissolved in the other
    [ ] They formed a suspension that will separate after several hours
    Explanation: Rubbing alcohol (isopropanol) and water are miscible, meaning they mix completely in any proportion to form a homogeneous solution with no separate layers.

  Q30. A student is making a solution for a lab and accidentally adds too much solute. The extra solute sits at the bottom of the beaker undissolved. What should the student identify the undissolved material as?
    [ ] It is the solvent that has settled out of the solution mixture
    [*] It is excess solute that did not dissolve because the solution is saturated
    [ ] It is a new compound that was created by a chemical reaction
    [ ] It is an immiscible liquid layer at the bottom of the beaker
    Explanation: The undissolved material is excess solute. The solution has reached its saturation point -- the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at that temperature.

--- Lesson 9.2: Concentration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Concentration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of concentration in chemistry?
Concentration is a measure of the amount of solute present in a given quantity of solution. Higher concentration means more solute per unit of solution.
What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative descriptions of concentration?
Qualitative descriptions use words like 'dilute' or 'concentrated' without exact numbers. Quantitative descriptions use precise numerical values like 0.5 M or 10%.
A student dissolves 20 g of NaCl in 80 g of water. What is the mass percent of NaCl in this solution?
Mass percent = (mass of solute / mass of solution) x 100 = (20 g / (20 g + 80 g)) x 100 = (20/100) x 100 = 20%.

--- Lesson 9.2: Concentration (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Concentration measures:
    [ ] The temperature of a solution at a given time
    [ ] The color or appearance of a solution mixture
    [ ] The volume of solvent used in the mixture only
    [*] The amount of solute in a given amount of solution
    Explanation: Concentration measures how much solute is present in a given quantity of solution or solvent. It can be expressed in units like g/L, percent, molarity, or ppm.

  Q2. A concentrated solution has:
    [ ] Very little solute dissolved in the solvent present
    [ ] No solute at all dissolved in the solvent present
    [*] A large amount of solute relative to solvent amount
    [ ] Only solvent with no solute dissolved in the mixture
    Explanation: A concentrated solution contains a large amount of solute relative to the amount of solvent. The solution has a high concentration.

  Q3. A dilute solution has:
    [ ] No solvent present in the solution mixture at all
    [ ] Solid particles that are visible floating in solution
    [*] A small amount of solute relative to solvent amount
    [ ] A very large amount of solute dissolved in the solvent
    Explanation: A dilute solution contains a small amount of solute relative to the amount of solvent. The solution has a low concentration.

  Q4. Parts per million (ppm) is used for:
    [*] Very small concentrations of solute in solution
    [ ] Very large concentrations of solute in solution
    [ ] Only gas-phase concentrations and nothing else
    [ ] Only solid-phase concentrations and nothing else
    Explanation: Parts per million (ppm) is a concentration unit used for very small amounts of solute, such as measuring pollutants in water or contaminants in food.

  Q5. Which solution is more concentrated: 5 g/L or 10 g/L?
    [ ] They are equal in concentration level
    [ ] Cannot determine from this information alone
    [ ] 5 g/L is more concentrated than 10 g/L
    [*] 10 g/L is more concentrated than 5 g/L
    Explanation: 10 g/L has more solute per liter of solution than 5 g/L, making it the more concentrated solution.

  Q6. Mass percent is calculated as:
    [*] (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) x 100
    [ ] Mass of solution divided by 100 only
    [ ] (Mass of solvent / Mass of solute) x 100
    [ ] Mass of solute multiplied by 100 only
    Explanation: Mass percent = (mass of solute / mass of solution) x 100%. The mass of solution equals mass of solute plus mass of solvent.

  Q7. If 10 g of sugar is dissolved in 90 g of water, the mass percent of sugar is:
    [ ] About 9% sugar by mass in the solution
    [ ] About 90% sugar by mass in the solution
    [ ] About 11% sugar by mass in the solution
    [*] About 10% sugar by mass in the solution
    Explanation: Mass percent = (10 g / (10 g + 90 g)) x 100 = (10/100) x 100 = 10%. The total solution mass is 100 g.

  Q8. Volume percent is used when:
    [ ] Both solute and solvent are solids in the mixture
    [*] Both solute and solvent are liquids in the mixture
    [ ] The solute is a gas and the solvent is a solid material
    [ ] The concentration is extremely low at parts per million
    Explanation: Volume percent is typically used when both the solute and solvent are liquids, such as in alcoholic beverages where the alcohol content is given as volume percent.

  Q9. A 5% salt solution means:
    [ ] 5 g of salt is dissolved in 100 g of water only
    [*] 5 g of salt per 100 g of total solution mixture
    [ ] 5 mL of salt per 1000 mL of water solvent only
    [ ] 5 moles of salt per liter of total solution volume
    Explanation: A 5% salt solution means 5 grams of salt per 100 grams of total solution. Percent concentration is always relative to the total solution, not just the solvent.

  Q10. Increasing the amount of solute in a fixed volume of solvent will:
    [ ] Decrease the concentration of the resulting solution
    [ ] Have no effect on the concentration of the solution
    [*] Increase the concentration of the resulting solution
    [ ] Change the solvent into a different chemical substance
    Explanation: Adding more solute to the same volume of solvent increases the ratio of solute to solution, which increases the concentration.

  Q11. What is the definition of concentration in chemistry?
    [ ] The total mass of solvent in a solution mixture
    [*] The measure of how much solute is in a given amount of solution
    [ ] The rate at which a solute dissolves in a particular solvent
    [ ] The temperature at which a solution becomes saturated fully
    Explanation: Concentration is a measure of the amount of solute present in a given quantity of solution. Higher concentration means more solute per unit of solution.

  Q12. What does 'dilute' mean when describing a solution?
    [ ] The solution contains a large amount of solute per unit volume
    [*] The solution contains a small amount of solute per unit volume
    [ ] The solution has been heated to its boiling point temperature
    [ ] The solution has been cooled to its freezing point temperature
    Explanation: A dilute solution has a relatively small amount of solute compared to the amount of solvent, resulting in a low concentration.

  Q13. What does 'concentrated' mean when describing a solution?
    [ ] The solution has no solute dissolved in the solvent at all
    [*] The solution has a large amount of solute per unit volume
    [ ] The solution cannot dissolve any more solvent molecules
    [ ] The solution has been filtered to remove all the solute
    Explanation: A concentrated solution has a relatively large amount of solute compared to the amount of solvent, resulting in a high concentration.

  Q14. What does parts per billion (ppb) measure?
    [ ] Very large concentrations of common laboratory chemicals
    [*] Extremely small concentrations like trace contaminants in water
    [ ] The number of molecules in exactly one mole of a substance
    [ ] The volume of gas produced in a chemical reaction at STP
    Explanation: Parts per billion (ppb) is used to measure extremely small concentrations, even smaller than ppm. It is commonly used for trace pollutants and contaminants.

  Q15. What does 'mass/volume percent' express?
    [*] Grams of solute per 100 mL of solution expressed as a percent
    [ ] Liters of solvent per kilogram of total solution as a percent
    [ ] Moles of solute per liter of solvent expressed as a percentage
    [ ] Grams of solvent per 100 mL of solute expressed as a percent
    Explanation: Mass/volume percent expresses the mass of solute (in grams) per 100 mL of solution as a percentage. It is commonly used in pharmaceutical and medical contexts.

  Q16. Define the term 'saturated solution' in terms of concentration.
    [ ] A solution that can still dissolve more solute at that temperature
    [*] A solution that holds the maximum amount of dissolved solute possible
    [ ] A solution that has had all its solvent evaporated away completely
    [ ] A solution that contains no solute dissolved at the given temperature
    Explanation: A saturated solution holds the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at a given temperature. Adding more solute will not increase concentration further.

  Q17. What does 'g/L' stand for as a concentration unit?
    [ ] Grams of solvent per liter of pure solute only
    [*] Grams of solute per liter of total solution volume
    [ ] Gallons of liquid per liter of container space used
    [ ] Grams of solution per liter of the pure solvent only
    Explanation: The unit g/L means grams of solute per liter of solution. It is a common way to express concentration, especially in everyday and medical contexts.

  Q18. What does 'percent concentration' mean?
    [*] The fraction of solute in a solution expressed as parts per hundred
    [ ] The temperature at which exactly one percent of solute dissolves
    [ ] The number of moles of solute that equals one percent of the total
    [ ] The pressure needed to dissolve exactly one percent of the solute
    Explanation: Percent concentration expresses the proportion of solute in a solution as parts per hundred. It can be mass/mass, mass/volume, or volume/volume percent.

  Q19. What does 'stock solution' mean in laboratory chemistry?
    [ ] A very dilute solution ready for immediate use in an experiment
    [*] A concentrated solution that can be diluted to desired concentrations
    [ ] A solution that has been stored past its expiration date in the lab
    [ ] A solution that contains only water with no solute dissolved in it
    Explanation: A stock solution is a concentrated solution kept in a laboratory that can be diluted with solvent to make solutions of lower concentrations as needed for experiments.

  Q20. What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative descriptions of concentration?
    [ ] There is no real difference between these two descriptions
    [*] Qualitative uses words like dilute/concentrated; quantitative uses exact numbers
    [ ] Qualitative uses exact numbers while quantitative uses general words
    [ ] Both always require numerical values and precise measurements
    Explanation: Qualitative descriptions use words like 'dilute' or 'concentrated' without exact numbers. Quantitative descriptions use precise numerical values like 0.5 M or 10%.

  Q21. A student makes two solutions: Solution A has 5 g of salt in 100 mL, and Solution B has 15 g of salt in 100 mL. Which is more concentrated?
    [ ] Solution A because it was made first in the experiment
    [ ] They are equally concentrated since both use 100 mL
    [*] Solution B because it has more solute in the same volume
    [ ] Solution A because less solute means higher concentration
    Explanation: Solution B is more concentrated because it has three times more solute (15 g vs 5 g) dissolved in the same volume of solution (100 mL).

  Q22. A student dissolves 20 g of NaCl in 80 g of water. What is the mass percent of NaCl in this solution?
    [ ] 25% because 20 divided by 80 times 100 equals 25
    [*] 20% because 20 divided by 100 times 100 equals 20
    [ ] 80% because water is most of the solution by mass
    [ ] 10% because the mass of the solute must be halved
    Explanation: Mass percent = (mass of solute / mass of solution) x 100 = (20 g / (20 g + 80 g)) x 100 = (20/100) x 100 = 20%.

  Q23. A student adds water to a dark-colored juice concentrate. The color becomes lighter. What happened to the concentration?
    [ ] The concentration increased because more liquid was added
    [*] The concentration decreased because more solvent diluted the solute
    [ ] The concentration stayed the same because no solute was removed
    [ ] The concentration cannot change by simply adding water to it
    Explanation: Adding water (solvent) to the concentrate increases the total volume while the amount of solute stays the same, decreasing the concentration and making the color lighter.

  Q24. A lab technician needs to measure the fluoride concentration in drinking water at 0.7 ppm. Why is ppm an appropriate unit here?
    [ ] Because the concentration is too large for regular percent units
    [*] Because ppm measures very small concentrations like trace amounts
    [ ] Because ppm is the only unit that works for measuring liquids
    [ ] Because ppm is easier to calculate than any other unit of measure
    Explanation: Fluoride in drinking water is present in very small amounts. PPM is appropriate for trace concentrations; 0.7 ppm = 0.00007%, which would be awkward to express as a percent.

  Q25. A student evaporates 200 mL of a salt solution and collects 10 g of salt. What was the concentration in g/L?
    [ ] 20 g/L of salt was dissolved in the solution
    [*] 50 g/L of salt was dissolved in the solution
    [ ] 10 g/L of salt was dissolved in the solution
    [ ] 100 g/L of salt was dissolved in the solution
    Explanation: 200 mL = 0.2 L. Concentration = 10 g / 0.2 L = 50 g/L. The student had 10 grams of salt in 0.2 liters of solution.

  Q26. A student makes sweet tea by adding 3 tablespoons of sugar. Her friend adds 6 tablespoons of sugar to the same amount of tea. Whose tea is more concentrated?
    [ ] The first student's tea because less sugar means more concentrated
    [ ] Both are equally concentrated since they used the same amount of tea
    [*] The friend's tea because it has more solute in the same amount of solvent
    [ ] Neither is concentrated because tea is not a true chemical solution
    Explanation: The friend's tea is more concentrated because more sugar (solute) was dissolved in the same volume of tea (solvent), giving a higher solute-to-solution ratio.

  Q27. A student reads that a saline IV solution is 0.9% NaCl. What does this mean in practical terms?
    [*] There are 0.9 grams of NaCl in every 100 grams of the solution
    [ ] There are 0.9 liters of NaCl in every 100 liters of the solution
    [ ] There are 9 grams of NaCl dissolved in every gram of the water
    [ ] There are 0.9 moles of NaCl dissolved in every liter of the solution
    Explanation: A 0.9% NaCl solution means 0.9 grams of NaCl per 100 grams (or approximately 100 mL) of solution. This is the standard concentration for normal saline used in IVs.

  Q28. A student boils a sugar solution on a hot plate until half the water evaporates. What happens to the sugar concentration?
    [ ] The concentration stays the same because the sugar amount is unchanged
    [*] The concentration approximately doubles because half the solvent was removed
    [ ] The concentration decreases because heat destroys the sugar molecules
    [ ] The concentration drops to zero because all the sugar evaporated away
    Explanation: When half the water evaporates, the amount of sugar stays the same but the volume of solution is halved. Since concentration = solute/solution, the concentration approximately doubles.

  Q29. A student has two bottles: one labeled '1 M HCl' and another labeled '6 M HCl.' The student needs a dilute acid for the experiment. Which should be chosen?
    [ ] 6 M HCl because a higher number means it is more dilute
    [*] 1 M HCl because a lower molarity means it is more dilute
    [ ] Either one because both concentrations are exactly the same
    [ ] Neither one because HCl cannot be used in a dilute form
    Explanation: 1 M HCl is more dilute than 6 M HCl because it has fewer moles of solute per liter. Lower molarity means lower concentration.

  Q30. A student prepares a solution of 25 g of KNO3 in 75 g of water. She needs to report the mass percent. What value should she report?
    [ ] 33% because 25 divided by 75 equals about 0.33
    [*] 25% because 25 divided by 100 equals 0.25 times 100
    [ ] 75% because the solvent makes up most of the solution
    [ ] 50% because the solute and solvent are roughly equal amounts
    Explanation: Mass percent = (25 g / (25 g + 75 g)) x 100 = (25/100) x 100 = 25%. Total solution mass is 100 g.

--- Lesson 9.3: Dilution (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Dilution
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of dilution?
Dilution is the process of reducing the concentration of a solution by adding more solvent. The total amount of solute remains constant.
What is a dilution factor?
The dilution factor is the ratio of the final volume to the initial volume (V2/V1). A dilution factor of 10 means the solution was diluted to 10 times its original volume.
What is a 'stock solution' in the context of dilution?
A stock solution is a concentrated solution from which more dilute solutions can be prepared by adding solvent. Labs keep stock solutions to efficiently make solutions at various concentrations.
What is the meaning of 'volumetric flask' in dilution procedures?
A volumetric flask is precisely calibrated laboratory glassware designed to contain an exact volume of liquid at a specific temperature, commonly used for preparing solutions of known concentration.
A scientist performs a 1:10 serial dilution three times starting with a 1 M solution. What is the final concentration?
Each 1:10 dilution divides the concentration by 10. Starting at 1 M: after step 1 = 0.1 M, step 2 = 0.01 M, step 3 = 0.001 M.
A student mixes 25 mL of 8 M acid with enough water to make 200 mL total. What is the concentration of the diluted acid?
M1V1 = M2V2: (8 M)(25 mL) = M2(200 mL). M2 = 200/200 = 1 M.

--- Lesson 9.4: Molarity (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Molarity
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of a mole in chemistry?
A mole is a counting unit representing 6.022 x 10^23 particles (Avogadro's number). It connects the atomic scale to measurable amounts.
What is molar mass?
Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in grams per mole (g/mol). For NaCl, the molar mass is about 58.44 g/mol.
What is meant by 'moles of solute' in the molarity formula?
Moles of solute (n) is the amount of dissolved substance measured in moles. To find moles: n = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol).
What is Avogadro's number?
Avogadro's number (6.022 x 10^23) is the number of particles (atoms, molecules, ions) in exactly one mole of any substance.
What is the relationship between molarity and the number of particles in solution?
Higher molarity means more moles of solute per liter, and since each mole contains 6.022 x 10^23 particles, higher molarity means more particles per liter.
A student dissolves 40 g of NaOH (molar mass 40 g/mol) in enough water to make 500 mL of solution. What is the molarity?
Moles = 40 g / 40 g/mol = 1 mol. Volume = 500 mL = 0.5 L. M = 1 mol / 0.5 L = 2 M.
A student has 1 L of 3 M HCl. She pours out 500 mL. What is the molarity of the remaining solution?
Removing part of a solution does not change its concentration. Every portion of a homogeneous solution has the same molarity. The remaining 500 mL is still 3 M.
A student weighs out 98 g of H2SO4 (molar mass 98 g/mol) and dissolves it to make 2 L of solution. What is the molarity?
Moles = 98 g / 98 g/mol = 1 mol. M = 1 mol / 2 L = 0.5 M.
A student notices two bottles: one labeled '1 M NaCl' and another '1 M CaCl2'. She asks if they contain the same number of dissolved particles. What is the answer?
NaCl dissociates into 2 ions (Na+ and Cl-), while CaCl2 dissociates into 3 ions (Ca2+ and 2 Cl-). At 1 M, CaCl2 produces 50% more particles per liter.
A student accidentally makes 1.5 L of solution instead of the intended 1 L when dissolving 2 moles of solute. What is the actual molarity?
M = 2 mol / 1.5 L = 1.33 M. The larger-than-intended volume resulted in a lower molarity than the planned 2 M.
A student needs to convert 500 mL to liters for a molarity calculation. What is 500 mL in liters?
To convert mL to L, divide by 1000. 500 mL / 1000 = 0.5 L. This conversion is essential for molarity calculations.

--- Lesson 9.5: Solution Types (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solution Types
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a saturated solution?
A saturated solution contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at a specific temperature. Any additional solute will remain undissolved.
What is an unsaturated solution?
An unsaturated solution has less dissolved solute than the maximum possible. More solute can be added and it will dissolve.
What is a supersaturated solution?
A supersaturated solution is an unstable state where more solute is dissolved than should be possible at that temperature. Any disturbance can cause crystallization.
What is a suspension in chemistry?
A suspension is a heterogeneous mixture containing large particles (over 1000 nm) that are visible and will settle out of the mixture over time due to gravity.
What is the Tyndall effect?
The Tyndall effect is the scattering of a light beam by the particles in a colloid, making the beam visible. It is used to distinguish colloids from true solutions.
What is an emulsion?
An emulsion is a type of colloid where tiny droplets of one liquid are dispersed in another immiscible liquid, such as oil droplets dispersed in water (like mayonnaise).
What is a 'precipitate' in solution chemistry?
A precipitate is an insoluble solid that forms from a reaction in solution and separates out. It can also form when a supersaturated solution crystallizes.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of a colloid?
Milk is a colloid where tiny fat droplets are dispersed in water. The particles are small enough not to settle but large enough to scatter light, which is why milk appears white.

--- Lesson 9.6: Factors Affecting Solubility (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Factors Affecting Solubility
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the 'rate of dissolving'?
The rate of dissolving is how fast a solute dissolves. It is affected by temperature, stirring, and surface area, but these factors do not change the total solubility.
What is the difference between 'solubility' and 'rate of dissolving'?
Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that CAN dissolve (a capacity), while rate of dissolving is how quickly it dissolves (a speed). They are different concepts.
What is 'hydration' in the context of dissolving ionic compounds?
Hydration occurs when water molecules surround and stabilize individual ions after an ionic compound dissolves. The partially charged water molecules attract the positive and negative ions.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student learns that carbonated beverages are bottled under high pressure. Using Henry's Law, why is this done?
According to Henry's Law, increased pressure increases gas solubility. Bottling under high CO2 pressure forces more carbon dioxide to dissolve, creating the fizzy carbonation.

--- Lesson 9.6: Factors Affecting Solubility (Quiz) ---

  Q1. For most solid solutes, increasing temperature:
    [ ] Makes them completely insoluble in the solvent
    [ ] Decreases their solubility in the solvent
    [ ] Has no effect on their solubility at all
    [*] Increases their solubility in the solvent
    Explanation: For most solid solutes, increasing temperature increases solubility because higher kinetic energy helps solvent molecules break apart the solute structure more effectively.

  Q2. For gas solutes, increasing temperature:
    [*] Decreases their solubility in the liquid
    [ ] Has no effect on their solubility at all
    [ ] Makes them much more soluble in the liquid
    [ ] Increases their solubility significantly overall
    Explanation: For gases, increasing temperature decreases solubility because gas molecules gain kinetic energy and escape from the liquid more easily.

    [ ] All types of solutes equally at all times
    [ ] Only solid solutes dissolved in liquid solvents
    [ ] Only liquid solutes dissolved in liquid solvents

    Explanation: Pressure significantly affects gas solubility (Henry's Law) but has very little effect on the solubility of solids or liquids.

    [ ] Any solvent can dissolve any type of solute
    [ ] Nonpolar solvents dissolve polar solutes easily
    [*] Polar solvents dissolve polar solutes effectively
    [ ] Oil easily dissolves in water without any effort
    Explanation: Like dissolves like means polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes, and nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes. Substances with similar polarity mix well.

    [ ] Sugar is a gas that mixes with any liquid
    [ ] Both sugar and water are nonpolar molecules
    [ ] Water is a nonpolar universal solvent liquid

    [*] Directly proportional to the pressure above the liquid
    [ ] Inversely proportional to the pressure above the liquid
    [ ] Completely unrelated to the pressure above the liquid

    Explanation: Henry's Law states that the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the pressure of that gas above the liquid. Higher pressure forces more gas into solution.

    [*] Increases the rate of dissolving by increasing surface area
    [ ] Decreases the rate of dissolving significantly over time
    [ ] Changes the chemical formula of the solute to a new one
    Explanation: Crushing increases surface area, allowing more solvent molecules to contact the solute at once, which speeds up dissolving. It does not change the total amount that can dissolve.

    [ ] Large crystals in hot water without any stirring

    [ ] Powdered solute in cold water without any stirring

    [ ] All substances dissolve equally in every type of solvent available
    [ ] Only identical substances can dissolve in each other effectively
    [ ] Solubility depends only on temperature and not on polarity at all
    Explanation: Like dissolves like means substances with similar polarity characteristics dissolve well in each other. Polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.

    Explanation: The rate of dissolving is how fast a solute dissolves. It is affected by temperature, stirring, and surface area, but these factors do not change the total solubility.

  Q17. What is the difference between 'solubility' and 'rate of dissolving'?

    [ ] Rate is the maximum amount; solubility is how fast the solute dissolves
    [ ] Neither term has a precise definition in chemistry at this level
    Explanation: Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that CAN dissolve (a capacity), while rate of dissolving is how quickly it dissolves (a speed). They are different concepts.

    [ ] The process where water freezes around dissolved particles in the solution

--- Lesson 9.7: Colligative Properties (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Colligative Properties
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a colligative property?
A colligative property is a physical property of a solution that depends on the concentration (number) of solute particles, regardless of what the solute is.
What is boiling point elevation?
Boiling point elevation is the increase in the boiling point of a solvent when a non-volatile solute is dissolved in it. More solute particles cause a greater elevation.
What is freezing point depression?
Freezing point depression is the lowering of the freezing point of a solvent when a solute is added. The solute particles disrupt crystal formation.
What is osmosis?
Osmosis is the movement of solvent molecules through a semipermeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration.
What is vapor pressure?
Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by the gas (vapor) of a substance in equilibrium with its liquid phase. Adding solute reduces vapor pressure.
What is a 'semipermeable membrane'?
A semipermeable membrane selectively allows some substances (typically small solvent molecules) to pass through while blocking larger solute particles.
What is the 'van't Hoff factor' (i)?
The van't Hoff factor (i) represents the number of particles a solute produces in solution. For NaCl, i = 2 (Na+ and Cl-). For glucose, i = 1 (does not dissociate).
What is Raoult's Law?
Raoult's Law states that the vapor pressure of a solution is proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent. Adding solute reduces the vapor pressure proportionally.

--- Lesson 9.8: Solubility Curves (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Solubility Curves
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a solubility curve?
A solubility curve is a graphical representation showing the maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a solvent at different temperatures.
What is 'recrystallization' and how does it use solubility curves?
Recrystallization uses the difference in solubility at different temperatures. A substance is dissolved in hot solvent (where solubility is high) then cooled, causing pure crystals to form.

    Explanation: The saturation point is the exact solubility value shown on the curve at a particular temperature. It represents the boundary between unsaturated and supersaturated regions.

  Q21. A student looks at a solubility curve and sees that KNO3 has a solubility of 30 g/100 mL at 20 degrees C. She dissolves 25 g in 100 mL at 20 degrees C. What type of solution does she have?
    [ ] A saturated solution because she is close to the maximum
    [*] An unsaturated solution because 25 g is less than the 30 g maximum
    [ ] A supersaturated solution because she dissolved a large amount
    [ ] A suspension because the KNO3 will settle out of the water
    Explanation: Since 25 g is less than the maximum solubility of 30 g/100 mL at 20 degrees C, the solution is unsaturated. She could dissolve 5 more grams before reaching saturation.

  Q22. A student reads that NaCl has a solubility of 36 g/100 mL at 20 degrees C and 39 g/100 mL at 100 degrees C. What can the student conclude?
    [ ] NaCl solubility changes dramatically with temperature changes
    [*] NaCl solubility changes very little with temperature (nearly flat curve)
    [ ] NaCl is insoluble at temperatures below 100 degrees Celsius
    [ ] NaCl is a gas that becomes less soluble at higher temperatures
    Explanation: NaCl only goes from 36 to 39 g/100 mL over an 80-degree temperature range, indicating its solubility curve is nearly flat. Temperature has little effect on NaCl solubility.

  Q23. A student heats 100 mL of water to 80 degrees C and dissolves 160 g of KNO3 (solubility at 80 degrees C is 170 g). She cools the solution to 20 degrees C (solubility is 30 g). How much KNO3 crystallizes out?
    [ ] 30 grams of KNO3 crystallizes from the cooled solution
    [*] 130 grams of KNO3 crystallizes from the cooled solution
    [ ] 160 grams of KNO3 crystallizes from the cooled solution
    [ ] 170 grams of KNO3 crystallizes from the cooled solution
    Explanation: At 20 degrees C, only 30 g can stay dissolved. She started with 160 g dissolved, so 160 - 30 = 130 g crystallizes out when cooled.

  Q24. A student uses a solubility curve to find that substance X is more soluble than substance Y at 60 degrees C, but less soluble at 20 degrees C. What does this mean about their curves?
    [ ] The curves never cross and X is always above Y on the graph
    [*] The curves intersect at some temperature between 20 and 60 degrees C
    [ ] Both substances have identical flat solubility curves on the graph
    [ ] Substance X is a gas and substance Y is a solid on the graph
    Explanation: If X is more soluble than Y at 60 degrees C but less soluble at 20 degrees C, their curves must cross at some temperature between 20 and 60 degrees C, where both have equal solubility.

  Q25. A student wants to purify a sample of KNO3 that contains a small amount of NaCl impurity. Using solubility curves, how could this be done?
    [ ] Cool the mixture because NaCl is more sensitive to temperature changes
    [*] Dissolve in hot water then cool; KNO3 crystallizes out while NaCl stays dissolved
    [ ] Heat the mixture until both substances evaporate from the solution
    [ ] Add more NaCl to the mixture until the KNO3 precipitates out of solution
    Explanation: KNO3 has a steep solubility curve while NaCl is nearly flat. Dissolving both in hot water then cooling causes KNO3 to crystallize out (pure), while the small amount of NaCl remains dissolved.

  Q26. A student plots a solubility curve for a substance and notices the curve goes downward as temperature increases. What type of substance is this most likely?
    [ ] A typical solid salt like KNO3 or sugar dissolved in water
    [*] A gas like CO2 or O2 dissolved in water at various temperatures
    [ ] An ionic compound like NaCl that has a flat solubility curve
    [ ] A substance that is insoluble in water at all temperatures shown
    Explanation: A downward-sloping solubility curve is characteristic of gases, whose solubility in water decreases as temperature increases. Gases escape from warmer liquids more readily.

  Q27. A student looks at a solubility curve and determines that 50 g of substance Z dissolves in 100 mL of water at 40 degrees C. She has exactly 50 g dissolved in 100 mL at 40 degrees C. What happens if she adds 10 more grams?
    [ ] All 10 grams dissolve because she has an unsaturated solution
    [*] The extra 10 grams will not dissolve and will remain as solid at the bottom
    [ ] The solution becomes supersaturated and holds all 60 grams dissolved
    [ ] The temperature of the solution increases to dissolve the extra 10 grams
    Explanation: At 50 g/100 mL, the solution is already saturated. The additional 10 g cannot dissolve and will sit at the bottom of the container as undissolved solid.

  Q28. A student has a saturated solution of KCl at 60 degrees C. She wants to make it unsaturated without adding more water. What should she do?
    [ ] Cool the solution down to a lower temperature level
    [*] Heat the solution to a higher temperature where more can dissolve
    [ ] Add more KCl to the saturated solution until it dissolves completely
    [ ] Filter the solution to remove all of the dissolved solute present
    Explanation: Increasing the temperature increases the solubility of KCl (its curve slopes upward). At the higher temperature, the same amount of KCl is now below the maximum, making it unsaturated.

  Q29. A student compares the solubility curves of KNO3 and Ce2(SO4)3. KNO3 slopes steeply upward while Ce2(SO4)3 slopes downward. At what temperature region is Ce2(SO4)3 most soluble?
    [ ] At the highest temperatures shown on the solubility curve graph
    [*] At the lowest temperatures shown on the solubility curve graph
    [ ] At the middle temperature shown on the solubility curve graph
    [ ] Ce2(SO4)3 has the same solubility at every temperature shown
    Explanation: Since Ce2(SO4)3 has a downward-sloping curve, it is most soluble at low temperatures and becomes less soluble as temperature increases. This is an example of inverse solubility.

  Q30. A student needs to dissolve the maximum amount of sugar possible. The solubility curve shows sugar solubility is 200 g/100 mL at 20 degrees C and 490 g/100 mL at 100 degrees C. How much more dissolves at 100 degrees C?
    [ ] 100 grams more sugar dissolves at the higher temperature
    [*] 290 grams more sugar dissolves at the higher temperature
    [ ] 490 grams more sugar dissolves at the higher temperature
    [ ] 690 grams more sugar dissolves at the higher temperature
    Explanation: At 100 degrees C, 490 g dissolves. At 20 degrees C, 200 g dissolves. The difference is 490 - 200 = 290 g more sugar per 100 mL of water.
