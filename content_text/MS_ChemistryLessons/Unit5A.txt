--- Lesson 5A.1: Introduction to Nomenclature (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Introduction to Nomenclature
Core Concepts
  • Chemical Nomenclature: The systematic method of naming chemical compounds using IUPAC rules.  • Ionic Compounds: Formed between metals and nonmetals; name the cation first, then the anion with -ide suffix.  • Covalent Compounds: Formed between nonmetals; use Greek prefixes to indicate atom counts.  • Binary Compounds: Contain exactly two different elements.  • Stock System: Uses Roman numerals to indicate transition metal charges.

--- Lesson 5A.1: Introduction to Nomenclature (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is chemical nomenclature?
    [*] The system of naming chemical compounds
    [ ] The process of balancing equations
    [ ] A method of measuring atomic mass
    [ ] The study of electron configurations
    Explanation: Chemical nomenclature is the systematic method of naming chemical compounds according to IUPAC rules.

  Q2. Which organization sets the standard rules for naming chemical compounds?
    [*] IUPAC
    [ ] NASA
    [ ] WHO
    [ ] ACS
    Explanation: IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) sets the standard rules for chemical nomenclature.

  Q3. What are the two main categories of chemical compounds?
    [*] Ionic and covalent
    [ ] Organic and metallic
    [ ] Acidic and basic
    [ ] Simple and complex
    Explanation: Chemical compounds are broadly categorized as ionic (metal + nonmetal) or covalent (nonmetal + nonmetal).

  Q4. In a chemical formula, what does a subscript number indicate?
    [*] The number of atoms of that element
    [ ] The charge of the ion
    [ ] The oxidation state
    [ ] The atomic number
    Explanation: A subscript in a chemical formula shows how many atoms of that element are present in one formula unit.

  Q5. What suffix is typically used for naming simple anions?
    [*] -ide
    [ ] -ate
    [ ] -ite
    [ ] -ous
    Explanation: Simple anions (single-element negative ions) are named by changing the element ending to -ide (e.g., chlorine → chloride).

  Q6. What is the correct name for NaCl?
    [*] Sodium chloride
    [ ] Sodium chlorine
    [ ] Sodium chlorate
    [ ] Sodide chlorine
    Explanation: NaCl is sodium chloride: the cation (Na⁺) is named first, then the anion (Cl⁻) with the -ide suffix.

  Q7. Which type of compound is formed between a metal and a nonmetal?
    [*] Ionic compound
    [ ] Covalent compound
    [ ] Organic compound
    [ ] Metallic compound
    Explanation: Ionic compounds are formed when a metal transfers electrons to a nonmetal, creating cations and anions held together by electrostatic forces.

  Q8. What is the correct formula for calcium oxide?
    [*] CaO
    [ ] Ca₂O
    [ ] CaO₂
    [ ] Ca₂O₂
    Explanation: Calcium (Ca²⁺) and oxide (O²⁻) combine in a 1:1 ratio since their charges balance, giving CaO.

  Q9. When naming ionic compounds, which ion is named first?
    [*] The cation (positive ion)
    [ ] The anion (negative ion)
    [ ] The larger ion
    [ ] The ion with the higher charge
    Explanation: In ionic compound nomenclature, the cation (metal/positive ion) is always named first, followed by the anion.

  Q10. What distinguishes a molecular compound from an ionic compound?
    [*] Molecular compounds are formed between nonmetals sharing electrons
    [ ] Molecular compounds always contain metals
    [ ] Molecular compounds have higher melting points
    [ ] Molecular compounds conduct electricity in solution
    Explanation: Molecular (covalent) compounds form between nonmetals that share electrons, unlike ionic compounds where electrons are transferred.

  Q11. What is the stock system in nomenclature used for?
    [*] Indicating the charge of transition metal cations with Roman numerals
    [ ] Counting the total number of atoms
    [ ] Measuring the molecular weight
    [ ] Classifying elements by group
    Explanation: The stock system uses Roman numerals in parentheses after the metal name to indicate its charge, e.g., iron(III) chloride for FeCl₃.

  Q12. What does the prefix 'di-' mean in chemical nomenclature?
    [*] Two
    [ ] One
    [ ] Three
    [ ] Four
    Explanation: The prefix 'di-' means two. It is used in naming covalent compounds to indicate two atoms of an element (e.g., CO₂ is carbon dioxide).

  Q13. Which of the following is a binary compound?
    [*] NaCl
    [ ] NaOH
    [ ] H₂SO₄
    [ ] Ca(NO₃)₂
    Explanation: A binary compound contains exactly two different elements. NaCl (sodium and chlorine) is binary, while the others contain three or more elements.

  Q14. In the name 'iron(II) oxide', what does (II) represent?
    [*] The charge on the iron ion is 2+
    [ ] There are two iron atoms
    [ ] Iron is in group 2
    [ ] The compound has two bonds
    Explanation: The Roman numeral (II) indicates the charge of the iron cation (Fe²⁺), distinguishing it from iron(III) compounds.

  Q15. What is the formula for aluminum oxide?
    [*] Al₂O₃
    [ ] AlO
    [ ] Al₃O₂
    [ ] AlO₃
    Explanation: Aluminum is Al³⁺ and oxide is O²⁻. Cross-multiplying the charges gives Al₂O₃ (2 aluminum ions and 3 oxide ions).

  Q16. Which of these is NOT a rule of chemical nomenclature?
    [*] Always use the atomic number as a subscript
    [ ] Name the cation first in ionic compounds
    [ ] Use -ide suffix for monatomic anions
    [ ] Use Greek prefixes for covalent compounds
    Explanation: The atomic number is not used as a subscript in formulas. Subscripts indicate the number of atoms, not the atomic number.

  Q17. What type of elements typically form cations?
    [*] Metals
    [ ] Nonmetals
    [ ] Noble gases
    [ ] Halogens
    Explanation: Metals tend to lose electrons and form positively charged cations, while nonmetals tend to gain electrons and form anions.

  Q18. What is the correct name for MgBr₂?
    [*] Magnesium bromide
    [ ] Magnesium dibromide
    [ ] Magnesium(II) bromide
    [ ] Dimagnesium bromide
    Explanation: MgBr₂ is magnesium bromide. Since magnesium only forms Mg²⁺, no Roman numeral is needed. Greek prefixes are not used for ionic compounds.

  Q19. Which prefix means 'one' in covalent compound naming?
    [*] Mono-
    [ ] Uni-
    [ ] Prim-
    [ ] Sin-
    Explanation: The prefix 'mono-' means one. It is typically only used for the second element in a covalent compound name (e.g., carbon monoxide).

  Q20. What determines whether a compound is named using ionic or covalent rules?
    [*] Whether it contains a metal or only nonmetals
    [ ] The total number of atoms
    [ ] The physical state at room temperature
    [ ] The molecular weight
    Explanation: If a compound contains a metal and nonmetal, it is named using ionic rules. If it contains only nonmetals, covalent (molecular) naming rules apply.

--- Lesson 5A.2: Net Charge (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Charge
Core Concepts
  • Net Charge: Ionic compounds must be electrically neutral (total + charge = total - charge).  • Cation Charges: Metals lose electrons; Group 1 → +1, Group 2 → +2, Group 13 → +3.  • Anion Charges: Nonmetals gain electrons; Group 17 → -1, Group 16 → -2, Group 15 → -3.  • Transition Metals: Can form multiple charges due to d electrons.  • Balancing: Adjust subscripts so total positive charge equals total negative charge.

--- Lesson 5A.2: Net Charge (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What must the net charge of an ionic compound be?
    [*] Zero
    [ ] Positive
    [ ] Negative
    [ ] It varies
    Explanation: Ionic compounds are electrically neutral — the total positive charge must equal the total negative charge, making the net charge zero.

  Q2. What is the charge of a sodium ion?
    [*] +1
    [ ] +2
    [ ] -1
    [ ] 0
    Explanation: Sodium (Na) is in Group 1 and loses one electron to form Na⁺ with a +1 charge.

  Q3. What charge does a chloride ion have?
    [*] -1
    [ ] +1
    [ ] -2
    [ ] 0
    Explanation: Chlorine (Cl) is in Group 17 and gains one electron to form Cl⁻ with a -1 charge.

  Q4. How can you determine the charge of a main-group element's ion?
    [*] From its position in the periodic table (group number)
    [ ] From its atomic mass
    [ ] From the number of neutrons
    [ ] From its electron configuration only
    Explanation: Main-group elements form ions with charges predictable from their group: Group 1 → +1, Group 2 → +2, Group 16 → -2, Group 17 → -1.

  Q5. What is the charge on a calcium ion?
    [*] +2
    [ ] +1
    [ ] -2
    [ ] +3
    Explanation: Calcium is in Group 2 and loses two electrons to form Ca²⁺.

  Q6. In MgCl₂, why are there two chloride ions?
    [*] To balance Mg²⁺ charge, two Cl⁻ ions are needed
    [ ] Because chlorine is diatomic
    [ ] Because magnesium has two electron shells
    [ ] Because Cl always comes in pairs
    Explanation: Mg²⁺ has a +2 charge, so two Cl⁻ ions (each -1) are needed to make the compound electrically neutral: +2 + 2(-1) = 0.

  Q7. What is the charge on an oxide ion?
    [*] -2
    [ ] -1
    [ ] +2
    [ ] 0
    Explanation: Oxygen is in Group 16 and gains two electrons to form O²⁻ with a -2 charge.

  Q8. Why do transition metals often form ions with different charges?
    [*] They can lose different numbers of d electrons
    [ ] They have unstable nuclei
    [ ] They are always diamagnetic
    [ ] They have only one valence electron
    Explanation: Transition metals can lose varying numbers of d electrons in addition to s electrons, resulting in multiple possible oxidation states.

  Q9. What is the net charge calculation for Al₂O₃?
    [*] 2(+3) + 3(-2) = 0
    [ ] 2(+2) + 3(-3) = 0
    [ ] 2(+3) + 3(-3) = 0
    [ ] 3(+2) + 2(-3) = 0
    Explanation: In Al₂O₃: 2 aluminum ions (Al³⁺) give +6 total, and 3 oxide ions (O²⁻) give -6 total. Net charge = +6 + (-6) = 0.

  Q10. If an element forms a 3+ cation, how many electrons did it lose?
    [*] 3
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 6
    Explanation: A 3+ cation has lost 3 electrons, giving it 3 more protons than electrons and a net +3 charge.

  Q11. What is the formula for a compound of potassium and sulfur?
    [*] K₂S
    [ ] KS
    [ ] KS₂
    [ ] K₂S₂
    Explanation: Potassium forms K⁺ and sulfur forms S²⁻. Two K⁺ ions are needed to balance one S²⁻: 2(+1) + (-2) = 0, giving K₂S.

  Q12. Which group of elements typically forms 2- anions?
    [*] Group 16 (chalcogens)
    [ ] Group 1 (alkali metals)
    [ ] Group 17 (halogens)
    [ ] Group 18 (noble gases)
    Explanation: Group 16 elements (O, S, Se, Te) gain 2 electrons to achieve a noble gas configuration, forming 2- anions.

  Q13. What is the charge on a nitride ion (N³⁻)?
    [*] -3
    [ ] -2
    [ ] -1
    [ ] +3
    Explanation: Nitrogen is in Group 15 and gains 3 electrons to form N³⁻ with a -3 charge.

  Q14. In the formula Fe₂O₃, what is the charge of each iron ion?
    [*] +3
    [ ] +2
    [ ] +1
    [ ] +6
    Explanation: In Fe₂O₃, total negative charge is 3×(-2)=-6. For the 2 iron ions to balance: 2x=-(-6), so x=+3. Each iron ion is Fe³⁺.

  Q15. What does 'electrically neutral' mean for a compound?
    [*] The total positive charges equal the total negative charges
    [ ] The compound has no ions
    [ ] The compound cannot conduct electricity
    [ ] All atoms have full valence shells
    Explanation: Electrically neutral means the sum of all positive charges equals the sum of all negative charges, so the net charge is zero.

  Q16. What is the formula for lithium fluoride?
    [*] LiF
    [ ] Li₂F
    [ ] LiF₂
    [ ] Li₂F₂
    Explanation: Li⁺ and F⁻ have equal and opposite charges, so they combine 1:1 to give LiF.

  Q17. How many electrons does a sulfide ion (S²⁻) have compared to a neutral sulfur atom?
    [*] 2 more
    [ ] 2 fewer
    [ ] The same number
    [ ] 6 more
    Explanation: S²⁻ gained 2 electrons from a neutral S atom (16 electrons → 18 electrons) to achieve a -2 charge.

  Q18. What is the formula for barium chloride?
    [*] BaCl₂
    [ ] BaCl
    [ ] Ba₂Cl
    [ ] Ba₂Cl₂
    Explanation: Barium forms Ba²⁺ and chlorine forms Cl⁻. Two Cl⁻ ions balance one Ba²⁺: +2 + 2(-1) = 0, giving BaCl₂.

  Q19. Why does aluminum form a 3+ ion?
    [*] It loses 3 valence electrons to achieve a noble gas configuration
    [ ] It gains 3 electrons
    [ ] It has 3 neutrons
    [ ] It is in Period 3
    Explanation: Aluminum (Group 13) has 3 valence electrons. Losing all 3 gives it the electron configuration of neon (noble gas).

  Q20. What is the charge of a phosphide ion?
    [*] -3
    [ ] -2
    [ ] +5
    [ ] +3
    Explanation: Phosphorus is in Group 15 and gains 3 electrons to form P³⁻ (phosphide), achieving an argon electron configuration.
