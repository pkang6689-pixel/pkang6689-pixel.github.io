--- Lesson 5A.1: Introduction to Nomenclature (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Introduction to Nomenclature
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is chemical nomenclature?
Chemical nomenclature is the systematic method of naming chemical compounds according to established rules.
What is the correct name for NaCl?
NaCl is sodium chloride: the cation (Na+) is named first, then the anion (Cl-) with the -ide suffix.
What is the correct formula for calcium oxide?
Calcium (Ca2+) and oxide (O2-) combine in a 1:1 ratio since their charges balance, giving CaO.
What is the stock system in chemical nomenclature?
The stock system uses Roman numerals in parentheses after the metal name to indicate its charge, such as iron(III) chloride for FeCl3.
What is a binary compound?
A binary compound contains exactly two different elements, such as NaCl (sodium and chlorine) or H2O (hydrogen and oxygen).
What is a cation?
A cation is a positively charged ion that forms when an atom (usually a metal) loses one or more electrons.
What is an anion?
An anion is a negatively charged ion that forms when an atom (usually a nonmetal) gains one or more electrons.
What is a chemical formula?
A chemical formula uses element symbols and numerical subscripts to show which elements and how many atoms of each are in a compound.
What is a monatomic ion?
A monatomic ion is formed from a single atom that has gained or lost electrons, such as Na+ or Cl-.
What is the prefix 'di-' in chemical nomenclature?
The prefix 'di-' means two and is used in naming covalent compounds to indicate two atoms of an element, such as CO2 being carbon dioxide.
What is an empirical formula?
An empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of elements in a compound, such as CH2O for glucose instead of C6H12O6.
What is IUPAC nomenclature?
IUPAC nomenclature is the internationally standardized system for naming chemical compounds, ensuring scientists worldwide use consistent names.
What is a polyatomic ion?
A polyatomic ion is a group of two or more atoms covalently bonded together that carries an overall electric charge, such as SO4 2- (sulfate).
A chemist needs to write the formula for a compound of aluminum and fluorine. Aluminum forms Al3+ and fluorine forms F-. What is the correct formula?
Three F- ions are needed to balance one Al3+ ion: (+3) + 3(-1) = 0, giving the formula AlF3 (aluminum fluoride).

--- Lesson 5A.2: Net Charge (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Net Charge
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the charge of a sodium ion?
Sodium is in Group 1 and loses one electron to form Na+ with a +1 charge.
What is the charge on a calcium ion?
Calcium is in Group 2 and loses two electrons to form Ca2+ with a +2 charge.
What is the charge on an oxide ion?
Oxygen is in Group 16 and gains two electrons to form O2- with a -2 charge.
What is the net charge calculation for Al2O3?
In Al2O3, two aluminum ions (Al3+) contribute +6 total, and three oxide ions (O2-) contribute -6 total. The net charge is +6 + (-6) = 0.
What is net charge in the context of ionic compounds?
Net charge is the sum of all positive and negative charges in a formula unit. For a valid ionic compound, this sum must equal zero.
What is a cation?
A cation is a positively charged ion that forms when an atom, usually a metal, loses one or more electrons. Examples include Na+, Ca2+, and Al3+.
What is an anion?
An anion is a negatively charged ion that forms when an atom, usually a nonmetal, gains one or more electrons. Examples include Cl-, O2-, and N3-.
What is electrical neutrality?
Electrical neutrality means the sum of all positive charges equals the sum of all negative charges, resulting in a net charge of zero for the compound.
What is an oxidation state?
An oxidation state is the hypothetical charge an atom would have if all bonds were treated as purely ionic. It helps predict formulas and balance charges in compounds.
What is a multivalent metal?
A multivalent metal can form ions with different positive charges. For example, iron can be Fe2+ or Fe3+, and copper can be Cu+ or Cu2+.
What is a charge balance?
Charge balance is the fundamental principle that the total positive charge from all cations must equal the total negative charge from all anions in an ionic compound.
What is a noble gas configuration?
A noble gas configuration is the stable electron arrangement atoms achieve by gaining or losing electrons. For example, Na+ has the same electron configuration as neon.
What is a formula unit?
A formula unit represents the lowest whole-number ratio of ions in an ionic compound. For example, NaCl is one formula unit containing one Na+ and one Cl-.
What is a monatomic ion?
A monatomic ion is formed from a single atom that has gained or lost electrons, such as Na+, Cl-, Ca2+, or O2-.
A student is given the ions Cu2+ and Cl-. She writes the formula CuCl. Her teacher marks it wrong. What is the correct formula and why?
Cu2+ has a +2 charge and each Cl- has a -1 charge. Two Cl- ions are needed: +2 + 2(-1) = 0. The correct formula is CuCl2.

What is the electron configuration of Na+ after bond formation?
When sodium loses its one valence electron, Na+ has 10 electrons arranged like neon (1s2 2s2 2p6), achieving a stable noble gas configuration.
What is a crystal lattice?
A crystal lattice is the orderly, repeating three-dimensional pattern in which cations and anions are arranged in an ionic solid, maximizing attractive forces between opposite charges.
What is the octet rule?
The octet rule states that atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a stable configuration of eight valence electrons, similar to noble gases.
What is lattice energy?
Lattice energy is the energy released when separated gaseous ions form a solid ionic crystal. Higher lattice energy means a more stable and harder-to-melt compound.
What is an electrolyte?
An electrolyte is a substance that dissociates into ions when dissolved in water, allowing the solution to conduct electricity. Ionic compounds are common electrolytes.
What is electron transfer?
Electron transfer is the process in which one or more electrons move from one atom (typically a metal) to another atom (typically a nonmetal), forming ions and an ionic bond.
What is a coordination number in a crystal lattice?
The coordination number is the count of nearest-neighbor ions surrounding a given ion in a crystal lattice. In NaCl, each Na+ is surrounded by 6 Cl- ions, giving a coordination number of 6.
What is ionization energy?
Ionization energy is the minimum energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom. Metals have low ionization energies, making it easier for them to form cations.
What is electron affinity?
Electron affinity is the energy change that occurs when a gaseous atom gains an electron. Nonmetals like halogens have large negative electron affinities, meaning they readily accept electrons.
What is dissociation in the context of ionic compounds?
Dissociation is the process by which an ionic compound separates into its constituent cations and anions when dissolved in water or melted, allowing ion movement.
What is electronegativity?
Electronegativity measures how strongly an atom attracts electrons in a bond. A large electronegativity difference between two atoms (greater than 1.7) typically indicates ionic bonding.
A student drops an ionic crystal on a hard surface and it shatters into pieces. Her lab partner asks why it broke instead of bending. What is the correct explanation?
When force shifts a layer of ions, cations end up next to cations and anions next to anions. The resulting repulsion between like charges causes the crystal to crack and shatter.
Outliers and Resistant Measures
MgO has Mg2+ and O2- (charges of 2+/2-) compared to NaF's Na+ and F- (charges of 1+/1-). Higher ion charges and smaller sizes produce much greater lattice energy and higher melting points.
Real-World Applications
Example: A student examines a white solid and finds it dissolves in water, conducts electricity in solution, and has a high melting point. She concludes it is ionic. Which observation is most important for identifying ionic bonding?
Electrical conductivity in solution directly indicates the presence of free ions, which is the hallmark of ionic compounds. Many non-ionic substances also dissolve in water or are white solids.

--- Lesson 5A.4: Ionic Bonds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Ionic Bonds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an ionic bond?
An ionic bond is the electrostatic attraction between a positively charged cation and a negatively charged anion, formed after electron transfer from a metal to a nonmetal.
What is the typical state of ionic compounds at room temperature?
Ionic compounds are typically crystalline solids at room temperature because the strong electrostatic forces in the crystal lattice keep ions in fixed positions.
What is Coulomb's law as it relates to ionic bonds?
Coulomb's law describes the electrostatic force between charged particles: higher charges and shorter distances produce stronger attraction, which directly explains ionic bond strength.
What is a monovalent ion?
A monovalent ion carries a single unit of charge, either +1 or -1. Examples include Na+ and Cl-.
What is a divalent ion?
A divalent ion carries a charge of +2 or -2. Examples include Mg2+, Ca2+, O2-, and S2-.
What is lattice energy?
Lattice energy is the energy released when gaseous ions come together to form a solid ionic compound. Greater lattice energy means a more stable compound with higher melting points.
What is hydration in the context of ionic compounds?
Hydration is the process by which polar water molecules surround dissolved ions, stabilizing them in solution. The oxygen end of water attracts cations, and the hydrogen end attracts anions.
What is ionic radius?
Ionic radius is the effective size of an ion in a crystal. Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (lost electrons), while anions are larger (gained electrons).
What is an isoelectronic series?
An isoelectronic series is a set of ions and atoms with the same electron count. For example, O2-, F-, Ne, Na+, and Mg2+ all have 10 electrons.
What is a crystalline solid?
A crystalline solid has particles arranged in a highly ordered, repeating three-dimensional pattern. Ionic compounds typically form crystalline solids with a defined lattice structure.
What is electrostatic attraction?
Electrostatic attraction is the force that draws opposite electric charges together. In ionic bonds, it is the attraction between positive cations and negative anions.
What is a binary ionic compound?
A binary ionic compound contains exactly two different elements, one metal and one nonmetal. Examples include NaCl (sodium chloride) and CaO (calcium oxide).
Real-World Applications
Example: A jeweler is examining a mineral and finds it is very hard, has a high melting point, and forms geometric crystals. She suspects it is an ionic compound. What additional test could confirm this?
Dissolving the mineral and testing conductivity would confirm ionic character. If the solution conducts electricity, free ions are present, confirming ionic bonding.

--- Lesson 5A.5: Crisscross Method (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Crisscross Method
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
Using the crisscross method, what is the formula for aluminum chloride (Al3+ and Cl-)?
Crisscrossing Al3+ and Cl-: the 1 from chlorine becomes aluminum's subscript (Al1 = Al) and the 3 from aluminum becomes chlorine's subscript, giving AlCl3.
What is the formula for iron(III) oxide using the crisscross method?
Iron(III) is Fe3+ and oxide is O2-. Crisscrossing: the 2 goes to iron and the 3 goes to oxygen, yielding Fe2O3.
What is the formula for tin(IV) chloride using the crisscross method?
Tin(IV) is Sn4+ and chloride is Cl-. Crisscrossing: the 1 from Cl goes to Sn and the 4 from Sn goes to Cl, giving SnCl4.
What is the crisscross method?
The crisscross method is a shortcut for writing ionic compound formulas. You take the magnitude of each ion's charge and use it as the subscript of the other ion, then reduce to the lowest whole-number ratio.
What is a subscript in a chemical formula?
A subscript is the small number written to the lower right of an element symbol that tells how many atoms of that element are in one formula unit. For example, in H2O the 2 is a subscript showing there are 2 hydrogen atoms.
What is a formula unit?
A formula unit is the simplest ratio of cations to anions in an ionic compound. For NaCl, the formula unit is one Na+ and one Cl-. For CaF2, it is one Ca2+ and two F-.
What is electrical neutrality in the context of ionic formulas?
Electrical neutrality means that the sum of all positive charges in a formula must exactly balance the sum of all negative charges, giving the compound a net charge of zero. The crisscross method automatically achieves this.
What is the lowest whole-number ratio?
After crisscrossing, if both subscripts share a common factor, you divide each by their greatest common factor to get the simplest ratio. For example, Pb2O2 reduces to PbO (dividing both by 2).
What is a monatomic ion?
A monatomic ion consists of a single atom that has lost or gained electrons, giving it a positive or negative charge. Na+, Cl-, Ca2+, and O2- are all monatomic ions used in the crisscross method.
What is a polyatomic ion?
A polyatomic ion is a group of two or more covalently bonded atoms that together carry a net charge. Examples include SO4 2-, NO3-, and PO4 3-. The crisscross method treats each polyatomic ion as a single unit.
What is a coefficient in a chemical formula?
A coefficient is a number placed in front of a chemical formula (like the 2 in 2NaCl) that indicates how many formula units are present. It is different from a subscript, which appears inside the formula to show atom ratios.
What is the charge magnitude?
Charge magnitude is the numerical value of an ion's charge without regard to sign. In the crisscross method, you use only the magnitude (e.g., 2 from Ca2+, 3 from N3-) as subscripts.
What is a greatest common factor (GCF) as used in formula simplification?
The greatest common factor is the largest whole number that divides evenly into both subscripts. When simplifying crisscross results like Ti2O4, the GCF of 2 and 4 is 2, reducing the formula to TiO2.
A student crisscrosses Fe2+ and S2- and writes Fe2S2. The teacher asks the student to fix the answer. What is the correct formula?
Fe2S2 has subscripts sharing a common factor of 2. Dividing both by 2 gives FeS (iron(II) sulfide). When both ions have the same charge magnitude, the formula is always a 1:1 ratio.
In a lab report, a student writes the formula for magnesium hydroxide as MgOH2. The teacher corrects it. What is the proper formula and why?
Mg2+ and OH-: crisscrossing gives Mg1(OH)2 = Mg(OH)2. The parentheses around OH are essential because hydroxide is a polyatomic ion. Without them, the subscript 2 would incorrectly apply only to H.

--- Lesson 5A.6: Naming Ionic Compounds (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Ionic Compounds
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the correct name for NaCl?
NaCl is sodium chloride. Name the metal first (sodium), then change the nonmetal ending to -ide (chlorine becomes chloride). No Roman numeral is needed because sodium only forms +1 ions.
What is the correct name for CaBr2?
CaBr2 is calcium bromide. Calcium is a Group 2 metal that only forms Ca2+, so no Roman numeral or prefix is needed. The nonmetal bromine gets the -ide suffix.
What is the correct name for CuCl2?
Copper is a transition metal with multiple possible charges. Since each Cl- is -1 and there are two, the total negative charge is -2, meaning Cu must be +2. The Roman numeral (II) is required: copper(II) chloride.
What is the name for FeO?
In FeO, oxygen is O2- (charge -2). For the compound to be neutral, iron must be Fe2+. Since iron can form multiple charges, the Roman numeral (II) is required: iron(II) oxide.
What is the name for Al2S3?
Al2S3 is aluminum sulfide. Aluminum only forms Al3+ so no Roman numeral is needed. Greek prefixes (di-, tri-) are not used for ionic compounds because the ratio is determined by the charges.
What is the name for ZnO?
Zinc practically only forms Zn2+, so no Roman numeral is needed. The name is simply zinc oxide. Zinc, silver, and aluminum are transition or post-transition metals that have only one common charge.
What is the name for MnO2?
Two O2- ions contribute -4 total charge, so manganese must be +4. Since manganese has multiple possible charges, the Roman numeral is required: manganese(IV) oxide.
What is the Stock system of naming ionic compounds?
The Stock system, preferred by IUPAC, places a Roman numeral in parentheses after the metal name to specify its charge. For example, Fe2+ compounds use iron(II) and Fe3+ compounds use iron(III).
What is a binary compound?
A binary compound contains exactly two different elements. NaCl (sodium and chlorine), Al2O3 (aluminum and oxygen), and CaF2 (calcium and fluorine) are all binary compounds.
What is a multivalent metal?
A multivalent (or variable-charge) metal can form ions with different charges. Iron can be Fe2+ or Fe3+, copper can be Cu+ or Cu2+, and lead can be Pb2+ or Pb4+. These metals require Roman numerals in their compound names.
What is a monovalent ion?
A monovalent ion forms only one possible charge. Na is always +1, Ca is always +2, and Al is always +3. Since their charges are predictable, Roman numerals are unnecessary when naming their compounds.
What is an anion?
An anion is a negatively charged ion. In ionic compound naming, the anion is named second and receives the -ide suffix (for monatomic anions). Examples: Cl- is chloride, O2- is oxide, S2- is sulfide.
What is a cation?
A cation is a positively charged ion. In naming ionic compounds, the cation (usually a metal) is always named first. For example, in sodium chloride, Na+ is the cation and is named first.
What is IUPAC nomenclature?
IUPAC nomenclature provides a universal, standardized system for naming compounds so that chemists worldwide use the same names. For ionic compounds, IUPAC recommends the Stock system with Roman numerals for multivalent metals.
What is the -ide suffix?
The -ide suffix replaces the ending of a nonmetal's name when it forms a monatomic anion: chlorine becomes chloride, oxygen becomes oxide, nitrogen becomes nitride, and fluorine becomes fluoride.
What is a Roman numeral in ionic compound naming?
Roman numerals (I, II, III, IV, etc.) are placed in parentheses after a multivalent metal's name to specify its charge. Iron(II) means Fe2+ and iron(III) means Fe3+. They are only needed when the metal can have more than one charge.
What is charge back-calculation?
When naming a compound like FeO, you back-calculate: O is -2, and the formula has one Fe and one O. For neutrality, Fe must be +2. So the name is iron(II) oxide. This technique is essential for naming transition metal compounds.
A student sees the formula Fe2O3 on a test and writes 'iron oxide' as the name. The teacher marks it partially incorrect. What is the complete correct name and why?
Iron can be Fe2+ or Fe3+, making it multivalent. In Fe2O3, total oxygen charge is 3 x (-2) = -6, so total iron charge must be +6 across 2 iron atoms, meaning each Fe is +3. The Roman numeral (III) is required.
In a lab, a student discovers that silver chloride (AgCl) does not need a Roman numeral, but iron(II) chloride (FeCl2) does. She asks why. What is the best explanation?
Silver practically only forms +1 ions, so there is no ambiguity and no Roman numeral is needed. Iron, however, commonly forms both Fe2+ and Fe3+, making the Roman numeral essential to distinguish iron(II) from iron(III) compounds.
A student sees Co2O3 and names it 'cobalt oxide.' The teacher asks her to redo the problem. What is the correct name?
Back-calculate: 3 oxygens at -2 each = -6, divided by 2 cobalt atoms = +3 each. Cobalt is multivalent, so the Roman numeral is required: cobalt(III) oxide. 'Cobalt oxide' is incomplete without the charge specification.

--- Lesson 5A.7: Polyatomic Ions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Polyatomic Ions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula and charge of the nitrate ion?
The nitrate ion is NO3- (one nitrogen, three oxygens, with a single negative charge). It is one of the most common polyatomic ions encountered in chemistry.
What is the formula and charge of the sulfate ion?
The sulfate ion is SO4 2- (one sulfur, four oxygens, with a 2- charge). Sulfate (-ate) has one more oxygen than sulfite (-ite), which is SO3 2-.
What is the name for NaNO3?
NaNO3 contains the sodium cation (Na+) and the nitrate polyatomic ion (NO3-). The compound is named sodium nitrate. Nitride (N3-) is a monatomic ion; nitrite (NO2-) has one fewer oxygen than nitrate.
What is the formula for the hydroxide ion?
The hydroxide ion is OH- (one oxygen bonded to one hydrogen, with a single negative charge). It is found in bases such as NaOH (sodium hydroxide) and Ca(OH)2 (calcium hydroxide).
What is the charge of the ammonium ion (NH4)?
Ammonium (NH4+) carries a +1 charge. It is the only common polyatomic cation (positive ion) that students must memorize. All other common polyatomic ions are anions.
What is the formula for calcium carbonate?
Ca2+ and CO3 2- both have a charge magnitude of 2, so they combine in a 1:1 ratio: CaCO3. No parentheses are needed because only one carbonate ion is present.
What is the name for K2SO4?
K2SO4 is potassium sulfate. Potassium is Group 1 (always +1), so no Roman numeral or prefix is needed. Sulfate (SO4 2-) is named as the complete polyatomic ion, not broken into individual elements.
What is the formula for aluminum sulfate?
Al3+ and SO4 2-: crisscrossing gives Al2(SO4)3. Parentheses are required because there are 3 sulfate ions. Check: 2(+3) = +6 and 3(-2) = -6, so the compound is neutral.
What is a polyatomic ion?
A polyatomic ion is a charged species consisting of two or more atoms covalently bonded together. The group acts as a single unit in ionic compounds. Examples include NO3-, SO4 2-, OH-, and NH4+.
What is the carbonate ion?
The carbonate ion (CO3 2-) contains one carbon atom bonded to three oxygen atoms, with a net charge of 2-. It is found in compounds like CaCO3 (calcium carbonate, the main component of limestone).
What is the phosphate ion?
The phosphate ion is PO4 3- (one phosphorus, four oxygens, 3- charge). Its high charge means it often requires multiple cations in formulas, such as Ca3(PO4)2 (calcium phosphate).
What is the permanganate ion?
The permanganate ion is MnO4- (one manganese, four oxygens, 1- charge). It is a strong oxidizing agent recognizable by its deep purple color in solution, commonly used in KMnO4 (potassium permanganate).
What is the acetate ion?
The acetate ion (CH3COO- or equivalently C2H3O2-) is a polyatomic anion derived from acetic acid. It has a 1- charge and is found in compounds like sodium acetate (NaCH3COO).
What is the difference between the -ate and -ite suffixes in polyatomic ion names?
In a related pair, the -ate ion has one more oxygen than the -ite ion, but both carry the same charge. Examples: sulfate (SO4 2-) vs. sulfite (SO3 2-); nitrate (NO3-) vs. nitrite (NO2-).
What is the ammonium ion?
Ammonium (NH4+) is the only common polyatomic cation (positive ion). It behaves like a metal ion in formulas and naming. For example, ammonium chloride is NH4Cl and ammonium sulfate is (NH4)2SO4.
What is the chromate ion?
The chromate ion is CrO4 2- (one chromium, four oxygens, 2- charge). It is yellow in solution. It should not be confused with dichromate (Cr2O7 2-), which is orange and contains two chromium atoms.
What is the bicarbonate ion?
Bicarbonate (also called hydrogen carbonate) is HCO3- with a 1- charge. It is formed when carbonate (CO3 2-) picks up one hydrogen ion. Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) is common baking soda.
What is the cyanide ion?
The cyanide ion is CN- (one carbon triple-bonded to one nitrogen, 1- charge). Despite ending in -ide like monatomic ions, cyanide is polyatomic because it contains two different atoms bonded together.
A student writes the formula for magnesium hydroxide as MgOH2. Her teacher marks it wrong. What is the correct formula and what mistake did the student make?
When a formula requires more than one polyatomic ion, the entire ion must be enclosed in parentheses before the subscript. Writing MgOH2 incorrectly implies only the hydrogen has a subscript of 2, changing the meaning.
A student is comparing sodium sulfate (Na2SO4) and sodium sulfite (Na2SO3). She notices both have Na2 and a sulfur-oxygen anion. What is the key difference between sulfate and sulfite?
Sulfate (-ate) is SO4 2- with four oxygens, while sulfite (-ite) is SO3 2- with three oxygens. Both carry a 2- charge. The -ate form always has one more oxygen than the -ite form in related pairs.
A student needs to write the formula for ammonium sulfate. She uses NH4+ and SO4 2-. She writes NH4SO4. Her lab partner says this is wrong. What is the correct formula?
NH4+ is +1 and SO4 2- is -2. Two ammonium ions are needed: 2(+1) = +2 balances -2. Since we need more than one NH4+, parentheses are required: (NH4)2SO4.
A geology student learns that stalactites and stalagmites are made of calcium carbonate (CaCO3). She wonders why the formula does not need parentheses around CO3. What is the correct explanation?
Ca2+ and CO3 2- both have charge magnitude 2, so they combine in a 1:1 ratio. Parentheses are only needed when the subscript on a polyatomic ion is 2 or more. One carbonate means no parentheses: CaCO3.
A farmer is told that his field needs 'ammonium phosphate' fertilizer. He asks a chemist to write the formula. The chemist combines NH4+ and PO4 3-. What is the correct formula?
Crisscrossing NH4+ (charge 1+) and PO4 3- (charge 3-): the 3 crosses to ammonium giving (NH4)3 and the 1 crosses to phosphate giving PO4. The formula is (NH4)3PO4. Check: 3(+1) + (-3) = 0.

--- Lesson 5A.8: Common Exceptions & Traditional Names (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Common Exceptions & Traditional Names
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the traditional name for Fe2+ compounds?
Ferrous is the traditional name for Fe2+ (the lower charge of iron). The Latin root 'ferrum' gives iron its symbol Fe, and the -ous suffix indicates the lower of two possible charges.
What is the traditional name for Cu2+ compounds?
Cupric is the traditional name for Cu2+ (the higher charge of copper). The Latin root 'cuprum' gives copper its symbol Cu, and the -ic suffix indicates the higher of two possible charges.
What is the traditional name for SnCl2?
In SnCl2, tin is Sn2+ (the lower charge). The Latin root for tin is 'stannum,' so the -ous form is stannous. Stannous chloride is the traditional name for tin(II) chloride.
What is the modern (Stock) name for PbCl4?
In PbCl4, four Cl- ions total -4, so Pb must be +4. The modern Stock system names it lead(IV) chloride using the English name and a Roman numeral. The traditional name would be plumbic chloride.
What is the traditional name for SnO2?
In SnO2, two O2- ions total -4, so Sn = +4 (the higher charge). The Latin root stannum with the -ic suffix gives stannic oxide. The modern Stock name is tin(IV) oxide.
What is the traditional naming system for ionic compounds?
The traditional (or classical) naming system uses Latin-derived metal names (ferrum for iron, cuprum for copper, stannum for tin, plumbum for lead) with -ous for the lower charge and -ic for the higher charge.
What is a Latin root in the context of chemical naming?
Latin roots are the original Latin names of elements used in traditional nomenclature: ferrum (Fe/iron), cuprum (Cu/copper), stannum (Sn/tin), plumbum (Pb/lead), aurum (Au/gold), and argentum (Ag/silver).
What is the Stock system?
The Stock system (named after German chemist Alfred Stock) uses the English name of the metal followed by a Roman numeral in parentheses. For example, Fe3+ compounds are named iron(III), which is clearer than the traditional 'ferric.'
What is the meaning of the term 'ferrous' in chemistry?
Ferrous refers specifically to iron in its +2 state (the lower charge). The term comes from the Latin ferrum (iron) plus the -ous suffix indicating the lower oxidation state. Ferrous sulfate (FeSO4) is a common iron supplement.
What is the meaning of the term 'ferric' in chemistry?
Ferric refers to iron in its +3 state (the higher charge). The Latin root ferrum plus -ic suffix indicates the higher oxidation state. Ferric chloride (FeCl3) is used in water treatment and circuit board etching.
What is meant by 'plumbous' in traditional naming?
Plumbous means lead in its +2 state (lower charge), derived from the Latin plumbum. Plumbous oxide is PbO (lead(II) oxide). The word 'plumber' also derives from plumbum, since lead was historically used in pipes.
What is the diatomic mercury(I) ion?
The mercury(I) ion is Hg2 2+, where two mercury atoms are covalently bonded together and share a total +2 charge (each effectively +1). This is a unique exception that affects formula writing -- mercury(I) chloride is Hg2Cl2, not HgCl.
What is the meaning of 'stannous' in chemistry?
Stannous refers to tin in its +2 state (lower charge). It comes from the Latin stannum (tin) plus -ous. Stannous fluoride (SnF2) is the active ingredient in some toothpastes.
What is the meaning of 'cuprous' in chemistry?
Cuprous means copper in its +1 state (the lower charge), from the Latin cuprum. Cu2O is cuprous oxide (copper(I) oxide) and CuCl is cuprous chloride (copper(I) chloride).
What is the Latin root 'aurum'?
Aurum is the Latin word for gold, giving it the symbol Au. Other Latin roots: argentum (Ag/silver), ferrum (Fe/iron), cuprum (Cu/copper), stannum (Sn/tin), plumbum (Pb/lead).
A student sees FeCl2 and FeCl3 in a lab. She labels them both 'iron chloride.' Her teacher explains why this is problematic. What is the issue?
Iron is multivalent, so 'iron chloride' could mean either compound. FeCl2 (iron(II) chloride / ferrous chloride) and FeCl3 (iron(III) chloride / ferric chloride) have different properties, colors, and uses. Distinguishing them is essential for safety.
During a quiz, a student writes 'mercurous chloride' for HgCl2. The teacher marks it wrong. What is the correct name for HgCl2, and what compound is actually mercurous chloride?
HgCl2 has Hg2+ (the higher charge), making it mercuric chloride or mercury(II) chloride. Mercurous chloride (-ous = lower charge) is Hg2Cl2, using the diatomic Hg2 2+ ion. These are very different compounds with very different toxicities.
A student needs to convert 'ferrous sulfate' to its Stock name for a report on iron supplements. What is the correct conversion?
Ferrous means Fe2+ (lower charge, -ous suffix). The Stock system replaces the Latin root with the English name and states the charge: iron(II) sulfate. The formula is FeSO4.
A student preparing for an exam makes a study chart matching traditional names to Stock names. She writes 'stannic = tin(II).' Her study partner corrects her. What is the right match?
The -ic suffix always means the higher charge. Tin's two common charges are +2 (stannous) and +4 (stannic). So stannic = tin(IV), not tin(II). The student confused -ic (higher) with -ous (lower).

Plumbic (-ic suffix) means the higher charge of lead, which is Pb4+. The Stock name is lead(IV) oxide. With O2-, crisscrossing Pb4+ and O2- gives Pb2O4, which simplifies to PbO2.
