--- Lesson 1.1: States of Matter (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: States of Matter
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the fourth state of matter, commonly found in stars and lightning?
Plasma is an ionized gas made of free-moving electrons and ions. It is created at extremely high temperatures and is the most abundant state of matter in the universe (stars, lightning, neon signs).
What is the definition of 'solid' in chemistry?
A solid is a state of matter where particles are tightly packed in fixed, ordered positions. They vibrate but do not translate freely, giving solids both a definite shape and a definite volume.
What is the definition of 'plasma'?
Plasma is the fourth state of matter, formed when a gas is heated to such high temperatures that atoms become ionized — electrons are stripped away, creating a mix of free electrons and positive ions.
What is 'viscosity'?
Viscosity measures how much a fluid resists flowing. Honey has high viscosity (flows slowly) while water has low viscosity (flows easily). Stronger intermolecular forces lead to higher viscosity.
What is the definition of 'thermal energy'?
Thermal energy is the sum of the kinetic energies of all particles in a substance. It increases with temperature and with the amount of matter in the sample.
What is 'absolute zero'?
Absolute zero (0 Kelvin, −273.15°C) is the theoretical lower limit of temperature. At this point, particles would have minimum possible energy and essentially no thermal motion. It has never been fully reached in practice.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which state of matter has the highest average kinetic energy at a given temperature?
Gas particles move the fastest and most freely, giving them the highest kinetic energy among the three common states. Solids have the lowest kinetic energy because their particles only vibrate in place.
Example: A welder's arc produces temperatures above 10,000°C and a bright glowing stream. What state of matter is most likely present at the tip of the welding arc?
At temperatures above 10,000°C, atoms gain enough energy to ionize — electrons are stripped from their atoms, creating plasma. The bright glow comes from ions and electrons recombining and emitting photons of light.

--- Lesson 1.1: States of Matter (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Which state of matter has a definite shape and a definite volume?
    [ ] Gas
    [*] Solid
    [ ] Plasma
    [ ] Liquid
    Explanation: Solids have particles tightly packed in fixed positions, giving them both a definite shape and a definite volume. Liquids have definite volume but not shape; gases have neither.

  Q2. Which state of matter has no definite shape and no definite volume?
    [*] Gas
    [ ] Solid
    [ ] Liquid
    [ ] Crystal
    Explanation: Gas particles are far apart and move freely, so a gas will expand to fill whatever container it occupies — it has neither a definite shape nor a definite volume.

  Q3. In which state of matter can particles slide past one another while staying close together?
    [*] Liquid
    [ ] Gas
    [ ] Solid
    [ ] Plasma
    Explanation: Liquid particles are close together (giving liquids a definite volume) but can move past each other, which is why liquids flow and take the shape of their container.

  Q4. What is the fourth state of matter, commonly found in stars and lightning?
    [ ] Vapor
    [*] Plasma
    [ ] Gel
    [ ] Condensate
    Explanation: Plasma is an ionized gas made of free-moving electrons and ions. It is created at extremely high temperatures and is the most abundant state of matter in the universe (stars, lightning, neon signs).

  Q5. What happens to particle motion when the temperature of a substance increases?
    [ ] Particles slow down
    [*] Particles move faster
    [ ] Particles stop moving
    [ ] Particle motion stays the same
    Explanation: Temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy. When temperature increases, particles gain energy and move faster and more vigorously.

  Q6. Which state of matter has particles that vibrate in place but do not move freely?
    [ ] Gas
    [*] Solid
    [ ] Plasma
    [ ] Liquid
    Explanation: In a solid, particles are locked in fixed positions and can only vibrate. They do not move freely like liquid or gas particles. This is why solids maintain a definite shape.

  Q7. Which state of matter has the highest average kinetic energy at a given temperature?
    [*] Gas
    [ ] Solid
    [ ] Liquid
    [ ] Equal
    Explanation: Gas particles move the fastest and most freely, giving them the highest kinetic energy among the three common states. Solids have the lowest kinetic energy because their particles only vibrate in place.

  Q8. A gas will expand to fill its container because its particles are:
    [ ] Completely stationary
    [ ] Close together and sliding past each other
    [*] Far apart and moving at high speeds
    [ ] Tightly packed and vibrating
    Explanation: Gas particles are widely spaced and move rapidly in all directions. Because of this, they spread out to occupy the entire volume of any container they are placed in.

  Q9. Which state of matter is the most common in the visible universe?
    [ ] Gas
    [*] Plasma
    [ ] Solid
    [ ] Liquid
    Explanation: Plasma makes up over 99% of the visible universe. Stars, including our Sun, are made of plasma. On Earth, plasma is less common but appears in lightning, neon signs, and fluorescent lights.

  Q10. Liquids have a definite _____ but not a definite _____.
    [ ] Shape; volume
    [ ] Temperature; density
    [ ] Mass; color
    [*] Volume; shape
    Explanation: Liquid particles are close enough together to maintain a fixed volume, but they can slide past each other, so a liquid takes the shape of whatever container it is in.

  Q11. What is the definition of 'solid' in chemistry?
    [ ] Matter that flows freely and has no definite shape
    [ ] Any material that is hard to the touch
    [ ] A substance that always dissolves in water
    [*] Matter with a definite shape and definite volume whose particles vibrate in fixed positions
    Explanation: A solid is a state of matter where particles are tightly packed in fixed, ordered positions. They vibrate but do not translate freely, giving solids both a definite shape and a definite volume.

  Q12. What does 'kinetic energy' mean in the context of states of matter?
    [*] The energy of particle motion — faster particles have more kinetic energy
    [ ] The total mass of all particles in a sample
    [ ] The energy required to break a molecule apart
    [ ] The energy stored in chemical bonds
    Explanation: Kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²) is the energy a particle possesses due to its motion. In the context of states of matter, higher kinetic energy means particles move faster, which is directly related to higher temperature.

  Q13. What is the definition of 'plasma'?
    [ ] A liquid with extremely low viscosity
    [ ] A very cold gas found in deep space
    [*] An ionized gas of free electrons and ions formed at very high temperatures
    [ ] A solid that conducts electricity
    Explanation: Plasma is the fourth state of matter, formed when a gas is heated to such high temperatures that atoms become ionized — electrons are stripped away, creating a mix of free electrons and positive ions.

  Q14. What does the term 'phase' mean in chemistry?
    [ ] A step in a chemical reaction
    [ ] The speed at which particles move
    [ ] The color of a substance
    [*] A physically distinct form of matter — solid, liquid, gas, or plasma
    Explanation: A phase is a distinct form of matter with uniform physical properties. The most common phases are solid, liquid, gas, and plasma. Phase changes occur when energy is added or removed.

  Q15. What is 'viscosity'?
    [ ] The temperature at which a liquid boils
    [ ] The density of a solid material
    [ ] The amount of gas dissolved in a liquid
    [*] A liquid's resistance to flow, caused by intermolecular forces between particles
    Explanation: Viscosity measures how much a fluid resists flowing. Honey has high viscosity (flows slowly) while water has low viscosity (flows easily). Stronger intermolecular forces lead to higher viscosity.

  Q16. What are 'intermolecular forces'?
    [ ] Gravitational pull between large objects
    [*] Attractive forces between molecules that influence a substance's physical properties and state
    [ ] Forces inside the nucleus of an atom
    [ ] The bonds between atoms within a single molecule
    Explanation: Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are attractions between neighboring molecules. They determine melting point, boiling point, viscosity, and which state of matter a substance exists in at a given temperature. Stronger IMFs mean higher melting/boiling points.

  Q17. What is the definition of 'thermal energy'?
    [*] The total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance
    [ ] The energy released during a nuclear reaction
    [ ] The energy needed to change an object's shape
    [ ] The potential energy stored in chemical bonds
    Explanation: Thermal energy is the sum of the kinetic energies of all particles in a substance. It increases with temperature and with the amount of matter in the sample.

  Q18. What does 'surface tension' mean?
    [ ] The pressure exerted by a gas on container walls
    [ ] The hardness of a solid surface
    [*] The tendency of a liquid's surface to resist being stretched or broken, due to intermolecular forces
    [ ] The rate at which a liquid evaporates
    Explanation: Surface tension is caused by the net inward pull of intermolecular forces on molecules at a liquid's surface. This creates a 'skin-like' barrier that allows small objects (like water striders) to rest on the surface.

  Q19. What is 'absolute zero'?
    [ ] The temperature at which water freezes (0°C)
    [ ] The average temperature in outer space
    [ ] The temperature at which all matter becomes plasma
    [*] The lowest possible temperature (0 K or −273.15°C) where particle motion theoretically stops
    Explanation: Absolute zero (0 Kelvin, −273.15°C) is the theoretical lower limit of temperature. At this point, particles would have minimum possible energy and essentially no thermal motion. It has never been fully reached in practice.

  Q20. What does 'amorphous solid' mean?
    [*] A solid that lacks an ordered, repeating crystalline structure
    [ ] A solid with a very high melting point
    [ ] A solid that is always transparent
    [ ] A solid that easily dissolves in water
    Explanation: Amorphous solids (like glass, rubber, and some plastics) lack the long-range ordered arrangement of particles found in crystalline solids. They soften gradually over a range of temperatures rather than melting at a sharp melting point.

  Q21. A student leaves an ice cube on the kitchen counter. After 20 minutes, there is a puddle of water. Which states of matter are demonstrated in this observation?
    [ ] Gas changing to liquid — water vapor condensed on the counter
    [ ] Plasma changing to solid — the ice was formed from plasma
    [*] Solid changing to liquid — the ice melted as it absorbed thermal energy from the room
    [ ] Liquid changing to gas — the water evaporated quickly
    Explanation: The ice cube (solid) absorbed heat energy from the warmer room. Once enough energy was gained to overcome the intermolecular forces holding the solid structure, the particles began moving more freely, producing liquid water.

  Q22. A helium balloon rises into the sky and eventually pops. As it rose, the balloon got larger. Which property of gases explains why the balloon expanded?
    [*] Gas particles spread out to fill available space — lower atmospheric pressure at altitude allows expansion
    [ ] The helium changed from gas to plasma at high altitude
    [ ] Gravity compressed the gas, making the balloon wider
    [ ] The rubber of the balloon melted in the sunlight
    Explanation: Gases have no definite volume and will expand to equalize pressure. As the balloon rose, atmospheric pressure decreased, so the helium inside pushed outward more strongly than the outside air pushed in, causing the balloon to expand until it popped.

  Q23. A welder's arc produces temperatures above 10,000°C and a bright glowing stream. What state of matter is most likely present at the tip of the welding arc?
    [ ] Solid — the metal stays solid but emits light
    [ ] Gas — the metal simply evaporates at high temperature
    [ ] Liquid — the metal melts and glows red
    [*] Plasma — the extreme temperature ionizes atoms into free electrons and ions
    Explanation: At temperatures above 10,000°C, atoms gain enough energy to ionize — electrons are stripped from their atoms, creating plasma. The bright glow comes from ions and electrons recombining and emitting photons of light.

  Q24. A sealed syringe filled with air is compressed by pushing the plunger in. The volume decreases but no air escapes. Which property of gases allows this compression?
    [ ] The air converts to a liquid under pressure
    [ ] Gas particles are tightly packed and slide past each other
    [*] Gas particles are far apart with empty space between them, allowing them to be pushed closer together
    [ ] The syringe walls absorb the gas molecules
    Explanation: Unlike solids and liquids, gas particles have large amounts of empty space between them. When pressure is applied, the particles can be forced closer together, reducing the volume. This is why gases are compressible while liquids and solids are essentially incompressible.

  Q25. Dry ice (solid CO₂) is placed on a table and produces white fog while shrinking, but no puddle forms underneath. What process is occurring?
    [*] Sublimation — the solid CO₂ is converting directly to gas without becoming a liquid
    [ ] Deposition — the gas is forming ice crystals
    [ ] Evaporation — the liquid CO₂ is turning into gas too fast to see
    [ ] Condensation — the CO₂ gas is turning back into a solid
    Explanation: At normal atmospheric pressure, CO₂ goes directly from solid to gas (sublimation) without passing through the liquid phase. The white 'fog' is actually water vapor from the surrounding air condensing due to the extreme cold of the dry ice.

  Q26. A chef notices that honey flows much more slowly than water when poured from a spoon. Both are liquids, so what property explains why honey flows more slowly?
    [*] Viscosity — honey has stronger intermolecular forces, making it more resistant to flow
    [ ] Density — honey is denser so gravity pulls it down faster
    [ ] Surface tension — honey has a stronger surface skin
    [ ] Temperature — honey is always colder than water
    Explanation: Viscosity is a liquid's resistance to flow. Honey has strong intermolecular forces between its sugar molecules, making it highly viscous. Water has weaker intermolecular forces and therefore lower viscosity. Warming honey reduces its viscosity.

  Q27. A camper notices that water boils at about 95°C at their mountain campsite instead of the usual 100°C at sea level. What causes this difference?
    [ ] The campfire produces less heat at high altitude
    [*] Lower atmospheric pressure at high altitude means less energy is needed for particles to escape as gas
    [ ] Mountain water contains different minerals that lower the boiling point
    [ ] Cold mountain air cools the water faster so it boils sooner
    Explanation: Boiling occurs when a liquid's vapor pressure equals the atmospheric pressure above it. At higher altitude, atmospheric pressure is lower, so less energy (lower temperature) is needed for water molecules to escape into the gas phase.

  Q28. A scientist places an iron block, a wooden block, and a sealed balloon in a box. She compresses only the balloon significantly. Why can the gas be compressed but the solid objects cannot?
    [*] Gas particles have large spaces between them that can be reduced; solid particles are already packed closely together
    [ ] Gas particles are smaller than solid particles
    [ ] Solids are heavier than gases so they resist compression
    [ ] The balloon is made of rubber, which is more flexible than iron or wood
    Explanation: Compressibility depends on the spacing between particles, not their size or weight. Gas particles are widely spaced with mostly empty space, so they can be pushed closer together. Solid (and liquid) particles are already close, leaving almost no room for compression.

  Q29. Neon signs glow with bright colors when electricity passes through the gas inside the tube. What state of matter produces the characteristic glow?
    [ ] Gas — neon gas naturally glows at room temperature
    [ ] Liquid — melted neon produces light when heated
    [ ] Solid — solid neon crystals emit light when struck by electricity
    [*] Plasma — electricity ionizes the neon gas, and ions emit light when electrons return to lower energy levels
    Explanation: When high-voltage electricity passes through a sealed tube of neon gas, it ionizes the atoms — stripping electrons and creating plasma. As the excited electrons fall back to lower energy levels, they emit photons of visible light, producing the characteristic neon glow.

  Q30. A forensic scientist discovers an unknown clear liquid at a crime scene. She measures its boiling point as 78.37°C and its density as 0.789 g/mL, which match known values for ethanol. Why can these two measurements identify the substance?
    [ ] Every liquid has a unique color that corresponds to its boiling point
    [*] Boiling point and density are intensive properties — they are characteristic of a substance regardless of sample size
    [ ] The amount of liquid determines its boiling point
    [ ] Mass and volume always identify a substance
    Explanation: Boiling point and density are intensive properties — they do not change with the amount of substance. Each pure substance has unique intensive property values that act like a fingerprint. Mass and volume alone (extensive properties) change with sample size and cannot identify a substance.

--- Lesson 1.2: Phase Changes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Phase Changes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the phase change from solid to liquid called?
Melting is the transition from solid to liquid. It occurs when a solid absorbs enough energy to overcome the forces holding its particles in fixed positions, allowing them to move more freely.
What is the phase change from gas to liquid called?
Condensation occurs when gas particles lose energy and slow down enough for intermolecular forces to pull them into the closer arrangement of a liquid. It is an exothermic process (releases energy).
What is the phase change from liquid to gas called?
Vaporization is the transition from liquid to gas. It can occur as evaporation (at the surface, below boiling point) or boiling (throughout the liquid, at the boiling point). Both are endothermic.
What is deposition?
Deposition is the reverse of sublimation — gas converts directly to solid without passing through the liquid phase. Frost forming on a cold window is a common example. It is an exothermic process.
What is the difference between evaporation and boiling?
Both are forms of vaporization (liquid → gas), but evaporation occurs only at the liquid surface and can happen at any temperature. Boiling occurs throughout the entire liquid when vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure at the boiling point.
What is the definition of 'melting point'?
The melting point is the specific temperature at which a solid's particles gain enough energy to break free from their fixed positions and transition to the liquid phase. At standard pressure, ice melts at 0°C.
What is 'heat of fusion'?
Heat of fusion (ΔHfus) is the amount of energy needed per gram (or mole) to convert a solid to a liquid at its melting point. For water, this is 334 J/g. The reverse process (freezing) releases the same amount of energy.
What is 'heat of vaporization'?
Heat of vaporization (ΔHvap) is the energy needed per gram (or mole) to convert a liquid to gas at its boiling point. For water, it is 2,260 J/g — much larger than the heat of fusion because all intermolecular forces must be fully overcome.
What is a 'heating curve'?
A heating curve plots temperature on the y-axis against energy (or time of heating) on the x-axis. Sloped sections show temperature rising within a single phase; flat plateaus show phase changes where temperature stays constant as energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces.
What is the 'triple point' of a substance?
The triple point is a unique set of temperature and pressure conditions where all three phases of a substance exist simultaneously in equilibrium. For water, the triple point is 0.01°C at 611.73 Pa.
Real-World Applications
Example: A scientist examines a phase diagram and notices that CO₂ has no liquid phase at normal atmospheric pressure (1 atm). What does this tell us about dry ice at room conditions?
The phase diagram for CO₂ shows that the triple point is at 5.11 atm. Below this pressure, the liquid phase does not exist, so solid CO₂ at 1 atm can only sublimate directly to gas. Liquid CO₂ requires pressures above 5.11 atm.

--- Lesson 1.2: Phase Changes (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is the phase change from solid to liquid called?
    [ ] Condensation
    [*] Melting
    [ ] Sublimation
    [ ] Freezing
    Explanation: Melting is the transition from solid to liquid. It occurs when a solid absorbs enough energy to overcome the forces holding its particles in fixed positions, allowing them to move more freely.

  Q2. What is the phase change from gas to liquid called?
    [ ] Melting
    [ ] Evaporation
    [*] Condensation
    [ ] Deposition
    Explanation: Condensation occurs when gas particles lose energy and slow down enough for intermolecular forces to pull them into the closer arrangement of a liquid. It is an exothermic process (releases energy).

  Q3. Which phase change goes directly from solid to gas without passing through the liquid phase?
    [*] Sublimation
    [ ] Melting
    [ ] Deposition
    [ ] Evaporation
    Explanation: Sublimation is the direct transition from solid to gas. A common example is dry ice (solid CO₂), which turns directly into carbon dioxide gas at normal atmospheric pressure.

  Q4. During a phase change, what happens to the temperature of the substance?
    [ ] It decreases rapidly
    [ ] It increases rapidly
    [*] It remains constant
    [ ] It fluctuates randomly
    Explanation: During a phase change, all added or removed energy goes toward breaking or forming intermolecular forces — not changing temperature. This is why a heating curve shows flat plateaus at the melting and boiling points.

  Q5. Which phase change releases energy to the surroundings?
    [*] Freezing
    [ ] Evaporation
    [ ] Sublimation
    [ ] Melting
    Explanation: Freezing (liquid → solid) is exothermic — particles slow down and form stronger intermolecular bonds, releasing energy. Melting, evaporation, and sublimation all absorb energy (endothermic).

  Q6. What is the phase change from liquid to gas called?
    [ ] Freezing
    [ ] Deposition
    [ ] Condensation
    [*] Vaporization
    Explanation: Vaporization is the transition from liquid to gas. It can occur as evaporation (at the surface, below boiling point) or boiling (throughout the liquid, at the boiling point). Both are endothermic.

  Q7. What is deposition?
    [ ] Liquid changing to solid
    [*] Gas changing directly to solid without becoming liquid first
    [ ] Solid changing to gas
    [ ] Gas changing to liquid
    Explanation: Deposition is the reverse of sublimation — gas converts directly to solid without passing through the liquid phase. Frost forming on a cold window is a common example. It is an exothermic process.

  Q8. Which phase change requires the most energy input: melting or evaporation?
    [ ] Neither requires energy — both are exothermic
    [*] Evaporation — because all intermolecular forces must be overcome for particles to become gas
    [ ] Melting — because solid particles are harder to separate than liquid particles
    [ ] They require exactly the same amount of energy
    Explanation: Evaporation (or boiling) requires more energy than melting because particles must completely overcome all intermolecular forces to enter the gas phase. In melting, particles only loosen their fixed positions while staying close together.

  Q9. What is the difference between evaporation and boiling?
    [*] Evaporation occurs at the surface below the boiling point; boiling occurs throughout the liquid at the boiling point
    [ ] Boiling only happens in water; evaporation happens in all liquids
    [ ] There is no difference — they are the same process
    [ ] Evaporation produces steam; boiling does not
    Explanation: Both are forms of vaporization (liquid → gas), but evaporation occurs only at the liquid surface and can happen at any temperature. Boiling occurs throughout the entire liquid when vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure at the boiling point.

  Q10. On a heating curve, flat horizontal sections (plateaus) represent:
    [*] Phase changes — energy is used to break intermolecular forces, not raise temperature
    [ ] Errors in the measurement equipment
    [ ] Temperature increases within a single phase
    [ ] Periods when no heat is being added
    Explanation: During a phase change, the temperature stays constant even though heat is being added. All the energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces (changing the arrangement of particles) rather than increasing kinetic energy (temperature).

  Q11. What is the definition of 'melting point'?
    [ ] The temperature at which a liquid changes to a gas
    [ ] The temperature at which a gas becomes plasma
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance decomposes
    [*] The temperature at which a solid changes to a liquid at standard pressure
    Explanation: The melting point is the specific temperature at which a solid's particles gain enough energy to break free from their fixed positions and transition to the liquid phase. At standard pressure, ice melts at 0°C.

  Q12. What does 'endothermic' mean in the context of phase changes?
    [ ] A process that releases energy to the surroundings
    [ ] A process that creates new chemical bonds
    [*] A process that absorbs energy from the surroundings
    [ ] A process that destroys energy
    Explanation: Endothermic means 'heat in' — the process absorbs energy from the surroundings. Melting, evaporation, and sublimation are all endothermic because energy must be added to overcome intermolecular forces.

  Q13. What does 'exothermic' mean in the context of phase changes?
    [ ] A process that only occurs at high temperatures
    [ ] A process that absorbs energy from the surroundings
    [*] A process that releases energy to the surroundings
    [ ] A process that breaks chemical bonds
    Explanation: Exothermic means 'heat out' — the process releases energy to the surroundings. Freezing, condensation, and deposition are exothermic because particles form stronger intermolecular bonds and release the stored energy.

  Q14. What is 'heat of fusion'?
    [ ] The total thermal energy in a solid object
    [ ] The temperature at which two metals fuse together
    [ ] The energy required to change a substance from liquid to gas
    [*] The energy required to change a substance from solid to liquid at its melting point
    Explanation: Heat of fusion (ΔHfus) is the amount of energy needed per gram (or mole) to convert a solid to a liquid at its melting point. For water, this is 334 J/g. The reverse process (freezing) releases the same amount of energy.

  Q15. What is 'heat of vaporization'?
    [ ] The temperature at which a liquid begins to boil
    [ ] The energy required to change a substance from solid to liquid
    [*] The energy required to change a substance from liquid to gas at its boiling point
    [ ] The speed at which gas particles move
    Explanation: Heat of vaporization (ΔHvap) is the energy needed per gram (or mole) to convert a liquid to gas at its boiling point. For water, it is 2,260 J/g — much larger than the heat of fusion because all intermolecular forces must be fully overcome.

  Q16. What does 'boiling point' mean?
    [ ] The temperature at which bubbles first appear in any liquid
    [*] The temperature at which a liquid's vapor pressure equals the atmospheric pressure above it
    [ ] The highest temperature a liquid can reach
    [ ] The temperature at which a solid melts
    Explanation: Boiling point is the temperature at which the vapor pressure of a liquid equals the external atmospheric pressure, causing vaporization throughout the liquid (not just at the surface). At sea level, water boils at 100°C.

  Q17. What is a 'heating curve'?
    [*] A graph showing temperature vs. energy added, with plateaus at phase changes
    [ ] A measurement of how fast a substance heats up
    [ ] A table listing boiling points of all elements
    [ ] A device used to heat liquids in a lab
    Explanation: A heating curve plots temperature on the y-axis against energy (or time of heating) on the x-axis. Sloped sections show temperature rising within a single phase; flat plateaus show phase changes where temperature stays constant as energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces.

  Q18. What does 'vapor pressure' mean?
    [*] The pressure exerted by the vapor above a liquid in a closed container at equilibrium
    [ ] The force needed to compress a gas into a liquid
    [ ] The weight of water vapor in the atmosphere
    [ ] The temperature at which vapor turns back into liquid
    Explanation: Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by a vapor in equilibrium with its liquid (or solid) in a closed container. It increases with temperature because more molecules have enough energy to escape into the gas phase.

  Q19. What is the 'triple point' of a substance?
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance boils three times faster
    [ ] The point at which three different substances mix together
    [ ] The third phase change in a heating curve
    [*] The specific temperature and pressure at which solid, liquid, and gas phases coexist in equilibrium
    Explanation: The triple point is a unique set of temperature and pressure conditions where all three phases of a substance exist simultaneously in equilibrium. For water, the triple point is 0.01°C at 611.73 Pa.

  Q20. What does 'condensation point' mean?
    [*] The temperature at which a gas changes to a liquid — numerically equal to the boiling point
    [ ] The pressure at which a gas becomes a solid
    [ ] The point at which a liquid becomes saturated
    [ ] The temperature at which dew forms, which varies based on humidity
    Explanation: The condensation point and the boiling point are the same temperature for a given substance at the same pressure. The process is just reversed: boiling adds energy (endothermic), while condensation releases energy (exothermic).

  Q21. On a cold morning, a student sees frost forming on a car windshield even though it did not rain overnight. Which phase change produced the frost?
    [ ] Freezing — liquid water on the windshield froze overnight
    [ ] Condensation — water vapor became liquid droplets that then froze
    [ ] Sublimation — ice from the ground rose up to the windshield
    [*] Deposition — water vapor in the air changed directly to solid ice on the cold surface
    Explanation: Frost forms through deposition — water vapor (gas) in the air contacts the cold windshield surface and transitions directly to solid ice crystals without first becoming liquid. This is the reverse of sublimation and is exothermic.

  Q22. A pot of water is boiling on a stove. A student measures the temperature and finds it stays at 100°C even as more heat is added. Why doesn't the temperature increase?
    [ ] Water cannot absorb any more energy after reaching 100°C
    [*] During the phase change, all added energy goes into breaking intermolecular forces rather than raising temperature
    [ ] The stove stopped producing heat once the water reached 100°C
    [ ] The thermometer is broken and cannot read above 100°C
    Explanation: At the boiling point, energy is used to overcome intermolecular forces and convert liquid molecules to gas — this is the heat of vaporization. Temperature only rises again once all the liquid has been converted to steam.

  Q23. Wet clothes hung on a clothesline dry even on a cool day when the temperature is well below 100°C. What process is responsible?
    [ ] Condensation — moisture leaves the clothes and enters the air as liquid droplets
    [*] Evaporation — surface water molecules with enough kinetic energy escape into the air
    [ ] Boiling — the water boils at lower temperatures outdoors
    [ ] Sublimation — the water turns directly from liquid to solid
    Explanation: Evaporation occurs at any temperature when surface molecules have enough kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular forces and escape into the gas phase. Wind and low humidity speed up evaporation by removing vapor from the surface.

  Q24. A hiker at high altitude (5,000 m) tries to cook pasta but notices the water boils at about 83°C instead of 100°C. The pasta takes much longer to cook. Why?
    [ ] The thinner air makes the flame on the stove weaker
    [ ] The water contains fewer minerals at high altitude
    [*] Lower atmospheric pressure lowers the boiling point, so the water is less hot and cooks food more slowly
    [ ] UV radiation at high altitude prevents water from reaching 100°C
    Explanation: Boiling occurs when vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure. At 5,000 m, atmospheric pressure is much lower, so water boils at ~83°C. Since the water is cooler, chemical reactions in cooking (which depend on temperature) proceed more slowly.

  Q25. A student places mothballs in a closet. Over several weeks, the mothballs shrink and eventually disappear, but no liquid residue is found. What phase change occurred?
    [ ] Condensation — the mothball gas became liquid and drained away

--- Lesson 1.3: Intensive & Extensive Properties (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Intensive & Extensive Properties
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of an 'intensive property'?
An intensive property is independent of sample size. Examples include density, boiling point, melting point, color, conductivity, and hardness. These are the same whether you have a gram or a kilogram of a substance.
What is the definition of an 'extensive property'?
An extensive property changes proportionally with the amount of matter. Mass, volume, length, weight, and total energy are all extensive — double the sample, double the value.
What is the definition of 'density' and why is it classified as intensive?
Density equals mass divided by volume (d = m/v). When you double the amount of substance, both mass and volume double, so their ratio remains unchanged. This makes density an intensive property useful for substance identification.
What is 'hardness' and how is it classified?
Hardness measures how well a material resists being scratched or deformed. It depends on the material's internal structure and bonding, not on how much of it you have, making it an intensive property.
What is the definition of 'luster'?
Luster describes how a material's surface appears when it reflects light — metallic, glassy, waxy, dull, etc. A small piece of gold has the same metallic luster as a large piece, making it an intensive property.
A jeweler has a small gold ring and a large gold bar. A customer asks, 'Does the ring have the same density as the bar?' What is the correct answer and why?
Density (d = m/v) is intensive — when sample size changes, both mass and volume change proportionally, keeping the ratio constant. Pure gold always has a density of 19.3 g/cm³ regardless of whether it is a ring or a bar.
Two identical beakers each contain 100 mL of water at 25°C. A student pours them together into one large beaker. What is the temperature of the combined water?
Temperature is intensive. When you combine two samples at the same temperature, the result is that same temperature — not the sum. The volume and mass (extensive) will double to 200 mL, but the temperature stays at 25°C.

--- Lesson 1.4: Mass, Volume, & Density (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Mass, Volume, & Density
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for density?
Density is calculated as d = m/v (mass divided by volume). Common units include g/mL, g/cm³, and kg/m³. You can rearrange to find mass (m = d·v) or volume (v = m/d).
If an object has a mass of 50 g and a volume of 25 mL, what is its density?
Density = mass / volume = 50 g / 25 mL = 2 g/mL. The other choices result from multiplying, dividing reversed, or adding the values.
What is the density of water at standard conditions?
Pure water at 4°C has a density of 1.00 g/mL (or equivalently, 1.00 g/cm³ or 1,000 kg/m³). This is a standard reference point for comparing other materials.
What is the definition of 'density'?
Density is defined as mass per unit volume. It describes how tightly matter is packed in a given space. Higher density means more mass concentrated in the same volume.
What is 'mass' in chemistry?
Mass is the quantity of matter in an object. It is constant regardless of location (unlike weight, which depends on gravity). Mass is measured using a balance and reported in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).
A student measures the mass of a rock as 150 g. She places it in a graduated cylinder that contains 50 mL of water, and the water level rises to 80 mL. What is the density of the rock?
The rock's volume equals the water displaced: 80 mL − 50 mL = 30 mL. Density = mass/volume = 150 g / 30 mL = 5.0 g/mL. Common errors include dividing by the final reading or the initial reading instead of the difference.

    Explanation: Specific gravity = density of substance / density of water. Since it is a ratio of the same units, it is dimensionless. A specific gravity greater than 1 means the substance sinks in water; less than 1 means it floats.

  Q15. What does 'graduated cylinder' refer to?
    [ ] A device that heats liquids to their boiling point
    [ ] A tube used to measure the density of gases
    [ ] A cylinder used to measure mass of solids
    [*] A tall, narrow container with volume markings used to precisely measure liquid volume
    Explanation: A graduated cylinder has volume markings (graduations) along its side and is used to measure liquid volumes precisely. Read the volume at the bottom of the meniscus (the curved surface of the liquid).

  Q16. What does 'meniscus' mean?
    [*] The curved surface of a liquid in a container, caused by the interaction between the liquid and the container walls
    [ ] The flat surface of a liquid at rest when considering all relevant factors
    [ ] The steam rising from a heated liquid when evaluated systematically
    [ ] The bubbles formed when a liquid boils as typically measured in practice
    Explanation: The meniscus is the curve at the top of a liquid column. Water forms a concave (dip down) meniscus in glass because water adheres to glass. Always read the volume at the bottom of a concave meniscus.

  Q17. What is 'mass' in chemistry?
    [ ] The space an object occupies, measured in liters
    [*] The amount of matter in an object, measured in grams or kilograms
    [ ] The density of an object times its temperature
    [ ] The force of gravity acting on an object
    Explanation: Mass is the quantity of matter in an object. It is constant regardless of location (unlike weight, which depends on gravity). Mass is measured using a balance and reported in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).

  Q18. What does 'volume' mean in the context of density?
    [ ] The weight of a substance at sea level
    [*] The amount of three-dimensional space an object or substance occupies
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance changes state
    [ ] The loudness of a sound produced by a substance
    Explanation: Volume is the space occupied by matter. It is measured in mL, L, or cm³. For regular shapes, volume = length × width × height. For irregular shapes, water displacement is used.

  Q19. What does 'Archimedes' Principle' state?
    [ ] All objects sink in water unless they contain air
    [ ] Mass and volume are always proportional to temperature
    [ ] Density determines the color of a substance
    [*] An object submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced
    Explanation: Archimedes' Principle: the buoyant force on a submerged object equals the weight of the displaced fluid. If this buoyant force exceeds the object's weight, the object floats. This principle allows us to use water displacement to measure volume.

  Q20. What does it mean for a substance to be 'neutrally buoyant'?
    [ ] The substance dissolves completely in the fluid
    [ ] The substance has zero mass in the fluid
    [*] The substance's density equals the fluid's density, so it neither floats nor sinks
    [ ] The substance bounces between the surface and the bottom
    Explanation: Neutral buoyancy occurs when an object's density exactly matches the surrounding fluid's density. The buoyant force equals gravity, so the object remains suspended at whatever depth it is placed. Submarines and scuba divers use this principle.

  Q21. A student measures the mass of a rock as 150 g. She places it in a graduated cylinder that contains 50 mL of water, and the water level rises to 80 mL. What is the density of the rock?
    [ ] 3.0 g/mL — divide mass by final water level (150/50)
    [ ] 2.0 g/mL — subtract water volume from mass (150 − 50 = 100, 150/100)
    [*] 5.0 g/mL — the rock's volume is 30 mL (80 − 50), and 150/30 = 5.0
    [ ] 1.875 g/mL — divide mass by total volume (150/80)
    Explanation: The rock's volume equals the water displaced: 80 mL − 50 mL = 30 mL. Density = mass/volume = 150 g / 30 mL = 5.0 g/mL. Common errors include dividing by the final reading or the initial reading instead of the difference.

  Q22. A cruise ship made of steel (density = 7.8 g/cm³) floats on water. If steel is much denser than water, why doesn't the ship sink?
    [*] The ship's hull encloses a large volume of air, making the overall average density less than water
    [ ] Salt water is denser than steel, so all ships float in the ocean
    [ ] The ship's steel becomes less dense when shaped into a hull
    [ ] The engines push the ship upward to keep it from sinking
    Explanation: Although steel is denser than water, the ship's hull is shaped to enclose vast volumes of air. The total mass of the ship (steel + air + cargo) divided by the total volume is less than 1 g/mL, so the ship's overall density allows it to float.

  Q23. A chef makes a layered drink by pouring honey, water, and oil into a glass. The honey settles at the bottom, water in the middle, and oil on top. What does this demonstrate?
    [*] Liquids layer by density — honey is densest (bottom), water is in the middle, oil is least dense (top)
    [ ] The liquids are arranged by the order they were poured
    [ ] Lighter colors always float on top of darker ones
    [ ] Viscosity determines which liquid is on top
    Explanation: When immiscible liquids are combined, they separate into layers based on density. Honey (~1.4 g/mL) is the densest and sinks. Water (1.0 g/mL) is less dense than honey but denser than oil (~0.9 g/mL), so it sits in the middle.

  Q24. A scientist has two metal cubes of the same size. Cube A has a mass of 27 g and Cube B has a mass of 216 g. She needs to determine which is aluminum (d = 2.7 g/cm³) and which is silver (d = 10.5 g/cm³). If each cube's volume is 10 cm³, which cube is which?
    [ ] Cube A is silver and Cube B is aluminum
    [ ] Cube A is aluminum (27/10 = 2.7 g/cm³) and Cube B is neither standard value — need to recheck mass
    [*] Cube A is aluminum (27/10 = 2.7 g/cm³) and Cube B has a density of 21.6 g/cm³ — denser than silver, possibly gold or tungsten
    [ ] Both are aluminum because they are the same size
    Explanation: Cube A: d = 27 g / 10 cm³ = 2.7 g/cm³, matching aluminum. Cube B: d = 216 g / 10 cm³ = 21.6 g/cm³, which does not match silver (10.5) — it is closer to platinum (21.4) or gold (19.3). Density calculations can reveal unexpected identities.

  Q25. Ice floats in liquid water, which is unusual for a solid-liquid pair of the same substance. How does this relate to density?
    [ ] Ice is denser than water but contains trapped air bubbles that make it float
    [*] Ice is less dense (0.917 g/cm³) than liquid water (1.00 g/cm³) because its crystal structure has open spaces
    [ ] Water pushes ice upward due to surface tension, not density
    [ ] Ice and water have the same density, so ice just barely floats
    Explanation: Water is one of the few substances whose solid form is less dense than its liquid. When water freezes, hydrogen bonds create an open hexagonal crystal structure with more space between molecules, reducing density to 0.917 g/cm³. This is why ice floats.

  Q26. A gold dealer receives a bar claimed to be pure gold (d = 19.3 g/cm³). She measures the mass as 500 g and the volume as 38 cm³. Is the bar likely pure gold?
    [ ] Cannot determine — mass and volume alone cannot identify a substance
    [*] No — the calculated density is 13.2 g/cm³, much lower than gold; the bar is likely a cheaper metal or alloy
    [ ] Yes — 500 g is a reasonable mass for a gold bar
    [ ] Yes — 38 cm³ is a reasonable volume for gold
    Explanation: Density = 500 g / 38 cm³ = 13.2 g/cm³. Pure gold has a density of 19.3 g/cm³, so this bar is significantly less dense. It could be lead (11.3) plated with gold, or a gold-copper alloy. Density is a reliable way to detect counterfeits.

  Q27. A hot air balloon rises because the air inside the balloon is heated. How does heating the air change its density?
    [ ] Heating removes air molecules from inside the balloon
    [*] Heating makes air particles move faster and spread apart, decreasing the density below that of the surrounding cooler air
    [ ] The flame adds lighter gases that replace the original air
    [ ] Heating increases the air's density, which pushes the balloon upward
    Explanation: Heating a gas increases particle speed and spacing, causing expansion. In an open balloon, some air escapes as it expands, reducing the mass inside while volume stays large. This lowers the overall density below the surrounding cool air, creating buoyancy.

  Q28. Dead Sea water has a density of about 1.24 g/mL. A swimmer who normally sinks in a freshwater pool finds they float easily in the Dead Sea. Why?
    [ ] The high salt content makes the swimmer lighter
    [ ] The Dead Sea has stronger waves that push swimmers up
    [ ] The warm temperature of the Dead Sea increases the swimmer's buoyancy
    [*] The human body's density (~1.06 g/mL) is less than the Dead Sea water, so the buoyant force exceeds gravity
    Explanation: The average human body has a density of about 1.06 g/mL. In freshwater (1.00 g/mL), this means we barely sink. In the Dead Sea (1.24 g/mL), the water is denser than the body, so the buoyant force is greater than the person's weight, and they float easily.

  Q29. A lab technician needs to separate a mixture of plastic beads: Bead A (d = 0.85 g/cm³), Bead B (d = 1.05 g/cm³), and Bead C (d = 1.35 g/cm³). She places them all in water. What happens?
    [*] Bead A floats (less dense than water); Beads B and C sink (denser than water)
    [ ] Bead C floats and Bead A sinks
    [ ] All three sink because they are solid
    [ ] All three float because they are plastic
    Explanation: Objects float if their density is less than the fluid they're in. Water's density is 1.00 g/cm³. Bead A (0.85) floats. Beads B (1.05) and C (1.35) are denser than water and sink. To further separate B and C, a denser liquid (e.g., salt water at 1.2 g/mL) could be used.

  Q30. An engineer designs a submarine that must operate at two depths: 100 m and 500 m. At 500 m, the water pressure compresses the sub's hull slightly, reducing its volume. If the sub's mass stays the same, what happens to its density, and how does this affect buoyancy?
    [*] Density increases (same mass, less volume), reducing buoyancy — the sub tends to sink further unless it compensates
    [ ] Density decreases because water pressure pushes the sub upward
    [ ] Density stays the same because the sub is a solid object
    [ ] The sub floats more easily at 500 m because deeper water is denser
    Explanation: If the hull compresses and volume decreases while mass stays the same, d = m/v increases. A denser submarine displaces less water relative to its weight, reducing buoyancy. Real submarines compensate by pumping water in or out of ballast tanks to adjust overall density.

--- Lesson 1.5: Heterogeneous & Homogeneous Mixtures (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Heterogeneous & Homogeneous Mixtures
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of a 'mixture'?
A mixture is a physical combination of substances that retain their individual properties. Unlike compounds, the components are not chemically bonded, the ratio is variable, and they can be separated by physical means (filtration, distillation, etc.).
What is a 'colloid'?
Colloids have particles sized 1-1,000 nm. They are small enough to stay dispersed (unlike suspensions) but large enough to scatter light (Tyndall effect, unlike solutions). Examples: milk, fog, smoke, mayonnaise, gelatin.
What is a 'suspension'?
Suspensions contain particles larger than 1,000 nm that are visibly dispersed but settle out over time. Muddy water, Italian dressing, and snow globes are examples. They can be separated by filtration.
A factory produces steel, which is an alloy of iron and carbon. The carbon content is precisely controlled at 1.5%. A new employee asks, 'How is this different from iron carbide (Fe₃C), which also contains iron and carbon?' What is the key difference?
Steel is a mixture — iron and carbon atoms are physically blended, and the carbon percentage can vary (0.2% to 2.1%). Iron carbide (Fe₃C) is a compound — iron and carbon are chemically bonded in a fixed 3:1 ratio. This distinction between mixture and compound is fundamental in chemistry.
A lab technician needs to determine whether an unknown white liquid is a solution, colloid, or suspension. She lets it sit undisturbed for 24 hours (no settling), then shines a laser pointer through it (the beam is clearly visible). What is her conclusion?
Two key tests distinguish the three types: (1) Settling — suspensions settle, solutions and colloids don't. Since it didn't settle, it's not a suspension. (2) Tyndall effect — colloids scatter light (visible beam), solutions don't. Since the beam was visible, it's a colloid, not a solution.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of a heterogeneous mixture?
A salad is heterogeneous because you can see and separate its individual components (lettuce, tomatoes, croutons). Salt water, air, and vinegar are all examples of homogeneous mixtures (solutions).
Example: Milk, fog, and gelatin are all examples of:
Colloids contain particles larger than those in solutions but smaller than suspensions. They remain dispersed (don't settle) and often scatter light (the Tyndall effect). Milk, fog, and gelatin are classic examples.

  Q1. A homogeneous mixture is also called a:
    [*] Solution
    [ ] Element
    [ ] Suspension
    [ ] Compound
    Explanation: A homogeneous mixture has a uniform composition throughout and is also known as a solution. The solute is evenly distributed in the solvent at the molecular level, so the mixture looks the same in every part.

  Q2. Which is an example of a heterogeneous mixture?
    [ ] Vinegar
    [ ] Air
    [ ] Salt water
    [*] Salad
    Explanation: A salad is heterogeneous because you can see and separate its individual components (lettuce, tomatoes, croutons). Salt water, air, and vinegar are all examples of homogeneous mixtures (solutions).

  Q3. In a solution, the substance being dissolved is called the:
    [ ] Mixture
    [ ] Element
    [ ] Solvent
    [*] Solute
    Explanation: The solute is the substance that dissolves (usually present in smaller amount). The solvent is the substance doing the dissolving (usually present in larger amount). In salt water, salt is the solute and water is the solvent.

  Q4. Which of the following is a homogeneous mixture?
    [*] Vinegar
    [ ] Oil and water
    [ ] Trail mix
    [ ] Sand and water
    Explanation: Vinegar is a homogeneous mixture of acetic acid dissolved in water — it has a uniform appearance throughout. Trail mix is heterogeneous (visible parts), and sand/water and oil/water are heterogeneous (they separate into visible layers).

  Q5. What distinguishes a heterogeneous mixture from a homogeneous one?
    [*] The parts are visibly different and not uniformly distributed
    [ ] It contains only one type of element
    [ ] It can only exist as a liquid
    [ ] It has a uniform composition throughout
    Explanation: Heterogeneous mixtures have a non-uniform composition — you can see distinct regions or components (e.g., salad, trail mix, sand in water). Homogeneous mixtures look the same throughout.

  Q6. Which is the best technique for separating a homogeneous mixture like salt water?
    [ ] Picking by hand
    [ ] Using a magnet
    [*] Distillation
    [ ] Filtration
    Explanation: Distillation separates components by differences in boiling point — you boil the water away and collect it, leaving the salt behind. Filtration works for heterogeneous mixtures with visible solid particles, and magnets only work with magnetic materials.

  Q7. Blood is classified as what type of mixture?
    [ ] Homogeneous
    [*] Heterogeneous
    [ ] Pure substance
    [ ] Element
    Explanation: Blood contains red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, and plasma, which can be separated by centrifugation. Although it may look uniform to the naked eye, at the microscopic level it is clearly heterogeneous.

  Q8. Which type of mixture has particles that eventually settle out over time?
    [*] Suspension
    [ ] Colloid
    [ ] Solution
    [ ] Element
    Explanation: A suspension contains large particles that are dispersed but will eventually settle out due to gravity (like muddy water). Solutions have dissolved particles that never settle, and colloids have intermediate particles that stay dispersed.

  Q9. Milk, fog, and gelatin are all examples of:
    [ ] Suspensions
    [*] Colloids
    [ ] Solutions
    [ ] Pure substances
    Explanation: Colloids contain particles larger than those in solutions but smaller than suspensions. They remain dispersed (don't settle) and often scatter light (the Tyndall effect). Milk, fog, and gelatin are classic examples.

  Q10. The substance in a solution that is present in the largest amount is called the:
    [ ] Mixture
    [ ] Solute
    [ ] Compound
    [*] Solvent
    Explanation: The solvent is the dissolving medium, typically present in the greatest amount. Water is the most common solvent and is called the 'universal solvent' because of its ability to dissolve many substances.

  Q11. What is the definition of a 'mixture'?
    [ ] A single type of atom arranged in a crystal structure
    [ ] Two or more elements bonded in a fixed ratio
    [ ] A chemical reaction that produces new substances
    [*] A combination of two or more substances that are NOT chemically bonded and can be separated by physical means
    Explanation: A mixture is a physical combination of substances that retain their individual properties. Unlike compounds, the components are not chemically bonded, the ratio is variable, and they can be separated by physical means (filtration, distillation, etc.).

  Q12. What does 'homogeneous' mean?
    [ ] Made of particles that settle over time based on established principles
    [ ] Visible to the naked eye, particularly regarding evenly when applying
    [*] Having a uniform composition throughout — every sample of the mixture has the same proportions
    [ ] Containing only one element, particularly regarding what as typically measured in practice
    Explanation: Homogeneous means 'same throughout.' In a homogeneous mixture (solution), the solute is evenly dispersed at the molecular level, so any sample taken from any part of the mixture will have the same composition.

  Q13. What does 'heterogeneous' mean?
    [*] Having a non-uniform composition — different regions have different proportions of components
    [ ] Made of a single pure substance
    [ ] Living organisms mixed with non-living matter
    [ ] Completely uniform in appearance
    Explanation: Heterogeneous means 'different throughout.' The components are not evenly distributed and can often be distinguished visually. A sample from one part may differ from a sample taken from another part.

  Q14. What is a 'colloid'?
    [*] A mixture with particles larger than a solution but smaller than a suspension, that stay dispersed and don't settle out
    [ ] A pure substance with no dissolved particles
    [ ] A mixture where all particles are individual atoms
    [ ] A chemical compound that dissolves easily in water
    Explanation: Colloids have particles sized 1-1,000 nm. They are small enough to stay dispersed (unlike suspensions) but large enough to scatter light (Tyndall effect, unlike solutions). Examples: milk, fog, smoke, mayonnaise, gelatin.

  Q15. What is a 'suspension'?
    [ ] A pure substance in the liquid phase
    [ ] A homogeneous mixture of two liquids
    [ ] A solution with dissolved gases
    [*] A heterogeneous mixture with large visible particles that will eventually settle out due to gravity
    Explanation: Suspensions contain particles larger than 1,000 nm that are visibly dispersed but settle out over time. Muddy water, Italian dressing, and snow globes are examples. They can be separated by filtration.

  Q16. What does 'Tyndall effect' mean?
    [ ] The dissolution of a solute in a solvent
    [ ] The settling of large particles in a suspension over time
    [*] The scattering of a light beam by particles in a colloid, making the beam visible
    [ ] The separation of a mixture using heat
    Explanation: The Tyndall effect is observed when light passes through a colloid — the beam becomes visible because particles scatter the light. It does not occur in true solutions (particles too small) or clear liquids. Think of car headlights visible in fog.

  Q17. What does 'solubility' mean?
    [ ] The ability of a substance to separate from a mixture
    [*] The maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature
    [ ] The speed at which a solute dissolves
    [ ] The temperature at which a solid melts
    Explanation: Solubility is a quantitative measure of how much solute can dissolve in a solvent at a given temperature. For example, about 36 g of NaCl dissolve in 100 mL of water at 25°C. Solubility generally increases with temperature for solids.

  Q18. What does 'filtration' mean?
    [ ] Dissolving a solid completely in a liquid
    [ ] A method of combining two solutions together
    [*] A separation method that uses a porous barrier to separate solid particles from a liquid or gas
    [ ] Heating a liquid to separate it from dissolved solids
    Explanation: Filtration passes a mixture through a filter (paper, cloth, membrane) that allows small particles and liquid to pass through while trapping larger solid particles. It works for heterogeneous mixtures and suspensions but not for solutions.

  Q19. What does 'distillation' mean?
    [*] A separation technique that uses differences in boiling points — heating to vaporize one component, then cooling to collect it
    [ ] Filtering a mixture through paper to remove solids
    [ ] Adding a chemical to break down a compound
    [ ] Freezing a solution to separate its components
    Explanation: Distillation exploits boiling point differences. The mixture is heated until the component with the lower boiling point vaporizes, leaving the other behind. The vapor is then condensed and collected. This can separate solutions (e.g., salt water → pure water + salt).

  Q20. What does 'alloy' mean?
    [*] A homogeneous mixture of two or more metals (or a metal and a nonmetal), such as brass or steel
    [ ] A pure metal that has been melted and re-solidified
    [ ] A chemical compound containing two metal elements
    [ ] A heterogeneous mixture of different metals
    Explanation: An alloy is a solid solution of metals. Brass (copper + zinc), steel (iron + carbon), and bronze (copper + tin) are common examples. Alloys are homogeneous mixtures because the atoms are uniformly distributed throughout.

  Q21. A student shines a flashlight through a glass of lemonade (sugar water + lemon juice) and a glass of whole milk. The beam is invisible in the lemonade but clearly visible in the milk. What explains this difference?
    [ ] Milk is darker in color, which makes the light beam visible

    [ ] Lemonade absorbs all the light, so none is scattered
    [*] Milk is a colloid with particles large enough to scatter light (Tyndall effect); lemonade is a true solution with particles too small to scatter light
    Explanation: The Tyndall effect occurs in colloids: milk contains fat globules (1-10 μm) that scatter light, making the beam visible. Lemonade is a true solution with dissolved sugar and citric acid molecules too small to scatter visible light.

  Q22. A gold ring is labeled '14 karat,' meaning it is 58.3% gold and 41.7% other metals (copper, silver). Is this a mixture, compound, or pure substance? What type?

    [*] Homogeneous mixture (alloy) — the metals are uniformly blended but not chemically bonded
    Explanation: 14K gold is an alloy — a solid solution of gold with copper, silver, and/or other metals. The metals are physically mixed at the atomic level (homogeneous) but not chemically bonded. The proportions can vary, unlike a compound with fixed ratios.

  Q23. A water treatment plant needs to remove sand, bacteria, and dissolved minerals from river water. What sequence of separation methods would be most effective?

  Q26. A student mixes iron filings and sulfur powder in a bowl. He says, 'I can separate them with a magnet, so this is a mixture.' He then heats them strongly and tries the magnet again, but it no longer works. What happened?
    [ ] The heat melted the magnet, so it lost its pull, particularly regarding filings following the established framework

--- Lesson 1.6: Physical & Chemical Properties (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Physical & Chemical Properties
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the definition of a 'physical property'?
Physical properties (color, density, melting point, hardness, conductivity, luster, solubility) describe the substance as it is — no new substance is formed during observation. The substance retains its chemical identity.
What is the definition of a 'chemical property'?
Chemical properties describe what a substance CAN become — they can only be observed when the substance undergoes a chemical change. Examples: flammability, reactivity, oxidation potential, toxicity.

--- Lesson 1.7: Physical & Chemical Changes (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Physical & Chemical Changes
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a 'physical change'?
Physical changes alter how matter looks or what phase it's in, but the molecules remain the same. Ice, liquid water, and steam are all H₂O — only the arrangement and energy of molecules differ. The substance can usually be recovered.
What is a 'chemical change'?
In a chemical change (also called a chemical reaction), bonds between atoms break and reform in new arrangements, producing substances with different chemical formulas and properties. The original substances cannot be recovered by physical means.
What is a 'precipitate'?
When two clear solutions are mixed and a solid suddenly appears (often cloudy or as particles settling out), that solid is a precipitate. For example, mixing silver nitrate and sodium chloride solutions produces a white precipitate of silver chloride (AgCl).
What is meant by 'reversibility' when comparing physical and chemical changes?
Melting ice can be reversed by refreezing (physical). Burning paper cannot be reversed — you can't turn ash and CO₂ back into paper (chemical). Some chemical reactions are reversible under specific conditions (like weak acid dissociation), but most everyday chemical changes are not.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which is an example of a chemical change?
Burning wood is a chemical change — combustion produces new substances (carbon dioxide, water vapor, and ash). Cutting, breaking, and melting only change form or size without creating new substances.
Example: Which is an example of a physical change?
Freezing water changes its state from liquid to solid but the substance remains H₂O. Rusting, burning, and cooking all produce new substances with different chemical compositions.
Example: Baking a cake is an example of a:
Baking causes chemical reactions — proteins denature, sugars caramelize, and baking soda releases CO₂ gas. The batter transforms into cake with completely different properties, and you cannot un-bake a cake.
Example: A forensic scientist examines a crime scene and finds a white powder. She adds a drop of acid and observes no reaction. She then heats the powder — it melts at 801°C without decomposing. She dissolves it in water and tests conductivity — it conducts electricity. What can she conclude?
Melting point 801°C (physical property) matches NaCl. No reaction with acid (chemical property) rules out bases/carbonates. Dissolving and conducting electricity (physical properties) confirm an ionic compound. Together, these physical AND chemical property tests identify the substance without fully destroying it.

    [*] No — browning is a chemical change called enzymatic oxidation, where enzymes react with oxygen to form melanin (a new brown substance)
    [ ] Yes — the banana just dried out, which is a physical change
    [ ] Yes — the color change is temporary and will reverse on its own
    Explanation: Enzymatic browning is a chemical change. When banana cells are damaged by cutting, the enzyme polyphenol oxidase reacts with oxygen to produce melanin — a new, brown-colored compound. Lemon juice (acid) can slow this by denaturing the enzyme, but it's still a chemical process.

  Q23. A camper mixes baking soda and vinegar in a bottle and quickly puts a balloon over the opening. The balloon inflates rapidly. What type of change occurred, and what evidence is there?
    [ ] Physical change — the gas was already trapped in the baking soda and was released
    [ ] No change — the balloon inflated due to heat from the vinegar
    [*] Chemical change — the gas (CO₂) inflating the balloon is a new substance produced by the reaction between NaHCO₃ and acetic acid
    [ ] Physical change — the baking soda just dissolved in the vinegar
    Explanation: NaHCO₃ + CH₃COOH → NaCH₃COO + H₂O + CO₂. The vigorous bubbling and gas production (inflating the balloon) are classic signs of a chemical change. New substances formed: sodium acetate, water, and carbon dioxide gas.

  Q24. A student weighs a sealed flask containing a mixture of solutions before and after mixing (causing a precipitate to form). The mass reads 150.0 g both times. She is confused — 'A new substance formed, so shouldn't mass change?' What explains this?
    [ ] The scale is broken — chemical changes always change mass
    [*] The Law of Conservation of Mass — atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed, so total mass stays the same in a sealed system
    [ ] The precipitate weighs the same because solids are denser than liquids
    [ ] No chemical change actually occurred — if mass didn't change, it must be physical
    Explanation: The Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier) states that mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. All the same atoms are present before and after — they've just been rearranged into new compounds. In a sealed system, the total mass remains constant.

  Q25. A child's glow stick works by bending the stick (breaking an inner glass vial) and mixing two chemicals. The stick glows green for several hours then fades. Is the glow a physical or chemical change?
    [ ] Physical change — the chemicals just mixed together like salt and water
    [*] Chemical change — the two chemicals react to form new substances that release energy as light (chemiluminescence)
    [ ] Physical change — bending the stick is a physical action that created the glow
    [ ] No change — glow sticks use batteries to produce light
    Explanation: Glow sticks produce light through chemiluminescence — a chemical reaction between hydrogen peroxide and a phenyl oxalate ester, catalyzed by a fluorescent dye. New substances form and energy is released as light (not heat). The reaction is irreversible, which is why glow sticks can't be 'recharged.'

  Q26. A construction worker mixes cement powder with water. Over the next few hours, the mixture becomes rock-hard and hot to the touch. Is this a physical or chemical change? What evidence supports your answer?
    [ ] Chemical change — but the heat is just from sunlight, not the reaction
    [ ] Physical change — the water just evaporated, leaving the dry cement behind
    [ ] Physical change — the cement just froze like water turning to ice
    [*] Chemical change — cement undergoes a hydration reaction (combining with water) that forms new crystalline compounds and releases heat (exothermic)
    Explanation: Cement hardening is a chemical change called hydration. Calcium silicate in cement reacts with water to form calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-H gel) — a completely new substance. Evidence: heat release (exothermic), irreversibility (you can't soften hardened concrete with water), and new properties (rock-hard vs. powder).

  Q27. During a cooking class, students observe these events: (1) boiling water, (2) caramelizing sugar, (3) chopping onions, (4) frying an egg. Which of these involve chemical changes?
    [ ] All four — heat is involved in each, so they are all chemical
    [ ] None — cooking is always a physical change
    [*] Caramelizing sugar and frying an egg — both produce new substances with different properties
    [ ] Only frying an egg — it's the only one that changes color
    Explanation: Boiling water (liquid → gas, same H₂O) and chopping onions (shape change) are physical changes. Caramelizing sugar breaks sucrose into new compounds (color change, new flavor — chemical). Frying an egg denatures proteins irreversibly, producing new substances (chemical).

  Q28. An antacid tablet is dropped into a glass of water and fizzes vigorously, then dissolves. A student argues this is a physical change because the tablet just dissolved. Another says it's chemical because of the fizzing. Who is right?
    [ ] Both are right — it's simultaneously physical and not a change at all
    [*] The second student — the fizzing (CO₂ gas) is evidence of a chemical reaction between the base in the tablet and the citric acid
    [ ] Both are wrong — it's neither physical nor chemical
    [ ] The first student — all dissolving is a physical change
    Explanation: Antacid tablets contain a base (NaHCO₃) and an acid (citric acid). When water allows them to mix, they react: NaHCO₃ + H₃C₆H₅O₇ → CO₂ + H₂O + sodium citrate. The gas production (fizzing) is a clear sign of chemical change. While dissolving alone is physical, the reaction is chemical.

  Q29. A forensic scientist examines a crime scene and finds a white powder. She adds a drop of acid and observes no reaction. She then heats the powder — it melts at 801°C without decomposing. She dissolves it in water and tests conductivity — it conducts electricity. What can she conclude?
    [ ] No identification is possible without knowing the color in most standard conditions
    [*] The tests revealed physical properties (melting point, solubility, conductivity) and one chemical property (no reactivity with acid); the substance is likely table salt (NaCl)
    [ ] The substance is sugar because it dissolves in water, particularly regarding drop under normal circumstances
    [ ] All the tests were chemical changes, so the evidence is destroyed based on established principles
    Explanation: Melting point 801°C (physical property) matches NaCl. No reaction with acid (chemical property) rules out bases/carbonates. Dissolving and conducting electricity (physical properties) confirm an ionic compound. Together, these physical AND chemical property tests identify the substance without fully destroying it.

  Q30. A student burns a 10.0 g piece of steel wool in a sealed jar containing oxygen. After the reaction, the remaining solid (iron oxide) weighs 14.3 g. She says, 'This violates the Law of Conservation of Mass — the product weighs MORE than the steel wool!' Is she correct?
    [ ] Yes — burning always creates mass from energy according to E = mc²
    [ ] Yes — the extra mass was created during the reaction, violating the law
    [ ] No — the scale is wrong because the jar is sealed
    [*] No — the iron combined with oxygen from the jar (4.3 g of O₂), so the total mass of reactants (10.0 + 4.3 = 14.3 g) equals the product mass
    Explanation: The law is not violated. The iron (10.0 g) combined with oxygen gas (4.3 g) from inside the jar: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃. The total mass of reactants (14.3 g) equals the product mass (14.3 g). She only weighed the solid — she forgot that oxygen gas also has mass and was consumed in the reaction.

--- Lesson 1.8: Energy & Matter (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The Law of Conservation of Energy states that energy:
    [ ] Always decreases over time following conventional understanding
    [ ] Only exists as heat, particularly regarding down according
    [ ] Can be created from nothing according to conventional analysis
    [*] Cannot be created or destroyed — only transformed from one form to another
    Explanation: Energy is conserved in all processes. It can change forms (kinetic to potential, chemical to thermal) but the total energy of an isolated system remains constant. A ball at the top of a hill has potential energy that converts to kinetic energy as it rolls down.

  Q2. Kinetic energy is the energy of:
    [*] Motion
    [ ] Position
    [ ] Chemical bonds
    [ ] Nuclear reactions
    Explanation: Kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²) is the energy an object has because it is moving. The faster an object moves and the greater its mass, the more kinetic energy it has. Gas particles have kinetic energy as they zoom around.

  Q3. Potential energy is stored energy based on:
    [ ] Speed and acceleration
    [*] Position or chemical composition
    [ ] Temperature only
    [ ] Friction alone
    Explanation: Potential energy is stored energy. Gravitational PE depends on height and mass (PE = mgh). Chemical PE is stored in bonds — food, fuel, and batteries all contain chemical potential energy waiting to be released.

  Q4. When a substance is heated, which type of energy increases?
    [ ] Nuclear energy
    [ ] Electrical resistance
    [*] Kinetic energy of its particles
    [ ] Gravitational potential energy
    Explanation: Heating adds thermal energy to a substance, making its particles move faster. This increases the average kinetic energy of the particles. Temperature is a direct measure of this average particle kinetic energy.

  Q5. An endothermic process:
    [ ] Destroys energy permanently
    [*] Absorbs energy from its surroundings
    [ ] Releases energy to its surroundings
    [ ] Creates energy from nothing
    Explanation: Endothermic processes take in energy from the surroundings (endo = in, thermic = heat). Examples include melting ice, evaporating water, and photosynthesis. The surroundings feel cooler because thermal energy is absorbed.

  Q6. An exothermic process:
    [ ] Absorbs energy from its surroundings
    [ ] Creates energy
    [ ] Destroys energy
    [*] Releases energy to its surroundings
    Explanation: Exothermic processes release energy (exo = out). Combustion, freezing, condensation, and hand warmers are exothermic. The surroundings feel warmer because energy is flowing out of the system.

  Q7. What happens to molecular kinetic energy when a substance is cooled?
    [ ] It increases
    [*] It decreases
    [ ] It stays the same
    [ ] It disappears completely
    Explanation: Cooling removes thermal energy from a substance, causing particles to slow down. This decreases their kinetic energy. At absolute zero (0 K, -273.15°C), molecular motion would theoretically reach its minimum.

  Q8. Which of the following is an example of an endothermic process?
    [ ] Burning wood
    [ ] Freezing water
    [*] Melting ice
    [ ] Condensation of steam
    Explanation: Melting ice absorbs heat from the surroundings to break intermolecular bonds in the solid structure. That's why ice in a drink makes it cold — the ice absorbs thermal energy from the liquid. Burning, freezing, and condensation all release energy (exothermic).

  Q9. Thermal energy is best described as:
    [*] The total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance
    [ ] The potential energy stored in chemical bonds
    [ ] The energy required to change a substance's state
    [ ] The energy stored due to an object's position
    Explanation: Thermal energy is the sum of kinetic energies of all particles in a substance. It depends on both temperature (average KE per particle) and the amount of substance. A bathtub of warm water has more thermal energy than a cup of hot water, even though the cup is at a higher temperature.

  Q10. Heat always flows naturally from:
    [ ] A cooler object to a warmer object
    [ ] A larger object to a smaller object
    [*] A warmer object to a cooler object
    [ ] A solid to a liquid
    Explanation: The Second Law of Thermodynamics tells us heat flows spontaneously from high temperature to low temperature until thermal equilibrium is reached. A hot mug of coffee cools down because thermal energy transfers to the cooler surrounding air.

  Q11. What does 'kinetic energy' mean?
    [ ] Energy stored in the nucleus of an atom
    [ ] Heat energy that flows between objects
    [*] The energy an object possesses due to its motion, calculated as KE = ½mv²
    [ ] The energy used to break chemical bonds
    Explanation: Kinetic energy depends on mass (m) and velocity (v). Doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy (since v is squared). In chemistry, particle kinetic energy determines temperature — hotter substances have faster-moving particles.

  Q12. What does 'potential energy' mean?
    [ ] Energy that can only exist as light as predicted by standard models
    [ ] The energy of particles in random motion as typically measured in practice
    [ ] The total energy in any closed system as typically measured in practice
    [*] Energy stored in an object due to its position, condition, or chemical composition — it has the potential to do work
    Explanation: Potential energy is 'stored' energy. Chemical PE is stored in molecular bonds (food, fuel). Gravitational PE depends on height (PE = mgh). Elastic PE is stored in stretched or compressed objects. All can convert to kinetic energy when released.

  Q13. What does 'thermal energy' mean?
    [ ] The energy needed to change a substance from liquid to gas
    [*] The total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance, which increases with temperature and amount of matter
    [ ] The energy stored in chemical bonds of a substance
    [ ] The energy released during a nuclear reaction
    Explanation: Thermal energy = sum of all particle kinetic energies in a system. It depends on three factors: temperature (average KE), mass (number of particles), and state of matter. It's different from temperature — a swimming pool at 25°C has far more thermal energy than a cup of tea at 80°C.

  Q14. What does 'endothermic' mean?
    [ ] A process that occurs without any energy change
    [ ] A process that only happens in living organisms
    [*] A process that absorbs heat energy from its surroundings, causing the surroundings to cool down
    [ ] A process that releases heat to the surroundings
    Explanation: Endo = in, thermic = heat. Endothermic processes take in thermal energy from the surroundings. Examples: melting, evaporation, photosynthesis, dissolving ammonium nitrate (cold packs). On an energy diagram, products are at a higher energy level than reactants.

  Q15. What does 'exothermic' mean?
    [*] A process that releases heat energy to its surroundings, causing the surroundings to warm up
    [ ] A process that can only occur at very low temperatures
    [ ] A process where no energy transfer occurs
    [ ] A process that absorbs heat from the environment
    Explanation: Exo = out, thermic = heat. Exothermic processes release thermal energy to the surroundings. Examples: combustion, freezing, condensation, neutralization reactions, hand warmers. On an energy diagram, products are at a lower energy level than reactants.

  Q16. What does 'temperature' measure?
    [ ] The total amount of matter in a substance
    [ ] The potential energy stored in chemical bonds
    [ ] The total energy in a closed system
    [*] The average kinetic energy of particles in a substance
    Explanation: Temperature measures the AVERAGE kinetic energy per particle, not the total. Two substances at the same temperature have particles with the same average KE, regardless of how much substance there is. Common scales: Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin.

  Q17. What does 'heat' mean in chemistry?
    [ ] A measure of how hot a substance feels
    [*] The transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures
    [ ] The total potential energy in a system
    [ ] The temperature of an object
    Explanation: Heat (q) is energy in transit — it flows from a warmer object to a cooler one. Heat is measured in joules (J) or calories (cal). It is NOT the same as temperature: heat is energy transferred, temperature is average kinetic energy.

  Q18. What does 'chemical energy' mean?
    [ ] Energy produced only by burning fossil fuels
    [ ] The kinetic energy of electrons orbiting the nucleus
    [ ] The speed at which a chemical reaction occurs
    [*] Potential energy stored in the bonds between atoms — released or absorbed during chemical reactions
    Explanation: Chemical energy is a form of potential energy stored in the arrangement of atoms and their bonds. When bonds break and new ones form during a reaction, energy is either released (exothermic) or absorbed (endothermic). Food, gasoline, and batteries are stores of chemical energy.

  Q19. What does 'specific heat capacity' mean?
    [ ] The maximum temperature a substance can reach
    [ ] The rate at which a substance releases heat
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance changes state
    [*] The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1°C
    Explanation: Specific heat capacity (c) varies by substance. Water has a high specific heat (4.18 J/g°C), meaning it takes a lot of energy to change its temperature. Metals have low specific heat — they heat up and cool down quickly. The formula is q = mcΔT.

  Q20. What does 'Law of Conservation of Energy' state?
    [ ] Energy is always lost as heat in every process
    [*] Energy cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system — it can only change forms, and the total energy remains constant
    [ ] Energy can be created by chemical reactions
    [ ] Only kinetic energy is conserved, not potential energy
    Explanation: Also called the First Law of Thermodynamics, this principle means the total energy of the universe is constant. In any process, energy may convert between forms (chemical → thermal → light), but the total amount never changes.

  Q21. A student places a metal spoon in a cup of hot soup. After one minute, the handle of the spoon feels hot. Describe the energy transfers that occurred.
    [*] Thermal energy transferred from the hot soup to the cool spoon by conduction — faster-moving soup particles collided with slower spoon particles
    [ ] The spoon absorbed light energy from the room
    [ ] The soup lost all its thermal energy to the spoon
    [ ] The spoon generated its own heat from chemical energy in the metal
    Explanation: Heat flows from hot to cold. The soup's faster-moving particles transfer kinetic energy to the metal through direct contact (conduction). The vibrating metal atoms pass energy along the handle. The soup cools slightly while the spoon warms — but total energy is conserved.

  Q22. A cold pack used for sports injuries contains ammonium nitrate and water in separate compartments. When squeezed, they mix and the pack becomes very cold. Is this endothermic or exothermic, and how does energy flow?
    [ ] Exothermic — the chemicals release cold energy
    [ ] Exothermic — the pack feels cold because it releases all its energy quickly
    [*] Endothermic — dissolving ammonium nitrate absorbs thermal energy from the water and surroundings, which is why it feels cold
    [ ] Neither — the pack just redistributes existing cold
    Explanation: When NH₄NO₃ dissolves, it absorbs heat from the water (endothermic). The water cools down, and since the pack is against your skin, it absorbs thermal energy from your body too — creating the cooling sensation. Energy isn't destroyed; it's absorbed into the dissolving process.

  Q23. A roller coaster car is at rest at the top of the first hill. As it descends, it speeds up, then slows as it climbs the next hill. Explain the energy transformations using conservation of energy.
    [*] At the top: maximum gravitational PE, minimum KE. Descending: PE converts to KE. Climbing: KE converts back to PE. Total energy is conserved (minus friction losses as heat)
    [ ] Gravity provides constant energy that increases at every hill
    [ ] The car uses chemical energy from its engine for the entire ride
    [ ] The car creates energy as it goes downhill and destroys it going uphill
    Explanation: At the top, all energy is gravitational PE (mgh). As the car drops, PE decreases and KE increases (½mv²). At the bottom, KE is maximum. Going up the next hill, KE converts back to PE. Some energy is lost to friction (heat/sound), which is why each subsequent hill must be shorter.

  Q24. Two objects at the same temperature — a wooden block and a metal block — are placed in a freezer. The metal block feels colder to the touch immediately. Does the metal actually become colder faster, and why?
    [ ] No — both are at the same temperature, so they feel the same
    [ ] Yes — metal generates cold energy that wood cannot
    [ ] No — the metal just looks colder because of its shiny surface
    [*] Yes — metal has higher thermal conductivity, so it transfers heat away from your hand more quickly, making it feel colder sooner
    Explanation: Both blocks ARE at the same temperature, but metal conducts heat much faster than wood. When you touch the metal, thermal energy flows rapidly from your warm hand into the cold metal (high conductivity), making your hand cool quickly. Wood insulates, so heat transfers slowly — it 'feels' warmer even at the same temperature.

  Q25. A candle burns in a sealed, insulated room. After several hours, a thermometer shows the room temperature has increased by 2°C. Where did the energy come from?
    [ ] The room absorbed energy from outside through the insulation
    [ ] The oxygen in the room spontaneously released thermal energy
    [ ] The flame created new energy from nothing
    [*] The chemical potential energy stored in the candle wax was converted to thermal energy (heat and light) through combustion
    Explanation: Candle wax (hydrocarbon) stores chemical potential energy in its molecular bonds. During combustion (wax + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O), this energy is released as heat and light, warming the room. The energy was always there — stored in chemical bonds — and was transformed, not created.

  Q26. A student heats 100 g of water and 100 g of iron (same starting temperature) with identical burners for 2 minutes. The iron's temperature increases much more. What property explains this difference?
    [ ] The iron burner produces more heat than the water burner
    [ ] Iron is magnetic, which makes it absorb heat better
    [*] Specific heat capacity — water's is much higher (4.18 J/g°C) than iron's (0.45 J/g°C), so water needs more energy per degree of temperature change
    [ ] Water evaporates, so it cannot be heated
    Explanation: Using q = mcΔT, if both receive the same energy (q), water's high specific heat (c = 4.18) means a smaller ΔT, while iron's low c (0.45) means a larger ΔT. Water needs about 9× more energy per gram per degree than iron. This is why coastal areas have milder temperatures than inland areas.

  Q27. A car traveling at 60 mph suddenly brakes to a stop. The brake pads become extremely hot. Explain the energy transformation using the Law of Conservation of Energy.
    [*] The car's kinetic energy was converted to thermal energy (heat) through friction between the brake pads and rotors — energy was conserved but changed forms
    [ ] The kinetic energy was absorbed by the road surface
    [ ] The car's energy was destroyed by the brakes
    [ ] The brake pads generated new energy from their chemical bonds
    Explanation: A moving car has kinetic energy (½mv²). When braking, friction converts KE into thermal energy — the brake pads, rotors, and surrounding air heat up. The total energy is conserved: all KE ends up as heat. This is why brakes get hot during heavy use, and why regenerative braking in EVs captures this energy.

  Q28. Photosynthesis in plants absorbs sunlight to convert CO₂ and H₂O into glucose and O₂. Cellular respiration in animals reverses this, breaking down glucose. Classify both processes and explain the energy flow.
    [ ] Photosynthesis is exothermic and respiration is endothermic in most standard conditions
    [*] Photosynthesis is endothermic (absorbs light energy, stores it as chemical PE in glucose); respiration is exothermic (releases stored chemical energy as thermal energy and ATP)
    [ ] Both are exothermic because they both involve energy, particularly regarding into within the typical range of values
    [ ] Both are endothermic because living things always absorb energy as typically measured in practice
    Explanation: Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (endothermic — light energy is stored as chemical PE in glucose bonds). Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy (exothermic — chemical PE is released as heat and ATP). Together, they cycle energy.

  Q29. A thermos keeps hot drinks hot and cold drinks cold. A student says, 'The thermos adds heat to hot drinks and removes heat from cold drinks.' Is this correct?
    [*] No — the thermos simply minimizes heat transfer between the drink and the environment through insulation (vacuum, reflective walls); it doesn't add or remove energy
    [ ] Yes — the vacuum inside the thermos generates heat
    [ ] Yes — the thermos has a built-in heating and cooling mechanism
    [ ] No — the thermos works by changing the specific heat of the liquid inside
    Explanation: A thermos works by reducing all three modes of heat transfer: conduction (vacuum eliminates contact), convection (sealed lid prevents air flow), and radiation (reflective inner surface). It doesn't add or remove energy — it simply slows energy exchange, keeping the drink at its original temperature longer.

  Q30. During a fireworks display, chemical pellets are ignited and explode with bright colors and loud sounds high in the air. Trace the energy transformations from the stored chemicals to the final display.
    [*] Chemical PE in the powder → thermal energy (ignition) → kinetic energy (launch upward) → gravitational PE (at peak) → thermal, light, and sound energy (explosion) — all conserved
    [ ] The gunpowder creates energy from nothing when ignited when considering all relevant factors
    [ ] Energy is destroyed when the firework explodes, particularly regarding throughout when considering all relevant factors
    [ ] The firework only converts chemical energy to light — no other forms in the context of this analysis
    Explanation: The full energy chain: chemical PE (stored in gunpowder and metal salts) → thermal energy (fuse ignition) → rapid gas expansion gives KE (launches shell upward) → KE converts to gravitational PE at peak → burst charge converts remaining chemical PE to thermal, light (colors from metal ions), and sound energy. Total energy is conserved throughout.

--- Lesson 1.9: Law of Conservation of Mass (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Law of Conservation of Mass
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
If 10 grams of reactants undergo a chemical reaction in a sealed container, what is the total mass of the products?
In a closed system, the total mass of the products must equal the total mass of the reactants. Since the reactants had a mass of 10 grams, the products must also total 10 grams.
If 5 grams of iron reacts with 3 grams of sulfur in a closed container, what is the mass of iron sulfide produced?
By the law of conservation of mass, 5 g of iron + 3 g of sulfur = 8 g of iron sulfide. The total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants.
In a balanced chemical equation, what is equal on both sides?
A balanced chemical equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both the reactant and product sides, which reflects the conservation of mass.
What is the law of conservation of mass?
The law of conservation of mass states that in any chemical reaction, the total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products — mass is neither created nor destroyed.
What is a closed system in chemistry?
A closed system is one where no matter can enter or leave, though energy can still be transferred. This is important for demonstrating conservation of mass.
What is a reactant?
A reactant is a starting material that is consumed or transformed during a chemical reaction. Reactants appear on the left side of a chemical equation.
What is a product in a chemical reaction?
A product is a substance that is formed during a chemical reaction. Products appear on the right side of a chemical equation, after the arrow.
What is an open system?
An open system allows matter (and energy) to be exchanged with its surroundings. A campfire is an example — gases escape freely into the air.
What is a chemical equation?
A chemical equation uses chemical symbols and formulas to show the reactants and products in a chemical reaction, with an arrow pointing from reactants to products.
What is a balanced chemical equation?
A balanced chemical equation has the same number of atoms for each element on both the reactant and product sides, reflecting the law of conservation of mass.

--- Lesson 1.10: Elements, Compounds, & Mixtures (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Elements, Compounds, & Mixtures
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is an element made up of?
An element is a pure substance made up of only one type of atom. It cannot be broken down into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means.
What is the key difference between a compound and a mixture?
In a compound, elements are chemically bonded in a fixed ratio and can only be separated by chemical means. In a mixture, substances are physically combined and can be separated by physical methods.
What is an element?
An element is a pure substance consisting of only one type of atom. Elements cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.
What is a compound?
A compound is a pure substance made of two or more different elements that are chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio, such as water (H2O) or carbon dioxide (CO2).
What is a mixture?
A mixture is a combination of two or more substances that are physically combined but not chemically bonded. Each substance retains its own properties.
What is a homogeneous mixture?
A homogeneous mixture has a uniform composition throughout — you cannot distinguish the individual components. Saltwater and air are common examples.
What is a heterogeneous mixture?
A heterogeneous mixture is not uniform — you can see or identify the different components. Trail mix and pizza are everyday examples.
What is a pure substance?
A pure substance has a definite, constant composition. Elements and compounds are both pure substances — elements have one type of atom, compounds have atoms bonded in a fixed ratio.
What is a solution?
A solution is a type of homogeneous mixture in which one substance (the solute) is evenly dissolved in another substance (the solvent).
A student says that air is an element because it is invisible. What is wrong with this reasoning?
Air is a homogeneous mixture of gases, primarily nitrogen and oxygen, along with small amounts of argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases. Being invisible does not make something an element.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an example of a compound?
Table salt (NaCl) is a compound because it is made of two different elements — sodium and chlorine — chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio.
Example: Which of the following is an example of an element?
Aluminum foil is made of the element aluminum (Al), which consists of only one type of atom. Carbon dioxide and baking soda are compounds; lemonade is a mixture.

--- Lesson 1.11: Separation Techniques (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Separation Techniques
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is paper chromatography used to separate?
Paper chromatography separates the different colored dyes or pigments in a mixture based on how well each component travels through the paper with a solvent.
What is filtration?
Filtration is a separation technique that uses a porous barrier (like filter paper) to trap solid particles while allowing the liquid portion to pass through.
What is evaporation as a separation technique?
Evaporation is a technique where a liquid is heated until it becomes vapor, leaving any dissolved solids behind. It is commonly used to recover salt from saltwater.
What is distillation?
Distillation is a process that separates a liquid mixture by heating it so one liquid evaporates first, then cooling the vapor so it condenses back into a purified liquid.
What is chromatography?
Chromatography separates components of a mixture based on how quickly each component moves through a stationary medium (like paper) when carried by a solvent.
What is decanting?
Decanting is the simple process of carefully pouring a liquid off the top of a container, leaving the heavier sediment or solid behind at the bottom.
What is a solvent?
A solvent is the substance (usually a liquid) that dissolves another substance. Water is often called the 'universal solvent' because it dissolves many different substances.

--- Lesson 1.12: Classifying Chemical Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Classifying Chemical Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a synthesis reaction?
A synthesis reaction (also called a combination reaction) occurs when two or more substances combine to form a single new product. Example: A + B -> AB.
What is a decomposition reaction?
A decomposition reaction occurs when a single compound is broken down into two or more simpler substances. It is the reverse of a synthesis reaction.
What is a single replacement reaction?
In a single replacement reaction, one element takes the place of another element in a compound. The more reactive element pushes out the less reactive one.
What is a double replacement reaction?
In a double replacement reaction, the positive ions of two compounds switch places, producing two new compounds. The general form is AB + CD -> AD + CB.
What is a combustion reaction?
A combustion reaction occurs when a substance reacts with oxygen, releasing energy as heat and often light. Burning fuels like wood, gasoline, or natural gas are examples.
Real-World Applications
Example: Burning methane gas (CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O) is an example of which reaction type?
Methane reacts with oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water, releasing heat and light. This is a classic combustion reaction.

--- Lesson 1.13: Temperature vs. Heat (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Temperature vs. Heat
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is heat?
Heat is the transfer of thermal energy from a warmer object to a cooler object. It is not the same as temperature — heat involves energy movement between objects.
What is the unit used to measure heat energy?
Heat is measured in joules (the SI unit of energy) or calories. Temperature is measured in degrees (Celsius, Fahrenheit, or Kelvin), but heat is measured in energy units.
What is thermal energy?
Thermal energy is the total kinetic energy of all the particles in a substance. It depends on both the temperature and the amount of matter present.
What is kinetic energy?
Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. All moving particles have kinetic energy, and the faster they move, the more kinetic energy they possess.
What is the Kelvin scale?
The Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero (0 K), the lowest possible temperature where particle motion theoretically stops. It is widely used in scientific calculations.
What is a conductor of heat?
A conductor is a material that allows heat to transfer through it quickly and easily. Metals like copper and aluminum are good conductors of heat.
What is an insulator?
An insulator is a material that does not transfer heat easily. Wood, plastic, and rubber are good insulators, which is why pot handles are often made from these materials.

--- Lesson 1.14: Thermal Equilibrium (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Thermal Equilibrium
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
At thermal equilibrium, what is true about the temperatures of two objects in contact?
At thermal equilibrium, both objects have reached the same temperature. There is no longer a net flow of heat between them.
What is thermal equilibrium?
Thermal equilibrium is the state reached when two objects in contact no longer have a net transfer of heat between them because they have reached the same temperature.
What is conduction?
Conduction is the transfer of heat through direct particle-to-particle contact. When you touch a hot stove, heat transfers to your hand through conduction.
What is convection?
Convection is heat transfer through the bulk movement of fluids (liquids or gases). Warm fluid rises and cool fluid sinks, creating a circular current.
What is radiation as a method of heat transfer?
Radiation is the transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves, such as infrared radiation. It does not require a medium — this is how the Sun's heat reaches Earth through space.
What is the zeroth law of thermodynamics?
The zeroth law states that if object A is in thermal equilibrium with object C, and object B is also in thermal equilibrium with object C, then A and B are in equilibrium with each other.
Two sealed containers of water are placed touching each other. Container A is at 90 degrees C and Container B is at 30 degrees C. After several hours, both are at 60 degrees C. A student says 'the cold was transferred from B to A.' What is wrong with this statement?
'Cold' is not a substance or form of energy. Only heat (thermal energy) can be transferred. Heat flowed from the warmer container A to the cooler container B until they reached 60 degrees C.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which scenario is an example of thermal equilibrium?
The book and the room are at the same temperature, meaning there is no net heat transfer between them. This is thermal equilibrium.

--- Lesson 1.15: Kinetic Theory of Matter (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Kinetic Theory of Matter
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the kinetic theory of matter?
The kinetic theory of matter states that all matter is made of particles that are in constant motion. The speed of the particles and the forces between them determine the state of matter.
What is kinetic energy in the context of particle motion?
Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. Every moving particle has kinetic energy, and faster-moving particles have more kinetic energy.
What is Brownian motion?
Brownian motion is the random, zigzag movement of small particles (like pollen in water) caused by constant bombardment from surrounding molecules. It provides evidence for the kinetic theory.
A scientist observes tiny pollen grains jerking around randomly when viewed under a microscope in water. What is this evidence of?
This is Brownian motion. The invisible water molecules are in constant motion and collide with the visible pollen grains, pushing them in random zigzag patterns.

--- Lesson 1.16: Gas Behavior & Pressure (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Gas Behavior & Pressure
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is atmospheric pressure?
Atmospheric pressure is the force exerted on surfaces by the weight of the air in Earth's atmosphere above them. At sea level, it is approximately 101,325 Pascals (1 atm).
What is the standard unit of pressure in the SI system?
The Pascal (Pa) is the SI unit of pressure. One atmosphere of pressure equals approximately 101,325 Pascals.
What is gas pressure?
Gas pressure is the force per unit area exerted by gas particles as they collide with the surfaces around them. It is measured in Pascals, atmospheres, or other pressure units.
What is Boyle's law?
Boyle's law states that for a given amount of gas at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional — if one doubles, the other halves.
What is Charles's law?
Charles's law states that at constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature. When temperature goes up, volume goes up proportionally.
What is a barometer?
A barometer is an instrument that measures atmospheric pressure. Changes in barometric pressure help predict weather — falling pressure often signals approaching storms.
What is an atmosphere (atm) as a unit?
One atmosphere (1 atm) is a unit of pressure defined as the average atmospheric pressure at sea level, equal to approximately 101,325 Pascals.

--- Lesson 1.17: Sublimation & Deposition (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Sublimation & Deposition
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is sublimation?
Sublimation is the phase change where a solid transforms directly into a gas without first becoming a liquid. Dry ice (solid CO2) is a common example.
What is deposition?
Deposition is the reverse of sublimation — a gas changes directly into a solid without first becoming a liquid. Frost forming on a cold window is a common example.
What is a phase change?
A phase change is a physical change where a substance transitions between states of matter — such as solid, liquid, or gas — without changing its chemical identity.
What is dry ice?
Dry ice is solid carbon dioxide (CO2). It is called 'dry' because it sublimes directly into gas at -78.5 degrees C under normal pressure, leaving no liquid behind.
What is a 'phase diagram'?
A phase diagram is a graph that shows which state of matter (solid, liquid, or gas) a substance exists in at various combinations of temperature and pressure.
A student notices that snow slowly disappears from the ground on a cold, sunny, dry day even though the temperature stays below 0 degrees C. What is happening?
On a cold, dry, sunny day, snow can sublime — the ice changes directly to water vapor without melting. Dry air and sunlight provide the energy for sublimation even below freezing.
On Mars, the polar ice caps are made of frozen CO2. During the Martian summer, parts of these caps shrink. What is most likely happening?
Mars has very low atmospheric pressure, so solid CO2 sublimes directly into gas rather than melting into liquid. This is why the polar caps shrink in summer without forming liquid.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an everyday example of sublimation?
Dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) sublimes at room temperature — it goes directly from a solid to a gas, creating a foggy effect without ever forming a liquid puddle.
Example: Frost forming on a cold window on a winter morning is an example of which phase change?
Frost forms when water vapor in the air changes directly into ice crystals on a cold surface — gas to solid. This is deposition, the reverse of sublimation.
Example: Snowflakes forming in clouds are an example of which phase change?
Snowflakes can form by deposition — water vapor in clouds changes directly into solid ice crystals without first becoming liquid water. This creates the intricate crystal patterns.

--- Lesson 1.18: Plasma: The Fourth State (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Plasma: The Fourth State
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is plasma?
Plasma is the fourth state of matter, consisting of ionized gas — atoms that have lost or gained electrons. It forms when gas is heated to extremely high temperatures.
What is the main component of stars like our Sun?
Stars are made primarily of plasma. The extreme temperatures in stars (millions of degrees) ionize hydrogen and helium atoms, creating plasma.
What is ionization?
Ionization is the process where atoms gain or lose electrons, becoming electrically charged particles called ions. This is what happens to gas atoms when plasma forms.
What is an ion?
An ion is an atom or molecule that has gained or lost one or more electrons, giving it a positive or negative electrical charge. Plasma is full of ions and free electrons.
What is an electron?
An electron is a negatively charged subatomic particle that orbits the nucleus of an atom. In plasma, electrons are stripped from atoms and move freely.
What is a 'plasma ball' (the decorative science gadget)?
A plasma ball is a sealed glass sphere filled with noble gases. A high-voltage electrode at the center ionizes the gas, creating colorful tendrils of plasma.
A student asks: 'If a candle flame contains plasma, why can I put my hand near it without getting shocked?' What is the best explanation?
A candle flame does contain a small amount of weakly ionized plasma, but the degree of ionization is very low and the energy is small, so there is no noticeable electric shock.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is a natural example of plasma on Earth?
Lightning is natural plasma on Earth. The extreme heat of a lightning bolt ionizes the air, creating a channel of plasma that glows brightly.
Example: Which of these is NOT an example of plasma?
Water vapor (steam) is simply water in the gas state — its molecules are not ionized. Neon signs, lightning, and the Sun's surface all involve ionized gas, which is plasma.
Example: A student wonders why plasma TVs can display such vivid colors. How does a plasma TV produce its image?
Plasma TVs contain millions of tiny cells filled with gas. Electricity ionizes the gas into plasma, which emits UV light that strikes phosphor coatings to produce red, green, and blue colors.

--- Lesson 1.19: Chromatography (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Chromatography
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is chromatography used for?
Chromatography is a technique used to separate the individual components of a mixture based on how they interact with a stationary phase and a mobile phase.
What is the stationary phase in paper chromatography?
In paper chromatography, the paper is the stationary phase — it stays in place while the solvent (mobile phase) moves through it, carrying components at different rates.
What is the mobile phase in paper chromatography?
The mobile phase is the solvent (often water or alcohol) that moves up through the paper by capillary action, carrying dissolved substances with it at different rates.
What is the Rf value in chromatography?
The Rf (retention factor) value equals the distance traveled by a substance divided by the distance traveled by the solvent front. It is always a number between 0 and 1.
What is a 'stationary phase'?
The stationary phase is the part of the chromatography system that does not move. In paper chromatography, the paper is the stationary phase.
What is a 'mobile phase'?
The mobile phase is the solvent that moves through the stationary phase, carrying dissolved mixture components with it. Different components travel at different rates.
What is a 'chromatogram'?
A chromatogram is the visual result of a chromatography experiment — the paper (or other medium) showing the pattern of separated spots or bands from the original mixture.
A student places a spot of ink at the bottom of chromatography paper and dips the paper into water. After 15 minutes, one dye has traveled 6 cm and the solvent front traveled 10 cm. What is the Rf value of that dye?
Rf = distance traveled by component / distance traveled by solvent front = 6 cm / 10 cm = 0.60. This Rf value can help identify the dye by comparison with known values.
Real-World Applications
Example: A leaf is crushed and its pigments are separated by chromatography. Multiple colored bands appear: green, yellow, and orange. What does this tell you about leaves?
Leaves contain multiple pigments including chlorophyll (green), carotenoids (orange), and xanthophylls (yellow). Chromatography reveals these hidden pigments that are normally masked by the dominant green.

--- Lesson 1.20: Distillation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Distillation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the basic principle behind distillation?
Distillation separates mixtures by heating them so that the substance with the lower boiling point evaporates first, then condensing the vapor back into a purified liquid.
What is the purpose of the condenser in a distillation setup?
The condenser cools the hot vapor so it changes back into a liquid (condensation). It typically uses cold water flowing around the tube to absorb heat from the vapor.
What is the liquid collected at the end of a distillation process called?
The purified liquid collected after distillation is called the distillate. It is the substance that evaporated first and was then condensed back into liquid form.
What is fractional distillation used for?
Fractional distillation uses a fractionating column to separate liquids with similar boiling points more effectively. The column provides multiple evaporation-condensation cycles.
What is distillation?
Distillation is a separation technique that heats a mixture to evaporate the component with the lower boiling point, then cools the vapor to condense it back into a purified liquid.
What is a 'distillate'?
The distillate is the purified liquid that is collected at the end of the distillation process. It is the substance that evaporated and was then condensed back into liquid form.
What is a 'condenser' in a distillation setup?
A condenser is a tube (often with cold water flowing around it) that cools hot vapor, causing it to condense back into a liquid that can be collected.
What is 'fractional distillation'?
Fractional distillation uses a fractionating column that provides multiple condensation-evaporation cycles. This gives much better separation of liquids with similar boiling points.
What is 'crude oil'?
Crude oil is a natural mixture of hundreds of different hydrocarbons found underground. Fractional distillation at refineries separates it into useful products like gasoline and diesel.

--- Lesson 1.21: Crystallization & Filtration (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Crystallization & Filtration
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is crystallization?
Crystallization is the process where dissolved solute particles come together and form an organized solid structure (crystals) as the solution cools or the solvent evaporates.
What is filtration?
Filtration is a separation technique that uses a porous barrier (like filter paper) to trap solid particles while allowing the liquid to pass through.
What is the solid material left on the filter paper after filtration called?
The solid trapped by the filter paper is called the residue. The liquid that passes through is called the filtrate.
What is a saturated solution?
A saturated solution holds the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a specific temperature. Adding more solute will cause it to remain undissolved at the bottom.
What is a supersaturated solution?
A supersaturated solution contains more dissolved solute than is normally possible at that temperature. It is unstable and can crystallize suddenly when disturbed or when a seed crystal is added.
What is a 'seed crystal'?
A seed crystal is a small crystal introduced into a supersaturated solution to trigger crystallization. Dissolved solute particles arrange themselves around the seed, growing larger crystals.
Real-World Applications
Example: Making rock candy by growing sugar crystals on a string is an example of which process?
Rock candy is made by dissolving sugar in hot water to create a supersaturated solution. As the solution slowly cools and water evaporates, sugar crystals grow on the string.

    [*] A process where a dissolved substance forms solid crystals from a solution
    [ ] A process where a solid is broken down into a liquid by applying strong heat
    [ ] A process where two liquids are separated based on their different densities
    [ ] A process where a gas is converted directly into a solid by strong compression
    Explanation: Crystallization is the process where dissolved solute particles come together and form an organized solid structure (crystals) as the solution cools or the solvent evaporates.

  Q2. What is filtration?
    [ ] A method of heating a liquid until it completely evaporates
    [*] A method of separating a solid from a liquid using a filter
    [ ] A method of cooling a gas until it becomes a liquid substance
    [ ] A method of dissolving a solid in water to make a clear solution
    Explanation: Filtration is a separation technique that uses a porous barrier (like filter paper) to trap solid particles while allowing the liquid to pass through.

  Q3. What must happen to a solution before crystals can form?
    [ ] It must become unsaturated by adding more solvent to the mix
    [*] It must become saturated or supersaturated with dissolved solute
    [ ] It must be heated to its boiling point and kept there for an hour
    [ ] It must be placed under high pressure in a sealed metal container
    Explanation: For crystals to form, the solution must become saturated (holding the maximum dissolved solute) or supersaturated (holding more than normal). Cooling or evaporation causes excess solute to crystallize out.

  Q4. What is the solid material left on the filter paper after filtration called?
    [ ] The filtrate
    [ ] The distillate
    [*] The residue
    [ ] The solvent
    Explanation: The solid trapped by the filter paper is called the residue. The liquid that passes through is called the filtrate.

  Q5. How does cooling a hot saturated solution cause crystals to form?
    [ ] Cold water can hold more dissolved substance, so crystals dissolve
    [*] Cold water holds less dissolved substance, so excess comes out as crystals
    [ ] The cold temperature freezes the water and traps the solute in ice crystals
    [ ] Cooling causes a chemical reaction that converts the solute into solid crystals
    Explanation: Most substances are less soluble in cold water than hot water. When a hot saturated solution cools, it can no longer hold as much dissolved solute, so the excess forms solid crystals.

  Q6. What common item is used as a filter in a basic filtration setup?
    [ ] Aluminum foil
    [ ] Plastic wrap
    [*] Filter paper
    [ ] Wax paper
    Explanation: Filter paper is a porous paper specially designed to allow liquids to pass through while trapping solid particles. It is placed in a funnel during the filtration process.

  Q7. Which type of mixture can filtration separate?
    [ ] A mixture of two miscible liquids blended together
    [*] A mixture of an insoluble solid and a liquid together
    [ ] A mixture of two different gases mixed in a container
    [ ] A mixture of dissolved salt and water in a beaker now
    Explanation: Filtration works best for separating an insoluble solid from a liquid. The solid particles are too large to pass through the filter paper, while the liquid flows through.

  Q8. Making rock candy by growing sugar crystals on a string is an example of which process?
    [ ] Filtration
    [ ] Distillation
    [*] Crystallization
    [ ] Chromatography
    Explanation: Rock candy is made by dissolving sugar in hot water to create a supersaturated solution. As the solution slowly cools and water evaporates, sugar crystals grow on the string.

  Q9. Why does a funnel hold the filter paper during filtration?
    [ ] The funnel adds chemicals that help separate the solid from the liquid
    [*] The funnel supports the paper and directs the filtrate into a container
    [ ] The funnel heats the mixture so the liquid evaporates more efficiently
    [ ] The funnel makes the solid particles smaller so they pass through the paper
    Explanation: The funnel provides physical support for the filter paper and channels the liquid filtrate downward into the collection container below.

  Q10. Which factor most affects the size of crystals that form during crystallization?
    [ ] The color of the solute being crystallized from the solution
    [*] The speed at which the solution cools down during the process
    [ ] The type of container used to hold the solution while cooling
    [ ] The room lighting present during the crystallization experiment
    Explanation: Slow cooling allows more time for solute particles to arrange in an orderly pattern, producing larger crystals. Rapid cooling creates many small crystals because particles have less time to organize.

  Q11. What is a saturated solution?
    [*] A solution that contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a given temperature
    [ ] A solution that has no solute dissolved in it and is made only of pure solvent liquid
    [ ] A solution that has been heated to its boiling point and is actively producing vapor
    [ ] A solution that contains two or more different solvents mixed together in one container
    Explanation: A saturated solution holds the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a specific temperature. Adding more solute will cause it to remain undissolved at the bottom.

  Q12. What is a supersaturated solution?
    [*] A solution containing more dissolved solute than it normally can hold at that temperature
    [ ] A solution that has been diluted with extra water until it is very weak in concentration
    [ ] A solution that has been frozen solid and no longer contains any liquid solvent at all
    [ ] A solution that contains exactly half the maximum amount of solute it could possibly hold
    Explanation: A supersaturated solution contains more dissolved solute than is normally possible at that temperature. It is unstable and can crystallize suddenly when disturbed or when a seed crystal is added.

  Q13. What does 'solute' mean?
    [*] The substance that is dissolved in a solution
    [ ] The liquid that does the dissolving in a solution
    [ ] The solid left behind after filtration is complete
    [ ] The gas produced when a solution is heated up high
    Explanation: The solute is the substance that dissolves in a solvent to form a solution. For example, in saltwater, salt is the solute and water is the solvent.

  Q14. What does 'insoluble' mean?
    [ ] Able to dissolve easily in a particular solvent to form a clear solution
    [*] Unable to dissolve in a particular solvent and remaining as a separate solid
    [ ] Able to melt when heated to a temperature above its normal melting point
    [ ] Unable to be filtered because the particles are too small for filter paper
    Explanation: An insoluble substance does not dissolve in a given solvent. Sand is insoluble in water, which is why filtration can separate sand from water.

  Q15. What is a 'seed crystal'?
    [*] A small crystal added to a solution to encourage more crystals to grow around it
    [ ] The first liquid drop that passes through filter paper during a filtration process
    [ ] A type of mineral found in soil that plants use as nutrients for their growth
    [ ] A chemical substance added to a solution to help dissolve more solute in the mix
    Explanation: A seed crystal is a small crystal introduced into a supersaturated solution to trigger crystallization. Dissolved solute particles arrange themselves around the seed, growing larger crystals.

  Q16. What does 'solubility' mean?
    [*] The maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature
    [ ] The speed at which a solid substance dissolves when placed into a liquid solvent at room temperature
    [ ] The ability of a filter to trap solid particles and prevent them from passing through the paper
    [ ] The temperature at which a substance changes from a solid state into a liquid state of matter
    Explanation: Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a specific amount of solvent at a given temperature. It usually increases with temperature for solid solutes.

  Q17. What does 'filtrate' mean?
    [ ] The solid material that remains trapped on the filter paper surface
    [*] The liquid that passes through the filter during the filtration process
    [ ] The funnel that holds the filter paper during the filtration experiment
    [ ] The crystals that form when a saturated solution is cooled down slowly
    Explanation: The filtrate is the liquid that successfully passes through the filter paper during filtration. It should be free of the insoluble solid particles that were trapped.

  Q18. What does 'residue' mean in filtration?
    [ ] The clear liquid collected beneath the filter funnel in the flask
    [ ] The filter paper itself that is used in the separation procedure
    [*] The solid material left behind on the filter after the liquid passes
    [ ] The solvent used to dissolve the solute before performing filtration
    Explanation: The residue is the solid that remains on the filter paper after filtration. It consists of the insoluble particles that were too large to pass through the pores.
