--- Lesson 3.1:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Electrons, Protons & Neutrons

Core Definitions

  • Proton: A positively charged subatomic particle found in the nucleus; defines the element (atomic number).
  • Neutron: A neutral subatomic particle found in the nucleus; contributes to atomic mass.
  • Electron: A negatively charged particle orbiting the nucleus in energy levels; very small mass.
  • Atomic Number: The number of protons in an atom; identifies the element.
  • Mass Number: The total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.
  • Charge Balance: In a neutral atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons.

--- Lesson 3.1:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What subatomic particle determines the identity of an element?
    [*] Proton
    [ ] Neutron
    [ ] Electron
    [ ] Photon
    Explanation: The number of protons (atomic number) defines which element an atom is. Changing the proton count changes the element entirely.

  Q2. What is the charge of a neutron?
    [*] No charge (neutral)
    [ ] Positive (+1)
    [ ] Negative (−1)
    [ ] It varies by element
    Explanation: Neutrons carry zero electric charge. Their name literally comes from 'neutral.' They add mass to the nucleus without affecting charge.

  Q3. Where are protons and neutrons located in an atom?
    [*] In the nucleus
    [ ] In the electron cloud
    [ ] Orbiting the nucleus
    [ ] Between atoms
    Explanation: Protons and neutrons are packed tightly in the nucleus, the dense central core of the atom. Electrons orbit outside the nucleus.

  Q4. What is the charge of an electron?
    [*] Negative (−1)
    [ ] Positive (+1)
    [ ] Neutral (0)
    [ ] It depends on the element
    Explanation: Each electron carries a charge of −1. This negative charge balances the positive charge of protons in a neutral atom.

  Q5. How is an atom electrically neutral?
    [*] It has equal numbers of protons and electrons
    [ ] It has no charged particles
    [ ] Neutrons cancel all charges
    [ ] Electrons have no charge
    Explanation: In a neutral atom the number of protons (+) equals the number of electrons (−), so the charges cancel to zero.

  Q6. The mass number of an atom equals:
    [*] Protons + neutrons
    [ ] Protons only
    [ ] Protons + electrons
    [ ] Neutrons only
    Explanation: Mass number (A) = number of protons (Z) + number of neutrons (N). Electrons contribute negligible mass.

  Q7. How does the mass of an electron compare to a proton?
    [*] About 1/1836 the mass of a proton
    [ ] Equal to a proton
    [ ] Twice a proton's mass
    [ ] About half a proton's mass
    Explanation: An electron's mass is roughly 1/1836 that of a proton, which is why electrons contribute almost nothing to an atom's total mass.

  Q8. Which particle was discovered first?
    [*] Electron (J.J. Thomson, 1897)
    [ ] Proton (Rutherford, 1920)
    [ ] Neutron (Chadwick, 1932)
    [ ] They were all discovered simultaneously
    Explanation: J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897 using cathode ray tubes. The proton was identified by Rutherford in 1920, and the neutron by Chadwick in 1932.

  Q9. If an atom has 11 protons and 12 neutrons, what is its mass number?
    [*] 23
    [ ] 11
    [ ] 12
    [ ] 1
    Explanation: Mass number = protons + neutrons = 11 + 12 = 23. This atom is sodium-23.

  Q10. What happens to an atom if you change its number of protons?
    [*] It becomes a different element
    [ ] It becomes an ion
    [ ] It becomes an isotope
    [ ] Nothing changes
    Explanation: The atomic number (proton count) defines the element. Changing the number of protons transforms it into an entirely different element.

  Q11. What is the definition of atomic number?
    [*] The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom
    [ ] The total number of protons and neutrons
    [ ] The number of electrons in the outer shell
    [ ] The atom's position in the periodic table by mass
    Explanation: Atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in an atom's nucleus. It uniquely identifies the element and determines its place on the periodic table.

  Q12. What does the term 'mass number' refer to?
    [*] The sum of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
    [ ] The atomic mass in grams
    [ ] The number of electrons
    [ ] The weight of the nucleus in kilograms
    Explanation: Mass number (A) is the total count of protons plus neutrons (nucleons) in the nucleus. It is always a whole number, unlike atomic mass.

  Q13. What is a nucleon?
    [*] A proton or neutron found in the nucleus
    [ ] Another name for an electron
    [ ] A particle with no mass
    [ ] A type of chemical bond
    Explanation: Nucleon is the collective term for particles residing in the nucleus — protons and neutrons. The mass number counts the total nucleons.

  Q14. What term describes an atom that has gained or lost electrons?
    [*] Ion
    [ ] Isotope
    [ ] Molecule
    [ ] Allotrope
    Explanation: An ion is an atom with a net electric charge because it has unequal numbers of protons and electrons. Cations are positive; anions are negative.

  Q15. What is the correct definition of a proton?
    [*] A positively charged particle in the nucleus that defines the element
    [ ] A neutral particle that adds mass to the atom
    [ ] A negatively charged particle in the electron cloud
    [ ] A particle found between atoms in a bond
    Explanation: A proton carries a +1 charge and resides in the nucleus. Its count (atomic number) determines which element the atom is.

  Q16. What does 'charge balance' mean in the context of atoms?
    [*] A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons
    [ ] Protons and neutrons always occur in equal numbers
    [ ] Electrons and neutrons must be equal
    [ ] The nucleus has no net charge by itself
    Explanation: Charge balance means the total positive charge from protons equals the total negative charge from electrons, making the atom electrically neutral.

  Q17. What is the term for particles that orbit the nucleus and determine chemical bonding?
    [*] Electrons
    [ ] Protons
    [ ] Neutrons
    [ ] Photons
    Explanation: Electrons occupy energy levels around the nucleus and are responsible for chemical bonding and reactivity. Their arrangement dictates how atoms interact.

  Q18. Which symbol represents atomic number?
    [*] Z
    [ ] A
    [ ] N
    [ ] M
    Explanation: Z is the standard symbol for atomic number (proton count). A represents mass number, and N is often used for neutron number.

  Q19. What is the definition of an isotope?
    [*] Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons
    [ ] Atoms with the same mass number but different elements
    [ ] Atoms that have gained extra protons
    [ ] Atoms with no electrons
    Explanation: Isotopes share the same atomic number (same element) but differ in neutron count, giving them different mass numbers.

  Q20. What does the notation ¹⁴₆C represent?
    [*] Carbon with mass number 14 and atomic number 6
    [ ] Carbon with 14 protons and 6 neutrons
    [ ] Carbon with 14 electrons
    [ ] A carbon molecule with 14 atoms
    Explanation: In isotope notation the superscript is the mass number (14) and the subscript is the atomic number (6). This atom has 6 protons and 8 neutrons.

  Q21. A forensic scientist identifies an unknown element with 79 protons. Which element is it?
    [*] Gold (Au)
    [ ] Silver (Ag)
    [ ] Platinum (Pt)
    [ ] Mercury (Hg)
    Explanation: Atomic number 79 corresponds to gold (Au) on the periodic table. The number of protons uniquely identifies every element.

  Q22. A neutral chlorine atom has 17 protons. How many electrons does it have?
    [*] 17
    [ ] 18
    [ ] 35
    [ ] 7
    Explanation: A neutral atom has equal protons and electrons. With 17 protons, chlorine must have 17 electrons to balance the charge.

  Q23. An atom of oxygen-18 has 8 protons. How many neutrons does it contain?
    [*] 10
    [ ] 8
    [ ] 18
    [ ] 26
    Explanation: Neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 18 − 8 = 10. The mass number includes both protons and neutrons.

  Q24. A sodium atom loses one electron. What is the charge of the resulting ion?
    [*] +1
    [ ] −1
    [ ] 0
    [ ] +2
    Explanation: Sodium has 11 protons. Losing one electron leaves 10 electrons, so the net charge is 11(+) − 10(−) = +1. It forms the Na⁺ cation.

  Q25. A medical imaging lab uses technetium-99m. If technetium has 43 protons, how many neutrons are in this isotope?
    [*] 56
    [ ] 43
    [ ] 99
    [ ] 142
    Explanation: Neutrons = mass number − protons = 99 − 43 = 56.

  Q26. Two atoms both have 6 protons but one has 6 neutrons and the other has 8. What is the relationship between them?
    [*] They are isotopes of the same element (carbon)
    [ ] They are different elements
    [ ] They are ions
    [ ] One is a molecule and one is an atom
    Explanation: Both have 6 protons, so both are carbon. Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons; carbon-14 has 8 neutrons. They are isotopes — same element, different mass numbers.

  Q27. A chemistry student measures an atom with 26 protons, 30 neutrons, and 24 electrons. What can be concluded?
    [*] It is an iron ion with a +2 charge (Fe²⁺)
    [ ] It is a neutral iron atom
    [ ] It is a nickel atom
    [ ] It is an iron atom with a −2 charge
    Explanation: 26 protons = iron (Fe). With only 24 electrons the charge is 26 − 24 = +2, so it is the Fe²⁺ cation.

  Q28. A researcher discovers an atom with mass number 40 and 20 neutrons. What element is it?
    [*] Calcium (Ca, atomic number 20)
    [ ] Argon (Ar, atomic number 18)
    [ ] Potassium (K, atomic number 19)
    [ ] Scandium (Sc, atomic number 21)
    Explanation: Protons = mass number − neutrons = 40 − 20 = 20. Atomic number 20 is calcium.

  Q29. In a nuclear reactor, uranium-235 is used as fuel. If uranium has 92 protons, what is the neutron count of U-235?
    [*] 143
    [ ] 92
    [ ] 235
    [ ] 327
    Explanation: Neutrons = 235 − 92 = 143. Heavy elements like uranium have many more neutrons than protons to help stabilize the large nucleus.

  Q30. A fluorine atom gains one electron to form F⁻. How many protons and electrons does F⁻ have?
    [*] 9 protons and 10 electrons
    [ ] 10 protons and 10 electrons
    [ ] 9 protons and 9 electrons
    [ ] 8 protons and 10 electrons
    Explanation: Fluorine has 9 protons (atomic number 9). Gaining one electron gives 10 electrons. Proton count never changes — only electrons are gained or lost when forming ions.

--- Lesson 3.2:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Atomic Structure

Core Definitions

  • Nucleus: The dense, central core of an atom containing protons and neutrons.
  • Electron Cloud: The region around the nucleus where electrons are most likely to be found.
  • Energy Levels: Regions around the nucleus where electrons orbit; numbered 1, 2, 3, etc.
  • Atomic Radius: The size of an atom measured from the nucleus to the outer electron cloud.
  • Nuclear Force: The strong force that holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus.
  • Atom: The smallest unit of an element that retains the element's chemical properties.

--- Lesson 3.2:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is the dense, central part of an atom called?
    [*] Nucleus
    [ ] Electron cloud
    [ ] Orbital
    [ ] Valence shell
    Explanation: The nucleus is the tiny, dense core of an atom where protons and neutrons are packed together. It contains nearly all the atom's mass.

  Q2. Where are electrons most likely to be found?
    [*] In the electron cloud surrounding the nucleus
    [ ] Inside the nucleus
    [ ] Between two nuclei
    [ ] Outside the atom entirely
    Explanation: The electron cloud is the region of space around the nucleus where electrons have the highest probability of being found.

  Q3. Most of an atom's mass is concentrated in the:
    [*] Nucleus
    [ ] Electron cloud
    [ ] Outer shell
    [ ] Empty space between atoms
    Explanation: Protons and neutrons in the nucleus account for over 99.9% of the atom's mass. Electrons are roughly 1/1836 the mass of a proton.

  Q4. Most of an atom's volume comes from:
    [*] The electron cloud (mostly empty space)
    [ ] The protons
    [ ] The neutrons
    [ ] The nucleus
    Explanation: The nucleus is extremely tiny compared to the whole atom. If an atom were a stadium, the nucleus would be a marble at center field — the rest is electron cloud.

  Q5. What determines the chemical properties of an atom?
    [*] The arrangement of its electrons
    [ ] The number of neutrons
    [ ] The total mass of the nucleus
    [ ] The size of the nucleus
    Explanation: Electrons, especially valence electrons in the outermost energy level, govern how atoms bond and react with each other.

  Q6. Energy levels in an atom are numbered starting from:
    [*] n = 1 (closest to the nucleus)
    [ ] n = 0 (inside the nucleus)
    [ ] n = 10 (farthest from the nucleus)
    [ ] They are not numbered
    Explanation: Energy levels are labeled n = 1, 2, 3, etc. The first energy level (n = 1) is closest to the nucleus and has the lowest energy.

  Q7. What force holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus?
    [*] The strong nuclear force
    [ ] Gravity
    [ ] Electromagnetic force
    [ ] Covalent bonding
    Explanation: The strong nuclear force is extremely powerful at short range and overcomes the electromagnetic repulsion between positively charged protons in the nucleus.

  Q8. As you move to higher energy levels (n = 1 → 2 → 3), the electrons are:
    [*] Farther from the nucleus with higher energy
    [ ] Closer to the nucleus with higher energy
    [ ] At the same distance but more stable
    [ ] Inside the nucleus
    Explanation: Higher energy levels are farther from the nucleus. Electrons in these levels have more energy and are held less tightly by nuclear attraction.

  Q9. The maximum number of electrons in the first energy level (n = 1) is:
    [*] 2
    [ ] 8
    [ ] 18
    [ ] 1
    Explanation: The formula 2n² gives the maximum electrons per level. For n = 1: 2(1²) = 2. The first shell holds at most 2 electrons.

  Q10. What happens to atomic radius as you move across a period (left to right) on the periodic table?
    [*] It decreases
    [ ] It increases
    [ ] It stays the same
    [ ] It doubles
    Explanation: Across a period, protons increase while electrons fill the same energy level. Greater nuclear charge pulls electrons closer, shrinking the atomic radius.

  Q11. What is the definition of the nucleus?
    [*] The dense central core containing protons and neutrons
    [ ] The outermost electron shell of the atom
    [ ] The cloud of electrons surrounding the atom
    [ ] A bond between two atoms
    Explanation: The nucleus is the small, dense center of an atom that houses all the protons and neutrons (nucleons). It has a positive charge.

  Q12. What does 'electron cloud' refer to?
    [*] The region around the nucleus where electrons are most likely found
    [ ] A type of chemical bond
    [ ] A cloud of neutrons
    [ ] The space inside the nucleus
    Explanation: The electron cloud model shows electrons as a probability distribution around the nucleus rather than fixed orbits, reflecting quantum mechanics.

  Q13. What is an energy level in atomic structure?
    [*] A region around the nucleus where electrons of a specific energy reside
    [ ] The energy stored in a neutron
    [ ] The total energy of the nucleus
    [ ] A measure of radioactivity
    Explanation: Energy levels (shells) are fixed regions numbered n = 1, 2, 3… where electrons with quantized energies are found.

  Q14. What is atomic radius?
    [*] The distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron
    [ ] The diameter of the nucleus
    [ ] The number of protons in the nucleus
    [ ] The distance between two nuclei in a molecule
    Explanation: Atomic radius measures the size of an atom from the center of its nucleus to the boundary of the outermost electron cloud.

  Q15. What is an ion?
    [*] An atom that has gained or lost electrons, giving it a net charge
    [ ] An atom with extra neutrons
    [ ] A neutral atom
    [ ] A molecule with two atoms
    Explanation: An ion forms when an atom gains electrons (anion, negative charge) or loses electrons (cation, positive charge). The number of protons stays the same.

  Q16. What does the term 'nuclear force' describe?
    [*] The strong force that holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus
    [ ] The force of gravity inside an atom
    [ ] The attraction between electrons and photons
    [ ] The repulsion between electrons in different shells
    Explanation: The strong nuclear force acts at very short range within the nucleus, binding nucleons together despite the electromagnetic repulsion between protons.

  Q17. What is the correct definition of an atom?
    [*] The smallest unit of an element that retains its chemical properties
    [ ] A group of molecules bonded together
    [ ] A single proton
    [ ] Any particle smaller than a molecule
    Explanation: An atom is the fundamental unit of an element. It consists of a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons and keeps the element's identity.

  Q18. What is a cation?
    [*] A positively charged ion (lost electrons)
    [ ] A negatively charged ion
    [ ] A neutral atom
    [ ] A type of isotope
    Explanation: A cation has fewer electrons than protons, resulting in a net positive charge. Metals typically form cations by losing valence electrons.

  Q19. What is an anion?
    [*] A negatively charged ion (gained electrons)
    [ ] A positively charged ion
    [ ] An atom with no electrons
    [ ] A proton by itself
    Explanation: An anion has more electrons than protons, giving a net negative charge. Nonmetals typically form anions by gaining electrons to fill their valence shell.

  Q20. The formula 2n² is used to calculate:
    [*] The maximum number of electrons in energy level n
    [ ] The number of protons in the nucleus
    [ ] The mass number of an atom
    [ ] The speed of electrons
    Explanation: 2n² gives the maximum electron capacity: n = 1 → 2, n = 2 → 8, n = 3 → 18, n = 4 → 32.

  Q21. Rutherford's gold foil experiment showed that most alpha particles passed straight through the foil. What did this prove?
    [*] Atoms are mostly empty space with a tiny, dense nucleus
    [ ] Gold atoms have no nucleus
    [ ] Electrons are inside the nucleus
    [ ] Atoms are solid spheres
    Explanation: Most alpha particles passed through because the atom is mostly electron cloud (empty space). The few that bounced back hit the small, dense, positive nucleus.

  Q22. A lithium atom has 3 electrons. How are they distributed among energy levels?
    [*] 2 in the first level, 1 in the second level
    [ ] 3 in the first level
    [ ] 1 in the first level, 2 in the second
    [ ] All 3 in the third level
    Explanation: The first energy level holds a maximum of 2 electrons. The third electron must go into the second energy level, giving lithium a configuration of 2-1.

  Q23. Why does sodium (Na) easily form Na⁺ ions?
    [*] It has one electron in its outermost energy level that is easily lost
    [ ] It has a very large nucleus
    [ ] It needs one more electron to be stable
    [ ] Its nucleus repels all electrons
    Explanation: Sodium has the electron configuration 2-8-1. Losing the single outer electron gives it a stable 2-8 arrangement (like neon), forming the Na⁺ cation.

  Q24. An atom has atomic radius 186 pm while its cation has radius 98 pm. Why is the cation smaller?
    [*] Losing electrons reduces electron-electron repulsion and the same nuclear charge pulls remaining electrons closer
    [ ] The nucleus shrinks when electrons leave
    [ ] Protons are removed along with electrons
    [ ] The cation has more neutrons
    Explanation: When an atom loses electrons, there is less electron-electron repulsion and the same nuclear charge holds the fewer remaining electrons more tightly, shrinking the radius.

  Q25. A neon sign glows because electrons in neon atoms absorb energy, jump to higher energy levels, then:
    [*] Fall back to lower levels and release light energy
    [ ] Are permanently removed from the atom
    [ ] Fuse with the nucleus
    [ ] Stay at the higher energy level forever
    Explanation: Excited electrons are unstable and quickly drop back to lower energy levels, emitting the energy difference as photons of visible light — producing the characteristic neon glow.

  Q26. Fluorine has 9 electrons (configuration 2-7). How does it typically achieve a stable electron arrangement?
    [*] It gains 1 electron to complete 8 in its outer shell (F⁻)
    [ ] It loses 7 electrons
    [ ] It gains 7 protons
    [ ] It does not form ions
    Explanation: Fluorine needs only 1 more electron to fill its second energy level to 8 (octet), so it readily gains an electron to form F⁻.

  Q27. Potassium (K) is larger than calcium (Ca) even though they are in the same period. Why?
    [*] Calcium has one more proton, pulling electrons closer and reducing atomic radius
    [ ] Potassium has more energy levels
    [ ] Calcium has fewer electrons
    [ ] Potassium's nucleus is larger
    Explanation: Across a period, increasing nuclear charge (more protons) with electrons in the same shell pulls the electron cloud inward, decreasing atomic radius.

  Q28. In a PET scan, positron-emitting isotopes are injected into a patient. What holds the protons together inside these isotopes' nuclei despite their mutual repulsion?
    [*] The strong nuclear force
    [ ] Electron pressure
    [ ] Chemical bonding
    [ ] Gravitational attraction
    Explanation: The strong nuclear force operates at femtometer distances inside the nucleus and is about 100 times stronger than electromagnetic repulsion at that scale.

  Q29. A chlorine atom (2-8-7) gains one electron to form Cl⁻. How does its radius compare to native Cl?
    [*] Cl⁻ is larger because extra electron repulsion expands the cloud
    [ ] Cl⁻ is smaller because more electrons are compressed
    [ ] The radius stays the same
    [ ] Cl⁻ has no electron cloud
    Explanation: Adding an electron increases electron-electron repulsion while the nuclear charge stays the same, so the electron cloud expands, making Cl⁻ larger than Cl.

  Q30. Silicon has the electron configuration 2-8-4. How many more electrons can its third energy level hold?
    [*] 14 more (max is 2n² = 18 for n = 3)
    [ ] 4 more
    [ ] 8 more
    [ ] 0 — it is full
    Explanation: The third energy level can hold 2(3²) = 18 electrons. Silicon has 4 there, so 18 − 4 = 14 more electrons could fit, though in practice only 8 are in the valence shell.

--- Lesson 3.3:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Quantum Mechanical Model & Orbitals

Core Definitions

  • Quantum Mechanical Model: The modern model of the atom that treats electrons as waves and defines probability regions (orbitals).
  • Orbital: A region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron.
  • s Orbital: Spherical-shaped orbital; each energy level has one s orbital (holds 2 electrons).
  • p Orbital: Dumbbell-shaped orbital; exists starting from the 2nd energy level (3 orbitals, holds 6 electrons).
  • d Orbital: Clover-shaped orbital; exists starting from the 3rd energy level (5 orbitals, holds 10 electrons).
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: You cannot simultaneously know both the exact position and momentum of an electron.

--- Lesson 3.3:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. The quantum mechanical model treats electrons as:
    [*] Probability clouds rather than fixed orbits
    [ ] Tiny solid balls circling the nucleus
    [ ] Stationary particles inside the nucleus
    [ ] Waves that travel only in straight lines
    Explanation: The quantum mechanical model describes electrons as wave-like entities existing in probability regions (orbitals), not fixed circular paths like the Bohr model.

  Q2. What is an orbital?
    [*] A 3-D region where an electron is most likely to be found
    [ ] A fixed path an electron follows around the nucleus
    [ ] The center of the atom
    [ ] A bond between two atoms
    Explanation: An orbital is a mathematical probability map showing the region in space where there is about a 90% chance of finding an electron.

  Q3. How many electrons can a single orbital hold?
    [*] 2
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 4
    [ ] 8
    Explanation: The Pauli exclusion principle states that each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, and they must have opposite spins.

  Q4. What is the shape of an s orbital?
    [*] Spherical
    [ ] Dumbbell
    [ ] Cloverleaf
    [ ] Ring-shaped
    Explanation: The s orbital is spherical, centered on the nucleus. Each energy level contains exactly one s orbital that holds up to 2 electrons.

  Q5. What is the shape of a p orbital?
    [*] Dumbbell (figure-eight)
    [ ] Spherical
    [ ] Cubic
    [ ] Flat disc
    Explanation: P orbitals have a dumbbell shape with two lobes on opposite sides of the nucleus. There are three p orbitals per sublevel (px, py, pz).

  Q6. How many p orbitals exist in a single p sublevel?
    [*] 3
    [ ] 1
    [ ] 5
    [ ] 7
    Explanation: A p sublevel has 3 orbitals oriented along x, y, and z axes. Each holds 2 electrons, so a full p sublevel holds 6 electrons total.

  Q7. The principal quantum number (n) indicates:
    [*] The energy level and average distance from the nucleus
    [ ] The shape of the orbital
    [ ] The spin of the electron
    [ ] The number of protons
    Explanation: The principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3… indicates the energy level. Higher n means the electron is farther from the nucleus and has greater energy.

  Q8. At which energy level do p orbitals first appear?
    [*] n = 2
    [ ] n = 1
    [ ] n = 3
    [ ] n = 4
    Explanation: The first energy level (n = 1) only has an s orbital. P orbitals begin at n = 2, d orbitals at n = 3, and f orbitals at n = 4.

  Q9. How many orbitals are in a d sublevel?
    [*] 5
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 7
    [ ] 1
    Explanation: A d sublevel has 5 orbitals, each holding 2 electrons, for a total capacity of 10 electrons. D orbitals start at n = 3.

  Q10. The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle states that you cannot simultaneously know:
    [*] Both the exact position and momentum of an electron
    [ ] The charge and mass of a proton
    [ ] The number of protons and neutrons
    [ ] The atomic number and mass number
    Explanation: Heisenberg showed that the more precisely you know an electron's position, the less precisely you can know its momentum, and vice versa. This is fundamental to quantum mechanics.

  Q11. What does the angular momentum quantum number (l) describe?
    [*] The shape of the orbital (s, p, d, f)
    [ ] The energy level of the electron
    [ ] The spin direction of the electron
    [ ] The number of protons in the atom
    Explanation: The angular momentum quantum number l determines orbital shape: l = 0 (s), l = 1 (p), l = 2 (d), l = 3 (f).

  Q12. What does the magnetic quantum number (mₗ) specify?
    [*] The orientation of the orbital in space
    [ ] The energy level
    [ ] The shape of the orbital
    [ ] The number of electrons in the orbital
    Explanation: The magnetic quantum number mₗ ranges from −l to +l and specifies which direction the orbital points in 3D space (e.g., px, py, or pz).

  Q13. What does the spin quantum number (mₛ) indicate?
    [*] The spin direction of the electron (+½ or −½)
    [ ] The speed of the electron
    [ ] The size of the orbital
    [ ] The number of orbitals
    Explanation: The spin quantum number mₛ is either +½ or −½, representing the two opposite spin states. Two electrons in the same orbital must have opposite spins.

  Q14. What is the Aufbau principle?
    [*] Electrons fill the lowest energy orbitals first
    [ ] Electrons fill the highest energy orbitals first
    [ ] Orbitals can hold unlimited electrons
    [ ] Electrons always orbit in pairs
    Explanation: The Aufbau principle (German for 'building up') states that electrons occupy the lowest available energy orbitals before filling higher ones.

  Q15. What does the Pauli exclusion principle state?
    [*] No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers
    [ ] Electrons always fill lower energy levels first
    [ ] Electrons spread out among equal-energy orbitals before pairing
    [ ] Electrons cannot exist in orbitals
    Explanation: The Pauli exclusion principle limits each orbital to 2 electrons with opposite spins, since no two electrons can share all four quantum numbers.

  Q16. What does Hund's rule state?
    [*] Electrons fill degenerate orbitals singly before pairing up
    [ ] Electrons always pair up immediately
    [ ] Only s orbitals are filled first
    [ ] Electrons skip energy levels
    Explanation: Hund's rule says that within a sublevel, electrons occupy each orbital singly (with parallel spins) before any orbital gets a second electron.

  Q17. What is the definition of a quantum number?
    [*] A number that describes the energy, shape, orientation, or spin of an electron in an atom
    [ ] The number of protons in an atom
    [ ] The mass number of an isotope
    [ ] The speed of light divided by wavelength
    Explanation: Four quantum numbers (n, l, mₗ, mₛ) completely describe the state of an electron: its energy level, orbital shape, spatial orientation, and spin direction.

  Q18. What is a sublevel (subshell)?
    [*] A division of an energy level containing orbitals of the same shape (s, p, d, or f)
    [ ] Another name for the nucleus
    [ ] The space between two atoms
    [ ] A type of chemical bond
    Explanation: Each energy level n contains sublevels: n = 1 has s; n = 2 has s, p; n = 3 has s, p, d; n = 4 has s, p, d, f.

  Q19. What does the term 'degenerate orbitals' mean?
    [*] Orbitals that have the same energy level within a sublevel
    [ ] Orbitals that are unstable
    [ ] Orbitals that cannot hold electrons
    [ ] Orbitals in the nucleus
    Explanation: Degenerate orbitals have equal energy. For example, the three 2p orbitals (px, py, pz) are degenerate — electrons fill them singly before pairing (Hund's rule).

  Q20. How many total orbitals are in the third energy level (n = 3)?
    [*] 9 (one s, three p, five d)
    [ ] 3
    [ ] 5
    [ ] 18
    Explanation: n = 3 has sublevels 3s (1 orbital) + 3p (3 orbitals) + 3d (5 orbitals) = 9 orbitals total, holding up to 18 electrons.

  Q21. A scientist wants to find the exact location of an electron in a hydrogen atom. Why is this impossible according to quantum mechanics?
    [*] The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle prevents knowing exact position and momentum simultaneously
    [ ] Electrons are too large to measure
    [ ] Hydrogen does not have electrons
    [ ] Instruments are not accurate enough yet
    Explanation: This is a fundamental limit of nature, not a technological one. Measuring position precisely disturbs momentum, making simultaneous exact knowledge of both impossible.

  Q22. Write the electron configuration for nitrogen (Z = 7). Which is correct?
    [*] 1s² 2s² 2p³
    [ ] 1s² 2s² 2p⁶
    [ ] 1s² 2p⁵
    [ ] 1s⁷
    Explanation: Nitrogen has 7 electrons: 2 fill the 1s, 2 fill the 2s, and the remaining 3 go into the 2p sublevel (one in each p orbital, per Hund's rule).

  Q23. An orbital diagram for carbon shows 2p with two singly-occupied orbitals. Which rule explains why the electrons are unpaired?
    [*] Hund's rule
    [ ] Aufbau principle
    [ ] Pauli exclusion principle
    [ ] Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
    Explanation: Hund's rule states electrons singly occupy degenerate orbitals before pairing. Carbon's 2 electrons in 2p go into separate p orbitals with parallel spins.

    [*] A 2p orbital
    [ ] A 1s orbital
    [ ] A 3d orbital
    [ ] A 2s orbital
    Explanation: n = 2 means the second energy level, l = 1 means a p orbital, mₗ = 0 is one of the three p orientations, and mₛ = +½ is spin up.

  Q25. Transition metals have partially filled d orbitals. Why does this give them their characteristic colors?
    [*] Electrons in d orbitals absorb specific visible wavelengths and transmit the complementary color
    [ ] D orbitals produce gamma radiation
    [ ] Transition metals have no electrons
    [ ] S orbitals determine color, not d
    Explanation: Partially filled d orbitals have energy gaps that match visible light frequencies. Electrons absorb certain colors and the remaining light gives the ion its observed hue.

  Q26. Why does the 4s subshell fill before the 3d subshell in the Aufbau order?
    [*] 4s has lower energy than 3d due to electron shielding effects
    [ ] 4s is closer to the nucleus than 3d
    [ ] 3d does not exist
    [ ] 4s and 3d fill at the same time
    Explanation: Due to electron shielding and penetration effects, the 4s orbital is slightly lower in energy than 3d, so it fills first according to the Aufbau principle.

  Q27. An MRI machine exploits the spin of hydrogen nuclei. Which quantum number describes electron spin?
    [*] mₛ (spin quantum number)
    [ ] n (principal quantum number)
    [ ] l (angular momentum)
    [ ] mₗ (magnetic quantum number)
    Explanation: The spin quantum number mₛ describes the intrinsic angular momentum (spin) of a particle. For electrons it takes values of +½ or −½.

  Q28. A student claims an electron has quantum numbers (1, 1, 0, +½). Is this valid?
    [*] No — when n = 1, l can only be 0 (s orbital), not 1
    [ ] Yes — any combination of numbers works
    [ ] No — mₛ cannot be +½
    [ ] Yes — l = 1 is always allowed
    Explanation: The angular momentum quantum number l ranges from 0 to (n − 1). For n = 1, l can only be 0. A p orbital (l = 1) cannot exist in the first energy level.

    [ ] The Aufbau principle never applies to transition metals
    [ ] 4s cannot hold any electrons
    Explanation: Half-filled (d⁵) and fully-filled (d¹⁰) d subshells are unusually stable due to exchange energy. Chromium promotes one 4s electron to 3d to achieve this.

  Q30. In a chemistry lab, a student heats a compound and sees a bright blue flame. This color arises because electrons transition between orbitals and emit photons. The blue color indicates:
    [*] A relatively large energy gap between orbitals (short wavelength, high energy)
    [ ] A very small energy gap (long wavelength)

    Explanation: Blue light has a short wavelength and high energy. A larger gap between electron energy levels produces higher-energy (bluer) photons when electrons fall back down.

--- Lesson 3.4:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: EM Spectrum & Atomic Emission Spectra

Core Definitions

  • Electromagnetic Spectrum: The full range of electromagnetic radiation from radio waves to gamma rays.
  • Wavelength (λ): The distance between two consecutive wave peaks; measured in meters or nanometers.
  • Frequency (ν): The number of wave cycles per second; measured in hertz (Hz).
  • Speed of Light: c = λ × ν = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s; wavelength and frequency are inversely related.
  • Atomic Emission Spectrum: The unique pattern of light frequencies emitted by an element's excited electrons.
  • Photon: A particle (quantum) of electromagnetic radiation; energy = hν.

--- Lesson 3.4:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. As wavelength increases, frequency:
    [*] Decreases
    [ ] Increases
    [ ] Stays the same
    [ ] Becomes zero
    Explanation: Wavelength and frequency are inversely related: c = λν. Since the speed of light is constant, a longer wavelength means a lower frequency.

  Q2. Which type of electromagnetic radiation has the highest energy?
    [*] Gamma rays
    [ ] Radio waves
    [ ] Microwaves
    [ ] Infrared
    Explanation: Gamma rays have the shortest wavelength and highest frequency on the EM spectrum, giving them the most energy per photon (E = hν).

  Q3. The speed of light in a vacuum is approximately:
    [*] 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s
    [ ] 3.0 × 10⁶ m/s
    [ ] 3.0 × 10¹⁰ m/s
    [ ] 1.5 × 10⁸ m/s
    Explanation: All electromagnetic radiation travels at c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum. This constant relates wavelength and frequency: c = λν.

  Q4. What produces an atomic emission spectrum?
    [*] Electrons falling from higher to lower energy levels and releasing photons
    [ ] Neutrons leaving the nucleus
    [ ] Protons absorbing energy
    [ ] Atoms splitting in half
    Explanation: When excited electrons drop to lower energy levels, they release specific amounts of energy as photons, producing a line spectrum unique to each element.

  Q5. The relationship between photon energy and frequency is:
    [*] Directly proportional (E = hν)
    [ ] Inversely proportional
    [ ] No relationship exists
    [ ] Exponential
    Explanation: Planck's equation E = hν shows energy is directly proportional to frequency. Higher frequency photons carry more energy.

  Q6. Which region of the EM spectrum is visible to the human eye?
    [*] Approximately 400–700 nm wavelength
    [ ] Below 10 nm (X-ray region)
    [ ] Above 1 mm (radio region)
    [ ] All wavelengths are visible
    Explanation: Visible light spans roughly 400 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red). This is a tiny portion of the full electromagnetic spectrum.

  Q7. What is wavelength (λ)?
    [*] The distance between two consecutive wave crests
    [ ] The number of waves per second
    [ ] The height of the wave
    [ ] The speed of the wave
    Explanation: Wavelength is the peak-to-peak distance of a wave, measured in meters or nanometers. It determines the type and color of electromagnetic radiation.

  Q8. What is frequency (ν)?
    [*] The number of wave cycles passing a point per second
    [ ] The distance between wave peaks
    [ ] The brightness of a light wave
    [ ] The speed of the wave
    Explanation: Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second. Higher frequency means more wave cycles pass each second.

  Q9. Planck's constant (h) has a value of approximately:
    [*] 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
    [ ] 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s
    [ ] 6.02 × 10²³
    [ ] 9.8 m/s²
    Explanation: Planck's constant h ≈ 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s is the proportionality constant in E = hν, linking photon energy to frequency.

  Q10. Which color of visible light has the longest wavelength?
    [*] Red (~700 nm)
    [ ] Violet (~400 nm)
    [ ] Green (~550 nm)
    [ ] Blue (~475 nm)
    Explanation: Red light sits at the long-wavelength end of visible light (~700 nm), while violet is at the short-wavelength end (~400 nm).

  Q11. What is the electromagnetic spectrum?
    [*] The full range of EM radiation from radio waves to gamma rays
    [ ] Only the visible light portion of radiation
    [ ] A chart showing chemical element properties
    [ ] The energy stored in the nucleus
    Explanation: The EM spectrum spans from low-energy radio waves through microwaves, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, to high-energy gamma rays.

  Q12. What is a photon?
    [*] A particle (quantum) of electromagnetic radiation
    [ ] A subatomic particle in the nucleus
    [ ] A type of electron
    [ ] A unit of mass
    Explanation: A photon is the smallest discrete unit of light energy. It has no mass but carries energy proportional to its frequency: E = hν.

  Q13. What is an emission spectrum?
    [*] A unique pattern of wavelengths emitted by an excited element — a chemical fingerprint
    [ ] A continuous rainbow of colors from white light
    [ ] The amount of heat an element releases
    [ ] The radioactive decay pattern of an element
    Explanation: Each element's electrons have unique energy level spacings, so they emit distinct sets of wavelengths — producing a line spectrum that can identify the element.

  Q14. What is an absorption spectrum?
    [*] A continuous spectrum with dark lines where specific wavelengths are absorbed
    [ ] A spectrum with only bright lines
    [ ] The total energy output of a star
    [ ] Light that passes through a prism unchanged
    Explanation: When white light passes through a gas, atoms absorb specific wavelengths (electrons jump up), leaving dark lines — the inverse of an emission spectrum.

  Q15. What does the equation c = λν express?
    [*] The speed of light equals wavelength times frequency
    [ ] Energy equals mass times velocity squared
    [ ] Frequency equals charge times voltage
    [ ] Wavelength equals the speed of sound
    Explanation: c = λν links the speed of light (3.0 × 10⁸ m/s) to wavelength and frequency. If you know one, you can find the other.

  Q16. What does the term 'quantum' mean in physics?
    [*] The smallest discrete amount of energy that can be emitted or absorbed
    [ ] An infinitely large amount of energy
    [ ] A type of chemical bond
    [ ] A measurement of mass
    Explanation: A quantum is the minimum discrete packet of energy. Planck proposed that energy is quantized — it comes in fixed chunks (E = hν) rather than continuous amounts.

  Q17. What is the definition of wavelength?
    [*] The distance between two consecutive wave peaks, measured in meters or nanometers
    [ ] The total energy of a wave
    [ ] The number of crests per second
    [ ] The amplitude of the wave

    [*] Hertz (Hz)
    [ ] Meters (m)

    [*] A spectrum with only specific discrete wavelengths, appearing as bright lines on a dark background
    [ ] A continuous rainbow of colors
    [ ] A spectrum with no visible colors

--- Lesson 3.5:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Radioactivity & Stability
Core Definitions

  • Radioactivity: The spontaneous emission of radiation from an unstable nucleus.
  • Nuclear Stability: Depends on the ratio of neutrons to protons; too many or too few neutrons = unstable.
  • Band of Stability: The zone on a graph of neutrons vs. protons where stable nuclei exist.
  • Alpha Decay: Emission of an alpha particle (²₄He); reduces atomic number by 2 and mass number by 4.
  • Beta Decay: A neutron converts to a proton and emits a beta particle (electron); atomic number increases by 1.
  • Gamma Radiation: High-energy electromagnetic radiation emitted from the nucleus; no change in mass or atomic number.

--- Lesson 3.5:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. What is radioactivity?
    [*] The spontaneous emission of radiation from an unstable nucleus
    [ ] The absorption of electrons by the nucleus
    [ ] The movement of protons to other atoms
    [ ] A type of chemical bonding
    Explanation: Radioactivity is the process by which unstable atomic nuclei release energy in the form of particles or electromagnetic radiation to reach a more stable state.

  Q2. Nuclear stability depends primarily on the ratio of:
    [*] Neutrons to protons
    [ ] Electrons to protons
    [ ] Mass to volume
    [ ] Energy to temperature
    Explanation: Too many or too few neutrons relative to protons leads to nuclear instability. Neutrons help buffer proton-proton repulsion in the nucleus.

  Q3. What is alpha decay?
    [*] Emission of a helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons)
    [ ] Emission of an electron from the nucleus
    [ ] Emission of a photon only
    [ ] Loss of all electrons
    Explanation: Alpha decay emits an alpha particle (⁴₂He), reducing the mass number by 4 and the atomic number by 2, transforming the element into a new one.

  Q4. In beta decay, what happens inside the nucleus?
    [*] A neutron converts to a proton and an electron (beta particle) is emitted
    [ ] A proton is destroyed
    [ ] Two protons fuse
    [ ] An alpha particle is absorbed
    Explanation: In beta-minus decay, a neutron transforms into a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino. The atomic number increases by 1.

  Q5. Gamma radiation is:
    [*] High-energy electromagnetic radiation with no mass or charge
    [ ] A positively charged particle
    [ ] A negatively charged electron
    [ ] A neutron ejected from the nucleus
    Explanation: Gamma rays are pure energy — photons emitted from excited nuclei. They change neither mass number nor atomic number.

  Q6. Elements with atomic number greater than 83 (bismuth) are generally:
    [*] Radioactive (no stable isotopes)
    [ ] Completely stable
    [ ] Only found in stars
    [ ] All noble gases
    Explanation: Beyond bismuth (Z = 83), the electrostatic repulsion between protons overwhelms the strong nuclear force, making all isotopes unstable and radioactive.

  Q7. What holds protons and neutrons together despite proton-proton repulsion?
    [*] The strong nuclear force
    [ ] Gravity
    [ ] Electromagnetic force
    [ ] Chemical bonds
    Explanation: The strong nuclear force acts at extremely short range (~10⁻¹⁵ m) and is about 100 times stronger than the electromagnetic repulsion between protons at nuclear distances.

  Q8. Atoms with too many neutrons tend to undergo which type of decay?
    [*] Beta decay (neutron → proton + electron)
    [ ] Alpha decay only
    [ ] No decay occurs
    [ ] The atom explodes
    Explanation: Beta decay converts excess neutrons into protons, moving the nucleus closer to the band of stability's optimal neutron-to-proton ratio.

  Q9. Which type of radiation is the most penetrating?
    [*] Gamma rays
    [ ] Alpha particles
    [ ] Beta particles
    [ ] All three penetrate equally
    Explanation: Gamma rays have no mass or charge, allowing them to pass through most materials. Alpha particles are stopped by paper; beta by aluminum foil; gamma requires thick lead or concrete.

  Q10. Which type of radiation is the LEAST penetrating?
    [*] Alpha particles
    [ ] Beta particles
    [ ] Gamma rays
    [ ] X-rays
    Explanation: Alpha particles are large (2p + 2n) and carry a +2 charge, so they interact strongly with matter and are stopped by a sheet of paper or skin.

  Q11. What is the definition of radioactive decay?
    [*] The process by which an unstable nucleus loses energy by emitting radiation
    [ ] The chemical decomposition of a compound
    [ ] The loss of electrons from an atom
    [ ] The breaking of chemical bonds
    Explanation: Radioactive decay is a nuclear process (not chemical) where the nucleus emits particles or energy to move toward a more stable configuration.

  Q12. What is the band of stability?
    [*] The zone on a neutron-vs-proton graph where stable nuclei are found
    [ ] A range of electron energies in an atom
    [ ] The group of noble gases on the periodic table
    [ ] The temperature range where solids exist
    Explanation: The band of stability plots neutrons vs. protons. Nuclei within this belt are stable; those outside decay to reach it via alpha, beta, or other decay modes.

  Q13. What is nuclear stability?
    [*] A condition where the strong nuclear force overcomes proton-proton repulsion
    [ ] When an atom has no electrons
    [ ] When all isotopes of an element are identical
    [ ] A chemical property of noble gases
    Explanation: Nuclear stability exists when the attractive strong nuclear force between nucleons balances or exceeds the electromagnetic repulsion between protons.

  Q14. What is an alpha particle?
    [*] A helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons) with a +2 charge
    [ ] A high-speed electron
    [ ] A photon of light
    [ ] A single proton
    Explanation: An alpha particle is identical to a helium-4 nucleus: 2 protons and 2 neutrons bound together, carrying a +2 charge.

  Q15. What is a beta particle?
    [*] A high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus during neutron-to-proton conversion
    [ ] A helium nucleus
    [ ] A proton
    [ ] A gamma ray
    Explanation: In beta-minus decay, a neutron converts to a proton inside the nucleus, ejecting an electron (beta particle) and an antineutrino.

  Q16. What is half-life?
    [*] The time required for half of a radioactive sample to decay
    [ ] The time for all atoms to decay
    [ ] Half the mass of an atom
    [ ] The charge of a beta particle
    Explanation: Half-life is a fixed time interval during which exactly half the radioactive atoms in a sample undergo decay. It is a characteristic constant for each isotope.

  Q17. What does 'transmutation' mean in nuclear chemistry?
    [*] The conversion of one element into another through nuclear reactions
    [ ] A change in electron configuration
    [ ] The formation of a chemical compound
    [ ] The melting of a solid
    Explanation: Transmutation changes the number of protons, transforming one element into another. It occurs naturally through radioactive decay or artificially in particle accelerators.

  Q18. What effect does alpha decay have on an atom's atomic number and mass number?
    [*] Atomic number decreases by 2, mass number decreases by 4
    [ ] Atomic number increases by 2, mass number increases by 4
    [ ] Atomic number decreases by 1, mass number stays the same
    [ ] No change in either
    Explanation: An alpha particle carries away 2 protons and 2 neutrons, so the parent nucleus loses 2 from its atomic number and 4 from its mass number.

  Q19. What effect does beta decay have on an atom's atomic number?
    [*] Atomic number increases by 1 (mass number unchanged)
    [ ] Atomic number decreases by 1
    [ ] Atomic number decreases by 2
    [ ] No change
    Explanation: A neutron becomes a proton, adding 1 to the atomic number. Mass number stays the same because the total nucleon count is unchanged.

  Q20. Gamma emission affects which properties of the nucleus?
    [*] Neither atomic number nor mass number change
    [ ] Mass number decreases by 4
    [ ] Atomic number increases by 1
    [ ] Atomic number decreases by 2
    Explanation: Gamma radiation is pure energy (photons). The nucleus releases excess energy without changing its proton or neutron count.

  Q21. Uranium-238 undergoes alpha decay. What is the daughter nucleus?
    [*] Thorium-234
    [ ] Plutonium-242
    [ ] Radium-234
    [ ] Uranium-234
    Explanation: Alpha decay: U-238 → Th-234 + He-4. Atomic number drops from 92 to 90 (thorium), mass drops from 238 to 234.

  Q22. Carbon-14 undergoes beta decay. What element does it become?
    [*] Nitrogen-14
    [ ] Boron-14
    [ ] Carbon-12
    [ ] Oxygen-14

    [*] A sheet of paper or skin
    [ ] Thick lead shielding

    [ ] A magnetic field only
    Explanation: Alpha particles are large and heavily charged, so they are easily stopped by a few centimeters of air, paper, or the outer layer of skin.

    [ ] All radiation is fully stopped by lead
    Explanation: While lead is excellent at blocking most radiation, extremely high-energy gamma rays may partially penetrate thin lead. Facilities use thick concrete and lead shields for full protection.

--- Lesson 3.6:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Isotopes & Radioisotopes
Core Definitions

  • Isotope: Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons (same protons, different mass numbers).
  • Radioisotope: An unstable isotope that undergoes radioactive decay.
  • Atomic Mass: The weighted average mass of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element.
  • Mass Number: The total number of protons and neutrons in a specific isotope.
  • Isotope Notation: Written as Element-mass number (e.g., Carbon-14) or with superscript mass number.
  • Percent Abundance: The percentage of each isotope found naturally; used to calculate atomic mass.

--- Lesson 3.6:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Isotopes of an element differ in their number of:
    [*] Neutrons
    [ ] Protons
    [ ] Electrons
    [ ] Valence electrons
    Explanation: Isotopes have the same number of protons (same element) but different numbers of neutrons, giving them different mass numbers.

  Q2. Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 have the same number of:
    [*] Protons (6 each)
    [ ] Neutrons
    [ ] Total nucleons
    [ ] Mass numbers
    Explanation: Both are carbon isotopes with 6 protons. C-12 has 6 neutrons while C-14 has 8 neutrons, giving different mass numbers.

  Q3. Which hydrogen isotope has one neutron?
    [*] Deuterium (H-2)
    [ ] Protium (H-1)
    [ ] Tritium (H-3)
    [ ] Helium-3
    Explanation: Deuterium has 1 proton and 1 neutron (mass number 2). Protium has 0 neutrons; tritium has 2 neutrons.

  Q4. A radioisotope is an isotope that:
    [*] Is unstable and undergoes radioactive decay
    [ ] Is always synthetic
    [ ] Has no neutrons
    [ ] Is completely stable
    Explanation: Radioisotopes have unstable nuclei that emit particles or energy to reach stability. They can be natural (like C-14) or synthetic (like Tc-99m).

  Q5. The average atomic mass on the periodic table is calculated from:
    [*] The weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes
    [ ] Only the most common isotope
    [ ] Only protons
    [ ] The lightest isotope only
    Explanation: Atomic mass is a weighted average: each isotope's mass is multiplied by its percent abundance, then summed. More abundant isotopes contribute more to the average.

  Q6. Carbon-14 is useful for dating organic materials because it:
    [*] Has a half-life of about 5,730 years
    [ ] Is completely stable
    [ ] Is only found in water
    [ ] Decays in seconds
    Explanation: C-14 is continuously produced in the atmosphere and incorporated into living organisms. After death, C-14 decays with a half-life of ~5,730 years, allowing dating up to ~50,000 years.

  Q7. Isotopes of the same element have identical:
    [*] Chemical properties (same number of electrons)
    [ ] Mass numbers
    [ ] Numbers of neutrons
    [ ] Atomic masses
    Explanation: Since isotopes have the same number of protons and electrons, they undergo the same chemical reactions. Their physical properties (mass, density) differ due to different neutron counts.

  Q8. What is the mass number of an isotope?
    [*] The total number of protons and neutrons
    [ ] The number of electrons only
    [ ] The average atomic mass
    [ ] The atomic number
    Explanation: Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons. It is always a whole number, unlike atomic mass which is a weighted average.

  Q9. An instrument that measures isotope masses and abundances is called a:
    [*] Mass spectrometer
    [ ] Geiger counter
    [ ] Thermometer
    [ ] Spectrophotometer
    Explanation: A mass spectrometer ionizes atoms, accelerates them through a magnetic field, and separates them by mass-to-charge ratio to determine each isotope's mass and relative abundance.

  Q10. How many neutrons does Oxygen-18 have?
    [*] 10
    [ ] 8
    [ ] 18
    [ ] 2
    Explanation: Oxygen has 8 protons (Z = 8). Neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 18 − 8 = 10.

  Q11. What is the definition of an isotope?
    [*] Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons
    [ ] Atoms of different elements with the same mass
    [ ] Atoms with no electrons
    [ ] Any radioactive atom
    Explanation: Isotopes share the same atomic number (same element) but differ in neutron count and therefore mass number. Not all isotopes are radioactive — most are stable.

  Q12. What does 'percent abundance' mean?
    [*] The percentage of each isotope found naturally for a given element
    [ ] The total number of atoms in a sample
    [ ] The percentage of protons in an atom
    [ ] The rate of radioactive decay
    Explanation: Percent abundance indicates how much of each isotope exists in nature. For example, about 98.9% of carbon is C-12 and 1.1% is C-13.

  Q13. What is atomic mass (in the context of isotopes)?
    [*] The weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes
    [ ] The mass of the most common isotope only
    [ ] The number of protons
    [ ] The same as atomic number
    Explanation: Atomic mass is calculated as: Σ(isotope mass × fractional abundance). It is measured in atomic mass units (amu) and appears as a decimal on the periodic table.

  Q14. What is the term for isotope notation written as Element-mass number (e.g., Carbon-14)?
    [*] Hyphen notation
    [ ] Lewis notation
    [ ] Bohr notation
    [ ] Nuclear notation
    Explanation: Hyphen notation (e.g., Uranium-235) uses the element name followed by the mass number. Nuclear notation shows the mass number as a superscript and atomic number as a subscript.

  Q15. Protium, deuterium, and tritium are all isotopes of which element?
    [*] Hydrogen
    [ ] Helium
    [ ] Carbon
    [ ] Oxygen
    Explanation: Hydrogen is unique in having named isotopes: protium (0 neutrons), deuterium (1 neutron), and tritium (2 neutrons, radioactive).

  Q16. What does a mass spectrometer measure?
    [*] The masses and relative abundances of isotopes in a sample
    [ ] The color of a chemical
    [ ] The temperature of a reaction
    [ ] The speed of electrons
    Explanation: Mass spectrometers separate ionized atoms by mass-to-charge ratio in a magnetic field, producing a spectrum showing each isotope's mass and how common it is.

  Q17. What is the unit used for atomic mass?
    [*] Atomic mass unit (amu)
    [ ] Gram
    [ ] Mole
    [ ] Joule
    Explanation: One amu is defined as 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom. It is the standard unit for expressing masses on the atomic scale.

  Q18. Why is the atomic mass of chlorine approximately 35.5 amu rather than a whole number?
    [*] It is a weighted average of Cl-35 (75.8%) and Cl-37 (24.2%)
    [ ] Chlorine atoms lose half a neutron
    [ ] Chlorine has fractional protons
    [ ] The periodic table rounds incorrectly
    Explanation: Chlorine's two stable isotopes have different masses. The weighted average 0.758 × 35 + 0.242 × 37 ≈ 35.5 amu explains the decimal value.

  Q19. Isobars are atoms that have the same:
    [*] Mass number but different atomic numbers
    [ ] Atomic number but different mass numbers
    [ ] Number of electrons
    [ ] Chemical properties
    Explanation: Isobars are different elements with the same mass number (e.g., C-14 and N-14 both have A = 14 but Z = 6 and Z = 7 respectively).

  Q20. Tritium (H-3) is a radioisotope because it:
    [*] Has an unstable nucleus and undergoes beta decay
    [ ] Has no neutrons
    [ ] Is the most common hydrogen isotope
    [ ] Is completely stable
    Explanation: Tritium has 1 proton and 2 neutrons. Its excess neutrons make it unstable; it undergoes beta decay with a half-life of about 12.3 years.

  Q21. A geologist uses potassium-40 dating on a rock sample. K-40 decays to Ar-40 with a half-life of 1.25 billion years. If 75% of the original K-40 has decayed, how old is the rock?
    [*] 2.5 billion years
    [ ] 1.25 billion years
    [ ] 3.75 billion years
    [ ] 625 million years
    Explanation: 75% decayed means 25% remains = (1/2)². Two half-lives: 2 × 1.25 billion = 2.5 billion years.

  Q22. An archaeologist finds a wooden artifact with only 12.5% of its original C-14 remaining. Approximately how old is the artifact?
    [*] About 17,190 years
    [ ] About 5,730 years
    [ ] About 11,460 years
    [ ] About 28,650 years
    Explanation: 12.5% = (1/2)³ = 3 half-lives. 3 × 5,730 years = 17,190 years old.

  Q23. A chemist needs to calculate the atomic mass of an element with two isotopes: X-10 (20% abundance) and X-11 (80% abundance). What is the atomic mass?
    [*] 10.8 amu
    [ ] 10.5 amu
    [ ] 11.0 amu
    [ ] 10.2 amu
    Explanation: Weighted average: (0.20 × 10) + (0.80 × 11) = 2.0 + 8.8 = 10.8 amu. The heavier isotope dominates because it is more abundant.

  Q24. A nuclear medicine technician prepares Tc-99m (half-life 6 hours) for a patient scan at 8 AM. By what time will only 25% of the original activity remain?
    [*] 8 PM the same day
    [ ] 2 PM the same day
    [ ] 8 AM the next day
    [ ] Noon the same day
    Explanation: 25% = (1/2)² = 2 half-lives. 2 × 6 hours = 12 hours after 8 AM = 8 PM.

  Q25. A mass spectrometer analysis shows two peaks for neon: one at mass 20 (90.5%) and one at mass 22 (9.5%). What is the approximate atomic mass of neon?
    [*] 20.19 amu
    [ ] 21.0 amu
    [ ] 20.0 amu
    [ ] 22.0 amu
    Explanation: (0.905 × 20) + (0.095 × 22) = 18.10 + 2.09 = 20.19 amu. This is very close to the periodic table value of 20.18 for neon.

  Q26. Heavy water (D₂O) is used in some nuclear reactors. What makes it 'heavy'?
    [*] It contains deuterium (H-2) instead of protium (H-1), adding extra neutrons
    [ ] It contains tritium instead of oxygen
    [ ] It is denser because it is frozen
    [ ] It contains dissolved lead
    Explanation: Deuterium has 1 proton + 1 neutron (mass 2) vs. protium's 1 proton (mass 1). D₂O has molecular mass ~20 vs. H₂O's ~18, making it about 11% heavier.

  Q27. A forensic scientist uses isotope ratios of strontium (Sr-87/Sr-86) in teeth to determine where a person grew up. This works because:
    [*] Different geographic regions have distinct isotope ratios in soil and water
    [ ] All teeth contain the same isotope ratios
    [ ] Strontium is radioactive and decays in teeth
    [ ] Sr-87 converts to Sr-86 after death
    Explanation: Strontium isotope ratios vary by geological region. Since teeth form during childhood and preserve these ratios, they serve as a geographic fingerprint.

  Q28. An element has three isotopes: A-24 (78.99%), A-25 (10.00%), and A-26 (11.01%). Which isotope's mass contributes most to the weighted average?
    [*] A-24, because it is most abundant
    [ ] A-26, because it is heaviest
    [ ] A-25, because it is the middle value
    [ ] They all contribute equally
    Explanation: In a weighted average, the most abundant isotope has the largest weight factor. A-24 at 78.99% dominates the calculation, pulling the average close to 24.

  Q29. A hospital uses I-131 (radioisotope, half-life 8 days) to treat thyroid conditions. Why is a short-lived radioisotope preferred for medical use?
    [*] It delivers the treatment quickly and doesn't remain radioactive in the body for long
    [ ] Short half-lives produce more radiation overall
    [ ] Short half-lives mean the isotope is not radioactive
    [ ] Long-lived isotopes cannot target specific organs
    Explanation: Short-lived radioisotopes decay quickly, minimizing prolonged radiation exposure. I-131 targets the thyroid, treats the condition, and largely decays within weeks.

  Q30. Scientists discover a new mineral containing element Z. Mass spectrometry shows Z-50 at 60% and Z-52 at 40%. What is the approximate atomic mass of element Z?
    [*] 50.8 amu
    [ ] 51.0 amu
    [ ] 50.0 amu
    [ ] 52.0 amu
    Explanation: (0.60 × 50) + (0.40 × 52) = 30 + 20.8 = 50.8 amu. The result sits between the two isotope masses, weighted toward the more abundant Z-50.

--- Lesson 3.7:  (Summary) ---

  Notes
Key Concepts: Element Synthesis

Core Definitions

  • Element Synthesis: The creation of new elements by bombarding target nuclei with high-speed particles.
  • Particle Accelerator: A machine that speeds up charged particles to collide with target atoms.
  • Transuranium Elements: Elements with atomic numbers greater than 92 (uranium); all are synthetic.
  • Nuclear Fusion: Combining two lighter nuclei to form a heavier one; releases enormous energy.
  • Synthetic Elements: Elements not found naturally; created in labs using nuclear reactions.
  • Super-heavy Elements: Elements with very high atomic numbers (e.g., oganesson, element 118); extremely unstable.

--- Lesson 3.7:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Nuclear fusion in stars primarily converts hydrogen into:
    [*] Helium
    [ ] Iron
    [ ] Carbon
    [ ] Oxygen
    Explanation: In main-sequence stars, hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium, releasing enormous energy (E = mc²). This is the dominant energy source for most of a star's lifetime.

  Q2. Elements heavier than iron are primarily formed by:
    [*] Supernova explosions and neutron star mergers
    [ ] Normal stellar fusion
    [ ] Chemical reactions on Earth
    [ ] Solar wind
    Explanation: Fusion up to iron releases energy, but forming heavier elements requires energy input. The extreme conditions of supernovae and neutron star mergers provide this energy via rapid neutron capture (r-process).

  Q3. Transuranium elements have atomic numbers greater than:
    [*] 92 (uranium)
    [ ] 82 (lead)
    [ ] 100 (fermium)
    [ ] 118 (oganesson)
    Explanation: Uranium (Z = 92) is the heaviest naturally occurring element in significant quantities. All elements beyond it (neptunium, plutonium, etc.) are synthetic — created in labs.

  Q4. Synthetic elements are elements that:
    [*] Are created in laboratories using nuclear reactions
    [ ] Occur naturally on Earth
    [ ] Are always stable
    [ ] Have no protons
    Explanation: Synthetic elements are produced by bombarding target nuclei with accelerated particles. They are generally unstable, with short half-lives.

  Q5. What device is used to accelerate particles for creating new elements?
    [*] Particle accelerator
    [ ] Mass spectrometer
    [ ] Geiger counter
    [ ] Nuclear reactor
    Explanation: Particle accelerators use electromagnetic fields to speed up charged particles to near the speed of light, then slam them into target atoms to produce new elements.

  Q6. The heaviest naturally occurring element is:
    [*] Uranium (Z = 92)
    [ ] Plutonium (Z = 94)
    [ ] Oganesson (Z = 118)
    [ ] Fermium (Z = 100)
    Explanation: While trace amounts of Np and Pu exist naturally, uranium is the heaviest element found in significant quantities in nature.

  Q7. Stellar nucleosynthesis is the process of:
    [*] Creating elements inside stars through nuclear fusion
    [ ] Splitting atoms on Earth
    [ ] Destroying elements in space
    [ ] Cooling stars
    Explanation: Stars build heavier elements by fusing lighter ones: H → He → C → O → ... → Fe. Each stage requires higher temperatures and pressures.

  Q8. Iron (Fe) is significant in stellar fusion because:
    [*] Fusing elements heavier than iron consumes energy rather than releasing it
    [ ] Iron cannot exist in stars
    [ ] Iron is the lightest element
    [ ] Iron fusion produces the most energy
    Explanation: Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon. Fusing elements beyond iron is endothermic (energy-consuming), which is why stars cannot sustain fusion past iron and may collapse into supernovae.

  Q9. Superheavy elements (Z > 103) are characterized by being:
    [*] Extremely unstable with very short half-lives
    [ ] Found commonly in nature
    [ ] Very stable and long-lived
    [ ] Used widely in industry
    Explanation: Superheavy elements exist for fractions of a second to minutes. Their enormous nuclear charge creates extreme instability, and they are detected by their decay products.

  Q10. The Big Bang primarily produced which light elements?
    [*] Hydrogen and helium (with traces of lithium)
    [ ] Iron and nickel
    [ ] Carbon and oxygen
    [ ] Uranium and plutonium
    Explanation: Big Bang nucleosynthesis occurred minutes after the Big Bang, producing ~75% hydrogen, ~25% helium, and trace lithium. All heavier elements were made later in stars.

  Q11. What is the definition of nuclear fusion?
    [*] The combining of two lighter nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy
    [ ] The splitting of a heavy nucleus into lighter fragments
    [ ] The emission of gamma rays from a nucleus
    [ ] The absorption of neutrons by a nucleus
    Explanation: Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei (e.g., H + H → He) and releases energy because the product has lower mass per nucleon. This powers all main-sequence stars.

  Q12. What is a particle accelerator?
    [*] A machine that speeds up charged particles using electromagnetic fields for nuclear collisions
    [ ] A device that slows down radioactive decay
    [ ] An instrument that measures atomic mass
    [ ] A container for storing nuclear waste
    Explanation: Particle accelerators (like cyclotrons and linear accelerators) use electric and magnetic fields to accelerate ions to enormous speeds before colliding them with target nuclei.

  Q13. What does 'transuranium' mean?
    [*] Beyond uranium — elements with atomic number greater than 92
    [ ] Elements lighter than uranium
    [ ] Isotopes of uranium only
    [ ] Elements that do not exist
    Explanation: The prefix 'trans-' means 'beyond.' Transuranium elements (Np, Pu, Am, Cm, etc.) all have Z > 92 and are synthetic.

  Q14. What is nucleosynthesis?
    [*] The formation of new elements through nuclear reactions in stars or the Big Bang
    [ ] The chemical combination of elements into compounds
    [ ] The decay of radioactive isotopes
    [ ] The synthesis of proteins in cells
    Explanation: Nucleosynthesis encompasses Big Bang nucleosynthesis (light elements), stellar nucleosynthesis (up to iron), and supernova/merger nucleosynthesis (elements heavier than iron).

  Q15. What is the definition of nuclear fission?
    [*] The splitting of a heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei, releasing energy
    [ ] The combining of light nuclei
    [ ] Radioactive decay only
    [ ] The emission of beta particles
    Explanation: Fission splits heavy nuclei (like U-235) into smaller fragments plus neutrons and energy. It is the process used in nuclear power plants and atomic weapons.

  Q16. What is the s-process in nucleosynthesis?
    [*] Slow neutron capture in red giant stars, building heavier elements over thousands of years
    [ ] Rapid proton capture in supernovae
    [ ] Solar fusion of hydrogen
    [ ] Chemical synthesis of compounds
    Explanation: The s-process (slow process) occurs in AGB stars. Nuclei slowly capture neutrons one at a time, with time to beta-decay between captures, building elements up to bismuth.

  Q17. What is the r-process in nucleosynthesis?
    [*] Rapid neutron capture during supernovae or neutron star mergers, producing the heaviest elements
    [ ] Regular fusion in main-sequence stars
    [ ] Chemical reactions in laboratories
    [ ] Slow proton addition in red dwarfs
    Explanation: The r-process captures many neutrons in seconds during extreme events, forming highly neutron-rich nuclei that then beta-decay to create stable heavy elements like gold and platinum.

  Q18. The element oganesson (Og, Z = 118) is notable because it:
    [*] Is the heaviest element currently confirmed on the periodic table
    [ ] Is found naturally in seawater
    [ ] Is the most stable synthetic element
    [ ] Was discovered in the 1800s
    Explanation: Oganesson was first synthesized in 2002 and officially named in 2016. Only a few atoms have ever been created; it decays in milliseconds.

  Q19. Why does fusion require extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees)?
    [*] To overcome the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged nuclei
    [ ] To melt the nuclei into liquid
    [ ] To slow the nuclei down enough to combine
    [ ] To remove all electrons first
    Explanation: Nuclei are positively charged and repel each other. Only at extreme temperatures do they move fast enough to overcome this Coulomb barrier and get close enough for the strong nuclear force to bind them.

  Q20. The 'island of stability' refers to:
    [*] A predicted region of superheavy elements with relatively long half-lives
    [ ] A group of naturally occurring stable elements
    [ ] The noble gases on the periodic table
    [ ] Stable isotopes of iron
    Explanation: Theoretical models predict that certain superheavy nuclei with 'magic numbers' of protons and neutrons should be more stable than neighboring elements, forming an 'island' amid a 'sea' of instability.

    [*] 118 (oganesson)
    [ ] 92 (uranium)
    [ ] 100 (fermium)
    [ ] 82 (lead)
    Explanation: Ca has Z = 20, Cf has Z = 98. Combined: 20 + 98 = 118 (oganesson). This is the actual reaction used to first create element 118.

    [*] The reaction accelerates uncontrollably (meltdown risk)
    [ ] The reactor shuts down automatically
    [ ] Fusion begins
    [ ] Nothing — extra neutrons are harmless
    Explanation: In fission, each U-235 split releases 2-3 neutrons that can trigger more fissions. Without control rods absorbing excess neutrons, the chain reaction grows exponentially.

    [*] Sustaining the extreme temperatures and pressures needed to confine plasma
    [ ] Finding enough hydrogen
    [ ] Fusion produces too much waste
    [ ] Fusion reactions are too slow
    Explanation: Fusion requires temperatures over 100 million °C to overcome Coulomb repulsion. Containing this plasma with magnetic fields (tokamaks) or lasers (inertial confinement) remains the primary engineering challenge.

  Q25. Technetium (Z = 43) is notable because it is the lightest element with:
    [*] No stable isotopes — all are radioactive

    Explanation: Despite its low atomic number, technetium has no stable isotopes due to its nuclear structure. It was the first element to be artificially produced (1937).

  Q26. A newly synthesized superheavy element decays in 0.9 milliseconds. Scientists detect it by observing:
    [*] Its chain of alpha decays to known daughter elements
    [ ] Its color under a microscope
    [ ] Its chemical reactions with water
    [ ] Its melting point
    Explanation: Superheavy elements are so short-lived that they are identified by their decay signatures — a specific chain of alpha decays producing known isotopes, confirming the parent element.

    [ ] Fusion doesn't actually consume hydrogen
    [ ] The Sun absorbs hydrogen from other stars
    Explanation: The Sun's mass is about 2 × 10³⁰ kg. Even at 600 million tons/second, it has enough hydrogen to sustain fusion for approximately 10 billion years total.

  Q28. Plutonium-239 is produced in nuclear reactors when U-238 captures a neutron and undergoes two beta decays. This is an example of:
    [*] Artificial transmutation producing a transuranium element

    [ ] Nuclear fusion
    [ ] Chemical synthesis
    Explanation: U-238 + n → U-239 → Np-239 (beta) → Pu-239 (beta). This converts a natural element into a synthetic transuranium element through neutron capture and beta decays.

    [*] Stars that exploded before our solar system formed
    [ ] The Big Bang
    [ ] Earth's core
    [ ] The Moon
    Explanation: The Big Bang only made H, He, and trace Li. Heavier elements like C, N, and O were forged by nuclear fusion in previous generations of stars and scattered by supernovae — making us literally 'star stuff.'

  Q30. Scientists predict an 'island of stability' near Z ≈ 114. Element flerovium (Fl, Z = 114) has a longest-lived isotope with a half-life of ~2.6 seconds. This suggests:
    [*] It is relatively long-lived for a superheavy element, partially supporting the theory
    [ ] The island of stability theory is completely wrong
    [ ] Flerovium is completely stable

--- Lesson 3.8:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Atomic Theory
Core Definitions

  • Democritus: Greek philosopher who first proposed that matter is made of small, indivisible particles called 'atomos.'
  • Dalton's Atomic Theory: All matter is made of atoms; atoms of the same element are identical; atoms combine in whole-number ratios.
  • Thomson's Model: Discovered the electron; proposed the 'plum pudding' model with negative charges in positive mass.
  • Rutherford's Model: Discovered the nucleus through the gold foil experiment; most of the atom is empty space.
  • Bohr's Model: Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels (like planets around the sun).
  • Modern Model: The quantum mechanical model; electrons exist in probability clouds (orbitals), not fixed paths.

--- Lesson 3.8:  (Quiz) ---

  Q1. Who first proposed that matter is made of tiny indivisible particles called 'atomos'?
    [*] Democritus
    [ ] Aristotle
    [ ] Dalton
    [ ] Newton
    Explanation: The Greek philosopher Democritus (~460–370 BC) proposed that all matter consists of indivisible particles he called 'atomos.' His was a philosophical idea, not based on experiments.

  Q2. John Dalton's atomic theory (early 1800s) stated that:
    [*] All matter is made of atoms; atoms of the same element are identical and combine in whole-number ratios
    [ ] Atoms contain a nucleus
    [ ] Electrons orbit in fixed energy levels
    [ ] Atoms are infinitely divisible
    Explanation: Dalton provided the first experimental atomic theory. While some postulates were later revised (atoms are divisible; isotopes show not all atoms of an element are identical), his framework was groundbreaking.

  Q3. J.J. Thomson discovered the electron using:
    [*] Cathode ray tubes
    [ ] Gold foil experiments
    [ ] Mass spectrometry
    [ ] Spectral analysis
    Explanation: Thomson (1897) observed cathode rays deflecting in electric/magnetic fields, proving they were negatively charged particles (electrons) much lighter than atoms.

  Q4. Thomson's model of the atom is called the:
    [*] Plum pudding model
    [ ] Planetary model
    [ ] Quantum model
    [ ] Nuclear model
    Explanation: Thomson proposed electrons were embedded in a uniform sphere of positive charge — like plums in a pudding. This was disproved by Rutherford's gold foil experiment.

  Q5. Rutherford's gold foil experiment revealed that:
    [*] Most of the atom is empty space with a dense, positive nucleus
    [ ] Electrons orbit in fixed shells
    [ ] Atoms are solid spheres
    [ ] Atoms have no internal structure
    Explanation: Most alpha particles passed through the gold foil (empty space), but some bounced back at large angles, revealing a tiny, dense, positive nucleus at the center.

  Q6. Bohr's model proposed that electrons:
    [*] Orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels (quantized orbits)
    [ ] Are embedded in positive charge
    [ ] Exist in probability clouds
    [ ] Cannot move between energy levels
    Explanation: Bohr (1913) proposed electrons travel in circular orbits at fixed distances from the nucleus, each corresponding to a specific energy level. Electrons jump between levels by absorbing or emitting photons.

  Q7. The modern quantum mechanical model describes electrons as:
    [*] Existing in probability regions (orbitals), not fixed paths
    [ ] Particles in fixed circular orbits
    [ ] Stationary in the nucleus
    [ ] Embedded in positive charge
    Explanation: The quantum mechanical model (Schrödinger, Heisenberg) treats electrons as wave-particle entities described by probability distributions (orbitals), not definite orbits.

  Q8. Which atomic model correctly explained the hydrogen emission spectrum?
    [*] Bohr's model
    [ ] Dalton's model
    [ ] Thomson's model
    [ ] Democritus's idea
    Explanation: Bohr's quantized energy levels explained why hydrogen emits specific wavelengths: electrons jumping between levels emit photons of exact energies, producing a discrete line spectrum.

  Q9. Dalton's atomic theory was the first to include:
    [*] Experimental evidence and the law of definite proportions
    [ ] The concept of electrons
    [ ] The nuclear model
    [ ] Quantum numbers
    Explanation: Unlike Democritus's purely philosophical idea, Dalton's theory was backed by experimental observations about chemical reactions and the fixed ratios in which elements combine.

  Q10. The correct chronological order of atomic models is:
    [*] Democritus → Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Bohr → Quantum
    [ ] Dalton → Democritus → Bohr → Thomson → Rutherford → Quantum
    [ ] Thomson → Rutherford → Democritus → Dalton → Bohr → Quantum
    [ ] Bohr → Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Quantum → Democritus
    Explanation: Democritus (~400 BC) → Dalton (1803) → Thomson (1897) → Rutherford (1911) → Bohr (1913) → Quantum Mechanical (1920s). Each built on the shortcomings of the previous model.

  Q11. What is Dalton's atomic theory?
    [*] A theory stating atoms are indivisible, identical for each element, and combine in fixed ratios
    [ ] A theory about electron orbitals
    [ ] A description of nuclear structure
    [ ] The quantum wave equation
    Explanation: Dalton's key postulates: (1) all matter is made of atoms, (2) atoms of the same element are identical, (3) atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios, (4) atoms are not created or destroyed in reactions.

  Q12. What is the plum pudding model?
    [*] Thomson's model showing electrons embedded in a sphere of positive charge
    [ ] Bohr's model of electron orbits
    [ ] Rutherford's nuclear model
    [ ] The quantum orbital model
    Explanation: The plum pudding model envisioned the atom as a sphere of diffuse positive charge with negative electrons scattered throughout — like raisins (plums) in a pudding.

  Q13. What is the nuclear model of the atom?
    [*] Rutherford's model with a small, dense, positive nucleus surrounded by electrons
    [ ] Thomson's plum pudding model
    [ ] Dalton's solid sphere model
    [ ] The quantum mechanical model
    Explanation: Rutherford (1911) proposed that nearly all atomic mass is concentrated in a tiny nucleus, with electrons orbiting at relatively large distances — mostly empty space.

  Q14. What is the Bohr model?
    [*] A model where electrons occupy fixed circular orbits at specific energy levels around the nucleus
    [ ] A model with electrons in probability clouds
    [ ] A model with no nucleus
    [ ] A model with electrons embedded in positive charge
    Explanation: Bohr's model successfully explained hydrogen's line spectrum by quantizing electron orbits. Electrons can only exist at certain distances from the nucleus, each with a fixed energy.

  Q15. What is the quantum mechanical model?
    [*] The current model treating electrons as waves described by probability distributions (orbitals)
    [ ] Bohr's circular orbit model
    [ ] Dalton's indivisible sphere model
    [ ] Thomson's plum pudding model
    Explanation: Developed by Schrödinger and Heisenberg in the 1920s, this model uses wave functions to calculate the probability of finding an electron in a given region (orbital).

  Q16. What is the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle?
    [*] It is impossible to know both the exact position and momentum of an electron simultaneously
    [ ] Electrons always have known positions
    [ ] Atoms are indivisible
    [ ] Energy levels are continuous, not quantized
    Explanation: Heisenberg showed that the more precisely you know an electron's position, the less precisely you can know its momentum, and vice versa. This is a fundamental limit, not a measurement error.

  Q17. What experiment disproved Thomson's plum pudding model?
    [*] Rutherford's gold foil experiment
    [ ] Thomson's cathode ray experiment
    [ ] Millikan's oil drop experiment
    [ ] Dalton's gas law experiments
    Explanation: If the plum pudding model were correct, alpha particles would pass straight through gold foil with minimal deflection. The large-angle deflections Rutherford observed proved a concentrated nucleus exists.

  Q18. The principal limitation of Bohr's model is that it:
    [*] Only works accurately for hydrogen (single-electron atoms)
    [ ] Could not explain any atomic spectra
    [ ] Placed electrons inside the nucleus
    [ ] Predicted continuous spectra
    Explanation: Bohr's model worked beautifully for hydrogen but failed for multi-electron atoms because it didn't account for electron-electron repulsion and wave-particle duality.

  Q19. Which part of Dalton's theory has been disproved?
    [*] Atoms are indivisible — they actually contain subatomic particles
    [ ] Matter is made of atoms
    [ ] Atoms combine in whole-number ratios
    [ ] Atoms are conserved in reactions
    Explanation: We now know atoms are divisible into protons, neutrons, and electrons. Also, not all atoms of the same element are identical (isotopes). The conservation and ratio postulates remain valid.

  Q20. Who discovered the neutron?
    [*] James Chadwick (1932)
    [ ] J.J. Thomson (1897)
    [ ] Ernest Rutherford (1911)
    [ ] Niels Bohr (1913)
    Explanation: Chadwick proved the existence of neutrons by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles. The neutral particles emitted had nearly the same mass as protons.

  Q21. A student shines a beam of alpha particles at thin gold foil and finds that 1 in 8,000 bounces back. According to Rutherford's model, why?
    [*] Those particles hit the tiny, dense nucleus and were repelled by its positive charge
    [ ] The gold atoms absorbed the alpha particles
    [ ] Electrons deflected the alpha particles
    [ ] The gold foil was too thick
    Explanation: The nucleus occupies only ~10⁻¹⁵ m of a ~10⁻¹⁰ m atom. The rare large deflections occur when a positively charged alpha particle directly approaches the tiny, dense, positive nucleus.

  Q22. A chemistry class observes hydrogen's emission spectrum showing specific colored lines (red, blue-green, blue, violet). This observation best supports which model?
    [*] Bohr's model — quantized energy levels explain discrete spectral lines
    [ ] Thomson's model — electrons are spread uniformly
    [ ] Dalton's model — atoms are indivisible
    [ ] Democritus's model — atoms are the smallest particles
    Explanation: Discrete spectral lines mean electrons can only have certain energies. When they drop from higher to lower levels, they emit photons of specific wavelengths — exactly what Bohr predicted.

  Q23. An electron in a hydrogen atom jumps from n = 3 to n = 1. According to Bohr's model, it will:
    [*] Emit a photon with energy equal to the difference between the two levels
    [ ] Absorb a photon
    [ ] Remain at the same energy
    [ ] Leave the atom entirely
    Explanation: Dropping from a higher to lower energy level releases the energy difference as a photon: E_photon = E₃ − E₁. The photon's frequency is given by E = hf.

  Q24. A researcher measures the exact position of an electron but now knows almost nothing about its momentum. This illustrates:
    [*] The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
    [ ] Dalton's atomic theory
    [ ] Bohr's fixed orbits
    [ ] Thomson's plum pudding model
    Explanation: Heisenberg's principle states Δx · Δp ≥ ħ/2. Pinpointing position (small Δx) necessarily increases momentum uncertainty (large Δp). This is a fundamental quantum limit.

  Q25. Millikan's oil drop experiment determined that electrons have:
    [*] A specific, quantized charge (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
    [ ] No charge
    [ ] A positive charge
    [ ] A variable charge
    Explanation: Millikan (1909) suspended charged oil droplets in an electric field and calculated the charge on individual electrons as e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs.

  Q26. A textbook published in 1900 describes atoms as solid, indivisible spheres. What discovery two years earlier had already challenged this view?
    [*] Thomson's discovery of the electron (1897) proved atoms have internal structure
    [ ] Rutherford's gold foil experiment
    [ ] Bohr's hydrogen model
    [ ] Chadwick's neutron discovery
    Explanation: Thomson's 1897 cathode ray experiments proved electrons exist within atoms, demonstrating that atoms are not the smallest unit of matter and have internal structure.

  Q27. A student produces an energy level diagram for helium but finds Bohr's model gives incorrect predictions. The most likely reason is:
    [*] Bohr's model doesn't account for electron-electron interactions in multi-electron atoms
    [ ] Helium has no electrons
    [ ] Bohr's model only applies to negative ions
    [ ] Helium is not a real element
    Explanation: Helium has 2 electrons that repel each other. Bohr's model treats electrons independently with no inter-electron repulsion, so it fails for any atom with more than one electron.

  Q28. If Rutherford's gold foil experiment had shown ALL alpha particles passing straight through with zero deflection, it would have supported:
    [*] Thomson's plum pudding model (uniform positive charge, no concentrated nucleus)
    [ ] Bohr's orbital model
    [ ] The quantum mechanical model
    [ ] Dalton's solid sphere model
    Explanation: A diffuse, uniform positive charge (plum pudding) would deflect alpha particles only slightly. It was the large back-scattering that disproved this and established the nuclear model.

  Q29. Scientists observe that heated sodium emits a characteristic yellow-orange glow at specific wavelengths. Which atomic model best explains this?
    [*] The quantum mechanical model — electrons transition between quantized energy states
    [ ] Dalton's solid sphere model
    [ ] Thomson's plum pudding model
    [ ] Democritus's atomos idea
    Explanation: Sodium's emission spectrum arises from electron transitions between specific energy levels. The quantum mechanical model (and Bohr's for simple cases) explains these discrete emission wavelengths.

  Q30. An orbital diagram shows a '90% probability cloud' for locating an electron. This concept comes from which model?
    [*] The quantum mechanical (Schrödinger) model
    [ ] Bohr's model (fixed circular orbits)
    [ ] Rutherford's nuclear model
    [ ] Thomson's plum pudding model
    Explanation: The Schrödinger equation gives |ψ|², the probability density for finding an electron. Orbitals are drawn as regions where there is a 90% probability of finding the electron.
