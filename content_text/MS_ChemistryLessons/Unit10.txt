--- Lesson 10.1:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acid & Base Properties
Core Definitions

  • Acid: A substance that produces H⁺ (hydrogen) ions in solution; tastes sour; pH below 7.
  • Base: A substance that produces OH⁻ (hydroxide) ions in solution; tastes bitter, feels slippery; pH above 7.
  • Arrhenius Definition: Acids produce H⁺ in water; bases produce OH⁻ in water.
  • Brønsted-Lowry Definition: Acids are proton (H⁺) donors; bases are proton acceptors.

--- Lesson 10.2:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Binary Acids vs Oxyacids
Core Definitions

  • Binary Acid: An acid made of hydrogen and one other nonmetal element (e.g., HCl, HBr).
  • Oxyacid: An acid containing hydrogen, oxygen, and another element (e.g., H₂SO₄, HNO₃).
  • Binary Acid Naming: Use the prefix "hydro-" + root of the element + "-ic acid" (e.g., hydrochloric acid).
  • Oxyacid Naming: If the polyatomic ion ends in "-ate," the acid ends in "-ic acid"; if "-ite," then "-ous acid."

--- Lesson 10.3:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Acids
Core Definitions

  • Naming Binary Acids: hydro- + element root + -ic acid. Example: HBr → hydrobromic acid.
  • Naming Oxyacids (-ate): Drop "-ate," add "-ic acid." Example: H₂SO₄ (sulfate → sulfuric acid).
  • Naming Oxyacids (-ite): Drop "-ite," add "-ous acid." Example: H₂SO₃ (sulfite → sulfurous acid).
  • Writing Formulas: Identify the anion, determine its charge, balance with H⁺ ions.

--- Lesson 10.4:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Identifying Acids & Bases
Core Definitions

  • Indicators: Chemicals that change color depending on pH (e.g., litmus, phenolphthalein).
  • Litmus Test: Blue litmus turns red in acid; red litmus turns blue in base.
  • Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acid, turns pink/magenta in base (pH > 8.2).
  • pH Paper: Universal indicator paper that shows a range of colors for different pH values.

--- Lesson 10.5:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Strong vs Weak Acids & Bases
Core Definitions

  • Strong Acid: Completely dissociates (ionizes) in water. Examples: HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄.
  • Weak Acid: Only partially dissociates in water; establishes equilibrium. Example: CH₃COOH (acetic acid).
  • Strong Base: Completely dissociates in water. Examples: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂.
  • Weak Base: Only partially dissociates in water. Example: NH₃ (ammonia).

--- Lesson 10.6:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Neutralization Reactions
Core Definitions

  • Neutralization: A reaction between an acid and a base that produces water and a salt.
  • General Equation: Acid + Base → Salt + Water (HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O).
  • Titration: A lab technique to determine the concentration of an acid or base using a neutralization reaction.
  • Equivalence Point: The point in a titration where moles of acid equal moles of base.

--- Lesson 10.7:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Salts from Neutralization
Core Definitions

  • Salt: An ionic compound formed from the cation of a base and the anion of an acid.
  • Naming Rule: The cation comes from the base (metal), and the anion comes from the acid.
  • From Binary Acids: The anion keeps the "-ide" ending. Example: HCl + NaOH → NaCl (sodium chloride).
  • From Oxyacids: The anion keeps the polyatomic name. Example: HNO₃ + KOH → KNO₃ (potassium nitrate).

Core Definitions

  • Buffer: A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added.

--- Lesson 10.10:  (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Calculating pH and pOH using Log
Core Definitions

  • pH Formula: pH = −log[H⁺], where [H⁺] is the molar concentration of hydrogen ions.
  • pOH Formula: pOH = −log[OH⁻], where [OH⁻] is the molar concentration of hydroxide ions.
  • Reverse Calculation: [H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ and [OH⁻] = 10⁻ᵖᴼᴴ.
