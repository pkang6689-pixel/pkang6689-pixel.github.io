--- Lesson 10.1: Acid & Base Properties (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Acid & Base Properties
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is pH?
pH is a scale from 0 to 14 that measures how acidic or basic a solution is. A pH below 7 is acidic, exactly 7 is neutral, and above 7 is basic (alkaline).
What is an indicator in chemistry?
An indicator is a substance that changes color depending on whether a solution is acidic or basic. Litmus paper and phenolphthalein are common examples of indicators.
What is a neutralization reaction?
A neutralization reaction occurs when an acid and a base react together to form water and a salt. For example, HCl + NaOH produces NaCl (salt) and H₂O (water).
What is an electrolyte?
An electrolyte is a substance that conducts electricity when dissolved in water because it produces ions. Acids and bases are electrolytes because they dissociate into ions in solution.
What is litmus paper used for in a chemistry lab?
Litmus paper is used to test whether a solution is acidic or basic. Blue litmus turns red in acids, and red litmus turns blue in bases. It is one of the simplest pH indicators.
Real-World Applications
Example: Which of the following is an everyday example of a base?
Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is a common household base with a pH above 7. Lemon juice, vinegar, and orange juice are all acidic substances with a pH below 7.

    [ ] Blue

--- Lesson 10.2: Binary Acids vs. Oxyacids (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Binary Acids vs. Oxyacids
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a binary acid?
A binary acid is an acid composed of exactly two elements: hydrogen and one other nonmetal. The word 'binary' means two, referring to the two elements. Examples include HCl, HBr, and HF.
What is an oxyacid?
An oxyacid is an acid that contains hydrogen, oxygen, and at least one other element. The 'oxy-' in the name refers to the presence of oxygen. Examples include H₂SO₄ and HNO₃.
What is a polyatomic ion?
A polyatomic ion is a group of two or more covalently bonded atoms that together carry an electrical charge. Examples include sulfate (SO₄²⁻), nitrate (NO₃⁻), and phosphate (PO₄³⁻).
What is the key structural difference between binary acids and oxyacids?
The key difference is the presence of oxygen. Binary acids contain only hydrogen and one nonmetal (no oxygen), while oxyacids always contain oxygen along with hydrogen and another element.
A student is naming H₃PO₄ and knows it comes from the phosphate ion (PO₄³⁻). What is the correct acid name?
Phosphate ends in '-ate,' so the acid name uses the '-ic acid' suffix: phosphoric acid. The 'hydro-' prefix is only used for binary acids. Phosphorous acid comes from phosphite (PO₃³⁻).
A student needs to classify a mystery acid with the formula HClO₂. She determines it is derived from the chlorite ion (ClO₂⁻). What type of acid is it, and what is its name?
HClO₂ contains hydrogen, chlorine, and oxygen, so it is an oxyacid. It comes from chlorite (ClO₂⁻), which ends in '-ite,' so the acid name uses '-ous acid': chlorous acid.
Real-World Applications
Example: H₂SO₄ is an example of:
H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid) contains hydrogen, sulfur, and oxygen, making it an oxyacid. It is not a binary acid because it has more than two elements.

  Q3. The naming prefix for binary acids is:
    [ ] Oxy-

  Q4. The suffix for binary acid names is:

  Q5. HCl is named:
    [*] Hydrochloric acid
    [ ] Hydrogen chloride acid
    [ ] Chloric acid

  Q6. H₂SO₄ is an example of:
    [ ] A binary acid with two elements
    [*] An oxyacid with three elements
    [ ] A neutral salt compound

  Q7. HBr is named:
    [ ] Bromic acid
    [ ] Bromous acid
    [ ] Perbromic acid
    [*] Hydrobromic acid

  Q8. HF is named:
    [ ] Fluoric acid

    [ ] A base that produces hydroxide ions

  Key Concepts: Naming Acids

What is the rule for naming an acid from a polyatomic ion ending in '-ate'?
When a polyatomic ion ends in '-ate,' you change the ending to '-ic acid' and use the element root. For example, sulfate becomes sulfuric acid, and nitrate becomes nitric acid.

Bromate (BrO₃⁻) ends in '-ate,' so the acid name uses '-ic acid': bromic acid. Hydrobromic acid is the binary acid HBr, and bromous acid would come from bromite (BrO₂⁻).

--- Lesson 10.4: Identifying Acids & Bases (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Identifying Acids & Bases
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the Arrhenius definition of an acid?
The Arrhenius definition states that an acid is a substance that produces H⁺ (hydrogen) ions in aqueous (water) solution. This was one of the first scientific definitions of acids.
What is the Bronsted-Lowry definition of a base?
The Bronsted-Lowry definition says a base is a proton (H⁺) acceptor. This is broader than Arrhenius because it works in any solvent, not just water.
What is a conjugate acid-base pair?
A conjugate acid-base pair consists of two species that differ by exactly one proton (H⁺). When an acid donates H⁺, it becomes its conjugate base, and vice versa.
What is the key limitation of the Arrhenius acid-base definition?
The Arrhenius definition only works for aqueous solutions (dissolved in water). It cannot explain acid-base behavior in non-aqueous solvents or gas-phase reactions, which is why broader definitions were developed.
What is the definition of a proton in acid-base chemistry?
In acid-base chemistry, a proton refers to a hydrogen ion (H⁺). A hydrogen atom has one proton and one electron; when it loses its electron, only the proton remains, so H⁺ is called a proton.
What is the difference between the Arrhenius and Bronsted-Lowry definitions of an acid?
The Arrhenius definition requires water (aqueous solution), while Bronsted-Lowry works in any solvent. Both involve H⁺, but Bronsted-Lowry is broader because it describes H⁺ transfer, not just H⁺ production.
A student finds that a mystery solution has a pH of 7, does not change litmus paper, and does not feel slippery. What is the solution most likely?
A pH of 7, no litmus change, and no slippery feel all indicate a neutral substance. Pure water is the most common neutral substance with a pH of exactly 7.
A student is asked to identify the conjugate base of HNO₃ in this reaction: HNO₃ + H₂O -> H₃O⁺ + NO₃⁻. What is the conjugate base?
When HNO₃ donates its H⁺ proton, it becomes NO₃⁻ (nitrate). The conjugate base is what remains after an acid loses one proton. HNO₃ and NO₃⁻ are a conjugate acid-base pair.

    Explanation: Amphoteric means a substance can act as either an acid or a base depending on what it reacts with. Water is a classic example: it can donate H⁺ (act as acid) or accept H⁺ (act as base).

    [ ] A negatively charged subatomic particle

    [ ] Bronsted-Lowry only works in water; Arrhenius works everywhere
    Explanation: The Arrhenius definition requires water (aqueous solution), while Bronsted-Lowry works in any solvent. Both involve H⁺, but Bronsted-Lowry is broader because it describes H⁺ transfer, not just H⁺ production.

--- Lesson 10.5: Strong vs. Weak Acids/Bases (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Strong vs. Weak Acids/Bases
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the difference between acid strength and acid concentration?
Strength refers to how completely an acid ionizes (strong = 100%, weak = partial). Concentration refers to how many moles of acid are dissolved per liter of solution. A strong acid can be dilute, and a weak acid can be concentrated.
What is a weak electrolyte?
A weak electrolyte only partially ionizes in water, producing fewer free ions, so it conducts electricity poorly compared to a strong electrolyte. Weak acids and weak bases are weak electrolytes.
What is the definition of 'ionization' in acid-base chemistry?
Ionization is the process of a neutral molecule breaking apart into positively and negatively charged ions. In acid-base chemistry, it describes how acids produce H⁺ and bases produce OH⁻.
What is the meaning of 'equilibrium' in the context of weak acid dissociation?
In a weak acid solution, equilibrium is reached when the rate of dissociation (breaking apart into ions) equals the rate of recombination (ions rejoining). Both processes continue, but the concentrations remain constant.
A student has a 2 M solution of acetic acid (weak) and a 0.01 M solution of HCl (strong). She asks: 'Which is the stronger acid?' What is the correct answer?
Acid strength is about the degree of ionization, not concentration. HCl completely ionizes (strong acid) while acetic acid only partially ionizes (weak acid). Concentration does not change strength.
A student is studying for a test and wants to know: can a weak acid be more corrosive than a dilute strong acid? What is the answer?
Yes, a concentrated weak acid can produce more total H⁺ ions than a very dilute strong acid. For example, 10 M acetic acid has more H⁺ than 0.0001 M HCl, even though HCl is the stronger acid.

--- Lesson 10.6: Neutralization Reactions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Neutralization Reactions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is neutralization in chemistry?
Neutralization is specifically the reaction of an acid with a base to produce water and a salt. It is a key concept in acid-base chemistry.
What is a titration?
A titration is a quantitative lab technique in which a solution of known concentration (titrant) is used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution.
What is the role of an acid-base indicator?
An acid-base indicator is a chemical that changes color at a certain pH range, signaling that the endpoint of the titration (near the equivalence point) has been reached.
What is a strong acid in chemistry?
A strong acid completely dissociates (ionizes) in water, meaning 100% of its molecules break apart into H+ ions and the corresponding anion. Examples include HCl, HNO3, and H2SO4.
What is a spectator ion?
A spectator ion is an ion that appears on both sides of the equation and does not participate in the actual chemical reaction. In neutralization, Na+ and Cl- are spectator ions in HCl + NaOH.
What is the 'endpoint' of a titration?
The endpoint is the point during a titration where the indicator visibly changes color, signaling that the equivalence point has been approximately reached.
Real-World Applications
Example: A farmer adds lime (calcium hydroxide) to acidic soil. This is an example of:
Adding lime (a base) to acidic soil is a practical application of neutralization. The base reacts with the acid in the soil, raising the pH to make it more suitable for plant growth.

--- Lesson 10.7: Naming Salts from Neutralization (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Naming Salts from Neutralization
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a binary acid?
A binary acid contains hydrogen and one other element (no oxygen). Examples include HCl (hydrochloric acid), HBr (hydrobromic acid), and HF (hydrofluoric acid).
What is an oxyacid?
An oxyacid contains hydrogen, oxygen, and at least one other element. Examples include H2SO4 (sulfuric acid), HNO3 (nitric acid), and H3PO4 (phosphoric acid).
What is a polyatomic ion?
A polyatomic ion is a group of two or more covalently bonded atoms that together carry a net electrical charge. Examples include NO3- (nitrate), SO4 2- (sulfate), and PO4 3- (phosphate).
What is the naming rule for salts from oxyacids ending in '-ic acid'?
When the parent acid ends in '-ic acid,' the corresponding salt anion ends in '-ate.' For example, sulfuric acid (H2SO4) produces sulfate salts.
What is the naming rule for salts from oxyacids ending in '-ous acid'?
When the parent acid ends in '-ous acid,' the corresponding salt anion ends in '-ite.' For example, sulfurous acid (H2SO3) produces sulfite salts.

--- Lesson 10.8: Buffer Solutions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Buffer Solutions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is a buffer solution?
A buffer solution resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added, due to the equilibrium between a weak acid and its conjugate base.
What is a conjugate base?
A conjugate base is the species that remains after an acid has donated a proton (H+). For example, when acetic acid (CH3COOH) loses H+, it becomes acetate (CH3COO-), its conjugate base.
What is a conjugate acid?
A conjugate acid is the species formed when a base accepts a proton (H+). For example, when ammonia (NH3) accepts H+, it becomes ammonium (NH4+), its conjugate acid.
What is meant by 'dynamic equilibrium' in a buffer system?
In a buffer at dynamic equilibrium, the weak acid and its conjugate base are constantly interconverting at equal rates. This allows the buffer to respond to added acid or base.
What is the 'buffering range' of a buffer?
The buffering range is typically pKa +/- 1 pH unit. Within this range, the buffer effectively resists pH changes. Outside this range, the buffer becomes ineffective.
What is the difference between a weak acid and a strong acid in buffer context?
A weak acid only partially dissociates in water, establishing equilibrium. A strong acid fully dissociates. Buffers require weak acids because the undissociated form is needed to neutralize added base.
Real-World Applications
Example: An example of a buffer system is:
Acetic acid (CH3COOH, a weak acid) and sodium acetate (CH3COONa, its conjugate base) form a classic buffer system.
Example: A swimming pool manager adds a buffer system to the pool water to maintain a pH around 7.4. This is important because:
Pool water without a buffer would experience large pH swings when chemicals, rain, or organic matter are added. A buffer keeps pH stable, protecting swimmers and equipment.

--- Lesson 10.9: pH & pOH Scale (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: pH & pOH Scale
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the ion product of water (Kw)?
The ion product of water (Kw) = [H+] x [OH-] = 1.0 x 10^-14 at 25 degrees C. This constant relationship is why pH + pOH = 14.
What is the relationship between pH and pOH?
At 25 degrees Celsius, pH + pOH = 14. This comes from the relationship Kw = [H+][OH-] = 10^-14, so -log(Kw) = pH + pOH = 14.
What is the definition of a neutral solution?
A neutral solution has equal concentrations of H+ and OH- ions. At 25 degrees C, this means pH = pOH = 7.
What is the autoionization of water?
Autoionization of water is the process where water molecules spontaneously transfer protons: H2O + H2O <-> H3O+ + OH-. This produces equal amounts of H+ and OH- in pure water.

--- Lesson 10.10: Calculating pH and pOH using Log (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Calculating pH and pOH using Log
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the base-10 logarithm (log) of a number?
The base-10 logarithm (log) of a number is the exponent to which 10 must be raised to get that number. For example, log(1000) = 3 because 10^3 = 1000.
What is the 'antilog' operation used for in pH problems?
The antilog (inverse log) is used to convert pH back to [H+]. If pH = 4, then [H+] = 10^(-4). The antilog operation undoes the logarithm.
What is the relationship between [H+] and [OH-] in any aqueous solution at 25 degrees C?
[H+] x [OH-] = Kw = 1.0 x 10^-14 at 25 degrees C. This means if you know one concentration, you can calculate the other.
What is the formula to convert from pOH to pH?
Since pH + pOH = 14, you can convert by subtracting: pH = 14 - pOH. This works at 25 degrees C.

    [*] 3
    [ ] 11
    Explanation: pH = -log(1 x 10^-3) = -(-3) = 3. The pH is simply the positive value of the exponent when the coefficient is 1.

  Q4. If pH = 5, the [H+] concentration is:
    [ ] 5 M
    [*] 1 x 10^-5 M
    [ ] 1 x 10^-9 M
    [ ] 1 x 10^5 M
    Explanation: [H+] = 10^(-pH) = 10^(-5) = 1 x 10^-5 M. To reverse the pH calculation, raise 10 to the negative power of the pH.

  Q5. If pH = 2, the pOH is:
    [ ] 2
    [ ] 7
    [ ] 14
    [*] 12
    Explanation: pH + pOH = 14, so pOH = 14 - 2 = 12. A very acidic solution (low pH) has a high pOH.

  Q6. A solution with [OH-] = 1 x 10^-4 M has a pOH of:
    [ ] 10
    [ ] 8
    [*] 4
    [ ] -4
    Explanation: pOH = -log(1 x 10^-4) = 4. The pOH is the positive exponent when the coefficient is 1.

  Q7. To find [H+] from pH, you use:
    [*] [H+] = 10^(-pH)
    [ ] [H+] = pH / 14
    [ ] [H+] = pH x 10
    [ ] [H+] = log(pH)
    Explanation: To find [H+] from pH, use the reverse formula: [H+] = 10^(-pH). Similarly, [OH-] = 10^(-pOH).

  Q8. A solution has [H+] = 1 x 10^-11 M. Its pH is:
    [ ] 3
    [*] 11
    [ ] -11
    [ ] 14
    Explanation: pH = -log(1 x 10^-11) = 11. This is a basic solution because pH > 7.

  Q9. If pOH = 6, the [OH-] concentration is:
    [*] 1 x 10^-6 M
    [ ] 1 x 10^-8 M
    [ ] 6 M exactly
    [ ] 1 x 10^6 M
    Explanation: [OH-] = 10^(-pOH) = 10^(-6) = 1 x 10^-6 M.

  Q10. A 0.01 M HCl solution (strong acid) has a pH of:
    [ ] 1
    [*] 2
    [ ] 7
    [ ] 12
    Explanation: HCl is a strong acid that fully dissociates: [H+] = 0.01 M = 1 x 10^-2 M. pH = -log(10^-2) = 2.

  Q11. What does the 'negative log' mean in the pH formula?
    [*] Take the base-10 logarithm and then change the sign to positive
    [ ] Take the natural logarithm and then multiply the result by ten
    [ ] Subtract the concentration from 14 to find the final pH value
    [ ] Divide the concentration by 10 and then round to whole numbers
    Explanation: The negative log means: first take the base-10 logarithm of the concentration, then multiply by -1. This converts small concentrations like 10^-5 into easy-to-use positive numbers like 5.

  Q12. What is the base-10 logarithm (log) of a number?
    [*] The power to which 10 must be raised to equal that number
    [ ] The number of times 10 can be divided into that number evenly
    [ ] The product of that number multiplied by 10 one time exactly
    [ ] The sum of all digits in the number added together completely
    Explanation: The base-10 logarithm (log) of a number is the exponent to which 10 must be raised to get that number. For example, log(1000) = 3 because 10^3 = 1000.

  Q13. Define the term 'strong acid' in the context of pH calculations.
    [*] An acid that fully dissociates so [H+] equals the acid concentration
    [ ] An acid that partially dissociates requiring Ka to find the H+ value
    [ ] An acid with a concentration greater than 1 M in aqueous solution
    [ ] An acid that can only react with strong bases during neutralization
    Explanation: A strong acid fully dissociates in water, meaning 100% of its molecules produce H+ ions. Therefore, [H+] equals the original acid concentration, making pH calculations straightforward.

  Q14. What is the 'antilog' operation used for in pH problems?
    [*] Converting from pH back to [H+] concentration by raising 10 to a power
    [ ] Converting from [H+] concentration to pH by taking the negative log
    [ ] Adding pH and pOH together to confirm they equal 14 at all times
    [ ] Subtracting the pH from 14 to find the corresponding pOH value
    Explanation: The antilog (inverse log) is used to convert pH back to [H+]. If pH = 4, then [H+] = 10^(-4). The antilog operation undoes the logarithm.

  Q15. What does [H+] represent in the pH formula?
    [*] The molar concentration of hydrogen ions in the solution
    [ ] The total number of hydrogen atoms in the chemical formula
    [ ] The mass of hydrogen gas produced during the reaction
    [ ] The volume of acid needed to produce one mole of hydrogen
    Explanation: [H+] represents the molar concentration (molarity) of hydrogen ions in solution, measured in moles per liter (M). It is the key variable in pH calculations.

  Q16. What does the expression 10^(-pH) calculate?
    [*] The hydrogen ion concentration [H+] from a known pH value
    [ ] The hydroxide ion concentration [OH-] from a known pH value
    [ ] The pOH of the solution from the known pH value directly
    [ ] The total ion concentration from the known pH value given
    Explanation: 10^(-pH) calculates [H+]. For example, if pH = 4, then [H+] = 10^(-4) = 0.0001 M. This is the reverse of the pH = -log[H+] formula.

  Q17. Define 'scientific notation' as used in pH calculations.
    [*] A way to write numbers as a coefficient times a power of 10
    [ ] A method of rounding numbers to the nearest whole number value
    [ ] A system for converting between Celsius and Fahrenheit scales
    [ ] A technique for balancing chemical equations using coefficients
    Explanation: Scientific notation expresses numbers as a coefficient (1-10) times a power of 10. For example, 0.001 = 1 x 10^-3. This is essential for pH calculations with very small concentrations.

  Q18. What is the relationship between [H+] and [OH-] in any aqueous solution at 25 degrees C?
    [*] Their product always equals 1.0 x 10^-14 at that temperature
    [ ] Their sum always equals 1.0 x 10^-14 at that temperature value
    [ ] Their ratio always equals 1.0 x 10^-7 at that temperature value
    [ ] Their difference always equals 1.0 x 10^-14 at that temperature
    Explanation: [H+] x [OH-] = Kw = 1.0 x 10^-14 at 25 degrees C. This means if you know one concentration, you can calculate the other.

  Q19. What does 'significant figures' mean when reporting pH values?
    [*] The decimal places in the pH should match the significant figures in [H+]
    [ ] pH values should always be rounded to the nearest whole number only
    [ ] Only the digits before the decimal point count as significant in pH
    [ ] All pH values should be reported with exactly four decimal places
    Explanation: In pH values, the number of decimal places should match the number of significant figures in the [H+] concentration. For example, [H+] = 1.0 x 10^-3 (2 sig figs) gives pH = 3.00.

  Q20. What is the formula to convert from pOH to pH?
    [*] pH = 14 - pOH at standard temperature of 25 degrees Celsius
    [ ] pH = pOH x 2 at standard temperature of 25 degrees Celsius
    [ ] pH = pOH / 14 at standard temperature of 25 degrees Celsius
    [ ] pH = 7 - pOH at standard temperature of 25 degrees Celsius
    Explanation: Since pH + pOH = 14, you can convert by subtracting: pH = 14 - pOH. This works at 25 degrees C.

  Q21. A student has a 0.001 M NaOH solution (strong base). To find the pH, the student should first find the pOH. The correct pH is:
    [ ] 3, because NaOH has a concentration of 10^-3
    [*] 11, because pOH = 3 and pH = 14 - 3 = 11
    [ ] 7, because all solutions of NaOH are neutral
    [ ] 14, because NaOH is a very strong base compound
    Explanation: NaOH is a strong base: [OH-] = 0.001 M = 10^-3 M. pOH = -log(10^-3) = 3. pH = 14 - 3 = 11.

  Q22. A lab technician measures [H+] = 2.5 x 10^-4 M. Using a calculator, the pH is approximately:
    [ ] 4.00 because the exponent is negative four
    [*] 3.60 because -log(2.5 x 10^-4) = 3.60
    [ ] 2.50 because the coefficient is 2.5 exactly
    [ ] 10.4 because 14 minus the exponent equals 10
    Explanation: pH = -log(2.5 x 10^-4). Using a calculator: log(2.5 x 10^-4) = -3.60, so pH = 3.60. When the coefficient is not 1, you need a calculator.

  Q23. A student dissolves 0.1 mol of HNO3 (strong acid) in enough water to make 1 liter of solution. The student calculates the pH as:
    [*] 1, because [H+] = 0.1 M = 10^-1 and pH = -log(10^-1) = 1
    [ ] 7, because all solutions start at a neutral pH of seven
    [ ] 13, because pOH = 1 and pH = 14 minus 1 equals 13 here
    [ ] 0.1, because pH always equals the molarity of the acid
    Explanation: HNO3 is a strong acid: [H+] = 0.1 M = 1 x 10^-1 M. pH = -log(10^-1) = 1.

  Q24. In a forensics lab, a scientist finds a substance with pH = 13. To determine the [OH-] concentration, the scientist calculates:
    [*] pOH = 1, so [OH-] = 10^-1 = 0.1 M in the substance
    [ ] pOH = 13, so [OH-] = 10^-13 M in the substance tested
    [ ] [OH-] = 13 M because pOH equals the OH- concentration
    [ ] [OH-] cannot be found from pH without additional data
    Explanation: pH = 13, so pOH = 14 - 13 = 1. [OH-] = 10^(-1) = 0.1 M. This is a very basic solution.

  Q25. A student accidentally mixes two solutions and measures pH = 4.5. To find the exact [H+], the student enters -4.5 into the calculator and presses the 10^x button, getting:
    [*] 3.16 x 10^-5 M, which is the correct H+ concentration
    [ ] 4.50 x 10^-1 M, which is the correct H+ concentration
    [ ] 1.00 x 10^-4 M, which is the correct H+ concentration
    [ ] 9.50 x 10^-5 M, which is the correct H+ concentration
    Explanation: [H+] = 10^(-4.5) = 3.16 x 10^-5 M. The antilog of -4.5 gives a value between 10^-4 and 10^-5.

  Q26. A chemist prepares a 0.05 M solution of Ba(OH)2, a strong base that produces 2 OH- ions per formula unit. The pH of this solution is:
    [*] 13, because [OH-] = 0.10 M and pOH = 1, so pH = 13
    [ ] 12, because [OH-] = 0.05 M and pOH = 1.3, so pH = 12.7
    [ ] 1, because the concentration is 0.05 and pH = -log(0.05)
    [ ] 7, because all barium compounds produce neutral solutions
    Explanation: Ba(OH)2 produces 2 OH- per formula unit: [OH-] = 2 x 0.05 = 0.10 M. pOH = -log(0.10) = 1. pH = 14 - 1 = 13.

  Q27. A student is given a mystery solution with [H+] = 1 x 10^-7 M. After calculating, the student determines the solution is:
    [ ] Strongly acidic because the H+ concentration is very high
    [*] Neutral with a pH of 7 and a pOH of 7 as well
    [ ] Strongly basic because 10^-7 is a very small number
    [ ] Cannot be classified without knowing the OH- concentration
    Explanation: pH = -log(10^-7) = 7. A pH of 7 is neutral. Both [H+] and [OH-] are 10^-7 M, so pH = pOH = 7.

  Q28. A water treatment plant needs the treated water to have a [H+] no greater than 1 x 10^-6 M. This means the minimum acceptable pH is:
    [*] 6, because pH = -log(1 x 10^-6) = 6 for this concentration
    [ ] 8, because 14 minus 6 equals 8 and that is the minimum pH
    [ ] 14, because the water must be completely free of hydrogen ions
    [ ] 1, because any pH above 1 is safe for human consumption use
    Explanation: pH = -log(1 x 10^-6) = 6. If [H+] must be at most 10^-6 M, the pH must be at least 6 (since lower [H+] means higher pH).

  Q29. A student measures pH = 8.3 for a lake water sample. To find the [OH-], the student correctly works through these steps:
    [*] pOH = 14 - 8.3 = 5.7, then [OH-] = 10^-5.7 = 2.0 x 10^-6 M
    [ ] [OH-] = 10^-8.3 directly because pH gives OH- concentration
    [ ] [OH-] = 8.3 / 14 = 0.59 M because you divide pH by 14 always
    [ ] [OH-] = 14 - 8.3 = 5.7 M because pOH equals the concentration
    Explanation: First find pOH: 14 - 8.3 = 5.7. Then [OH-] = 10^(-5.7) = approximately 2.0 x 10^-6 M. You must convert pH to pOH first, then use the antilog.

  Q30. A pharmaceutical company requires a drug solution to have a pH between 3.0 and 4.0. A chemist prepares a solution with [H+] = 5.0 x 10^-4 M. Does it meet the requirement?
    [*] Yes, because pH = -log(5.0 x 10^-4) = 3.30, which is between 3 and 4
    [ ] No, because pH = 4.0 exactly, which is at the boundary not between
    [ ] No, because pH = 2.70, which is below the required range of 3 to 4
    [ ] Yes, because any H+ concentration in scientific notation is acceptable
    Explanation: pH = -log(5.0 x 10^-4) = -log(5.0) - log(10^-4) = -0.70 + 4 = 3.30. Since 3.30 is between 3.0 and 4.0, the solution meets the requirement.
