--- Lesson 2.1: Scientific Notation (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Scientific Notation
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 4,500,000 expressed in scientific notation?
Move the decimal 6 places left to get 4.5, so 4,500,000 = 4.5 × 10⁶. The coefficient must be between 1 and 10.
What is 0.00032 written in scientific notation?
Move the decimal 4 places right to get 3.2. Since the original number is less than 1, the exponent is negative: 3.2 × 10⁻⁴.
What is (3 × 10⁴) × (2 × 10³)?
Multiply the coefficients (3 × 2 = 6) and add the exponents (4 + 3 = 7), giving 6 × 10⁷.
What is 7.3 × 10⁻² written in standard form?
A negative exponent of −2 means move the decimal 2 places to the left: 7.3 → 0.073.
What is (8 × 10⁶) ÷ (4 × 10²)?
Divide coefficients (8 ÷ 4 = 2) and subtract exponents (6 − 2 = 4), giving 2 × 10⁴.
What is the result of (5.0 × 10³) + (3.0 × 10³)?
When adding numbers in scientific notation with the same exponent, add the coefficients and keep the exponent: (5.0 + 3.0) × 10³ = 8.0 × 10³.
What is 250,000 in scientific notation?
Move the decimal 5 places left: 250,000 = 2.5 × 10⁵. The coefficient 2.5 is between 1 and 10.
What is the 'coefficient' in scientific notation?
The coefficient is the number 'a' in a × 10ⁿ. It must satisfy 1 ≤ a < 10 for the notation to be in proper scientific form.
What is 'order of magnitude'?
Order of magnitude is the power of 10 nearest to a value. For example, 5,000 has an order of magnitude of 10³, and 50,000 has an order of magnitude of 10⁴.
A pharmaceutical company produces 6.0 × 10⁸ tablets per year. If each tablet contains 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ g of active ingredient, what is the total mass of active ingredient used per year?
Multiply: (6.0 × 10⁸)(2.5 × 10⁻⁴) = 15.0 × 10⁴ = 1.5 × 10⁵ g. Coefficients are multiplied and exponents are added (8 + (−4) = 4), then adjusted.
Real-World Applications
Example: How do you multiply two numbers in scientific notation?
For (a × 10ᵐ)(b × 10ⁿ), multiply the coefficients (a × b) and add the exponents (m + n). If the resulting coefficient is ≥ 10, adjust by increasing the exponent by 1.

--- Lesson 2.2: Significant Figures (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Significant Figures
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 3.456 rounded to 3 significant figures?
To round 3.456 to 3 sig figs, look at the 4th digit (6). Since 6 ≥ 5, round the 5 up to 6, giving 3.46.
What is a 'captive zero'?
Captive zeros (also called sandwiched zeros) sit between non-zero digits, such as the 0 in 1002. They are always significant because they represent a measured value.
What is a 'leading zero'?
Leading zeros are placeholders that locate the decimal point (e.g., 0.0045). They are never significant because they do not represent measured values.
What is a 'trailing zero'?
Trailing zeros come at the end of a number. In 2500 (no decimal), the trailing zeros are ambiguous. In 2500. or 2.500, the trailing zeros are significant because the decimal point confirms they were measured.
What is the multiplication/division rule for significant figures?
For multiplication and division, the result must have the same number of significant figures as the input with the fewest sig figs. The decimal-places rule applies only to addition and subtraction.
What is the addition/subtraction rule for significant figures?
For addition and subtraction, the result is rounded to the fewest decimal places of the input values, because the least precise measurement limits the precision of the sum.
A student measures 25.36 mL of solution and adds it to 100.2 mL already in a beaker. What is the total volume reported with proper sig figs?
Addition uses the decimal-places rule. 100.2 has 1 decimal place (the limiting value) and 25.36 has 2. The sum 125.56 rounds to 125.6 (1 decimal place).
A pharmacist weighs 2.50 g of a drug on an analytical balance and dissolves it in 100.0 mL of water. What is the concentration in g/mL reported correctly?
2.50 g has 3 sig figs; 100.0 mL has 4 sig figs. Division uses the fewest sig figs (3). 2.50 ÷ 100.0 = 0.0250 g/mL (3 sig figs; the leading zeros are not significant).
In a lab, a student measures the length of a metal strip as 15.2 cm and its width as 3.05 cm. What is the area with correct significant figures?
15.2 has 3 sig figs; 3.05 has 3 sig figs. The product 15.2 × 3.05 = 46.36, rounded to 3 sig figs = 46.4 cm².
Real-World Applications
Example: When multiplying 4.56 × 1.4, how many significant figures should the answer have?
In multiplication and division, the answer has the same number of sig figs as the factor with the fewest. 4.56 has 3 and 1.4 has 2, so the answer gets 2 sig figs.
Example: What is the multiplication/division rule for significant figures?
For multiplication and division, the result must have the same number of significant figures as the input with the fewest sig figs. The decimal-places rule applies only to addition and subtraction.
Example: A chemist multiplies a mass of 5.0 g by a volume of 12.345 mL to calculate density. How many sig figs should the answer have?
For multiplication, the answer takes the fewest sig figs. 5.0 g has 2 sig figs and 12.345 mL has 5, so the answer is reported with 2 sig figs.

--- Lesson 2.3: Accuracy vs. Precision (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Accuracy vs. Precision
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the percent error if a student measures the density of aluminum as 2.85 g/cm³ when the accepted value is 2.70 g/cm³?
|2.85 − 2.70| / 2.70 × 100% = 0.15 / 2.70 × 100% ≈ 5.56%. The percent error quantifies how far the experimental value is from the accepted value.
What is 'percent error'?
Percent error calculates how far an experimental result deviates from the accepted value as a percentage. A smaller percent error means better accuracy.
What is a 'systematic error'?
Systematic errors are predictable and consistent — for example, a scale that always reads 2 g too heavy. They shift all measurements in one direction, reducing accuracy.
What is a 'random error'?
Random errors are irregular fluctuations that cause readings to scatter above and below the true value. They reduce precision but can be minimized by averaging multiple trials.
What is an example of a systematic error?
A miscalibrated balance always shifts readings by the same amount in the same direction — the hallmark of systematic error. The other options describe random variations.
A student obtains 78.5°C for the boiling point of ethanol. The accepted value is 78.4°C. What is the percent error?
|78.5 − 78.4| / 78.4 × 100% = 0.1 / 78.4 × 100% ≈ 0.13%. A very small percent error shows high accuracy.
A forensic chemist measures a drug sample mass as 1.234 g. The certified value is 1.250 g. What is the percent error?
|1.234 − 1.250| / 1.250 × 100% = 0.016 / 1.250 × 100% = 1.28%. This moderate percent error might be acceptable depending on the analytical method used.
Real-World Applications

Random errors scatter in both directions and tend to cancel when averaged over many trials. Systematic errors are consistent and do not cancel with averaging.

--- Lesson 2.4: Metric System (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Metric System
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the SI system?
SI stands for Système International d'Unités. It provides a universal set of base units (meter, kilogram, second, kelvin, mole, ampere, candela) used worldwide in science.
What is a 'base unit' in the metric system?
Base units are the fundamental, defined units in the SI system (meter, kilogram, second, etc.). All other units (like cm, mL, km) are derived by adding prefixes.
What is the SI base unit of temperature?
The kelvin (K) is the SI base unit of temperature. Celsius is commonly used in labs but is not the official SI unit. Kelvin starts at absolute zero.
What is the SI base unit for amount of substance?
The mole is the SI unit for amount of substance. One mole contains Avogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³) of particles.
What is a 'derived unit'?
Derived units are combinations of base units. For example, speed (m/s), density (kg/m³), and force (kg·m/s² = newton) are all derived SI units.
A biologist measures a red blood cell at 7 μm in diameter. What is this in meters?
Micro- (μ) means 10⁻⁶, so 7 μm = 7 × 10⁻⁶ m = 0.000007 m.
A lab technician measures 0.250 L of a stock solution. She needs to express this in milliliters for a smaller graduated cylinder. What is the value?
To convert liters to milliliters, multiply by 1,000: 0.250 L × 1,000 mL/L = 250 mL.
An environmental scientist reports a pollutant concentration of 5 μg/L. What is this in mg/L?
Micro- (μ) = 10⁻⁶ and milli- = 10⁻³. Since 1 mg = 1,000 μg, divide: 5 μg ÷ 1,000 = 0.005 mg. The concentration is 0.005 mg/L.

--- Lesson 2.5: Unit Conversions (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Unit Conversions
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is 25°C in Kelvin?
K = °C + 273 = 25 + 273 = 298 K. This is standard room temperature.
What is 'dimensional analysis'?
Dimensional analysis (also called the factor-label method) systematically converts units by multiplying by conversion factors arranged so unwanted units cancel.
What is a 'conversion factor'?
A conversion factor expresses the same quantity in two units as a fraction equal to 1 (e.g., 1000 m / 1 km = 1). Multiplying by it changes units without changing the value.
What is 'unit cancellation'?
Unit cancellation is the key mechanism in dimensional analysis: when a unit appears in both the numerator and denominator, it cancels, leaving the desired target unit.
What is a 'multi-step conversion'?
Multi-step conversions are needed when no direct conversion factor exists. For example, converting inches to kilometers requires going inches → cm → m → km.
What is a 'derived unit'?
Derived units result from mathematical operations on base units. Speed (m/s), density (kg/m³), and pressure (Pa = kg/(m·s²)) are all derived SI units.
A reaction requires heating to 500 K. What is this temperature in Celsius?
°C = K − 273 = 500 − 273 = 227°C.
A student needs to convert 5.0 km to meters and then to centimeters. What is the final answer?
5.0 km × (1,000 m/km) = 5,000 m. Then 5,000 m × (100 cm/m) = 500,000 cm. This is a multi-step conversion.
A chemist measures the density of a liquid as 0.85 g/mL. What is its density in kg/L?
Since 1 kg = 1,000 g and 1 L = 1,000 mL, the conversion factors cancel: (0.85 g/mL) × (1 kg/1,000 g) × (1,000 mL/1 L) = 0.85 kg/L.
A European weather report gives the temperature as 35°C. A tourist wants to know the temperature in Kelvin. What is it?
K = °C + 273 = 35 + 273 = 308 K.
A student converts 0.045 kg to milligrams. The setup is: 0.045 kg × (1000 g / 1 kg) × (1000 mg / 1 g). What is the answer?
0.045 kg × 1,000 g/kg = 45 g. Then 45 g × 1,000 mg/g = 45,000 mg. Each conversion factor cancels the previous unit.
A gas cylinder label reads a pressure of 2.5 atm. A lab manual requires the value in kPa. Using 1 atm = 101.325 kPa, what is the pressure?
2.5 atm × 101.325 kPa/atm = 253.3 kPa. The atm units cancel, leaving kilopascals.

--- Lesson 2.6: Graphing Data (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Graphing Data
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What must every axis on a graph include to describe what is being measured?
Every axis needs a label that tells the reader what variable is being measured and the units used (such as grams, seconds, or degrees Celsius).
What is the name for the text at the top of a graph that describes what it shows?
The title of a graph appears at the top and tells the reader what the graph is about, usually describing the relationship being shown.
What is the point called where the x-axis and y-axis meet on a graph?
The origin is the point where the x-axis and y-axis cross, and it usually represents a value of zero for both axes.
What is an independent variable in an experiment?
The independent variable is the factor that the experimenter intentionally changes or controls to observe its effect on the dependent variable.
What is a dependent variable?
The dependent variable is the outcome being measured in an experiment. It depends on and responds to changes made to the independent variable.
What is a best-fit line?
A best-fit line (or trend line) is a straight or curved line drawn through a scatter plot that best represents the overall pattern of the data, even if it does not pass through every point.
What is a data point on a graph?
A data point is a single value or observation plotted on a graph. It represents one pair of measurements for the independent and dependent variables.
What is a trend in graphing?
A trend is the general direction or pattern that data follows on a graph. It can be increasing, decreasing, or showing no clear pattern.
What is a legend on a graph?
A legend (or key) is a box on a graph that identifies what different colors, symbols, or line styles represent, which is especially useful when multiple data sets are displayed.
A student's graph uses intervals of 2, 5, 10, and then 25 on the y-axis. What is wrong with this graph's axis?
Graph axes must use equal intervals (like counting by 5s or 10s) so the visual spacing accurately represents the data. Unequal intervals distort the graph and misrepresent the data.
A graph shows that as altitude increases, temperature decreases. A student uses this graph to predict the temperature at an altitude much higher than any data collected. What is the student doing?
Extrapolation is the process of predicting values beyond the range of collected data by extending the observed trend. This can be less reliable because the trend may change outside the measured range.
Real-World Applications
Example: What type of graph shows parts of a whole as slices or percentages?
A circle graph (also called a pie chart) divides a circle into slices to show how different parts make up a whole, often displayed as percentages.
Example: A class surveys students about their favorite type of music. They want to show what percentage of students prefer each genre. Which graph should they use?
A pie chart (circle graph) is ideal for showing what percentage each category makes up of the whole. Since the class wants to show the proportion of students preferring each genre, a pie chart is the best choice.
Example: A researcher collected data on hours studied versus test scores for 30 students. She wants to see if there is a relationship between the two. Which graph type is most appropriate?
A scatter plot is best for showing the relationship between two numerical variables. Each student becomes a data point plotted by hours studied (x) and test score (y).

  Key Concepts: Experimental Design
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the first step of the scientific method?
The scientific method begins with asking a question about something you observe. This question guides the rest of the investigation.
What is the purpose of collecting data during an experiment?
Data is collected during an experiment so it can be analyzed to determine whether the results support or reject the hypothesis.
What is a hypothesis?
A hypothesis is a testable prediction or educated guess about the outcome of an experiment. It is often written as an 'if...then' statement.
What is a control group in an experiment?
The control group is the standard of comparison in an experiment. It is treated exactly like the experimental group except it does not receive the treatment or independent variable being tested.
What is a variable in a scientific experiment?
A variable is any factor in an experiment that can change or be changed. The three main types are independent, dependent, and controlled variables.
What is a scientific theory?
A scientific theory is a well-tested, well-supported explanation for observations and phenomena, backed by a large body of evidence from many experiments and observations.
What is a scientific law?
A scientific law is a statement based on repeated experimental observations that describes a consistent pattern in nature, such as the law of gravity or the law of conservation of mass.
What is a sample size in an experiment?

--- Lesson 2.8: Density Calculations (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Density Calculations
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for calculating density?
Density is calculated by dividing the mass of an object by its volume: D = M / V. This tells you how much matter is packed into a given space.
What is the standard unit of density for solids in the metric system?
The standard unit for the density of solids is grams per cubic centimeter (g/cm cubed). For liquids, grams per milliliter (g/mL) is commonly used, and 1 cm cubed = 1 mL.
What is the density of pure water at room temperature?
Pure water at room temperature has a density of approximately 1.0 g/mL (or 1.0 g/cm cubed). This value is commonly used as a reference point for comparing densities.
An object has a mass of 50 grams and a volume of 25 cubic centimeters. What is its density?
Using D = M / V: 50 g divided by 25 cm cubed = 2.0 g/cm cubed. The object has a density of 2.0 grams per cubic centimeter.
What is density?
Density is a measure of how much mass is contained in a given volume. It describes how tightly matter is packed together in a substance.
What is mass?
Mass is the amount of matter in an object, measured in grams or kilograms. Unlike weight, mass does not change based on location or gravity.
What is volume?
Volume is the amount of three-dimensional space an object or substance takes up, measured in units such as cubic centimeters, milliliters, or liters.
What is an intensive property?
An intensive property does not depend on the amount of substance present. Density, color, and boiling point are all intensive properties that remain the same regardless of sample size.
What is a graduated cylinder?
A graduated cylinder is a tall, narrow container with measurement markings used to accurately measure the volume of liquids. It can also be used for water displacement.
What is an extensive property?
An extensive property depends on the amount of substance. Mass and volume are extensive properties because they change when you have more or less of a substance.
A student has a rock with a mass of 30 g. She places it in a graduated cylinder containing 20 mL of water, and the water level rises to 32 mL. What is the density of the rock?
First find the volume: 32 mL - 20 mL = 12 mL. Then use D = M/V: 30 g divided by 12 mL = 2.5 g/mL.
A student has a metal cube that measures 2 cm on each side and has a mass of 64 g. What is the density of the metal?
First find the volume of the cube: 2 cm x 2 cm x 2 cm = 8 cm cubed. Then D = M/V: 64 g divided by 8 cm cubed = 8.0 g/cm cubed.

--- Lesson 2.9: Percent Error (Summary) ---

  Key Concepts: Percent Error
Overview
This lesson covers the fundamental measures of center used to analyze and summarize data sets. You'll learn how to calculate and apply the mean, median, and mode, and understand when each is most appropriate.
Key Definitions
What is the formula for percent error?
Percent error = |Experimental value - Accepted value| / Accepted value x 100. The absolute value bars ensure the result is always positive.
A student measures the boiling point of water as 99 degrees Celsius. The accepted value is 100 degrees Celsius. What is the percent error?
|99 - 100| / 100 x 100 = 1/100 x 100 = 1%. The student's measurement was only 1% off from the accepted value, which is excellent accuracy.
What is the 'accepted value' in a percent error calculation?
The accepted value is the true, correct, or standard value as determined by scientists and found in reference materials, textbooks, or scientific literature.
What is the 'experimental value' in a percent error calculation?
The experimental value is the value that a student or scientist actually measures during an experiment. It is compared to the accepted value to determine accuracy.
What is percent error?
Percent error is a calculation that expresses the difference between an experimental value and the accepted value as a percentage of the accepted value.
What is a systematic error?
A systematic error is a consistent, repeatable error that shifts all measurements in the same direction, often caused by faulty equipment, incorrect calibration, or flawed technique.
What is a random error?
Random errors are unpredictable variations that cause measurements to scatter around the true value. They can be caused by slight changes in conditions, reading instruments, or human judgment.
What is human error in a lab setting?
Human error refers to mistakes made by the person conducting the experiment, such as misreading a measurement, spilling a substance, or not following the procedure correctly.
A student measures the density of aluminum as 2.5 g/cm cubed. The accepted density is 2.7 g/cm cubed. What is the percent error?
|2.5 - 2.7| / 2.7 x 100 = 0.2 / 2.7 x 100 = 7.4%. The student's measurement was about 7.4% off from the true density of aluminum.
A recipe requires exactly 250 mL of vinegar. A student measures 245 mL. What is the percent error of the student's measurement?
|245 - 250| / 250 x 100 = 5/250 x 100 = 2.0%. The student's measurement was 2% off from the desired amount.
A student measures the density of a liquid as 1.15 g/mL. The accepted value is 1.00 g/mL. What is the percent error?
|1.15 - 1.00| / 1.00 x 100 = 0.15 / 1.00 x 100 = 15%. This is a relatively high percent error, suggesting significant measurement issues.
A student measures the freezing point of a solution as -5 degrees Celsius. The accepted value is -4 degrees Celsius. What is the percent error?
|-5 - (-4)| / |-4| x 100 = |-1| / 4 x 100 = 1/4 x 100 = 25%. Even though the difference is only 1 degree, the percent error is high because the accepted value itself is small.
